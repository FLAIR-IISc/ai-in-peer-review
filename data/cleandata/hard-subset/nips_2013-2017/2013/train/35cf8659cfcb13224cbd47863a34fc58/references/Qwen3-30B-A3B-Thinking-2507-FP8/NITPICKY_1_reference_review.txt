

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

This paper proposes a Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD) method for inferring hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering, extending them to relational data. The key contribution is an efficient greedy inference algorithm that achieves O(n² log n) time complexity, significantly faster than existing Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM). The paper demonstrates BHCD on Sampson's monastery network and NIPS co-authorship data, claiming it produces comparable predictive performance to IRM while being two orders of magnitude faster.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents a well-motivated approach to hierarchical community discovery that addresses a gap in Bayesian nonparametric methods. The theoretical development of the hierarchical model as a nested mixture of stochastic blockmodels is sound, and the dynamic programming approach to computing the marginal likelihood is clever. The experimental results on real-world networks are promising, and the comparison to IRM provides useful context for the method's efficiency. The authors clearly articulate the computational advantages of BHCD over existing methods, particularly its independence from the number of communities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical inconsistencies and unclear notation:** The paper contains multiple notational inconsistencies that undermine clarity. In equation (1), "n1pq" and "n0pq" are used without prior definition. Equation (2) uses "B(α+ n1pp, β + n0pp)" with inconsistent spacing that could cause confusion with the Beta function notation. The notation "σpq = σqp" is confusing as it's not clear what the equality implies in the context of directed vs. undirected graphs. The paper states "σ{x}{x} = 0 for each vertex x" but doesn't explain why this notation is used or how it relates to the adjacency matrix representation.

2. **Lack of technical justification:** The paper claims BHCD is "two orders of magnitude faster than a single iteration of the IRM" but provides no timing data to support this assertion. The comparison in Table 1 lists time complexities but doesn't provide actual runtime measurements. The claim that "BHCD is able to make reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan" is unsupported by concrete timing data in the experiments.

3. **Methodological gaps:** The paper mentions "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap" but never explains what "BHCD global" refers to or why this occurs. The paper also doesn't adequately address why the proposed model would outperform simpler approaches like greedy hierarchical clustering without the Bayesian framework, which is a key question for the contribution.

4. **Experimental details:** The hyperparameter values (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) are chosen without justification or sensitivity analysis. The paper claims BHCD sparse "runs faster for sparse networks" but doesn't provide evidence beyond the claim, and the timing comparisons are not sufficiently detailed.

5. **Formatting and presentation:** The paper contains grammatical inconsistencies (e.g., "lead astray" instead of "led astray") and unclear sentences. The reference to "Figure 3" and "Figure 4" in the text is problematic since the figures are not included in the submitted paper, making it impossible to verify the claims about the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a technically interesting approach to hierarchical community discovery with potentially useful computational advantages, the numerous technical inconsistencies, lack of concrete evidence to support key claims, and insufficient experimental detail significantly undermine its contribution. The paper would benefit from a more careful proofreading to resolve notational inconsistencies, providing actual runtime data to support the efficiency claims, and clarifying the methodological distinctions from related work.

The paper's main contribution appears to be an efficient inference algorithm rather than a fundamentally new model. The claim that BHCD produces "good results" is supported only by qualitative observations without sufficient quantitative comparison to baselines. The paper also fails to adequately address how the proposed method compares to non-Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches, which would be a natural baseline.

**Recommendation:** Reject

The paper's numerous technical inconsistencies and lack of concrete evidence to support its key claims prevent it from being considered a solid scientific contribution. While the problem addressed is relevant to NIPS, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the novelty or significance of its approach. The authors should address the technical inconsistencies, provide concrete runtime data, and conduct more thorough experimental comparisons before resubmission.
