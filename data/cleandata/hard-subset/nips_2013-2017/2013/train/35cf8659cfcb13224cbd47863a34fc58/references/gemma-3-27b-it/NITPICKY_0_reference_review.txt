This paper proposes a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea is to extend existing Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering approaches to relational data, leveraging stochastic blockmodels within a hierarchical framework. While the authors identify a genuine need for scalable Bayesian methods for hierarchical community detection, and the proposed approach demonstrably outperforms the IRM in speed, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of sufficient justification for several design choices that ultimately undermine its overall impact and warrant rejection.

The introduction, while adequately motivating the problem, is somewhat verbose and lacks precision. The claims regarding speed improvements (“two orders of magnitude faster”) are impressive but lack context until Table 1, and even then, the comparison to a “single iteration of the IRM” feels misleading – a fairer comparison would be to a converged IRM solution. The brief mention of limitations in existing Bayesian methods is accurate, but could be strengthened by more specific citations regarding the computational bottlenecks. The organization of the paper, as stated, is followed, but section numbering is not included in the paper itself. 

Section 2 provides a standard review of stochastic blockmodels, but its inclusion feels somewhat unnecessary. While foundational, the content is readily familiar to the NIPS audience, and the section could be significantly condensed. The transition to the Beta prior discussion is abrupt; a clearer explanation of the rationale behind choosing conjugate priors despite acknowledging the computational benefits of non-conjugate alternatives would be beneficial. Equation (3) appears without adequate motivation. Why present a general exponential family formulation if the remainder of the paper focuses on the beta-Bernoulli case?

Section 3 introduces the core Bayesian hierarchical model. The tree-based prior on partitions is a clever idea, and the explanation using Figure 1 is helpful. However, the notation is inconsistent. 'T' is used both to denote the entire tree and subtrees, creating ambiguity. The recursive definition of Φ(T) in (5) is awkwardly presented and difficult to parse without careful study. The justification for choosing πS = 1− (1− γ)|ch(S)| is weak, simply stating it gives higher likelihood to non-binary trees *when there is no hierarchical structure*. What about when there *is* hierarchical structure? Furthermore, the derivation of (7) and (8) lacks crucial details, leaving the reader to infer the logical steps. The phrase “tree-consistent partitions” is used repeatedly without formal definition beyond (5).

Section 4 details the agglomerative model selection procedure. The explanation of the likelihood calculation (11) and the SCORE function (12) is clear, but the motivation for using *local* data for the merge score is insufficiently explained. Why is this assumption valid? The discussion of variations (BHCD sparse, binary versions) is brief, and the claim about better interpretability of non-binary hierarchies is subjective and unsupported. Algorithm 1 is well-structured, but the description of the caching scheme lacks detail. The claim of O(1) time for inner loop computations seems overly optimistic, given the operations described in (13). The explanation of tie-breaking is inadequate—simply stating “at random” is unsatisfactory.

Section 5 presents experimental results. The qualitative analysis of Sampson’s monastery network is convincing, but the comparison to other algorithms is limited. The NIPS co-authorship experiment is more compelling, but the presentation of results in Figure 4 is unclear. The axes are not labelled appropriately, and the legend is confusing. The statement that BHCD “makes reasonable predictions before the IRM has completed a single Gibbs scan” is vague. The discussion of the full NIPS dataset is overly descriptive and lacks critical analysis. The choice of the 0.5 cutoff for retaining clusters is arbitrary and not justified.

Throughout the paper, several minor issues detract from its polish. There are numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., “rose trees” should be “rooted trees”). The figure captions are often incomplete or poorly worded. The citation style is inconsistent. Equations are not consistently numbered. The code is not made available, hindering reproducibility.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, terminology, and presentation.
*   Lack of sufficient justification for key design choices (e.g., choice of priors, local merge score).
*   Incomplete derivations and explanations, hindering understanding.
*   Limited experimental evaluation and lack of reproducibility.
*   Overly verbose and occasionally unclear writing style.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important problem in network analysis.
*   Proposes a novel approach to Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
*   Demonstrates a significant speed advantage over existing methods.

**Overall:**

Despite the novelty of the proposed approach and its potential for scalability, the paper suffers from a pervasive lack of rigor and attention to detail. The numerous minor flaws, inconsistencies, and unjustified choices collectively undermine its scientific credibility. While the speed improvement is a positive contribution, it is not sufficient to outweigh the paper’s weaknesses. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. A major revision addressing the points outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration.
