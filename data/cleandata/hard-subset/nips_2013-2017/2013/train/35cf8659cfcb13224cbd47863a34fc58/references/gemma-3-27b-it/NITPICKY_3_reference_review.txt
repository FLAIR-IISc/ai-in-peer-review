## Review of Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

This paper introduces a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea – extending Bayesian approaches to greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data – is a reasonable direction, building upon existing work in stochastic blockmodels, the infinite relational model, and Bayesian hierarchical clustering. The authors position their work as a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian nonparametric methods, which often suffer from scalability issues.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Computational Efficiency:** The claimed efficiency – two orders of magnitude faster than a single IRM iteration and quadratic runtime – is a significant potential contribution.  The caching scheme detailed in Section 4.1 appears to be a key enabler of this efficiency, and the O(n+m) likelihood calculation is a notable achievement.
*   **Greedy Approach:** Leveraging a greedy approach offers a practical solution for scaling to larger networks, bypassing the computational bottlenecks of full Bayesian inference.
*   **Clear Model Description:** The mathematical formulation of the model, particularly the integration of stochastic blockmodels and the tree-based prior (Section 3), is relatively clear and well-defined. The use of conjugate priors, while potentially limiting expressiveness, is justified by computational expediency.
*   **Empirical Validation:**  The paper presents results on Sampson’s monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship data, demonstrating the method’s ability to recover known community structure and achieve competitive performance with existing approaches.
*   **Variants explored:** The inclusion of sparse variants and binary tree restrictions shows a thoughtful consideration of practical implementation trade-offs.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigorous Comparison:** While the comparison to IRM is presented, it feels somewhat superficial. The IRM implementation details (e.g., number of MCMC steps, initialization) are not fully specified, hindering a fair assessment. The comparison to other methods (LFRM, ILA) in Table 1 is useful, but the discussion is limited.  Specifically, stating “BHCD performs similarly to LFRM, worse than ILA, and better IRM” lacks quantitative substantiation beyond the presented figures.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The choice of hyperparameters (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) is stated as “working well”, but a sensitivity analysis to these hyperparameters is entirely absent. The impact of varying these parameters on performance, particularly on the discovered hierarchy and predictive accuracy, remains unclear.
*   **Tie-Breaking Procedure:** The random tie-breaking procedure in the greedy search is a potential source of instability and suboptimal results.  A more principled tie-breaking strategy, or an investigation of the effect of random tie-breaking, would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the application to relational data is reasonable, the overall approach feels incremental. Combining Bayesian hierarchical clustering with stochastic blockmodels is a natural extension, and the core algorithmic innovation (the caching scheme) while efficient, doesn't fundamentally alter the landscape of community detection.
*   **Minor Technical Issues & Formatting:**
    *   Equation (5) – the formalization of Φ(T) appears incomplete and lacks sufficient detail for full comprehension.
    *   The notation in Equation (13) is slightly unclear; it is not immediately obvious what the sums are summing over.
    *   Section 2 refers to "flat communities" repeatedly; a more consistent terminology would be preferable.
    *   Several sentences are awkwardly phrased (e.g., “BHCD finds the most likely tree, and rose trees typically better explain the data”).
    *   Figure 3 lacks a clear caption explaining the different BHCD variants.
*   **Predictive Evaluation:** The predictive evaluation focuses on link prediction. While relevant, assessing the quality of the *hierarchy itself* is crucial for a hierarchical community discovery method. Metrics evaluating the alignment of the discovered hierarchy with ground truth (if available) or human expert opinion would strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the claimed O(n^2 log n) complexity is an improvement, the authors acknowledge a potential bottleneck for sparse networks where *m* < *n*^2.  Further investigation into exploiting sparsity for improved scalability is needed.

**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

*   Provides a computationally efficient alternative for Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
*   Demonstrates reasonable performance on standard datasets.
*   Presents a clear and well-defined model.

**Con Acceptance:**

*   Lacks a rigorous and comprehensive comparison to existing methods.
*   Hyperparameter sensitivity is not addressed.
*   The core contribution feels incremental.
*   Minor technical and formatting issues detract from the presentation.
*   Insufficient validation of the discovered hierarchy's quality.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the promising aspects of BHCD, the weaknesses, particularly the lack of a thorough comparative analysis and the absence of a hyperparameter sensitivity study, are substantial. The paper’s claims of efficiency are compelling, but require stronger empirical justification. While the work is technically sound, it doesn’t present a sufficiently significant advance to warrant acceptance to NIPS in its current form.

**Recommendation:** **Reject**.  I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit a revised version. Specifically, a more comprehensive experimental evaluation, a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters, and a more detailed discussion of the method’s limitations are crucial. A more robust justification for the chosen tie-breaking strategy would also be beneficial.
