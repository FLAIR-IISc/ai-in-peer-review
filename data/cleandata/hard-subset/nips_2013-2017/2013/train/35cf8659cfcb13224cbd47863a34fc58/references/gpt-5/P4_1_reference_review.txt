Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a probabilistic model and greedy inference procedure to uncover hierarchical community structure in networks. The model is a hierarchical mixture of stochastic blockmodels (SBMs). Given a fixed tree T over the vertices, the likelihood marginalizes exactly over all partitions φ that are consistent with T, using a prior over partitions derived from a generative “stop-or-split” process at each internal node with stop probabilities πS = 1 − (1 − γ)|ch(S)| (as in Bayesian Rose Trees). This yields a dynamic-programming recursion for the marginal likelihood p(D|T), where on-diagonal blocks are scored by f (e.g., beta-Bernoulli marginal for within-cluster edges) and inter-child blocks by g (for between-cluster edges).

To learn T, the authors introduce a bottom-up greedy agglomerative procedure that maintains a forest, and at each step merges a pair of subtrees by evaluating a local Bayes factor over three merge types (join, absorb-left, absorb-right), again in the spirit of Bayesian Rose Trees. With appropriate caching of sufficient statistics, the per-merge evaluation is O(1), leading to overall O(n^2 log n) time due to the merge heap, and likelihood evaluation for a fixed T in O(n + m). A sparse variant disallows merges between disconnected subgraphs. A recursive formula is also provided for link prediction using the learned tree.

Experiments include: qualitative results on Sampson’s monastery network; predictive performance and runtime on a 234-node NIPS co-authorship subset compared to IRM (both MCMC and greedy search), with qualitative comparison to LFRM and ILA from prior work; and an exploratory hierarchy on the full NIPS 1–17 co-authorship network (2,864 nodes).

This work connects to: SBMs and mixed-membership SBMs; Bayesian nonparametric community models such as IRM; hierarchical clustering with Bayesian Rose Trees; and hierarchical network models (e.g., extensions of IRM to trees). A closely related line not cited is the hierarchical random graph (HRG) of Clauset, Moore, and Newman (Nature, 2008), which also associates a dendrogram with link probabilities via the LCA and performs link prediction; and more recent hierarchical SBMs with efficient inference (e.g., Peixoto, PRX 2014). Compared to these, BHCD’s novelty lies in summing over tree-consistent partitions via dynamic programming and using a fast greedy Bayesian model selection procedure adapted from Rose Trees to the SBM setting.

Strengths:
- Clear probabilistic construction: integrating out block parameters yields closed-form f and g; the prior over tree-consistent partitions is well motivated and enables exact marginalization for fixed T.
- Efficient inference: a practical O(n^2 log n) agglomerative algorithm with local Bayes factors and careful caching; a sparse variant is sensible for large, sparse graphs.
- Link prediction: a simple, interpretable recursive predictive distribution along the tree path.
- Empirical evidence of speed: on NIPS-234, BHCD reaches reasonable predictive performance much faster than IRM; qualitative results on Sampson match known groupings reasonably well.
- Independence from the number of communities in worst-case runtime; fixed number of steps (no MCMC iterations).

Weaknesses:
- Relation to key prior work is incomplete. HRG (Clauset, Moore, Newman, 2008) also learns dendrograms for network data and performs link prediction; nested/hierarchical SBMs (e.g., Peixoto, 2014) provide scalable hierarchical community discovery. A discussion and empirical comparison to at least HRG would clarify novelty and positioning.
- Limited evaluation. Only one medium-sized dataset with predictive comparisons; reliance on prior papers for LFRM/ILA numbers without a unified experimental setup; no comparison to hierarchical baselines; no large-scale runtime scaling study; no robustness analysis over hyperparameters (α,β,δ,λ,γ) despite strong prior bias (α>β, δ<λ).
- Greedy inference with random tie-breaking and multiple restarts lacks uncertainty quantification over trees; no posterior sampling or variational alternative, and no guarantees of optimality or consistency.
- The choice of πS as a function only of |ch(S)| is inherited from Rose Trees; γ is fixed and not inferred. The effect of γ on depth/branching and predictive performance is not explored.
- Some clarity issues: counting conventions for undirected graphs in Eq. (1)–(2) could be made explicit to avoid double-counting in symmetric blocks; Algorithm 1 is referenced but not fully presented; details of caching and constant-time updates could be elaborated.
- Claims about complexity vs. other methods would be stronger with consistent, optimized implementations and wall-clock comparisons across sizes and densities.

Quality:
- The model and recursions (Eqs. 6–10, 12, 14) are technically sound and align with prior Bayesian hierarchical clustering. The Bayes factor for merges is local and consistent with the forest likelihood factorization.
- The computational claims are plausible given the caching scheme, though a more formal accounting of maintained quantities would aid reproducibility.
- Experimental claims are moderate and generally supported, but breadth and baselines are limited.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. Minor notational clarifications would help (undirected counting, σ definitions); explicitly stating all algorithmic steps would improve reproducibility.

Originality:
- The core novelty is adapting Bayesian Rose Trees to relational data via an SBM marginal, enabling exact marginalization over tree-consistent partitions and fast greedy model selection for hierarchical communities. This appears distinct from IRM-based hierarchical extensions and from HRG/hSBM parameterizations, though conceptual overlap exists; a clearer comparison would strengthen the originality claim.

Significance:
- Provides a practical, fast method for hierarchical community discovery with competitive predictive performance relative to IRM and good qualitative structure. If positioned and compared properly against HRG/hSBM, it can be a useful contribution for scalable hierarchical modeling.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Simple, elegant probabilistic construction with exact marginalization for a fixed tree.
- Efficient and scalable greedy inference with strong empirical speedups.
- Useful link prediction mechanism and qualitative hierarchies on real data.
- Bridges Bayesian hierarchical clustering and network blockmodels.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Missing comparison/discussion with closely related hierarchical network models (HRG, hierarchical SBMs).
- Limited experimental breadth and ablations; no hyperparameter sensitivity or uncertainty quantification.
- Greedy inference may be brittle; results depend on tie-breaking and restarts.
- Some clarity/reproducibility gaps (algorithmic details, caching specifics).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add comparisons to HRG (Clauset–Moore–Newman, Nature 2008) and hierarchical SBMs (e.g., Peixoto 2014) in discussion and, if feasible, experiments.
- Include sensitivity analysis for α,β,δ,λ,γ and possibly infer γ; discuss prior influence explicitly.
- Expand datasets and report scaling with n and m; provide wall-clock benchmarks with optimized baselines.
- Clarify counting conventions for undirected graphs and provide full algorithmic details and code.
- Consider a simple posterior over trees (e.g., stochastic greedy, MCMC over merge trees) to quantify uncertainty.
