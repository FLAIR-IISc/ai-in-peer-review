**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a greedy agglomerative method for inferring hierarchical community structures in networks. The work builds directly upon prior research in Bayesian hierarchical clustering, specifically the "Bayesian Rose Trees" framework of Blundell et al. (2010), adapting it for relational data. The model defines a marginal likelihood over tree structures by summing over all partitions consistent with a given tree. This marginal likelihood can be computed efficiently via dynamic programming. Inference of the hierarchy is performed greedily, starting with each node as a singleton community and iteratively merging pairs of sub-trees that yield the largest increase in the Bayes factor. The authors propose an efficient implementation with a worst-case time complexity of O(n² log n), which is notably independent of the number of communities. The paper demonstrates through experiments on the Sampson's monastery and NIPS co-authorship datasets that BHCD is significantly faster—by orders of magnitude—than comparable Bayesian nonparametric methods like the Infinite Relational Model (IRM), while achieving competitive predictive performance.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound adaptation of an existing clustering framework to the problem of community detection. The core contribution is the demonstration of a significant speed advantage over existing MCMC-based methods, which is a valuable practical result.

However, the manuscript suffers from a number of minor but distracting technical and clarity issues that undermine confidence in the work.
1.  **Algorithmic Complexity:** The authors claim in Section 4 that "each inner loop of the algorithm to take O(1) time" due to caching sufficient statistics. This appears to be incorrect. The update step for a newly merged tree `I` (formed from `X` and `Y`) requires computing `σIJ` for all other trees `J` in the forest. This requires a loop over the `k-1` remaining trees, making the update `O(k)`, not `O(1)`. While the final `O(n² log n)` complexity may still hold, the justification provided is imprecise.
2.  **Predictive Complexity:** Section 4.2 claims the predictive complexity is `Θ(n)`. The recursive formula in (14), however, traverses a single path from the root to a leaf. This suggests a complexity of `O(depth of tree)`, which can be as low as `O(log n)`. The `Θ(n)` claim is overly pessimistic and likely incorrect.
3.  **Experimental Rigor:** Hyperparameters (`α, β, δ, λ, γ`) are stated to be fixed to values that "work well," with no justification, ablation study, or sensitivity analysis. This is insufficient for a rigorous experimental evaluation. Furthermore, the discussion of the Sampson's monastery results is confusing. The paper mentions that "BHCD global is lead astray by out of date scores on its heap". The term "BHCD global" is never defined, and the notion of "out of date scores" raises serious questions about the correctness of the algorithm or its implementation. A properly implemented heap-based greedy algorithm should not suffer from such an issue. This requires significant clarification.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but key sections lack the detail required for full comprehension and reproducibility.
1.  **Model Derivation:** The jump from the sum over partitions in Equation (6) to the dynamic programming recursion in Equation (10) is too abrupt. While this may be detailed in the cited work [14], the current paper would benefit from a more self-contained and intuitive derivation of this central result.
2.  **Notation and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits numerous minor formatting inconsistencies. The footnote on the first page is unattached. The bibliography is poorly formatted: references [1], [5], [17], and [20] are incomplete or inconsistently styled (e.g., missing years, venues, or using bare URLs). Equation (3) introduces a generalization to exponential families that is immediately abandoned, adding unnecessary clutter.
3.  **Ambiguous Terminology:** As mentioned, "BHCD global" is used without definition. The distinction between "BHCD" and "BHCD sparse" is clear, but the results for the base "BHCD" are presented in a way that casts doubt on its utility or correctness.

**Originality:** The work's originality is modest. The core probabilistic model and the greedy inference algorithm are directly imported from Heller & Ghahramani (2005) and Blundell et al. (2010). The primary contribution is the application of this machinery to relational data and a thorough empirical validation of its speed and performance against established network models. While incremental, this is a valid and useful contribution. The authors are transparent about building on prior work.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is potentially high. The community detection literature is replete with methods that are either non-probabilistic or computationally intractable for large networks. BHCD offers a compelling middle ground: a fully Bayesian treatment of hierarchical structure that is orders of magnitude faster than MCMC-based alternatives. If the technical concerns can be addressed, this method could be widely adopted by practitioners who require fast inference of hierarchical communities with probabilistic underpinnings. The demonstration of strong performance in a fraction of the time taken by IRM is the paper's most significant result.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses the important problem of scalable hierarchical community detection.
*   The proposed method (BHCD) is demonstrated to be significantly faster (by two orders of magnitude) than a widely recognized Bayesian nonparametric baseline (IRM).
*   Achieves competitive predictive performance despite its greedy nature and speed.
*   The model is probabilistic, providing a principled way to handle uncertainty and perform model selection (via marginal likelihood).

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Several technical claims (e.g., O(1) inner loop complexity, `Θ(n)` predictive complexity) appear to be incorrect or are poorly justified.
*   The unexplained experimental artifact regarding "out of date scores" for the base BHCD model is a major concern that questions the implementation's correctness or the algorithm's description.
*   The experimental evaluation lacks rigor regarding hyperparameter selection.
*   The paper suffers from a distracting number of minor clarity issues, formatting errors, and inconsistencies.
*   The core technical novelty is limited, as the method is a direct adaptation of prior work in clustering.

**Decision:**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents promising results regarding computational efficiency, the accumulation of minor technical inaccuracies, clarity issues, and unexplained experimental behavior is substantial. The comment about "out of date scores" is particularly troubling. I cannot confidently recommend acceptance without significant clarification and revision of these points. Therefore, I lean towards rejection in its current form.
