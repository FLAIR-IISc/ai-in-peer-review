This paper introduces a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for discovering community structure in social networks. The core idea – leveraging a tree-based prior to enable efficient marginal likelihood calculation for a hierarchical stochastic blockmodel – is a genuinely novel and compelling approach. While the paper doesn’t introduce a groundbreakingly new theoretical framework, the pragmatic focus on scaling Bayesian nonparametric methods for hierarchical community detection is highly valuable. It elegantly sidesteps the computational bottlenecks of existing approaches like the IRM, achieving significant speedups while maintaining reasonable predictive performance. The connection to existing Bayesian greedy clustering work ([13, 14]) is clearly articulated, and the extensions to relational data are well-motivated.

The strengths of the paper are substantial. The proposed method is demonstrably faster than existing Bayesian alternatives, as shown on the NIPS co-authorship dataset. The experiments, while not exhaustive, provide compelling evidence of BHCD’s practical utility. The qualitative analysis on Sampson’s monastery network is insightful, and the scaling analysis is clearly presented. The inclusion of both standard and sparse variants of the algorithm is a positive feature, acknowledging the varying characteristics of real-world networks. The authors thoughtfully address potential limitations, such as the possibility of merging disconnected communities, and provide a mitigation strategy (BHCD sparse). The clear presentation of the marginal likelihood recursion and the caching scheme highlights the technical ingenuity behind the speed improvements.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. The theoretical analysis is somewhat limited. While the complexity analysis is sound, a more in-depth discussion of the statistical properties of the algorithm (e.g., convergence guarantees, posterior consistency) would be beneficial. The experiments, while illustrative, could be expanded. The comparison to other non-Bayesian hierarchical community detection methods (e.g., Louvain algorithm, label propagation) is missing. A more detailed ablation study of the hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) would also strengthen the empirical validation. The predictive performance on the NIPS dataset, while promising, doesn’t dramatically surpass existing methods, suggesting that further refinement may be necessary. The reliance on a Beta prior, while computationally convenient, might be limiting; exploring other prior distributions could lead to improved performance.  Finally, the discussion of how to interpret the learned hierarchies is somewhat brief, and providing guidelines or tools for visualization and analysis would be helpful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The tree-based prior and the resulting efficient marginal likelihood calculation represent a significant step forward in scaling Bayesian hierarchical community detection.
* **Practicality:** The speed advantage over existing methods is substantial, making BHCD applicable to larger and more complex networks.
* **Soundness:** The technical foundations of the algorithm are well-established, and the complexity analysis is rigorous.
* **Potential Impact:**  This work opens up new avenues for exploring hierarchical community structure in large-scale networks, with potential applications in diverse fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** The statistical properties of the algorithm are not thoroughly investigated.
* **Modest Predictive Improvement:** The predictive performance on the NIPS dataset is not a dramatic improvement over existing methods.
* **Missing Comparisons:** The lack of comparison to non-Bayesian methods limits the assessment of BHCD’s overall effectiveness.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The novelty and potential impact of the proposed method outweigh its limitations. BHCD represents a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis, providing a practical and scalable approach to hierarchical community detection. It’s a bold idea, executed well, and addresses a significant challenge in the field. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the problem and proposed a solution that is both theoretically sound and empirically promising. While further refinements and a more comprehensive evaluation would be beneficial, the paper as it stands is a significant advance and deserving of publication in NIPS. I believe this work will inspire further research in this area.
