

# Review of "Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery"

## Summary
This paper proposes a Bayesian hierarchical community discovery (BHCD) method that extends stochastic blockmodels to hierarchical structures using a tree-based representation. The authors frame the problem as greedy model selection over tree structures, claiming their method is two orders of magnitude faster than the Infinite Relational Model (IRM) while achieving comparable predictive performance. The approach uses conjugate priors for computational efficiency and proposes an O(n² log n) algorithm that maintains sufficient statistics to enable O(1) updates during tree merging.

## Quality
The paper presents a technically sound approach to hierarchical community detection, but contains several critical technical inconsistencies that undermine the claims of computational efficiency and model correctness:

1. **Inconsistent complexity claims**: The paper states that the marginal likelihood can be computed in O(n+m) time (Section 3), yet Algorithm 1 is claimed to have O(n² log n) complexity (Section 4). This discrepancy is not explained, making it impossible to verify the claimed efficiency improvements.

2. **Equation (10) inconsistency**: The recursive formula for the marginal likelihood contains a term g(σ¬chTT) multiplied by the product of children's likelihoods. However, according to Equation (8), g(σ¬chSS) represents edges *between* children, not within the parent node. This creates a logical inconsistency in the model definition.

3. **Notation errors**: 
   - Equation (9) uses "σ¬chSS" without defining the "¬ch" operator
   - Section 4.1 contains "BHCD global is lead astray" (should be "led astray")
   - The phrase "whilst" appears in the introduction (British English, NIPS uses American English)

4. **Implementation details**: The paper states hyperparameters were "set to α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, and γ = 0.4 which we found to work well" but provides no justification for these values or analysis of parameter sensitivity.

5. **Table 1 inconsistencies**: The IRM (naive) complexity is listed as O(n²K²_IR), but K_IR is never defined in the paper.

## Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized, but suffers from several clarity issues:

1. The definition of "tree-consistent partitions" (Equation 5) is not sufficiently intuitive for readers unfamiliar with hierarchical partitioning.

2. The predictive distribution formula (Equation 14) contains a misleading description: "the predictive distribution is a mixture of a number of on-diagonal posterior f terms... and finally an off-diagonal posterior g term." This implies a single g term, but the formula shows multiple g terms depending on the tree structure.

3. The description of BHCD sparse (Section 4.1) states it "only needs to perform computations on the parts of the network with edges present," but the algorithm (Algorithm 1) doesn't explicitly handle sparse networks differently.

4. The paper claims "BHCD sparse" has the same worst-case complexity as BHCD, but the sparse variant should have O(m log n) complexity for sparse networks (m << n²), not O(n² log n).

## Originality
The paper builds on prior work in Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13, 14] and extends it to relational data. The main novelty is the efficient implementation using dynamic programming and sufficient statistics caching. However, the paper fails to adequately distinguish its contributions from related work:

1. The Mondrian process [12] is described as "proposing a flexible prior on trees," but the Mondrian process is actually a process for partitioning time, not for relational data. This mischaracterization undermines the novelty claim.

2. The paper claims "Current Bayesian nonparametric methods do not scale well," but this is an overgeneralization that doesn't account for efficient implementations of existing methods.

3. The binary tree restriction is mentioned but not sufficiently justified as a novel contribution.

## Significance
The paper makes a potentially useful contribution to community detection, but the significance is diminished by the technical flaws:

1. The claimed speedup (two orders of magnitude faster than IRM) lacks proper experimental context. The paper doesn't specify what "single iteration of IRM" means (Gibbs step? Greedy search step?) or provide comparable computational resources.

2. The experimental section on NIPS-234 compares to IRM but doesn't include results from the most recent Bayesian hierarchical methods like [8], which the paper cites as related work.

3. The paper claims "BHCD always terminates after a fixed number of steps," but the number of steps depends on the number of vertices (n), not being truly fixed.

## Recommendations for Improvement
1. Fix all technical inconsistencies (particularly the complexity claims and Equation 10)
2. Provide proper justification for hyperparameter choices
3. Clarify the relationship between the O(n+m) marginal likelihood computation and the O(n² log n) algorithm complexity
4. Correct the mischaracterization of the Mondrian process
5. Include more details about the IRM implementation for fair comparison

## Final Recommendation
While the paper presents a potentially useful approach to hierarchical community discovery, the technical inconsistencies and lack of clarity in key methodological details significantly undermine the validity of the claims. The paper would benefit from a complete technical revision to resolve these issues before it could be considered for publication. The flaws are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's potentially interesting contribution.

The paper should be rejected due to critical technical inconsistencies that prevent verification of the claimed efficiency improvements.
