- Presents BHCD: a novel greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method using a tree-based prior over stochastic blockmodels for efficient, scalable community discovery.  
- Claims significant speedup (≈100x over a single IRM iteration) with worst-case runtime quadratic in vertices and independent of number of communities.  
- Correctly situates BHCD among stochastic blockmodels, greedy hierarchical clustering, and Bayesian approaches like the IRM; cites related Bayesian nonparametrics and Mondrian/rose tree work.  
- Major strength: computational efficiency via clever caching and algorithmic design.  
- Major strength: scalability—runtime independent of community count, suitable for large/deep hierarchies.  
- Major strength: retains a probabilistic foundation, unlike purely greedy methods.  
- Major strength: empirical validation on Sampson’s network, NIPS subset, and full NIPS showing speed and reasonable accuracy.  
- Major strength: clear exposition and well-structured paper.  
- Major strength: BHCD sparse variant addresses real-world sparse network structure.  
- Weakness: lacks hyperparameter sensitivity analysis and practical guidance for choosing α, β, δ, λ, γ.  
- Weakness: limited empirical comparison to other relevant models (e.g., LFRM, ILA) and shallow analysis of relative strengths/weaknesses.  
- Weakness: predictive performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art; potential model/prior improvements not explored.  
- Weakness: visualization of the full NIPS hierarchy is limited; more levels and highlighted communities would help.  
- Weakness: explanation for BHCD global’s poor performance is brief and needs more detail.  
- Qualitative: technically sound with clear derivations, though some sections (recursive likelihood) are dense.  
- Qualitative: approach is original (hierarchical tree prior + SBM + greedy inference) and the caching scheme is a notable contribution.  
- Significance: offers a practically valuable, efficient tool for hierarchical community detection in large networks.  
- For acceptance: large speedup, scalability, probabilistic framework, clear writing, and promising empirical results.  
- Against acceptance: insufficient hyperparameter analysis, limited state-of-the-art comparisons, and non-SOTA predictive performance.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper; accept with minor revisions to address hyperparameter sensitivity, stronger comparisons, and improved visualizations.
