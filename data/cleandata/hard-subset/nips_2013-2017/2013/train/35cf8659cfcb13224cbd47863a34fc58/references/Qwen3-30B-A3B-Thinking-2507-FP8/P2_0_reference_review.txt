

# Review: Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery

## Summary of Main Ideas
This paper introduces Bayesian Hierarchical Community Discovery (BHCD), a method for discovering hierarchical community structure in social networks. The authors build upon Bayesian hierarchical clustering approaches [13,14] and extend them to relational data. BHCD represents communities as a nested hierarchy (tree structure) where each internal node corresponds to a community and leaves represent individual vertices. The model uses a stochastic blockmodel with conjugate priors (Beta-Bernoulli) and develops an efficient greedy inference algorithm based on dynamic programming. The key contribution is an O(n² log n) inference algorithm that is two orders of magnitude faster than the IRM (Infinite Relational Model) while maintaining comparable predictive performance. The paper demonstrates BHCD on Sampson's monastery network and the NIPS co-authorship network, showing its ability to discover meaningful hierarchical community structures.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and presents a well-constructed probabilistic model. The authors provide a clear mathematical formalization of their model (Equations 2-10) and derive the marginal likelihood using dynamic programming, which is a significant contribution. The inference algorithm (Algorithm 1) is described with sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the time complexity analysis is thorough.

The experimental results are well-supported, with comprehensive comparisons against relevant baselines (IRM, LFRM, ILA) on both predictive performance and runtime. The authors carefully evaluate different variants of BHCD (sparse, binary, etc.) and provide log likelihoods for comparison. The paper acknowledges limitations, such as the tendency to merge disconnected communities (addressed in BHCD sparse), and discusses trade-offs between binary and non-binary trees.

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation with well-defined probabilistic model
- Thorough experimental evaluation with appropriate baselines
- Comprehensive analysis of different algorithm variants
- Good comparison of computational complexity (Table 1)

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't clearly demonstrate *why* hierarchical structure is better than flat structure for the datasets tested. While they show hierarchical structures, they don't provide evidence that hierarchical models lead to better predictive performance or more interpretable results than flat community models.
- The comparison with IRM is primarily focused on runtime and predictive performance, but doesn't explore how the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD compares to the flat structure discovered by IRM in terms of interpretability or downstream utility.
- The "non-binary trees better explain the data" claim (page 5) is somewhat under-supported. While log likelihoods are higher for non-binary trees, the paper doesn't provide a convincing argument for why this is meaningful in the context of community discovery.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly positions the work within the context of existing community detection methods, and the technical sections flow naturally from problem definition to model specification to inference. The figures (Figures 1-5) effectively illustrate key concepts like the tree structure and hierarchical partitions. Algorithm 1 is presented in a standard format that should be easy to implement.

The paper provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the method, including hyperparameter choices (α = δ = 1.0, β = λ = 0.2, γ = 0.4) and implementation details. The notation is consistent throughout, and the mathematical derivations are well-explained.

**Strengths:**
- Clear and logical organization
- Effective use of figures to illustrate concepts
- Sufficient detail for reproducibility
- Well-defined notation

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the hierarchical structure and interpretability. For example, in the NIPS co-authorship network (Figure 5), it's not clear why the hierarchical structure is more useful than a flat community structure for understanding co-authorship patterns.
- The "binary vs non-binary" comparison could be more clearly justified with specific examples from the data.

## Originality

The paper builds upon existing work on Bayesian hierarchical clustering [13,14] but extends it to relational data in a novel way. The key innovation is the application of this approach to community discovery with the specific model and efficient inference algorithm. The paper clearly positions itself against related work, noting limitations of previous methods (e.g., IRM's computational cost, methods limited to specific hierarchy depths).

The paper does cite relevant literature, including recent NIPS papers like [12] (Mondrian process) and [9,10] (hierarchical Bayesian methods). However, it might have cited more recent work on hierarchical community detection, such as the work by Palla et al. [10] (Infinite Latent Attributes model) which they reference in the comparison.

**Strengths:**
- Clear positioning against existing work
- Novel application of Bayesian hierarchical clustering to community discovery
- Efficient inference algorithm with improved complexity

**Weaknesses:**
- The novelty is somewhat incremental; the paper builds on [13,14] rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach to hierarchical community detection.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from other hierarchical community detection methods that have appeared in recent literature (e.g., the work by Ho et al. [11] on multiscale community blockmodels).

## Significance

The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis: efficiently discovering hierarchical community structure in large social networks. The computational speedup (two orders of magnitude over IRM) is significant for practitioners working with large datasets, and the method's scalability (O(n² log n) worst-case complexity) makes it suitable for large dense networks.

The results are important for advancing the state of the art in Bayesian hierarchical community detection, though the paper doesn't demonstrate a major theoretical advance. The method provides a practical solution to a common problem, and the experimental results show that BHCD can make reasonable predictions faster than existing Bayesian methods.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a practical problem with significant computational implications
- Provides a scalable solution for hierarchical community detection
- Demonstrates good predictive performance comparable to state-of-the-art methods

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the hierarchical structure is necessary for the datasets tested. If flat community models perform similarly well, the hierarchical aspect might not be as valuable as claimed.
- The significance would be strengthened if the authors could show how the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD leads to better insights or applications compared to flat community models.

## Overall Assessment

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
1. The paper presents a technically sound model with clear theoretical foundations
2. The inference algorithm is efficient and well-analyzed (O(n² log n) complexity)
3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and includes meaningful comparisons
4. The method provides a significant speedup over existing Bayesian approaches
5. The paper is well-written and clearly organized

**Con Against Acceptance Arguments:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate the value of hierarchical structure over flat community models
2. The "non-binary trees better explain the data" claim is under-supported
3. The novelty is somewhat incremental, building on existing Bayesian hierarchical clustering work
4. The comparison with IRM doesn't explore interpretability or downstream utility of the hierarchical structure

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field with a well-designed and efficient method for hierarchical community discovery. The computational advantages are significant and well-demonstrated, and the method has clear practical value for network analysis. While the paper could be strengthened by providing more evidence for the value of hierarchical structure over flat community models, the technical quality, clarity, and significance of the work are strong enough to warrant acceptance.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the interpretability of the hierarchical structure and how it provides advantages over flat community models, but these are relatively minor points given the overall quality of the work.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Add a section discussing the interpretability of the hierarchical structure discovered by BHCD, with concrete examples from the NIPS dataset.
2. Provide a more thorough comparison between flat community models and hierarchical models in terms of predictive performance and interpretability.
3. Clarify the relationship between the model's hierarchical structure and the actual community structure in the data (e.g., does the hierarchy reflect meaningful real-world groupings?).
4. Consider adding a small experiment showing how the hierarchical structure might be used for specific downstream tasks (e.g., predicting missing links in a way that leverages the hierarchy).

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis, with a clear focus on computational efficiency that addresses an important practical challenge.
