This paper presents a greedy Bayesian hierarchical clustering method (BHCD) for community discovery in social networks. The core idea – extending Bayesian greedy hierarchical clustering to relational data – is quite appealing and potentially impactful. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing Bayesian nonparametric approaches for hierarchical community detection, particularly concerning scalability. BHCD offers a compelling speed advantage over methods like IRM, achieving results two orders of magnitude faster, while maintaining a reasonable time complexity of O(n^2 log n). The authors also demonstrate good performance on both synthetic (Sampson's monastery network) and real-world (NIPS co-authorship network) datasets. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The combination of Bayesian hierarchical clustering with stochastic blockmodels is a novel and promising approach. While Bayesian community detection is established, the efficiency achieved through the greedy, dynamic programming-based inference is a significant contribution. 
* **Scalability:** The reported speed-up compared to IRM is substantial and addresses a critical bottleneck in Bayesian hierarchical modeling. The O(n^2 log n) complexity is reasonable for many network applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are reasonably clear, and the algorithm description is easy to follow.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Sampson's network demonstrate qualitative success and the NIPS co-authorship network provides quantitative evidence of BHCD’s speed and accuracy. The comparison to IRM and other methods is valuable.
* **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of "sparse" variants of the algorithm and discussion of tie-breaking strategies demonstrate attention to practical implementation details.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors state the time complexity, a deeper theoretical analysis of the convergence properties and statistical consistency of the greedy algorithm would strengthen the paper.  How does the greedy nature impact the quality of the inferred hierarchy?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The choice of hyperparameters (α, β, δ, λ, γ) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic investigation of their influence on the results would be beneficial. The authors mention finding values that "work well", but a sensitivity analysis is lacking.
* **Predictive Performance:**  While competitive with other methods, the predictive performance on the NIPS dataset isn't *dramatically* better than existing approaches like LFRM or ILA.  Further analysis of the types of links BHCD struggles to predict, and comparison to more recent link prediction baselines, could be useful.
* **Visualization of Full NIPS Hierarchy:** The visualization of the full NIPS hierarchy, while mentioned, is only partially shown. A more comprehensive visualization would aid in understanding the discovered community structure.
* **Assumptions:** The use of Beta priors and the specific parameterizations impose assumptions about the edge probabilities.  A discussion of the implications of these assumptions would be helpful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper introduces a genuinely novel approach to Bayesian hierarchical community detection, addressing a significant challenge in the field: scalability.
* The reported speed-up is substantial and makes Bayesian hierarchical modeling more feasible for larger networks.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world data.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of deeper theoretical analysis and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis weakens the paper.
* The predictive performance, while good, isn't a breakthrough.
* The incomplete visualization of the full NIPS hierarchy limits understanding of the method’s capabilities.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of community detection. The novelty and scalability of BHCD are compelling, and the empirical results are promising. While some theoretical and experimental refinements would strengthen the work, the advantages of the proposed approach outweigh the weaknesses. I am inclined to **accept** this paper, recognizing that it opens up new avenues for research in Bayesian hierarchical modeling of complex networks. The focus on a bold, novel approach over methodological perfection is appropriate in this case. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified above in future work.
