This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for optimization problems involving a smooth, convex loss function and trace norm regularization. The standard convergence rate for PGM is sublinear. While a linear rate can be shown under a strong convexity assumption on the loss function, many important problems in machine learning, such as matrix completion with a square loss, do not satisfy this condition.

The main contribution of this paper is to prove that PGM can still achieve a linear convergence rate for a broad class of trace norm regularized problems, without requiring strong convexity. The analysis applies to loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex and smooth, and A is an arbitrary linear operator. The key technical tool enabling this result is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized objective, which relates the distance to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map. This work extends a powerful analysis framework, previously applied to lasso and group lasso problems by Luo, Tseng, and others, to the more challenging case of the trace norm, whose epigraph is non-polyhedral.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper establishes the linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization without the restrictive assumption of strong convexity. This is a novel and important theoretical result that advances our understanding of first-order methods for this class of problems.
2.  **Novel Technical Tool:** The development of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem is a key contribution of independent interest. It successfully resolves an open question and provides a new analytical tool for a problem structure that could not be handled by previous techniques used for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The theoretical framework covers many important applications in machine learning, including matrix completion with square loss and multi-task learning with logistic loss, thereby enhancing its relevance and potential impact.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity of Contribution vs. Prior Work:** The paper builds heavily on the error bound analysis framework established by Luo and Tseng. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger if it more explicitly detailed how the proof techniques for the trace norm error bound diverge from those for the ℓ1 and group lasso norms. Highlighting the novel arguments required to handle the coupled, non-separable nature of singular values would better showcase the paper's specific technical novelty.
2.  **Experimental Depth:** The numerical experiments in Section 5 successfully demonstrate the linear convergence predicted by the theory. However, they could be more insightful. The authors could strengthen this section by including experiments that explore the boundaries of their theory. For example, a study showing how the convergence rate is affected by problem parameters that influence the error bound constant (η) would be very valuable. Furthermore, a discussion of practical scenarios where the assumptions might be violated and linear convergence fails would provide a more complete picture.
3.  **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** The contribution is presented as primarily theoretical. A discussion on the practical implications of this linear convergence result would be beneficial. For example, does this analysis suggest how to set the regularization parameter τ or design the operator A to ensure faster convergence?

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Dependence of Constants:** In the proof of Theorem 3.2, the dependence of the constants κ1, κ2, κ3, and κ4 on the step size bounds (α and ᾱ) is not explicitly discussed. Adding a brief explanation of how these constants are affected by the choice of step size would improve the clarity and completeness of the analysis.
*   **Clarity in Proof Derivation:** The derivation of inequality (18) in the proof of Theorem 3.1 could be made more explicit. Specifically, clarifying that the second inequality used in the derivation is a direct consequence of the subgradient inequality for the convex function τ‖·‖∗ at the optimal point X̄k would prevent potential confusion for the reader.
*   **Potential for Extension:** The established error bound is a powerful tool. The paper could be enhanced by including a short discussion on whether this analysis could be extended to other popular algorithms used for trace norm minimization, such as the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM).

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that presents a significant and non-trivial result on the convergence rate of PGM for an important class of machine learning problems. The primary contribution is technically sound and well-motivated. The main weaknesses relate to the depth of the experimental validation and the clarity in positioning the novel aspects of the proof technique. Addressing the suggestions above would further improve the quality and impact of the manuscript.
