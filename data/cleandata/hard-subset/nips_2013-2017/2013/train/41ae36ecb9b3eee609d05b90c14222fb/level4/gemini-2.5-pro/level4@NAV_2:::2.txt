This paper establishes the linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method (PGM) when applied to optimization problems involving a smooth convex loss function and trace norm regularization. The key contribution is that this result is achieved under weaker conditions than the standard strong convexity assumption on the loss function. Instead, the analysis relies on a specific composite structure for the loss, `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex and `A` is a linear operator. The authors' analysis hinges on a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems, which resolves a previously open question and is of independent interest. The overall proof strategy successfully adapts the powerful analysis framework of Luo and Tseng to the matrix setting of trace norm regularization.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant problem. Many machine learning applications, such as matrix completion and multi-task learning, utilize trace norm regularization with loss functions that are not strongly convex. Proving linear convergence for PGM in this common setting is a significant theoretical advancement that better explains the strong empirical performance of the method.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The main technical contribution—the new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—is novel and theoretically sound. The proof is non-trivial, requiring new insights based on matrix perturbation theory to handle the spectral nature of the trace norm, which distinguishes it from prior work on `l1` or group LASSO regularization. The technical details appear correct and are presented rigorously.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and outlining their proof strategy.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Comparison to the `l1`-norm case:** While the introduction briefly mentions why existing techniques for `l1`-norm regularization do not apply, the paper would be substantially strengthened by a more explicit and detailed comparison. A dedicated discussion explaining the core technical challenges introduced by the trace norm (e.g., the non-polyhedral epigraph and the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries) and how the proposed proof technique overcomes them would be very insightful. It would also be helpful for the authors to clarify whether the established result for the trace norm implies the known results for the `l1`-norm as a special case.
*   **Accessibility of concepts:** The terms "Q-linear" and "R-linear" convergence are standard in the optimization literature but may not be familiar to the entire NIPS audience. Briefly defining these terms in Section 3 would improve the paper's self-containedness and accessibility.
*   **Context for the residual map:** For readers familiar with the error bound literature, it would be beneficial to explicitly connect the residual map `R(X)` defined in (7) to the original formulation by Luo and Tseng [10]. This would help clarify its role as a measure of proximity to the optimal set.
*   **Title refinement:** The current title focuses on the trace norm regularization. However, the crucial enabling factor for the analysis is the assumed structure of the loss function `f(X) = h(A(X))`. The authors might consider a slightly more descriptive title that reflects the importance of this composite loss structure.

**Minor Issues:**

*   In the experiments, the operator `P` is referred to as a "sampling operator." Since the operator is deterministic for a given problem instance, "masking operator" or "selection operator" might be a more precise term to avoid connotations of a random process at each step.
*   There appears to be a broken reference in the bibliography (the original review mentioned line 337). Please check all citations.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable and non-trivial theoretical result for a widely used class of optimization problems in machine learning. The analysis is rigorous and the presentation is clear. Addressing the points above, particularly by adding a more detailed comparison to the `l1`-norm case, would further enhance the paper's impact and clarity.
