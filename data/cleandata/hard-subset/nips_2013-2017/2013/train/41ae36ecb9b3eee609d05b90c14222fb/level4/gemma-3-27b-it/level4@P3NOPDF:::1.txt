This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method when applied to trace-norm regularized learning problems, specifically for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strongly convex on compact sets with a Lipschitz gradient. The work extends previous results by Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which demonstrated linear convergence for Lasso and Group Lasso under an “error-bound condition,” to the trace norm regularization setting. While this is a non-trivial extension, the contribution feels largely technical in nature. The presentation of the proofs is generally clear, although some areas could benefit from more precise referencing.

**Strengths:**

*   Successfully demonstrates the linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace-norm regularized problems, building upon the foundations laid by Tseng et al.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The primary contribution appears to be a technical extension of existing theory rather than a fundamentally new insight.
*   The lack of experimental validation is a significant omission. A numerical example demonstrating the established linear convergence rate is crucial for substantiating the theoretical findings.
*   The dependence of key constants (κ<sub>1</sub> through κ<sub>4</sub>) on the parameters ᾱ and ᾱ is not clearly articulated. 

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  The proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 share substantial similarity with those presented by Tseng [20]. To better highlight the novelty of this work, the authors should provide more explicit comparisons and detailed explanations of how their approach differs and extends the original contributions. Specifically, pointing out where adaptations were required to handle the trace norm and clarify the differences in the proof structure would be valuable.

2.  A numerical example illustrating the linear convergence behavior is strongly recommended. A semilog plot of the objective function value against the number of iterations would provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical results. It would be particularly insightful to examine scenarios where the loss function is *not* strongly convex, and to demonstrate linear convergence for varying levels of “stability” (as defined, or potentially re-defined, within the context of this paper). Critically, the authors should explicitly discuss the limitations of their theory and scenarios where it might fail to predict linear convergence. Identifying such failure modes would strengthen the analysis and provide a more nuanced understanding of the conditions required for the theoretical guarantees to hold.

3.  The relationship between the constants κ<sub>1</sub>, κ<sub>2</sub>, κ<sub>3</sub>, and κ<sub>4</sub> and the parameters ᾱ and ᾱ remains unclear. A more detailed explanation of this dependence is necessary to fully understand the practical implications of the theoretical results and how parameter choices affect the convergence rate. 

**Minor Issue:**

4.  The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat confusing. The final inequality is justified by the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that -Ḡ belongs to ∂||X||<sub>*</sub> (as noted on p289 of Tseng [20]), and not by the intermediate inequality. This clarification would improve the clarity of the derivation.

**Potential Future Work:**

The authors should consider discussing the potential for extending this analysis to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). Given the widespread use of ADMM for trace-norm regularized problems, investigating its convergence properties under similar conditions would be a valuable extension of this work.



In summary, this paper presents a solid theoretical contribution extending existing linear convergence results to the trace-norm setting. However, the lack of experimental validation and the somewhat limited discussion of the practical implications of the results detract from its overall impact. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a numerical example and clarifying the parameter dependence of key constants, would significantly strengthen this submission.
