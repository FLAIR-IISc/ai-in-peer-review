### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization problems. While the sublinear global convergence of this method is well-established, and a linear rate is known for strongly convex loss functions, this work extends the analysis to a less restrictive setting. By leveraging the framework of Luo and Tseng, the authors establish a local error bound which they use to prove that the proximal gradient algorithm achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence without requiring strong convexity. The core of their proof relies on a novel observation that allows for the simultaneous diagonalization of the matrices involved, reducing the problem to a more tractable vector case.

### **Evaluation**

**Quality and Technical Soundness:**
The paper appears to be technically sound. The arguments are detailed, and the mathematical derivations follow a logical progression. The central contribution—the establishment of a local error bound for the trace norm problem—is well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix.

**Clarity and Presentation:**
The paper is generally well-written and I was able to follow the main arguments without significant difficulty. However, the presentation could be improved to better guide the reader through its dense technical content. After stating the main local error bound, the authors immediately dive into its highly technical proof. A more reader-friendly approach would be to first show *how* this error bound implies the main result of linear convergence (which is a relatively standard step), and *then* present the detailed proof of the bound itself. This would allow the reader to understand the importance and role of the technical lemma before being immersed in its derivation.

**Originality:**
The originality of this paper is moderate. The overall strategy of establishing a local error bound to prove a linear convergence rate follows the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng. The primary novel contribution is the clever observation that enables the simultaneous diagonalization of the relevant matrices. This is an elegant technique that simplifies the analysis considerably. The remaining steps of the proof, however, do not introduce substantially new concepts.

**Significance:**
The significance of this work is primarily theoretical. Establishing a linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient algorithm in a more practical setting (i.e., without requiring strong convexity) is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature. However, the impact is lessened by the asymptotic nature of the result, which concerns the algorithm's terminal behavior and may not translate to significant speedups in practice. The paper does not propose new algorithms or demonstrate empirical advantages. Nevertheless, the matrix diagonalization technique may be of independent interest to researchers working on related problems.

### **Update After Author Rebuttal**

My initial review raised a question regarding the inequality `R(X^k) <= kappa_3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F` in the appendix (line 085). The authors' rebuttal successfully clarified this point by providing a reference to Lemma 4 in "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting" (Sra, NIPS 2012), where this result is explicitly established. This resolves my main technical concern about the proof's correctness.

### **Conclusion**

This paper provides a solid, though incremental, theoretical contribution. The authors successfully prove that the proximal gradient algorithm achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate for trace norm regularized problems under a relaxed convexity condition. While the overall approach is not new, the work contains a novel and interesting technical idea for handling the matrix analysis. The result is theoretically significant, though its practical implications for algorithm design are limited. Overall, this is a moderately interesting paper that advances our theoretical understanding of a widely used optimization algorithm.
