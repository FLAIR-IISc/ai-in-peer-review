This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving convex optimization problems involving a smooth loss function and a trace norm regularizer. While the PGM is known to have a sublinear rate of convergence, or a linear rate if the loss function is strongly convex, this work demonstrates that a linear rate can be achieved under a less restrictive condition. Specifically, the authors show that for loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function, the PGM converges linearly. This result is significant as it covers many practical applications in machine learning, such as matrix completion with a square loss, where the loss function is not strongly convex. The analysis is based on the framework established by Luo and Tseng, and the primary technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem. The proof of this bound relies on a novel observation about the shared singular vectors of the optimal solution and its corresponding gradient.

**Quality**
The paper's technical arguments appear to be sound. The core of the contribution lies in the proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which enables the application of a known analysis framework to establish linear convergence. However, one step in the proof requires further clarification. In the proof of Theorem 3.2, the inequality `‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk −Xk+1‖F` (line (11)) is stated with a reference to the supplementary material. A more detailed derivation or a more direct citation for this specific result would strengthen the argument, as it is a crucial link in the overall proof structure.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and the main arguments are generally easy to follow. However, the organization could be improved to better highlight the main contributions. Given the technical depth of the error bound proof (Section 4), the paper's readability might benefit from presenting the proof of the main linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) immediately after the statement of the error bound theorem (Theorem 3.1). The technical proof of the error bound could then be deferred. This would allow the reader to first appreciate the connection between the error bound and the linear rate—a connection that is largely standard—before delving into the paper's most technical and novel component.

**Originality**
The originality of this work is moderate but contains a key novel element. The overall strategy of using a local error bound to establish linear convergence for first-order methods follows the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng. However, extending this framework to the trace norm setting is non-trivial and has been an open question. The main novelty lies in the proof of the error bound itself, specifically the clever use of matrix perturbation theory and the observation (Proposition 4.2) that the optimal solution and the negative gradient can be simultaneously diagonalized. This insight allows the authors to overcome the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph, successfully adapting techniques previously used for `l1`-norm and group LASSO problems.

**Significance**
This paper provides an important theoretical contribution by extending the understanding of PGM's convergence behavior. It formally proves that the linear convergence often observed in practice for problems like matrix completion is not an accident, but a predictable outcome under a relaxed set of conditions that do not require strong convexity. The main limitation is that the result is asymptotic, guaranteeing linear convergence only once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. This tempers the direct practical impact, as it does not change the algorithm itself or its global worst-case guarantees. Nonetheless, the result provides valuable theoretical justification for the efficiency of PGM in this problem class. Furthermore, the newly established error bound and the associated proof techniques may be of independent interest to researchers in optimization and matrix analysis.

In summary, this paper proves the asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularized problems under a relaxed convexity assumption common in machine learning. The analysis leverages a well-known framework but is enabled by a novel and technically interesting proof of a local error bound for the trace norm. While the work is primarily theoretical and the result is local in nature, it is a solid, incremental contribution that resolves an open question and deepens our theoretical understanding of a widely used algorithm.
