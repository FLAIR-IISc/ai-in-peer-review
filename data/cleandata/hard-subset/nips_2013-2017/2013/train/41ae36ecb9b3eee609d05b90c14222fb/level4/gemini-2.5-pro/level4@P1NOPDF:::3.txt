This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization. While the sublinear global convergence rate of this algorithm is well-understood, and a linear rate is known for strongly convex loss functions, this work relaxes the strong convexity assumption. Following the framework established by Luo and Tseng, the authors prove a local error bound which they then use to show that the proximal gradient method converges asymptotically at a linear rate. The analysis largely follows the standard template for proving such error bounds, but introduces a new and interesting observation that enables the simultaneous diagonalization of the matrices involved.

**Technical Quality**
The paper appears to be technically sound. My primary initial concern was with a step in the appendix (line 085), specifically the inequality `R(X^k) <= kappa |X^k - X^{k+1}|_F`. In their rebuttal, the authors clarified that the proof for this has been explicitly established in Lemma 4 of "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting" (Sra, NIPS 2012). This reference satisfactorily resolves my question.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written, and I could follow the arguments without major difficulty. However, given the technical nature of the proofs, the presentation could be improved. I would suggest that the authors first present their main claims and their implications before delving into the technical details. For example, after stating the local error bound, it might be more effective to immediately show how it implies the linear convergence rate—a relatively standard step—and defer the highly technical proof of the error bound itself to a later section or the appendix. This restructuring would help the reader grasp the overall structure of the argument before becoming immersed in the proof details.

**Originality and Significance**
The originality of this work is moderate. The main novel contribution is the observation that allows for the simultaneous diagonalization of the relevant matrices, which is a key step in reducing the problem to the vector case. This is an elegant and non-trivial insight that may be of independent interest. Beyond this, the paper's methodology adheres closely to the existing framework for establishing local error bounds, and the subsequent steps to prove the linear rate are standard.

The paper's significance is primarily theoretical. It is a valuable contribution to extend the analysis of linear convergence for the proximal gradient algorithm to more practical scenarios where strong convexity does not hold. However, the significance is tempered by the asymptotic nature of the result, which does not guarantee faster convergence in the initial phases of optimization. The work does not propose new algorithms, but rather provides a deeper analysis of an existing one.

In summary, this paper proves that the proximal gradient algorithm, when applied to trace norm regularized risk minimization, converges asymptotically at a linear rate under a relaxed convexity condition. The analysis builds upon the established Luo and Tseng framework, introducing a clever diagonalization technique. Overall, this is a solid, albeit incremental, theoretical work that will be of moderate interest to the optimization and machine learning theory community.
