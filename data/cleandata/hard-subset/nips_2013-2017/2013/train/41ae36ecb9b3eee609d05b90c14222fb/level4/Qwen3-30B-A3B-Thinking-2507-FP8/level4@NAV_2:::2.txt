

The paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm-regularized problems under weaker conditions than requiring strong convexity of the loss function, addressing a significant gap in the literature for machine learning and signal processing applications. This work resolves an open question regarding Lipschitzian error bounds for trace norm regularization, which had remained unresolved despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization cases.

The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and clearly written. It makes a substantial contribution by providing a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm-regularized problems, which is the key to establishing linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions. The proof technique builds upon and extends the framework developed by Luo and Tseng [10], which was previously applied to ℓ1-norm regularization but not to trace norm regularization. The main technical contribution appears to be correct and well-motivated.

A notable gap in the paper is the lack of a clear comparison to the vector case of ℓ1-norm regularization (e.g., as in [20,22]), which would help contextualize the work and explain why techniques from the ℓ1 case do not directly translate to the trace norm case. This comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution, as some readers might initially perceive the trace norm result as merely an extension of the ℓ1 result. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating how the trace norm case differs from the ℓ1 case and why the ℓ1 techniques don't apply directly.

Regarding the title, I suggest a minor modification as the primary contribution concerns the flexibility of the loss function (via the linear operator A) rather than making the trace norm regularization "structured" - the regularization term itself remains unchanged. The current title may be slightly misleading.

Minor issues to address:
- In Section 5, the operator P should be consistently referred to as a "mask operator" rather than "sampling operator" to avoid implying randomness (the operator is fixed).
- Reference l337 appears to be broken.
- The definitions of "Q-linear" and "R-linear" convergence could be briefly repeated for clarity, as these terms are not universally known.
- The residual map R(X) defined in equation (7) could be better connected to the optimality condition (4) and the definition in [20] for improved interpretation.

While the technical proof of Lemma 4.1 is thorough, the paper could benefit from slightly more discussion in the main text to help readers navigate the complex technical arguments. A brief summary of the key insights before diving into the proof would help readers appreciate the significance of the technical steps.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. The results are important for practitioners working with low-rank matrix problems in machine learning. The paper should be accepted with minor revisions.
