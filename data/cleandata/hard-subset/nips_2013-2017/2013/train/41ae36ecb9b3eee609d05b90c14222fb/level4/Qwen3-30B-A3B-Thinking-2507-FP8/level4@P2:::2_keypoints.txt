- Addresses proving linear convergence of proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss.
- Shows linear convergence for losses of the form f(X)=h(A(X)) where A is linear and h meets mild convexity/smoothness conditions.
- Main technical contribution: a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, resolving an open question unlike the ℓ₁ case.
- Proof leverages matrix perturbation theory and the observation that optimal solutions share singular vectors with the gradient term.
- Uses the Luo–Tseng analysis framework to convert the error bound into linear convergence results for PGM.
- Theoretical results are technically sound and rigorously proved according to the reviewer.
- Numerical experiments (matrix completion, classification) validate the theoretical linear convergence in practice.
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a clear logical flow from problem setup to proofs.
- Needs a more explicit comparison to the vector/ℓ₁-norm case to explain why ℓ₁ techniques do not directly extend (non-polyhedral epigraph issue).
- Should clarify distinction between Q-linear and R-linear convergence and explicitly connect their residual map R(X) to existing definitions.
- Original and significant: first linear-convergence result for first-order methods with trace-norm regularization without strong convexity; error bound may have independent use.
- High practical significance for matrix completion, multi-task learning, and potential extensions to other matrix norms.
- Recommendation: accept, with minor revisions addressing ℓ₁ comparison and some notational/explanatory clarifications.
- Pros: solves open problem, novel error bound, well-written, validated experimentally.
- Cons: lacks explicit ℓ₁ comparison, title could better reflect contribution scope, minor notational/explanatory issues (Q/R-linear, residual definition).
