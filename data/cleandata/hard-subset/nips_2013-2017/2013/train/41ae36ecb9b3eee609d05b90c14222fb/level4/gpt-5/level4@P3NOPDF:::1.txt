Summary
This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized problems of the form minimize h(A(X)) + τ||X||*, where A is linear and h has Lipschitz continuous gradient and is strongly convex on any compact set. The work extends prior linear-convergence results based on error-bound conditions (e.g., Tseng [20]; Tseng and Yun; Zhang et al. [22]) from ℓ1/group-lasso settings to the nuclear norm.

Strengths
- Provides a rigorous linear convergence guarantee for PG in trace-norm regularized learning under composite structure f(X) = h(A(X)).
- Extends influential error-bound based analyses beyond vector sparsity to low-rank matrix regularization, which is nontrivial due to the nonpolyhedral geometry of the nuclear norm.
- Proofs are mostly clear and technically careful.

Weaknesses
- The contribution appears primarily technical; the conceptual novelty relative to Tseng [20], Tseng and Yun, and Zhang et al. [22] is not sufficiently articulated.
- No numerical results are provided to corroborate the theory or illustrate its scope and limitations.

Detailed comments and suggestions
1) Relation to prior work and novelty
- The outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely parallel arguments in Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22]. Please cite the specific propositions/lemmas and identify precisely where your analysis departs from theirs (e.g., in establishing an error bound for the nuclear norm composite with a linear map; handling nonpolyhedral subdifferentials; exploiting properties of singular values/SVD geometry; or verifying calmness/metric subregularity).
- Clarify whether your linear rate derives from a global error bound, a local quadratic growth condition on level sets, restricted strong convexity of h∘A near the solution set, or a combination. An explicit statement of the exact regularity used will help distinguish your contribution.

2) Numerical illustration
- Please include a semilog plot of objective suboptimality versus iterations for PG on a standard trace-norm problem (e.g., matrix completion or matrix sensing). Show:
  - Linear regimes across different step sizes α ∈ [α_, α‾].
  - Sensitivity to conditioning of A or to a tunable stability parameter (e.g., sampling rate or noise level).
  - A case where h is not globally strongly convex (e.g., logistic loss with rank-deficient A), yet the method empirically converges linearly; relate this to local strong convexity on level sets or restricted strong convexity.
- Report the observed linear rates and compare with theoretical bounds to indicate tightness and conservatism.

3) Constants and step-size interval
- The dependence of κ1,…,κ4 on α_ and α‾ is unclear. Please:
  - Provide explicit expressions or bounds showing how these constants depend on the Lipschitz constant L of ∇h, the operator norms of A and A*, τ, and the geometry (e.g., radius) of the relevant level set.
  - Explain the trade-off in choosing α_ and α‾ (e.g., smaller α_ may worsen constants but increase robustness; larger α‾ may violate descent unless bounded by 1/L_eff).
  - Indicate whether the linear rate constant can be made monotone in the conditioning parameters to aid practical step-size selection.

4) Assumptions and scope
- “Strongly convex on any compact set” is weaker than global strong convexity. Please clarify:
  - How boundedness of the level set is ensured (e.g., via coercivity from τ||X||* or assumptions on A).
  - Whether a local quadratic growth (or EB) condition near the solution suffices and how it is verified for typical h (squared loss, logistic) and problem structures (matrix completion/sensing).
  - Provide examples where the theory fails: e.g., if A has a large nullspace intersecting the tangent space at the solution so that restricted curvature collapses; or if level sets are unbounded.
- State whether the result covers both unique and nonunique minimizers, and how the error bound is formulated relative to the solution set.

5) Inequalities before (13)
- The sequence of inequalities leading to (13) is confusing. The final inequality follows from convexity of the nuclear norm together with −Ĝ ∈ τ∂||X||* (cf. p. 289 in [20]), not from the intermediate inequality as currently written. Please revise the chain and justification accordingly, or remove the misleading intermediate step.

6) Extension to ADMM and other first-order methods
- Please comment on whether your error-bound (or calmness/metric subregularity) arguments can be translated to:
  - ADMM applied to the split formulation with an auxiliary variable for A(X).
  - Proximal coordinate or block methods.
  - Accelerated PG (noting that Nesterov acceleration can disrupt monotonicity; clarify whether local linear convergence can still be guaranteed under restart).
- For ADMM, specify what additional assumptions (e.g., exactness of subproblems, parameter ranges, full column/row rank conditions) would be required for linear convergence in your setting.

7) Presentation and clarity
- Define all constants (κ1–κ4, α_, α‾) on first use and summarize their roles in the rate statement.
- When invoking prior lemmas from [20] and [22], provide precise pointers and note any modifications needed for the nuclear norm.
- Check notation for consistency (bars/underbars, adjoints, norms, and subgradients) and fix minor typographical issues.

Overall assessment
The paper makes a meaningful, though largely technical, extension of established error-bound-based linear convergence results to the trace-norm setting. The topic is timely and relevant, and the proofs are generally clear. Strengthening the positioning relative to prior work, clarifying the dependence of constants and assumptions, and adding a concise numerical study (including cases beyond global strong convexity and a discussion of failure modes) would significantly improve the paper’s impact and clarity.
