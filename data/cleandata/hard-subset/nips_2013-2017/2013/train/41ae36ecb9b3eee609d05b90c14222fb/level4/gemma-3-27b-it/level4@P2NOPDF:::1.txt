This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient (PG) method when applied to trace-norm regularized learning problems, specifically those where the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A being a linear operator and h being strongly convex and Lipschitz continuous on compact sets. The work extends prior results by Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which demonstrated linear convergence for lasso and group lasso using an “error-bound condition,” to the trace-norm setting. While a non-trivial extension, the contribution appears largely technical. The proofs are generally presented clearly.

**Strengths:**

*   Demonstrates the linear convergence of the PG algorithm for trace-norm regularized problems, building upon existing theoretical foundations laid by Tseng et al.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The primary contribution feels incremental and technical rather than offering a significant conceptual advancement.
*   The paper lacks a numerical demonstration of the established linear convergence. An illustrative example, such as a semilog plot of the function value versus the number of iterations, would substantially strengthen the empirical support for the theoretical findings.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  The proof structures for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 bear a strong resemblance to those in Tseng [20]. To better highlight the novel aspects of this work, the authors should provide more precise references and a detailed discussion outlining the specific differences and extensions made compared to the original work. This will aid in clarifying the unique contribution of this paper.

2.  A numerical example illustrating the linear convergence behavior is crucial. The authors should include such an example, clearly showing the expected scaling in a semilog plot. Further, it would be beneficial to explore scenarios where the loss function is not strongly convex, but the PG algorithm still exhibits linear convergence for varying levels of stability. Critically, a discussion of the conditions under which the theory *fails* would greatly enhance the practical understanding of the result's limitations.

3.  The dependence of the constants κ<sub>1</sub> through κ<sub>4</sub> on the parameters  <u>α</u> and  ̄α is unclear. Providing a more explicit description of this relationship would improve the practical applicability of the convergence analysis.

4.  The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat convoluted. The final inequality is justified by the convexity of the trace norm and the inclusion of -̄G in τ∂||X||<sub>*</sub>, as detailed on page 289 of Tseng [20], and not by the intervening inequality. Clarifying this point will improve the readability and correctness of the presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This work represents a logical extension of existing linear convergence results—specifically, adapting the “error-bound condition” from lasso and group lasso to the trace-norm setting. However, the impact of this extension is limited by its primarily technical nature and the absence of empirical validation. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a numerical example and discussing limitations, would significantly strengthen the paper. Additionally, exploring potential extensions to the analysis of ADMM would be a valuable addition, hinting at future research directions.
