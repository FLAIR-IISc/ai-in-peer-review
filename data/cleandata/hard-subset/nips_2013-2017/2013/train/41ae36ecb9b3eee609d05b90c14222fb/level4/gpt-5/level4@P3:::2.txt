Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies convex optimization problems of the form F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h strictly convex, smooth, and Lipschitz-gradient on an open domain. The main result is that the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) enjoys an asymptotic linear convergence rate under these assumptions, despite f generally not being strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with underdetermined A). The key technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound: the distance to the optimal solution set is proportional to the norm of a residual map R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. The authors then invoke the Luo–Tseng framework to obtain linear convergence of the objective values (Q-linear) and iterates (R-linear). The analysis hinges on a structural result showing that any optimal solution X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors, enabling a restricted invertibility argument via matrix perturbation theory.

This work builds on the error-bound literature for composite minimization, notably Luo–Tseng and Tseng’s analyses for polyhedral-epigraph regularizers such as the ℓ1 norm and group LASSO, and on proximal-gradient convergence results for strongly convex objectives. Prior linear rates have required strong convexity; here the authors avoid that by exploiting the composition structure f = h ∘ A and deriving a new error bound for the nonpolyhedral trace-norm regularizer, an open question in the literature. The paper also verifies the theory numerically on matrix completion and matrix classification (logistic loss), illustrating linear convergence behavior once iterates are sufficiently close to the solution set.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The main arguments are careful and, to the extent I could verify, correct. The error bound proof is nontrivial and introduces new ideas beyond the ℓ1 case (simultaneous SVD and restricted invertibility), addressing the nonpolyhedral nature of the trace norm. The Luo–Tseng-based convergence derivation is standard once the error bound holds.
- Assumptions: The h assumptions are natural and cover square and logistic loss. The step size assumptions are standard; the line search implementation for logistic loss is appropriate.
- Experiments: Small but relevant; they confirm linear convergence in practice in the asymptotic regime.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The main proof is long but reasonably structured. A few definitions and comparisons could be made more explicit (see suggestions).

Originality:
The Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization appears new and addresses a previously open case. The simultaneous SVD observation and its use in the error-bound proof are insightful. Establishing linear convergence for PGM without strong convexity in this setting is a meaningful addition.

Significance:
Error bounds and linear rates for nonpolyhedral regularizers are of broad interest in machine learning and optimization. The result applies to important applications (matrix completion, multitask learning, logistic models) and clarifies when simple first-order methods are rapidly convergent. The techniques may inspire analogous results for other spectral regularizers.

Strengths:
- Resolves an open question by proving a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm and deriving linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity.
- Clear assumptions that cover widely used losses (square/logistic).
- Solid, well-structured analysis; useful structural lemma (simultaneous SVD).
- Empirical validation consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Comparison to ℓ1 case: The paper would benefit from a more explicit analogy and contrast with the ℓ1/group-LASSO error bound analyses (e.g., Tseng and follow-ups). It would be helpful to:
  - Summarize why the variable-splitting arguments for ℓ1/group LASSO do not directly carry over to the trace norm (non-separability via singular values; nonpolyhedral epigraph).
  - Explain how the simultaneous SVD and perturbation tools substitute for separability.
  - Note that the ℓ1 case is a corollary (vector-as-diagonal-matrix), or clarify why additional care is or is not needed.
- Scope of linear rate: Emphasize that the linear rate is asymptotic and local (in a neighborhood where the error bound applies), and comment on how large that neighborhood may be or how to detect entry into it in practice.
- Constants and practicality: A brief discussion about the (unknown) constants in the error bound and rate, and whether adaptive step-size/backtracking influences the onset of linear convergence, would help practitioners.
- Experiments: Consider including runtime or SVD counts, and a comparison to an accelerated method or alternative solvers to contextualize the practical impact.

Minor issues:
- Terminology: If P denotes a fixed masking operator, consider calling it a masking or sampling-mask operator to avoid implying stochastic sampling.
- Define Q-linear and R-linear convergence where first used, as many readers may not recall the precise definitions.
- Residual map: Provide intuition by explicitly relating your residual to the residual used by Luo–Tseng/Tseng to aid readers familiar with the vector case.
- Fix broken reference at line 337 and check all citations and numbering.
- Figures: Ensure captions and axes clearly indicate problem size, θ, and stopping criteria; if possible, add semilog slope references to visually support linear behavior claims.
- Title/abstract: If the manuscript uses “structured” to describe the regularizer, consider clarifying that the structural assumption is on the loss via composition with A; the current title is otherwise accurate.

Pros and cons for acceptance:
Pros:
- Novel and technically solid error bound for the trace norm.
- Linear convergence of a widely used method under broad and practically relevant assumptions.
- Clear exposition and supporting experiments.
- Likely to influence further work on error bounds for nonpolyhedral regularizers.

Cons:
- Limited discussion contrasting techniques with the ℓ1/group-LASSO literature.
- Linear rate is asymptotic; practical guidance on when it kicks in could be expanded.
- Experimental section could be more comprehensive (runtime and method comparisons).
