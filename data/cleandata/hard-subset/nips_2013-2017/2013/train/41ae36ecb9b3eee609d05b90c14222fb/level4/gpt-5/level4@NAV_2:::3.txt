For trace-norm regularized risk minimization, the proximal gradient method is widely used. Its global sublinear rate is well known, and linear convergence is established when the loss is strongly convex. This paper weakens the strong convexity requirement by leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework. Specifically, the authors prove a local error bound that enables them to show that proximal gradient achieves asymptotic linear convergence for trace-norm regularization. The proof largely follows the established template for local error bounds, with a new observation that enables simultaneous diagonalization of the matrices.

Quality:
The paper appears technically correct. The only issue I noted is in the appendix, line 085: please clarify why the particular choice of κ3 (e.g., assuming α < 1) implies the claimed inequality R(X^k) ≤ κ3 ||X^k − X^{k+1}||_F.

Clarity:
The manuscript is generally readable. Given the technical density, it may help to present the main claims up front and then develop the details. In particular, after stating the local error bound, consider postponing its technical proof and immediately deriving the linear rate (which, as written, contains little novelty).

Originality:
The novelty is moderate. The observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization appears new and interesting; aside from this, the development of the local error bound largely follows existing methods. The subsequent steps are standard.

Significance:
The contribution is primarily theoretical, with limited implications for algorithmic design. While extending linear convergence of proximal gradient to more realistic settings (i.e., beyond strong convexity) is valuable, the impact is tempered by the asymptotic nature of the result. The matrix diagonalization argument may be of independent interest. Overall, this work shows that proximal gradient for trace-norm regularized risk minimization converges at an asymptotic linear rate under a relaxed condition relative to strong convexity. The approach mainly follows the Luo–Tseng framework, with a new simultaneous diagonalization insight that effectively reduces the analysis toward the vector case. The contribution is incremental and moderately interesting from a theoretical standpoint.
