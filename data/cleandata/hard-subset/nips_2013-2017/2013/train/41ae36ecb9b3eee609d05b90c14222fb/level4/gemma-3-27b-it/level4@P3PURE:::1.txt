This paper proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method applied to trace-norm regularized learning problems where the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A being a linear operator and h being strongly convex on any compact set and possessing a Lipschitz gradient. This work extends the results of Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which established similar linear convergence guarantees using the “error-bound condition” for lasso and group lasso, to the trace norm regularization setting. While a non-trivial extension, the contribution appears primarily technical. 

The presentation of the proofs is generally clear.

**Strengths:**
- Demonstrates the linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, extending prior work by Tseng et al.

**Weaknesses:**
- The contribution is largely technical in nature.
- A numerical example illustrating the linear convergence behavior is lacking.

**More Details:**

1. The outlines of the proofs for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 bear a strong resemblance to those presented by Tseng. The authors should provide more precise references to the original work to better delineate their contribution.
2. I request the inclusion of a numerical example demonstrating the linear convergence empirically (e.g., a semilog plot of the function value versus the number of iterations).
3. The dependence of the constants κ<sub>1</sub>, ..., κ<sub>4</sub> on the choices of  <u>α</u> and <u>α</u> is not explicitly stated and would benefit from clarification. 

**Minor Issue:**
4. The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat unclear. The final inequality is justified by the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that -<u>G</u> ∈ τ∂||X||<sub>*</sub>, rather than through the intermediate inequality (as noted on p289 in [20]).

**Overall:** This work represents an extension of previous linear convergence results, grounded in the “error-bound condition” from lasso and group lasso, to the context of trace norm regularization. 

**Request for Revision:** Please include a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex but the proximal gradient algorithm still converges linearly (for different levels of stability), and importantly, discuss scenarios where the theoretical guarantees fail to hold. Furthermore, please elaborate on the potential to extend this analysis to the ADMM algorithm.
