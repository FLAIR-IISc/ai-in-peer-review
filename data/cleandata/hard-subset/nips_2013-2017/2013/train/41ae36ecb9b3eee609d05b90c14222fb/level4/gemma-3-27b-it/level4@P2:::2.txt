This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems, a significant result as it relaxes the common strong convexity assumption on the loss function – a critical limitation in many machine learning and signal processing applications. The work is presented with clarity and rigor, closely building upon the Lipschitzian error bound framework developed by Luo and Tseng [10]. The core argument appears technically sound to the best of my understanding.

The primary strength of the paper lies in addressing an open question regarding the applicability of Tseng’s technique to the trace norm case. The authors successfully demonstrate linear convergence without requiring strong convexity, expanding the scope of problems for which PGM can be efficiently applied. This is a valuable contribution to the optimization community.

However, a more detailed discussion comparing and contrasting this work with the well-established results for l1-norm regularization (e.g., [10, 22]) would significantly strengthen the paper. Specifically, elaborating on why existing techniques developed for the l1 case do not directly translate to the trace norm setting would highlight the novelty and technical challenges overcome in this work. Demonstrating the l1-norm result as a direct corollary of the presented theory would further solidify the contribution. Some readers might perceive the linear convergence for trace norm as a natural extension of the l1 case; a more explicit comparison would preempt this view and underscore the paper’s originality.

Furthermore, the title could be refined. While technically accurate, the phrase “structured” feels somewhat unnecessary. The key contribution lies in the broader class of loss functions achievable through the use of the linear operator *A*, rather than a fundamental change to the trace norm regularization itself. A title focusing on the loss function’s flexibility might better reflect the core advancement.

**Minor Points:**

*   The term “sampling operator” vs. “mask operator” for *P* warrants clarification. While "sampling" might imply randomness, *P* is presented as a fixed operator.
*   Reference [33] appears broken.
*   The definitions of “Q-linear” and “R-linear” convergence could benefit from repetition for broader accessibility.
*   The residual *R(X)* (defined in Eq. 7) could be explicitly linked to the definition in [10] (Eq. 4.6) to aid in interpretation.
*   The paper is heavily focused on technical proofs (particularly Lemma 4.1 in Section 3). Including a more substantial discussion of implications and broader conclusions would enhance the overall impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel theoretical result regarding the linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization under weaker assumptions than previously established.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous and builds upon established techniques in convex optimization.
*   **Relevance:** The result addresses a significant problem in machine learning and signal processing where strong convexity is often not satisfied.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Discussion:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the connection to existing work on l1-norm regularization and the implications of the results.
*   **Title:** The current title might not fully capture the core contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Addressing the points raised regarding the comparison to l1-norm regularization and refining the title would significantly strengthen the paper. The technical contribution is substantial, and the clarity of presentation is commendable.
