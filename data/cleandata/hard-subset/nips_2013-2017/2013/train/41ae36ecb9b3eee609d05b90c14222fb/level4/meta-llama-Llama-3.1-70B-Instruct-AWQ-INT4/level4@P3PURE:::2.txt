The paper proves the linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace norm regularized problems under weaker assumptions than strong convexity of the loss function, a crucial problem in many machine learning and signal processing applications.

The paper is carefully and clearly written, closely following the Lipschitzian error bound technique of Tseng [20], whose application to the nuclear norm case was still an open question. The main part of the proof appears correct to the best of my understanding.

One aspect that is missing in this paper is a clear comparison or analogy to the vector case of l1-norm regularization, as seen in [20,22] and related work. It would be very helpful to relate the results and techniques used in that case and explain why the l1-case techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm case. Additionally, since the l1-norm case should follow from the trace norm case as a corollary, this connection should be explicitly stated. When evaluating the value of the paper, this omission should be taken into account, as some readers might find it unsurprising that the l1-result also holds for the trace norm (although I still consider it a valuable contribution).

Independent of this, I suggest a slight change to the title of the paper, as the main new contribution affects the loss part more than making the trace norm regularization a "structured" one. Since the newly gained flexibility is provided by the linear operator A on the loss side, not in the regularizer, the term "structured" might be misleading.

Minor issues include:

* Notation: Instead of calling P a "sampling or mask operator", it would be more accurate to simply call it a "mask operator" since it is fixed and does not imply randomness.
* The reference [l337] appears to be broken.
* The meanings of "Q-" and "R-" linear convergence might not be widely known, and repeating them for clarity would be beneficial.
* The residual R(X) defined in (6) could be additionally explained in relation to the original definition (46) by [20] for better interpretation.

In general, I would recommend including more discussion and possibly conclusions to support the long technical part of the proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3.
