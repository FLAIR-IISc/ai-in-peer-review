Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for convex risk minimization with trace-norm (nuclear-norm) regularization. While the global sublinear convergence of PGM is well known, and linear rates are standard under strong convexity of the loss, this work shows that PGM attains an asymptotic linear rate for a broader class of losses without assuming strong convexity of f. Specifically, for losses of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h strictly convex, continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient, and strongly convex on any convex compact subset of its domain (conditions satisfied by square and logistic losses), the authors establish a local Lipschitzian error bound that yields linear convergence via the Luo–Tseng framework.

This contributes to a line of results where error bounds drive linear rates for first-order methods (e.g., Luo and Tseng 1993; Tseng 2010; Zhang et al. 2013). Prior error bounds exist for composite problems with polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO); the trace norm, however, is nonpolyhedral, and error bounds have been an open question. The key new observation is that at an optimal solution X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same left and right singular vectors, enabling a spectral perturbation analysis and a reduction (locally) to a vector-like structure. This supports a local error bound of the form dist(X, X*) ≤ η ∥R(X)∥F for points near the solution set, where R is the residual map defined by the proximal optimality condition, which in turn implies asymptotic linear convergence of PGM.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound. The main technical contribution is the Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, and the derivation is careful, leveraging the simultaneous singular vector structure to overcome the nonpolyhedral difficulty. The convergence result then follows from the Luo–Tseng analysis. One gap I noted is in the appendix (line 085): please clarify why the chosen κ3 (e.g., under α < 1) yields ∥R(Xk)∥F ≤ κ3 ∥Xk − Xk+1∥F. After the rebuttal, I note that this inequality is established in Sra (NIPS 2012), Lemma 4, which resolves this concern provided the assumptions match your setting.

Clarity:
The presentation is largely clear despite the technical nature. For readability, consider restructuring Section 3 to (i) state the local error bound and its intuition, (ii) immediately derive linear convergence from this bound (the steps are standard), and then (iii) defer the lengthy proof of the error bound to a later section or appendix. This would help readers see the main storyline before delving into technical details. The numerical section is concise and supports the asymptotic linear phase.

Originality:
Originality is moderate. The overall proof strategy closely follows established frameworks for deriving linear rates from error bounds. The novel and interesting component is the observation enabling simultaneous singular vector alignment for X̄ and −∇f(X̄), which appears new and is key to handling the nonpolyhedral trace norm. Apart from this, the remaining steps mirror prior approaches.

Significance:
The contribution is primarily theoretical. It broadens the class of losses for which linear convergence of PGM can be guaranteed, without strong convexity of f, which is relevant for common machine learning models (e.g., matrix completion, classification). However, the result is asymptotic and does not directly lead to new algorithmic designs or improved worst-case global rates. The spectral argument may be of independent interest for other trace-norm analyses.

Pros for acceptance:
- Establishes a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, previously open for this nonpolyhedral setting.
- Delivers asymptotic linear convergence of PGM under broadly used loss models.
- Provides a neat spectral observation (shared singular vectors) that may enable further advances.
- Solid and careful analysis, with supporting experiments showing linear phases.

Cons against acceptance:
- Largely incremental in methodology; follows the Luo–Tseng framework with limited novel analysis beyond the spectral alignment observation.
- The linear rate is asymptotic; no new algorithmic mechanisms or non-asymptotic improvements are proposed.
- Some technical steps (e.g., the bound linking residual and iterate differences) rely on known results and could be clarified in the text.

Overall evaluation:
This is a solid theoretical contribution that resolves an error-bound question for the trace norm and extends asymptotic linear convergence guarantees of PGM to a widely used class of losses without strong convexity. The work is incremental but technically interesting; the matrix diagonalization insight is a noteworthy addition. I find it moderately interesting and suitable for a theory-focused audience.
