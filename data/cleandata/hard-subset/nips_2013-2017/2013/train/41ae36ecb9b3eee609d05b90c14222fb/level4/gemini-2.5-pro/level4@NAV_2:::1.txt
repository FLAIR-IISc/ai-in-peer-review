This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient method for a class of trace norm regularization problems. The core contribution is showing that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex on compact sets and has a Lipschitz continuous gradient, the method converges linearly even when the composite function f is not strongly convex. This result extends a line of work on convergence analysis based on establishing a Lipschitzian error bound, previously developed for problems with polyhedral regularizers like the lasso and group lasso (e.g., by Tseng and colleagues). The key theoretical innovation is the development of a new error bound for the trace norm regularizer, which is non-trivial due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph.

**Strengths:**

*   The main strength is the rigorous proof of linear convergence for the proximal gradient method on trace norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a significant theoretical result that addresses a common scenario in machine learning applications like matrix completion, where the loss is often not strongly convex.
*   The derivation of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm is a valuable contribution in its own right and resolves an open question. This tool could potentially be of independent interest for analyzing other algorithms.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Novelty:** While the paper extends the error bound-based analysis framework of Luo and Tseng, the presentation could more clearly highlight the specific novel techniques required to handle the trace norm. The proof structure is similar to prior work on lasso-type problems, so a more detailed discussion contrasting the new challenges (e.g., the reliance on matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure) with the techniques used for separable, polyhedral norms would better underscore the paper's unique contribution.

*   **Experimental Analysis:** The numerical experiments in Section 5 confirm the paper's theoretical claims, demonstrating the linear convergence on matrix completion and classification tasks. To make this section more insightful, the authors could consider including an experiment that explores the boundaries of the theory. For example, demonstrating how the convergence rate is affected as the problem parameters change or as the assumptions (e.g., the curvature properties of h) are relaxed.

*   **Theoretical Details:** In the statement of Theorem 3.2, the constants κ1, κ2, and κ3 are introduced without much discussion. The analysis would be more complete if the authors briefly explained how these constants depend on key problem parameters like the step size bounds (α, ᾱ) and the Lipschitz constant of the gradient (Lf).

*   **Minor Proof Clarification:** The proof of Theorem 3.1 contains a sequence of inequalities leading to equation (18) that could be presented more clearly. The inequality `τ‖X̄k‖∗ ≤ 〈Ḡ,X k +Rk − X̄k〉+ τ‖Xk +Rk‖∗` follows directly from the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient optimality condition for X̄k, `-Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄k‖∗`. Stating this explicitly would avoid any potential confusion that it depends on the preceding inequality in the derivation.
