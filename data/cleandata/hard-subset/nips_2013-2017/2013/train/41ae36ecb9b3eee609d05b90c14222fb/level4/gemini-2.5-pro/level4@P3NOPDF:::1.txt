This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized problems. The analysis applies to composite objective functions where the smooth part has the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A being a linear operator and h being a function that is strongly convex on any compact set and has a Lipschitz continuous gradient.

The work extends a line of analysis based on "error-bound conditions," which has previously been used by Tseng [20], Tseng and Yun, and Zhang et al. [22] to prove linear convergence for lasso and group lasso problems. The extension of this framework from vector-based regularization (lasso) to matrix-based regularization (trace norm) is the central contribution of this paper.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work is the successful extension of the error-bound-based linear convergence analysis to the important and more complex setting of trace-norm regularization. This is a non-trivial theoretical result that provides valuable insights into the behavior of proximal gradient methods on a significant class of problems (e.g., matrix completion, multi-task learning) without requiring the restrictive assumption of strong convexity for the overall objective function. The proofs are generally well-presented and follow a logical structure.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the technical result is sound, the paper could be significantly strengthened in a few key areas:

1.  **Limited Scope of Contribution:** The contribution, while technically solid, is primarily an extension of an existing analytical framework to a new problem class. The paper would have a greater impact if it discussed the broader implications of this result or any novel insights gained from the analysis that are specific to the trace-norm setting and differ from the lasso case.

2.  **Lack of Empirical Validation:** The paper's claims are entirely theoretical. It lacks any numerical experiments to support or illustrate the proven convergence rates. The inclusion of even a simple simulation would substantially enhance the paper by demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical findings and giving readers a more intuitive feel for the algorithm's performance under these conditions.

**Detailed Comments**

1.  **Clarification of Novelty:** The proof outlines for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 appear to closely follow the template established by Tseng [20]. To better highlight this paper's specific innovations, the authors should provide a more detailed comparison with the original work within the proofs. Explicitly pinpointing the novel steps and challenges required to handle the trace norm would make the contribution much clearer.

2.  **Request for Numerical Example:** A numerical example that empirically demonstrates the linear convergence would be highly beneficial. A semi-log plot of the objective function value (or distance to the optimum) versus the iteration count would provide clear, empirical validation of the main theoretical result.

3.  **Dependence of Constants:** The analysis introduces constants κ1, ..., κ4. However, the paper does not clearly explain their dependence on the parameters \(\underline{\alpha}\) and \(\bar{\alpha}\), which define the local strong convexity of h. Clarifying this relationship is important for understanding the factors that govern the convergence rate.

4.  **Minor Correction in Proof:** There appears to be a minor issue in the derivation preceding inequality (13). The sequence of inequalities is presented in a confusing manner. The final inequality seems to follow from the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient optimality condition (-\(\bar{G}\) ∈ τ∂||X||*), rather than from the intermediate inequality shown. This logic resembles the argument in Tseng [20, p. 289], and the authors should revise this section for accuracy and clarity.

**Suggestions for the Final Version**

To further strengthen the paper, I encourage the authors to consider the following points:

*   **Simulation under Weaker Conditions:** It would be very insightful to include a simulation where the loss function is not globally strongly convex but the conditions of the theorem are met, leading to linear convergence. Showing how this rate changes for different problem parameters (e.g., those affecting the error-bound condition) and discussing the boundaries where the theory might fail would be a valuable addition.
*   **Discussion on Extensions:** A discussion on how this analysis could be extended to other popular optimization algorithms would broaden the paper's appeal. For instance, could this error-bound approach be applied to analyze the linear convergence of ADMM for trace-norm regularized problems?
