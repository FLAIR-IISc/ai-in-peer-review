This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace norm regularized optimization problems. The key insight is that this linear convergence can be achieved without the standard assumption of strong convexity on the loss function. Instead, the authors require the loss to have the structure f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is a strictly convex function and A is a linear operator. This is a significant result for many machine learning and signal processing applications, such as matrix completion, where the loss function is often not strongly convex. The proof's main technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem, which resolves what had been an open question. The analysis builds upon the powerful framework developed by Luo and Tseng for analyzing first-order methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial and practical problem. Proving linear convergence for non-strongly convex objectives is an important theoretical step forward and is highly relevant to applications where PGM is a method of choice.
*   **Originality:** The primary contribution, a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm case, is novel and resolves an open question. While the analysis follows the established framework of Luo and Tseng, its application to the trace norm setting requires new technical insights, particularly the use of matrix perturbation theory to handle the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm epigraph.
*   **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is carefully structured and clearly written. The arguments are presented logically, and the main theorems are well-motivated. The proof appears to be technically sound.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is a strong contribution, but its presentation and impact could be further enhanced with the following improvements:

1.  **Comparison with l1-norm Regularization:** The paper would benefit greatly from a more explicit comparison to the analogous results for l1-norm regularization (e.g., in Tseng [22], Tseng & Yun [23]). The introduction correctly notes that existing techniques for the l1-norm, which rely on separability, do not apply here. However, elaborating on *why* they fail and how the authors' spectral approach overcomes this hurdle would better highlight the paper's novelty. A more detailed discussion would help readers understand the unique challenges posed by the trace norm and appreciate the new techniques developed. It would also be valuable to explicitly state that the l1-norm result for diagonal matrices is now a corollary of this more general theorem.

2.  **Title and Framing:** The authors may wish to reconsider the title. The key innovation is enabled by the structural assumption on the *loss function* (f(X) = h(A(X))), rather than a property of the trace norm regularizer alone. A title that reflects the importance of the loss function's structure might more accurately frame the paper's contribution.

3.  **Clarity on Minor Points:**
    *   For the benefit of a wider audience, it would be helpful to briefly define Q-linear and R-linear convergence in the main text when they are first introduced.
    *   The residual map R(X) in equation (7) is central to the analysis. Explaining its relationship to the original, more general definition of the residual in Luo and Tseng [10] could provide helpful context for readers familiar with that work.
    *   In the experimental section, the operator P is referred to as a "sampling operator." This term might suggest randomness. Using "masking operator" or "sub-sampling operator" might be more precise, given that P is fixed for each problem instance.
    *   There appears to be a broken reference around line 337.

4.  **Balance of Content:** The proof of the error bound is necessarily technical and detailed. The authors might consider moving some of the more intricate steps of the proof of Lemma 4.1 to the supplementary material. This would free up space in the main paper for a more extended discussion of the results' implications and a deeper dive into the comparison with the l1-norm case, which would increase the paper's overall impact.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a very valuable paper that makes a solid theoretical contribution to an important class of optimization problems. It is well-executed and addresses a clear gap in the literature. While some aspects of the presentation could be refined to better contextualize the work and broaden its accessibility, the core results are significant and technically sound. I recommend acceptance.
