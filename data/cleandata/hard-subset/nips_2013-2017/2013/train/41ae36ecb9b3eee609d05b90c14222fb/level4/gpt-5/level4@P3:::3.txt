Summary
This paper studies minimizing a convex smooth loss plus a trace (nuclear) norm regularizer and analyzes the proximal gradient method (PGM) in this setting. While sublinear rates (O(1/k), O(1/k^2) with acceleration) are standard, linear rates typically require strong convexity of the loss. The authors consider losses of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A linear and h strictly convex, L-smooth, and strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain. Under these assumptions, they establish a local Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal set to the residual of the proximal fixed-point condition. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, they deduce asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (R-linear for iterates, Q-linear for objective values) with stepsizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf. The key technical ingredient is an error bound for nuclear norm regularization that does not rely on strong convexity of f; its proof uses a structural observation that an optimal solution and the negative gradient share singular vectors, enabling a spectral analysis and a “restricted invertibility” argument.

Relation to prior work
The analysis follows the classical Luo–Tseng methodology for deriving linear convergence from error bounds. Existing error bounds and linear rates covered polyhedral nonsmooth terms such as ℓ1 and (group) LASSO; the trace norm is non-polyhedral, and previous techniques based on separability fail. The paper fills this gap by proving a tailored error bound for the trace norm, answering an open question raised by the similarity to ℓ1 in spirit but not structure. It also connects to accelerated and fixed-point proximal methods for nuclear-norm problems (e.g., Beck–Teboulle; Ma–Goldfarb–Chen; Toh–Yun) by explaining the often observed linear regime. After rebuttal: the inequality ‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F used in the linear-rate proof can be justified via optimality of the proximal step and nonexpansiveness (see also Sra, “Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting,” NIPS 2012, Lemma 4). It would be helpful for the authors to include a short derivation or this citation explicitly.

Quality
The paper appears technically sound. The main contributions are:
- A new Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularized problems under f = h∘A with mild curvature assumptions on h.
- A careful spectral argument: at optimality X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors; this enables a decomposition and a restricted invertibility inequality central to the error bound.
- A standard but correct application of Luo–Tseng to obtain linear convergence of PGM.

I flagged one gap prior to rebuttal: the inequality linking the residual to the step length (‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F). After rebuttal this is clarified; still, adding a self-contained proof or an explicit citation (Sra, NIPS’12, Lemma 4) would strengthen the presentation. Experiments are small-scale but consistent with the theory, showing linear behavior once near the solution.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable given the technical content. Two suggestions:
- Reorder the exposition: state assumptions, the error bound (Theorem 3.1), and the linear-rate result (Theorem 3.2) up front, with a brief intuitive proof sketch; relegate the full proof of the error bound to a later section or appendix.
- Clearly highlight the new structural proposition (simultaneous singular vectors) and how it differs from the ℓ1/group LASSO separability approach.

Minor presentation suggestions:
- Specify the dependence of constants (η, ε, κi) and the neighborhood where the error bound holds.
- Clarify stepsize requirements and how backtracking guarantees αk < 1/Lf.
- Standardize notation (operator norms, domains) and ensure all referenced results are cited when used.

Originality
Originality is moderate but meaningful. The main novelty is the nuclear-norm-specific error bound and the spectral argument enabling it. The rest of the analysis closely follows known frameworks (Luo–Tseng) and standard proximal gradient arguments, which is appropriate here.

Significance
Significance is primarily theoretical. The result explains and justifies the empirically observed linear regime for PGM on nuclear-norm problems without global strong convexity, and it broadens the class of problems with provable linear rates. The error bound may also inform algorithmic enhancements (e.g., adaptive restarts, stopping rules). Limitations include:
- The rate is asymptotic/local; explicit radii and constants are not quantified.
- The result does not directly yield new algorithms or global linear rates.
- Experiments are illustrative rather than comprehensive.

Pros for acceptance
- First linear convergence result for PGM on nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f, under widely used losses (square, logistic).
- Technically solid with a nontrivial new error bound and a clean structural insight about singular vectors at optimality.
- Clear connection to a well-established convergence framework; results likely to be cited and built upon.

Cons for acceptance
- Linear rate is asymptotic; practical implications (constants, neighborhood size) are not quantified.
- Novelty concentrated in one lemma; the remainder follows known templates.
- Empirical section is limited; no large-scale or comparative studies.

Recommendations to the authors
- Include a brief, self-contained derivation (or explicit citation) for ‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F.
- Emphasize and intuitively explain the simultaneous singular-vector property and its role in the proof.
- Consider adding guidance on estimating the neighborhood of linear convergence and on choosing stepsizes in practice (e.g., with backtracking).
- Expand discussion contrasting polyhedral vs non-polyhedral regularizers and why separability fails for the trace norm.

Overall evaluation
A solid theoretical contribution that answers a natural and previously open question about linear convergence of PGM for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity. While incremental in methodology, the main technical insight is nontrivial and of independent interest. I view this as moderately strong for acceptance on theoretical grounds.
