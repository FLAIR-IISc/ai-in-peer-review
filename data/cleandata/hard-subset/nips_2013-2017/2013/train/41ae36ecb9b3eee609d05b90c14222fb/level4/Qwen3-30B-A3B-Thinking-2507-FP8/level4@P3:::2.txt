

The paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems under weaker assumptions than previous work, demonstrating that linear convergence holds for a broad class of loss functions without requiring strong convexity of the loss. Specifically, the authors prove that when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with appropriate smoothness properties, the PGM achieves linear convergence. This resolves an important open question about whether linear convergence can be established for trace norm regularization without strong convexity assumptions, which is crucial for many applications in machine learning including matrix completion and multi-task learning.

The paper's main contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm-regularized problems, which is the key technical innovation. Unlike previous error bounds for ℓ1-norm regularization (which rely on splitting variables into non-interfering groups), the authors develop a new approach using matrix perturbation theory, leveraging the observation that optimal solutions and their gradients share singular vectors. This insight allows them to overcome the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The paper carefully relates this contribution to previous work, noting that while similar error bounds exist for ℓ1-norm regularization, the trace norm case has remained open due to fundamental structural differences.

The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is thorough, with the authors developing a key lemma (Lemma 4.1) that establishes important properties of the optimal solution set. The proof techniques are sound, and the authors successfully apply the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10] to establish linear convergence (Theorem 3.2). The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems provide empirical support for the theoretical results, demonstrating linear convergence in practice when iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal solution set.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important open question in optimization with trace norm regularization, resolving a significant gap in the literature.
2. The technical contribution (the Lipschitzian error bound) is novel and well-motivated, with clear explanations of why standard techniques for ℓ1-norm do not apply.
3. The paper is clearly written with logical flow and appropriate mathematical rigor.
4. The numerical experiments complement the theoretical results effectively.

Weaknesses:
1. While the paper discusses the connection to ℓ1-norm regularization, it would benefit from a more detailed comparison with the vector case. Specifically, it should explicitly explain why the standard techniques for ℓ1-norm (which rely on variable splitting) fail for trace norm, and how the authors' approach overcomes this limitation. This would help readers understand the conceptual contribution more clearly.
2. The title "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization" could be refined to better reflect that the main novelty concerns the loss function rather than the regularization (as the authors' technique depends on the specific structure of the loss function f(X) = h(A(X)) rather than the trace norm itself).
3. Some technical details could be clarified for broader accessibility: the distinction between Q-linear and R-linear convergence (common in optimization but not universally known) could be briefly defined, and the residual map R(X) could be more explicitly related to the optimality condition.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The result extends the applicability of PGM to a much wider class of loss functions used in practice (such as square loss in matrix completion and logistic loss in classification) without requiring strong convexity. The new error bound is of independent interest and could potentially be applied to other problems involving trace norm regularization.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it makes a clear theoretical contribution with significant implications for machine learning applications. It advances the state of the art by resolving an open question and providing a more general convergence guarantee. The quality is high, the writing is clear, the approach is original, and the results are significant for practitioners working with low-rank matrix problems.

I recommend acceptance. The minor weaknesses noted are easily addressable in the final version and do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution to the field. The authors have provided a rigorous, well-motivated, and technically sound paper that will be valuable to the optimization and machine learning communities.
