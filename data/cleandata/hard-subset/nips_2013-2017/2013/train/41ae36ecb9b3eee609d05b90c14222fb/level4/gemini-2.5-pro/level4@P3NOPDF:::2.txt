This paper proves the linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace norm regularized problems. The analysis is performed under an assumption on the loss function that is weaker than strong convexity, which is a significant result for many applications in machine learning and signal processing.

The paper is carefully prepared and clearly written. The authors' approach closely follows the Lipschitzian error bound technique introduced by Tseng [20], and their successful application of this framework to the trace norm setting resolves what was, to my knowledge, an open question. The main theoretical arguments and proofs appear to be sound and correct.

**Major Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

My main suggestion for improving the paper is to include a more explicit comparison and analogy to the vector l1-norm regularization case, as studied in works like [20, 22]. While the trace norm is often considered the matrix analogue of the l1-norm, the technical challenges in extending theoretical results are frequently non-trivial. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a dedicated discussion on this relationship.

Specifically, it would be very helpful for the authors to elaborate on the key difficulties that prevent a direct translation of existing proof techniques from the l1-norm setting to the trace norm setting. Such a discussion would better highlight the technical novelty of the current work and help readers appreciate why this result is not a straightforward generalization. This would also help contextualize the paper's value for readers who might otherwise assume the extension is trivial. Furthermore, it would be beneficial to explicitly state that the linear convergence for the l1-norm case can now be derived as a corollary of the main results presented here.

**Title and Presentation**

I would also suggest a slight change to the title. The term "structured" seems to imply a modification to the trace norm regularizer itself. However, the main novelty concerning the linear operator A is on the loss function side. A title that more accurately reflects the analysis of a more general loss term, rather than a structured regularizer, might be more appropriate. I leave this to the authors' discretion.

**Minor Issues**

*   **Notation:** The operator `P` is referred to as a "sampling" operator. As `P` is fixed and deterministic in this context, the term "mask operator" might be more precise, since "sampling" can carry connotations of randomness.
*   **Broken Reference:** The reference on line 337 appears to be broken.
*   **Clarity on Convergence:** The definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence may not be universally known. To improve self-containment and clarity, it would be helpful to briefly restate these definitions in the text.
*   **Residual Definition:** The residual R(X) is defined in equation (6). For readers familiar with the original work, it would be beneficial to explicitly connect this definition to the one provided in equation (46) by Tseng [20] to facilitate interpretation.
*   **Paper Structure:** The proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3 is quite long and technical. The authors might consider moving some of the detailed steps to an appendix. This would free up space in the main body for more high-level discussion, intuition, and conclusions, making the paper's core contributions more accessible to a broader audience.

In summary, this is a strong and valuable contribution that advances our understanding of optimization for trace norm regularized problems. My recommendations are primarily aimed at improving the paper's framing and impact by better contextualizing its novelty relative to prior work.
