Summary
The paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for convex loss plus trace-norm regularization. While global rates for PGM are sublinear and linear rates usually require strong convexity, the authors prove that when f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient (and locally strongly convex on compact subsets), PGM enjoys an asymptotic linear convergence rate—even though f itself need not be strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A). The key technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound that relates dist(X, X⋆) to the residual ‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. This enables a direct application of the Luo–Tseng framework to obtain linear convergence. The proof overcomes the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm by showing that an optimal solution X⋆ and −∇f(X⋆) share left/right singular vectors, allowing a spectral perturbation argument akin to a reduction to the vector case. The result covers losses commonly used in machine learning (e.g., square and logistic), and experiments illustrate the local linear regime.

Relation to prior work
- Standard PGM rates are O(1/k) (or O(1/k²) with acceleration) under Lipschitz gradients; linear rates are known under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011).
- Error bounds for composite problems with non-smooth parts having polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ1 or group lasso) are established (e.g., Tseng, 2010; Zhang–Jiang–Luo, 2013). Extending such bounds to the trace norm has been open due to its non-polyhedral epigraph.
- The paper follows the Luo–Tseng error-bound-based convergence framework, with a new spectral observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization in the trace-norm setting.

Quality
- The analysis appears technically sound and carefully executed. The main ingredients—local error bound plus Luo–Tseng—are applied correctly, and the structural lemma on simultaneous singular vectors is both plausible and well-motivated.
- Minor gap to clarify: in the appendix (line 085), please explain precisely how the stated choice of κ3 (for αk < 1/Lf) implies ‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F. As noted above, this follows from Lemma 4 of Sra (NIPS 2012); adding that citation and a brief derivation would remove ambiguity.
- The numerical experiments are small-scale but consistent with the theory, showing a linear regime once iterates are near the solution set.

Clarity
- The paper is readable despite technical depth. To improve exposition:
  - State the main contributions and the error bound early, then immediately present the linear convergence theorem and its short proof via the error bound and Luo–Tseng.
  - Move the bulk of the error-bound proof to an appendix, keeping in the main text only the key ideas (the shared singular vectors and the perturbation argument).
  - Define all constants (e.g., η, ε, κi) when first used and briefly discuss their dependence on problem data.
  - In experiments, specify step-size policies (fixed vs. backtracking) and any estimates of Lf.

Originality
- The overall approach follows established templates (error bounds + Luo–Tseng). The novel part is the spectral argument showing that X⋆ and −∇f(X⋆) share singular vectors, which enables a non-polyhedral error bound for the trace norm. This is a meaningful and nontrivial extension beyond ℓ1/group lasso settings. Other steps largely mirror prior analyses.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is solid: establishing an asymptotic linear rate for trace-norm regularized problems without strong convexity broadens the regime where fast local rates are guaranteed, aligning theory with practical observations.
- Practical impact on algorithm design is limited, as the result is asymptotic and does not deliver new methods or easily verifiable global conditions. Still, it provides reassurance for widely used PGM implementations and may inspire further work on sharper non-asymptotic bounds or broader regularizers.

Pros
- First linear-rate result for trace-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- New Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm; spectral insight is elegant and potentially useful elsewhere.
- Broad applicability to common losses (square, logistic).
- Analysis is careful; experiments match the expected local linear regime.

Cons
- Linear rate is asymptotic/local; practical constants and radius of the linear region are not characterized.
- Algorithmic novelty is limited; the convergence proof largely follows Luo–Tseng once the error bound is in place.
- Some presentation could be streamlined; a few technical steps (e.g., κ3 bound) need clarification/citation.

Recommendation
A theoretically interesting and incremental contribution. With minor clarifications (especially the κ3 inequality and improved exposition), the paper would make a useful addition for readers interested in error bounds and convergence theory for low-rank regularization.
