### Review

**Summary**

This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized risk minimization. The sublinear global convergence rate of PGM is well-understood, and it is also known that the rate improves to linear when the loss function is strongly convex. This work relaxes the strong convexity assumption, showing that PGM still achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate for a broad class of loss functions commonly used in machine learning (e.g., square loss, logistic loss). The analysis follows the established framework of Luo and Tseng, which relies on proving a local Lipschitzian error bound. The main technical contribution is a new error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, which is established using a novel observation that allows for the simultaneous diagonalization of the relevant matrices.

**Quality**

The paper appears to be technically sound. The core of the argument rests on establishing the local error bound in Theorem 3.1, and the proof strategy seems valid. A key step involves relating the residual `‖R(X^k)‖_F` to the iteration progress `‖X^k - X^{k+1}‖_F` (inequality 11). While the appendix provides some justification, a more explicit derivation of this inequality would strengthen the proof. It is worth noting that a similar result (`‖R(X^k)‖_F ≤ κ_3 ‖X^k - X^{k+1}‖_F`) has been formally established in Lemma 4 of "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting" by S. Sra (NIPS 2012). Citing or adapting this existing proof could clarify this point for the reader.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. However, the presentation could be improved by altering the order of the proofs. After stating the main error bound result (Theorem 3.1), the authors immediately dive into its highly technical proof. It might be more effective to first show how this error bound leads to the linear convergence of PGM (the proof of Theorem 3.2), as this part is a more standard application of the Luo-Tseng framework and would help motivate the importance of the error bound. The intricate proof of the error bound itself could then be presented afterward. This restructuring would allow the reader to grasp the overall logic before delving into the most complex technical details.

**Originality**

The paper's originality is moderate. The overall approach of using a local error bound to prove linear convergence for PGM is not new and follows a well-established line of research. The primary novelty lies in the technique used to prove the error bound for the trace norm case. The observation that allows for simultaneous singular value decomposition of the solution `X̄` and the gradient term `-∇f(X̄)` is clever and interesting. It enables the authors to analyze the spectral properties of the iterates and effectively reduce the matrix problem to a more tractable, vector-like analysis. Aside from this key insight, the remainder of the analysis largely adapts existing techniques to the trace norm setting.

**Significance**

The paper's main contribution is theoretical. It provides a valuable extension of the linear convergence guarantees for PGM to a more practical and wider class of problems where strong convexity does not hold. This is an important step in better understanding the behavior of one of the most common algorithms for trace norm regularization. However, the significance is somewhat tempered by the asymptotic nature of the result; the linear rate is only guaranteed locally, once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. The work is unlikely to lead to new algorithm designs, but it provides a more solid theoretical foundation for an existing one. The matrix diagonalization technique may also be of independent interest to researchers working on related problems.

**Pros:**

*   Addresses an important theoretical question by extending linear convergence results for PGM beyond the restrictive strong convexity assumption.
*   The proof contains a novel and elegant technical contribution concerning the simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, which is key to establishing the error bound for the trace norm.
*   The result applies to several important machine learning problems, including matrix completion and multi-task learning.

**Cons:**

*   The work is largely incremental, building heavily on the established analytical framework of Luo and Tseng.
*   The linear convergence result is asymptotic, which may limit its direct practical implications, as it does not characterize the "pre-asymptotic" sublinear phase.
*   The overall theoretical contribution, while solid, is of moderate impact.

In summary, this is a solid theoretical paper that proves an interesting and useful result about the asymptotic convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularization. While the overall framework is not new, the paper introduces a novel proof technique to handle the specific challenges of the trace norm. It represents a valuable, albeit incremental, contribution to the optimization literature in machine learning.
