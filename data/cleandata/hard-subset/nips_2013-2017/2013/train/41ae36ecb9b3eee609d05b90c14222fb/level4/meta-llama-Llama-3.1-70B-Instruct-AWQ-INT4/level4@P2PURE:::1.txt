This paper proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for the trace-norm regularized learning problem when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear, h is strongly convex on any compact set, and has a Lipschitz gradient. The paper extends the results of Tseng [20], Tseng and Yun, and Zhang et al. [22] from lasso and group lasso to the trace norm, leveraging the "error-bound condition." Although this extension is non-trivial, the contribution appears to be purely technical.

The presentation of the proofs is mostly clear. The strengths of the paper include demonstrating the linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, building upon the results of Tseng et al. However, the weaknesses of the paper are that the contribution is primarily technical and lacks a numerical example illustrating the linear convergence.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following:

* Include a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex but the proximal gradient algorithm converges linearly, exploring different levels of stability. It is also crucial to discuss the scenarios in which the theory fails.
* Discuss how the analysis can be extended to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM).

Specifically, I have the following comments:

1. The proof outlines of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 bear a strong resemblance to those in Tseng's work. To clarify their contribution, the authors should provide more precise references to the original work.
2. A numerical example demonstrating linear convergence (e.g., a semilog plot of function value versus the number of iterations) would be beneficial.
3. The dependence of constants κ1, ..., κ4 on the choice of ᾱ and α̿ is unclear and warrants further explanation.

Additionally, I have a minor issue with the sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13), which is somewhat confusing. The last inequality follows from the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that -Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X‖∗, rather than the intermediate inequality (as seen on p289 in [20]).
