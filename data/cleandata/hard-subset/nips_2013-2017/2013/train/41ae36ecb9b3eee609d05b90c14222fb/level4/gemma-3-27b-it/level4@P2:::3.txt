This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, a common task in machine learning. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under conditions weaker than strong convexity of the loss function, extending prior work. Their approach leverages a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, facilitated by a key observation regarding the spectral structure of solutions.

The paper is technically sound and presents a logical progression of arguments. The main result – linear convergence of the PGM without strong convexity – is a valuable addition to the theoretical understanding of this widely used algorithm. However, a clarification regarding the inequality R(X^k) <= kappa3 |X^k - X^{k+1}|_F in the appendix (line 85) would strengthen the presentation. Specifically, explaining how a choice of kappa3 (with alpha < 1) leads to this bound is necessary. 

The paper is generally well-written and accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization and matrix factorization techniques. While the technical details are dense, the overall structure is clear. Consider restructuring the presentation to first highlight the main claims (error bound and linear convergence) before delving into the detailed proof of the error bound. This would improve the initial impact and readability. Presenting the linear rate proof earlier, as it builds directly on the error bound and involves less novelty, could also streamline the narrative.

The originality of the work is moderate. The insightful observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices is a noteworthy contribution. However, the majority of the analysis relies on established techniques, particularly the framework developed by Luo and Tseng. While the application to trace norm regularization and the diagonalization technique are valuable, the work is largely an extension of existing methodologies.

The significance of this paper primarily lies in its theoretical contribution. While the relaxation of the strong convexity assumption is practically relevant, the asymptotic nature of the convergence result somewhat limits its direct impact on algorithm design or practical implementations. The diagonalization technique may be of independent interest to researchers working with matrix optimization problems. The paper establishes a theoretical foundation for understanding the behavior of the PGM in a broader class of problems, but the incremental nature of the improvement means the impact is moderate.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and rigorous analysis of the PGM’s convergence rate.
*   The extension of linear convergence to cases without strong convexity is a valuable theoretical result.
*   The novel observation regarding matrix diagonalization is a potentially useful contribution.
*   The work builds upon a well-established framework, adding a specific and meaningful extension.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The asymptotic nature of the convergence result limits its immediate practical impact.
*   The originality of the work is moderate, relying heavily on existing techniques.
*   A clarification regarding the inequality in the appendix would enhance the paper’s completeness.
*   The work is primarily theoretical, with limited discussion of algorithmic implications or experimental validation beyond what's included.
