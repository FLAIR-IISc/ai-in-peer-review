The paper proves linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace norm regularized problems, under weaker assumptions than strong convexity of the loss function, which is a crucial problem for many machine learning (ML) and signal processing applications.

The paper is carefully and clearly written, closely following the Lipschitzian error bound technique of Tseng, whose application to the nuclear norm case was still left as an open question. The main part of the proof seems correct, as far as I can understand.

However, I would have liked to see a clear comparison or analogy to the vector case of l1-norm regularization, as in related work. It would be helpful to relate the results and techniques used in that case and explain why the l1-case techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm case. This comparison would also highlight the significance of the paper's contribution, as some readers might find it not extremely surprising that the l1-result also holds for the trace norm.

I suggest a slight change of the title of the paper, as the main new contribution affects the loss part more than making the trace norm regularization a structured one. The newly gained flexibility is on the loss side, not in the regularizer.

Minor issues include:

- Notation: Instead of calling P a sampling or mask operator, consider using a more specific term, as "sampling" might imply randomness, but P is fixed.
- Reference l337 is broken.
- The meanings of "Q-" and "R-" linear convergence could be repeated for clarity, as they might not be widely known.
- The residual R(X) defined in (6) could be explained in relation to the original definition by Tseng for better interpretation.

In general, I would suggest including a bit more discussion and conclusions in favor of the long technical part of the proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3.

After receiving feedback from the authors, I appreciate their responses to most of my questions. Regarding the title, I referred to the word "structured," which I found unnecessary in the context. However, I leave this to the authors' discretion.

Overall, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field, and with some minor revisions, it has the potential to be even stronger.
