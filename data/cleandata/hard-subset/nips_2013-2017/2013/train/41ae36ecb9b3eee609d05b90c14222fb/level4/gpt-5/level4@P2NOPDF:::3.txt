Summary:
For trace-norm–regularized risk minimization, the proximal gradient method is a standard approach. Its sublinear global rate is well known, and it achieves linear convergence under strong convexity. This paper relaxes strong convexity via the Luo–Tseng framework. The authors prove a local error bound that implies asymptotically linear convergence of proximal gradient for trace-norm regularization. The overall proof strategy largely follows established techniques, with a new observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound. My only concern was in the appendix (line 085): please clarify why the proposed choice of κ3 (e.g., with α < 1) implies R(X^k) ≤ κ3 ||X^k − X^{k+1}||_F. After reading the rebuttal, I note that a closely related inequality is justified by Sra (NIPS 2012), Lemma 4, which helps address this gap. Beyond this point, the arguments and conclusions are coherent, and the derivations are careful.

Clarity:
The exposition is generally clear, though the technical density is high. The paper would benefit from restructuring:
- State the main results upfront (the local error bound and asymptotic linear rate), along with their assumptions.
- After stating the local error bound, defer its highly technical proof to a later section or appendix, and proceed directly to show how it yields linear convergence. That part is largely standard and can be presented succinctly.
This reordering would improve readability without sacrificing rigor.

Originality:
Originality is moderate. The key novel element is the observation that enables simultaneous diagonalization, which is interesting and potentially useful on its own. Apart from this, the analysis largely follows the Luo–Tseng framework and existing techniques for establishing local error bounds; the subsequent steps to obtain linear convergence are standard.

Significance:
The contribution is mainly theoretical, with limited direct impact on algorithm design. It is valuable to extend linear convergence guarantees of proximal gradient to settings weaker than strong convexity, especially for trace-norm regularization. However, the significance is tempered by the asymptotic nature of the rate (rather than nonasymptotic bounds or explicit iteration complexity in a broad regime). The diagonalization idea may have independent value and could inspire simplifications in related analyses.

Overall assessment:
The paper provides a solid, incremental theoretical contribution: it establishes asymptotic linear convergence of proximal gradient for trace-norm–regularized risk minimization under relaxed conditions, following the Luo–Tseng approach and leveraging a useful simultaneous diagonalization insight. The work is technically sound and moderately interesting for theory-focused readers. Clarifying the appendix inequality (as addressed via the rebuttal and related literature) and reorganizing the presentation would further strengthen the paper.
