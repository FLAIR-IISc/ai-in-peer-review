This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization. While this algorithm's sublinear global convergence rate is well-understood, and a linear rate is known for strongly convex loss functions, this work relaxes the strong convexity assumption. Following the framework established by Luo and Tseng, the authors prove a local error bound that allows them to show the proximal gradient algorithm converges asymptotically at a linear rate. The key technical insight is a new observation that enables the simultaneous diagonalization of the relevant matrices, effectively reducing the problem to the vector case.

**Quality:**
The paper appears to be technically sound.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and can be followed without much difficulty. However, given its technical nature, the presentation could be improved. The paper would be easier to follow if it first stated the main claims and then delved into the details of each. Specifically, after introducing the local error bound, the authors could immediately use it to prove the linear convergence rate—a part of the proof that is relatively standard—before presenting the highly technical derivation of the error bound itself.

**Originality:**
The originality of this work is moderate. The main novel contribution is the observation that enables the simultaneous diagonalization of the matrices involved, which is an interesting technique. Beyond this, the paper largely follows the established framework of Luo and Tseng for proving local error bounds, and the subsequent steps for showing linear convergence are not new.

**Significance:**
The significance of this paper is primarily theoretical, with limited direct impact on algorithm design. Extending the linear convergence analysis of the proximal gradient algorithm to settings without strong convexity is a valuable contribution. However, this significance is somewhat tempered by the asymptotic (local) nature of the convergence guarantee. The matrix diagonalization technique may be of independent interest. Overall, this is a solid, albeit incremental, theoretical contribution that will be of moderate interest to the community.
