Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization with a trace-norm regularizer, focusing on problems of the form minX f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h has a Lipschitz gradient and is strongly convex on every compact convex subset of its open domain. The main result shows that the proximal gradient method (PGM) enjoys an asymptotic linear convergence rate for this broad class of losses, even though f itself need not be strongly convex (e.g., when A has a nontrivial nullspace). The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal set to the proximal residual ‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. Leveraging this error bound and the Luo–Tseng framework yields linear convergence of both the iterates (R-linear) and the objective values (Q-linear) under standard step-size conditions α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf.

This work extends error-bound based linear convergence results previously established for separable nonsmooth regularizers with polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) by Tseng; Tseng and Yun; and Zhang et al., to the trace norm, whose epigraph is nonpolyhedral. The analysis avoids variable-group separability by exploiting a structural property of optimal solutions: at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors. This allows a perturbation analysis in the spectral domain to prove the error bound.

Quality:
- The technical development appears sound, and the high-level logic (derive a local error bound, then invoke Luo–Tseng) is appropriate. The spectral-structure argument needed for the trace norm is nontrivial.
- The presentation of the proofs is mostly clear, but see detailed comments below on attribution and a few technical points (constants, a confusing inequality chain).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well written and organized. A few places would benefit from more precise citations and explanations (see items 1 and 4 below).
- It would be helpful to state explicitly how the constants κ1,…,κ4 in Theorem 3.2 depend on the step-size bounds and Lf.

Originality:
- Conceptually, the contribution is an extension of known error-bound based linear convergence results (from ℓ1/group-LASSO) to the trace norm. Given the nonpolyhedral geometry and coupling through singular values, this is a meaningful technical advance, though largely technical in nature rather than introducing a new algorithmic idea.

Significance:
- The result is relevant to many problems in machine learning (matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification), where trace-norm regularization is standard and strong convexity of f often fails. Establishing linear convergence under realistic structural assumptions should be of interest to both theory and practice.

Pros:
- Proves linear convergence of PGM for a widely used nonpolyhedral regularizer without strong convexity of f.
- Introduces a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, likely of independent interest.
- Uses a clear analytical pathway via Luo–Tseng once the error bound is established.

Cons:
- The main novelty is technical; the overall approach follows the existing error-bound framework.
- The empirical section could do more to directly illustrate the theoretical regime (see requests below).

Detailed comments and requests:
1) Attribution and positioning: The outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and the linear-rate result follow the Luo–Tseng framework and are close in spirit to the developments in Tseng; Tseng and Yun; and Zhang et al. Please make the links to these prior proofs more explicit in the main text (not only in the supplement), clarifying precisely which parts are adapted from prior work and which are new to handle the trace norm’s nonpolyhedral geometry and spectral coupling.

2) Numerical evidence: Please include a semilog plot of suboptimality F(Xk) − F(X∗) and of dist(Xk, X ) versus iterations to clearly demonstrate the linear rate. It would be helpful to show multiple problem instances and to indicate the onset of the local linear regime.

3) Dependence of constants: In Theorem 3.2, clarify how κ1, κ2, κ3, κ4 depend on α, ᾱ, and Lf, and whether they can be made explicit (or at least bounded) for common losses (e.g., squared and logistic). This will help readers understand practical step-size choices and the sensitivity of the linear rate to conditioning.

4) Minor technical point: The sequence of inequalities before inequality (13) is confusing. The last inequality follows from the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that −Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄‖*, not from the intermediate inequality (see p. 289 in [Tseng and Yun]). Please revise this derivation for clarity.

Additional requests after author feedback:
- Please include a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex but PGM still converges linearly (e.g., matrix completion with a non-injective A), and explore different levels of “stability”/conditioning to show how quickly the linear regime appears and how the rate changes.
- Discuss explicitly where the theory may fail: for example, if h lacks curvature on the relevant compact set, if the step size violates the prescribed bounds, or if the iterates do not enter the neighborhood where the error bound holds.
- Comment on potential extensions to ADMM. Which parts of your error-bound analysis would carry over to ADMM applied to trace-norm regularization, and what additional difficulties arise (e.g., dual residual control, over-relaxation, penalty parameter selection)? Even a brief discussion would be valuable.

Overall assessment:
This is a solid and carefully executed technical contribution that extends linear convergence guarantees for first-order methods to a widely used nonpolyhedral regularizer. With clearer attribution, more explicit constants, and empirical evidence targeted at the non-strongly-convex regime (plus a brief discussion of ADMM), the paper would be strengthened.
