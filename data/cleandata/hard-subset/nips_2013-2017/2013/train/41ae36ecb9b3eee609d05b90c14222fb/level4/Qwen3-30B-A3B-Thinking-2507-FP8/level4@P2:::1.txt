

This paper establishes linear convergence for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace-norm regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h strongly convex with Lipschitz gradient. The authors prove a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, which is the key technical contribution enabling their linear convergence result without requiring strong convexity of the loss function.

The paper builds upon previous work by Tseng and collaborators [20, 22] who established similar linear convergence results for l1-norm and group lasso regularization using error-bound conditions. This extension to trace norm regularization is non-trivial due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm, which prevents direct application of the splitting techniques used for l1-norm. The authors' key insight is recognizing that optimal solutions and their gradients share singular vectors, enabling the development of a new error bound through matrix perturbation theory.

Strengths:
1. The theoretical contribution is technically sound, with rigorous proofs that resolve an open question about trace-norm regularization. The new error bound may be of independent interest beyond this specific convergence analysis.
2. The paper correctly identifies and addresses the gap in the literature regarding linear convergence for trace-norm problems without strong convexity assumptions.
3. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice, as predicted by the theory.

Weaknesses:
1. The contribution appears primarily technical rather than conceptually novel. The paper extends existing error-bound techniques to a new setting, but the core approach is similar to previous work. The authors should more precisely articulate how their approach differs from Tseng's framework to clarify the novelty.
2. The numerical examples lack sufficient detail to verify the linear convergence claim. The authors should include semilog plots of objective value versus iterations to clearly demonstrate the linear convergence rate, as requested in the revision.
3. The dependence of constants κ1-κ4 on the step size parameters α and ᾱ is not specified, which limits the practical applicability of the theoretical result.
4. The connection to previous work (e.g., Tseng [20]) could be more precisely articulated to better position the contribution within the literature.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs. The error bound is well-established, and the linear convergence result follows from standard analysis frameworks. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the analysis. The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of how the constants in the convergence rate depend on problem parameters.

Clarity: The presentation is generally clear, but the connection to prior work could be improved. The proof outlines of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 are similar to Tseng's approach, and the authors should more precisely cite the specific elements of prior work that they build upon. The sequence of inequalities before inequality (13) is confusing; the final inequality follows from the convexity of the trace norm and the optimality condition (-Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄‖*), not the intermediate inequality as suggested.

Originality: The extension of linear convergence results from l1-norm/group lasso to trace norm is non-trivial but represents a technical extension rather than a fundamentally new approach. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how this differs from previous error-bound approaches for composite optimization problems.

Significance: The results have practical relevance for machine learning applications involving trace-norm regularization, particularly in matrix completion and classification. However, the significance is limited to the specific class of loss functions considered (f(X) = h(A(X))). The paper could better articulate how this extends beyond current practice and why this specific case is particularly important.

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. Including a semilog plot demonstrating linear convergence for a non-strongly convex loss function
2. Providing more detailed discussion of the constants in the convergence rate
3. Clarifying the relationship to prior work and precisely articulating the novel contributions
4. Addressing the specific case where the theory might fail (e.g., when the linear operator A has a non-trivial nullspace)

This work makes a valuable technical contribution to the analysis of first-order methods for trace-norm regularization, but the novelty is primarily in the application of error-bound techniques to a new setting rather than in the development of fundamentally new theoretical tools. The extension to ADMM would be an interesting future direction, as the authors suggest, but is not addressed in the current work.

Overall, the paper presents sound theoretical results that fill an important gap in the literature, but the contribution is primarily technical rather than conceptually innovative. With the requested revisions, this would be a strong contribution to the field.
