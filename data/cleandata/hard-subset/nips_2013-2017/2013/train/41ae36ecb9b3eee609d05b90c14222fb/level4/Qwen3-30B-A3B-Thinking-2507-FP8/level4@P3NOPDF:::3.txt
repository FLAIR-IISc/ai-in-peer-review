

This paper addresses the convergence rate of proximal gradient (PG) methods for trace norm regularized risk minimization. The authors extend prior work by establishing asymptotic linear convergence under a relaxed strong convexity assumption, leveraging the framework of Luo and Tseng. The key novelty lies in a new observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, which reduces the problem to a vector case and facilitates the local error bound proof. The main contribution is the proof of a local error bound for trace norm regularization, leading to asymptotic linear convergence.

**Quality**  
The technical content is sound overall. The proof of the local error bound relies on Lemma 4 from Sra’s NIPS 2012 paper ("Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting"), which explicitly establishes $|R_k| \leq \kappa |X_k - X_{k+1}|$ under analogous conditions. This resolves the concern raised in the initial review regarding the derivation of the error bound. However, a gap remains in Appendix A (line 085): the choice of $\kappa_3$ (specifically for $\alpha < 1$) must be justified to rigorously derive $R(X^k) \leq \kappa_3 \|X^k - X^{k+1}\|_F$. The authors should clarify the dependence of $\kappa_3$ on $\alpha$ and the underlying constants.

**Clarity**  
The paper is generally well-structured and readable, though the flow could be improved. The authors state the local error bound early but delay its technical proof until after introducing the convergence result. A more effective organization would first present the main claims (local error bound, linear convergence), then outline the proof strategy, and finally provide the technical details. This would help readers grasp the significance before engaging with the intricate diagonalization argument. The convergence proof itself (Section 4) is standard within the Luo-Tseng framework and adds little novelty beyond the error bound. The current presentation risks obscuring the paper’s primary contribution—the diagonalization insight—by burying it within a lengthy technical proof.

**Originality**  
The paper’s originality is moderate. The simultaneous diagonalization technique is a novel and valuable observation for trace norm problems, as it simplifies the non-vector case to a scalar one. However, the broader approach—using a local error bound to establish linear convergence—builds directly on Luo and Tseng’s framework and Sra’s prior work. The rest of the analysis (e.g., convergence rate derivation, asymptotic arguments) is incremental. Thus, while the diagonalization step is interesting and non-trivial, the paper does not introduce a fundamentally new methodology for trace norm optimization.

**Significance**  
The theoretical significance is modest. The result extends PG convergence to a broader class of problems (relaxed strong convexity), which is meaningful for understanding the algorithm’s behavior in non-ideal settings. However, the asymptotic nature of the convergence (i.e., linear rate holds only as $k \to \infty$) limits practical impact, as finite-step guarantees are typically required in applications. The diagonalization technique may have independent utility for other matrix optimization problems, but the paper does not explore this further. Consequently, the work contributes to the theoretical understanding of PG methods but offers limited guidance for algorithmic design or empirical improvements.

**Summary**  
This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the convergence analysis of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. The core innovation—a simultaneous diagonalization argument enabling a local error bound under relaxed strong convexity—is non-trivial and well-motivated. The proof is valid but would benefit from clearer exposition, particularly regarding the choice of $\kappa_3$ in Appendix A. While the overall approach follows established frameworks, the diagonalization insight provides a useful technical tool. The significance is primarily theoretical, with limited practical implications due to the asymptotic convergence guarantee. The paper is suitable for publication at NIPS 2013, provided the authors address the gap in Appendix A and refine the presentation to highlight the novel diagonalization step earlier.
