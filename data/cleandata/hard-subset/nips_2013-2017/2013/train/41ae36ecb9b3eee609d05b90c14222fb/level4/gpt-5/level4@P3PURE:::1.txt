Summary
This paper proves linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm–regularized learning problems where the loss takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A linear and h strongly convex on any compact set and with Lipschitz-continuous gradient. The work extends results based on the error-bound condition—established by Tseng [20], Tseng and Yun (“A coordinate gradient descent method for nonsmooth separable minimization”), and Zhang et al. [22] for Lasso and group Lasso—to the trace norm. While this is a nontrivial extension, the contribution appears primarily technical. The presentation of the proofs is mostly clear.

Strengths
- Establishes linear convergence of the proximal-gradient algorithm, extending the error-bound–based results of Tseng et al. to trace-norm regularization.

Weaknesses
- The contribution is primarily technical.
- The paper lacks a numerical example that empirically demonstrates the claimed linear convergence.

Detailed comments
1) The outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 appear very similar to those in Tseng’s work. Please cite and discuss the connections to the original arguments more precisely to clarify what is new here and what is adapted.

2) Please include a numerical experiment that clearly shows linear convergence (e.g., a semilog plot of suboptimality or function value versus iteration count).

3) It is unclear how the constants κ1, …, κ4 depend on the choices of underbar α and overbar α. Please make these dependencies explicit.

Minor issue
4) The sequence of inequalities leading up to inequality (13) is confusing. The final inequality follows from the convexity of the trace norm and from −Ḡ ∈ τ ∂||X||∗, not from the intermediate inequality (see p. 289 in [20]).

Overall assessment
A technically solid extension of prior linear-convergence results based on the error-bound condition from Lasso and group Lasso to the trace norm. Including empirical validation, clarifying the dependence of constants, tightening attribution to prior proofs, and discussing applicability beyond strong convexity (and to ADMM) would significantly strengthen the paper.
