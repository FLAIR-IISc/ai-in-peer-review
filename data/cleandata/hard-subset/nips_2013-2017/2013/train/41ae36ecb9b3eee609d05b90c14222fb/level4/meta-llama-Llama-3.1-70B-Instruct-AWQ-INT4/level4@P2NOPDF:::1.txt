This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized learning problems when the loss function is of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strongly convex on any compact set with a Lipschitz gradient. The paper builds upon the work of Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which demonstrated the same result using the "error-bound condition" for lasso and group lasso, and extends it to the trace norm.

The presentation of the proofs is mostly clear, but the contribution appears to be primarily technical. A key strength of the paper is its demonstration of the linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, extending the results of Tseng et al. However, the lack of a numerical example illustrating this linear convergence is a notable weakness.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should consider including a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex but the proximal gradient algorithm converges linearly, discussing the implications of different levels of stability and the limitations of the theory. Furthermore, exploring the extension of this analysis to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) would be beneficial.

Several minor issues and suggestions for improvement are noted. Firstly, the outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 bear a strong resemblance to those in Tseng [20]. To clarify their contribution, the authors should provide more precise references to the original work. Secondly, a numerical example demonstrating the linear convergence, such as a semilog plot of the function value versus the number of iterations, would be a valuable addition. Thirdly, the dependence of the constants κ1, …, κ4 on the choice of ᾱ and ᾱ is not clearly explained. Lastly, the sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is confusing, and it would be helpful to rephrase this section for clarity.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a technical contribution to the field by extending the linear convergence result to the trace norm, its impact would be enhanced by the inclusion of numerical examples and a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of the theory.
