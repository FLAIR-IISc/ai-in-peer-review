This paper analyzes the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization. While the sublinear global convergence rate of this algorithm is well-established, its linear convergence rate is only known to hold when the loss function is strongly convex. This work relaxes the strong convexity assumption, following the framework established by Luo and Tseng. The authors prove a local error bound which they then use to establish that the proximal gradient method converges asymptotically at a linear rate. The proof of the local error bound generally follows a standard pattern, but leverages a new and interesting observation that allows for the simultaneous diagonalization of the matrices involved.

**Quality:**
The paper appears to be technically sound. My primary question concerned the justification for an inequality in the appendix (line 085): `R(X^k) <= kappa3 |X^k - X^{k+1}|_F`. In their rebuttal, the authors clarified that the proof for this has been established in Lemma 4 of "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting," by S. Sra, NIPS 2012. This resolves my concern.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and I was able to follow the arguments without significant difficulty. However, given the technical nature of the work, its clarity could be improved by presenting the main claims upfront before delving into the detailed proofs. For instance, after stating the local error bound, the authors could immediately use it to prove the linear convergence rate—a result that is a standard consequence of the bound—and postpone the technical proof of the bound itself.

**Originality:**
The originality of this work is moderate. The key novel contribution is the observation that allows for the simultaneous diagonalization of the relevant matrices. Beyond this, the paper largely follows an existing framework to establish the local error bound, and the subsequent steps of the analysis are standard.

**Significance and Summary:**
The significance of this paper is primarily theoretical, with limited immediate impact on algorithm design. Extending the linear convergence analysis of the proximal gradient algorithm to a more practical scenario without strong convexity is a valuable contribution. However, this significance is somewhat diminished by the asymptotic nature of the claim. The technique for handling matrix diagonalization may be of independent interest.

In summary, this paper proves that the proximal gradient algorithm, when applied to trace norm regularized risk minimization, converges with an asymptotic linear rate under a relaxed convexity condition. The analysis relies on an established framework but introduces a novel diagonalization technique to reduce the matrix problem to a vector-level analysis. Overall, the work is an incremental but moderately interesting theoretical contribution.
