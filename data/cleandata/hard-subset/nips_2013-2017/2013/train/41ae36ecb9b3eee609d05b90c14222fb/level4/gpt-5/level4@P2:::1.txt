Post-rebuttal requests
- Please add a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex (e.g., A has a nontrivial nullspace) but PGM still exhibits linear convergence. Show several levels of conditioning/stability, and discuss when the theory does not apply or when linear convergence is not observed.

Summary
The paper studies composite minimization with trace-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗ where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h has a Lipschitz-continuous gradient and is strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its open domain (h is thus strictly convex). The main result is a Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal set to the proximal-gradient residual ‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. Using the Luo–Tseng framework, this error bound yields asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) under fixed step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf), without requiring f itself to be strongly convex (A may be non-injective). The analysis hinges on a spectral-structure observation: at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same left/right singular vectors; this, together with matrix perturbation arguments, enables control of the nonpolyhedral trace-norm term and delivers the desired error bound. The results cover commonly used losses such as squared and logistic loss.

Relation to prior work
The paper extends error-bound based linear-convergence analyses previously established for separable nonsmooth regularizers with polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ1 and group LASSO) by Tseng (2009), Tseng and Yun (2009), and Zhang et al. (2013), to the nonpolyhedral, nonseparable trace norm. The extension is nontrivial because the trace norm couples all matrix entries via singular values, precluding blockwise arguments used for ℓ1/group regularizers. The simultaneous SVD property and subsequent perturbation analysis provide the new ingredient required for the trace norm.

Quality
The results appear technically sound, and the proof strategy is clear overall. The key lemmas are well-motivated, and the use of the Luo–Tseng framework is appropriate once the error bound is in place. However, some constants and their dependence on algorithmic parameters are not explicit, and the paper would benefit from clearer attribution and delineation of what is new versus adapted from prior analyses.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Suggestions:
- More precisely identify which parts of the proofs follow Tseng/Tseng–Yun and which parts are novel (e.g., the simultaneous SVD and perturbation steps).
- Make explicit how κ1, κ2, κ3, κ4 depend on α, ᾱ, and Lf in Theorem 3.2 (or provide bounds).
- Clarify the sequence of inequalities preceding (13): the last inequality follows from convexity of the trace norm and −Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄‖∗ (cf. p. 289 in Tseng, 2009); the intermediate inequality as written is confusing.
- Include a discussion of failure modes: which assumptions on h and A are essential, and what breaks if, for example, h is not strictly convex on dom(h) or ∇h is not Lipschitz?

Originality
The core novelty is the Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization and its proof via spectral alignment and perturbation arguments. While the high-level convergence framework is known, obtaining an error bound for a nonpolyhedral, spectrally defined regularizer is a meaningful contribution beyond a routine extension. That said, the contribution is primarily technical.

Significance
Trace-norm regularization is central in matrix completion, multitask learning, and related problems. Establishing linear convergence of a widely used first-order method without global strong convexity of f is practically important and may inform analyses of other algorithms (e.g., ADMM, proximal Newton). Providing clear guidance on step-size choices and constants would further enhance applicability.

Pros
- First linear-convergence result for PGM on trace-norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of f, covering common losses.
- New Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm; likely of independent interest.
- Proofs are largely clear, with a plausible and well-motivated structure.

Cons
- Contribution is mainly technical; the broader algorithmic landscape (e.g., ADMM) is not discussed.
- Dependence of constants in the rate on algorithmic parameters is not explicit.
- Empirical validation could be strengthened to illustrate the asymptotic linear regime and boundary cases where assumptions fail.

Requests to authors
1) Attribution and novelty: Refine the comparison with Tseng (2009), Tseng and Yun (2009), and Zhang et al. (2013), pinpointing precisely which arguments are reused and which are new for the trace norm.
2) Constants and step sizes: Specify or bound κ1–κ4 in terms of α, ᾱ, and Lf, and comment on practical step-size selection (fixed vs. backtracking) consistent with the theory.
3) Experiments: Provide semilog plots of objective residuals and distance to the solution set (or residual norm) versus iterations in settings where f is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with underdetermined A), and vary conditioning to illustrate the onset of linear convergence.
4) Failure cases: Discuss when the error bound may not hold (e.g., if ∇h is not Lipschitz on relevant level sets, or if h is not strictly convex on dom(h)), and whether only sublinear convergence is then guaranteed.
5) Proof detail: Clarify the inequality chain before (13) as noted above; ensure all assumptions used in Lemma 4.1(c) and Proposition 4.2 are stated when invoked.
6) Outlook: Comment on extending the error-bound approach to ADMM for trace-norm problems and what technical obstacles arise (e.g., primal–dual residual control, nonexpansiveness of the Douglas–Rachford operator in the spectral setting).

Overall assessment
A solid and carefully executed technical contribution that extends error-bound based linear-convergence theory to trace-norm regularization. While the novelty is primarily technical, the result is useful and relevant to NeurIPS. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the dependence of constants and the relation to prior work would significantly improve the paper.
