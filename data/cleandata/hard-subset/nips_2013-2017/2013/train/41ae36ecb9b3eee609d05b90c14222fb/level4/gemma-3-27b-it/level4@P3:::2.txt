This paper presents a significant contribution to the theory of proximal gradient methods by establishing linear convergence for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a crucial result for applications in machine learning and signal processing where strongly convex loss functions are not always available. The paper is well-written and clearly structured, building logically upon existing work, particularly the Lipschitzian error bound techniques of Luo and Tseng [10]. The proof appears sound based on my understanding.

However, a more thorough connection to the well-established literature on l1-norm regularization would strengthen the paper. While the authors acknowledge the relatedness, a detailed comparison of techniques and challenges in the vector l1-norm case (e.g., [10, 22]) versus the matrix trace norm case would be valuable. Specifically, explicitly outlining why existing methods for the l1-norm do *not* directly translate to the trace norm setting would highlight the novelty of the contribution. Demonstrating that the l1-norm result follows as a direct corollary from the presented theorem would further solidify this connection.

The current title, "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization," feels slightly misleading. The primary advance lies in the handling of the loss function’s structure via the linear operator *A*, rather than a fundamental property of the trace norm itself. A title reflecting this (e.g., “Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Composite Problems with Trace Norm Regularization”) might be more accurate. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant theoretical result:** Proving linear convergence without strong convexity is a substantial advancement.
*   **Clear and well-organized writing:** The paper is easy to follow and understand.
*   **Strong technical foundation:** The proof appears rigorous and builds upon established techniques.
*   **Relevance to practical applications:** The result has direct implications for various machine learning tasks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited discussion of l1-norm regularization:** A more detailed comparison to existing work on l1-norm regularization would enhance the paper's context and impact.
*   **Potentially misleading title:** The current title downplays the importance of the loss function’s structure.
*   **Minor notational ambiguity:** The term “sampling operator” (P) could be clarified, as it describes a fixed mapping.
*   **Broken reference:** Reference [33] appears to be missing.
*   **Specialized convergence terminology:** The definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence might benefit from a brief repetition for broader accessibility.
*   **Limited discussion of Lemma 3.2:** The lengthy technical proof of Lemma 3.2 could be balanced with a more substantial discussion of its implications.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically significant result.
*   The proof is well-structured and appears to be correct.
*   The result has broad applicability to machine learning and signal processing.
*   The paper addresses a relevant and open question in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a detailed comparison to l1-norm regularization weakens the paper's contextualization.
*   The title is slightly misleading.
*   The minor issues mentioned above (notation, reference, terminology) could detract from the overall clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend accepting this paper, contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the comparison to l1-norm regularization and clarifying the title. The theoretical contribution is significant, and the paper is generally well-written and rigorous. Addressing the minor issues will further enhance its clarity and impact.
