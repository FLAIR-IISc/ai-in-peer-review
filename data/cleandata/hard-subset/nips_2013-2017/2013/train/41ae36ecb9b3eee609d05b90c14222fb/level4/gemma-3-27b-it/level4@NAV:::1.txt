This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient (PG) method for trace-norm regularized learning problems, specifically when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strongly convex on compact sets with a Lipschitz gradient. The work builds upon prior results by Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which demonstrated similar convergence properties for Lasso and group Lasso using an “error-bound condition”, extending this framework to the trace norm setting. While a non-trivial technical undertaking, the core contribution appears primarily incremental. 

The proofs are presented with reasonable clarity. 

**Strengths:**

- Demonstrates linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, successfully extending previous results by Tseng et al.

**Weaknesses:**

- The contribution is largely technical in nature.
- A numerical illustration of the established linear convergence is lacking.

**Detailed Comments:**

1. The proof structure for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely mirrors that of Tseng [20]. The authors should provide more explicit references to highlight the novel aspects of their work and better delineate their contribution.

2. Inclusion of a numerical experiment verifying the linear convergence behavior would significantly strengthen the paper. A semilog plot showcasing the function value versus iteration number would be particularly informative.

3. The dependence of the constants κ<sub>1</sub> through κ<sub>4</sub> on the parameters  <u>α and </u>α is unclear and warrants further explanation.

**Minor Issue:**

4. The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat convoluted. The final inequality’s justification relies on the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient inclusion  -<u>G ∈ τ∂||X||</u><sub>*</sub>, as detailed on page 289 of Tseng [20], rather than the preceding intermediate inequality. 

**Request for Revision:**

To enhance the paper, I request the authors to: 

*   Include a simulation demonstrating linear convergence, even in scenarios where the loss function is not strongly convex. Vary the level of stability in the simulation and analyze the observed convergence rates. Critically, discuss the limitations of the current theoretical analysis—specifically, under what conditions the theory might fail to predict observed behavior. 
*   Discuss the potential for extending this analysis to the more general Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) algorithm.



This work represents an extension of existing linear convergence results, leveraging the “error-bound condition” previously applied to Lasso and group Lasso problems, now adapted for the trace norm case.
