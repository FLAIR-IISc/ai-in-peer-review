This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace norm regularized optimization problems. The key contribution is showing that this rate can be achieved without the standard assumption of strong convexity on the loss function, a condition that is often not met in important machine learning applications like matrix completion. The analysis hinges on a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem, which is a significant result in its own right.

The work builds directly upon the convergence analysis framework developed by Luo and Tseng for composite convex optimization. While similar error bounds and subsequent linear convergence results have been established for L1-norm and group LASSO regularization, the authors correctly note that the extension to the trace norm was an open question. This is because previous proof techniques relied on separability properties that are not present in the trace norm, whose singular values depend on all matrix entries. The paper overcomes this challenge by leveraging matrix perturbation theory and a key structural property of the optimal solution set.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a crucial and highly relevant problem. PGM is a workhorse algorithm for trace norm minimization, and understanding its convergence properties under realistic assumptions is of great theoretical and practical importance. Proving linear convergence without strong convexity significantly broadens the applicability of these theoretical guarantees.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The primary contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—is novel and resolves a known open problem. The proof technique, which relies on analyzing the spectral structure of matrices near the optimal set, is sophisticated and appears to be technically sound. This is a solid theoretical contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is carefully structured and clearly written. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, stating their assumptions, and outlining the structure of their proof, making a dense technical subject accessible.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Context and Comparison with L1-norm Regularization:** The paper's impact could be enhanced by a more explicit comparison to the vector case of L1-norm regularization (e.g., Tseng [22]). While the introduction mentions that the L1-norm proof techniques do not directly translate, a more detailed discussion would be valuable. Specifically, explaining *why* the non-separability of singular values presents a fundamental challenge and how the proposed matrix-theoretic approach (e.g., Proposition 4.2) overcomes this would better highlight the paper's novelty for readers familiar with the L1-norm literature. A brief comment on whether the L1-norm case can be recovered as a special case (for diagonal matrices) would also be illuminating.

2.  **Expanding Discussion:** The paper is heavily focused on the technical proof. While this is its core, a slightly expanded discussion or conclusion section could broaden its appeal. For example, the authors could briefly discuss the implications of their error bound beyond the PGM algorithm, or touch upon the challenges of extending this analysis to other matrix norms.

3.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   The definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence are standard in optimization but may not be universally known in the broader NIPS community. Including a brief, one-sentence definition for each would improve readability.
    *   In the experiments, the operator `P` is referred to as a "sampling operator." This term might suggest a random process, whereas the operator is fixed within the optimization problem. Using a more precise term like "masking operator" or "selection operator" could avoid this minor ambiguity.
    *   To better connect with the foundational work of Tseng [22], the authors could add a short sentence explaining how their definition of the residual map `R(X)` in equation (7) relates to the general optimality conditions used in that line of work.
    *   There appears to be a broken reference on line 337.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a strong theoretical result—linear convergence without strong convexity—for a widely used class of optimization problems in machine learning.
*   It successfully resolves a non-trivial open question by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization.
*   The work is technically sound, novel, and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Some might view the contribution as an extension of an existing theoretical framework (Luo and Tseng's error bounds) to a new regularizer, albeit a very important and challenging one.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable and non-trivial contribution to the optimization literature relevant to machine learning. The theoretical results are significant, and the paper is well-executed. Despite the minor suggestions for improvement, I believe the paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
