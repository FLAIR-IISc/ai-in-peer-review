This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. It is well-known that PGM exhibits a sublinear convergence rate for this class of problems. While this rate can be improved to linear if the smooth part of the objective (the loss function) is strongly convex, this assumption often does not hold in practice.

The main contribution of this paper is to show that PGM achieves an *asymptotic* linear convergence rate under a less restrictive condition. Specifically, the authors assume the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function and `A` is an arbitrary linear operator. This setting covers common loss functions like the square loss and logistic loss, which are not necessarily strongly convex. The key technical tool enabling this result is a new local Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. The overall analytical framework follows the established approach of Luo and Tseng (1993), which connects such error bounds to the linear convergence of descent methods. The novelty lies in successfully establishing this error bound for the trace norm, which, unlike the `l1`-norm, has a non-polyhedral epigraph and requires a more sophisticated analysis of the matrix's spectral properties.

### Quality
The paper appears to be technically sound, and the theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs. The argument is carefully constructed, building from the properties of the optimal set to the main error bound theorem and its subsequent application to the PGM convergence analysis.

A point that could be clarified is the derivation of inequality (11) in the proof of Theorem 3.2. This inequality, `‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F`, is a crucial step linking the residual to the progress of the iterates. While the proof is deferred to the supplementary material, this relationship is a known result in the analysis of proximal gradient methods. For completeness and to make the paper more self-contained, it would be beneficial to either provide a brief derivation or cite prior work where this has been established (e.g., Lemma 4 in "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting" by Sra, NIPS 2012). Otherwise, the technical development is rigorous and convincing.

### Clarity
The paper is well-written and organized, and I was able to follow the main arguments without significant difficulty. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the contribution within the existing literature.

For improved readability, the authors might consider a slight restructuring of Section 3. After presenting the main error bound result (Theorem 3.1), it could be beneficial to immediately proceed with the proof of linear convergence (Theorem 3.2). This would show the reader how the error bound is leveraged to achieve the final result, thus presenting the high-level structure of the argument first. The highly technical and intricate proof of the error bound itself (currently Section 4) could then be presented afterward. This is a minor suggestion, as the current structure is also logical and easy to follow for an expert in the field.

### Originality
The originality of the paper is moderate but contains a significant technical novelty. The high-level strategy of using a local error bound to prove linear convergence is not new and follows the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng. The subsequent steps to prove linear convergence from the error bound are also standard.

However, the core contribution—the successful derivation of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem—is novel and non-trivial. Previous work on error bounds for problems like LASSO relied on separability properties that do not apply to the trace norm, as singular values depend on all matrix entries. The authors overcome this challenge with a clever observation about the structure of the optimal solution set, which allows for a simultaneous singular value decomposition of the solution and the gradient at the optimum (Proposition 4.2). This insight and the subsequent matrix perturbation analysis represent a new and valuable technical contribution.

### Significance
The primary significance of this work is theoretical. It deepens the understanding of a fundamental, widely used algorithm (PGM) by proving a faster convergence rate in practical scenarios where strong convexity does not hold. This is an important step in closing the gap between theory and the empirical performance of first-order methods.

The impact is somewhat limited by the asymptotic nature of the result; the linear convergence is only guaranteed to kick in "close enough" to the optimal set, without a characterization of when that occurs. This limits the result's direct utility for designing practical algorithms or stopping criteria. Nonetheless, the theoretical insight is valuable. Furthermore, the analytical techniques developed to prove the error bound, particularly the method for handling the spectral structure of matrices near the solution set, may be of independent interest and could potentially be adapted to analyze other matrix optimization problems.

### Summary and Recommendation
This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution by establishing the asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularization without assuming strong convexity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   It addresses an important, open theoretical question regarding the convergence rate of a popular optimization algorithm.
*   The main technical result, a new local error bound for trace norm regularization, is novel, non-trivial, and relies on an interesting new analytical technique.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a clear and rigorous analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The overall proof strategy builds on a well-known framework, which may make the high-level contribution appear incremental.
*   The linear convergence result is asymptotic, which limits its direct practical impact.

Overall, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. The novel technical machinery developed to establish the error bound is a significant contribution to the optimization and machine learning theory literature. I recommend acceptance.
