This paper establishes linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace-norm–regularized problems under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss. This is an important contribution for many machine learning and signal processing applications. The paper is carefully written and, to my understanding, the main proof is correct. It closely follows Tseng’s Lipschitzian error bound framework [20], addressing the nuclear norm case that had remained open.

What I find missing is a clear comparison with the vector case of l1-norm regularization (e.g., [20, 22] and related work). It would help readers to:
- Explicitly relate your assumptions, error bounds, and proof steps to the l1 literature.
- Clarify why standard l1 techniques do not immediately carry over to the trace-norm setting (or show how they do).
- Highlight that the l1 case should emerge as a corollary of your analysis, and state this explicitly if true.

This context will help assess novelty. Some readers may view a trace-norm analogue of the l1 result as unsurprising; nevertheless, I still consider the result valuable and practically relevant.

Regarding the title, I suggest a slight revision. The main new contribution appears to concern the loss side (via the linear operator A) more than any added “structure” in the regularizer. Since the flexibility arises in the loss rather than in the trace norm, describing the regularization as “structured” may be unnecessary.

Minor comments:
- Notation: Consider calling P a “mask” operator instead of a “sampling” operator, as “sampling” can suggest randomness while P is fixed here.
- Line 337: There appears to be a broken reference; please fix.
- The meanings of Q-linear and R-linear convergence may not be widely known. A brief reminder of these definitions would improve clarity.
- The residual R(X) defined in (6) could be better connected to Tseng’s original definition (46) in [20] to aid interpretation.
- Section 3, Lemma 3.2: Given the length and technicality of this part, please add more high-level discussion, intuition, and a concise summary of what is essential for the main result.
