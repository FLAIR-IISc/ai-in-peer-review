Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proves an asymptotic linear convergence rate for the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace/nuclear norm–regularized problems of the form minX f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X) = h(A(X)). The assumptions on h are weaker than strong convexity of f: h has a Lipschitz gradient on its open domain and is strongly convex on any compact convex subset, while A is an arbitrary linear map. This setting covers many loss functions used in machine learning (e.g., squared and logistic losses), even when f itself is not strongly convex (e.g., A has a nontrivial nullspace).

The main technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound that controls the distance to the optimal solution set by the residual norm ‖proxτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. Combining this bound with the Luo–Tseng framework yields linear convergence of PGM with fixed steps in (0, 1/Lf). Prior error bounds and linear convergence results existed for composite problems with polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1 and group LASSO) but, to the best of my knowledge, such a bound was open for trace norm regularization due to its nonpolyhedral epigraph and the global coupling induced by singular values. The paper overcomes this via a spectral analysis showing X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors at optimality, enabling a delicate perturbation argument.

The paper also includes numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification illustrating linear convergence once iterates are sufficiently close to optimality.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and timely question: can we obtain linear convergence of first-order methods for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of f?
- The main theorem (a Lipschitzian error bound) appears novel and technically nontrivial, resolving an open question by extending error-bound techniques beyond polyhedral regularizers.
- The convergence argument is clean: once the error bound is established, the Luo–Tseng framework applies directly.
- The paper is generally careful and clear, and the proofs (to the extent I could check) are sound.
- The experiments, while modest, are consistent with the theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The paper would benefit from a clearer comparison with the ℓ1-regularized vector case (e.g., Tseng and subsequent work). Please explain concretely which parts of the ℓ1 analysis fail to carry over (polyhedrality, separability, group-wise decoupling), and where your argument departs (the simultaneous SVD and perturbation control). It would also help to state explicitly whether the ℓ1 case follows as a corollary of your framework.
- Some readers may find the result unsurprising given the ℓ1 analogs; framing the contribution as resolving a specific obstacle (nonpolyhedral epigraph, lack of variable splitting) would underscore the novelty.
- Title suggestion: if the word “structured” appears in the title or abstract in earlier drafts, consider removing it. The new flexibility is chiefly on the loss side via A and h, not in making the trace norm “structured.” The current title (without “structured”) is appropriate.
- The paper is proof-heavy around the error bound. A bit more high-level discussion before the technical core (what must be shown; why straightforward ℓ1 arguments fail; the key role of Proposition 4.2) would improve readability.

Minor issues:
- Notation: If P denotes a fixed masking operator in matrix completion, consider calling it a mask or projection operator rather than “sampling,” which can suggest additional randomness.
- Line 337: broken reference (please fix).
- The notions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence are not universally known; you do define them, but repeating the standard definitions would aid readability.
- The residual map R(X) in Eq. (7) could be related more explicitly to Tseng’s original residual (e.g., his Eq. (46)) to aid interpretation and connect to prior analyses.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves an open problem by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization and deriving linear convergence without strong convexity of f.
- Broad applicability to important ML problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification).
- Technically solid and well presented.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited discussion of the relationship to the ℓ1 case may make the contribution appear incremental to some readers.
- Empirical section is brief and primarily illustrative.
