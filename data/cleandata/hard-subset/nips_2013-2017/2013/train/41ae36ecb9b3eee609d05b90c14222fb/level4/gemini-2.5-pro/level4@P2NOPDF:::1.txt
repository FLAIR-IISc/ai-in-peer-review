### **Review**

**Summary**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized minimization problems. The authors prove that the algorithm achieves a linear convergence rate under the condition that the smooth part of the objective, f(X) = h(A(X)), is strongly convex on any compact set and has a Lipschitz continuous gradient, where A is a linear operator.

**Assessment**

The paper extends the analytical framework based on the "error-bound condition," previously used by Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22] to establish linear convergence for lasso and group lasso problems, to the trace-norm regularization setting.

**Strengths:**
The primary strength of this work is the successful extension of the linear convergence proof for the proximal gradient method to trace-norm regularized problems. This is a non-trivial theoretical result, as the trace norm introduces distinct technical challenges compared to vector norms. The proofs are generally well-presented and follow a logical structure.

**Weaknesses:**
The main weakness is that the contribution feels primarily technical and incremental. While the extension is valuable, it largely adapts an existing analytical template to a new problem class without offering significant new conceptual insights into the optimization process itself. Furthermore, the paper lacks empirical validation to complement the theoretical analysis. The claims would be much more compelling if supported by numerical evidence.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarifying the Contribution:** The outlines of the proofs for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 appear to follow the structure of those in Tseng [20] very closely. To better highlight the novelty of this work, the authors should provide a more precise discussion of how their analysis differs from the original. Specifically, they should detail the unique challenges posed by the trace norm and explain the new technical steps required to overcome them. This would help readers more clearly appreciate the paper's specific contribution.

2.  **Numerical Experiments:** The theoretical claim of linear convergence should be substantiated with numerical examples. I would like to see a semilog plot of the objective function value versus the number of iterations to visually confirm the linear convergence rate. It would be particularly insightful to include a simulation where the loss function is not globally strongly convex, but the conditions for linear convergence are met. Demonstrating the algorithm's performance for different levels of problem stability (e.g., as determined by the error-bound condition) would further strengthen the paper. Importantly, a discussion of scenarios where the theory is expected to fail would provide a valuable understanding of the analysis's limitations.

3.  **Dependence of Constants:** The analysis introduces constants κ1, ..., κ4. It is currently unclear how these constants depend on the choice of the sublevel set parameters, α_underline and α_bar. The authors should explicitly clarify this relationship to make the theoretical results more transparent.

4.  **Discussion on Extensions:** The paper would benefit from a discussion on how this analytical approach could be extended to other relevant algorithms, such as the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which is also widely used for this class of problems.

**Minor Issue:**

*   There appears to be a minor error in the sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13). The presentation is confusing. The final inequality is a direct consequence of the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient optimality condition (namely, that -G_bar ∈ τ∂||X||*), rather than the intermediate inequality shown. This point is clarified in Tseng [20] (p. 289), and correcting this step would improve the rigor of the proof.
