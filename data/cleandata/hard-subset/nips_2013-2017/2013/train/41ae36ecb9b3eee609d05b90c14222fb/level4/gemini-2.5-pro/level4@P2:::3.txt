This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems. It is well-established that PGM achieves a sublinear convergence rate for general smooth convex loss functions, which improves to a linear rate if the loss function is also strongly convex. This work aims to bridge this gap by demonstrating that for a broad class of loss functions common in machine learning (e.g., square loss, logistic loss), PGM converges at an asymptotic linear rate even without the strong convexity assumption on the overall loss function.

The authors focus on problems where the loss function `f(X)` has the structure `h(A(X))`, with `h` being strictly convex but the linear operator `A` potentially having a non-trivial nullspace, thus making `f` non-strongly convex. The core of their analysis rests on establishing a new local Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This error bound relates the distance from a given point to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map. By leveraging this bound within the established analytical framework of Luo and Tseng (1993), the paper proves the R-linear convergence of the PGM iterates and the Q-linear convergence of the objective values. A key technical insight in the proof of the error bound involves a structural property of the optimal solutions, which allows for a simultaneous singular value decomposition of the solution and its corresponding gradient.

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper appears to be technically sound and presents a complete piece of theoretical work. The claims are well-supported by a detailed proof structure. The main technical contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, is non-trivial and addresses difficulties not present in analogous proofs for `l1`-norm or group LASSO regularization. The core difficulty stems from the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph and the fact that singular values depend on all entries of the matrix, precluding simple variable-splitting arguments. The authors' approach to overcoming this via matrix perturbation theory and structural properties of the gradient at optimality is innovative.

A minor point for improvement concerns the derivation of the linear convergence rate from the error bound. The proof of Theorem 3.2 relies on the inequality `||R(X^k)||_F ≤ κ_3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F` (Eq. 11). This is a known property of the proximal gradient update, but the proof in the supplementary material could be strengthened by citing existing literature where this or a very similar result is formally established. For example, Lemma 4 of Sra (NIPS 2012, "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting") provides a similar bound. Including such a citation would make the derivation more rigorous and properly attribute this standard step.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and organized, and I was able to follow the main arguments without significant difficulty. The logical flow is clear, moving from the problem statement to the main results and then to the proof of the central error bound.

For improved readability, the authors might consider restructuring the presentation of the main results. The proof of linear convergence (Theorem 3.2) is a relatively straightforward application of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) using the Luo-Tseng framework. In contrast, the proof of the error bound itself (Section 4) is highly technical and dense. It may improve the reader's experience to present the proof of Theorem 3.2 immediately after stating Theorem 3.1, thereby completing the main narrative arc of the paper. The more involved proof of the error bound could then be presented in a subsequent section, allowing readers to first appreciate its implications before delving into its intricate derivation.

### Originality

The originality of this work is moderate but contains a key novel element. The overall strategy of using a local error bound to prove linear convergence for non-strongly convex problems follows a well-established line of research (e.g., Luo and Tseng, Tseng and Yun). In that sense, the high-level framework is not new.

However, the primary contribution—extending this framework to trace norm regularization—is original and significant. As the authors correctly point out, this extension is not straightforward. The crucial observation in Proposition 4.2 regarding the shared singular vectors of the optimal solution `X` and the gradient term `-∇f(X)` appears to be a new and powerful insight. This is the key that unlocks the proof of the error bound, effectively allowing the matrix problem to be analyzed in a manner analogous to separable vector problems. While the other steps in the analysis are more standard, this specific contribution is novel and important.

### Significance

The paper's significance is primarily theoretical. It provides a valuable extension to the convergence theory of first-order methods, showing that the empirically observed fast convergence of PGM on certain trace norm problems has a firm theoretical basis in linear convergence, even without strong convexity. This advances our understanding of why PGM works so well in practice for applications like matrix completion. The error bound itself may also be of independent interest to researchers in optimization theory.

The practical significance is somewhat limited by the asymptotic nature of the result. The linear convergence is only guaranteed locally, once the iterates are "close enough" to the optimal set. The analysis does not provide a characterization of this region, so it does not directly lead to new algorithmic designs or improved stopping criteria in the pre-asymptotic regime. Nonetheless, confirming that a widely used algorithm possesses this desirable property for a broad and practical class of problems is a solid contribution to the field.
