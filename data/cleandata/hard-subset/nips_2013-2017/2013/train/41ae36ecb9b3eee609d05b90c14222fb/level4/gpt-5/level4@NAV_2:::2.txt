The paper establishes linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace-norm–regularized problems under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss, addressing an important question for many machine learning and signal processing applications.

The manuscript is careful and clearly presented. It builds on the Lipschitzian error-bound framework of Tseng and collaborators, and its extension to the nuclear norm setting had remained open. To the best of my understanding, the core proof components are sound.

What I find missing is a more explicit comparison/analogy with the vector ℓ1-regularized case (e.g., [20,22] and related work). It would help to articulate the relationship to those results and techniques, and to clarify precisely why the ℓ1 arguments do not immediately carry over to the trace norm. Conversely, one might also point out that the ℓ1 case should now follow as a corollary of the present analysis. From an evaluation perspective, this context matters, since some readers may view a trace-norm analogue of the ℓ1 result as somewhat expected (though I still view the contribution as valuable).

Separately, I suggest a modest adjustment to the title: the main new generality comes from the structure of the loss via the linear operator A, rather than from additional structure in the regularizer itself. In this sense, “structured” may be unnecessary or potentially misleading here.

Minor issues:
- Notation: Consider calling P a “mask” operator rather than “sampling,” since P is fixed (sampling often suggests randomness).
- l.337: broken reference.
- The meanings of “Q-” and “R-” linear convergence are not universally familiar; briefly restating the definitions would improve readability.
- It would aid interpretation to connect the residual map R(X) defined in (6) to the original definition (46) in [20].

Overall, I recommend adding a bit more discussion and takeaways to balance the long technical proof (in particular the proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3).
