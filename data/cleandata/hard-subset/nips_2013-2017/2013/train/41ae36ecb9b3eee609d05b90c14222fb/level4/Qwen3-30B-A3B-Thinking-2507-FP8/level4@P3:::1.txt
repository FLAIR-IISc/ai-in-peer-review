

This paper investigates the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems, extending prior work on linear convergence rates for first-order methods. The authors establish that for problems of the form min_X {h(A(X)) + τ‖X‖*}, where A is a linear operator, h is strongly convex with Lipschitz gradient, and τ > 0 is a regularization parameter, the PGM converges linearly without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves an open question about whether such bounds hold for trace norm (unlike ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization).

The paper builds on the error-bound framework established by Luo and Tseng [10] and extends the linear convergence results of Tseng and Yun [22, 25] from ℓ1-norm and group LASSO regularization to trace norm regularization. This is a non-trivial extension, as the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph and cannot be analyzed using the same variable-splitting approach as ℓ1-norm regularization. The authors' key insight is that optimal solutions share singular vectors with -∇f(X̄), enabling them to use matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound.

Strengths:
- The paper provides a technically sound proof of linear convergence for a broader class of loss functions than previously known, addressing an important open question in the literature.
- The theoretical results are well-supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems, showing linear convergence in practice.
- The paper is clearly written with well-structured arguments, though some proofs could benefit from more explicit connections to prior work.
- The error bound result is potentially of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization.

Weaknesses:
- The contribution is primarily technical rather than conceptual, extending existing error-bound frameworks to a new setting without introducing fundamentally new theoretical insights. The authors should more precisely distinguish their contribution from Tseng's prior work on error bounds for ℓ1 and group LASSO regularization.
- The paper lacks a specific numerical example demonstrating linear convergence (e.g., a semilog plot of objective value versus iterations), which would strengthen the empirical validation. The authors' recent feedback request for such a simulation is well-founded.
- The dependence of constants κ1, ..., κ4 on the step size parameters α and ᾱ is not explicitly analyzed, making it difficult to understand the practical implications of the theoretical results.
- The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) contains a confusing sequence of inequalities before equation (13), which appears to misattribute the reasoning to the intermediate inequality rather than the convexity of the trace norm and the optimality condition.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The error bound and convergence analysis are rigorous, though the numerical experiments could better demonstrate the linear convergence behavior. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their results.

Clarity: The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and logical flow. However, the connection between the current work and prior results by Tseng should be more explicitly articulated to better position the contribution.

Originality: While the extension to trace norm regularization is non-trivial, the approach follows the same error-bound framework used for ℓ1 and group LASSO, making the contribution more incremental than groundbreaking. The paper should more clearly articulate how this differs from existing work in the literature.

Significance: The results have important implications for machine learning applications involving low-rank matrix recovery, where trace norm regularization is commonly used. However, the paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of how these results advance the state of the art beyond previous work on linear convergence rates.

Pro/con acceptance:
- Pro: Provides a valuable theoretical result for a broad class of machine learning problems; technically sound proof; clear presentation of key ideas.
- Con: Contribution is primarily technical rather than conceptual; lacks specific numerical demonstration of linear convergence; insufficient distinction from prior work.

Recommendation: I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable technical contribution to the optimization literature, but the authors should:
1. Add a semilog plot showing linear convergence for a specific problem instance where the loss is not strongly convex (as requested in the authors' feedback).
2. More precisely distinguish their contribution from prior work by Tseng and Yun, explicitly referencing their methodology and highlighting the novel technical challenges in adapting it to trace norm.
3. Clarify the dependence of the convergence constants on the step size parameters.
4. Correct the confusing proof sequence in the derivation of inequality (13).

The paper's main contribution is the resolution of an open question about error bounds for trace norm regularization, which is a significant technical step. However, the authors should more explicitly address when their theory fails (e.g., when the loss function does not satisfy the required assumptions) and how their approach might extend to other algorithms like ADMM. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the literature.
