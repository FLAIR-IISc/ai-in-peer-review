

This paper investigates the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized optimization problems. The authors establish that PGM achieves linear convergence for a broad class of loss functions without requiring strong convexity of the loss function—a significant relaxation compared to prior work. Their key contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm-regularized problems, which enables the application of the Luo-Tseng framework for proving linear convergence. This work extends previous results that required strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011) to more practical settings where strong convexity may not hold, such as matrix completion with non-injective measurement operators.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The proof of the error bound is thorough, though the argument for the inequality |R(X^k)| ≤ κ₃|X^k - X^{k+1}|_F in the appendix (line 085) requires clearer justification. The authors should explicitly explain how the choice of κ₃ (with α < 1) leads to this bound. The numerical experiments in Sections 5.1 and 5.2 provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims, demonstrating linear convergence in both matrix completion and classification tasks.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though the technical depth of the error bound proof might challenge some readers. The authors could improve accessibility by presenting the main results and their implications earlier in the paper, before delving into the detailed proof. The connection between the error bound and linear convergence could also be made more explicit for readers less familiar with optimization theory.

Originality: The paper's primary novelty lies in the observation that optimal solutions and their gradients share singular vector structures (Proposition 4.2), enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices. This insight is new and crucial for establishing the error bound. While the overall framework follows the established Luo-Tseng approach for error bound analysis, the specific application to trace norm regularization represents a meaningful extension, as prior work on error bounds for composite minimization focused on polyhedral regularizers like ℓ₁-norm (e.g., Tseng, Math. Program. 2009).

Significance: The theoretical contribution is significant as it extends linear convergence guarantees of PGM to a wider class of loss functions relevant to machine learning applications. However, the asymptotic nature of the convergence result (which only holds when iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal solution set) somewhat limits practical impact. The paper does not provide guidance on estimating the "sufficiently close" threshold or initialization strategies to enter the linear convergence regime. Despite this limitation, the technique for establishing error bounds for trace norm regularization could prove valuable for future work on matrix optimization.

Pro:
- Establishes linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization without strong convexity
- Provides a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm-regularized problems
- Numerical experiments robustly support theoretical claims
- The simultaneous diagonalization observation is novel and insightful

Con:
- The convergence result is asymptotic (only holds for iterates sufficiently close to optimal set)
- The connection to practical implementation requires more development
- The approach follows an established framework rather than introducing a fundamentally new method

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that extends our understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. While the result is somewhat incremental (following the Luo-Tseng framework), the specific application to trace norm with the new diagonalization insight makes it a meaningful advance. The paper would benefit from more discussion of practical implementation considerations, particularly regarding initialization strategies to reach the linear convergence regime. Given its theoretical importance and rigorous analysis, I recommend acceptance.
