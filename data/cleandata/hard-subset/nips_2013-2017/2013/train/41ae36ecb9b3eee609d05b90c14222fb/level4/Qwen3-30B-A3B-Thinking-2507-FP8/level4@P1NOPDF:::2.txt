

The paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularized problems under weaker assumptions than strong convexity of the loss function—a critical advance for machine learning and signal processing applications. The writing is clear and well-structured, and the proof largely follows Tseng's Lipschitz error bound framework [20], resolving an open question regarding its application to nuclear norm regularization. The core proof appears sound based on my review.

A significant gap, however, is the absence of a clear comparison to the well-studied vector case of $\ell_1$-norm regularization (e.g., [20, 22]). The authors should explicitly relate their techniques to those used in the $\ell_1$ setting, explaining why direct translation of $\ell_1$-based arguments fails for trace norm. Crucially, the $\ell_1$-norm case should be recoverable as a special case of the trace norm framework (not merely a corollary), and this connection would strengthen the paper's contribution. Without it, some readers may view the extension as unsurprising, despite its technical value.

Regarding the title, "Structured Trace Norm Regularization" is misleading. The primary novelty lies in relaxing assumptions on the *loss function* (via the linear operator $A$), not in the regularization structure itself. A more accurate title would emphasize the weakened loss conditions, e.g., "Linear Convergence of Proximal Gradient for Trace Norm Regularization under Weaker Loss Assumptions."

Minor issues require attention:
- Notation: $P$ is termed a "sampling operator," but as it is fixed (not random), "mask operator" is preferable.
- Line 337: Broken reference.
- Definitions of "Q-linear" and "R-linear" convergence (Section 2) should be briefly reiterated for clarity.
- The residual $R(X)$ (Equation 6) should be explicitly linked to Tseng's original definition (Equation 46, [20]) for better interpretation.
- The proof of Lemma 3.2 (Section 3) is technically dense; a brief concluding remark summarizing its significance would aid readability.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution. Addressing the $\ell_1$ comparison and title issue would substantially enhance its impact and accessibility. The technical content is robust, and the writing is generally strong—these are refinements, not fundamental flaws.
