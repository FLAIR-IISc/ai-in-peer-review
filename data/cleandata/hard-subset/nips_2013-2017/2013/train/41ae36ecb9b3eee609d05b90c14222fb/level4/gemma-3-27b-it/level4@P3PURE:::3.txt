For trace norm regularized risk minimization, a popular method is the proximal gradient algorithm. Its sublinear global convergence rate is well-understood, and a linear rate is established when the loss function is strongly convex. This paper relaxes the strong convexity assumption, building upon the framework introduced by Luo and Tseng. Specifically, the authors prove a local error bound that enables them to demonstrate that the proximal gradient algorithm converges asymptotically at a linear rate for trace norm regularization. The paper largely follows a standard approach to proving the local error bound, featuring a novel observation allowing for simultaneous diagonalization of matrices.

Quality:
This paper appears to be technically sound. I have one remaining question regarding the appendix, line 085. Please clarify why the chosen value of kappa3 (specifically, alpha < 1) leads to the claimed inequality R(X^k) <= kappa3 |X^k - X^{k+1}|_F ? 
(Note: The proof for |Rk| <= kappa |Xk - X_{k+1}| has been explicitly established in "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting," Suvrit Sra, NIPS’12 – Lemma 4.)

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and readily understandable. However, to enhance clarity, it would be beneficial to first present the main claims and subsequently delve into the detailed proofs. In particular, after stating the local error bound, the authors could postpone the technical proof and instead immediately focus on demonstrating the linear convergence rate, which, incidentally, does not introduce significant novelty.

Originality:
The originality of this work is moderate. The observation facilitating simultaneous matrix diagonalization is a new and interesting contribution. Beyond this, the authors largely adhere to existing techniques for establishing the local error bound, and the remaining steps are not novel.

Significance:
The significance of this paper is primarily theoretical, with limited impact on algorithm design. While extending the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm to more practical scenarios – those with a relaxed form of strong convexity – is valuable, the asymptotic nature of the proof (and claim) diminishes this impact. The authors’ approach to matrix diagonalization may be of independent interest. In summary, this work demonstrates that the proximal gradient algorithm applied to trace norm regularized risk minimization converges asymptotically at a linear rate under a relaxed form of strong convexity. The authors largely follow a well-established framework by Luo and Tseng, with a novel observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, effectively reducing the problem to the vector case. Overall, this work is incremental but moderately interesting from a theoretical perspective.
