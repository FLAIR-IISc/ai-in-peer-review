This paper proves linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace-norm–regularized problems under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss. This addresses an important need in many machine learning and signal processing applications. The paper is carefully written and, to my reading, the main part of the proof is correct. It closely follows Tseng’s Lipschitzian error-bound technique [20], whose application to the nuclear norm had remained an open question.

What I miss is a clear comparison or analogy to the vector case with ℓ1-norm regularization, as studied in [20, 22] and related work. It would be very helpful to:
- Explicitly relate your results and proof techniques to those used for the ℓ1 case, and
- Explain why the ℓ1 techniques do not directly carry over to the trace norm, or in what sense they need to be modified.
Given your generality, the ℓ1 result should now follow as a corollary of the trace-norm analysis; making this connection explicit would strengthen the paper. In evaluating the contribution, this context matters: some readers may not find it surprising that an ℓ1 result extends to the trace norm, although I still view the extension and the technical resolution as valuable.

Title suggestion: consider revising the title. The main new contribution appears to relax assumptions on the loss (via the flexibility introduced by the linear operator A), rather than introducing additional “structure” in the trace-norm regularizer itself. Since the flexibility comes from the loss side, “structured” may be unnecessary or potentially misleading.

Minor issues:
- Notation: consider calling P a “mask” operator rather than a “sampling” operator, since P is fixed and “sampling” can suggest randomness.
- Line 337: broken reference.
- The meanings of Q-linear and R-linear convergence are not universally known; a brief reminder would aid readability.
- The residual R(X) defined in (6) could be better motivated by explicitly relating it to Tseng’s original definition (Eq. (46) in [20]) to help with interpretation.
- To balance the long technical proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3, I recommend adding more discussion and high-level takeaways.
