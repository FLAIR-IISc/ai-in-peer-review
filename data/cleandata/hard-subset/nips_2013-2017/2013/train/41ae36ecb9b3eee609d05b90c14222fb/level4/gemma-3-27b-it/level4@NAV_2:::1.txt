This paper proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method applied to trace-norm regularized learning problems when the loss function has the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strongly convex on any compact set and has a Lipschitz gradient. This work extends prior results—particularly those of Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22] which established similar linear convergence guarantees under an “error-bound condition” for lasso and group lasso regularization—to the trace norm setting. The contribution is primarily technical, representing a non-trivial extension of existing theory. 

The presentation of the proofs is generally clear and follows a logical structure.

**Strengths:**

- Establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, extending existing results to trace norm regularization.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon established frameworks for convergence analysis of first-order methods.

**Weaknesses:**

- The contribution is predominantly technical; the practical implications of this theoretical result could be highlighted more effectively.
- The paper lacks a compelling numerical demonstration of the linear convergence rate. While the theoretical result is established, empirical validation is crucial for demonstrating its relevance in practical scenarios.
- The discussion regarding the dependence of the convergence rate constants (κ1, κ2, κ3, κ4) on the step size parameters (α and ᾱ) is limited. More detail on this relationship would be beneficial.

**More Detailed Comments:**

1. The proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 4.1 closely resemble those found in Tseng [20]. The authors should provide more precise citations and emphasize the novel aspects of their derivation to better highlight their contribution. A clearer articulation of how the trace norm differs from, and extends, the analysis used for lasso and group lasso would be valuable.
2. A numerical example demonstrating the observed linear convergence rate is strongly recommended. A semilog plot of the objective function value versus the number of iterations, especially for a non-strongly convex loss, would significantly strengthen the paper. Exploring cases with varying levels of stability (e.g., different condition numbers) would be particularly informative.
3. Providing greater insight into how the constants κ1 through κ4 depend on the step size parameters α and ᾱ would enhance the practical applicability of the results.
4. The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat unclear. The final inequality in that sequence should be justified by convexity of the trace norm and the fact that -∇f(X̄) ∈ τ∂‖X̄‖∗, rather than relying on the intermediate inequality presented (as pointed out on p. 289 of Tseng [20]).

**Potential Extensions:**

It would be valuable to discuss the potential for extending this analysis to the ADMM algorithm, which is widely used for solving trace norm regularized problems. Investigating the conditions under which ADMM exhibits linear convergence under similar assumptions would be a logical next step.
