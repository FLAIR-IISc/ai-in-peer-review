Summary:
The paper studies proximal gradient methods for trace-norm regularized risk minimization. While global sublinear rates are standard and linear rates are known under strong convexity of the loss, this work relaxes strong convexity by leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework. The authors prove a local error bound that yields asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm in the trace-norm setting. The main technical novelty is an observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of certain matrices, effectively reducing parts of the argument to the vector case.

Quality:
The paper appears technically sound. The development follows a well-established blueprint: establish a local error bound, then combine it with standard proximal-gradient descent properties to derive linear convergence. The novel diagonalization step seems correct and useful for simplifying the analysis in the matrix setting.

One issue I flagged concerns the appendix (line 085 in the current draft): why a specific choice of κ3 (e.g., with α < 1) implies R(X^k) ≤ κ3 ||X^k − X^{k+1}||_F. After reading the rebuttal and cross-checking Sra (NIPS’12), Lemma 4, I am satisfied that this inequality holds under the stated conditions; please cite that lemma directly in the final version and clearly state the assumptions under which the bound applies.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable despite significant technical detail. The exposition would benefit from a clearer roadmap:
- State the main claims (local error bound and asymptotic linear rate) upfront, including precise assumptions and constants where possible.
- After presenting the local error bound, defer its technical proof to a later section or the appendix. Proceed directly to show how the bound yields linear convergence for proximal gradient. This would improve flow, given that the linear-rate derivation itself is fairly standard.
- When using the diagonalization argument, briefly summarize the key algebraic fact and its scope before diving into details.

It would also help to standardize notation for the residual R(X^k) and to provide a concise definition when it first appears, along with a short discussion of its role in the Luo–Tseng framework.

Originality:
Originality is moderate. The simultaneous diagonalization insight is interesting and seems new in this specific context, and it streamlines the matrix analysis. Beyond that, the structure of the proof and the use of local error bounds follow established methods, and the derivation of linear convergence from the error bound is standard.

Significance:
The contribution is primarily theoretical. It extends linear convergence guarantees of proximal gradient to a setting with a relaxed strong convexity assumption for trace-norm regularization. This is valuable for understanding algorithmic behavior in practice, where strict strong convexity may not hold. However, the impact is limited by the asymptotic nature of the result and by the lack of new algorithmic ideas or practical acceleration techniques. The diagonalization approach could be of independent interest and potentially useful in related matrix optimization problems.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clearly state the precise conditions and neighborhood for the local error bound (and how constants depend on problem parameters), to help readers apply the result.
- Cite Sra (NIPS’12, Lemma 4) where the residual-to-iterate-difference inequality is used, and summarize the assumptions ensuring its validity.
- Reorganize the presentation so that the main results and their implications are accessible before the technical proofs.
- If space permits, include a small empirical illustration indicating when the linear regime sets in, to complement the asymptotic theory.

Overall assessment:
A solid, incremental theoretical contribution. The paper establishes asymptotic linear convergence of proximal gradient for trace-norm regularization under a relaxed form of strong convexity, using the Luo–Tseng framework and a useful diagonalization observation. While the novelty beyond this observation is limited and the result is asymptotic, the work is technically sound and of moderate interest to the theory community.
