This paper proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace norm regularization problems. Specifically, it considers objective functions of the form F(X) = h(A(X)) + τ||X||*, where h is strongly convex on compact sets and has a Lipschitz continuous gradient. A key feature of this setup is that the overall loss function, f(X) = h(A(X)), is not necessarily strongly convex. The central theoretical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for this problem class. This work extends the error-bound-based convergence analysis framework, previously applied to lasso and group lasso problems, to the more complex setting of trace norm regularization. The presentation of the proofs is generally clear.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its significant theoretical result. The authors prove, for the first time, that PGM can achieve a linear convergence rate for trace norm regularized problems without requiring the standard assumption of strong convexity on the loss function. This is highly relevant for many machine learning applications, such as matrix completion or multi-task learning, where commonly used loss functions (e.g., square loss with an undersampled operator) lack strong convexity. The derivation of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm objective is a key technical achievement and may be of independent interest to the optimization community. The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the theoretical contribution is solid, its presentation and impact could be further strengthened.

*   **Positioning and Originality:** The contribution, while non-trivial, is a technical extension of an existing analysis framework. The proof strategy largely follows the template established by Luo and Tseng for ℓ1-norm and group lasso regularizers. To better highlight the novelty, the authors should be more explicit in detailing how their analysis overcomes the unique challenges posed by the trace norm's non-polyhedral and non-separable structure. Clarifying precisely where the proof departs from prior work would help the reader better appreciate the specific technical innovations required for this extension.

*   **Clarity of Analysis:** Some details in the analysis could be clarified. In the proof of Theorem 3.2, the dependence of the constants κ1 through κ4 on the step size bounds is not specified in the main text. A brief explanation of this relationship would be helpful for completeness. Additionally, the derivation leading to inequality (18) could be presented more clearly. A more direct justification based on the subgradient inequality for the trace norm might improve readability.

*   **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments provide a welcome validation of the theory. To make them more compelling, the authors could expand their discussion. It would be beneficial to explicitly connect the experimental setups (e.g., matrix completion where the operator has a non-trivial nullspace, leading to a non-strongly convex loss) to the theoretical conditions. Furthermore, a discussion on the practical boundaries of the theory would be valuable. For instance, are there conditions under which the error bound is weak or fails to hold, leading to sublinear convergence in practice? An experiment or discussion illustrating such a "failure case" would provide a more complete picture of the result's scope.

*   **Future Directions:** The paper's conclusion mentions extending the work to more general matrix norms. A more specific and potentially impactful direction would be to discuss how this error bound analysis could be applied to other popular optimization algorithms for this problem class, such as the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM).

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and non-trivial extension of the error-bound-based linear convergence analysis to trace norm regularization. The main weakness is its purely technical nature. Improving the clarity of the novel contributions and strengthening the experimental discussion would further enhance the paper's impact.
