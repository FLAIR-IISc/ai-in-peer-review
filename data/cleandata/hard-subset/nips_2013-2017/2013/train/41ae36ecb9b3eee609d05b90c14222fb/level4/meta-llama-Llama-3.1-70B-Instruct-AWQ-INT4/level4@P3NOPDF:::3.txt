**Review**

The authors have provided a proof for the inequality |Rk| <= kappa |Xk - X_{k+1}| in their rebuttal, referencing the work "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting" by Suvrit Sra, NIPS'12 (Lemma 4).

This paper focuses on the convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization. It is well-known that this algorithm has a sublinear global convergence rate, which becomes linear when the loss function is strongly convex. The authors relax the strong convexity assumption by following the framework established by Luo and Tseng. Specifically, they prove a local error bound, allowing them to show that the proximal gradient algorithm converges asymptotically at a linear rate for trace norm regularization.

The paper generally follows the established pattern for proving local error bounds, with a novel observation that enables simultaneous diagonalization of matrices. The authors' approach is technically sound, and the paper is well-organized.

However, I did identify a gap in the appendix (line 085). The authors should provide a clear explanation for why their choice of kappa3 (with alpha < 1) leads to the claimed inequality R(X^k) <= kappa3 |X^k - X^{k+1}|_F.

The paper is generally clear, but its technical nature may make it challenging for some readers to follow. To improve clarity, the authors could consider presenting the main claims first and then delving into the technical details. After stating the local error bound, they might postpone the proof and instead focus on demonstrating the linear convergence rate, which is relatively straightforward.

The originality of this paper is moderate. The observation that allows simultaneous diagonalization of matrices is new and interesting, but the authors largely follow existing approaches to establish the local error bound. The remaining steps are not particularly innovative.

The significance of this paper lies primarily in its theoretical contributions, with limited impact on algorithm design. While it is valuable to extend the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm to more practical scenarios (i.e., relaxed strong convexity), the asymptotic nature of the proof and claim somewhat reduces its significance. The authors' approach to diagonalizing matrices may be of independent interest.

In summary, this work proves that the proximal gradient algorithm converges asymptotically at a linear rate for trace norm regularized risk minimization under a relaxed form of strong convexity. The authors follow a well-established framework with a new observation that enables simultaneous diagonalization of matrices. Overall, this work is incremental and moderately interesting from a theoretical perspective.
