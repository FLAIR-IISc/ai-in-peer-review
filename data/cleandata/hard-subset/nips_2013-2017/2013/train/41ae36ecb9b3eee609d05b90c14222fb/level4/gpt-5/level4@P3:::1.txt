Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the composite optimization problem minX f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where the loss f takes the structured form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A linear and h continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex on its domain, and strongly convex on every convex compact subset of its domain. The main theoretical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm–regularized problems: near the optimal set, the distance to the solution set is bounded by a constant multiple of a natural residual norm ‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, the paper then proves that the proximal gradient method (PGM) enjoys an asymptotic linear rate (R-linear convergence of iterates and Q-linear convergence of objective values) under standard step-size conditions, without requiring strong convexity of f.

This extends error-bound–based linear convergence results previously established for polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1 and (group) LASSO) by Tseng, Tseng and Yun, and Zhang et al., to the nuclear norm. The extension is nontrivial because the nuclear norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph and its value couples all entries of X via singular values. The key new technical ingredient is a spectral alignment property at optimality (the left/right singular vectors of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) coincide), which enables a restricted invertibility argument using matrix perturbation theory.

Quality:
- The main theorems are clearly stated: Theorem 3.1 gives the local error bound; Theorem 3.2 gives asymptotic linear convergence of PGM under 0 < α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf. The proof strategy is sound: establish the error bound, then apply the Luo–Tseng descent and error-bound machinery. The restricted invertibility lemma and the spectral decomposition results (Proposition 4.2 and Proposition 4.3) address the core difficulty unique to nuclear norm regularization.
- Assumptions are reasonable for many ML losses (square, logistic). The analysis is local (asymptotic) rather than global, which is natural for error-bound–based results.
- The experimental section (matrix completion and matrix classification) provides semilog plots consistent with local linear convergence once near the solution set.
- Some details could be clarified. In Theorem 3.2, the constants κ1–κ4 are said to depend on α, ᾱ, and Lf; providing explicit expressions (or pointers to the supplement) would make the result more transparent and easier to reuse. The argument sequence leading to inequality (18) and the inequality chain before (13) would benefit from more precise attribution and a minor correction (see “Detailed comments” below).

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and readable. The motivation for the error bound and the residual map is clear. Notation is mostly consistent.
- Suggestions: define Lf and L (for ∇f and ∇h) once and use consistently; remind the reader early that the convergence is asymptotic; ensure all constants (σ, κ, κi) are defined at first use; keep the link to Luo–Tseng’s framework explicit to guide readers through the proof structure.

Originality:
- The extension of Lipschitzian error bounds from polyhedral regularizers to the nuclear norm is new and technically challenging. The spectral alignment observation and the restricted invertibility argument are insightful and of independent interest. While the convergence framework is standard, the core contribution is not merely a routine adaptation.

Significance:
- Nuclear-norm regularization is central to low-rank learning (completion, multitask, classification). Establishing linear convergence of a widely used first-order method under realistic assumptions is valuable for both theory and practice. The error bound may further enable linear-rate guarantees for related algorithms (e.g., coordinate updates, proximal Newton, and potentially ADMM) under similar structural conditions.

Pros:
- First error-bound and linear convergence result for nuclear-norm–regularized problems without strong convexity of f.
- Solid and careful analysis; resolves a previously open question noted in the literature.
- Experiments qualitatively support the theory’s asymptotic linear rate.

Cons:
- The contribution, while nontrivial, is primarily technical and builds on an existing analysis framework.
- Dependence of constants on algorithmic parameters is opaque.
- Discussion of scope and failure modes of the error bound is brief; broader empirical validation would strengthen the paper.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
1) Precise attribution: The outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Theorem 3.2 closely follow the error-bound framework of Luo–Tseng and the developments for ℓ1/group LASSO by Tseng, Tseng–Yun, and Zhang et al. Please cite the exact lemmas/sections (e.g., Tseng 2009, Section 3; Zhang et al. 2013, Section 6) wherever arguments are adapted, to make the novelty (spectral alignment and restricted invertibility for the nuclear norm) stand out.

2) Numerical validation: The current semilog plots are helpful. Please include an explicit experiment where the loss is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with a non-injective sampling operator) to illustrate local linear convergence, and vary a “stability” parameter (e.g., sampling ratio or condition number) to show how the linear rate constant depends on problem conditioning. Clearly indicate the “asymptotic” regime empirically.

3) Failure modes and scope: Discuss when the error bound may fail or become weak, e.g., if Assumption 2.1(b) is violated (non-smooth or merely convex h), if sublevel sets are not bounded, or if the restricted invertibility condition effectively degenerates (e.g., highly ill-conditioned A on the active subspace). Clarifying whether non-uniqueness of solutions affects the local rate (beyond a larger constant) would also help.

4) Constants κ1–κ4: Provide explicit formulas or tight bounds in terms of α, ᾱ, and Lf (and the error-bound constant). If space is tight, add a concise statement in the main text with a pointer to the supplement.

5) Minor correction: The sequence of inequalities before inequality (13) is confusing. The last inequality follows from convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient inclusion −Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄‖* (see, e.g., Tseng, Math. Program. 125(2): 263–295, p. 289), not from the intermediate inequality. Please revise.

6) Extensions: It would be valuable to discuss whether the same error bound implies linear convergence of other first-order methods:
- Accelerated PG: FISTA is not generally locally linear without restarts; with a local error bound and suitable restart, one can often obtain linear convergence—please comment.
- ADMM: Many recent linear-rate ADMM results rely on error bounds/metric subregularity in convex composite problems. Outline which parts of your proof (the error bound) would carry over to an ADMM splitting of f + τ‖·‖*, and what additional assumptions (e.g., full rank of coupling operators, exact subproblems) are required.

7) Notation/typos: Ensure consistent use of L and Lf; define the residual map R and Sτ at first mention; unify symbols for step sizes; check that all references are complete and correctly formatted.

Overall assessment:
A solid and carefully executed theoretical contribution that fills a gap in the convergence theory of nuclear-norm–regularized learning with first-order methods. While primarily technical, the result is relevant and likely to influence subsequent algorithmic analyses. I recommend acceptance after minor revisions addressing clarity, attribution, constants, and a brief discussion of extensions and failure modes.
