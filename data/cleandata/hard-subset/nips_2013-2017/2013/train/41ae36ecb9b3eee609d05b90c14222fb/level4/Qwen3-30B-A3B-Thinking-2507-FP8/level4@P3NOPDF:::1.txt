

This paper establishes linear convergence for the proximal gradient (PG) method applied to trace-norm regularized learning with loss functions of the form \(f(X) = h(A(X))\), where \(A\) is linear, \(h\) is strongly convex on compact sets, and \(h\) has a Lipschitz gradient. The work extends prior results by Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which proved linear convergence under the error-bound condition for lasso and group lasso, to the trace norm setting. While the extension to trace norm is non-trivial, the contribution appears primarily technical rather than conceptually novel.

**Strengths:**  
The paper successfully generalizes the error-bound-based linear convergence framework to the trace-norm regularized problem, filling a gap in the literature for this important class of low-rank optimization problems. The proofs are generally well-structured and accessible, with clear logical flow in key arguments.

**Weaknesses:**  
1.  **Limited conceptual contribution:** The core technique (error-bound condition) and convergence proof structure are directly adapted from Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22]. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its technical adaptation from the prior work. The authors should explicitly clarify *how* the trace-norm structure alters the error-bound derivation compared to the lasso/group lasso cases (e.g., the role of the dual norm, singular value properties). Merely stating the extension is insufficient; the specific challenges overcome for the trace norm must be highlighted.

2.  **Lack of empirical validation:** The theoretical claims require empirical support. The authors should include a numerical example demonstrating linear convergence (e.g., a semilog plot of \(\|f(X_k) - f(X^*)\|\) versus iteration count \(k\)) for a concrete trace-norm problem. This is essential to validate the theory and illustrate the practical relevance of the convergence rate.

3.  **Ambiguous dependence of constants:** The constants \(\kappa_1, \ldots, \kappa_4\) (critical for the convergence rate) depend on \(\underline{\alpha}\) and \(\overline{\alpha}\). The paper does not specify *how* these constants scale with these parameters or the problem dimensions. This lack of detail hinders practical applicability and understanding of the rate's tightness.

**Minor Issues:**  
4.  The sequence of inequalities preceding (13) is confusing. The final inequality relies on the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient condition \(-\bar{G} \in \tau \partial \|X\|_*\) (as noted in [20, p. 289]), not on the intermediate inequalities. This should be clarified to avoid misinterpretation.

**Added After Authors' Feedback (Critical):**  
The authors must address the following crucial points before acceptance:  
*   **Simulation with non-strongly convex loss:** Provide a simulation where the loss \(h\) is *not* strongly convex (e.g., \(h(z) = \frac{1}{2}(z - b)^2\) for a specific \(b\), or a non-convex loss in a controlled setting), yet the PG algorithm converges linearly *for different stability levels* (e.g., varying step sizes \(\tau\)). **Crucially, discuss explicitly when the theory fails** (e.g., for specific \(\tau\) or problem instances where strong convexity is violated). This is essential to test the robustness of the error-bound approach beyond its stated assumptions.  
*   **Extension to ADMM:** Discuss how the analysis could be extended to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) for trace-norm problems. This is a significant direction for future work, and the paper should outline the key challenges (e.g., handling the augmented Lagrangian, adapting the error-bound condition to the ADMM framework) and potential pathways. The current work focuses solely on PG; mentioning ADMM's relevance and the path to its analysis would strengthen the paper's impact.

**Overall Recommendation:**  
The paper presents a technically sound extension of existing linear convergence results to the trace-norm setting. However, the contribution is primarily incremental, lacking sufficient conceptual novelty or empirical validation to justify publication in NIPS. The critical gaps identified above—particularly the absence of a simulation demonstrating linear convergence *and* the failure case for non-strongly convex losses, plus the need to discuss ADMM extension—must be addressed. Without these, the paper's contribution remains limited to a technical adaptation of known methods. **Major Revision Required.** The authors must provide the requested simulation (including failure analysis), clarify the dependence of constants, improve the technical distinction from prior work, and discuss the ADMM extension. The current draft does not meet the standards for publication in NIPS.
