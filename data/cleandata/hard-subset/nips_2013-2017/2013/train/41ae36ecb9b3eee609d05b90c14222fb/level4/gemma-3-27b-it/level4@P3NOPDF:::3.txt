This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization, relaxing the standard strong convexity assumption typically required for linear convergence. The authors build upon the framework established by Luo and Tseng, proving a local error bound that ultimately demonstrates asymptotically linear convergence under a weaker convexity condition. The core contribution lies in a novel observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, which simplifies the analysis and facilitates the proof.

**Quality:**

The paper appears technically sound and well-motivated. I did, however, identify a point requiring clarification in the appendix (line 85). Specifically, the justification for the choice of κ<sub>3</sub> (with α < 1) leading to the inequality R(X<sup>k</sup>) ≤ κ<sub>3</sub> ||X<sup>k</sup> - X<sup>k+1</sup>||<sub>F</sub> would benefit from further explanation. The authors previously established in Sra, NIPS'12 (Lemma 4) that |R<sub>k</sub>| <= kappa |X<sub>k</sub> - X<sub>k+1</sub>|, and this connection should be explicitly drawn to support the claim in the appendix.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, allowing me to follow the technical details without significant difficulty. However, I suggest restructuring the presentation for improved clarity. Beginning with a clear statement of the main results – specifically, the local error bound and the resulting linear convergence rate – would be beneficial. Subsequently, the authors could present the technical details and proof of the local error bound. Currently, the immediate shift to the technical proof interrupts the flow and obscures the overall impact of the result. Furthermore, the proof of the linear convergence rate itself, while rigorous, largely follows established techniques. Postponing this section until *after* establishing the local error bound would allow the authors to focus on the genuinely novel aspects of their contribution.

**Originality:**

The originality of this work is moderate. The key innovation – the observation enabling simultaneous diagonalization of matrices – is a valuable contribution and is indeed interesting. This diagonalization simplifies the analysis, effectively reducing the problem to a more manageable vector-valued case. However, the remainder of the approach largely adheres to the existing framework of Luo and Tseng for proving local error bounds. Consequently, while the matrix diagonalization is novel, its impact is somewhat contained within a well-established methodology. 

**Significance:**

The significance of this paper is primarily theoretical. While extending the conditions under which linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularization can be guaranteed is a positive step, the practical impact on algorithm design appears limited. The asymptotic nature of the convergence proof and claim further moderates the significance. Nevertheless, the approach to matrix diagonalization may be of independent interest to researchers working in related areas. 

In summary, this work demonstrates that the proximal gradient algorithm applied to trace norm regularized risk minimization converges asymptotically at a linear rate under a relaxed form of strong convexity. The authors effectively leverage a well-established theoretical framework, augmented by a novel observation that simplifies the analysis through simultaneous diagonalization. Overall, this is a solid and incremental contribution with moderate theoretical interest.
