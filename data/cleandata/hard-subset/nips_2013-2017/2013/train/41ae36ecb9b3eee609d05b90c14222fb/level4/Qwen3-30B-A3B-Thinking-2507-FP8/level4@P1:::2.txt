

This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a broad class of trace norm-regularized optimization problems, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors prove a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves an open question regarding whether such bounds exist for trace norm (unlike for ℓ₁-norm or group LASSO regularization). This result is significant because it extends the applicability of linear convergence guarantees to important machine learning problems like matrix completion and classification, where the loss functions (e.g., square loss, logistic loss) are not strongly convex but still satisfy the authors' assumptions.

The paper is technically sound and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The authors carefully build upon the error bound framework developed by Luo and Tseng, while addressing the specific challenges posed by the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph. The proof strategy is well-motivated, with the key insight being that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors, enabling the use of matrix perturbation theory. The main technical contribution—the error bound for trace norm regularization—appears correct and represents a valuable tool that may find independent applications beyond the specific convergence analysis.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the complex technical content accessible. The authors effectively connect their work to prior literature, particularly highlighting how their approach differs from existing error bound techniques for ℓ₁-norm regularization. However, the review would benefit from a more explicit comparison to the vector case of ℓ₁-norm regularization (e.g., as in [20, 22]), explaining why techniques from that domain do not directly translate to the trace norm case. This would strengthen the paper's positioning within the broader context of regularization techniques.

The significance of this work is substantial, as it addresses a crucial gap in the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. Many important machine learning problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.) use loss functions that are not strongly convex yet still yield linear convergence with the PGM under the authors' conditions. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification effectively demonstrate the linear convergence behavior predicted by the theory.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a correct and novel proof of the error bound. The convergence analysis is rigorous and properly contextualized within the existing literature. The experimental results complement the theoretical claims.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though some technical details could be expanded for readers unfamiliar with matrix perturbation theory. The definitions and notation are consistent throughout, though the distinction between Q-linear and R-linear convergence might benefit from a brief explanation for broader accessibility.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the first linear convergence result for the PGM applied to trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity assumptions. The Lipschitzian error bound is novel and addresses an open question in the literature, as the authors correctly note that prior error bounds for composite optimization do not extend to trace norm regularization.

Significance: This work has clear importance for machine learning practitioners, as it provides theoretical justification for the practical success of the PGM in many low-rank matrix problems. The results apply directly to several important applications in machine learning, signal processing, and statistics, making them broadly relevant to the NIPS community.

Strengths:
- Resolves an important open question about error bounds for trace norm regularization
- Establishes linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions
- Provides a technically sound proof with clear connections to prior work
- Includes convincing numerical experiments supporting the theoretical claims
- The Lipschitzian error bound may find independent applications

Weaknesses:
- Could more explicitly compare to the vector ℓ₁-norm case and explain why techniques don't directly translate
- The title ("On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization") slightly misrepresents the main contribution, which is more about the loss function structure than the trace norm itself (the flexibility comes from the linear operator A in the loss function)
- Some technical terms (Q-linear/R-linear convergence) could be briefly defined for broader accessibility
- The connection between the trace norm result and the ℓ₁-norm case as a corollary is not fully developed

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important theoretical gap in optimization for machine learning. While the paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to the ℓ₁-norm case, this does not diminish the significance of the main result. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a valuable theoretical advance with practical implications for many machine learning applications, and the contribution is sufficiently novel and significant for NIPS. The minor weaknesses noted are easily addressable in the camera-ready version and do not undermine the paper's technical merits.
