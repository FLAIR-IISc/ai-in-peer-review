This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard PGM is known to have a sublinear rate of convergence for this class of problems. While linear convergence can be achieved if the smooth part of the objective (the loss function) is strongly convex, this assumption is often too restrictive for practical scenarios. The main contribution of this work is to prove that PGM can achieve a linear convergence rate under a much weaker set of assumptions, specifically when the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function and `A` is a linear operator. The key technical innovation is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized objective, which resolves an open question and may be of independent interest. This result builds upon the analytical framework established by Luo and Tseng for analyzing first-order methods, which had previously been applied to l1-norm and group LASSO regularization but not yet to the trace norm.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution by establishing linear convergence for PGM on an important class of non-strongly convex problems. This result is highly relevant to many machine learning applications where trace norm regularization is used and the loss function lacks strong convexity (e.g., the standard square loss in underdetermined matrix completion). This advances the theoretical understanding of a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The primary technical contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—is novel and non-trivial. The authors correctly point out that existing proof techniques for the l1-norm case, which rely on separability, do not directly translate to the trace norm due to the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. The proof appears technically sound and demonstrates a solid command of convex analysis and matrix perturbation theory. The empirical results, though brief, support the theoretical findings.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is carefully structured and clearly written. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating their contribution within the existing literature, and outlining their proof strategy. The technical arguments, while dense, are presented in a logical and organized manner.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Comparison with l1-Norm Regularization:** While the paper correctly identifies that its proof technique differs from the l1-norm case (due to the non-polyhedral epigraph and the global dependence of singular values), the discussion could be expanded. A more detailed comparison with the analyses for l1-norm regularization (e.g., in Tseng [22, 23]) would be highly beneficial. Specifically, clarifying precisely which parts of the l1-norm proof strategy break down for the trace norm would better highlight the novelty and difficulty of the authors' contribution. This would also help preempt the potential perception that this result is a straightforward extension of the vector case. Given the trace norm is often considered the matrix analogue of the l1-norm, this explicit comparison is crucial for contextualizing the work.

2.  **Balance Between Proof and Discussion:** The main body of the paper is heavily weighted towards the technical proof of the error bound (Lemma 4.1). While this is the core contribution, the paper could be improved by moving some of the more technical derivations to the supplementary material. This would create space for a broader discussion, including the l1-norm comparison mentioned above, and perhaps a more detailed interpretation of the conditions required for the error bound to hold.

**Minor Issues:**

*   For the benefit of a broader audience, it would be helpful to briefly define Q-linear and R-linear convergence in Section 3, rather than assuming the reader is familiar with these terms.
*   The definition of the residual map `R(X)` in equation (7) is central to the analysis. A brief sentence connecting it to the original fixed-point optimality condition in (4) would improve its interpretation for the reader.
*   In the numerical experiments, the operator `P` is described as a "sampling operator." Using "masking operator" might be clearer, as "sampling" can imply randomness in the operator itself at each step, whereas here it is fixed.
*   There appears to be a broken reference on line 337 (this may be a placeholder in the draft).

In conclusion, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The main result is significant, and the technical work appears solid. My primary recommendation is to enhance the paper by providing a more explicit and detailed comparison with the well-studied l1-norm case, which would better underscore the paper's originality and impact. Addressing the minor points would further improve its clarity and accessibility.
