This paper analyzes the convergence rate of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularized risk minimization. While the sublinear global convergence rate of this algorithm is well-established, a linear rate is typically only guaranteed when the loss function is strongly convex. This work extends the analysis to cases without strong convexity by leveraging the error bound framework of Luo and Tseng. The authors establish a local error bound for the trace norm problem, which in turn implies an asymptotic linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient algorithm. The core technical contribution is a novel observation that permits the simultaneous diagonalization of the relevant matrices, effectively reducing the problem to a more manageable vector-case analysis.

**Quality**
The paper appears to be technically sound. My primary question regarding a step in the appendix (the inequality `R(X^k) <= kappa3 |X^k - X^{k+1}|_F`) was satisfactorily addressed in the authors' rebuttal. They correctly pointed to an existing proof (Lemma 4 in "Scalable nonconvex inexact proximal splitting," Sra, NIPS 2012), which resolves my concern. With this clarification, I find the technical arguments to be complete.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written, and the arguments are followable. However, given the technical density of the material, the presentation could be improved by reordering the content. I suggest that after stating the main local error bound result, the authors immediately demonstrate its primary implication—the linear convergence rate. This part of the proof is a relatively standard derivation from the error bound. The highly technical proof of the error bound itself could then be presented afterward. This reordering would allow readers to grasp the main claims and their significance before diving into the most challenging technical details of the paper.

**Originality**
The originality of this paper is moderate. The central technical novelty lies in the observation that enables the simultaneous diagonalization of the matrices. This is an elegant and interesting technique. However, the overall analytical framework, which relies on establishing a local error bound to prove linear convergence, follows a well-established path. Consequently, while the key technical step is new, the broader approach is not.

**Significance and Summary**
The significance of this work is primarily theoretical. Establishing a linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient algorithm in this setting without assuming strong convexity is a valuable contribution to the optimization literature. However, the practical impact is somewhat tempered by the asymptotic nature of the result, which does not guarantee faster convergence in the initial stages of optimization. The technique for simultaneous diagonalization may be of independent interest and potentially applicable to other matrix optimization problems.

In summary, this paper provides a solid, albeit incremental, theoretical contribution. It proves the asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm for trace norm regularization under a relaxed convexity assumption. The proof relies on a clever matrix analysis technique situated within an existing theoretical framework. While the immediate practical implications are limited, the work is a worthwhile addition to the literature on the convergence analysis of first-order methods.
