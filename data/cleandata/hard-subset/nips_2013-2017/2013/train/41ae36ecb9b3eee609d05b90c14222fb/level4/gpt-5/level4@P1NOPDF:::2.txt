The paper proves linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace/nuclear norm–regularized problems under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss, addressing an important issue in many ML and signal processing applications. The paper is carefully and clearly written. It closely follows Tseng’s Lipschitzian error-bound technique [20], whose application to the nuclear norm case had remained open. To the best of my understanding, the main part of the proof is correct.

What I miss is a clear comparison to the vector case with l1-norm regularization, as in [20, 22] and related work. It would be very helpful to explicitly relate your results and techniques to those in the l1 case and to explain whether and why the l1 techniques do not directly transfer to the trace norm setting. Moreover, the l1 case should follow as a corollary of your trace norm result; stating this explicitly (and outlining how) would strengthen the paper. In evaluating novelty, note that some readers might not find it surprising that the l1 result extends to the trace norm, though I still view this as a valuable contribution.

I also suggest a slight change to the title. The main new contribution concerns the loss component rather than rendering the trace norm regularizer “structured.” The additional flexibility introduced by the linear operator A lies on the loss side, not in the regularizer, so the word “structured” may be unnecessary or potentially confusing.

Minor issues:
- Terminology for P: “sampling” may suggest randomness, whereas P is fixed here. Consider “mask,” “projection,” or simply “selection” operator.
- l. 337: broken reference.
- The meanings of Q-linear and R-linear convergence may not be widely known; a brief reminder would aid readability.
- Please further explain the residual R(X) defined in (6) and relate it to Tseng [20, Eq. (46)] to help readers interpret it.

More generally, I recommend adding a bit more discussion and a short conclusion to balance the long technical proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3 and to highlight the implications and limitations of your assumptions.
