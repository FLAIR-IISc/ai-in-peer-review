

This paper investigates the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems. The authors establish that for a broad class of loss functions (including square and logistic loss), the PGM converges linearly without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, which represents a significant extension of previous results. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, established through a novel observation that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors, enabling simultaneous diagonalization.

The paper follows a standard structure to prove this error bound and then applies the established framework of Luo and Tseng to derive linear convergence. The main novelty lies in the specific technical approach to handle the trace norm, which differs from previous work on ℓ₁-norm regularization due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph.

Quality: The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The proof of linear convergence is rigorous, though the draft review noted a potential gap in the appendix regarding the choice of kappa3 in the inequality R(X^k) ≤ kappa3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F. The authors should clarify why this specific choice of kappa3 (with alpha < 1) leads to the claimed inequality. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification support the theoretical findings, showing linear convergence in practice.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with a logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results. The technical depth is appropriate for the target audience. To further enhance clarity, the authors might consider stating the main contributions (the error bound and linear convergence result) earlier in the paper, before diving into the detailed proofs. This would help readers quickly grasp the paper's significance before engaging with the technical details.

Originality: The paper makes a moderate contribution to the field. The core novelty is the observation that optimal solutions and gradients share singular vectors, which enables the simultaneous diagonalization crucial for establishing the error bound. This insight is new and addresses a gap in the literature regarding error bounds for trace norm regularization. While the overall approach follows the framework established by Luo and Tseng, the specific application to trace norm regularization represents a meaningful extension. The rest of the proof (applying the error bound to show linear convergence) follows well-established methods, but the specific technical contribution to the error bound for trace norm is novel.

Significance: The theoretical contribution is significant for advancing the understanding of first-order methods for structured convex optimization. The result extends the applicability of linear convergence guarantees to more practical machine learning scenarios where loss functions are not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective measurement operators). However, the convergence guarantee is asymptotic, which somewhat limits its immediate practical impact. Despite this limitation, the paper's main significance lies in its theoretical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, particularly in bridging the gap between strongly convex and general convex settings for trace norm regularization.

Additional note: The draft review points out that the inequality R(X^k) ≤ kappa3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F has been established in Sra's NIPS 2012 paper (Lemma 4) for a more general setting. The authors should acknowledge this connection to provide proper context for their result.

Overall, the paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. While the result is incremental in the sense that it follows an established framework, the specific application to trace norm regularization with the novel technical insight represents a meaningful advancement. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important theoretical question in machine learning optimization. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of optimization algorithms for machine learning, particularly for problems involving low-rank matrix recovery.
