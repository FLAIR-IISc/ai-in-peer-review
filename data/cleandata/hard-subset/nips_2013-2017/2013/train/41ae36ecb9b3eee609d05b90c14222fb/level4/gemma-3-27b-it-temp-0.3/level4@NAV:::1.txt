This paper establishes the linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized learning problems, specifically when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strongly convex and Lipschitz continuous on compact sets. The work builds upon prior results by Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which demonstrated similar convergence properties for lasso and group lasso problems using an “error-bound condition,” extending this framework to the trace norm setting. While a non-trivial adaptation, the core contribution appears primarily technical in nature.

The proofs are generally well-presented. 

**Strengths:**

- Demonstrates linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, extending existing results from Tseng et al.

**Weaknesses:**

- The contribution is largely technical.
- A numerical illustration of the established linear convergence would significantly strengthen the paper.

**Detailed Comments:**

1. The proof structures for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 bear strong resemblance to those presented in Tseng’s work. More explicit referencing of the original work is needed to better delineate the novel aspects of this contribution.

2. Inclusion of a numerical example showcasing the linear convergence behavior – for instance, a semilog plot of the objective function value versus iteration number – is highly recommended.

3. The dependence of the constants κ<sub>1</sub> through κ<sub>4</sub> on the parameters  <u>α</u> and  ̄α is currently unclear and should be clarified.

**Minor Issue:**

4. The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat unclear. The final inequality is justified by the convexity of the trace norm and the inclusion of -̄G within τ∂||X||<sub>*</sub>, rather than the preceding intermediate inequality (refer to p289 in [20]).

In summary, this work represents an extension of previously established linear convergence results, based on the “error-bound condition” from lasso and group lasso, to the trace norm regularization context. To further strengthen the paper, the authors should consider adding a simulation demonstrating linear convergence, even in cases where the loss function is not strongly convex but the proximal gradient algorithm still exhibits linear convergence for varying levels of stability. Crucially, the limitations of the theoretical results should be discussed, specifically outlining scenarios where the theory may fail to hold. Furthermore, exploring potential extensions of this analysis to the ADMM algorithm would be valuable.
