

This paper extends Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22] to establish linear convergence of the proximal gradient (PG) method for trace-norm regularized learning when the loss is $f(X) = h(A(X))$, with $A$ linear and $h$ strongly convex on compact sets with Lipschitz gradient. The work builds on the "error-bound condition" framework previously applied to lasso and group lasso, generalizing it to the trace norm. While the extension is non-trivial, the contribution is primarily technical, lacking novel conceptual insights beyond the mathematical generalization.

**Strengths:**  
The paper successfully demonstrates linear convergence of PG under the specified conditions, extending prior results to a significant class of problems. The core analysis is logically structured, and the proofs are generally well-presented.

**Weaknesses and Required Revisions:**  
1.  **Technical Contribution:** The contribution is largely technical rather than conceptual. The authors should more explicitly contrast their approach with Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], clarifying *why* the trace-norm extension requires distinct arguments beyond the error-bound framework. Specifically, the outlines of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely mirror Tseng’s proofs; precise references to the analogous steps in [20] are needed to delineate the authors’ novel contributions.

2.  **Missing Numerical Validation:** A critical weakness is the absence of a numerical example demonstrating linear convergence. The authors **must include** a semi-log plot of the function value versus iteration count to empirically validate the claimed linear convergence rate. This is essential for assessing the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

3.  **Critical Gap in Theory:** The authors **must address a fundamental limitation** by adding a simulation where the loss $h$ is *not* strongly convex (e.g., $h$ is convex but not strongly convex, or has a non-strongly convex component). The paper must explicitly show:  
    (a) Whether the PG algorithm still converges linearly under such conditions, and  
    (b) **When the theory fails** (e.g., for specific non-strongly convex $h$ or parameter choices).  
    This is crucial for understanding the scope and limitations of the results. The current analysis assumes strong convexity, but real-world losses often lack this property.

4.  **Constants Dependence:** The dependence of constants $\kappa_1, \dots, \kappa_4$ on the choice of $\underline{\alpha}$ and $\overline{\alpha}$ is unclear. The authors should clarify how these constants scale with these parameters, as this impacts the practical applicability of the convergence rate.

5.  **Minor Technical Correction:** The sequence of inequalities preceding (13) is misleading. The final inequality follows from the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient condition $-\overline{G} \in \tau \partial \|X\|_*$ (as noted in Tseng [20, p. 289]), *not* from the intermediate inequalities. This should be corrected for precision.

6.  **ADMM Extension:** The paper concludes by mentioning an extension to ADMM. The authors should **discuss the feasibility and challenges** of extending their analysis to ADMM, rather than merely stating it as a future direction. How does the error-bound framework adapt to ADMM’s alternating updates? What additional assumptions might be required?

**Summary of Required Revisions:**  
The paper’s core contribution is a technically sound extension of linear convergence results to trace-norm regularization. However, it currently lacks empirical validation, fails to address the critical case where strong convexity does not hold (and when the theory breaks), and does not sufficiently distinguish its technical contribution from prior work. The required additions—specifically, the non-strongly-convex simulation with failure analysis and the ADMM discussion—are essential for a meaningful contribution. Without these, the paper’s significance is limited to a technical generalization with unclear practical implications.

**Recommendation:**  
**Major Revision Required.** The authors must address all points above, particularly the missing simulation for non-strongly convex losses and the explicit discussion of theoretical failure cases. The numerical example demonstrating linear convergence is non-negotiable. Once these revisions are made, the paper could make a valuable technical contribution. As-is, the work does not sufficiently advance the state of the art beyond the existing error-bound literature.
