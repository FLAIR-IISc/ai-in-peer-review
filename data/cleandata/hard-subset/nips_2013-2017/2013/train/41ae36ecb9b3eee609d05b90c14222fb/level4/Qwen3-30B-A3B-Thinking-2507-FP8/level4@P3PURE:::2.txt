

The paper establishes linear convergence of the standard proximal gradient method for trace norm regularized problems under weaker assumptions than strong convexity of the loss function—a crucial advancement for numerous machine learning and signal processing applications. The manuscript is well-written and clearly structured, closely following the Lipschitzian error bound technique pioneered by Tseng [20], whose application to nuclear norm problems had remained an open question. The core proof arguments appear sound and well-justified.

A significant gap is the absence of a clear comparison to the vector case of $\ell_1$-norm regularization (e.g., as in Tseng [20, 22]). The authors should explicitly relate their results and techniques to those established in the $\ell_1$ case, explaining why the $\ell_1$-techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm setting. Notably, the $\ell_1$ case should now follow as a corollary from the trace norm framework. This omission may lead some readers to perceive the contribution as less surprising than it is; while the contribution remains valuable, this context would strengthen the paper's impact.

Regarding the title, the phrase "structured" in "structured trace norm regularization" is misleading. The primary novelty lies in the flexibility introduced via the linear operator $\mathbf{A}$ on the *loss* side (not the regularizer), so the title should reflect this emphasis. I recommend revising to focus on the loss-side generalization.

**Minor issues:**  
- Notation: The operator $\mathbf{P}$ is termed a "sampling or mask operator," but "mask" implies randomness (whereas $\mathbf{P}$ is fixed). Clarify as a *fixed* mask operator.  
- Line 337: Broken reference.  
- The notations "Q-linear" and "R-linear" convergence are standard but not universally known; a brief definition would aid readability.  
- The residual $R(\mathbf{X})$ in (6) should be explicitly linked to its definition in (46) of Tseng [20] for better interpretation.  
- The lengthy proof of Lemma 3.2 warrants additional discussion or conclusions to contextualize its significance for the broader argument.

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful contribution. Addressing the comparative context and minor clarifications would substantially strengthen it.
