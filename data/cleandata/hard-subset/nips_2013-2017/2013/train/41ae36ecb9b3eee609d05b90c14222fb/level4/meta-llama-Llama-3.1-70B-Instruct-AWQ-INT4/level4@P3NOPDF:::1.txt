This paper proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method applied to trace-norm regularized learning problems when the loss function has the form of f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is strongly convex on any compact set and has a Lipschitz gradient. The authors extend the results of Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which established the same convergence for lasso and group lasso using the "error-bound condition", to the trace norm.

The presentation of the proofs is mostly clear, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the proximal gradient method. However, the contribution of the paper seems purely technical, and the extension to the trace norm, although non-trivial, does not provide significant new insights.

The strengths of the paper include the establishment of linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, which is a valuable contribution to the field. However, the weaknesses of the paper are notable. Firstly, the contribution is largely technical, and the authors could have explored more significant implications of their results. Secondly, the paper lacks numerical examples that demonstrate the linear convergence of the algorithm. A semilog plot of the function value versus the number of iterations would have been a valuable addition to the paper.

In terms of specific comments, the outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 seem very similar to those of Tseng [20]. The authors should provide more precise references to the original work to clarify their contribution. Additionally, the authors should consider including a numerical example that demonstrates the linear convergence of the algorithm.

Furthermore, it is not clear how the constants κ1, ..., κ4 depend on the choice of ᾱ and α. The authors should provide more details on how these constants are derived and how they relate to the choice of ᾱ and α.

On a minor note, the sequence of inequalities before inequality (13) is confusing. The last inequality follows due to the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that -Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X||* and not because of the intermediate inequality (see p289 in [20]).

In response to the authors' feedback, I would like to suggest the inclusion of a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex but the proximal gradient algorithm converges linearly (for different levels of stability). This would provide valuable insights into the robustness of the algorithm and its applicability to a wider range of problems. Additionally, a discussion on when the theory fails would be a valuable contribution to the paper. Finally, the authors should consider extending their analysis to the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), which would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proximal gradient method and its applications.
