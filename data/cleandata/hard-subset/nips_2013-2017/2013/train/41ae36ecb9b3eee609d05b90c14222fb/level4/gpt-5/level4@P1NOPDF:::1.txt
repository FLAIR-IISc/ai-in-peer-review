This paper proves linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm–regularized learning problems where the loss has the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with A linear and h strongly convex on any compact set and with Lipschitz gradient. The paper extends the “error-bound condition”–based linear convergence results known for lasso and group lasso (Tseng [20]; Tseng and Yun, “A coordinate gradient descent method for nonsmooth separable minimization”; Zhang et al. [22]) to the trace norm. This is a nontrivial extension, but the contribution appears primarily technical. The proofs are mostly clearly presented.

Strength:
- Establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm by extending prior results of Tseng et al. to the trace-norm setting.

Weaknesses:
- The contribution seems purely technical without introducing substantially new ideas.
- The paper lacks numerical evidence illustrating the claimed linear convergence.

More details:
1. The outlines of the proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 appear very similar to those in Tseng’s work. Please give more precise attributions and explicitly delineate what is new here versus what follows directly from prior arguments, to make the contribution clearer.
2. Please include a numerical example that empirically demonstrates linear convergence (e.g., a semilog plot of objective value versus iteration count).
3. It is unclear how the constants κ1, …, κ4 depend on the choices of \underbar{\alpha} and \bar{\alpha}. Please clarify this dependence and, if possible, provide bounds that make the practical implications transparent.

Minor issue:
4. The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is confusing. The final inequality follows from the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that −\bar{G} ∈ τ ∂‖X‖∗, not from the intermediate inequality (see p. 289 in [20]). Please revise this derivation for accuracy and clarity.

Overall assessment: This work extends linear convergence results based on the error-bound condition from lasso and group lasso to the trace norm. The extension is valuable but appears mainly technical; stronger positioning relative to prior work and a clear empirical demonstration of linear rates would significantly strengthen the paper. Additionally, discussing cases where the theory breaks (e.g., lack of strong convexity) and potential extensions to ADMM would improve the impact.
