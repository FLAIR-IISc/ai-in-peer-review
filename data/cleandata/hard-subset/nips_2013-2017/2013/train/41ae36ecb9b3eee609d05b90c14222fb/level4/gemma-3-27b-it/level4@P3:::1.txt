This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems, specifically when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strongly convex on compact sets with a Lipschitz gradient. The work extends existing linear convergence results – notably those of Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22] – originally established for lasso and group lasso problems, to the trace norm regularization setting. While technically sound, the contribution feels primarily incremental.

The paper is well-written and the presentation of the proofs is generally clear. However, the connection to prior work could be strengthened with more precise referencing, specifically highlighting the novel aspects of the extension.

**Strengths:**

*   Demonstrates the linear convergence of the PGM under stated conditions, extending previously known results to a relevant and widely used regularization technique.
*   The analysis leverages the powerful error-bound framework, providing a rigorous theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The core contribution appears to be a technical extension rather than a fundamentally new breakthrough. The novelty lies in adapting existing techniques to a different regularization term, rather than introducing a significantly new method or insight.
*   The absence of a numerical example demonstrating the linear convergence in practice is a significant drawback. A demonstration, particularly with a non-strongly convex loss, would greatly bolster the paper’s impact.
*   The dependence of the convergence constants (κ1 – κ4) on the step size parameters (α and ᾱ) is not explicitly discussed, hindering practical application of the results.
*   The authors were asked for a simulation to showcase the convergence in a non-strongly convex setting, and to discuss the conditions under which the theory fails. This was not addressed in the current revision.
*   The extension to the analysis of ADMM was requested and also not addressed.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  The proofs of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 4.1 closely resemble those found in Tseng [20]. The authors should explicitly acknowledge and detail *where* their contributions diverge from these established results. A more thorough comparison would clarify the technical innovation.
2.  A crucial addition would be a numerical experiment demonstrating the linear convergence behavior. A semi-log plot of the objective function value versus the number of iterations, for several relevant problem instances, would provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical findings. Consider demonstrating linear convergence for a case with a non-strongly convex loss function, as that's where the contribution is most relevant.
3.  Clarify the relationship between the step size parameters (α and ᾱ) and the convergence constants (κ1 – κ4). Providing explicit formulas or bounds would enhance the practical utility of the results.
4.  Minor Point: The inequality sequence preceding (13) contains a slight inaccuracy. The final inequality is a consequence of Fermat's rule and the optimality conditions, and not due to the stated convexity argument (refer to page 289 in Tseng [20] for a correct derivation).
5. The theory likely has limitations. A discussion on when the theory breaks down (e.g., for ill-conditioned problems, or when the assumptions on `h` and `A` are violated) would greatly improve the paper.
6. It would be interesting to discuss how these results might be extended to the analysis of ADMM, a popular algorithm for solving similar problems.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a technically sound extension of known convergence results.
*   The error bound presented could be of independent interest to researchers working on composite optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and lacks a strong practical demonstration.
*   The absence of numerical experiments and a discussion of failure cases significantly weakens the paper.
*   The lack of addressing reviewers' requests to demonstrate convergence in a non-strongly convex scenario and discuss ADMM limits the impact of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a solid technical contribution, but its impact is limited by the lack of practical validation and a somewhat incremental nature. Addressing the weaknesses, particularly adding numerical experiments and discussing the limitations of the theory, would substantially improve the paper. As it stands, I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the points raised.
