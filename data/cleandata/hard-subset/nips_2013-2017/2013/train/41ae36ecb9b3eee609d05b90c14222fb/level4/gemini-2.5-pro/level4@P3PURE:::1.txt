***

This paper proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized learning problems. The analysis applies to loss functions of the form f(X)=h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator, and h is a function that is strongly convex on any compact set and has a Lipschitz continuous gradient.

The work extends previous results by Tseng [20], Tseng and Yun, and Zhang et al. [22], which used an "error-bound condition" to establish linear convergence for lasso and group lasso problems. This paper applies a similar analysis to the trace norm case. While this is a non-trivial extension, the contribution appears to be primarily technical in nature. The presentation of the proofs is generally clear.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper successfully extends the linear convergence results of Tseng et al. from lasso and group lasso to trace norm regularization.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The contribution is mainly of a theoretical and technical nature.
*   The paper lacks numerical experiments to support the theoretical findings.

**Detailed Comments:**

1.  The proof outlines for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 appear very similar to those in Tseng's work. To better delineate their novel contributions, the authors should provide more precise references to the original work and clarify where their analysis diverges.

2.  The paper would be significantly strengthened by the inclusion of a numerical example that empirically demonstrates the claimed linear convergence. A semilog plot of the objective function value versus the number of iterations would be particularly illustrative.

3.  The dependency of the constants κ1, ..., κ4 on the choice of the parameters `\underbar{\alpha}` and `\bar{\alpha}` is not clearly explained.

4.  The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is confusing. The final inequality in the sequence appears to follow from the convexity of the trace norm and the subgradient optimality condition (`-\bar{G} ∈ τ∂||X||_*`), rather than from the preceding intermediate inequality (see p. 289 in [20]).
