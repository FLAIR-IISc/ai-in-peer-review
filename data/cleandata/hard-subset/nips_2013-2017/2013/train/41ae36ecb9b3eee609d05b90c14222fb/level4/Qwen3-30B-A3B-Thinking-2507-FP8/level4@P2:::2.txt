

This paper addresses an important open question in optimization: establishing linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors prove that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h satisfies mild convexity/smoothness conditions, the PGM achieves linear convergence. This significantly extends prior results that required strong convexity of f, which is often violated in practical applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning.

The main technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves an open question regarding whether such bounds exist for trace norm (unlike ℓ₁-norm where they are established [22, 23, 25]). The proof cleverly leverages matrix perturbation theory and the key observation that optimal solutions share singular vectors with the gradient term. The authors then apply the Luo-Tseng analysis framework [10] to establish linear convergence, demonstrating that the error bound is the crucial missing piece for extending linear convergence results to this important class of problems.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof that appears correct upon review. The theoretical contribution fills a significant gap in the literature, as previous linear convergence results for trace norm regularization required strong convexity [16, 19]. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification problems validate the theoretical findings, showing linear convergence in practice. The paper carefully addresses the non-trivial challenges in extending error bounds from ℓ₁ to trace norm regularization, demonstrating the authors' deep understanding of the problem.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making the complex technical content accessible. The logical flow from problem setup to main results to proof is clear. However, the paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison to the vector case (ℓ₁-norm regularization) that is standard in the literature [20, 22]. Such a comparison would help readers understand why the ℓ₁ techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm case (e.g., due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of trace norm [18]), and would strengthen the paper's position in the broader context of error bound theory. The authors should also clarify the distinction between Q-linear and R-linear convergence (common in optimization literature but not universally known) and explicitly connect their residual map R(X) to the definition in [20] for better interpretation.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by establishing linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions, which is a significant theoretical advance. While the trace norm has been widely used, this is the first linear convergence result for first-order methods applied to trace norm-regularized problems that doesn't require strong convexity of the loss function. The Lipschitzian error bound is itself a valuable technical contribution that could find independent use. The paper would be strengthened by explicitly noting that the ℓ₁ case (as in [22]) can now be seen as a special case of their trace norm result, which would clarify the relationship between these two important regularization techniques.

**Significance**: This work has substantial significance for machine learning and signal processing applications where trace norm regularization is widely used (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). The authors demonstrate that linear convergence can be achieved under much weaker assumptions than previously thought, which has important practical implications for algorithm design and implementation. The theoretical insights provided here could potentially extend to other matrix norm regularization problems, opening new research directions.

**Recommendation**: I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that addresses an important open question in optimization. While the comparison to the ℓ₁ case would strengthen the paper, this is a minor issue that could be addressed in revision. The technical content is sound, the writing is excellent, and the results are significant for the field.

**Pro**: 
- Solves an important open problem in trace norm optimization
- Provides the first linear convergence result for PGM without strong convexity
- The Lipschitzian error bound is a valuable technical contribution
- Well-written and logically structured
- Numerical experiments validate theoretical claims

**Con**:
- Lacks explicit comparison to ℓ₁-norm regularization case (a standard benchmark)
- Title could better reflect that the main contribution is in the loss function flexibility rather than the regularizer
- Some minor notational and explanatory issues (Q/R-linear convergence, residual definition)

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, and with minor revisions addressing the comparison to the ℓ₁ case and title, it would be an excellent addition to NIPS.
