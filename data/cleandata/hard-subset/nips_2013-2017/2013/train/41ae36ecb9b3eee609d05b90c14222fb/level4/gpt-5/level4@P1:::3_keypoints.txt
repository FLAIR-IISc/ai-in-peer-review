- Post-rebuttal note: the inequality ∥Rk∥F ≤ κ ∥Xk − Xk+1∥F is established in Sra (NIPS 2012, Lemma 4).  
- Paper studies proximal gradient methods for trace-norm regularized convex risk minimization and shows asymptotic linear convergence without strong convexity of f.  
- Assumes losses of form f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strictly convex, C^1, Lipschitz gradient, and locally strongly convex on compact sets (covers square and logistic losses).  
- Main technical contribution: a local Lipschitzian error bound dist(X,X*) ≤ η ∥R(X)∥F for trace-norm regularization.  
- Key novel observation: at an optimal solution X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors, enabling spectral perturbation analysis.  
- This spectral reduction locally turns the matrix problem into a vector-like structure, overcoming the nonpolyhedral difficulty of the trace norm.  
- Convergence result follows from the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework, yielding asymptotic linear rates for PGM.  
- Paper is technically sound and carefully derived; the convergence derivation is standard once the error bound is established.  
- Reviewer noted a gap (inequality linking residual to iterate differences) which is resolved by citing Sra (2012) if assumptions align.  
- Clarity is good overall; recommend restructuring Section 3 to state the error bound and derive linear convergence up front, deferring technical proofs.  
- Originality is moderate: the spectral alignment insight is new and important, but the overall proof strategy follows existing frameworks.  
- Significance is primarily theoretical: extends linear convergence guarantees to broader losses but yields asymptotic (not new non-asymptotic) rates and no new algorithms.  
- Pros: resolves an open error-bound question for the trace norm, provides asymptotic linear convergence under common losses, and offers a useful spectral insight plus supporting experiments.  
- Cons: methodological incrementality, asymptotic-only rates, and reliance on known results for some technical steps that need clearer exposition.  
- Overall: a solid, moderately interesting theoretical contribution suitable for a theory-focused audience.
