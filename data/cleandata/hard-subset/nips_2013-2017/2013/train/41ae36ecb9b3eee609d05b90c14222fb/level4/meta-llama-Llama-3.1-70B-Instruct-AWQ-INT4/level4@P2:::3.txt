This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving a class of trace norm-regularized problems, which is a popular approach in machine learning. The authors establish the linear convergence of the PGM without requiring the objective function to be strongly convex, making the result applicable to various settings in machine learning.

The paper begins by introducing the problem of minimizing a convex smooth loss function with trace norm regularization, which has received significant attention in machine learning. The authors then provide a detailed review of existing work on the proximal gradient method and its convergence rate guarantees. They highlight that while the standard PGM achieves a sublinear convergence rate, it can be improved to a linear convergence rate if the loss function is strongly convex.

The main contribution of the paper is the establishment of a local error bound that allows the authors to show that the PGM converges asymptotically at a linear rate for trace norm regularization. The authors follow the framework established by Luo and Tseng and prove a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem. This error bound is the key technical tool in the proof and could be of independent interest.

The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a detailed proof of the local error bound. However, the proof is quite technical, and the authors may want to consider reorganizing the paper to make it more accessible to readers. For example, they could state the main claims first and then provide the technical details.

The originality of the paper is moderate, as the authors build upon existing work and follow a well-established framework. However, the observation that allows them to simultaneously diagonalize matrices is new and interesting. The significance of the paper is mainly in theory, with little impact on algorithm design. While the result is certainly valuable, the asymptotic nature of the proof and claim lowers its significance.

In terms of quality, the paper appears to be technically sound, but the authors should clarify the choice of kappa3 in the appendix. In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but the authors may want to consider reorganizing it to make it more accessible to readers. Overall, the paper is a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the proximal gradient method and its convergence rate guarantees.
