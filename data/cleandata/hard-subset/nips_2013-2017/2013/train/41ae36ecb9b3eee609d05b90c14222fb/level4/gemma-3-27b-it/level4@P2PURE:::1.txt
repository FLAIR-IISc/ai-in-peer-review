This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized learning problems, specifically when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear mapping and h is strongly convex on compact sets with a Lipschitz gradient. This work extends the results of Tseng [20] and Zhang et al. [22], which demonstrated similar convergence properties using an “error-bound condition” for lasso and group lasso, to the setting of the trace norm. While a non-trivial extension, the contribution appears primarily technical.

The proofs are presented with reasonable clarity. 

**Strengths:**
- Demonstrates the linear convergence of the proximal gradient algorithm, extending prior work by Tseng et al.

**Weaknesses:**
- The contribution is largely technical in nature.
- A numerical example illustrating the linear convergence rate is lacking.

**Detailed Comments:**

1. The proof outlines for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 bear a strong resemblance to those presented by Tseng. The authors should provide more precise references to the original work to better highlight the novelty of their contribution.
2. Including a numerical example—such as a semilog plot of function value versus iteration count—would help validate the theoretical findings and demonstrate the linear convergence in practice.
3. The dependence of the constants κ<sub>1</sub>, ..., κ<sub>4</sub> on the parameter choices  <u>α</u> and  ̄α is unclear.

**Minor Issue:**
4. The sequence of inequalities preceding inequality (13) is somewhat convoluted. The final inequality is justified by the convexity of the trace norm and the fact that -̄G ∈ τ∂||X||<sub>*</sub>, and not by the intermediate inequality as stated (see p. 289 in [20]). 

In summary, this paper provides a linear convergence result, building upon the “error-bound condition” from lasso and group lasso analyses and extending it to the trace norm. 

**Further Request (added after authors’ feedback):** Please include a simulation where the loss function is not strongly convex, but the proximal gradient algorithm still exhibits linear convergence for different levels of stability. Critically, the authors should also discuss the conditions under which the theory presented in this paper would fail to hold. Finally, please discuss the potential for extending this analysis to the ADMM algorithm.
