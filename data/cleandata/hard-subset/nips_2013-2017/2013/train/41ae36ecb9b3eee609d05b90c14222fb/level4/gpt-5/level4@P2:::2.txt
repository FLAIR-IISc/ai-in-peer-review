Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proves that the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) achieves linear convergence for a broad class of trace-norm–regularized problems, without assuming strong convexity of the loss. The loss is of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient and local curvature on compact subsets of its domain. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound that controls the distance to the optimal set by the residual norm ‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. Using this error bound together with the Luo–Tseng analysis framework yields R-linear convergence of iterates and Q-linear convergence of objective values under standard step-size conditions.

This result fills an open gap in the literature: while linear rates via error bounds are known for composite problems with polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1 and (group) LASSO; see Tseng and subsequent work), extending these arguments to the nuclear norm was nontrivial because its epigraph is non-polyhedral and singular values couple all matrix entries. The paper leverages a structural property of optimal solutions—alignment of singular vectors of X̄ and −∇f(X̄)—to control local spectral behavior via matrix perturbation, enabling the desired error bound. The results cover common losses (squared and logistic) used in matrix completion, multi-task/multilabel learning, and matrix classification.

Quality:
The main arguments are sound to the best of my understanding. The use of a localized strong convexity of h on compact subsets, combined with a restricted invertibility property tied to A, is carefully justified. The convergence theorem follows cleanly from the error bound and standard PGM descent inequalities.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The residual map and its role in the fixed-point characterization are clear. The proofs are technical but readable; the matrix-perturbation argument and the simultaneous SVD property are explained with adequate detail.

Originality:
Establishing linear convergence of a first-order method for nuclear-norm regularization without assuming strong convexity of f is novel. The Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm appears to resolve an open question analogous to the ℓ1 case but requiring new ideas due to non-polyhedral geometry and spectral coupling.

Significance:
The result has practical impact for large-scale matrix problems where strong convexity fails (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A). It provides theoretical justification for the often observed fast local convergence of PGM in such settings and should influence analyses of related first-order methods.

Strengths:
- Resolves an open problem by proving a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm–regularized problems.
- Linear convergence of PGM under widely applicable and verifiable assumptions.
- Clear connection to the Luo–Tseng framework; conditions and constants are explicit.
- Empirical results corroborate the predicted linear rates.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The connection to the vector ℓ1 case (e.g., Tseng [20] and related work [22,25]) could be made more explicit. It would help readers to see:
  - Which parts of the ℓ1 analysis fail for the nuclear norm (non-polyhedral epigraph; lack of separability; singular-value coupling).
  - How the paper’s spectral alignment and perturbation arguments overcome these issues.
  - Whether the ℓ1 error bound can be recovered as a corollary of the current framework (e.g., viewing vectors as 1×n matrices).
- Positioning: while the novelty centers on the nonsmooth part (trace norm), the flexibility introduced by A is on the loss side. If the title includes the term “structured,” consider removing it to avoid suggesting a structured regularizer rather than a structured loss mapping.
- Provide a brief, self-contained reminder of Q- vs R-linear convergence in the main text (you already define them, but an earlier and more prominent reminder would help non-optimization specialists).
- Offer a short discussion section summarizing the proof’s key ideas (simultaneous SVD, restricted invertibility, localized curvature) to balance the long technical proof and help readers navigate the argument.

Minor comments:
- Terminology: Consider calling P a “mask” or “projection” operator rather than “sampling” operator if it is fixed (to avoid implying randomness).
- Broken reference around line 337.
- When introducing the residual map R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X, relate it explicitly to the residual used in Luo–Tseng (e.g., their equation (46)) to aid interpretation and comparison.
- Check equation numbering consistency when referencing the residual and shrinkage operator.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and timely problem with a clean, broadly applicable result.
- Introduces a genuinely new technical tool (error bound for the trace norm) with potential impact beyond this paper.
- Solid theory complemented by experiments demonstrating linear behavior.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited discussion of the relationship to the ℓ1 case and why prior techniques do not carry over could leave some readers feeling the result is incremental.
