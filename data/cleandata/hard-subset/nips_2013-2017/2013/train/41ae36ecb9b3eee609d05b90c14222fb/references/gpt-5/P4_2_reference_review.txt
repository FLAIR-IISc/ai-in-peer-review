Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the composite convex optimization problem min_X f(X)+τ||X||*, where ||·||* is the nuclear norm and f(X)=h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth and strictly convex. The main contributions are:
- A local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm–regularized problems: dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X||F for iterates within a bounded level set and sufficiently small residual.
- Linear (Q-linear for objective values and R-linear for iterates) asymptotic convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with step sizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf, derived via the Luo–Tseng analysis framework using the new error bound.

Prior linear-convergence results for PGM in composite settings typically require strong convexity of f, or rely on error bounds available when the nonsmooth part has a polyhedral epigraph (e.g., ℓ1 or group LASSO). Nuclear norm regularization is not polyhedral, making those arguments inapplicable. The paper’s analysis exploits an alignment property at optimality: optimal X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors, enabling a structured perturbation argument via simultaneous SVD and matrix perturbation theory. The results apply to widely used losses such as squared and logistic losses, even when A has nontrivial nullspace (so f is not strongly convex). The work relates to and builds on: Luo–Tseng error bound based convergence (Ann. OR 1994), accelerated/PGM for nuclear norm problems (e.g., Ma–Goldfarb–Chen; Toh–Yun), and error bounds for ℓ1/group norms (Tseng and collaborators). It would also be useful to connect to the broader KL-based convergence literature for semi-algebraic functions (e.g., Bolte–Daniilidis–Lewis; Attouch–Bolte–Svaiter), which can provide alternative pathways to linear rates under quadratic growth/EB conditions; the present paper contributes a specific EB for the nuclear norm setting.

Strengths:
- Technical novelty: Establishes a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization without assuming strong convexity of f. This addresses a genuine gap relative to ℓ1/group LASSO cases.
- Clear assumptions: f(X)=h(A(X)) with h C1, Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex, and locally (on compact subsets) strongly convex—a mild and standard condition satisfied by squared and logistic losses near the optimum.
- Sound analysis: The alignment of singular vectors at optimality and the resulting restricted invertibility bound are well-motivated and technically nontrivial. The reduction to residual-based EB and application of Luo–Tseng is standard but carefully executed.
- Practical relevance: The result explains empirically observed linear convergence of PGM for nuclear norm problems in matrix completion/classification.
- Empirical illustrations: Simple but consistent experiments show linear slopes once iterates are near the solution.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Scope of the EB: The error bound is local (requires small residual and bounded level set). The paper could better delineate the size of the neighborhood or provide practical diagnostics to detect entry into the linear regime (e.g., residual decay patterns).
- Step-size conditions: The theorem assumes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) with ᾱ<1/Lf. While a backtracking line search is used in one experiment, a theoretical treatment of line search or adaptive step sizes would strengthen applicability.
- Assumption 2.1(b): “Strongly convex on any convex compact subset” is somewhat unusual wording; clarifying that this is a local strong convexity around z̄ (and giving explicit constants for common h) would improve clarity.
- Completeness of proofs: Key structural results (e.g., Proposition 4.3 and some inequalities used in Theorem 3.2) are deferred to supplementary material. Given their centrality, including at least sketches/intuition in the main text would aid readability.
- Relation to broader frameworks: Adding discussion of KL-based linear convergence for semi-algebraic convex functions (which includes nuclear norm) and how this work identifies the specific EB and constants for the nuclear norm case would better situate the contribution.
- Experiments: Limited scale and breadth; no comparisons to accelerated variants or other first-order methods, and no study of sensitivity to conditioning of A. Including a plot comparing basic PGM vs. FISTA in the local regime and reporting estimated linear rates would be informative.

Evaluation by NeurIPS criteria:
- Quality: The paper appears technically sound. The main arguments are carefully set up, leveraging structural properties specific to the nuclear norm. Assumptions are reasonable for target applications. Some proofs are deferred but the presented logic is coherent.
- Clarity: Generally well organized, with precise definitions and assumptions. Minor notation inconsistencies/typos exist, and certain proofs relegated to the supplement could be summarized more. The narrative clearly motivates why ℓ1 arguments do not transfer and how the spectral structure is used instead.
- Originality: The error bound for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity and the ensuing linear convergence result appear novel and distinct from polyhedral cases. The simultaneous SVD–based argument is a clever element.
- Significance: Nuclear norm regularization is widely used; explaining and guaranteeing linear convergence of PGM under broad conditions is valuable for both theory and practice. The results are likely to influence subsequent analyses (e.g., identification, accelerated variants, and other matrix norms).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves an open question on EB and linear rates for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity.
- Solid, generalizable technique using spectral alignment and perturbation.
- Applicable to common losses (square, logistic) and relevant applications (matrix completion/classification).
- Clear theoretical contribution with illustrative experiments.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Local result; lacks guidance on global-to-local transition and practical criteria/parameters for the linear regime.
- Limited experimental validation; no comparisons or stress tests across diverse operators A.
- Some reliance on supplementary proofs for core propositions; presentation could be tighter in the main text.

Overall recommendation:
A theoretically strong and relevant contribution that fills a gap in the convergence theory of first-order methods for nuclear norm regularization. With modest improvements in exposition and broader empirical support, it makes a solid case for acceptance.
