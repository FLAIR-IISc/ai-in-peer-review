Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace/nuclear norm–regularized problems of the form min_X f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth and strictly convex. The main technical contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound of Luo–Tseng type: dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||R(X)||F for points with small residual R(X)=S_τ(X−∇f(X))−X and bounded objective (Theorem 3.1). Using this bound and standard descent properties of PGM under stepsizes α_k<1/L_f, the paper derives asymptotic linear convergence: objective values converge Q-linearly and iterates R-linearly (Theorem 3.2). The proof hinges on a spectral alignment property at optimality (Proposition 4.2), enabling a restricted invertibility estimate without assuming strong convexity of f. The results cover square and logistic losses and aim to close a gap between prior linear-rate results requiring strong convexity and the frequent non-injectivity of A in applications such as matrix completion.

This builds on: Luo & Tseng’s error-bound framework; known proximal theory for nuclear norm proximal maps; earlier linear rates under strong convexity or for polyhedral nonsmooth terms (ℓ1/group LASSO). The novelty claimed is an error bound for nuclear-norm regularization (non-polyhedral) and consequent linear rate without strong convexity of f.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and well-motivated question: linear rates for nuclear-norm regularization beyond strong convexity of f.
- The Lipschitzian error bound is technically interesting and, if correct, broadly useful.
- Clear linkage to the Luo–Tseng framework for turning error bounds into linear convergence of PGM.
- Covers losses of practical interest (quadratic, logistic).
- The proof strategy (spectral structure near optimality plus matrix perturbation) is original and insightful.

Weaknesses and concerns (technical and presentation)
Quality
- Proximal operator definition (Eq. (3)) appears incorrect or misprinted. Prox_{τ}(X) should minimize τ||Z||* + (1/2)||X−Z||_F^2. The 1/2 factor is missing and the typesetting suggests “+ 1” on a separate line. Yet later S_τ is asserted as the prox, which requires the 1/2 factor. Please fix, as constants in later inequalities depend on this scaling.
- Residual map mismatch: R(X)=S_τ(X−∇f(X))−X is tied to unit stepsize, while the PGM in (5) uses α_k and S_{τα_k}(X−α_k∇f(X)). Inequality (11) relates ||R(X^k)||F to ||X^k−X^{k+1}||F, but which R is used? If R is defined with α_k, state so; otherwise a rescaling argument must be explicit.
- Assumption 2.1(b) (“h strongly convex on any convex compact subset”) is unusual. It is true for common losses (e.g., logistic, square), but it would help to make precise sufficient conditions (e.g., lower bounds on the smallest eigenvalue of ∇^2h over sublevel sets reached by the iterates). As written, the dependence of σ in (15) on the sublevel set is implicit and may be arbitrarily small; this affects constants in the rate.
- Section 2.2: The set of minimizers of a convex problem is convex, but the given alternative description X={X: τ||X||*=v*−h(z̄), A(X)=z̄} does not by itself prove convexity (level sets with equality constraints on a convex function need not be convex). Best to cite the general fact directly and avoid possible confusion.
- Boundedness of sublevel sets: “Since X is bounded and F is convex, it follows … that {F≤ζ} is bounded.” This is generally false without further assumptions (boundedness of minimizers does not imply boundedness of all sublevel sets). Please correct the logic or the citation and state conditions under which the needed boundedness holds (e.g., coercivity due to τ||X||* dominating along directions with A(X) fixed).
- Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous SVD for X̄ and −∇f(X̄)): given −∇f(X̄)∈τ∂||X̄||*, the general subgradient has the form UV^T + W with U^T W=0, W V=0, ||W||_2≤1. One can indeed choose orthogonal complements to form Ū, V̄ making both simultaneously block-diagonalizable, but the statement/proof should be more precise about how W is handled and what “simultaneously SVDed” means (diagonal blocks versus exact singular vectors coincide on the support). This subtlety seems central to Proposition 4.3.
- Lemma 4.1(c) (“restricted invertibility”) for arbitrary A is surprising and powerful. The lengthy proof relies on a contradiction via projections onto X_c and a careful case analysis. Key steps and constants are deferred to the supplement (e.g., Proposition 4.3). Given the centrality of this lemma, more detail in the main text would be valuable; as is, it is hard to verify correctness.
- Inequalities (9)–(11) are referred to as “standard,” but the constants κ1–κ3 depend delicately on α, ᾱ, L_f and on the exact residual definition. Given the nonstandard residual choice, these should be either proved in the main text or precisely cited.
- Experiments use α=1 for matrix completion, but for quadratic loss with sampling operator P, L_f=||A||^2 L_h=1, so α=1 violates α_k<1/L_f (strict). This contradicts the theorem’s assumption. Please adjust step sizes or relax the theorem statement.

Clarity
- Notation inconsistencies: τ vs μ (regularization parameter), p reused for both measurement dimension and number of singular values, inconsistent norms (‖·‖2 vs ‖·‖F) sometimes without clarification, and missing vector/matrix dimensions in several places.
- Figure numbering/text mismatches: the text refers to Figure 1 for matrix completion, but the included caption says “Figure 2: Matrix Classification.” Axes, units, and legends are minimal; please standardize.
- In (14), “Rk/δk→0” omits norms; presumably ||R_k||F/δ_k→0.
- Many minor typos and LaTeX artifacts (en-dashes, spacing, broken equations). See detailed notes below.

Originality
- The claimed error bound for nuclear norm regularization (non-polyhedral) appears new and interesting. It would be helpful to discuss connections to KL inequality–based analyses (e.g., for semi-algebraic functions) and to compare to results using restricted strong convexity/RIP conditions in low-rank estimation that also yield linear rates (even if those impose stochastic/design assumptions).

Significance
- If correct, the error bound and linear convergence result broaden our theoretical understanding of first-order methods for nuclear-norm problems beyond strong convexity, with potential impact on algorithm analysis. However, empirical validation is limited: only small synthetic problems, no comparisons to accelerated PGM or other solvers, and no wall-clock/runtime data. This undermines practical significance.

Pros
- Tackles an important theoretical gap.
- Novel and technically nontrivial error bound for nuclear norm regularization.
- Clean asymptotic linear-rate result under mild (local) curvature of h.

Cons
- Several technical inconsistencies (prox definition/scaling, residual/stepsize mismatch, sublevel set boundedness argument).
- Heavy reliance on the supplement for key proofs.
- Experimental section is minimal and contains inconsistencies (stepsize vs theory, figure issues).
- Numerous notation and formatting problems; references incomplete (missing years, pages).

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify the exact residual map used in Theorem 3.1 and Theorem 3.2 and how it matches the stepsizes α_k.
2. Under what precise conditions on h and the reached sublevel set do you guarantee σ>0 in (15)? Can σ be arbitrarily small, and how does that affect the observed rates?
3. Can you provide a succinct, self-contained proof sketch for Proposition 4.2 in the main text clarifying the role of the W component in ∂||X̄||_*?
4. Can the error bound be extended to inexact prox or accelerated variants?

Typographical/formatting issues (non-exhaustive)
- Eq. (3): missing 1/2 factor; broken typesetting.
- Section 3: definition of Q-/R-linear convergence uses vector 2-norm; earlier norms are Frobenius—clarify.
- In multiple places, τ and μ are interchanged (experiments).
- References: many entries miss years, page numbers, venues; inconsistent styles.
- Figures: numbering, captions, axes labels inconsistent; ensure readability.
- (14): add norms; (6): specify the length of σ(X) and domain of s_μ; avoid reusing p.

Recommendation: Weak Reject
The core idea is promising and potentially significant, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage the authors to correct the proximal/residual inconsistencies, strengthen and/or move key proofs into the main text, clean up the experimental section (including step sizes consistent with theory and stronger baselines), and thoroughly revise notation, figures, and references.
