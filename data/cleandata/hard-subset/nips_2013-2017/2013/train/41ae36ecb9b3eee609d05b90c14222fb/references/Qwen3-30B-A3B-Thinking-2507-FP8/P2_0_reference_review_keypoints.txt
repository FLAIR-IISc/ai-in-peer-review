- Main contribution: Proves a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization and uses it to establish linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) without requiring strong convexity of the loss.  
- Problem setting: Considers problems min_X {f(X)+τ‖X‖_*} with f(X)=h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex and smooth and A is linear.  
- Novel insight: Shows optimal solutions and −∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), enabling spectral analysis.  
- Technical approach: Uses matrix perturbation theory and spectral-structure analysis to overcome the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm.  
- Gap filled: Resolves an open question in optimization literature by providing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization.  
- Empirical validation: Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification show linear convergence consistent with theory.  
- Clarity and presentation: Paper is well-organized, clearly states main results, and includes sufficient supplementary material for reproducibility.  
- Significance: Results are important for low-rank matrix recovery applications (e.g., matrix completion, multi-task learning) lacking strong convexity.  
- Scope limitation: Assumption f=h(A(X)) is restrictive—covers key cases like square/logistic loss but not all convex losses.  
- Unspecified constants: Does not quantify how error-bound constants (η, ε) depend on problem parameters such as rank or condition number of A.  
- Limited experiments: Empirical validation is limited to two applications and could be broader across sizes, ranks, and noise levels.  
- Recommendation: Strong accept—novel, technically sound contribution with clear theoretical and practical relevance.  
- Pro acceptance arguments: Resolves important open problem, first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm, proves broader linear convergence, theory supported by experiments, well-written.  
- Con acceptance arguments: Restrictive loss-form assumption, limited numerical experiments, and lack of characterization of constant dependence.
