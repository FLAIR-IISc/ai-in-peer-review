This paper addresses the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard analysis guarantees a sublinear O(1/k^2) convergence rate for PGM with acceleration. While linear convergence is known for strongly convex loss functions, many practical loss functions (e.g., square loss with an underdetermined linear operator) do not satisfy this condition.

The main contribution of this work is to prove that PGM converges linearly even when the loss function `f` is not strongly convex, provided it has the structure `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a smooth, strictly convex function. The core of the analysis is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing this error bound for the trace norm is non-trivial; unlike the l1-norm, its epigraph is not polyhedral, and analysis techniques based on variable splitting do not apply. The authors overcome this by identifying and exploiting a key structural property of the optimal solution: an optimal matrix `X*` and its corresponding negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows the use of matrix perturbation theory to control the spectral structure of iterates near the optimum, leading to the desired error bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng [10].

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper provides a theoretically important and practically relevant result. It guarantees a fast, linear convergence rate for a standard algorithm (PGM) on a wide class of problems for which such rates were not previously known. This bridges a significant gap between the theory (requiring strong convexity) and practice (where non-strongly convex losses are common). The derived error bound is a valuable result in its own right and could spur further research.

2.  **Originality and Quality:** The key technical contribution—the proof of the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—is highly novel and technically deep. The observation about the shared singular vectors (Proposition 4.2) is a clever insight that unlocks the analysis. The subsequent proof, sketched in Section 4, demonstrates a sophisticated use of matrix analysis and perturbation theory to overcome the challenges posed by the non-separable and non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm. The theoretical claims appear sound and are rigorously established.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, situates the work within the existing literature, and precisely states its assumptions and main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2). The authors do an excellent job of explaining why existing analysis techniques for similar problems (like LASSO) fail and how their new approach circumvents these issues. The proof sketch provides a clear roadmap of the technical arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature and Lack of Rate Characterization:** The main result is an *asymptotic* linear convergence rate. The error bound in Theorem 3.1 holds only for iterates `X` within a certain neighborhood of the optimal set (i.e., `F(X) <= ζ` and `||R(X)||F <= ε`). The analysis provides no information on the size of this neighborhood or the number of iterations required to enter it. Furthermore, the linear convergence rate `ρ = κ4 / (κ1 + κ4)` depends on several problem-dependent constants (`η`, `κ`, `σ`, `L`, `||A||`). A discussion on how these constants, and thus the rate `ρ`, scale with problem parameters (e.g., matrix dimensions, rank of the solution, condition number of `A`) would significantly enhance the practical implications of the result. Without this, it's hard to know if the guaranteed rate is meaningful or potentially very close to 1 for ill-conditioned problems.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments successfully demonstrate the phenomenon of linear convergence, corroborating the theory. However, they are conducted on small-scale problems (n=40). It would be more convincing to see this behavior demonstrated on larger problems where the efficiency of the algorithm is more critical. Additionally, the plots focus on the "tail" behavior where convergence is linear. Showing the entire convergence trajectory from the start would help illustrate the sublinear pre-asymptotic phase and give a sense of when the linear phase begins.

3.  **Proof Details:** While the proof sketch is clear, it relies heavily on technical propositions (4.2 and 4.3) whose proofs and further details are relegated to the supplementary material. A bit more intuition for Proposition 4.3 (the decomposition of the direction `Q̄`) in the main text would be helpful for the reader to better follow the logic of the contradiction argument in the proof of Lemma 4.1(c).

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Establishes linear convergence of PGM for a wide class of non-strongly convex trace norm regularized problems, a significant theoretical advance.
*   Introduces a novel and non-trivial Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, a result of independent interest.
*   The proof technique, based on a new structural insight about the optimal solution, is original and powerful.
*   The paper is very clearly written and well-motivated.

**Cons:**
*   The linear convergence is asymptotic, with no characterization of the pre-asymptotic phase.
*   The paper does not analyze the dependency of the convergence rate on key problem parameters.
*   The experimental validation is limited to small-scale problems.

**Recommendation:**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and novel theoretical result for a fundamental problem in machine learning and optimization. The strengths, particularly the originality and importance of the theoretical contribution, far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the scope of the analysis and experiments rather than its correctness. This work resolves an important open question and is likely to be highly cited and influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
