**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + τ||X||*`. While PGM is known to converge linearly for strongly convex `f`, many important machine learning applications, such as matrix completion with a square loss, feature loss functions that are convex but not strongly convex. The authors bridge this gap by establishing an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM under a weaker set of assumptions. The key assumption is that the smooth part of the objective, `f`, can be written as `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function and `A` is a linear operator.

The central technical contribution is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This bound relates the distance of a current iterate to the optimal solution set with the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the optimality conditions. Proving this bound for the trace norm is non-trivial because, unlike the L1-norm, its epigraph is not polyhedral and its structure does not permit a simple decomposition of variables. The authors overcome this by leveraging a key structural property of the optimal solutions and applying matrix perturbation theory. Once this error bound is established, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the established analytical framework of Luo and Tseng [10]. The authors validate their theoretical findings with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper provides a crucial piece of theoretical justification for the observed fast convergence of first-order methods on a wide range of important, non-strongly convex problems. Linear convergence is the gold standard for iterative methods, and proving it without the strong convexity assumption is a significant advancement. This result will be of great interest to both the optimization and machine learning communities.

2.  **Novel and Technically Sound Contribution:** The main technical result, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1), is novel and challenging. It resolves a known open question. The proof technique, which cleverly uses the shared singular vector structure of the optimal solution `X*` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X*)`, is elegant and represents a new approach for analyzing the geometry of the trace norm ball near the solution set. The technical quality of the paper is very high.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, precisely states the problem and assumptions, and presents the main results upfront. The proof sketch in Section 4 provides an excellent high-level overview of the argument, logically connecting the error bound to the linear convergence result. The motivation for why existing techniques for L1-norm error bounds fail is also clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic. The error bound (Eq. 8) and the subsequent linear rate only hold once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set (i.e., when `F(X) ≤ ζ` and `||R(X)||F ≤ ε`). While this is a common feature of such results, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the pre-asymptotic (sublinear) phase and what factors might influence the size of the region where linear convergence kicks in. This would provide a more complete picture for practitioners.

2.  **Over-reliance on Supplementary Material:** The proof of the main error bound is highly technical and intricate. Key results, such as Proposition 4.3, which describes the structure of the limiting direction `Q̄`, are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is necessary due to page limits, it makes it difficult to fully verify the correctness of the core argument from the main paper alone. Perhaps a sentence or two of intuition behind the proof of Proposition 4.3 could be added to the main text to make it more self-contained.

3.  **Numerical Experiments:** The experiments serve as a good proof of concept, visually demonstrating the linear convergence tail predicted by the theory. However, they are somewhat basic. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth experimental section that:
    *   Compares PGM to other state-of-the-art solvers for these problems to show that the theoretically-backed method is also practically competitive.
    *   Investigates how problem parameters (e.g., rank, dimension `n`, sampling ratio `θ`) affect the constant `κ4 / (κ1 + κ4)` in the linear convergence rate (Eq. 12). This would connect the theory more tightly to practical performance.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issue:** The captions for the figures in the Matrix Completion and Matrix Classification sections appear to be swapped or incorrectly labeled. Figure 1 seems to be missing, and Figure 2 is referenced for matrix classification, but the plots shown seem to correspond to the matrix completion experiment described. This should be corrected.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

*   The restricted invertibility property in Lemma 4.1(c) is central to the proof. It essentially states that the norm of the error in the parameter space, `||Xk - X̄k||F`, is controlled by the norm of the error in the measurement space, `||A(Xk) - A(X̄k)||`. This is reminiscent of restricted strong convexity (RSC) or similar conditions used to prove linear convergence for other problems like the LASSO. A brief comment connecting this property to that broader family of conditions could help place the work in a wider context.

*   Assumption 2.1(b) requires `h` to be strongly convex on any *convex compact subset* of its domain. This is a key technical assumption. While it holds for square and logistic loss, are there common loss functions for which it might fail? A brief comment on the scope of this assumption would be helpful.

**Recommendation**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and significant contribution to the optimization literature relevant to machine learning. The theoretical results are novel, the technical arguments are deep, and the implications are substantial. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily related to the scope of the experimental section and the challenges of presenting a complex proof within page limits. The paper is of high quality and will undoubtedly be of great interest to the NIPS audience. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM on trace norm problems without strong convexity.
*   Resolves an open question by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm.
*   The work is technically deep, rigorous, and clearly presented.
*   High potential impact for researchers working on low-rank matrix recovery and first-order optimization methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The numerical experiments are limited to demonstrations and lack comparative analysis.
*   The convergence is asymptotic, and the pre-asymptotic behavior is not discussed.
(In my view, the strengths far outweigh these minor weaknesses.)
