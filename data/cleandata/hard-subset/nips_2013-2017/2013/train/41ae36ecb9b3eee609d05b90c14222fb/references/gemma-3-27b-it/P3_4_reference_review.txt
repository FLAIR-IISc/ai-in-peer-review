## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized problems of the form min<sub>X</sub> F(X) + τ||X||<sub>*</sub>, where F(X) = h(A(X)) and A is a linear operator. The authors demonstrate that, under fairly mild assumptions on *h* (strict convexity, smoothness, and curvature), the PGM exhibits a *linear* convergence rate, even when the loss function *f* is not strongly convex. This is a significant result, as previous linear convergence guarantees for PGM typically relied on strong convexity of *f*. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the difficulty in decomposing the matrix entries for analysis. The authors support their theoretical findings with numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The main result – establishing linear convergence without strong convexity – is a substantial advancement. It expands the applicability of PGM to a wider class of problems prevalent in machine learning, particularly those like matrix completion where strong convexity doesn't naturally hold.
* **Novel Error Bound:**  The development of a Lipschitzian error bound specific to trace norm regularization is a clever and crucial step. The authors appropriately highlight the challenges associated with the trace norm (compared to l1 regularization) and skillfully address them using matrix perturbation theory.  The observation about the shared singular vector structure of optimal solutions is insightful.
* **Broad Applicability:** The framework and results apply to a wide range of loss functions *f*, encompassing square loss, logistic loss, and others commonly encountered in machine learning applications. 
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction provides good motivation and context, and the authors clearly state their assumptions.
* **Supporting Experiments:** The numerical experiments, although not extensive, provide initial evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrating the practical effectiveness of the PGM.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The authors do a good job situating their work within the broader landscape of convex optimization, proximal algorithms, and matrix regularization techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity & Accessibility:**  While the arguments appear sound, the proofs (especially those in the supplementary material) are quite dense and technically demanding. This might make the paper less accessible to a broader audience. More intuitive explanations of the key steps in the proof would be beneficial.
* **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited.  Exploring the impact of different problem sizes (n), regularization parameters (τ), and operators (A) on the convergence rate would strengthen the empirical validation.  Comparing PGM's performance to other first-order methods (e.g., accelerated gradient descent) would also be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** While the assumptions on *h* (smoothness, curvature, strict convexity) are reasonable, their implications in specific applications (e.g., what conditions on A and b guarantee these assumptions hold in matrix completion) could be discussed more explicitly.
* **Discussion of Constant Factors:** The theoretical results establish linear convergence, but don't explicitly address the *rate* constant.  Understanding the dependence of the convergence rate on problem parameters (e.g., condition number, rank) would be useful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization and machine learning. The theoretical results are significant, the technical approach is innovative, and the presentation is generally clear. The weaknesses are primarily related to the density of the proofs and the limited scope of the experimental validation, but they do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work. 

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:**  The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a novel and important contribution.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
* **Significance:** The findings have the potential to impact a wide range of applications involving trace norm regularization.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the key ideas.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Proof Complexity:**  The technical depth of the proofs may limit accessibility.
* **Limited Experimentation:** The experimental validation is somewhat sparse.



**Rating:**  **Strong Accept (8/10)**

**Recommendation:** I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It represents a significant theoretical advance and makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of PGM for trace norm regularized problems. I encourage the authors to consider expanding the experimental section and providing more intuitive explanations of the key steps in the proofs to enhance accessibility.
