

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a significant gap in the convergence analysis of proximal gradient methods (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for the problem min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*} where f(X) = h(A(X)), with h strictly convex and A linear. This enables them to prove linear convergence of PGM under conditions that do not require f to be strongly convex—a notable advancement over prior work that typically required strong convexity for linear convergence rates. The key technical contribution is resolving the open question of whether a Lipschitzian error bound exists for trace norm regularization, which was previously known for ℓ1-norm but not for trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Significance**: The paper makes a substantial contribution by establishing the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity assumptions. This is particularly valuable for applications like matrix completion where the loss function is not strongly convex.

2. **Technical Sophistication**: The proof of the error bound is sophisticated, particularly the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the key restricted invertibility property (Lemma 4.1c). The authors overcome the challenge of the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph by leveraging the shared singular vector structure between optimal solutions and the gradient (Proposition 4.2).

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and effective use of supplementary material for technical details.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence behavior as predicted by the theory, providing strong empirical support for the claims.

## Weaknesses and Critical Points

1. **Limited Scope of Loss Functions**: The paper focuses exclusively on f(X) = h(A(X)). While this covers many important cases (square loss, logistic loss), the authors should discuss whether their results extend to more general loss functions that cannot be expressed in this form. For example, how would their analysis apply to the sum of multiple loss terms with different structures?

2. **Global Assumption in Error Bound**: The error bound (Theorem 3.1) requires F(X) ≤ ζ for some ζ ≥ v*. This global assumption is significant because most convergence analyses for first-order methods are local. The paper should clarify the relationship between ζ and the initial iterate, and whether this condition is restrictive in practice.

3. **Comparison to Related Work**: The paper correctly identifies the gap in the literature regarding error bounds for trace norm, but it could better contextualize its contribution by comparing the error bound's constants to those for ℓ1-norm regularization. This would help readers understand the quantitative significance of the bound.

4. **Numerical Experiments**: The experiments are limited to two problems. To better demonstrate the generality of the results, additional experiments on other trace norm problems (e.g., multi-task learning with different loss structures) would strengthen the paper.

5. **Step Size Discussion**: The paper assumes constant step sizes (0 < α < α_k < ᾱ < 1/L_f), but in practice, adaptive step sizes like backtracking line search are commonly used. The authors should discuss how their convergence analysis extends to adaptive step size schemes.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a meaningful advance in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for trace norm-regularized problems. The key technical insight—the error bound for trace norm regularization—resolves an important open question and has potential implications for other non-polyhedral regularizations. The paper is technically sound, well-organized, and supported by empirical evidence.

The main limitations are the restricted scope of loss functions considered and the global assumption in the error bound. However, these do not undermine the paper's core contribution, which is significant for the field. The paper advances the state of the art in convergence analysis for non-strongly convex regularized problems and provides valuable theoretical tools.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that should interest the NIPS community. The authors should address the following points in revision:

1. Expand the discussion on the scope of loss functions (beyond f(X) = h(A(X))).
2. Clarify the practical implications of the global assumption F(X) ≤ ζ.
3. Provide a more detailed comparison of the error bound's constants to those for ℓ1-norm regularization.
4. Include at least one additional numerical experiment on a different trace norm problem.

The paper's contribution is novel, technically sound, and well-motivated, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
