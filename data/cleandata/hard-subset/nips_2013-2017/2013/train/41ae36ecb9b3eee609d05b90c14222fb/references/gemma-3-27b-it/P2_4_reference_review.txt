## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) when applied to trace norm regularized optimization problems.  The authors demonstrate linear convergence under relatively weak assumptions – specifically, they don't require strong convexity of the overall loss function *f*, but rather a smoothness and curvature condition on *h* in the form *f(X) = h(A(X))*.  They achieve this by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The paper rigorously proves their claims and includes numerical experiments to support their theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The linear convergence result is a meaningful advancement, especially as it relaxes the strong convexity assumption that plagued previous analyses.  This broadens the applicability of the PGM to a wider range of problems, including matrix completion where strong convexity doesn’t naturally hold.
* **Novel Error Bound:** Establishing the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization is a key technical achievement. The authors skillfully address the difficulties arising from the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm’s epigraph and its dependence on all entries of the matrix, which makes conventional splitting approaches ineffective.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is clearly defined, and the proof structure is outlined in a helpful manner.  The introduction provides good motivation and context.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments on both matrix completion and matrix classification lends credence to the theoretical results, demonstrating linear convergence in practice.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors clearly relate their work to existing literature, citing relevant papers on PGM, proximal operators, and related error bounds. They acknowledge the limitations of prior approaches and clearly position their contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Depth:** The paper is quite technically demanding, and fully understanding the proofs requires a strong background in convex optimization and matrix analysis. This makes it potentially less accessible to a broader audience.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** While the numerical experiments are helpful, they are relatively limited in scope. Exploring a wider range of problem sizes, regularization parameters, and different linear operators *A* could strengthen the empirical support.
* **Assumptions on *h*:** The assumptions on the function *h* (smoothness and curvature) are somewhat abstract. While justified, providing more concrete examples of functions *h* that satisfy these conditions and their impact on the convergence rate could be valuable.



**Evaluation based on NIPS Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by a rigorous mathematical analysis.  The proofs appear correct and complete.  The authors are careful to state their assumptions and limitations. **(Strong)**
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, though the technical details require significant effort to follow. The organization is logical, but some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations. **(Good)**
* **Originality:** The linear convergence result for the stated setting is novel and represents a significant advance over existing work. The development of the Lipschitzian error bound is also a substantial original contribution. **(Excellent)**
* **Significance:** The results are likely to be significant to researchers and practitioners working on low-rank matrix recovery, compressed sensing, and related fields. Relaxing the strong convexity assumption broadens the applicability of the PGM and could lead to faster convergence in many real-world applications. **(Excellent)**

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  The paper presents a strong theoretical result with practical implications.
*  The technical contribution (the error bound and the linear convergence proof) is substantial and original.
*  The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings.
*  The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key ideas.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The high technical complexity might limit its accessibility.
* The numerical experiments could be more extensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It addresses a significant problem in optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach. The relaxation of the strong convexity assumption is a key contribution, and the empirical results support the theoretical findings. While the technical depth may be challenging for some readers, the clarity of presentation and the overall quality of the work outweigh this concern.  This paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.
