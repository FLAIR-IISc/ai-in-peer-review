- Paper proves linear convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f.
- Key technical innovation: a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, using matrix perturbation theory.
- Results require only smoothness and a curvature property on h in f(X)=h(A(X)), broadening applicability to many losses.
- Empirical experiments (matrix completion and classification) show linear convergence consistent with theory.
- Theoretical contribution is significant and expands PGM applicability to problems lacking strong convexity.
- The error-bound derivation addresses challenges absent in ℓ1 analyses (non-polyhedral epigraph, coupling across variables).
- Paper is generally well-written, well-motivated, and connects clearly to related work.
- Proofs (Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 4.1) are dense and rely on substantial technical machinery, reducing accessibility.
- Experimental section lacks detail (parameters, resources, comparative baselines) and could be more comprehensive.
- Convergence-rate constants (κ1, κ2, κ3, etc.) are unspecified, limiting practical interpretability.
- Step-size condition in Theorem 3.2 is restrictive and lacks practical guidance for choosing α when Lf is unknown.
- Overall the paper is technically sound, original, and significant for both researchers and practitioners.
- Arguments for acceptance: strong theoretical contribution, novel error bound, supporting experiments, and clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: proof complexity, limited experimental detail, and missing explicit constant/step-size guidance.
- Recommendation: strong accept, contingent on careful supplementary-material review; suggest streamlining proofs and expanding experiments for the camera-ready version.
