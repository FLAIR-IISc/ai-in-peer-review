Summary
The paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for nuclear-norm regularized problems minX f(X)+τ||X||*, focusing on the common case f(X)=h(A(X)) where A is linear and h is smooth and strictly convex (with Lipschitz gradient) and “locally strongly convex” on compact subsets. The main technical contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound: for points in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual R(X)=prox(X−∇f(X))−X, dist(X,X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F. Using the Luo–Tseng framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (Q-linear in objective, R-linear in iterates) with constant step sizes αk bounded away from 0 and strictly less than 1/Lf. The proof relies on a spectral structural result at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) can be simultaneously SVD’ed), perturbation arguments, and a “restricted invertibility” property that ties dist(X,X*) to ||A(X−X̄)||.

Relation to prior work
- Classical PGM rates: O(1/k) and O(1/k^2) (accelerated), and linear under strong convexity.
- Luo–Tseng error bound-based linear rates for composite problems are known for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, (sparse) group LASSO).
- The paper claims the first local error bound and linear-rate result for nuclear norm without strong convexity of f.
- However, there is a substantial body of work not cited/positioned against:
  - Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz (KL) theory for composite convex optimization ensures local linear rates of PGM under the KL exponent 1/2; nuclear norm is semi-algebraic/definable, so KL conditions apply.
  - Partial smoothness/active manifold identification (Hare & Lewis; Drusvyatskiy et al.; Vaiter–Fadili–Peyré; Liang–Fadili–Peyré) gives identification for nuclear norm and linear convergence under restricted strong convexity along the identified manifold.
  - Results on linear rates for related algorithms (prox-linear/ADMM) under similar geometric conditions.
The paper should clearly articulate how its error bound differs from, strengthens, or complements these frameworks and whether it yields sharper constants/conditions.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and widely used model class (trace-norm regularization with structured smooth loss) where global strong convexity typically fails due to A’s null space.
- Provides a concrete, verifiable local error bound tailored to the nuclear norm (nonpolyhedral), which is technically nontrivial.
- The spectral alignment observation at optimality and its use in the perturbation-based “restricted invertibility” argument are insightful.
- The resulting linear rate covers square loss and logistic loss and is established with standard constant step sizes.

Weaknesses
- Novelty/positioning: The contribution is incremental relative to general KL/error-bound-based linear convergence theory for composite convex problems. The paper does not compare with or cite these general results, nor clarify whether its conditions are weaker/stronger or whether it recovers KL-based rates as a special case.
- Assumptions: The requirement that h be “strongly convex on any convex compact subset” is stronger than standard strict convexity and may be unexpected. While it holds for square/logistic losses on bounded sets, the paper should clarify the breadth of h covered and whether the needed lower curvature bound σ is ensured along the actual level set visited by PGM.
- Restricted invertibility: The key inequality ||X−X̄|| ≤ κ||A(X−X̄)|| is only established asymptotically and depends on the spectral structure of the optimal set. A clearer geometric condition (e.g., injectivity of A on the identified tangent space/manifold) would make the result more interpretable and verifiable by practitioners.
- Asymptotic nature: The error bound is local (requires small residual), so the linear rate is only asymptotic. The paper could better delimit the basin of attraction (in terms of ζ, ε) and discuss practical implications.
- Experiments are very limited:
  - Only small synthetic problems (n=40), no real datasets, no comparisons to established baselines (accelerated PGM/FISTA, proximal Newton, ADMM/prox-linear).
  - No study of constants or of how close one needs to be to observe linear rate, nor sensitivity to A and τ.
  - The matrix completion experiments use α=1. For the sampling operator, Lf=1, so the theorem’s requirement ᾱ<1/Lf is violated; standard theory often allows α≤1/L, but the presented proof assumes strict inequality. This mismatch should be addressed.
  - For logistic loss, backtracking is used; the theorem requires αk uniformly bounded away from 0, which is not guaranteed by backtracking unless enforced; no evidence is provided.
- Practical considerations such as inexact proximal computations (partial SVDs), which are standard in large-scale nuclear-norm problems, are not discussed; existing inexact PGM theory might affect the rate.

Questions for the authors
1. How does your error bound relate to KL-based linear-rate results for semi-algebraic composite convex problems? Does your analysis yield weaker/stronger assumptions or sharper constants?
2. Can you express the “restricted invertibility” as a verifiable condition on A relative to the tangent space of the identified rank manifold at X̄? Is A required to be injective on that space?
3. Can you relax the ᾱ<1/Lf restriction to ᾱ≤1/Lf, aligning with standard PGM analyses? If not, please explain why strict inequality is essential.
4. Does your analysis extend to inexact proximal steps (e.g., approximate SVD), and would the linear rate be preserved under summable or relative errors?
5. Please clarify the scope of h beyond square/logistic losses and provide explicit bounds for σ on the level sets you use.

Pros
- Solid and careful theoretical development for an important model class.
- Nontrivial handling of a nonpolyhedral regularizer.
- Clear main theorems with reasonable assumptions and a plausible proof strategy.

Cons
- Limited novelty relative to established KL/error-bound literature; insufficient positioning.
- Very limited empirical validation; no comparisons to strong baselines; step-size/theory mismatch.
- Asymptotic result with unclear basin; key conditions not expressed in readily checkable geometric terms.
- No discussion of inexact proximal computations pertinent to real applications.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting, but the novelty over general KL/error-bound frameworks is unclear, and the empirical section is too weak for NeurIPS. Strengthening the relation to existing theory, clarifying/verifying assumptions (including step-size), and adding substantial, comparative experiments (including inexact prox) would significantly improve the paper.

Confidence: Moderate.
