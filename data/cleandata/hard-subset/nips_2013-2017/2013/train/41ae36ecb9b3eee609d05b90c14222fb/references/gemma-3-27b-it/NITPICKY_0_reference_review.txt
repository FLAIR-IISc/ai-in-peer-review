This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems, specifically aiming to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under the assumption that the loss function can be expressed as a composition of a linear operator and a strictly convex function. They achieve this by deriving a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to address the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

The paper is generally well-written and tackles an important question in optimization. The motivation is clearly presented, and the connection to existing literature is established. The theoretical results, particularly the error bound, are the core contribution and appear to be technically sound, given the complexity of the analysis. The inclusion of numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence is a positive aspect.

However, despite the novelty of the result and apparent technical correctness, I have significant concerns regarding the level of detail and rigor in certain parts of the proof, as well as several minor (but cumulatively substantial) inconsistencies and omissions. These ultimately overshadow the potential significance of the work for me.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a valuable contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness (Apparent):** The core argument regarding the error bound and its connection to linear convergence seems logically consistent.
*   **Relevance:** The problem setting is widely applicable in machine learning and optimization.
*   **Numerical Validation:** The experiments provide empirical support for the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proof Detail & Rigor:** The proofs, especially Lemma 4.1 and its supporting propositions, lack sufficient detail. Several steps are asserted without adequate justification. For instance, the transition from establishing properties of Q̄ to a contradiction isn’t fully fleshed out. The claim that certain cases in Section 4.1.2 directly imply the contradiction needs significantly more explanation.
*   **Assumptions:** While the assumptions are stated, their implications and how they are used within the proofs aren't always clear. The justification for Assumption 2.2 (non-empty optimal solution set) feels superficial and could benefit from more discussion, especially given the context of the loss function f.
*   **Notation & Definitions:** The notation is sometimes inconsistent. For example, the definition of *R(X)* is initially given in terms of the proximity operator, then implicitly redefined with *Sτ*. This could cause confusion. The definition of *Xc* and the properties of matrices within it require clearer exposition.
*   **Minor Inconsistencies:** Several minor typographical errors and inconsistencies exist throughout the text. The citation formatting is not uniform (e.g., [17] vs. [1]).
*   **Experimental Details:** The numerical experiments lack sufficient detail regarding the parameter settings (e.g., step size selection, stopping criteria) and the scale of the problems. While the convergence appears to be linear, a more thorough analysis (e.g., plotting the convergence rate) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the result. For example, the conditions on *h* (Assumption 2.1) are relatively strong, and a discussion of how these affect the applicability of the result would be beneficial.
*    **Unnecessary verbosity:** There are many sentences that add little value to the overall paper. For example, the repeated emphasis on the difficulties with the trace norm compared to the l1 norm.

**Specific Points:**

*   In the proof of Theorem 3.1, the jump from σκ2 ||Xk − X̃k||F to a contradiction is insufficiently detailed.
*   Proposition 4.2 needs a more rigorous justification for its claim about simultaneous singular value decomposition.
*   The case analysis in Section 4.1.2 is confusing and lacks a clear explanation of how it leads to the desired contradiction.
*   The convergence analysis in the numerical experiments section lacks a proper quantification of the convergence rate.

**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   The result is novel and addresses an important theoretical question.
*   The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The lack of detail and rigor in the proofs raises concerns about their correctness.
*   The numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality of the paper.
*   The limited detail in the experimental section limits the ability to assess the practical impact of the results.
*   The paper feels incomplete and could benefit from significant revisions.

**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, the lack of meticulousness in the presentation and the insufficient detail in the proofs lead me to recommend **rejection**. The numerous minor flaws, combined with the significant concerns about the rigor of the analysis, outweigh the novelty of the result. A major revision addressing the points raised above, with a significantly more detailed and rigorous presentation of the proofs and experimental results, would be necessary for reconsideration. The authors need to demonstrate an uncompromising commitment to precision and clarity before this work can be considered suitable for NIPS.
