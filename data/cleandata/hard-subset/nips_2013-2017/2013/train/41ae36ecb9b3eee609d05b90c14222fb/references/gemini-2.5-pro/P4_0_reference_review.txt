### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. The primary contribution is establishing a linear rate of convergence under a specific structural assumption on the loss function `f`, namely that `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function. This result is significant because it does not require the overall loss function `f` to be strongly convex, which is the standard assumption for proving linear convergence of first-order methods. Loss functions of this form are common in machine learning applications such as matrix completion (with square loss) and matrix classification (with logistic loss), where `f` is often not strongly convex.

The key technical innovation is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This error bound relates the distance from a candidate solution `X` to the optimal set `X*` to the norm of a residual map `‖R(X)‖F`, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm has been an open problem, as prior techniques developed for l1-norm or group LASSO regularization, which rely on the polyhedral structure of the regularizer's epigraph, are not applicable here. The authors overcome this by analyzing the spectral properties of the solutions. They make a crucial observation that an optimal solution and its corresponding negative gradient share the same singular vectors. This insight allows them to use matrix perturbation theory to analyze points near the optimum and subsequently establish the error bound. This error bound, in conjunction with the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993), directly leads to the proof of R-linear convergence of the iterates and Q-linear convergence of the objective values. The paper supports its theoretical findings with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks, which visually demonstrate the predicted linear convergence behavior.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper appears to be technically sound. The main theoretical claims (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are well-supported by a detailed proof structure. The argument is rigorous and builds upon established frameworks while introducing novel elements to handle the specific challenges of the trace norm. The proof of the main error bound via a contradiction argument is intricate and relies on a careful spectral decomposition (Propositions 4.2 and 4.3), which seems to be the core technical insight. The assumptions on the function `h` (Assumption 2.1) are clearly stated and justified for common loss functions. The work is a complete piece of research, presenting a novel theoretical result and validating it with numerical experiments.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly situating the work within the existing literature on first-order methods for trace norm minimization, highlighting the gap concerning linear convergence without strong convexity. The problem setup and main results are presented before the technical proofs, which improves readability. The logical flow of the argument—from motivating the need for an error bound, to stating the bound, to using it to prove linear convergence, and finally to proving the bound itself—is clear and effective. The notation is standard and used consistently.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first result to establish linear convergence of the standard PGM for trace norm regularization without assuming strong convexity of the loss function. The primary novelty lies in the proof of the Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1). While error bound analysis is a known technique for proving linear convergence, its application to the trace norm regularizer is new and required a non-trivial departure from existing proof strategies used for l1-type regularizers. The technique of leveraging the shared singular vector structure to enable a matrix perturbation analysis is a clever and powerful contribution. The paper adequately references related work and clearly articulates how its contributions differ from and advance upon previous results.

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant for both the optimization and machine learning communities. Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone of low-rank matrix recovery, and the Proximal Gradient Method is one of the most widely used algorithms for solving such problems. This paper provides a formal theoretical justification for the fast convergence of PGM that is often observed in practice but was not previously explained by theory for non-strongly convex objectives. The error bound itself is a tool of independent interest that could be used to analyze other algorithms or aspects of trace norm regularized problems. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale low-rank matrix problems are likely to use and build upon these ideas.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Proof Self-Containment:** The proof of the error bound presented in Section 4 is quite dense, and several key steps, including the proof of Proposition 4.3 and the derivation of inequalities (9)-(11), are deferred to the supplementary material. While understandable due to space constraints, this makes it challenging for a reader to fully verify the technical correctness from the main paper alone. The main paper would benefit if a more detailed intuition or proof sketch for these deferred results could be included.

2.  **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments effectively illustrate the paper's theoretical claims, showing a clear linear convergence phase. However, the experiments are somewhat limited. They do not include comparisons against other state-of-the-art solvers for matrix completion or classification. While the paper's focus is theoretical, a comparison could have provided context on the practical implications of the proven linear convergence rate versus algorithms that may have different theoretical guarantees but strong empirical performance.

### **Arguments for/against acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper solves an important open problem by establishing the linear convergence of PGM for a broad and practical class of trace norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity.
2.  **Novel Technical Tool:** It introduces a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, a result of independent interest with a novel proof technique based on spectral analysis.
3.  **High Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** The main paper is not fully self-contained in its technical arguments, making a complete review of the proofs difficult without the supplement.
2.  **Limited Experiments:** The empirical validation is illustrative rather than comprehensive and does not benchmark against other methods.

In summary, the strengths of this paper, particularly its theoretical novelty and significance, substantially outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a solid and important contribution to the literature on large-scale optimization and machine learning.
