

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

This paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization: under what conditions does the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieve linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems, without requiring the loss function to be strongly convex? The authors establish that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties, the PGM exhibits linear convergence. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves an open question about whether such bounds exist for trace norm (unlike the well-established results for ℓ₁-norm or group LASSO regularization).

The paper builds on the important work of Luo and Tseng [10] on error bounds for composite optimization, but makes a crucial advancement by developing a new error bound specifically for trace norm regularization, which has a non-polyhedral epigraph. This distinguishes it from previous error bound results that relied on polyhedral structure or splitting techniques that don't apply to trace norm.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution and Novelty**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm regularization that does not require strong convexity of the loss function. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is the core innovation, and the authors provide a careful, detailed proof that overcomes the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm. The key insight about the simultaneous singular value decomposition of the optimal solution and the gradient (Proposition 4.2) is particularly elegant.

2. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous proofs for both the error bound and the linear convergence result. The authors carefully address the technical challenges of working with trace norm (unlike ℓ₁-norm, where variables can be decomposed into independent groups). The numerical experiments in Section 5 corroborate the theoretical claims by demonstrating linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and matrix classification problems.

3. **Relevance to Machine Learning**: The assumptions on the loss function are well-justified for important machine learning applications like matrix completion (square loss) and multi-task learning (logistic loss), making the result immediately applicable to real-world problems.

4. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear section organization and consistent notation. The motivation and problem setup are well-explained, and the technical contributions are presented in a logical sequence.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The main result applies only to loss functions of the specific form f(X) = h(A(X)). While the authors justify this covers many important cases, it excludes more general loss functions that might arise in practice. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of this assumption.

2. **Practical Implementation Considerations**: The paper assumes the Lipschitz constant Lf is known (for choosing step sizes), but in practice, computing Lf for large-scale problems can be challenging. The authors mention using backtracking line search in the experiments, but this is not incorporated into the theoretical analysis, leaving a gap between theory and practice.

3. **Limited Numerical Experiments**: While the numerical experiments support the theoretical claims, they are limited to just two specific problems (matrix completion and matrix classification). More extensive experiments on different trace norm-regularized problems would strengthen the practical relevance of the results.

4. **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Methods**: The paper doesn't compare the PGM with alternative approaches (e.g., alternating minimization, ADMM, or accelerated variants) for trace norm regularization. A discussion of when the PGM would be preferred over other methods would add practical value.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the optimization literature for trace norm regularization. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution that advances our understanding of the convergence properties of first-order methods for this important class of problems. The technical arguments are sound, the proofs are rigorous, and the numerical experiments support the theoretical claims.

The main limitations are the restrictive assumptions on the loss function and the limited scope of the numerical experiments. However, these are reasonable limitations given the technical difficulty of the problem, and the paper clearly articulates the scope of its contribution.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances the understanding of first-order optimization methods for trace norm regularization. The result is novel, technically sound, and addresses an important open question. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, the authors clearly state the scope of their contribution and justify why their assumptions cover many important machine learning applications. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides a valuable tool (the error bound) that could be of independent interest to the optimization community.
