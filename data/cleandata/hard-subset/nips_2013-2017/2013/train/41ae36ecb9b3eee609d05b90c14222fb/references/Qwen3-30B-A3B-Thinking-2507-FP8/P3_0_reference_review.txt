

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a fundamental question in convex optimization: under what conditions can the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieve linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and A linear. While PGM is known to achieve sublinear convergence (O(1/k)) for general convex f, and linear convergence (O(c^k)) when f is strongly convex, the authors establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f. The key technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which they prove by analyzing the spectral structure of the optimal solution set using matrix perturbation theory.

This work addresses an important open question in the optimization community. Previous error bounds for composite optimization typically required the non-smooth part to have a polyhedral epigraph (e.g., ℓ1-norm), but the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph made this question unresolved despite its similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization. The authors provide a novel approach that overcomes the challenges of the trace norm's spectral structure.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. This resolves an important open question in the field, as acknowledged by the authors, and enables the application of the Luo-Tseng convergence framework to prove linear convergence without strong convexity of f.

2. **Technical Innovation**: The key insight in Proposition 4.2 (that optimal solutions and -∇f share singular vectors) and the subsequent spectral analysis in Lemma 4.1(c) are elegant and novel. The proof technique using matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimal set is particularly impressive and addresses the core difficulty of trace norm's non-polyhedral nature.

3. **Applicability**: The authors successfully demonstrate that their framework covers a wide range of important machine learning problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification) without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, which is often violated in practice.

4. **Rigor and Completeness**: The paper is exceptionally rigorous, with careful definitions, clear assumptions, and complete proofs (with supplementary material for key technical details). The numerical experiments complement the theory by demonstrating linear convergence in practice.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumption**: The requirement that f(X) = h(A(X)) is quite specific. While it covers many important applications, it excludes loss functions that cannot be expressed in this form (e.g., some structured losses or non-linear measurements). The paper does not adequately discuss how restrictive this assumption is or whether it can be relaxed. For instance, could the result be extended to f(X) = h(A(X)) + g(X) where g is another convex function?

2. **Limited Numerical Experiments**: The experiments (matrix completion and matrix classification) only show linear convergence in the asymptotic regime (after iterates are close to the optimal set). They don't provide a thorough comparison with alternative methods or analyze how the convergence rate depends on problem parameters (e.g., rank, dimension, sampling ratio). A more comprehensive experimental section would strengthen the practical impact of the theoretical contribution.

3. **Clarity of Key Insight**: While the proof is technically sound, the paper could better explain why the standard error bound techniques for ℓ1-norm (which rely on variable splitting) fail for trace norm. The paper states this is because "each singular value depends on every single entry," but a more intuitive explanation would help readers understand the fundamental difference between ℓ1 and trace norm in this context.

4. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper cites relevant work but could better position itself against recent advances in trace norm optimization. For example, the work by Toh and Yun (2010) on accelerated proximal gradient methods for nuclear norm problems, or the more recent work by Zhang et al. (2013) on linear convergence for non-strongly convex problems, could be more thoroughly discussed.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization for matrix problems. The establishment of a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization is a significant advance that resolves an important open question. The authors provide a rigorous proof using novel techniques that overcome the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm.

The main weaknesses are the restrictive assumption on the form of f and the limited scope of the numerical experiments. However, these do not undermine the theoretical contribution, which is both novel and well-supported. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the understanding of convergence rates for trace norm regularization.

The significance of this work is high, as trace norm regularization is widely used in machine learning applications, and the linear convergence result without strong convexity assumptions expands the applicability of efficient optimization methods to a broader class of problems.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is strong, novel, and well-justified. While the restrictive assumption on the loss function and limited numerical experiments are weaknesses, they do not detract from the paper's main contribution, which is a significant step forward in understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field that will be of interest to both optimization and machine learning researchers.

For future work, the authors could explore whether their error bound can be extended to more general matrix norm regularization or to problems where f is not of the form h(A(X)), which would further broaden the applicability of their result.
