

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a theoretically important question: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization can achieve linear convergence without requiring the loss function to be strongly convex. The authors claim to establish the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems that does not require strong convexity of the loss function f, which is significant because many applications (e.g., matrix completion) use loss functions that are not strongly convex.

The main contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which the authors prove using matrix perturbation theory. They demonstrate that when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h satisfying certain convexity and smoothness properties, the PGM achieves linear convergence. The proof is technically sophisticated, particularly in establishing the restricted invertibility property needed for the error bound, which overcomes the challenge that the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph unlike the ℓ1-norm.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a theoretically significant contribution by resolving an open question about linear convergence for trace norm regularization without strong convexity assumptions.
2. The error bound proof is novel and technically sound, with the authors correctly identifying the key challenge (non-polyhedral epigraph) and developing appropriate techniques using matrix perturbation theory.
3. The result has clear applicability to important machine learning problems like matrix completion and classification, where the loss functions are not strongly convex.
4. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and the mathematical presentation is rigorous.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The numerical experiments are insufficient to support the theoretical claims. The authors present semi-log plots showing convergence for two problems, but fail to clearly demonstrate the linear convergence rate (a straight line on a semi-log plot). The paper claims "linear convergence" but doesn't provide the convergence rate constant or explicitly show the straight-line behavior that would confirm it.
2. There is no comparison with other methods or discussion of how much faster linear convergence is compared to the known sublinear O(1/k) or O(1/k²) rates. Without such comparison, it's difficult to assess the practical significance of the result.
3. The paper lacks discussion of practical implications. How many iterations would be saved by linear convergence in real applications? What is the computational cost of verifying the conditions for linear convergence?
4. The claim that this is "the first linear convergence result" for PGM on trace norm regularization without strong convexity of f appears accurate based on the cited literature, but the paper could better position this contribution against related work on error bounds for other norms.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a technically sound theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature on trace norm regularization. The proof of the error bound is novel and well-executed, and the result has clear implications for machine learning applications. However, the paper's weaknesses are significant for a NIPS submission: the numerical validation is inadequate to support the theoretical claims, and the practical significance is not sufficiently explored.

While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the lack of proper numerical demonstration of the linear convergence rate and insufficient discussion of practical implications weaken the paper's impact. The authors should provide more concrete evidence of the linear convergence behavior (e.g., showing the convergence rate constant) and compare the practical speedup over existing methods.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject. The paper presents a solid theoretical advance but fails to provide adequate experimental validation of the claimed linear convergence behavior. The current numerical results do not sufficiently support the main theoretical claim, and the paper lacks discussion of practical implications that would make the result more impactful for the machine learning community. For a NIPS submission, the experimental component should be strengthened to properly validate the theoretical claims.
