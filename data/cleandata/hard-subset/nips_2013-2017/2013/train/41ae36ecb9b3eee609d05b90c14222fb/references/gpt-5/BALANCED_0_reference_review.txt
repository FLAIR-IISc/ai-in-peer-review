Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the composite convex problem minX F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗, focusing on the popular proximal gradient method (PGM) with the proximal operator of the nuclear norm. The key setting is f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient and is strictly convex (indeed, strongly convex on compact subsets) over its domain. This covers standard losses such as squared and logistic loss used in matrix completion, matrix classification, and multitask learning, even when f is not strongly convex because A has a nontrivial nullspace.

The main contribution is an asymptotic linear convergence result for PGM without assuming strong convexity of f. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound: dist(X, X∗) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F for small residuals and bounded level sets, where R(X) is the proximal residual Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. This error bound, together with the Luo–Tseng analysis framework [10], yields R-linear convergence of iterates and Q-linear convergence of objective values for step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf).

Prior error bounds for non-smooth composite problems were known for polyhedral regularizers such as ℓ1 and (group) LASSO [23,25] but were open for the nuclear norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph. The paper’s technical novelty is to prove the needed error bound for trace-norm regularization by exploiting a spectral structure: at an optimum X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors (Proposition 4.2). This enables a careful perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” property (Lemma 4.1(c)) that connects distances in X-space to deviations in A(X). The paper concludes with small-scale experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification illustrating linear convergence in the asymptotic regime.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The high-level proof strategy is standard and appropriate: prove a local error bound and then invoke the Luo–Tseng machinery to obtain linear rates. The core technical contribution—simultaneous SVD at the optimum and the ensuing case-based perturbation analysis—appears plausible and addresses the main difficulty caused by the non-polyhedral nuclear norm. The presentation of Lemma 4.1 and the contradiction-based proof of the error bound are coherent.
- Assumptions: The assumptions on h (C1 with Lipschitz ∇h; locally strongly convex on compact sets) are reasonable for squared and logistic losses. The non-emptiness of the solution set and bounded level sets are standard. The step-size condition αk < 1/Lf is also standard; Lf could be stated explicitly as L‖A‖2.
- Scope of the result: The error bound is local (requires small residual and bounded level sets), so the linear rate is asymptotic; this is clearly stated. Constants are not explicit.
- Experiments: The experiments qualitatively corroborate the theory but are small-scale, with limited diagnostics.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized. The motivating discussion of why nuclear norm differs from ℓ1 (non-polyhedral epigraph; global coupling via singular values) is clear. The notation is heavy in Section 4; the argument would benefit from moving some critical technical steps (e.g., a more detailed proof of Proposition 4.2 and Proposition 4.3, especially handling multiplicities) from the supplement into the main text. There are a few minor inconsistencies/typos (see Suggestions).

Originality
The work appears to provide the first linear convergence result for PGM on nuclear-norm regularization without assuming global strong convexity of f, by establishing a new error bound specific to the trace norm. While linear convergence of PGM under error bounds/quadratic growth is known abstractly (e.g., Luo–Tseng), the novelty here is in proving that the required error bound holds for this important non-polyhedral regularizer. The spectral alignment insight and the restricted invertibility lemma look new and nontrivial.

Significance
This is a meaningful theoretical advance. Nuclear norm regularization is ubiquitous in ML. The result explains the often-observed linear convergence of PGM in practice for square/logistic losses even when f is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion), and it broadens the class of problems where one can expect linear rates. The error bound technique may be of independent interest and potentially extendable to other spectral regularizers.

Weaknesses and limitations
- The linear rate is local/asymptotic; no global linear rate or explicit attraction region/constant estimates are provided.
- Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity on compact subsets) is key; the paper could better articulate how to verify it in practice for losses like logistic (i.e., via boundedness of A(X) along the iterates/level sets).
- Some technical parts (e.g., handling equal singular values, uniqueness of projections onto Xc, and stability of the simultaneous SVD) are relegated to the supplement; more detail in the main text would aid reproducibility.
- Experiments are limited in scale and scope; no quantitative rate constants or comparisons (e.g., against accelerated variants with restart) are reported.
- Minor inconsistency: in the matrix completion experiment, α is set to 1; if Lf = ‖A‖2L = 1, theory requires α < 1/Lf, not equal. Also τ vs μ notation should be unified, and figure numbering seems off.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the relationship to broader error-bound/Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz/quadratic growth frameworks and discuss whether your proof implies a KL exponent of 1/2 in this setting.
- Provide explicit expressions or bounds for Lf and the constants κi in Theorem 3.2 in terms of L and ‖A‖, if possible, or at least a discussion of their dependence.
- Strengthen Proposition 4.2’s proof in the main text, particularly addressing singular value multiplicities and the stability of the shared singular subspaces under perturbation.
- Expand experiments: larger n, report measured linear rate constants, and compare vanilla PGM to (i) backtracking PG, (ii) accelerated PG with restart, and (iii) algorithms specialized for nuclear norm problems. Evaluate robustness to different τ and sampling ratios.
- Fix minor issues: unify τ/μ notation; ensure αk < 1/Lf in experiments (or use backtracking); correct figure labels; consider a relative stopping criterion.

Pros
- Addresses an important open gap: linear convergence of PGM with nuclear norm regularization beyond strong convexity.
- Technically nontrivial error bound leveraging spectral structure; potentially reusable elsewhere.
- Applicable to widely used losses (square, logistic) and linear measurement models.
- Clear linkage to Luo–Tseng framework; proofs are mostly self-contained.

Cons
- Only asymptotic/local linear rates; constants and attraction region not quantified.
- Experimental validation is minimal and has minor inconsistencies.
- Some technical details crucial to the spectral arguments are deferred.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is solid and novel for a widely used regularizer, fitting well within NeurIPS’s optimization-and-ML scope. Strengthening clarity and experiments as suggested would make the paper more compelling.
