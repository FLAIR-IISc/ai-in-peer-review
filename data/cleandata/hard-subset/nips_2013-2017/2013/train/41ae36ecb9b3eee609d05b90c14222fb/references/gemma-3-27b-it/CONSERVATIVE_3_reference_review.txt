This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization, a common technique for low-rank matrix recovery. The authors aim to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, a limitation of existing results. They achieve this by deriving a novel Lipschitzian error bound specific to trace norm regularization and leveraging established convergence analysis techniques. The problem is well-motivated, and the potential impact of removing the strong convexity assumption is significant, given the prevalence of non-strongly convex loss functions in applications like matrix completion.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its theoretical contribution: the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. Establishing this bound, particularly given the differences between trace norm and ℓ1 regularization, is a non-trivial achievement. The connection to the work of Luo and Tseng [10] is clearly articulated, and the proof structure appears sound, although the full details are relegated to the supplementary material. The inclusion of numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence in matrix completion and classification settings provides further support for the theoretical results.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the motivation is clear, the presentation is somewhat dense and lacks sufficient intuition. The introduction, while thorough in its survey of applications, could benefit from a more focused discussion of *why* establishing linear convergence is important beyond simply noting that strong convexity is often absent. The authors state that their result is the “first linear convergence result…that does not require the strong convexity of f,” but a more detailed comparison to existing work and a clear explanation of where previous approaches fall short would strengthen this claim.

The numerical experiments, while present, are limited. The experiments are somewhat cursory and lack a comprehensive comparison to other algorithms (e.g., accelerated PGM, alternating direction method of multipliers). The choice of parameters (e.g., step size) seems arbitrary; a justification or sensitivity analysis is missing.  Furthermore, the observed linear convergence is presented graphically but lacks quantitative analysis (e.g., reporting the convergence rate ρ).  The experimental section feels more like a demonstration of feasibility than a rigorous validation.

The theoretical contributions rely heavily on Proposition 4.2, which states that optimal solutions have the same left and right singular vectors. While this proposition simplifies the analysis, its justification feels insufficient. The authors mention it’s non-trivial, but the detailed explanation lacks depth, and a more complete proof would inspire greater confidence. The reliance on matrix perturbation theory is mentioned but not elaborated on, leaving the reader to trust the authors’ expertise without understanding the intricacies.

Finally, the writing, while technically correct, is often verbose and could benefit from streamlining. The repetition of phrases and ideas adds to the density.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization.
*   Removes the strong convexity assumption from linear convergence results, broadening applicability.
*   Numerical experiments demonstrate feasibility of the proposed approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited experimental validation and lack of comprehensive comparisons.
*   Insufficient justification for key theoretical components (Proposition 4.2).
*   Dense and verbose writing, hindering clarity.
*   The significance, while potentially high, isn't convincingly demonstrated through thorough empirical evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a technically sound theoretical result that addresses an important open question in the field of trace norm regularization. However, the lack of thorough experimental validation, the insufficient justification of certain key propositions, and the dense writing style detract from its overall impact. While the theoretical contribution is significant, I am hesitant to strongly recommend acceptance in its current form. I believe the paper would benefit substantially from a more rigorous experimental evaluation and a clearer, more intuitive presentation of the key theoretical arguments.

**Recommendation:** Weak Reject.  I recommend rejection but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit to a future conference.
