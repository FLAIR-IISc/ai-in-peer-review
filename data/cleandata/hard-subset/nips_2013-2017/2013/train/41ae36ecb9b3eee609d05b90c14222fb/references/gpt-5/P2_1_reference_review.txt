Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f has the structure f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, with Lipschitz gradient and locally (on any compact convex subset) strongly convex. The main contributions are:
- A local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear-norm regularized problem: in a bounded level set and sufficiently close to optimality, dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||R(X)||F, where R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X is the residual map.
- As a consequence (via the Luo–Tseng framework), asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with fixed step sizes 0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf: objective values converge Q-linearly and iterates converge R-linearly to the solution set, without assuming strong convexity of f.
The technical novelty is establishing the error bound for the nonpolyhedral nuclear norm (in contrast to known results for ℓ1/group LASSO where polyhedrality is crucial). The key observation is that at optimality X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors; this alignment plus matrix perturbation yields a “restricted invertibility” relation between distances in X-space and A(X)-space near X*. The paper connects to a substantial literature: accelerated/PGM for nuclear norm (e.g., Ji & Ye; Ma et al.; Toh & Yun), linear rates under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al.), and error bounds/Luo–Tseng analysis for polyhedral regularizers (Tseng, Zhang et al.). To my knowledge, a rigorous error bound specifically for nuclear norm had been open; this paper addresses it.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements are precise, assumptions are explicit, and the proof strategy is appropriate. The decomposition results (Prop. 4.2–4.3) and Lemma 4.1’s restricted invertibility are the crux; the arguments leverage spectral structure cleverly. The use of bounded level sets to invoke local strong convexity of h is standard and justified by Assumption 2.1(b). The linear convergence proof follows a well-established recipe once the error bound is available.
- Completeness: The core theorems are proved, with several technical parts deferred to the supplement. Inequalities (9)–(11) and proofs of Propositions 4.2–4.3 are cited as supplementary; while acceptable for space, the main text provides enough scaffolding to assess plausibility.
- Empirics: Experiments are small-scale and illustrative, confirming the asymptotic linear phase but not probing constants or scalability. No comparisons to alternative solvers or accelerated variants are provided. This is adequate for a primarily theoretical contribution, but stronger empirical support would help.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized: setup and assumptions, residual map, statement of main results, and a clear proof roadmap. The link between the error bound and linear convergence via Luo–Tseng is transparent.
- Some sections are dense; adding intuition around Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous SVD at optimality) and a brief worked toy example would improve readability. Clarify the role of step size in defining the residual (R uses unit step, but PGM uses αk) and how constants κ1–κ3 bridge this gap (currently relegated to the supplement).
- Minor issues: a few references lack years; a couple of typos (e.g., “trace norm1”).

Originality
- High. Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization despite its nonpolyhedral epigraph is a novel and nontrivial contribution. The spectral-alignment-based analysis is original relative to prior error-bound proofs that rely on separability/polyhedrality.
- The extension to a broad class f(X) = h(A(X)) (including square and logistic losses) without strong convexity of f increases novelty and scope.

Significance
- Theoretical significance is high: error bounds underpin modern convergence analyses, and this result closes a gap for a ubiquitous regularizer in machine learning. The asymptotic linear rate aligns with empirical observations and provides guarantees practitioners can rely on.
- Practical impact is moderate-to-high: while constants are existential and rates are asymptotic, the result justifies using simple PGM locally. Extensions to accelerated methods (e.g., linear rates under an error bound) seem within reach; discussing this would enhance impact.
- The restricted invertibility lemma and spectral arguments may be reusable for other spectral regularizers, suggesting broader methodological value.

Pros
- Resolves an open question: local error bound for nuclear norm regularization.
- Delivers linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f under broad, relevant assumptions.
- Technically deep and careful; leverages insightful spectral structure at optimality.
- Applicable to many ML problems (matrix completion, multitask/multilabel, logistic models).

Cons
- Linear convergence is asymptotic and local; no global rate or explicit constants.
- Experiments are minimal (small n, no baselines, no study of constants or step-size strategies).
- Key technical proofs are deferred; the main text could offer more intuition.
- No discussion of inexact proximal steps (partial SVD) commonly used at scale, or of accelerated methods.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can the error bound and linear-rate result be extended to FISTA or to inexact proximal steps (e.g., truncated SVD), perhaps under summable errors?
- Please clarify in the main text how the residual with unit step relates to the algorithmic step size αk; include a brief derivation of (9)–(11).
- Provide at least one larger-scale experiment and a comparison to an accelerated method or ADMM to contextualize practical benefits.
- Comment on potential extensions to other spectral penalties (e.g., Schatten p-norms) or structured nuclear-norm variants.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong theoretical paper that advances our understanding of first-order methods for nuclear-norm regularization by establishing a key error bound and deriving asymptotic linear convergence of PGM under broad and relevant conditions. Despite modest experiments and some deferred proofs, the contribution is original, technically sound, and significant for the NeurIPS community. I recommend acceptance after minor clarifications.
