This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems, specifically establishing linear convergence under fairly general conditions on the loss function. This is a valuable contribution, as existing linear convergence results typically require strong convexity of the loss, which is often not met in practical applications like matrix completion. The core novelty lies in establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, a challenging task due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The authors cleverly leverage matrix perturbation theory, building on the observation that optimal solutions share singular vectors, to overcome this hurdle.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result *without* strong convexity of the loss function is genuinely novel and fills a significant gap in the literature. This is the paper's strongest asset.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous, building on established techniques (Luo-Tseng framework) and carefully addressing the specific challenges posed by the trace norm. The use of matrix perturbation theory is elegant and effective.
*   **Significance:** The result has clear implications for the analysis and practical application of PGM in a wide range of matrix recovery tasks, particularly where strong convexity doesn’t hold (matrix completion, low-rank matrix estimation).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the assumptions are explicitly stated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Experimental Validation:** While the numerical experiments are encouraging, they feel somewhat limited. The settings are standard, but the focus is primarily on *demonstrating* linear convergence (as expected) rather than rigorously comparing performance to other methods or exploring sensitivity to parameter settings.  More detailed analysis of the relationship between the theory and the empirical observations would be beneficial. For example, how well does the empirically observed convergence rate align with theoretical predictions?
*   **Limited Scope of Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b) requires strong convexity on *compact* subsets of the domain. While reasonable, this isn’t always easily verifiable in practice. The impact of relaxing this assumption (or providing conditions under which it holds) could be explored.
*   **Proof Length and Detail:** The proof, particularly of Lemma 4.1, is complex and relies on a large number of propositions. While detailed, it might benefit from further streamlining or more explicit connections between key steps.  Readers unfamiliar with the background literature may find the proof challenging to follow.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   The established linear convergence result significantly expands the applicability of PGM to a broader class of problems.
*   The technical contributions (particularly the error bound) are substantial and potentially useful for future research.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is somewhat limited and could be strengthened with more comprehensive analysis.
*   The technical details and proof complexity might pose a barrier to entry for some readers.
*   While significant, the improvement is primarily theoretical; a more substantial empirical demonstration of practical benefits could further bolster the paper's impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While the experimental section could be more comprehensive and the technical details are demanding, the novelty and potential impact of the results outweigh these concerns.  The paper clearly advances the state of the art and addresses an important open question. I believe it warrants acceptance to NIPS. I would encourage the authors to consider expanding the experimental section and potentially simplifying the proof presentation where possible in a final version.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
