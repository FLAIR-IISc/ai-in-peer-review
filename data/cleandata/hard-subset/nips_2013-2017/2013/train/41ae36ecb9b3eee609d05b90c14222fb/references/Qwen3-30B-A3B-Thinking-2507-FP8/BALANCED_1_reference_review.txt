

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) for a linear operator A and a function h satisfying certain conditions. The main contribution is establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables proving linear convergence of PGM without requiring strong convexity of f - a significant gap in the literature.

**Strengths:**

1. **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by resolving an open question about Lipschitzian error bounds for trace norm regularization. The key insight about the shared singular vectors between optimal solutions and the gradient of the loss function (Proposition 4.2) is novel and elegantly leveraged. This is a significant advance over prior work on error bounds for composite optimization, which had established results only for polyhedral regularizers like ℓ1-norm or group LASSO.

2. **Theoretical Soundness:** The proofs are rigorous and well-structured, with careful attention to technical details. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) and its application to establish linear convergence (Theorem 3.2) are theoretically sound and address a clear limitation in the literature regarding the applicability of linear convergence results.

3. **Significance:** The results apply to many important machine learning problems including matrix completion and multi-task learning where strong convexity of f is often not satisfied. The paper demonstrates the practical relevance through numerical experiments on these problems, showing linear convergence behavior in the asymptotic regime.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and good exposition of the key technical ideas. The proof structure is well-motivated, and the relationship between the error bound and convergence analysis is clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Loss Functions:** While the paper covers a broad class of loss functions (square loss, logistic loss, etc.), the requirement that f(X) = h(A(X)) excludes many common loss functions used in machine learning applications. For example, loss functions that depend on the entire matrix in non-linear ways (e.g., certain forms of logistic regression where the loss depends on the full matrix output) are not covered. The authors should clarify the limitations of their framework.

2. **Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while supportive of the theoretical claims, are somewhat limited. The paper shows linear convergence in the asymptotic regime but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with alternative methods or a detailed analysis of how convergence rates depend on problem parameters. More comprehensive experiments would strengthen the practical relevance of the results.

3. **Practical Implementation Considerations:** The paper doesn't address how the theoretical results translate to practical implementation. For instance, it doesn't discuss how to estimate the constants in the error bound (η and ε in Theorem 3.1) or how to choose step sizes in practice. This is a significant gap for practitioners.

4. **Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper references prior work on error bounds, it could more clearly distinguish its contribution from existing results on error bounds for composite optimization (e.g., [22, 23, 25]). A more detailed comparison would help situate the paper's contribution more precisely.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes an important advance in understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature and provides a novel proof technique that could be applicable to other matrix norm regularization problems. The theoretical results are sound, well-justified, and have clear implications for machine learning applications.

However, the scope of loss functions covered is somewhat limited, and the practical implementation considerations are underdeveloped. The numerical experiments are supportive but not comprehensive enough to fully demonstrate the practical advantages of the method.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed, and the paper makes a valuable addition to the literature on optimization for machine learning. The authors should address the following points in revision:

1. Expand the discussion of the limitations of the loss function form f(X) = h(A(X)), and clarify which common loss functions are excluded.
2. Provide more comprehensive numerical experiments comparing convergence rates with alternative methods and analyzing the effect of different problem parameters.
3. Add a section discussing practical implementation considerations, including how to estimate the constants in the error bound and how to choose step sizes.

The paper is technically sound, makes a significant contribution to the field, and meets all the criteria for a NIPS paper. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable and do not undermine the paper's core contribution.
