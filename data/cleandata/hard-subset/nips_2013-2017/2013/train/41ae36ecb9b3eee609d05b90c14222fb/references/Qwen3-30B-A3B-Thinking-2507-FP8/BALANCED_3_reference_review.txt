

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the convergence theory of proximal gradient methods for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish a linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h satisfying certain smoothness and strong convexity conditions. This is important because previous linear convergence results for such problems required strong convexity of f, which does not hold in many machine learning applications (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A). The key contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound showing that dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖R(X)‖_F, where R(X) = S_τ(X - ∇f(X)) - X is the residual map. This resolves an open question about whether such an error bound holds for trace norm regularization, which differs from ℓ1-norm regularization due to its non-polyhedral epigraph.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by establishing a new error bound for trace norm regularization, which is the first of its kind for this problem. The proof is technically sophisticated, particularly the analysis of singular vectors in Proposition 4.2 and the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound. This is a significant advance over prior work that only established error bounds for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization.

2. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, clearly state the assumptions, and carefully motivate each step of the analysis. The theoretical development is logically presented, and the numerical experiments complement the theoretical claims.

3. **Significance:** The result has important implications for machine learning applications where trace norm regularization is used but strong convexity is not satisfied (e.g., matrix completion). The error bound itself could be of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization.

4. **Empirical Support:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice, supporting the theoretical claims. The results are presented clearly with appropriate visualizations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Assumption Restrictiveness:** The paper's main result requires f to be of the form h(A(X)), which may limit its applicability. While the authors provide examples where this form is satisfied (matrix completion, multi-task learning), it would be helpful to more thoroughly discuss how general this class of functions is and whether the assumption can be relaxed. For instance, it's unclear whether the result extends to loss functions like the square loss with multiple measurements (e.g., f(X) = ∑_i ‖A_i(X) - b_i‖^2/2).

2. **Comparison to Related Work:** The paper does not sufficiently compare its results to Zhang et al. (2013) "On the Linear Convergence of a Proximal Gradient Method for a Class of Nonsmooth Convex Minimization Problems," which appears to establish similar linear convergence results for other regularizers. The paper should clarify how this work differs from and extends Zhang et al.'s results.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope:** The numerical experiments are restricted to two specific applications (matrix completion and matrix classification). More diverse experiments across different trace norm-regularized problems would strengthen the empirical support for the theoretical claims.

4. **Proof Explanation:** Proposition 4.2 (which is central to the proof) is stated without sufficient intuition. A more intuitive explanation of why X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors would make the proof more accessible and strengthen the argument.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the optimization literature for trace norm-regularized problems. The main result is novel, technically sound, and addresses an important open question. The paper is well-written and provides meaningful empirical validation. However, the assumption that f must take the form h(A(X)) may limit the practical applicability of the results, and the comparison to related work could be strengthened.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper, as it makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The paper is of high quality and addresses a well-motivated problem. With minor revisions, it could be even stronger.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

1. Expand the discussion of the assumption f(X) = h(A(X)) to better explain its generality and potential limitations.

2. Provide a clearer comparison to Zhang et al. (2013) and other relevant work on linear convergence of proximal gradient methods.

3. Include additional numerical experiments across more diverse trace norm-regularized problems to better support the theoretical claims.

4. Add more intuitive explanation for Proposition 4.2 to make the proof more accessible.

5. Consider discussing practical implementation details, such as how to estimate the Lipschitz constant L_f for the gradient of f in specific applications.

This paper makes an important contribution to the understanding of convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm-regularized problems, and the results should be of interest to the NIPS community.
