

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization for low-rank matrix recovery: under what conditions can we establish linear convergence for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors make a significant contribution by proving a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves an open question that has persisted despite the apparent similarity between trace norm and ℓ1-norm regularization.

The paper's main novelty lies in their key insight that optimal solutions X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same left and right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2). This observation allows them to overcome the challenges of the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm, which had prevented previous approaches (used for ℓ1-norm and group LASSO) from being directly applicable. Their proof technique, which leverages matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure near the optimal solution set, is both elegant and novel.

The authors establish that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h satisfying appropriate smoothness and curvature properties (Assumption 2.1), the PGM achieves linear convergence (Theorem 3.2). This substantially broadens the applicability of linear convergence results beyond the strong convexity requirement previously needed. Their result covers important machine learning applications like matrix completion (with square loss) and matrix classification (with logistic loss), which typically do not satisfy strong convexity.

The paper's technical contribution is substantial. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is the first of its kind for trace norm regularization, and its proof requires careful handling of the spectral properties of matrices, which is a non-trivial extension of existing error bound techniques. The authors also provide numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification that empirically verify the linear convergence rate, which complements their theoretical results.

I find this paper to be highly novel and significant. It addresses a gap in the literature that has been recognized but unresolved for some time, and it does so with a clean theoretical contribution that could inspire further work on error bounds for other matrix norm regularizations. The result has immediate practical implications for practitioners using trace norm regularization, as it provides stronger theoretical guarantees for the PGM in important application domains.

The paper is well-written, with clear organization and good exposition of the key ideas. The authors effectively contrast their approach with previous work on error bounds for other regularizations (e.g., ℓ1-norm and group LASSO), making it clear why their approach is necessary and novel.

Minor weaknesses include:
1. The paper only considers loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), which, while covering many important cases, doesn't encompass all possible loss functions in trace norm regularization.
2. The numerical experiments, while supportive, are limited to just two applications (matrix completion and matrix classification).
3. The step size requirements (Theorem 3.2) are somewhat restrictive in practice, though the authors note they can use backtracking line search.

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find these limitations to be minor in the context of the paper's significant contribution. The main technical insight is genuinely novel and addresses a fundamental open question in the field. The paper represents a clear step forward in understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization.

This work is clearly within the scope of NIPS, addressing an important theoretical question with practical implications for machine learning. It advances the state of the art in optimization for structured matrix problems, and the error bound technique developed could be applied to other matrix norm regularization problems.

In conclusion, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution with significant practical implications. The novelty of the error bound technique and its resolution of an open question outweigh the minor limitations in scope and experimental validation. I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS.

Strengths:
- Novel resolution of an open question in optimization theory
- Elegant proof technique using matrix perturbation theory
- Addresses a fundamental limitation in current convergence guarantees
- Broad applicability to important machine learning problems
- Well-structured and clearly written

Weaknesses:
- Limited to loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X))
- Relatively limited numerical validation
- Step size requirements could be more practical

Overall, this paper presents a significant theoretical advance that should be of interest to the NIPS community, and I believe it will be a valuable contribution to the field.
