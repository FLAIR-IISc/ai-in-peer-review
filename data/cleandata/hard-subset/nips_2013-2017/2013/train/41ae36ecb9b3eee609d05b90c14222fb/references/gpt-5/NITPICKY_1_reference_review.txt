Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex problems minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h has Lipschitz gradient and is strictly (locally) convex. The main contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound that upper-bounds the distance to the optimal set by a constant multiple of the proximal residual norm R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X, under level-set boundedness and sufficiently small residual. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors deduce asymptotic linear (Q-linear in objectives, R-linear in iterates) convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with step sizes bounded away from zero and below 1/Lf, even when f is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A, logistic losses). This addresses a gap in prior results: standard O(1/k^2) rates without strong convexity and linear rates under strong convexity. Prior error bounds existed for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group lasso); establishing a similar bound for nuclear norm (nonpolyhedral epigraph) appears novel. The key technical idea is that at an optimum X̄, the matrices X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors via the fixed point X̄=Sτ(X̄−∇f(X̄)), enabling a spectral perturbation analysis and a restricted invertibility property, which, together with local strong convexity of h on compact sets, yields the error bound.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A tailored error bound for nuclear norm regularization that avoids global strong convexity of f.
- Sound high-level proof strategy: combines the proximal fixed-point structure, spectral decompositions, and the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework.
- Applicability: covers square and logistic losses and arbitrary linear operators A, matching common ML applications.
- The structural observation (simultaneous diagonalization due to soft-thresholding) is insightful and likely of independent interest.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical correctness and rigor
- Residual map scaling inconsistency: R(X) is defined as Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X (implicitly α=1), but Theorem 3.2 analyzes PGM with step sizes αk∈(α,ᾱ)<1/Lf and uses inequalities (9)–(11) linking ||R(Xk)||F to ||Xk−Xk+1||F. For variable αk, one expects Rα(X)=Sτα(X−α∇f(X))−X. As written, inequality (11) is unclear/mismatched. Please either define Rα and state Theorem 3.1 uniformly in α∈[α,ᾱ], or consistently set α=1 throughout.
- Equation (3) appears to miss the factor 1/2 in the proximal definition; the shrinkage identity (6) presumes the 1/2 factor.
- Equation (17) is not a valid “Fermat’s rule” formulation: the minimization should include the quadratic term 1/2||Gk+D||^2, and the correct optimality condition is Gk+Rk ∈ τ∂||Xk+Rk||*. The subsequent inequality can be derived from the subgradient inclusion, but (17) as written is incorrect.
- The “strongly convex on any convex compact subset” assumption is unusual; it is essentially local strong convexity with a set-dependent modulus σ. The dependence of all constants (η,ε,σ) on the chosen level set Z should be made explicit, as Theorem 3.1’s constants depend on ζ and the compact set containing {zk}.
- The crucial Propositions 4.2 and 4.3 are deferred to the supplementary material. Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −Ḡ) is central and should at least be sketched in the main text; its correctness follows from the fixed-point identity X̄=Sτ(X̄−Ḡ) but this link is not made explicit.
- In the matrix completion experiments, α=1 is used. For P being an entry-sampling operator, Lf=1, so α=1 equals 1/Lf, violating the theorem’s αk<1/Lf. This should be reconciled (e.g., choose α=0.99 or justify that the analysis extends to α=1 under additional assumptions).

Clarity and presentation
- Notational inconsistencies: τ vs µ for the regularization parameter; L for ∇h and Lf for ∇f—be careful to distinguish and keep symbols consistent; define all constants when first used.
- The optimal solution set is denoted by X (same symbol as the matrix variable); this is potentially confusing. Consider using X* or S for the set.
- Figure numbering and captions are inconsistent (“Figure 1” referenced, but the only labeled figure says “Figure 2: Matrix Classification”). Plots embedded in text appear twice labeled; axes/metrics are not clearly defined (e.g., “error in solution” = distance to what?).
- The experimental section lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility: initialization, stopping criteria beyond ΔF, how the “solution error” is computed, runtime/SVD costs, and comparison to accelerated methods (FISTA) or other solvers are missing.
- Several typographical issues: “trace norm1” footnote formatting; hyphenation/spaces (e.g., R m×n); some equations have line breaks breaking readability.

Originality and related work
- The positioning relative to KL-based analyses (e.g., Attouch–Bolte and follow-ups) and partly smooth regularizers is missing. KL properties can imply local linear rates for proximal-gradient on semi-algebraic functions, which include nuclear norm; discuss how your error bound relates/improves (e.g., gives a computable residual-based bound with explicit constants depending on A and h).
- The discussion of previous linear convergence results for nuclear-norm problems (e.g., under restricted strong convexity/restricted isometry in statistics) is absent.

Significance
- The theoretical result is potentially impactful, offering a clean route to linear convergence without strong convexity, and the spectral-structure argument may be reused elsewhere.
- However, the practical scope is limited to local/asymptotic behavior; constants are not quantified; and the empirical validation is too small-scale and qualitative.

Pros
- Novel error bound technique for nuclear-norm regularization.
- Clear high-level argument using proximal fixed-point and spectral structure.
- Broadly applicable assumptions (logistic and square loss included).

Cons
- Technical inconsistencies (residual scaling, α=1 in experiments) and a mislabeled optimality condition.
- Heavy reliance on supplemental proofs for core propositions.
- Weak, small-scale experiments; no baselines; figure/notation issues.
- References are incomplete (many missing years/page numbers) and related work coverage is insufficient.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The core idea is interesting and likely correct, but the paper, as written, has too many technical and presentation issues for NeurIPS. I encourage the authors to: (i) fix the residual/step-size inconsistency and (17), (ii) bring key proofs (at least sketches) of Propositions 4.2/4.3 into the main text, (iii) clean up notation/figures/references, (iv) strengthen experiments (larger-scale, comparisons, define metrics), and (v) contextualize with KL-based and restricted-convexity literature.
