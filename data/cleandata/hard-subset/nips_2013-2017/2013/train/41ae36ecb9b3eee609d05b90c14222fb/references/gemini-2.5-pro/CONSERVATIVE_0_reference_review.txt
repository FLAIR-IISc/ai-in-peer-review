### **Summary**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard analysis shows a sublinear `O(1/k^2)` convergence rate for accelerated PGM on convex problems, and a linear rate if the smooth part of the objective, `f`, is strongly convex. Many important problems, however, involve a loss function `f` that is convex but not strongly convex. The main contribution of this work is to establish an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM in this intermediate setting, under the structural assumption that `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex and smooth. The key technical result underpinning this claim is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem, which relates the distance to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map. The authors provide a detailed proof for this error bound and demonstrate the claimed linear convergence empirically on synthetic matrix completion and classification tasks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a well-defined and important theoretical question regarding the convergence of first-order methods. Closing the gap between the known rates for general convex and strongly convex objectives is a meaningful contribution. The main technical result, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1), is non-trivial and appears to be novel. The analysis required to handle the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm epigraph is sound and technically involved, representing a solid piece of theoretical work.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem, situating their work within the existing literature, and clearly stating their main assumptions and results. The proof structure is laid out logically, building towards the main convergence theorem from the key error bound lemma.

3.  **Broad Applicability of the Setting:** The problem formulation `min h(A(X)) + τ||X||*` covers a wide range of important models in machine learning, including those using square loss, logistic loss, etc. The theoretical guarantees are therefore relevant to a broad class of problems.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation and Lack of Baselines:** While the paper's primary contribution is theoretical, the experimental section is its most significant weakness. The experiments serve only to verify the theory (i.e., that PGM does converge linearly), but they fail to demonstrate the practical significance of this result. A crucial missing piece is a comparison against established and competitive baselines.
    *   **Accelerated PGM (e.g., FISTA):** Standard practice for this class of problems is to use an accelerated variant of PGM, which has a theoretically superior sublinear rate of `O(1/k^2)`. While the authors prove an asymptotic linear rate `O(ρ^k)`, the constant `ρ` can be very close to 1. It is entirely possible that for the number of iterations required to reach a reasonable precision, FISTA remains significantly faster. Without a direct comparison, the practical benefit of this linear convergence result is unclear.
    *   **Other Solvers:** For problems like matrix completion, specialized solvers based on methods like Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM) or Singular Value Thresholding (SVT) are often employed. A thorough evaluation would include comparisons against these established methods to see if standard PGM, even with its linear convergence, is competitive.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The linear convergence is established only asymptotically, and the error bound (Theorem 3.1) holds only for iterates `X` that are sufficiently close to the optimal set (i.e., `F(X) ≤ ζ` and `||R(X)||F ≤ ε`). The paper provides no characterization of how large this region of linear convergence is or how many iterations of sublinear convergence are required to enter it. In practice, the initial, slower phase of convergence could dominate the total optimization time, diminishing the practical impact of the asymptotic linear rate.

3.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are conducted on small-scale synthetic problems (e.g., `n=40`). The performance and practical relevance of optimization algorithms often change dramatically with problem scale. The validation would be much more convincing if performed on larger-scale problems or benchmark real-world datasets where computational efficiency is paramount.

4.  **Significance for Practitioners:** While theoretically elegant, it is not clear if this result will change how people solve trace norm regularized problems in practice. The paper analyzes the standard, non-accelerated PGM, which is rarely the method of choice. If the analysis does not extend to accelerated variants, its impact is limited. The paper proves that an established, simple method is better than previously thought, but fails to show it is competitive with other established, and often preferred, methods.

### **Summary of Arguments for and against Acceptance**

**For Acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and technically sound theoretical result (the error bound) for an important class of problems.
*   Addresses a known gap in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for non-strongly convex objectives.
*   The paper is well-written and the claims are clearly articulated and proven.

**Against Acceptance:**
*   The experimental validation is weak and lacks crucial comparisons to standard baselines like accelerated PGM (FISTA), making it impossible to judge the practical significance of the theoretical finding.
*   The linear convergence is asymptotic, and the paper does not characterize the pre-asymptotic behavior or the size of the region where linear convergence occurs.
*   The experiments are limited to small, synthetic datasets, failing to demonstrate scalability or performance on realistic problems.
*   The practical impact is questionable, as the analysis focuses on the non-accelerated PGM, which is not typically state-of-the-art.

### **Conclusion**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound and proving the asymptotic linear convergence of PGM for a class of trace norm regularized problems. However, the work falls short in demonstrating the significance of this result. The lack of rigorous empirical comparison to established and more commonly used methods (like FISTA) makes it difficult to assess whether this theoretical insight translates into a practical advantage. Given the high standards at NIPS for both theoretical novelty and demonstrable impact, the insufficient validation and unproven practical relevance are significant drawbacks. While the theoretical core is valuable, the paper in its current form feels incomplete as a contribution to the machine learning community.
