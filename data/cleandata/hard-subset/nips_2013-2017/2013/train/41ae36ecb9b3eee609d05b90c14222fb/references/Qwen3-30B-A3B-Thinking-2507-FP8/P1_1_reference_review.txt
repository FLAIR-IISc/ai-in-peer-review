

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for the problem min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties. This error bound enables them to prove linear convergence of the PGM without requiring strong convexity of the loss function f, which is a substantial improvement over prior work. The key technical insight is recognizing that at optimal solutions, both X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same left and right singular vectors, allowing the authors to use matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound. This resolves an open question about whether a Lipschitzian error bound exists for trace norm regularization (unlike ℓ1-norm, the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph).

The paper builds upon prior work by Luo and Tseng [10] on error bounds for composite optimization, but makes crucial advances specifically for trace norm regularization. It extends the applicability of linear convergence results to a broader class of loss functions used in machine learning (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.) where f is not strongly convex.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which is a long-standing open question. The key insight about the singular vectors at optimal solutions is novel and well-justified.

2. **Theoretical Rigor**: The proofs are thorough and well-structured. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) and linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) are solidly established, with careful attention to the assumptions required.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The authors demonstrate that their framework covers many important machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning) where f is not strongly convex but still satisfies their assumptions. This bridges a gap between theory and practice.

4. **Complementary Experiments**: The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification effectively complement the theoretical results, showing linear convergence behavior in practice.

5. **Clear Organization**: The paper is well-structured with clear progression from problem setup to main results to proofs and experiments. The notation is consistent and well-defined.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restricted Assumption**: The paper assumes f(X) = h(A(X)), which, while covering many important cases, is still a specific form. It would be helpful to discuss whether the results could be extended to more general loss functions or to characterize the limitations of this assumption.

2. **Technical Complexity**: The proof of Lemma 4.1, particularly the decomposition of Q̄ in Proposition 4.3, is quite technical. While the supplementary material is referenced, this might make the paper less accessible to some readers. A more intuitive explanation of the key insight about singular vectors could help.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The numerical experiments are limited to two specific problems (matrix completion and matrix classification). While these are representative, more diverse examples would strengthen the empirical validation.

4. **Constants in Error Bound**: The paper establishes the existence of constants η and ε in the error bound (8), but doesn't discuss how these constants behave or how they might be estimated in practice. This is a practical consideration for algorithm implementation.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The theoretical results are complete and properly contextualized within the literature. The authors are careful to discuss the limitations of their assumptions. (Score: 5/5)

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written with a logical flow from problem statement to results. The mathematical notation is well-defined, though the technical depth of the proof may challenge some readers. (Score: 4.5/5)

**Originality**: This work directly addresses a known open question in the field (error bounds for trace norm regularization) with a novel technical approach. The key insight about singular vectors at optimal solutions is original and not present in prior work. (Score: 5/5)

**Significance**: The linear convergence result without strong convexity of f is highly significant for machine learning applications where the loss function is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective measurement operators). This provides theoretical justification for the empirical success of PGM in these settings and opens the door for similar analyses of other matrix norms. (Score: 5/5)

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization, addressing a clear gap in the literature with a novel and rigorous approach. The results are significant for both theory and practice, as they apply to many important machine learning problems where previous linear convergence guarantees did not hold.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. While the technical depth of the proofs may limit accessibility, this is appropriate for a NIPS paper, and the main insights are clearly presented. The numerical experiments provide good empirical support for the theoretical claims.

The minor weaknesses identified (restricted assumption, technical complexity) do not detract from the paper's substantial contribution. The authors have properly contextualized their work within the literature and have identified appropriate applications.

This paper advances the state of the art in non-strongly convex optimization for matrix problems and will be of interest to both theoretical and applied researchers in machine learning. I believe it meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**
