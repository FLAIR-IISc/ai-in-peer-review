

**Review: On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors prove a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves a long-standing open question in convex optimization.

The main novelty lies in establishing linear convergence for PGM applied to trace norm regularization under conditions that do not require strong convexity of the loss function. Previous work had established linear convergence rates for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems only under strong convexity assumptions [16]. The authors show that when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, the PGM converges linearly. This is a substantial theoretical advance because many important applications (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective linear operators) fall into this category.

The key technical contribution is the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, established using a novel approach based on matrix perturbation theory. The authors carefully analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimal solution set, which is necessary because the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph (unlike ℓ1-norm regularization), making previous approaches for other norms inapplicable. This error bound is the first of its kind for trace norm regularization, resolving a question that has remained open despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the novelty of the contribution. The theoretical development is rigorous, with detailed proofs of the main results. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide empirical evidence of the linear convergence behavior, complementing the theoretical results.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novelty:** This work resolves an important open question that has remained unanswered for years. The error bound for trace norm regularization is a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of convex optimization for low-rank matrix problems.
2. **Theoretical Depth:** The authors develop a new technique (matrix perturbation theory) to analyze spectral structure, which is elegant and could be applicable to other matrix norm regularization problems.
3. **Significance:** The trace norm is widely used in machine learning for low-rank matrix problems. Removing the strong convexity assumption broadens the applicability of linear convergence guarantees, which is crucial for many practical applications.
4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making the complex theoretical development accessible to readers.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper focuses on a specific class of loss functions (f(X) = h(A(X))), which may not cover all possible applications of trace norm regularization. However, this class is broad and includes many important cases like square loss and logistic loss.
2. The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope (only two problems tested). More extensive experiments across different problem settings would strengthen the empirical validation.

This paper represents a substantial theoretical advance that will be of interest to the NIPS community. The authors have made a clear and important contribution to the theory of convex optimization for low-rank matrix problems. The novelty and significance of the work outweigh the minor weaknesses in the empirical validation.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a novel and important contribution to the field that will advance both theoretical understanding and practical applications of trace norm regularization. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find the theoretical contribution particularly compelling and believe it represents a significant step forward in the field.
