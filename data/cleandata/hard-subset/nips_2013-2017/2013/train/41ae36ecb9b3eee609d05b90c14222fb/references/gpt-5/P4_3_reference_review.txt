Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the composite convex problem minX F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where ‖·‖* is the trace (nuclear) norm and f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h convex smooth. The main contribution is an asymptotic linear convergence result for the proximal gradient method (PGM) without requiring strong convexity of f. The authors establish a local Lipschitzian error bound of the form dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F for points in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual R(X) = proxτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Combining this error bound with the Luo–Tseng analysis yields Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates, for fixed step sizes αk bounded away from 0 and strictly less than 1/Lf.

The technical novelty is an error bound for trace-norm regularization, which is nontrivial because the epigraph of the nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, whereas prior error-bound analyses for composite problems most commonly cover polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1 and group LASSO). The proof hinges on a spectral structural property at optimality: X* and −∇f(X*) share left/right singular vectors, which allows the use of matrix perturbation arguments to control nearby iterates and establish a “restricted invertibility” inequality ‖X − ΠX*(X)‖F ≤ κ‖A(X − ΠX*(X))‖2. The analysis applies to widely used losses: square loss (globally strongly convex) and logistic loss (not globally strongly convex but strongly convex on compact sets), among others.

The work relates to: proximal methods for nuclear norm minimization and their (sublinear) rates [Ji & Ye 2009; Ma et al. 2011; Toh & Yun 2010], linear-rate analyses under strong convexity or error bounds [Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011; Tseng 2010; Zhang et al. 2013], and to error bounds for ℓ1/group penalties [Tseng 2010; Tseng & Yun 2009]. To the best of my knowledge, a linear-rate result for PGM on nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f is new.

Strengths
- Novel theoretical contribution: a local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization, addressing a known gap versus ℓ1/group cases.
- Clear path from the error bound to linear convergence via established Luo–Tseng framework; assumptions on h (smooth, strictly convex, and strongly convex on compacts) are natural for square/logistic losses.
- Sound characterization of the optimal set and a clean spectral argument (simultaneous SVD at optimality) to overcome the nonpolyhedral difficulty.
- Empirical illustrations on matrix completion and matrix classification consistent with asymptotic linear behavior.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Asymptotic/local nature: The error bound (and hence linear rate) holds only for sufficiently small residuals within a level set; no quantitative constants or neighborhood size are provided. Suggest reporting explicit dependencies or computable bounds (even conservative) tied to Lipschitz constants and σ in Assumption 2.1(b).
- Step-size conditions: Theorem 3.2 requires αk < 1/Lf. In the matrix completion experiment with sampling operator, Lf = ‖A‖2 = 1, yet α = 1 is used, which violates the strict inequality. Recommend changing to α = 0.9 (or stating α ≤ 1/Lf and clarifying whether the proof admits equality) and confirming the step-size adherence in experiments.
- Backtracking: The theorem assumes αk ∈ [α, ᾱ] with ᾱ < 1/Lf. For the logistic-loss experiment using backtracking, please state that the line search enforces αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf and that a positive lower bound α is achieved (or extend the theorem to backtracking with a lower bound ensured).
- Comparison and positioning: It would strengthen the paper to discuss connections to general error-bound/Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz frameworks, which also imply linear rates under a 1/2-exponent EB/KL property; clarify whether your error bound implies such a property for F in this setting. Also mention how your result complements restricted strong convexity analyses for decomposable regularizers (e.g., Negahban & Wainwright 2012), which focus on statistical error but sometimes yield optimization guarantees under RSC.
- Experiments: Results are illustrative but limited. Consider adding:
  - Quantitative slopes of the semi-log plots (estimated contraction factors).
  - Runtime or SVD counts, and a comparison to accelerated proximal gradient (with and without restart) to show if the same linear phase is observed.
  - Sensitivity to τ and condition numbers, and larger-scale tests.
- Presentation: A few minor issues: Figure numbering appears inconsistent (Figure 1 vs Figure 2); several references lack years; clarify notation (τ vs μ) and ensure all propositions referenced in the main text have proofs in the supplement.

Quality
- The analysis is technically careful and leverages appropriate convex-analytic and spectral tools. The main arguments (optimality characterization, residual-based EB, restricted invertibility) are plausible and well connected, with detailed steps sketched and relegated proofs referenced.
- Assumptions are clearly stated and reasonable for target applications. The result is asymptotic, which is appropriate given local strong convexity on compact sets for logistic loss.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized: preliminaries, main results, proof outline, and experiments. The exposition of the residual map and its role is clear. Some details (proofs of Propositions 4.2 and 4.3) are in the supplement, which is acceptable.
- Minor editorial fixes as noted above would improve readability.

Originality
- The error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under the stated composite structure appears new and nontrivial. The spectral alignment insight at optimality and its use to bypass nonpolyhedral obstacles is original in this context.

Significance
- The result advances theoretical understanding of first-order methods for nuclear-norm problems beyond strong convexity, covering widely used losses. It may inform algorithm design (e.g., restarts, stopping criteria based on residuals) and could be extendable to other matrix norms.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an open gap by establishing a local error bound and linear rate for PGM with nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- Technically solid with broadly applicable assumptions and clear implications for common ML tasks.
- Provides a reusable analytical tool (error bound) potentially useful beyond PGM.

Arguments against acceptance
- Asymptotic/local nature without quantitative constants; practical guidance is limited.
- Experimental validation is modest and does not compare against baselines or explore scaling.
- Minor inconsistencies (step size choice; figure/reference details) detract from polish.

Overall, the paper makes a substantive and rigorous theoretical contribution with clear relevance to NeurIPS topics in optimization for ML; addressing the noted empirical and presentation issues would further strengthen it.
