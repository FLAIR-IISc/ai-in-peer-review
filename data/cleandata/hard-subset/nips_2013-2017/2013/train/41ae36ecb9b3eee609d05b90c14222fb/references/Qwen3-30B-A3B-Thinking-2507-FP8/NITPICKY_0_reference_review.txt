

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper claims to establish linear convergence for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors present a novel error bound for trace norm regularization, which they argue is the first such result for this setting. While the problem is well-motivated and the result has potential significance, the paper contains several critical technical flaws and inconsistencies that undermine its contribution.

**Major Technical Flaw:**
The most serious issue is in the definition of the proximal operator (equation 3). The paper states:
"proxτ(X) = arg min Z∈Rm×n {τ‖Z‖∗ + 1 ‖X − Z‖2F }"

This definition is incorrect. The standard proximal operator should have a factor of 1/2:
"proxτ(X) = arg min Z∈Rm×n {τ‖Z‖∗ + (1/2)‖X − Z‖2F }"

This error propagates throughout the paper. The residual map R(X) = Sτ(X - ∇f(X)) - X (equation 7) is defined based on this incorrect proximal operator. The matrix shrinkage operator Sτ is defined to be equal to proxτ, but with the missing 1/2 factor, the entire convergence analysis is built on a flawed foundation. This is a critical error that invalidates the main theoretical results.

**Notation Inconsistency:**
The paper uses τ for the regularization parameter in the problem statement (equation 1) but switches to µ in the numerical experiments section (matrix completion: "regularization parameter µ = 1"). This inconsistent notation creates significant confusion and undermines the paper's credibility.

**Lack of Supplementary Material:**
The paper repeatedly refers to "the supplementary material" for proofs of key results (e.g., Proposition 4.3, the derivation of inequalities (9)-(11)). However, no supplementary material is provided with the submission, making it impossible for reviewers to verify the correctness of these critical arguments.

**Theoretical Claims:**
The claim that "it is the first linear convergence result concerning the application of a first-order method to the trace norm-regularized problem (1) that does not require the strong convexity of f" appears questionable. The paper cites Zhang et al. (2013) in the references but does not discuss their work in the main text. Zhang et al. ("On the Linear Convergence of a Proximal Gradient Method for a Class of Nonsmooth Convex Minimization Problems") already established linear convergence under conditions that do not require strong convexity. The authors should clarify how their work differs from or extends Zhang et al.

**Minor Technical Issues:**
1. In Section 2.2, the paper claims "X is a non-empty convex compact set" based on [14, Corollary 8.7.1], but the cited reference is about convex functions, not about the optimal solution set being compact. The correct reference would be [14, Theorem 10.1] or similar.

2. The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) contains an unverified claim: "max { 0, σi(X̃k) + σi(−Ḡ)− τ } = σi(X̃k) for i = 1, . . . ,m." This requires justification based on the optimality conditions for trace norm regularization.

3. In the matrix classification experiments, the paper mentions "a backtracking line search was adopted" but provides no details about the backtracking parameters (e.g., reduction factor, sufficient decrease condition), making the experiments difficult to reproduce.

**Clarity and Presentation:**
The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, but the technical errors and inconsistencies overshadow the theoretical contribution. The numerical experiments are reasonable but lack sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**
- The problem addressed is relevant to machine learning and optimization.
- The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding error bounds for trace norm regularization.
- The numerical experiments provide some empirical validation of the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
- The critical error in the proximal operator definition invalidates the main theoretical results.
- Inconsistent notation (τ vs µ) throughout the paper.
- Lack of supplementary material for key proofs.
- The main theoretical claim requires clarification regarding prior work.
- Several minor technical issues that affect the paper's credibility.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper addresses an important problem and makes a potentially valuable contribution to the field, the critical technical error in the definition of the proximal operator fundamentally undermines the theoretical results. The lack of supplementary material for key proofs and the inconsistent notation further diminish the paper's credibility. Despite the significance of the problem, these issues prevent me from recommending acceptance. I would recommend rejection due to the fundamental flaw in the mathematical formulation, which makes the main results unreliable.

**Recommendation:** Reject
