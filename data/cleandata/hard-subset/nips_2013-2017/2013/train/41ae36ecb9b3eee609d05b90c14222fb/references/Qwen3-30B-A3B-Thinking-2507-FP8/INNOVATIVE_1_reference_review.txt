

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of first-order optimization methods for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish the first linear convergence result for the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is particularly important because many machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.) involve loss functions that are not strongly convex but take the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex.

The key contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which the authors prove by establishing a crucial relationship between the singular vectors of optimal solutions. The authors correctly note that this problem has remained open despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization, because the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, making standard error bound techniques for ℓ1 (which rely on splitting variables into non-interfering groups) inapplicable. Their proof cleverly uses matrix perturbation theory to overcome this challenge, which is the paper's most significant technical innovation.

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the literature, as previous linear convergence results for trace norm regularization required strong convexity of the objective function [16], which is not satisfied in many practical settings like matrix completion where the loss function is not strongly convex due to the non-injectivity of the measurement operator. The authors correctly position their work against prior results for ℓ1-norm regularization [22, 23, 25] that have established error bounds for problems with polyhedral epigraphs, highlighting why the trace norm case is fundamentally different.

The theoretical development is sound, with a well-structured proof that builds logically from the error bound to the linear convergence result. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems provide empirical validation of the linear convergence claim, showing that both objective values and solutions converge linearly once the iterates are close to the optimal set.

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a significant open question in optimization for low-rank matrix problems
- Introduces a novel error bound technique specifically for trace norm regularization
- The result has broad applicability to important machine learning problems
- The theoretical contribution is substantial and clearly distinct from prior work
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written
- Numerical experiments support the theoretical claims

**Weaknesses:**
- The practical implications of the step size conditions (0 < α < α_k < ᾱ < 1/L_f) could be discussed more thoroughly
- The paper doesn't address whether the error bound can be extended to other matrix norms
- The connection to practical implementation details (beyond the backtracking line search in the experiments) is limited

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- This is a significant theoretical advance that resolves a long-standing open question
- The novelty is substantial and not merely an incremental improvement
- The error bound technique is likely to be useful for future work on matrix regularization
- The paper makes a clear contribution to understanding first-order methods for non-strongly convex problems
- The result has broad applicability across multiple machine learning domains

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The result is primarily theoretical with limited immediate practical impact
- The proof is quite technical and may be challenging for some readers to follow
- The experimental section is somewhat brief and doesn't explore a wide range of problem instances

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The authors have made a significant contribution to the theory of optimization for matrix problems by resolving an open question that has been outstanding for some time. The error bound they establish is not only novel but also provides a new technical tool that could be applied to other problems involving trace norm regularization.

While the paper is primarily theoretical, it makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of optimization methods for machine learning problems. The authors have correctly identified a gap in the literature and provided a rigorous solution that advances the state of the art. Their result is not just a minor improvement but represents a fundamental extension of the theory of first-order methods for trace norm problems.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It meets NIPS standards for novelty and significance, and the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to outweigh any minor weaknesses in the experimental section. I recommend acceptance.
