

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized optimization problems, specifically establishing linear convergence under conditions that do not require the loss function to be strongly convex. The authors prove a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which they use to establish linear convergence for the PGM when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h satisfying certain convexity and smoothness properties. This resolves an open question regarding whether such error bounds exist for trace norm regularization, which has a non-polyhedral epigraph unlike ℓ1-norm regularization.

**Strengths:**

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first error bound for trace norm regularization that enables linear convergence analysis without strong convexity assumptions. The proof technique is novel, particularly the observation that optimal solutions and the negative gradient share the same singular vectors, which enables the use of matrix perturbation theory. The theoretical development is rigorous, with careful attention to assumptions and conditions. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior results, noting that previous error bounds for composite optimization apply to cases with polyhedral epigraphs (like ℓ1-norm) but not to trace norm. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide reasonable empirical validation of the theoretical claims, showing linear convergence behavior when iterates are close to the optimal set.

**Weaknesses:**

While theoretically sound, the paper's assumptions are quite restrictive. The requirement that f(X) = h(A(X)) excludes many practical loss functions that are not expressible in this form (e.g., loss functions with non-linear dependence on X beyond linear operators). The numerical experiments are limited to just two problem types, which may not sufficiently demonstrate the generality of the results. The paper does not address how the constants in the error bound (η and ε) scale with problem dimensions, which is critical for understanding practical convergence rates. The claim that this is "the first linear convergence result" for trace norm regularization without strong convexity of f appears accurate based on the cited literature, but the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to related work on non-strongly convex optimization with composite objectives.

**Critical Assessment:**

As a reviewer who values methodological rigor but prefers established approaches, I find the theoretical contribution valuable but question its practical significance. The restriction to loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) is substantial and may limit applicability to many real-world machine learning problems. The paper does not provide evidence that this form of loss function covers the majority of practical trace norm-regularized problems, which often involve more complex loss functions (e.g., logistic loss with non-linear dependencies). While the error bound itself is a notable technical achievement, its utility is limited by the restrictive assumptions.

The numerical experiments are well-executed but superficial. They show linear convergence in the asymptotic regime (as the iterates approach the optimal solution), which is exactly what the theory predicts, but do not compare the PGM with other algorithms or demonstrate computational advantages. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison to alternative approaches for the same problem settings, particularly those that have been used in practice.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of trace norm regularization. However, the restrictive assumptions and limited empirical validation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The work is important for the theoretical community but lacks sufficient practical relevance to justify publication at NIPS, where significant practical impact is expected. The authors should consider broadening their assumptions to cover more general loss functions or providing stronger empirical evidence of practical benefits before resubmitting.

**Pro-con Summary:**

*Pro:* 
- Novel theoretical contribution establishing the first error bound for trace norm regularization enabling linear convergence without strong convexity
- Rigorous proof with careful attention to mathematical details
- Appropriate contextualization against prior work

*Con:*
- Restrictive assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) excludes many practical loss functions
- Limited numerical experiments that only validate the asymptotic behavior
- No comparison to alternative methods or discussion of computational advantages
- No analysis of how error bound constants scale with problem size

The paper would be significantly stronger if the authors could demonstrate that their assumptions cover a substantial fraction of practical applications and provide evidence of computational benefits over existing methods. As it stands, the theoretical contribution is valuable but the practical significance is limited.
