Summary
This paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems min_X f(X) + τ||X||*, with f(X) = h(A(X)). The key contribution is an error bound that links the distance to the optimal solution set to the proximal-gradient residual ||Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||F, under the assumption that h is smooth, strictly convex, and “locally strongly convex” on any compact convex subset of its domain. Using this error bound and the Luo–Tseng framework, the paper derives asymptotic linear convergence of PGM without requiring f to be strongly convex (e.g., A may be rank-deficient as in matrix completion). The technical novelty lies in handling the nonpolyhedral epigraph of the nuclear norm via a new spectral analysis showing that an optimal solution and its negative gradient share singular vector subspaces, enabling a “restricted invertibility” inequality. Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification empirically illustrate linear convergence.

Relation to prior work
- Standard PGM and accelerated rates O(1/k) and O(1/k^2) for composite convex objectives are well known (e.g., Beck–Teboulle; Ma–Goldfarb–Chen; Ji–Ye; Toh–Yun).
- Linear convergence of first-order methods is often obtained under strong convexity of f (e.g., Schmidt–LeRoux–Bach) or under error bounds for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group LASSO; Tseng, Luo–Tseng; Zhang–Jiang–Luo).
- Nuclear norm presents difficulties because its epigraph is nonpolyhedral. To my knowledge, linear convergence of PGM for nuclear-norm regularized problems without strong convexity of f was open; this paper resolves it for a broad structured class f(X)=h(A(X)).

Strengths
- Novel and nontrivial error bound for nuclear-norm regularization, overcoming the nonpolyhedral barrier via a clever spectral argument (simultaneous SVD alignment at optimality) and matrix perturbation techniques.
- Clear articulation of assumptions on h that cover square and logistic losses, and linear operators A that may be non-injective, making the result relevant to matrix completion/classification and related problems.
- Theoretical result is clean: R-linear convergence of iterates and Q-linear convergence of objective, with standard step-size assumptions.
- Experiments, while modest, are consistent with the theory and demonstrate local linear behavior on two representative tasks.

Weaknesses / questions
- Empirical section is minimal. There is no comparison to accelerated variants or alternative solvers (e.g., APG, FISTA, ALM/ADMM) and no exploration of the size of the attraction region, practical constants, or robustness to step-size/tuning.
- The key spectral alignment proposition and handling of singular value multiplicities could be discussed more: when singular values are repeated, U,V are not unique—does the proof rely on a specific choice? The statement that X̄ and −∇f(X̄) “have the same set of left and right singular vectors” would benefit from a precise formulation (e.g., existence vs uniqueness, subspace-level alignment).
- The “strong convexity on any compact convex subset” assumption on h is reasonable for square/logistic losses, but it would be useful to discuss other common losses and whether the property fails on unbounded sequences or in ill-conditioned regimes.
- The error bound is local (requires small residual and bounded level set). It would help to emphasize the locality (asymptotic linear rate) more prominently in the abstract/intro and to state any verifiable sufficient conditions for entering the linear regime (e.g., descent + backtracking ensures bounded level sets).
- Constants κ1–κ4 and the contraction factor in Theorem 3.2 are abstract; even rough guidance on their dependence on Lf, α bounds, and problem geometry would make the result more actionable.

Quality
Technically solid and carefully argued. The proof strategy is sophisticated but plausible, with key steps deferred to the supplement. Some clarifications around spectral degeneracies and the exact scope of the alignment property would strengthen confidence further. Experiments are correct but thin.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The preliminaries, algorithm, and residual map are clearly presented. The proof is notation-heavy; brief intuition and a schematic of the main steps (geometry of the optimal set X, role of Xc, and how restricted invertibility arises) would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization and leveraging it to obtain linear convergence without strong convexity of f appears novel and addresses a recognized gap relative to ℓ1/group-structured settings.

Significance
High for the optimization/ML theory community. The result covers widely used losses and operators arising in matrix completion/classification and offers a principled explanation for the often observed local linear convergence of PGM in these problems. It may also catalyze similar analyses for other nonpolyhedral matrix norms.

Pros
- Resolves an open question: error bound and linear convergence for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- Broad applicability (square/logistic losses, general linear A).
- Elegant spectral argument that could be of independent interest.
- Clear theoretical statements; experiments support the claims.

Cons
- Empirical validation is limited; no comparisons or scalability studies.
- Some technical details about singular value multiplicities and constants are opaque; locality conditions could be emphasized/operationalized more.
- Practical guidance (step-size selection, entering the linear regime) is minimal.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The paper introduces a significant new concept—an error bound for nuclear-norm regularization—that enables a qualitatively stronger convergence guarantee for a widely used method under realistic assumptions. Despite modest experiments and some technical details that could be clarified, the theoretical contribution is novel and impactful.

Confidence
High.
