

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems, claiming to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function f. The authors introduce a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which they claim resolves an open question in the literature.

The paper is well-structured and technically sound. The authors establish a novel error bound (Theorem 3.1) and use it to prove linear convergence (Theorem 3.2) under certain conditions on the loss function. The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is technically sophisticated, with Lemma 4.1 and Proposition 4.2 providing the key insights about the spectral structure of optimal solutions. The authors correctly cite Luo and Tseng's framework [10] as the foundation for their convergence analysis.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical contribution is mathematically rigorous and addresses an important gap in the literature regarding trace norm regularization.
2. The paper makes a clear distinction between their approach and previous work on error bounds for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization.
3. The numerical experiments (Section 5) demonstrate linear convergence in practice for two common machine learning problems (matrix completion and matrix classification), supporting their theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Novelty claim needs stronger justification:** The paper claims this is "the first linear convergence result concerning the application of a first-order method to the trace norm-regularized problem that does not require the strong convexity of f." However, this claim appears overstated. For matrix completion with square loss (which is not strongly convex), [9] (Koltchinskii et al.) and [17] (Recht et al.) have established recovery guarantees under certain conditions, though they may not explicitly state linear convergence rates. The paper should more carefully compare with these works to justify their novelty claim.

2. **Limited empirical validation:** The numerical experiments are restricted to small-scale problems (n=40) with limited parameter variations. The paper shows linear convergence in the asymptotic regime but fails to demonstrate how much faster PGM converges compared to standard approaches like accelerated proximal gradient methods (which are known to have O(1/k²) rates for smooth objectives). The authors should compare against established baselines that achieve similar rates under different conditions.

3. **Practical significance is questionable:** The paper doesn't address the practical relevance of the linear convergence guarantee. For trace norm regularization, the linear convergence rate may only kick in after many iterations, making it less relevant for practical applications where the initial sublinear phase dominates. The paper would be strengthened by showing a clear computational advantage in real-world settings.

4. **Assumptions are restrictive:** The loss function f must be of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth. While this covers some common cases (square loss, logistic loss), it excludes many important loss functions used in practice. The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of the limitations of their assumptions.

5. **Insufficient comparison with related work:** The paper cites [22, 23, 25] for error bounds on ℓ1-norm and group LASSO, but fails to adequately explain why the same approach doesn't work for trace norm. The paper claims the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph prevents standard splitting techniques, but doesn't thoroughly explore why alternative approaches (e.g., using matrix perturbation theory directly) haven't been attempted before.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of trace norm regularization. The error bound and linear convergence result are mathematically rigorous and address an important gap in the literature. However, the novelty claim is overstated, and the practical significance is limited by the restrictive assumptions and insufficient empirical validation.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. More careful comparison with existing literature on matrix completion and trace norm regularization
2. More comprehensive numerical experiments comparing against established methods (e.g., accelerated PGM, ADMM)
3. A clearer discussion of the practical implications of the linear convergence guarantee
4. A more detailed analysis of the limitations of their assumptions

While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how this advances the state of the art in a way that would be meaningful to practitioners. The theoretical result is interesting but lacks the practical impact that would justify its publication at NIPS, especially given the limited empirical validation.

**Recommendation:** Weak reject. The paper makes a technically sound contribution, but the novelty is overstated, and the practical significance is limited. The authors should address the weaknesses noted above before reconsidering for publication.
