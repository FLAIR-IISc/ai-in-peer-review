This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems of the form min X {F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) and A is a linear operator. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under the assumption that h is strictly convex with certain smoothness and curvature properties, without requiring strong convexity of f itself – a significant improvement over existing results. The key contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges posed by its non-polyhedral epigraph.

The paper is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction of the problem and its applications, followed by preliminary definitions and the main results. The notation is largely standard and consistently used. The proof strategy is clearly outlined, and the paper acknowledges existing limitations and potential avenues for future research. The inclusion of numerical experiments strengthens the validity of the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result for PGM without requiring strong convexity of the loss function *f* is a substantial contribution. Existing literature typically relies on strong convexity.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically rigorous, with a careful analysis of the convergence properties and a well-defined Lipschitzian error bound. The connection to Luo and Tseng's framework is appropriate.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation behind the technical contributions is clearly explained.
*   **Significance:** The result broadens the applicability of PGM to a wider range of problems in machine learning, such as matrix completion, where strong convexity is often not present.
*   **Experimental Validation:** Inclusion of experiments demonstrating linear convergence is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Proof:** While technically sound, the proof is lengthy and requires a substantial level of expertise to fully grasp. More intuitive explanations for some of the crucial steps (e.g., Proposition 4.2 and its implications) could enhance understanding.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumptions on *h* (strictly convex with smoothness and curvature properties) are relatively strong. A discussion of the tightness of these assumptions or potential relaxation strategies would be beneficial.  The impact of the linear operator A is also not deeply explored, which could be relevant in certain application domains.
*   **Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  More comprehensive testing with different datasets, problem sizes, and parameter settings would increase confidence in the results. The choice of step size for the matrix classification experiment (backtracking line search) is mentioned but not detailed.
*   **Limited Discussion of Related Work:** While related work is cited, the discussion could be more detailed regarding recent advancements in accelerated proximal gradient methods and their convergence guarantees, especially in the context of non-convex optimization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with significant implications for the optimization community.
*   The linear convergence guarantee extends the applicability of PGM to a broader class of problems.
*   The experimental results support the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The technical complexity of the proof may limit its accessibility to a wider audience.
*   The assumptions on the loss function *h* could be considered restrictive.
*   The numerical experiments are somewhat limited.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization. The novelty of the result, combined with the technical rigor and experimental validation, outweighs the minor weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Providing more intuitive explanations for key steps in the proof (e.g., Proposition 4.2).
2.  Discussing the tightness of the assumptions on the function *h*.
3.  Expanding the numerical experiments to include a wider range of test cases.
4.  Providing more details regarding the backtracking line search used in the matrix classification experiment.
