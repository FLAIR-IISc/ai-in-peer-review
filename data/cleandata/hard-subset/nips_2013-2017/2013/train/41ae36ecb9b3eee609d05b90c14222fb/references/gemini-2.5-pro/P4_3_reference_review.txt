### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. The key contribution is establishing a linear convergence rate for this method under a set of assumptions that are notably weaker than the standard strong convexity requirement for the loss function `f`. Specifically, the authors show that linear convergence can be achieved when the loss function has the composite structure `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function with a Lipschitz continuous gradient. This setting is highly relevant to many machine learning applications, such as matrix completion with a square loss or multi-task learning with a logistic loss, where the overall loss function `f` is convex but not strongly convex.

The cornerstone of the authors' analysis is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This error bound provides a local upper bound on the distance from a point `X` to the optimal solution set in terms of the norm of a residual map, `‖R(X)‖F`, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm has been a challenging open problem, as prior techniques developed for `l1`-norm or group LASSO regularization, which rely on the separability of variables, are not applicable. The authors overcome this challenge by leveraging the spectral properties of the problem. A key insight is that an optimal solution and its corresponding negative gradient share the same singular vectors. This allows the use of matrix perturbation theory to analyze the structure of points near the optimal set, which is the crucial step in proving the error bound. Once the error bound is established, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the well-known analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993). Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems are provided to corroborate the theoretical findings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are significant and appear to be soundly supported by detailed proofs. The central contribution, the proof of the Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1), is non-trivial and demonstrates a deep understanding of both optimization theory and matrix analysis. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing proof techniques and develop a novel approach tailored to the non-polyhedral and non-separable nature of the trace norm. The work is presented as a complete and self-contained contribution, with the main theoretical result being complemented by numerical experiments that clearly illustrate the claimed linear convergence behavior.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly situating the problem within the existing literature, explaining the gap between the sublinear rates for general convex problems and the linear rates for strongly convex problems. The authors articulate the main question and their contribution concisely. The flow of the paper is logical, proceeding from preliminaries and main results to the detailed proof of the key error bound. The notation is consistent, and the theoretical arguments, while complex, are presented in a step-by-step manner that aids comprehension. The high-level structure of the proof is explained before diving into technical details, which is very helpful for the reader.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. The primary result—demonstrating linear convergence of PGM for a class of non-strongly convex trace norm regularized problems—is a novel and important contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. While the general framework connecting error bounds to linear convergence is known (Luo and Tseng, 1993), the critical and original part of this paper is the successful derivation of such an error bound for the trace norm regularizer. This resolves a known open question and required new proof techniques based on spectral analysis, which differ substantially from prior work on `l1`-type regularizers (e.g., Tseng, 2010). The paper appropriately references and distinguishes its contributions from related work on PGM convergence and error bounds.

**Significance:** The results presented are highly significant. Trace norm regularization is a fundamental tool in modern machine learning for inducing low-rank structures, and PGM is one of the most widely used algorithms for solving the resulting optimization problems. This paper provides a much stronger convergence guarantee for this algorithm-problem pairing than was previously known, replacing sublinear `O(1/k^2)` rates with linear rates under practical assumptions. This result deepens our theoretical understanding of first-order methods and has practical implications, as it assures practitioners that PGM can converge very rapidly on these problems once the iterates are sufficiently close to the solution set. Furthermore, the error bound itself is a powerful theoretical tool that may be leveraged in the analysis of other algorithms or for other purposes.

**Weaknesses:** The weaknesses of the paper are minor and do not undermine its core contributions.

1.  The experimental section, while effective in demonstrating the primary claim of linear convergence, is somewhat limited. It would have been more comprehensive to include a comparison with an accelerated PGM variant to empirically show the point at which the asymptotic linear rate established here becomes superior to the accelerated sublinear rate.
2.  The proof of Theorem 3.2 is stated for a step size `αk` that is bounded within `(α, ᾱ)`. However, one of the experiments (matrix classification) employs a backtracking line search, as is common when the Lipschitz constant `Lf` is unknown. While the analysis likely extends to standard backtracking schemes, a brief discussion of this point would strengthen the connection between the theory and practice.

### Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel, significant, and long-sought-after result: the linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and the proof of the key Lipschitzian error bound introduces original techniques suited for the spectral nature of the trace norm.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex technical topic accessible and easy to follow.
*   **Impact:** The results are of high interest to both the optimization and machine learning communities, as they concern a widely used algorithm for a fundamental class of problems. The error bound is a valuable contribution in its own right.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The numerical experiments are illustrative but not exhaustive. They confirm the theory but could have provided deeper insights with more extensive comparisons.

In summary, the paper's strengths, particularly its novelty and technical significance, far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a substantial and elegant contribution to the field.
