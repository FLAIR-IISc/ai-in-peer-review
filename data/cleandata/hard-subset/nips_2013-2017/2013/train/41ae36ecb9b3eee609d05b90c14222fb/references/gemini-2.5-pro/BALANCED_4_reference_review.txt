**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems. The objective function is a composite of a smooth loss function `f(X)` and the non-smooth trace norm regularizer `τ||X||*`. While PGM is known to have a sublinear `O(1/k^2)` convergence rate for general convex `f`, and a linear rate for strongly convex `f`, many important machine learning problems (e.g., matrix completion with square loss) feature a loss function that is convex but not strongly convex. The main contribution of this paper is to establish a linear convergence rate for PGM under a weaker set of conditions. Specifically, the authors show that linear convergence can be achieved when the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties and `A` is an arbitrary linear operator. This result covers widely used loss functions like the square loss and logistic loss.

The key technical tool enabling this result is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such a bound for the trace norm is non-trivial because, unlike the l1-norm, its epigraph is not polyhedral, precluding the use of existing proof techniques. The authors overcome this by leveraging a crucial structural property of the optimal solutions and applying matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral properties of iterates near the optimal set. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from this error bound via the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10]. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated theoretical question: under what weaker-than-strong-convexity conditions can we guarantee linear convergence for PGM on trace norm regularized problems? This is highly relevant to the NIPS community, given the prevalence of such problems. The claim to be the first to establish such a result without requiring strong convexity of the overall loss function appears to be correct and represents a substantial theoretical advance. The development of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm is a novel contribution in its own right and could be impactful for analyzing other algorithms.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The analysis is rigorous, and the proof of the error bound (the paper's main technical contribution) is sophisticated. The key insight that an optimal solution `X*` and `-∇f(X*)` share singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), and the subsequent use of this structure to analyze perturbations, is elegant and powerful. The overall argument, which builds on the established Luo-Tseng framework but requires a novel core component (the error bound), constitutes a complete and sound piece of theoretical work.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly situates the work within the existing literature, articulates the research gap, and outlines the main contributions and technical challenges. The problem setup and main theorems are presented with precision. The high-level structure of the proof is explained before diving into technical lemmas, which aids readability.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** While the numerical experiments successfully illustrate the theoretical findings (i.e., they show linear convergence), they are somewhat basic. The experiments are conducted on relatively small-scale synthetic data (n=40). There are no comparisons against other state-of-the-art solvers for trace norm problems (e.g., accelerated PGM, ADMM variants, or Frank-Wolfe methods) to demonstrate the practical implications of the linear convergence. The primary value of a linear rate is faster convergence in practice, and a more comprehensive experimental section could have showcased this benefit more convincingly.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Details:** While the high-level structure is clear, the proof of the error bound in Section 4 is extremely dense and technical. The core of the argument relies on a detailed case-by-case analysis of singular values (Section 4.1.1 and 4.1.2), and key results like Proposition 4.3 are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is understandable due to space constraints, adding more intuition in the main text about why this spectral decomposition of `Q_bar` is the right tool for the job could significantly improve the paper's accessibility to a broader audience of optimization and machine learning researchers.

3.  **Scope of Assumptions:** The assumptions, particularly Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity of `h` on any convex compact subset of its domain), are central to the result. While the paper shows this holds for square and logistic loss, a more detailed discussion on the scope of this assumption would be beneficial. Are there common loss functions used with trace norm regularization where this assumption might be violated? A brief discussion of such limitations would provide a more balanced perspective.

**Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Enhancing Experiments:** To strengthen the paper's practical impact, I would suggest expanding the experimental section. This could include: (a) testing on larger or real-world datasets (e.g., from the matrix completion literature); (b) comparing the iteration count and wall-clock time of PGM against its accelerated version (FISTA) and at least one other popular solver to empirically show where the linear convergence provides a practical advantage; (c) plotting the norm of the residual `||R(X^k)||_F` vs. iteration `k` on a semi-log scale, which should also exhibit linear decay and directly connect to the error bound.

2.  **Improving Intuition in Proofs:** In Section 4.1, before the case analysis, the authors could add a short paragraph motivating the strategy. For example, explaining that the goal is to show the contradicting direction `Q_bar` must have a specific structure inherited from the singular vectors of the optimal set, and that this structure will ultimately prevent it from being a valid descent direction. This would help the reader navigate the subsequent technical details.

3.  **Clarifying Practicality of Assumptions:** A small addition discussing the applicability of Assumption 2.1 would be valuable. For instance, mentioning that it holds for any loss function that is twice continuously differentiable with a positive definite Hessian would be a helpful concrete condition for practitioners to check.

**Decision**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a strong theoretical contribution to an important area. The originality and significance of establishing linear convergence without strong convexity for trace norm regularization are high. The technical analysis is sound and novel. Despite the somewhat limited experimental section, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant publication at NIPS. The work deepens our understanding of the behavior of first-order methods and provides a new analytical tool (the error bound) that may prove useful in future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Significance:** Solves a long-standing open question about the convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization.
*   **High Originality:** Presents the first linear convergence result of this kind and introduces a novel error bound for the trace norm.
*   **Excellent Technical Quality:** The proofs are rigorous and sophisticated.
*   **High Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow at a high level.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental validation is weak and does not fully demonstrate the practical superiority that a linear rate would imply.

On balance, the strengths, particularly the theoretical novelty and significance, far outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
