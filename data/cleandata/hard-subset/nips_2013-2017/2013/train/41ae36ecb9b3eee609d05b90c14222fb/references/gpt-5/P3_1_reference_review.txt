Summary
The paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for nuclear-norm regularized problems min_X f(X)+τ||X||_* with f(X)=h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is differentiable with Lipschitz gradient and strictly convex (via strong convexity on compact subsets). The main contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound of the form dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||S_τ(X−∇f(X))−X|| for points in a bounded level set with small residual. Combined with the Luo–Tseng framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence: Q-linear decrease of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates for step sizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/L_f. The core technical idea overcomes the non-polyhedral nature of the nuclear norm by exploiting spectral alignment at optimality: any solution X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors (via the fixed point X* = S_τ(X*−∇f(X*))). This enables a perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” inequality that rules out null(A) directions near the solution manifold. The results cover square and logistic losses and are illustrated on synthetic matrix completion and classification tasks, where linear convergence is observed near optimum.

Relation to prior work
Previous error bounds and linear rates for PGM are well developed for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group lasso) and for strongly convex f; the present work extends linear convergence guarantees to nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of f. The approach builds on Luo–Tseng error bounds/analysis and differs from restricted strong convexity (RSC)/RIP-based analyses common in low-rank recovery, by instead deriving a local metric subregularity/error bound specific to the nuclear norm’s spectral structure. The paper could also situate itself relative to the Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz (KL) and partial smoothness/active manifold identification literature, which also imply local linear rates under error bounds; nonetheless, the specific nuclear norm error bound seems novel.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: Establishes a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization, long known for ℓ1/group-lasso but open for trace norm due to non-polyhedrality.
- Clear mechanism: The simultaneous SVD of X* and −∇f(X*) via the shrinkage fixed point is insightful and central to the proof.
- Broad applicability: Covers common losses (squared, logistic) through the composition structure h∘A with mild local curvature assumptions.
- Solid connection to standard convergence frameworks (Luo–Tseng), yielding clean asymptotic linear rates under standard step-size conditions.
- Numerics align with theory, showing linear convergence once sufficiently close.

Weaknesses and questions
- Local nature: The error bound and linear rate are asymptotic (require small residual). This is common, but the abstract/introduction could emphasize “asymptotic” earlier and clarify when the linear phase kicks in (e.g., characterize the neighborhood via computable quantities).
- Assumptions on step sizes: The theorem requires 0<α≤α_k≤ᾱ<1/L_f. With backtracking, upper bound is standard, but a uniform positive lower bound is nontrivial unless one uses bounded level sets to bound local Lipschitz constants. It would help to state this explicitly and provide conditions guaranteeing α_k≥α>0 under backtracking.
- Restricted invertibility (Lemma 4.1c): While the contradiction argument is convincing, it is a strong property in the presence of a nontrivial null(A). It would be helpful to interpret it geometrically (local injectivity of A on the identified active manifold X_c) and clarify any dependence of κ on problem conditioning (e.g., spectral gaps, rank).
- Relation to identification/partial smoothness: The alignment of singular vectors and the use of X_c suggest active manifold identification. Referencing and leveraging this framework (e.g., Lewis; Vaiter et al.) might simplify exposition and strengthen generality.
- Scope of h: “Strongly convex on any convex compact subset” is unusual but effectively ensures local quadratic growth on bounded regions. The paper could provide a short discussion/examples (e.g., logistic loss with bounded A(X)) to reassure readers.
- Missing related work: The KL-based linear convergence of proximal algorithms (Attouch–Bolte–Svaiter, Bolte–Sabach–Teboulle) and RSC-based linear rates in low-rank settings could be discussed to position the contribution more fully.
- Experiments: Synthetic and minimal. While acceptable for a theory paper, including at least one real dataset or a comparison (e.g., ALM/ADMM, SVT, Frank–Wolfe) would enhance practical relevance. Also, report ranks identified and whether the singular subspaces stabilize.

Quality
Technically sound, with a careful and original proof strategy. Some key inequalities/constants are deferred to the supplement; including a succinct sketch in the main text (especially for (9)–(11)) would improve readability. The assumptions are reasonable and clearly stated.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The proof outline is clear; however, crucial ideas (Propositions 4.2–4.3) are dense. A higher-level geometric interpretation (active manifold, local sharpness) and a brief schematic of the case analysis would aid readability. Correct minor typos (prox definition missing 1/2 factor in (3)).

Originality
Strong. The local error bound for nuclear norm is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The spectral alignment insight is elegant.

Significance
High theoretical significance: error bounds for non-polyhedral regularizers underpin linear-rate analysis for a broad class of first-order methods. Practically, the result justifies observed linear phases of PGM in popular low-rank learning tasks.

Pros for acceptance
- New and important theoretical result (local error bound) for nuclear norm.
- Clean linear convergence guarantees beyond strong convexity.
- Solid methodology with potential extensions to other spectral norms.

Cons for acceptance
- Asymptotic nature and lack of characterization of the neighborhood.
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing comparisons.
- Related work (KL/partial smoothness/RSC) could be better integrated.

Suggestions for improvement
- State explicitly that the rate is asymptotic and discuss conditions ensuring entry into the linear regime.
- Clarify step-size assumptions under backtracking and bounded level sets.
- Add discussion linking to partial smoothness/active manifold identification and KL-based linear rates.
- Include at least one real-data experiment or baseline comparisons.
- Fix minor typos and add brief sketches of deferred inequalities.

Overall recommendation
Accept (borderline-strong). The paper addresses a genuine open question with a clever and rigorous analysis, and its implications are relevant to a wide range of problems in machine learning involving low-rank structure. The exposition could be strengthened and experiments expanded, but the core contribution merits publication.
