### Summary

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard convergence analysis for PGM establishes a sublinear rate for general convex loss functions and a linear rate if the loss function is strongly convex. However, many practical loss functions (e.g., the square loss in matrix completion) are not strongly convex. This paper bridges this theoretical gap by proving that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate under a weaker condition. Specifically, it considers loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function (but not necessarily strongly convex itself).

The key to the analysis is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of trace norm regularized problems. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing this error bound for the trace norm is non-trivial due to the non-polyhedral nature of its epigraph. The authors overcome this challenge by leveraging a crucial structural property of the optimal solutions: an optimal matrix `X*` and the corresponding gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows the use of matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimum and ultimately establish the desired error bound. Building on this error bound, the linear convergence of PGM is then established using the framework of Luo and Tseng [10]. Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification problems are provided to corroborate the theoretical findings.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamentally important question in optimization for machine learning. PGM is a cornerstone algorithm for large-scale trace norm problems, and its convergence behavior under realistic, non-strongly convex settings is of great interest to both theorists and practitioners. This work provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM in this setting, significantly advancing our understanding of the algorithm's performance and potentially justifying its use over accelerated variants in certain regimes.

2.  **Strong Originality and Technical Contribution:** The main technical contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1)—is highly novel and resolves what the authors frame as an open problem. The proof technique is particularly insightful. Unlike analyses for `l1`-norm or group LASSO problems that rely on separability, this work introduces a new approach based on the spectral properties of the solutions and gradients. The observation about shared singular vectors (Proposition 4.2) and its use in conjunction with matrix perturbation theory is a clever and powerful method for handling the non-separability of the trace norm. This technical machinery is a valuable contribution in its own right and could potentially be extended to other matrix norm regularization problems.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears to be rigorous and correct. The paper follows a clear logical progression, first establishing the key error bound and then using it to prove the main convergence result. The argument is well-structured, and the reliance on the established framework by Luo and Tseng [10] places the work on solid theoretical footing.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem, reviewing prior work, and clearly stating the paper's contributions. The main results are presented cleanly, and the authors provide a high-level overview of the proof strategy before delving into the technical details, which greatly aids readability.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Clarity of the Core Proof:** While the overall structure is clear, the core of the proof for the error bound (Section 4.1) is extremely dense. The argument relies heavily on Proposition 4.3, whose proof is deferred to the supplementary material. Given that this proposition contains the key structural properties of the error term `Q̄`, the main paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why this decomposition holds and how the different cases (A, B, C) for the singular values arise. A brief, high-level sketch of the proof of Proposition 4.3 could significantly improve the self-containedness and accessibility of the paper's central argument.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The analysis establishes an *asymptotic* linear rate, which holds once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set (i.e., when `‖R(X)‖F ≤ ǫ`). This is a common feature of results based on local error bounds. The paper could be strengthened by a brief discussion on this "pre-asymptotic" phase. For example, what factors influence the size of the neighborhood `ǫ` where linear convergence takes hold? Is it possible for the sublinear phase to be prohibitively long in practice? The experimental plots indeed show this two-phase behavior, and connecting the theory to this observation would provide a more complete picture.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments effectively demonstrate the linear convergence predicted by the theory. However, they are somewhat limited. A valuable addition would be a comparison against an accelerated PGM (e.g., FISTA). While accelerated PGM has a better *global* sublinear rate (O(1/k^2)), the standard PGM's *asymptotic* linear rate should eventually dominate. Visualizing this crossover point would provide compelling evidence for the practical relevance of this work, showing when a practitioner might prefer the simpler PGM over its accelerated counterpart. Additionally, it is a minor point, but the notation for the regularization parameter should be consistent (`τ` in the theory, `µ` in the experiments).

4.  **Discussion of Assumptions:** The theory relies on Assumption 2.1(b), namely that the function `h` is strongly convex on any convex compact subset of its domain. This holds for common losses like square and logistic loss. A short discussion on the necessity and tightness of this assumption would be welcome. For example, would the result hold under strict convexity alone, or is strong convexity on compact sets essential for the analysis (e.g., to obtain the constant `σ` in inequality (15))?

### Decision

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. It resolves an important open question regarding the convergence of a widely used algorithm under practical assumptions. The technical novelty in the proof of the error bound is a standout feature. While there is room for improvement in the clarity of the most technical sections and the breadth of the experiments, these are minor points that do not detract from the importance of the core results. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and clearly written. I recommend acceptance.
