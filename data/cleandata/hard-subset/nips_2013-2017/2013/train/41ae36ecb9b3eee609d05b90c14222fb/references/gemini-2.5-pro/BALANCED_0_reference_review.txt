This paper studies the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications such as matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. The standard analysis of PGM establishes a sublinear O(1/k^2) convergence rate for general smooth convex loss functions and a linear rate when the loss function is strongly convex. This work addresses the significant gap between these two cases, focusing on problems of the form `min_X f(X) + τ‖X‖∗` where the loss `f(X)` has the composite structure `f(X) = h(A(X))`. Here, `h` is a strictly convex function, but `f` itself is not necessarily strongly convex (e.g., when the linear operator `A` is not injective).

The main contribution is a proof that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence under these weaker assumptions, which cover many practical loss functions like the square loss and logistic loss. The core of the analysis is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This error bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set with the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm was an open question, as prior techniques for `l1` or group LASSO regularization do not apply due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph. The authors overcome this by identifying and exploiting a crucial structural property of the optimal solutions related to their singular value decomposition, allowing them to use matrix perturbation theory to complete the analysis.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and highly relevant problem. Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone technique for inducing low-rank solutions, and PGM is a workhorse algorithm for solving the resulting optimization problems. Providing a theoretical justification for the linear convergence often observed in practice, without the restrictive strong convexity assumption, is a major contribution. It deepens our understanding of why these simple first-order methods work so well on a broad class of important machine learning tasks.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The primary technical contribution—the new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1)—is novel and appears to be a significant theoretical advance. The challenge of extending error bounds from polyhedral norms (like `l1`) to the trace norm is non-trivial, and the authors' approach of using the shared singular vector structure between the optimal solution `X*` and `-∇f(X*)` is both clever and powerful. The overall proof, which leverages this error bound within the established Luo and Tseng framework to show linear convergence, is technically sound and well-constructed.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work in the context of prior art and explicitly stating the main contribution and the technical hurdles overcome. The logical flow from the problem statement to the error bound and finally to the convergence theorem is easy to follow. This clarity makes a dense theoretical contribution much more accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Proof Details:** The proof of the central result (the error bound) is highly technical and relies on a series of lemmas and propositions (4.1, 4.2, 4.3). While this is necessary, many of the crucial proof details are deferred to the supplementary material. The claims, particularly the decomposition in Proposition 4.3 and the subsequent contradiction argument, are non-trivial. While the high-level sketch is convincing, a full assessment of the technical correctness hinges on the details in the supplement.

2.  **Numerical Experiments:** The experiments in Section 5 successfully illustrate the theoretical findings, showing the predicted linear convergence. However, they are somewhat basic. They serve as a proof of concept but miss an opportunity to provide deeper insights. For instance, a comparison with accelerated PGM (like FISTA) would have been valuable to explore the practical performance trade-offs between methods with different theoretical convergence rates in this specific setting.

3.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, meaning it is guaranteed to hold once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. This is a common feature of analyses based on local error bounds. The paper does not provide any characterization of this region of convergence, which can be important for practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first linear convergence guarantee for the standard PGM on a wide and practical class of trace norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity.
*   Resolves an open question by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, a result of independent interest.
*   The work is theoretically deep, and the technical approach for deriving the error bound is original and insightful.
*   The paper is written with outstanding clarity, making a complex topic understandable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The numerical experiments are purely illustrative and could be more comprehensive.
*   The reliance on the supplementary material for key proof steps makes full verification from the main paper difficult.

**Decision and Suggestions for Improvement:**

I recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant, original, and addresses a long-standing question of practical and theoretical importance. The clarity of the presentation is commendable. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract from the core value of the work.

For future versions of the manuscript, I would suggest the following improvements:

*   **Enhance Numerical Experiments:** Consider comparing PGM with accelerated PGM (FISTA). While FISTA has a better global rate for general convex problems, it would be interesting to see if PGM's linear convergence in the local region makes it competitive or even faster in practice on these problems. Also, plotting the residual norm `‖R(X_k)‖_F` could visually demonstrate its connection to the convergence rate.
*   **Provide More Intuition:** In Section 4.1, a bit more high-level intuition behind the key steps would be helpful. For example, a brief, intuitive explanation of why the limiting matrix `Q̄` must have the structure described in Proposition 4.3 could aid the reader in navigating the highly technical proof.
*   **Discuss Constants:** While likely beyond the scope of a conference paper, any discussion on what problem properties (e.g., properties of the operator `A`, the curvature of `h`) influence the constants `η`, `κ`, and `σ` would be valuable, as these constants govern the convergence factor and the size of the region of linear convergence.
