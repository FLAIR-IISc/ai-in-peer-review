

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems under a specific class of loss functions. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables them to prove linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a significant contribution as it extends the convergence analysis to a broader class of problems than previously known. The paper builds on the framework of Luo and Tseng (2002) and addresses an open question regarding error bounds for trace norm regularization.

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important theoretical gap in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for trace norm regularization.
- The error bound result (Theorem 3.1) appears to be novel and well-motivated.
- The numerical experiments support the theoretical claims for matrix completion and classification problems.
- The writing is generally clear and well-structured.

**Weaknesses (minor but cumulative):**

1. **Terminology and notation inconsistencies:**
   - The paper uses "trace norm" and "nuclear norm" interchangeably without establishing they are being used synonymously. While they are equivalent, this lack of consistency is confusing.
   - The regularization parameter is denoted by τ in the main text but by µ in the numerical experiments section (Section 5), creating confusion.
   - The paper uses en dashes (–) instead of hyphens (-) in multiple places (e.g., "non–empty" instead of "non-empty"), which is inconsistent with standard mathematical writing.

2. **Technical clarifications needed:**
   - Assumption 2.1(b) states "h is strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)," which is unusual. Typically, strong convexity is defined globally. The authors should clarify whether they mean "strongly convex on dom(h)" or "strongly convex on any convex compact subset."
   - In Theorem 3.1, v* is not defined in the theorem statement (it's defined in Section 2.2 as "the optimal value of (1)"), which is a minor but important omission.
   - Proposition 4.2 refers to "Ū and V̄" without defining them as the singular vectors of X̄ - Ḡ, which is critical to understanding the proposition.

3. **Proof and presentation issues:**
   - The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) claims uniqueness of the projection X̃k ∈ Xc but does not prove it. Uniqueness is essential for the argument but is simply assumed.
   - The paper states "a proof can be found in the supplementary material" for Proposition 4.3 without providing the supplementary material, which is common practice but creates a minor gap in the main paper's completeness.
   - In the numerical experiments, the paper states "n = 40" for matrix classification but doesn't specify the matrix dimensions or the number of samples, which would help readers assess the experimental setup.

4. **Citation and formatting issues:**
   - References [12] and [16] are cited but not properly formatted in the main text (e.g., "The sublinear O(1/k2) convergence rate is known to be optimal if f is simply given by a first–order oracle [12]").
   - Several references in the bibliography have incomplete information (e.g., "Proc. 24th ICML, pages 17–24, ," with an extra comma).
   - The abstract states "the trace norm is the tightest convex lower bound of the rank function over the set of matrices with spectral norm at most one" but this is slightly inaccurate - it should specify "with respect to the spectral norm constraint" or similar.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting and potentially significant theoretical contribution to the convergence analysis of first-order methods for trace norm regularization, the cumulative effect of numerous minor technical inconsistencies, notation errors, and unclear definitions diminishes its overall quality. As a perfectionist reviewer who values meticulous attention to detail, I find these issues particularly troubling because they suggest the paper may not have undergone sufficient polishing before submission.

The paper's theoretical contribution is meaningful, but the presentation flaws (particularly the inconsistent notation, unclear assumptions, and minor technical omissions) make it difficult to fully evaluate the mathematical content. These are not major flaws that invalidate the results, but they are significant enough to suggest the paper is not ready for publication in its current form.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The paper would be much stronger with careful attention to the minor technical and formatting details outlined above. As a reviewer, I believe these issues collectively indicate that the paper lacks the level of rigor and precision expected for NIPS publication, despite the potentially valuable theoretical contribution. The authors should address these concerns thoroughly before resubmitting.
