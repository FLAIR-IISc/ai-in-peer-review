- Paper proves linear convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for trace-norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss.
- Key technical contribution: a novel Lipschitzian error bound derived via matrix perturbation theory and shared singular structure of optima and gradients.
- Result broadens applicability to losses like square and logistic loss, relevant for matrix completion and low-rank learning.
- Theoretical analysis is significant and original, addressing an important open problem in optimization.
- Proof is rigorous but dense (notably Lemma 4.1), potentially inaccessible without strong background; more intuition would help.
- Experimental validation shows observed linear convergence but is limited in scope and simplicity.
- Experiments lack challenging scenarios, parameter sweeps, comparisons to other algorithms (e.g., accelerated PGM), and metrics like reconstruction error.
- Paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear motivation and precise assumptions.
- Practical implications (e.g., step-size choice, stopping criteria) are under-discussed and should be expanded.
- The loss form assumption f(X)=h(A(X)) is somewhat restrictive; discussion on extending to more general losses is needed.
- Quality scored 9/10 (technically sound), clarity 7/10 (dense proofs), originality 9/10, significance 8/10.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical result, rigorous analysis, broader applicability, clear writing, some empirical support.
- Arguments against acceptance: highly technical proofs may limit accessibility, limited experimental evaluation, insufficient practical discussion.
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add more intuition in proofs, expand experiments, and discuss practical implications.
