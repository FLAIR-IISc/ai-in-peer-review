Summary
The paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for nuclear-norm (trace norm) regularized problems of the form minX f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, and locally strongly convex on compact sets (with Lipschitz gradient on its open domain). The main contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound: in a bounded level set and for sufficiently small proximal gradient residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X, the distance to the optimal set dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F. Leveraging Luo–Tseng’s framework, the authors deduce asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (R-linear in iterates and Q-linear in objective values) under stepsizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/L.

The key technical insight is a spectral alignment property: at any optimal solution X̄, both X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors (via the characterization X̄ = Sτ(X̄ − ∇f(X̄))). This allows a careful perturbation analysis near X*, yielding a “restricted invertibility” inequality ||X − ProjX*(X)||F ≤ κ ||A(X − ProjX*(X))||2 locally, which is the crux of the error bound. Numerical experiments on matrix completion (least-squares loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) corroborate linear convergence in the final phase.

Relation to prior work
- PGM sublinear rates and acceleration are standard; linear rates typically require strong convexity of f. Here, f need not be strongly convex due to possible non-injectivity of A (e.g., matrix completion), yet linear convergence is shown.
- Luo–Tseng-type error bounds have been proven for composite objectives with polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group Lasso). Nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, and extending such EB has been an open question. The paper’s EB resolves this for trace-norm regularization under the stated structure on f.
- The approach is distinct from restricted strong convexity-based analyses often used in statistics (e.g., for high-dimensional M-estimation) and from KL-based generic linear convergence results; it provides a problem-specific EB via spectral structure.

Quality
- The main theorem (local error bound) is convincing and technically nontrivial. The pivotal Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −∇f(X̄)) is correctly motivated by the proximal characterization: since X̄ = Sτ(Ȳ) with Ȳ = X̄ − ∇f(X̄), X̄ shares the singular vectors of Ȳ, and thus −∇f(X̄) = Ȳ − X̄ is also diagonal in the same basis.
- The contradiction argument establishing restricted invertibility is elegant: if a near-optimal point had a large component in ker(A), one could move within Xc (the set of optimal solutions aligned with Ū, V̄) to reduce the projection distance, contradicting optimality of the projection.
- The assumptions on h (Lipschitz gradient; local strong convexity on compacts) are satisfied by square and logistic losses and justify the strong monotonicity inequality on bounded Z.
- The convergence result follows cleanly from Luo–Tseng’s framework once the EB is in place.

That said, some technical aspects would benefit from clarification:
- Treatment of singular value multiplicities: the simultaneous diagonalization argument should discuss how to select Ū, V̄ in the presence of eigenspace multiplicities, and ensure the stability used in the perturbation steps.
- The stepsize condition for linear convergence requires 0 < α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf uniformly. The experiments use backtracking; the paper should explicitly argue that, under global Lipschitz continuity of ∇f, standard backtracking yields a uniform positive lower bound on αk.

Clarity
- The paper is overall well organized and readable for an expert audience; the proof roadmap is clear. The notational setup in Section 4 is dense but standard for this literature.
- Minor issues: inconsistent use of τ/µ in experiments; figure numbering (Figure 1 vs. Figure 2) appears inconsistent; a brief reminder that proxταk is implemented via shrinkage Sταk would help reconcile (3), (5), (6).

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first EB and linear convergence result for nuclear norm regularization under the f(X) = h(A(X)) structure without global strong convexity of f. The spectral alignment insight and the restricted invertibility lemma are novel and technically interesting.
- The work complements generic KL-based linear convergence results by delivering a sharp, problem-structured EB and identifying the geometric mechanism (alignment of singular subspaces) behind linear rates for the nuclear norm.

Significance
- Nuclear norm regularization is ubiquitous (matrix completion, multitask learning, low-rank classification). Establishing linear convergence for a widely used first-order method under realistic loss structures is valuable for both theory and practice.
- The EB itself is likely to be useful beyond PGM (e.g., for inexact variants, coordinate methods, or other splitting schemes).
- The numerical section is modest, but it illustrates the predicted linear regime. A more comprehensive empirical study (larger-scale problems; comparison to accelerated PG and ADMM; sensitivity to initialization) would strengthen the practical message but is not essential for this theoretical contribution.

Pros
- Resolves an open question: EB and linear convergence for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- Clean, conceptually appealing analysis leveraging spectral structure.
- Broad applicability to common losses (least squares, logistic).
- Clear link to and appropriate use of Luo–Tseng framework.

Cons
- Linear rate is local/asymptotic; no quantitative constants or practical guidance on when the linear regime starts.
- Some technical details (multiplicities, step-size lower bound under backtracking) should be clarified.
- Experimental section is limited and contains minor presentation issues.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Clarify the handling of singular value multiplicities in Proposition 4.2 and the subsequent decomposition (Proposition 4.3).
- State explicitly how standard backtracking ensures a uniform positive lower bound on αk under global Lipschitz ∇f.
- Discuss connections to KL/partial smoothness frameworks and whether your EB implies KL exponent 1/2 locally for this class.
- Comment on possible extensions to other unitarily invariant norms or composite spectral regularizers.

Recommendation
This is a solid theoretical paper with a novel and meaningful contribution. I recommend acceptance, contingent on minor clarifications.
