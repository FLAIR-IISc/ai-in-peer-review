## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) for a linear operator A and a strictly convex function h. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions on h (smoothness and curvature), *without* requiring strong convexity of the loss function f itself – a significant departure from existing theoretical results. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored for trace norm regularization, circumventing difficulties stemming from the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.  The results are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification problems showing linear convergence in practice.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:**  The result of establishing linear convergence without strong convexity on *f* is a substantial advance. This broadens the applicability of PGM to a wider class of problems, particularly in machine learning where strongly convex loss functions are not always available.
* **Novel Error Bound:** Deriving a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization is a crucial step, and the authors successfully navigate the challenges posed by its non-polyhedral epigraph.  The use of matrix perturbation theory and recognizing the shared singular structure of optimal solutions and gradients is clever.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the contributions. The notation is reasonably consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence on relevant tasks.
* **Well-Connected to Existing Literature:** The paper positions its work within the existing literature on PGM, trace norm regularization, and related convergence analysis techniques, referencing key papers appropriately.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity:** While the core ideas are clearly presented, the proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and its associated lemmas (especially Lemma 4.1) are quite dense and require significant effort to follow in detail. A more streamlined presentation, or more detailed intermediate steps, could improve accessibility.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments are a good starting point, but are relatively limited in scope. Exploring a wider range of problem instances (different matrix sizes, rank values, and sampling ratios) and comparing performance against other solvers would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Backtracking Line Search:** The use of a backtracking line search in the matrix classification experiment is mentioned, but details are sparse. A discussion of the impact of the line search on the observed convergence rate would be valuable.  The reliance on this in practice suggests the stated theoretical guarantees might be sensitive to step size choice.
* **Proposition 4.2 – Lack of Intuition:** While technically correct, the importance and intuition behind Proposition 4.2 are not fully explained.  A more thorough discussion of *why* the optimal solutions share singular vectors would enhance understanding.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of PGM convergence for trace norm regularized problems. The authors address a challenging open question and provide a rigorous analysis supported by empirical results. While the proof details are complex and the numerical experiments could be more extensive, the core ideas are well-motivated and the results are likely to be of considerable interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a substantial contribution.
* **Significance:**  The result has broad implications for the analysis of PGM in machine learning applications.
* **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous and the theoretical results are supported by numerical evidence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Proof Complexity:** The technical details of the proof may be inaccessible to some readers.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope.

**Rating:**

**Strong Accept (8/10)**.  This paper represents a valuable addition to the literature and merits acceptance to NIPS.  Minor revisions addressing the clarity of the proofs and the expansion of the numerical experiments could further enhance its impact.
