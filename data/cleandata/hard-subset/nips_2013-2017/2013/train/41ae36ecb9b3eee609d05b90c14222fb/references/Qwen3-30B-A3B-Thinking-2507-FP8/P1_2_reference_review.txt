

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental question in convex optimization for low-rank matrix recovery: under what conditions can we establish linear convergence for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems? Previous work established linear convergence when the loss function f is strongly convex, but the authors show that linear convergence can be achieved under weaker conditions when f takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h satisfies certain smoothness and curvature properties. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves an open question about whether such a bound exists for trace norm (unlike for ℓ₁-norm or group LASSO regularization). This error bound, combined with the framework of Luo and Tseng, establishes linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that extends beyond prior work on error bounds for composite optimization problems. It relates to recent NIPS papers like "An Accelerated Gradient Method for Trace Norm Minimization" (Ji & Ye, 2009) and "Convergence Rates of Inexact Proximal-Gradient Methods" (Schmidt et al., 2011), but addresses a key gap in the convergence analysis for trace norm problems.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper provides a novel error bound for trace norm regularization that is both theoretically sound and practically significant. The authors successfully overcome the challenges of the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm by leveraging matrix perturbation theory and a key observation about the singular vectors of optimal solutions.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The proof is carefully constructed, with clear development of the key lemma and thorough treatment of the technical challenges. The authors properly contextualize their approach in relation to previous work on error bounds for ℓ₁-norm and group LASSO regularization.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems effectively demonstrate linear convergence in practice, aligning with the theoretical claims. The semi-log plots clearly show the linear convergence behavior once the iterates are close to the optimal solution set.

4. **Applicability**: The conditions under which linear convergence is established are quite general and cover many important machine learning problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.) where the loss function is not strongly convex but can be expressed as h(A(X)).

## Weaknesses

1. **Assumption Scope**: While the authors provide a comprehensive analysis, the requirement that h be strictly convex and satisfy certain smoothness properties might be restrictive for some applications. It would be valuable to discuss how these assumptions might be relaxed or whether they are necessary for the error bound to hold.

2. **Practical Implementation**: The paper doesn't address how to efficiently compute the Lipschitz constant Lf in practice, which is crucial for implementing the PGM with appropriate step sizes. The numerical experiments use fixed step sizes or backtracking line search, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to choose these parameters effectively.

3. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The numerical experiments are limited to just two problems (matrix completion and matrix classification). More extensive experiments across different problem sizes, ranks, and data distributions would strengthen the empirical validation.

4. **Connection to Recent Work**: The paper could better connect to more recent advances in low-rank optimization, such as non-convex approaches (e.g., "Guaranteed Minimum-Rank Solutions of Linear Matrix Equations via Nuclear Norm Minimization" by Recht et al., 2010) or adaptive step size methods for trace norm problems.

## Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The theoretical contributions are significant and properly validated by the numerical experiments. The authors are careful to acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though some of the technical details in the proof may be challenging for non-experts. The mathematical development is thorough and accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization.

**Originality**: The paper addresses an open question in the field (whether a Lipschitzian error bound exists for trace norm regularization) and provides a novel solution. This represents a clear advancement over previous work on error bounds for composite optimization.

**Significance**: The results have significant implications for the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for low-rank matrix problems, which are widely used in machine learning. The weaker convergence conditions make the results applicable to a broader range of problems than previous linear convergence results.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization methods for low-rank matrix problems. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The results have potential impact on both theory and practice in machine learning.

While the paper has minor weaknesses in the scope of assumptions and experimental validation, these do not detract from the overall significance of the theoretical contribution. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance**: 
- Novel theoretical contribution resolving an open question
- Rigorous proofs and clear mathematical development
- Applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems
- Supported by empirical validation

**Arguments against acceptance**: 
- Some restrictive assumptions on the loss function
- Limited numerical experiments
- No discussion of practical parameter selection

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization for machine learning and deserves publication at NeurIPS.
