This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically when the loss function is composed of a linear operator applied to the matrix variable and a strictly convex function. The authors establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the overall loss function, a significant contribution given the prevalence of non-strongly convex loss functions in applications like matrix completion. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and addresses an important open question.

**Summary of Contributions:** The authors demonstrate linear convergence of the PGM under relatively mild assumptions on the loss function, circumventing the typical requirement of strong convexity. This is achieved through a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace norm regularization, which is non-trivial due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The result broadens the applicability of the PGM to a wider range of machine learning problems.  The paper builds on existing work in proximal algorithms (Beck & Teboulle, 2009), convergence analysis (Luo & Tseng, 1992), and low-rank matrix recovery (Candes & Recht, 2009; Recht et al., 2010) but importantly extends the convergence guarantees to a broader class of loss functions.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:**  The linear convergence result for non-strongly convex loss functions is a significant theoretical advance.  It addresses a gap in the literature and has practical implications for various applications.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous proof based on a novel Lipschitzian error bound. The derivation appears careful and well-supported by existing results in convex analysis and optimization.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the assumptions are explicitly stated.  The motivation is well-explained, and the key ideas are presented in a logical manner.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:**  The authors provide a thorough overview of related work, appropriately citing relevant papers in optimization, matrix factorization, and machine learning.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification, demonstrating the empirically observed linear convergence, strengthens the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions on 'h':** Assumption 2.1(b), requiring strong convexity of *h* on any convex compact subset of its domain, seems potentially restrictive. While justified in the context of applications cited, a discussion of the sensitivity of the result to weaker conditions on *h* would be valuable.
*   **Complexity of the Proof:** While rigorous, the proof (especially the Lemma 4.1 and its proof sketch in the supplementary material) is quite involved. While detail is important, making it more accessible without sacrificing rigor would improve readability.
*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** While the experiments are encouraging, they only consider two specific problems. Exploring the convergence behavior on a wider range of loss functions and datasets would further validate the theoretical results.
*   **Practical Implications of Step Size:** The theoretical guarantee in Theorem 3.2 requires a carefully chosen step size αk.  A discussion of practical strategies for selecting αk in real-world applications, beyond the theoretical bounds, would be helpful.
*   **Connection to Existing Acceleration Techniques:** The introduction mentions acceleration techniques yielding O(1/k^2) convergence.  Discussing whether the linear convergence result can be similarly accelerated, or why acceleration might not be applicable in this setting, would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result addressing a key open problem in the convergence analysis of proximal gradient methods.
*   The technical arguments are sound and well-supported.
*   The results have the potential to impact a wide range of machine learning applications.
*   The inclusion of empirical validation strengthens the credibility of the findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong convexity assumption on *h* might be limiting.
*   The complexity of the proof could hinder broader adoption of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The theoretical results are significant, the presentation is clear, and the empirical validation is encouraging.  While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept**. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   Discuss the sensitivity of the results to weaker assumptions on the function *h*.
*   Consider strategies to improve the accessibility of the proof without sacrificing rigor.
*   Expand the numerical experiments to include a wider range of loss functions and datasets.
*   Provide guidance on practical step size selection strategies.
*   Explore the possibility of accelerating the convergence rate further.
