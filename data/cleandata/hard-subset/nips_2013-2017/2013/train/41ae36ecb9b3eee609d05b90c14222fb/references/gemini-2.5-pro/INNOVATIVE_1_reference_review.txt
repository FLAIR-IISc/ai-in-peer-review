This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. It is well-known that PGM achieves a linear convergence rate if the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is strongly convex. However, this assumption is often too restrictive for many practical problems where `f(X)` is convex but not strongly convex (e.g., the square loss in matrix completion when the measurement operator is underdetermined).

The main contribution of this work is to establish, for the first time, an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM on this class of problems without requiring strong convexity. The authors consider objective functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function. The core of their analysis is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. This bound relates the distance of an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map measuring the violation of optimality. Establishing such a bound has been an open question, as prior techniques for similar problems (e.g., L1-norm regularization) relied on separability properties that do not apply to the trace norm. The authors overcome this significant technical hurdle by exploiting a key structural property of the optimal solution: the optimal point `X*` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows them to leverage matrix perturbation theory to control the spectral structure of points near the optimum, leading to the desired error bound. The linear convergence result then follows by applying this bound within the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and important open problem in optimization for machine learning. Proving linear convergence for PGM under realistic assumptions that go beyond strong convexity is a significant theoretical advance. The result provides a much-needed theoretical justification for the strong empirical performance of PGM on problems like matrix completion. This work deepens our understanding of first-order methods and is likely to be influential.

2.  **Novel and Bold Technical Approach:** The central contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm—is highly novel. The existing analysis frameworks for L1-type problems fail here due to the non-separable nature of singular values. The authors' approach of analyzing the shared spectral structure of the solution and the gradient is an elegant and powerful new idea. This represents a genuine conceptual leap and provides a new tool for analyzing matrix optimization problems, which could be of independent interest to the community.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly situates the problem within the literature, articulates the gap it aims to fill, and presents its main contributions upfront. The proof sketch in Section 4 is particularly helpful, guiding the reader through the main arguments and highlighting the key new ingredients (Proposition 4.2) without getting lost in excessive detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The established linear convergence is asymptotic, meaning it is only guaranteed to hold once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. While the numerical experiments suggest this convergence kicks in relatively early, the theory does not provide explicit information about the size of this region of convergence or the magnitude of the constants involved. A discussion on how problem parameters (e.g., properties of `A`, curvature of `h`) might influence these constants would strengthen the paper, though this is admittedly a very challenging extension.

2.  **Illustrative Experiments:** The numerical experiments serve well to confirm the theoretical findings but are not exhaustive. They demonstrate the linear convergence on standard problems but do not include comparisons against other state-of-the-art solvers or explore the performance on a wider range of problem sizes and structures. However, for a primarily theoretical paper introducing a foundational result, this is a minor limitation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Solves an important open problem** in the convergence analysis of a widely used algorithm (PGM) for a popular class of problems (trace norm regularization).
*   **Introduces a significant new theoretical tool**—a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm—which was previously unavailable.
*   **The proof technique is highly novel and insightful**, moving beyond standard analysis methods and opening a new direction for analyzing matrix-valued optimization problems.
*   The work represents a **foundational contribution** that advances the state of the art in optimization theory relevant to NIPS.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The practical implications are not fully explored; the analysis is asymptotic and the experiments are illustrative.

**Recommendation:**

This is an outstanding theoretical paper that makes a bold and significant contribution. The core idea of using the shared spectral structure to derive an error bound for the trace norm is a genuine breakthrough. While methodological perfectionists might point to the asymptotic nature of the result or the limited scope of the experiments, these are minor points in the face of the paper's novelty and importance. This work substantially advances our theoretical understanding of a core class of optimization problems in machine learning. It is exactly the kind of foundational and conceptually novel research that NIPS should showcase. I strongly recommend acceptance.
