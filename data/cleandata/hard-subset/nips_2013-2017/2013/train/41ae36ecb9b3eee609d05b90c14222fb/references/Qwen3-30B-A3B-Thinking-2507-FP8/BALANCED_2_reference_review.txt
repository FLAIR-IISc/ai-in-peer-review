

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses an important gap in the convergence analysis of proximal gradient methods (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish linear convergence of PGM for problems of the form min_X {h(A(X)) + τ||X||_*}, where h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties, without requiring strong convexity of the overall objective. This is significant because many important machine learning problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning) use loss functions that are not strongly convex but fit the authors' framework.

The main contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which the authors establish through a careful analysis of the spectral structure of optimal solutions. They show that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors, enabling them to apply matrix perturbation theory to derive the error bound. This resolves an open question that has been noted in the literature (e.g., in [18]), as previous error bounds for similar regularization terms (like ℓ1-norm) did not extend to trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical Contribution:** The error bound is a substantial technical advance that fills a gap in the literature. The proof is carefully constructed, with the key insight about shared singular vectors being particularly elegant.

2. **Relevance:** The result extends linear convergence guarantees to a much broader class of problems than previously possible, directly addressing a practical limitation in the application of PGM to matrix problems.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, with clear organization and precise mathematical statements. The connection between the error bound and the convergence result is logically presented.

4. **Validation:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide good empirical support for the theoretical claims, showing linear convergence behavior in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Loss Functions:** The paper assumes f(X) = h(A(X)), which covers important cases but excludes other common loss functions (e.g., those involving non-linear transformations of the matrix). The authors should discuss the limitations of this framework and whether their approach could be extended to more general loss functions.

2. **Comparative Analysis:** The numerical experiments are limited to two problems and don't compare with alternative methods that might have different convergence properties (e.g., alternating minimization for matrix completion). A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the empirical claims.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't explore how sensitive the linear convergence rate is to the choice of step size or other algorithm parameters. A brief discussion of this would be valuable for practitioners.

4. **Relation to Recent Work:** The paper doesn't discuss how their results relate to recent advances in non-strongly convex optimization, such as the work by Lin et al. on "local linear convergence" for non-strongly convex problems. This would better situate their contribution in the current literature.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

1. The authors should explicitly discuss the limitations of their framework regarding loss functions and consider whether their approach could be extended to more general cases (e.g., f(X) = h(A(X)) + g(X) where g is another regularizer).

2. Adding a comparison with alternative methods (e.g., alternating minimization, ADMM) on the matrix completion problem would provide stronger empirical validation.

3. Including a brief analysis of how the linear convergence rate depends on the problem parameters (e.g., rank, condition number) would make the results more actionable for practitioners.

4. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their error bound relates to recent developments in error bounds for non-smooth optimization.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. The technical contribution is significant, the proof is sound, and the results are relevant to many important machine learning applications. While the scope is somewhat limited (only considering specific loss function forms), the paper successfully addresses an important open question in the literature.

The paper meets NeurIPS standards for quality and originality. The significance is high, as the results provide a theoretical foundation for the widespread use of trace norm regularization in machine learning. The clarity is excellent, and the writing is accessible to the target audience.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a novel and technically sound contribution that advances the theoretical understanding of proximal gradient methods for matrix regularization problems. The results are significant for both theoretical and applied machine learning communities. The minor weaknesses identified can be addressed in a revised version, but they do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.
