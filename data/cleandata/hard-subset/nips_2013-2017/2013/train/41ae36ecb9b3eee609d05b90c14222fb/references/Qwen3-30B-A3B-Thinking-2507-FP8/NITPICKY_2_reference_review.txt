

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables them to prove linear convergence of PGM without requiring strong convexity of the objective function. This is a significant contribution as it extends the applicability of linear convergence guarantees to a broader class of loss functions used in machine learning (square loss, logistic loss, etc.).

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses an important theoretical gap in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for trace norm regularization.
- The proof technique, particularly the use of matrix perturbation theory and the key observation about the singular vectors of optimal solutions, is novel and well-executed.
- The numerical experiments support the theoretical claims, showing linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and matrix classification problems.
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with proper context provided for the problem and related work.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, in Section 2.1, the loss function is written as both "f(X) = h(A(X))" and "f(·) = h(A(·))". The title uses "trace norm" while the text alternates between "trace norm" and "nuclear norm" without clarification. The definition of the trace norm in the introduction states "trace norm1" with a superscript 1, which appears to be a formatting error rather than a footnote reference.

2. **Technical Nuance in Assumption 2.1(b):** The authors claim that Assumption 2.1(b) (h is strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)) implies strict convexity on dom(h). This is not strictly true; strong convexity on compact subsets does not necessarily imply strict convexity on the entire domain (though it does imply strict convexity on the relative interior of dom(h)). This subtle point could lead to confusion in the theoretical development.

3. **Misapplication of Fermat's Rule:** In the proof of Lemma 4.1, the authors cite [15, Theorem 10.1] for Fermat's rule. However, Theorem 10.1 in Rockafellar and Wets' "Variational Analysis" concerns the subdifferential of the sum of functions, not Fermat's rule. Fermat's rule is typically stated as 0 ∈ ∂f(x*) for a minimizer x*, which is a more basic result. The incorrect reference undermines the technical precision of the paper.

4. **Reference Formatting Errors:** Several references in the bibliography contain formatting errors, such as "pages 17–24, ," (with a trailing comma) and inconsistent use of "Proc." versus "Proceedings." These are minor but indicate a lack of attention to detail.

5. **Confusing Notation in Theorem 3.1:** The error bound is stated as "dist(X,X) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F". The notation is confusing because X appears to be both the variable and the optimal solution set. This should be clarified as "dist(X, X*)" or "dist(X, X̄)" to avoid ambiguity.

6. **Numerical Experiment Inconsistency:** In the matrix classification section, the paper states "p = ⌊θn²⌋/2" but the floor function should apply to the entire expression, i.e., p = ⌊θn²/2⌋. The current notation implies floor(θn²) is computed first, then divided by 2, which could lead to implementation errors.

7. **Figure Reference Error:** The paper refers to "Figure 1" in the matrix completion section and "Figure 2" in the matrix classification section, but the text does not include any figure captions or references to these figures, creating a disconnect between the text and the paper's visual content.

**Recommendation:**
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution with clear practical implications, the accumulation of minor technical and formatting inconsistencies significantly detracts from its quality as a scientific contribution. The errors in referencing, notation, and terminology, while individually minor, collectively indicate a lack of careful proofreading that is unacceptable for a NeurIPS submission. The paper's main contribution is valuable, but the attention to detail required for publication at NeurIPS is not met.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but persistent flaws that undermine the paper's technical precision and presentation quality. A more carefully proofread version addressing these issues would be suitable for resubmission.
