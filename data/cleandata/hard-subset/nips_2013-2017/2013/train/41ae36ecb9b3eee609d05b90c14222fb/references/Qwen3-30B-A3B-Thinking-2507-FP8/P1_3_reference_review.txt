

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization: under what conditions can we establish linear convergence for the proximal gradient method (PGM) when solving trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) for a linear operator A and a strictly convex function h. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems, which they use to prove linear convergence of PGM without requiring strong convexity of f. This is significant because many machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning) involve loss functions that are not strongly convex despite having the form f(X) = h(A(X)). The key technical contribution is a novel error bound that overcomes the challenge of trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph, which previously prevented the application of error bound techniques used for ℓ1-norm regularization.

The paper builds on the framework of Luo and Tseng (1992) for error bounds in composite optimization, but develops new techniques to handle the spectral structure of the trace norm. The authors prove that for the optimal solution X̄, both X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors, which enables the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound.

## Strengths

**Quality (Excellent):** The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs. The main contribution (Theorem 3.1) is carefully established through a series of lemmas, with the critical restricted invertibility property (Lemma 4.1c) being particularly well-developed. The proof strategy overcomes significant technical challenges inherent to trace norm regularization. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems complement the theoretical results well, showing linear convergence in practice.

**Originality (High):** This paper addresses a well-known gap in the literature. Prior error bounds for composite optimization typically applied to problems with polyhedral epigraphs (like ℓ1-norm), but the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph had prevented similar results. The authors' insight that optimal solutions and their gradients share singular vectors is novel and enables the new error bound. The paper properly contextualizes this contribution by comparing to related work (e.g., [22, 23, 25]) and clearly explaining why prior techniques don't apply to trace norm.

**Significance (High):** Trace norm regularization is widely used across machine learning (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity of f is highly significant for practitioners. The result applies to important loss functions like square loss and logistic loss, which are commonly used in matrix completion and classification. The error bound itself could be valuable for future work on other matrix regularization problems.

**Clarity (Good):** The paper is well-organized with clear motivation, careful setup, and logical flow. The main contributions are well-articulated. The technical content is presented with appropriate mathematical precision. Some sections (particularly in the proof of Lemma 4.1) might benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key insights, but the mathematical arguments are sound.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope:** The paper establishes convergence for the specific class of problems where f(X) = h(A(X)). While this covers many important applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning), it doesn't cover all possible trace norm-regularized problems. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it would be helpful to discuss how their results might extend to broader classes of loss functions.

**Technical Depth in Proofs:** While the main theorems are well-proven, some technical steps in the proof of Lemma 4.1 (particularly Proposition 4.3) are somewhat condensed. A brief explanation of the intuition behind the singular value classification (Cases A, B, C) would make the proof more accessible.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper cites relevant work on error bounds (e.g., [22, 23, 25]), but it could better position itself against more recent advances in non-strongly convex optimization, such as the work by Lan (2015) or the recent NIPS papers on linear convergence for non-strongly convex problems.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the optimization of trace norm-regularized problems. It resolves an important open question about linear convergence of first-order methods for this class of problems, which has practical implications for many machine learning applications. The technical development of the error bound is novel and well-executed, and the numerical experiments provide good empirical validation.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. The quality is excellent, the significance is high, the originality is strong, and the clarity is good (with room for minor improvements). The authors have carefully addressed the limitations of their approach and properly contextualized their work within the existing literature.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a clear, important contribution to the theory of optimization for machine learning. It addresses a well-known gap in the literature with a technically sound approach, and the results have broad applicability across machine learning. The paper is well-written, properly contextualized, and provides both theoretical and empirical support for its claims.

The main strengths of the paper are the novel error bound for trace norm regularization (which appears to be the first such result), the clean proof strategy that overcomes the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm, and the practical significance of the linear convergence result for machine learning applications. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.

I believe this paper would be of great interest to the NIPS community and would make a valuable addition to the proceedings.
