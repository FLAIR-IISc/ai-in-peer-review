Summary
The paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems of the form minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where the loss has structure f(X)=h(A(X)), with A linear and h continuously differentiable, Lipschitz-gradient, strictly convex and strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain. This setting captures square and logistic losses used in matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification, even when A is rank-deficient so f is not strongly convex. The main technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound: near the solution set X (and within a level set), the distance to X is bounded by the residual norm ||Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X||F. Combining this error bound with the Luo-Tseng analysis yields asymptotic linear convergence of PGM: Q-linear in objective values and R-linear in iterates for step sizes bounded in (0, 1/Lf). The key novelty is handling the nonpolyhedral nuclear norm by exploiting a spectral alignment property at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors), and using matrix perturbation arguments to establish a restricted invertibility property along near-optimal sequences. Synthetic experiments on matrix completion (square loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) show an evident linear phase in both objective and iterate convergence once close to the solution set.

Relation to prior work
The paper builds on: (i) standard PGM rate results (O(1/k), O(1/k^2) with acceleration, linear rates under strong convexity), (ii) error bound frameworks for composite convex problems (Luo and Tseng), and (iii) error bounds for problems with polyhedral or separable regularizers (ℓ1, group lasso). The nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, and variables are spectrally coupled, so separability-based arguments do not apply. The presented error bound fills a gap and, to my knowledge, is not available for nuclear-norm regularization under such general A. The paper also relates to practical algorithms for nuclear-norm problems (SVT/FISTA variants) but focuses on theory.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: an asymptotic Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization without strong convexity of f, leading to linear PGM rates.
- Technically nontrivial and insightful spectral analysis: simultaneous SVD at optimality and decomposition of limiting directions to prove restricted invertibility.
- Broad applicability: covers square/logistic losses widely used in ML; does not assume injectivity of A or uniqueness of the solution.
- The convergence result is clean and leverages a well-known framework (Luo-Tseng), making it easy to interpret and potentially extend.
- Empirical evidence on two canonical tasks corroborating a linear phase.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Local/asymptotic nature: The error bound requires both a level-set condition and small residual. The region of attraction and constants are not quantified, limiting practical guidance. Suggest discussing how one might detect entry into the linear regime or bound the neighborhood size in terms of problem data (e.g., curvature of h on a bounded set, properties of A).
- Step-size conditions: The theorem requires αk < 1/Lf, but the matrix completion experiment uses α=1, which equals 1/Lf for a sampling operator with Lf=1. Please clarify Lf for your operators and ensure experimental step sizes satisfy the theorem’s conditions (or add a remark that convergence still holds at the boundary and why).
- Scope of h: Assumption 2.1(b) (“strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)”) is unusual. It holds for square and logistic losses, but a short lemma illustrating this for logistic loss (and any other intended examples) would help readers.
- Missing constants and proof details: Several key inequalities (kappa1–kappa3 in Theorem 3.2) are deferred. A brief proof sketch in the main text for these standard bounds would improve self-containment. Also, more intuition about Proposition 4.2 (why the subgradient relation forces simultaneous SVD) would help non-experts.
- Experiments are minimal: only small-scale synthetic problems, no timing or comparisons with alternative solvers, and no exploration of how the linear rate depends on τ, rank, sampling ratio, or noise. Consider adding: (i) wall-clock/time-to-accuracy plots, (ii) comparisons to accelerated PGM (with/without restarts) and proximal quasi-Newton methods, (iii) sensitivity to τ and condition of A, and (iv) larger-scale results or truncated SVD implementations.
- Extensions and context: It would be valuable to discuss the relation to general error-bound/Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz (KL) theory for semi-algebraic convex functions, under which local linear rates for proximal gradient can also be derived. Positioning your result relative to KL exponents and explaining why your bound is sharper or more informative for nuclear norm would broaden impact.
- Notational consistency: τ vs μ for the regularization parameter, ζ vs z̄, and occasional minor inconsistencies could be cleaned up.

Quality
Overall technically sound with a convincing high-level argument and appropriate use of established frameworks. Some steps are deferred and constants are not explicit, but the logic is coherent. The spectral arguments are delicate; additional details/intuition would strengthen trust.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The introduction motivates the problem and contribution clearly. Suggestions above would improve accessibility.

Originality
High. Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound and linear PGM convergence for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial.

Significance
Meaningful theoretical advance for a widely used model class. The result explains the commonly observed linear phase of PGM in practice and may inform algorithmic design (e.g., restarts, step-size selection). Stronger experimental validation would further demonstrate impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can your analysis be adapted to cover backtracking step sizes (as used for logistic loss) and still guarantee linear rates once the step size stabilizes below 1/Lf?
- Can you relate the constants (η, ε) in the error bound to problem data (τ, curvature bounds of h on Z, and norms of A)?
- How does the result extend to accelerated PGM/FISTA (with or without restart)? Is local linear convergence preserved?
- Do similar arguments extend to other unitarily invariant norms (e.g., Schatten p, group nuclear norms)?

Pros
- Novel error bound and linear convergence result for a central ML regularizer
- Solid and insightful analysis leveraging spectral structure
- Applicable to important loss models (square, logistic)
- Clean connection to established convergence frameworks

Cons
- Local result with unquantified constants
- Limited experiments and minor mismatch with step-size condition
- Some proofs deferred; clarity can be improved

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is original and relevant to the NeurIPS community, and the analysis is interesting and likely to be built upon. Strengthening empirical validation and clarifying minor theoretical details would make it a clear accept.
