Summary
The paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems of the form minX f(X)+τ||X||*, focusing on losses f(X)=h(A(X)) where A is linear and h is strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient and “locally strongly convex” (strongly convex on any compact subset of dom h). The main technical contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound: near the optimal set X*, dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F where R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X is the residual. Combining this with the Luo–Tseng framework yields asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (Q-linear in objective values, R-linear in iterates), without requiring f to be globally strongly convex (A may be rank-deficient). The proof hinges on a spectral alignment property at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors) and a “restricted invertibility” estimate linking ||X−X̄||F and ||A(X−X̄)||2. Synthetic experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification show linear-like convergence near the solution.

Relation to prior work
Prior linear-convergence results for composite optimization typically rely on strong convexity or error bounds for polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO). The authors position their work as the first linear convergence result for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f, by establishing an error bound in the non-polyhedral case. They build on the Luo–Tseng analysis and related error-bound literature. There is related statistical optimization work that achieves linear rates via restricted strong convexity (RSC) and decomposability for low-rank models, but those impose curvature on f; this paper claims to avoid such assumptions.

Strengths
- A clear theoretical target: obtain linear rates for PGM with nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- A nontrivial and interesting error bound for the nuclear norm despite its non-polyhedral epigraph; the spectral alignment argument is elegant.
- The analysis naturally plugs into the Luo–Tseng framework to derive asymptotic linear convergence.
- Assumptions cover common losses (square, logistic) composed with arbitrary linear operators A.
- The numerical section, though limited, is consistent with the asymptotic linear behavior predicted.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Local/asymptotic nature and unknown constants: The error bound holds only in a neighborhood (||R(X)||≤ε) and on a level set. The radius and constants (η, ε) are unspecified and seemingly problem-dependent, limiting practical implications and complexity guarantees. The experiments do not quantify when the linear regime is entered or the rates obtained.
- Potential gaps in spectral arguments: Proposition 4.2 asserts simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) using a particular SVD of X̄−Ḡ. With repeated singular values, Ū, V̄ are not unique; the result may require additional nondegeneracy or careful subspace handling. The manuscript should clarify the role of multiplicities and whether the claims remain valid without spectral gaps.
- Restricted invertibility (Lemma 4.1(c)): The key inequality ||X−X̄||F ≤ κ||A(X−X̄)||2 is strong given A may have a large nullspace. The proof by contradiction uses projection onto Xc and compactness; however, it is not transparent under what conditions on A and the geometry of X* this inequality must hold. An explicit statement of assumptions guaranteeing this property (or examples where it fails) would improve confidence.
- Step-size assumptions vs experiments: Theorem 3.2 requires αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) with ᾱ < 1/Lf. In matrix completion experiments, α=1 equals 1/Lf for a sampling operator; the theorem requires strict inequality. In the logistic experiment, a backtracking line search is used, but the analysis assumes a positive lower bound α>0; this is not established. The theory/experiment mismatch should be addressed.
- Limited empirical validation: Experiments are small-scale, on synthetic data only, with no comparison to established solvers (e.g., APG/FISTA, SVT, Frank–Wolfe), no runtime or SVD cost reporting, and no exploration of how step-size choices or problem conditioning affect the linear regime. Figures are also inconsistently labeled (e.g., “Figure 1” vs “Figure 2”) and lack sufficient detail.
- Related work coverage: The connection to literature on restricted strong convexity and decomposable regularization for low-rank models (e.g., Negahban & Wainwright and follow-ups) is missing. While those works assume curvature, they provide linear convergence and could contextualize the novelty here.
- Presentation issues: Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity on any convex compact subset) is quite weak and essentially local; a discussion of its necessity/sufficiency would help. There are notational inconsistencies (µ vs τ), and several references lack years/details.

Questions for the authors
- Can you characterize the neighborhood size (ε) and constant (η) in the error bound in terms of problem data (Lipschitz constants, τ, spectral gaps at X̄)?
- Does Proposition 4.2 hold robustly under repeated singular values? If not, what additional conditions are needed?
- Can Theorem 3.2 be extended to standard backtracking schemes that ensure αk ≤ 1/Lf and αk bounded away from zero after finitely many steps?
- Can your results cover inexact proximal steps (e.g., truncated SVD), which are common in large-scale nuclear-norm problems?
- Does accelerated PGM (FISTA) inherit the same asymptotic linear rate under your error bound?

Pros
- Conceptually neat extension of error-bound-based linear convergence to the nuclear norm.
- Broad class of losses covered.
- The spectral alignment insight may be of independent interest.

Cons
- Asymptotic/local result with opaque constants; limited practical guidance.
- Technical conditions (simultaneous SVD, restricted invertibility) need clearer, more general statements.
- Experimental section is too weak for NeurIPS standards; lacks comparisons and scale.
- Some theory/experiment inconsistencies (step sizes).

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current presentation leaves important technical points under-justified and provides minimal empirical support. Strengthening the clarity around key lemmas/assumptions, aligning experiments with the theory, and adding substantive empirical comparisons would significantly improve the paper’s suitability.

Confidence
High on the concerns about assumptions/experiments; moderate on the novelty claim relative to all concurrent literature.
