### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace norm-regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`. While it is known that PGM converges linearly if the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is strongly convex, many important machine learning problems (e.g., matrix completion with square loss) do not satisfy this condition. The main contribution of this paper is to prove that PGM still achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence under a weaker condition, namely that `f(X)` can be expressed as `h(A(X))`, where `h` is a smooth and strongly convex function on any compact set, and `A` is an arbitrary linear operator. This structure allows for `f(X)` itself to be non-strongly convex if `A` has a non-trivial nullspace.

The cornerstone of the authors' analysis is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This bound relates the distance from a point `X` to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing this error bound for the trace norm is non-trivial because, unlike the L1-norm, its epigraph is not polyhedral, and existing analysis techniques based on variable splitting do not apply. The authors overcome this by leveraging a key structural insight: at an optimal solution `X*`, the matrix `X*` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This allows them to use matrix perturbation theory to control the spectral structure of points near the optimal set and ultimately establish the required error bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from this bound using the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous and detailed proof sketch. The core of the work, the proof of the error bound in Theorem 3.1, is substantial. The authors correctly identify the technical challenges posed by the trace norm (non-polyhedrality, non-separability) and present an elegant solution based on the spectral properties of the optimal solution. This approach appears sound and is a clever application of matrix analysis to optimization theory. The paper is a complete piece of work, providing the theoretical result, a clear outline of its proof, and numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification that corroborate the theoretical findings of linear convergence. The authors are also honest about building upon the established Luo and Tseng framework, clearly delineating their novel contribution which is the error bound itself.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work within the existing literature and precisely stating the research question. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are stated upfront, giving the reader a clear picture of the paper's contributions before diving into the technical details. The proof structure is laid out logically, with the main argument for linear convergence in Section 3 being contingent on the error bound, which is then proven in Section 4. This modular presentation greatly aids readability. The notation is standard and clearly defined.

**Originality:** The main result is highly original. While the general approach of using an error bound to prove linear convergence of first-order methods is not new (as acknowledged by the authors' reference to Luo and Tseng), the successful establishment of such a bound for trace norm regularization is a novel and significant achievement. Prior work on error bounds for composite optimization problems has largely focused on regularizers with polyhedral structure (like the L1-norm) or separable structure (like group LASSO). This paper resolves what the authors describe as an open question, providing the first such result for the non-polyhedral and non-separable trace norm. The technical insight regarding the shared singular vectors of the solution and the negative gradient at optimality is a key, original observation that enables the entire analysis.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for both the optimization and machine learning communities. Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone of low-rank matrix recovery, with applications spanning from collaborative filtering to multi-task learning. The standard convergence theory for PGM provides only sublinear rates for many of these applications, which does not match the linear convergence often observed in practice. This paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for this empirical observation. It significantly broadens the class of problems for which PGM is known to converge linearly, bringing the theory more in line with practice. The developed error bound itself is a powerful analytical tool that could be of independent interest and potentially used to analyze other algorithms for this problem class. Researchers and practitioners using PGM for problems like matrix completion can now be more confident in its fast local convergence.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   The proof of the error bound is intricate and relies on several key propositions (4.2 and 4.3), whose proofs are deferred to the supplementary material. While this is standard for a conference paper, it makes a full verification of the technical correctness challenging from the main text alone. Assuming the supplementary material is sound, this is not a major issue.
*   The analysis guarantees *asymptotic* linear convergence. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on what problem properties might influence the size of the region where linear convergence takes hold. For instance, how do the properties of the operator `A` (e.g., its restricted isometry constant) or the regularization parameter `τ` affect the constant `η` in the error bound? While a full analysis may be beyond the scope, some high-level intuition would add practical value.
*   The numerical experiments in Section 5 are a good validation of the theory. The plots clearly show the transition to a linear rate of convergence. However, they are labeled "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" while the text refers to "Figure 1" for matrix completion and "Figure 2" for matrix classification. There is only one figure in the PDF, containing two sets of plots, but it's labeled "Figure 2: Matrix Classification". This seems to be a minor typesetting error that should be corrected.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
*   **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** Establishes linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, a result of high importance for many machine learning applications.
*   **Novel Technical Approach:** Introduces a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, overcoming significant technical hurdles compared to previous work on L1 and group LASSO norms. The use of spectral properties and matrix perturbation theory is elegant and powerful.
*   **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly motivated, and logically structured.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Provides theoretical justification for the fast convergence of PGM often observed in practice for problems like matrix completion.

**Cons:**
*   The analysis is asymptotic, and a discussion on the pre-asymptotic behavior or the conditions for entering the linear convergence phase would be a welcome addition.
*   Full verification of the technical proof requires a careful review of the supplementary material.
*   Minor error in figure labeling/referencing.

### Recommendation

This is a very strong paper that makes a novel and significant contribution to the literature on first-order optimization methods in machine learning. It solves an important open problem with a technically impressive and original proof. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and will be of high interest to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
