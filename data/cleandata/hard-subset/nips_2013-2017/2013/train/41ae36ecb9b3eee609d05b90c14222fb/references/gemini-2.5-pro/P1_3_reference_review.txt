### Summary

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + τ||X||*`. While PGM is known to have a sublinear rate for general convex `f` and a linear rate for strongly convex `f`, many important machine learning problems (e.g., matrix completion with square loss) feature a loss function `f` that is convex but not strongly convex. The main contribution of this work is to establish that PGM still achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate under a more relaxed set of conditions. Specifically, the authors show that if the loss function `f` can be written as `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function, then PGM converges R-linearly.

The key technical innovation enabling this result is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Proving such a bound for the trace norm is non-trivial because, unlike the l1-norm, its epigraph is not polyhedral and its non-separable nature prevents variable-splitting proof techniques. The authors overcome this by identifying and exploiting a key structural property of the optimal solution set: an optimal solution `X*` and the corresponding negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows them to use matrix perturbation theory to analyze points near the optimal set and establish the desired error bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from applying the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks, which demonstrate the predicted linear convergence.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamentally important question in optimization for machine learning: under what conditions can we expect fast, linear convergence for first-order methods on non-strongly convex problems? The trace norm regularization problem is ubiquitous, and proving linear convergence for PGM in this common setting is a significant theoretical advance. It provides a much-needed theoretical justification for the strong empirical performance of PGM on problems like matrix completion.

2.  **Originality and Quality:** The primary contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1)—is highly novel and technically impressive. It resolves what the authors convincingly argue was an open question. The proof technique is particularly clever. The observation that `X*` and `-∇f(X*)` are simultaneously diagonalizable via SVD (Proposition 4.2) is a beautiful insight that elegantly circumvents the difficulties posed by the non-separability of the trace norm. The subsequent proof, while complex, appears sound and leverages this insight effectively within a contradiction argument. The overall work is a complete and solid piece of theoretical research.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, and precisely states the paper's contribution and main technical approach. The main results are presented upfront in Section 3, followed by a detailed proof sketch in Section 4 that effectively communicates the high-level ideas of the intricate proof. The separation of the main error bound theorem from the final convergence result makes the logic easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Proof Density and Reliance on Supplement:** While the high-level proof sketch is clear, the technical details in Section 4 are extremely dense. Key results, such as Proposition 4.3 and several inequalities used in the proof of Theorem 3.2, are deferred to the supplementary material. This is standard for conference submissions due to space constraints, but it makes a full assessment of the proof's correctness challenging from the main paper alone. A little more intuition in the main text behind the decomposition in Proposition 4.3, for instance, could have been helpful.

2.  **Characterization of Constants:** The analysis proves the existence of a linear rate but does not provide any characterization of the constants involved (e.g., `η` in the error bound, or the final convergence factor `κ4 / (κ1 + κ4)`). These constants depend on properties of the loss function and the optimal solution set in a complex way. While deriving tight bounds on these constants is likely outside the scope of this work, a brief discussion of what factors they depend on (e.g., the minimum non-zero singular value of the Hessian of `h`, properties of the operator `A` on a specific subspace) would strengthen the practical implications of the result. The convergence could be linear, but very slow if the constants are unfavorable.

3.  **Assumptions:** The assumption that `f(X) = h(A(X))` with `h` being strongly convex on any convex compact subset is central. While this covers many important applications, it would be valuable to briefly discuss cases it does *not* cover to better delineate the boundaries of the result.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Addresses a significant open problem:** Establishes linear convergence of PGM for a broad and important class of non-strongly convex problems.
*   **Novel and powerful technical contribution:** The new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm is a substantial theoretical result of independent interest.
*   **Elegant proof technique:** The use of the shared singular vector structure is a creative and effective method for tackling the non-separability of the trace norm.
*   **High clarity and impact:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the results are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in optimization and machine learning.
*   **Solid empirical validation:** The experiments on standard tasks clearly support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Technical density:** The core proof is very complex and relies heavily on the supplement, making it difficult for reviewers to fully verify every step.
*   **Asymptotic nature:** The result guarantees an *asymptotic* linear rate, and the pre-asymptotic phase or the magnitude of the convergence factor is not analyzed, which might limit immediate practical guidance.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The authors successfully prove the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity, a result of both theoretical and practical importance. The key technical tool, a novel Lipschitzian error bound, is a standout achievement and is derived using a clever and insightful proof strategy. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the theoretical claims are empirically verified. The weaknesses are minor and largely related to the inherent complexity of the topic and conference page limits. This paper is a clear accept and is likely to be a notable contribution to the NIPS proceedings.
