This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically aiming to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a notable and potentially impactful direction, as many practical applications, such as matrix completion, feature loss functions that are not strongly convex. The authors derive a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem and leverage existing convergence analysis frameworks to prove their main result.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The primary strength of this work lies in its novel result: establishing linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization *without* strong convexity assumptions. This significantly expands the class of problems for which such a rate can be guaranteed. This is a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization and its applications to machine learning.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof appears well-structured and leverages established techniques (Luo & Tseng framework) after establishing the critical error bound. The meticulous analysis and handling of the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm are commendable.
* **Broad Applicability:** The result is framed generally, applying to loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), making it applicable to a wide range of applications including matrix completion and multi-task learning, as the authors demonstrate.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is mostly standard and the motivations are well-explained.
* **Numerical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments, although not exhaustive, adds credibility to the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and matrix classification.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Scope:** While the numerical experiments are a positive addition, they are limited to two specific applications and relatively small problem sizes. Expanding the empirical evaluation to more diverse datasets and larger scales would strengthen the claims. The experiments don’t convincingly demonstrate *why* this is useful beyond the theoretical result—do we actually see improved performance in real-world scenarios compared to standard PGM iterations?
* **Error Bound Complexity:** The proof of the error bound appears intricate and relies on a fairly specialized argument involving spectral properties of the optimal solution. While technically sound, the complexity might limit the accessibility of the result and hinder future extensions.
* **Assumptions:** The assumptions on *h* (strictly convex, smooth) while standard, could be further relaxed in future work. Exploring the robustness of the convergence result to less stringent conditions would be valuable.
* **Discussion of Practical Implications:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the linear convergence result. What does this mean for algorithm design and parameter tuning in real-world applications?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant Novelty:** The main result is genuinely novel and addresses an important open question in the field.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis and justification for the proposed result.
* **Potential Impact:** The findings have the potential to impact the design and analysis of algorithms for a wide range of applications involving trace norm regularization.
* **Generally Well-Written:** The paper is clear and well-organized, making it accessible to researchers in the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation is relatively limited and could be strengthened.
* **Complexity:** The intricate nature of the error bound proof might hinder wider adoption and further research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the theory of convex optimization. While the empirical validation could be more extensive, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work outweigh this weakness. The result is a valuable addition to the literature and has the potential to influence future research in this area. I believe this paper is a good fit for NIPS and recommend **acceptance**, despite minor flaws. The boldness of the theoretical contribution and the genuine novelty are compelling. I would encourage the authors to consider expanding the empirical evaluation in future work.
