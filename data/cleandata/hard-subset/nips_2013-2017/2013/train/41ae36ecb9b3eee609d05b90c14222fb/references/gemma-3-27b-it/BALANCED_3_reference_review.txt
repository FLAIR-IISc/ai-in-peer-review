This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems. The authors establish linear convergence under fairly general conditions on the loss function, specifically requiring only smoothness and curvature properties, and not strong convexity, a significant improvement over prior work. The key contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace norm regularization, overcoming difficulties associated with its non-polyhedral epigraph and the interdependence of matrix entries. The paper is well-motivated, addresses an important problem, and presents a technically sound result.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of PGM to a wider range of machine learning problems, such as matrix completion where strong convexity doesn't hold.
* **Novel Error Bound:** Deriving a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization is a substantial technical achievement, overcoming obstacles present in previous approaches for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization. The matrix perturbation argument used is clever and well-justified.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are explained clearly. The motivation for the work is also well articulated in the introduction.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of relevant literature, positioning their work within the existing body of knowledge. The citations are appropriate and demonstrate a good understanding of the field.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide some support for the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** While generally clear, some parts of the proof (especially Lemma 4.1 and its proof) are quite dense and require significant effort to follow. More intermediate steps or explanations could improve readability.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. Varying the dimensions of the matrices and the regularization parameters more broadly could provide a more robust validation of the results. Specifically, exploring cases where the linear convergence is *not* observed would be insightful.
* **Step Size Selection:** The discussion of step size selection in the experiments feels a bit superficial. For the matrix classification experiment, a backtracking line search is mentioned but not detailed. More discussion of practical step size strategies would be useful.
* **Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b) requires strong convexity on any *convex compact* subset. While reasonable, it's worth briefly discussing the implications if this assumption doesn't strictly hold in certain applications.
* **Proposition 4.2:** While the proposition itself appears correct, a brief intuition for *why* the singular value decomposition can be shared by X̄ and -Ḡ would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically significant result in the area of convex optimization.
* The technical contributions (error bound and convergence analysis) are substantial and well-justified.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
* The empirical results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical findings.
* The work addresses a relevant problem with broad applications in machine learning.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The technical density of the proof might make it difficult for some readers to fully appreciate the contributions.
* The limited scope of the experiments doesn’t provide overwhelming empirical validation.
* While significant, the improvement over existing results might be viewed as incremental by some committee members.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, and the paper is well-written and technically sound. The weaknesses are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. 

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Expand the proofs:** Add more intermediate steps or explanations to make the proofs more accessible.
* **Broaden the experiments:** Conduct more comprehensive experiments, varying the dimensions of the matrices and the regularization parameters more broadly. Consider scenarios where linear convergence is not observed and analyze the reasons.
* **Detail step size selection:** Provide more detail on the step size selection strategies used in the experiments.
* **Discuss Assumption 2.1(b):** Briefly discuss the implications if the assumption of strong convexity on convex compact subsets doesn't hold.
* **Intuition for Proposition 4.2:** Add a brief explanation of why the singular value decomposition can be shared by X̄ and -Ḡ.
