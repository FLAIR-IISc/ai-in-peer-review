## Peer Review: On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) when applied to trace norm regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is a linear operator, and h is strictly convex. The authors demonstrate linear convergence of the PGM *without* requiring strong convexity of the loss function *f* itself – a significant improvement over existing results.  They achieve this by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound specifically tailored to trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the interdependence of its singular values.  The authors validate their theoretical findings with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established theoretical framework for PGM convergence (Luo & Tseng, 2010). Existing linear convergence results for trace norm regularization typically rely on strong convexity of the loss function (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, 2009), a condition often unmet in practical machine learning scenarios like matrix completion. This work directly addresses this limitation.  It also acknowledges connections to error bound results for ℓ1-norm and group LASSO regularization (e.g., Tseng, 2001; Zhang et al., 2016), highlighting the unique challenges posed by the trace norm and detailing how they overcome them using matrix perturbation theory and a specific property of optimal solutions (Proposition 4.2).  The numerical experiments are consistent with lines of work demonstrating the efficacy of PGM for low-rank problems (e.g., Ma et al., 2011; Toh & Yun, 2010).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The primary strength lies in establishing linear convergence for a broader class of loss functions than previously possible. Removing the strong convexity requirement expands the applicability of the theoretical results to a wider range of real-world problems.
* **Technical Contribution:**  The development of the Lipschitzian error bound is a significant contribution. The authors successfully navigate the difficulties associated with the trace norm’s properties through insightful analysis and clever use of matrix perturbation theory.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the results, with clear assumptions and a well-structured proof.
* **Practical Validation:** The numerical experiments, while not exhaustive, provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence in practice.
* **Clear Writing (generally):**  The paper is generally well-organized and attempts to explain concepts in a clear manner, with well-defined notation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity:** While rigorous, the proof, especially Lemma 4.1 and its supporting propositions, is dense and requires careful reading.  More intuitive explanations of the key steps, particularly concerning Proposition 4.2 and 4.3, would enhance clarity.
* **Limited Numerical Experiments:**  The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  Exploring more datasets, varying parameters more extensively, and comparing to other state-of-the-art solvers would strengthen the empirical validation.  A more thorough analysis of the step size selection is also missing.
* **Assumptions:** While the assumptions are standard, their practical implications could be discussed more thoroughly. Specifically, clarifying the conditions under which Assumption 2.1 and 2.2 hold in various applications would be beneficial.
* **Missing Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on convergence *rate* but doesn’t discuss the computational cost of each iteration. A brief discussion of the complexity of the proximity operator and its impact on the overall efficiency of the PGM would be valuable.
* **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation is generally good, but some sections (e.g., the decomposition of Q̄ in Proposition 4.3) could benefit from more explicit definitions and explanations.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of PGM convergence for trace norm regularization.
* The technical contributions are substantial and the analysis is rigorous.
* The results have the potential to impact the development and analysis of algorithms for a wide range of applications.
* The numerical results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The proof is complex and could be more accessible to a broader audience.
* The numerical experiments are limited and could be more comprehensive.
*  The lack of a discussion of computational cost is a minor drawback.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**.  The theoretical contribution is significant and the results are well-supported. While the paper could benefit from some improvements in clarity and empirical validation, the novelty and importance of the findings outweigh these weaknesses. The paper advances the state of the art in understanding the convergence properties of a widely used optimization algorithm for a crucial class of problems.

**Rating:** 8/10 (Strong Accept)

**Recommendations:**

* **Improve Clarity:**  Consider adding more intuitive explanations for the key steps in the proof, particularly Propositions 4.2 and 4.3.
* **Expand Numerical Experiments:**  Include more datasets, perform more extensive parameter sweeps, and compare with other solvers.
* **Discuss Computational Cost:**  Add a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the PGM.
* **Clarify Assumptions:** Discuss the practical implications of the assumptions and provide examples of when they hold in specific applications.
