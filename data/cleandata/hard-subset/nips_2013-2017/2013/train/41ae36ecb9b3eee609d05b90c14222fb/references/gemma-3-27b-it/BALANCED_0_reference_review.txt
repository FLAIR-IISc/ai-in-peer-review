This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically focusing on scenarios where the loss function is not strongly convex. The authors establish linear convergence under relatively weak assumptions on the loss function, a notable improvement over existing results. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a significant problem in optimization and machine learning.

**Summary of Ideas & Relation to Prior Work:**

The core problem addressed is minimizing a convex loss function plus a trace norm regularizer, a common setup in applications like matrix completion and low-rank matrix learning. Prior work establishes linear convergence of PGM when the loss function is strongly convex. However, many practical loss functions, such as the squared loss in matrix completion with noisy data (where the measurement matrix has a non-trivial nullspace), lack strong convexity. This paper bridges this gap by demonstrating linear convergence without requiring strong convexity, specifically under the condition that the loss function can be expressed as `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex with suitable smoothness and curvature properties, and `A` is a linear operator. The key contribution lies in establishing a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to overcome challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. This builds on existing error bound techniques for ℓ1-norm regularization but adapts them to the unique properties of the trace norm, particularly the interdependence of singular values.  The work draws connections to Luo & Tseng’s convergence analysis framework and addresses open questions highlighted in prior literature regarding error bounds for trace norm regularization.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant contribution. It expands the applicability of PGM to a broader class of problems commonly encountered in machine learning.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous and well-structured. The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound seems particularly insightful, cleverly using matrix perturbation theory.
* **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The notation is consistent, and the motivation is clearly explained.  The authors do a good job of contextualizing their work within the broader literature.
* **Thoroughness:**  The authors provide a detailed analysis and address potential challenges associated with the trace norm. The inclusion of numerical experiments further strengthens the findings.
* **Connection to Applications:** The paper explicitly highlights connections to matrix completion and matrix classification, demonstrating the practical relevance of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on *h*:** The assumption that `h` is strictly convex with certain smoothness and curvature properties, while reasonable, might be restrictive for some applications.  A discussion of the implications of relaxing these assumptions would be valuable.
* **Step Size Selection:** The theoretical analysis assumes a specific range for the step size (`0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf`).  While standard, in practice, finding a suitable step size can be challenging, particularly if `Lf` is unknown or difficult to estimate.  A brief discussion of practical step size selection strategies (e.g., backtracking line search) within the context of the theoretical analysis would be helpful.
* **Numerical Experiments Limited Scope:** While the numerical experiments validate the theoretical results, the scope is relatively limited to two problems. Expanding the experiments to other applications or datasets would further bolster the claims.
* **Proposition 4.2 Requires Further Justification:** While intuitively reasonable, the claim in Proposition 4.2 regarding the simultaneous singular value decomposition seems to require a more detailed justification or a citation to existing results. The statement feels slightly out of place without further grounding.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important open problem in the convergence analysis of PGM.
* The results are technically sound and supported by a rigorous proof.
* The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains the key ideas.
* The findings have the potential to impact a wide range of applications in machine learning and optimization.
* The numerical experiments provide empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The assumption on the function *h* might be too strong for some real-world applications.
* The practical implications of the step size selection requirements are not fully discussed.
* The scope of the numerical experiments is somewhat limited.
* The justification of Proposition 4.2 feels incomplete.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of proximal gradient methods. The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a valuable addition to the literature. While there are minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Discuss the limitations of the assumptions on *h*:** Elaborate on how the results might be affected if these assumptions are relaxed and suggest potential avenues for future research in this direction.
* **Expand discussion on step size selection:** Include a brief discussion of practical step size selection strategies, such as backtracking line search, and how they relate to the theoretical analysis.
* **Consider adding more diverse numerical experiments:** Exploring additional applications or datasets would further strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Provide more justification for Proposition 4.2:** Include a citation to existing results or provide a more detailed explanation of the underlying reasoning.
