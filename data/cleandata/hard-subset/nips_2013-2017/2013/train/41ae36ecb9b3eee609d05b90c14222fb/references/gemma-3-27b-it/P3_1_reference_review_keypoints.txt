- Main contribution: Proves linear convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f, via a novel Lipschitzian error bound.  
- Technical novelty: Derives a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization using matrix perturbation theory and properties of the optimal set.  
- Key technical insight: Proposition 4.2 (shared singular vectors) is pivotal to handling the non‑polyhedral structure and singular-value interdependence of the trace norm.  
- Problem setting: Considers F(X)=f(X)+τ||X||* with f(X)=h(A(X)), assuming smoothness and curvature of h but not strong convexity of f.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends PGM convergence theory beyond prior results (Beck & Teboulle, Luo & Tseng) and contrasts with known results for ℓ1/group LASSO.  
- Empirical validation: Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification show observed linear convergence supporting the theory.  
- Strength — novelty: Broadens applicability of PGM to more ML problems by relaxing strong-convexity requirements.  
- Strength — rigor: Proofs are rigorous and well-structured, with precise lemmas and theorems.  
- Strength — clarity: Paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  
- Weakness — assumptions on h: Conditions on h need clearer discussion of practical implications and examples of losses that fail them.  
- Weakness — step size: Theoretical step-size condition is restrictive; the paper should discuss more robust or practical selection strategies.  
- Weakness — experiments: Empirical study is limited (problem sizes, τ values, dimensionality) and lacks comparisons to specialized trace-norm solvers.  
- Weakness — accessibility: Theorem 3.1’s proof is involved and would benefit from more intuitive explanations and clearer supplementary material.  
- For acceptance: Significant theoretical advance, strong technical contribution, potential impact, and supportive experiments.  
- Against acceptance: Practical limits from h assumptions, restrictive step-size requirements, and limited empirical scope.  
- Overall assessment: Strong paper with valuable contributions; minor revisions recommended.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — specifically: expand discussion of h assumptions, address step-size strategies, and broaden numerical experiments and solver comparisons.
