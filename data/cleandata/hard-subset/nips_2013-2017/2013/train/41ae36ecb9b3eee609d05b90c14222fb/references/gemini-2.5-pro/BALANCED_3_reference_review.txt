### **Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper studies the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`. This formulation is central to many machine learning tasks like matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. The authors correctly point out that while PGM is known to converge linearly when the smooth loss function `f` is strongly convex, many practical loss functions (e.g., squared loss `f(X) = ||A(X) - b||^2` where `A` is not injective) do not satisfy this condition. For such cases, only sublinear convergence rates are typically guaranteed.

The main contribution of this paper is to establish an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM under a weaker set of assumptions. Specifically, they consider loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function with a Lipschitz continuous gradient. This setting covers many important non-strongly convex loss functions. The key to their analysis is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal set, `dist(X, X*)`, to the norm of a residual map, `||R(X)||`, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm was an open problem, as prior techniques for `l1` or group Lasso norms do not apply due to the non-polyhedral nature and coupled structure of the trace norm. The authors leverage matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of matrices near the optimal set, enabling them to derive this crucial bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from this error bound by applying the well-established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993).

This work significantly advances the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for low-rank matrix problems. It connects to the broad literature on PGM (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, 2009; Toh & Yun, 2010) and the more specific literature on error bounds and linear convergence for structured convex optimization (e.g., Luo & Tseng, 1993; Tseng, 2010), extending the latter to the important case of trace norm regularization.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a key gap in the convergence analysis of PGM for trace norm minimization. Proving linear convergence without the strong convexity assumption on `f` is a strong and important result. It provides theoretical justification for the fast convergence often observed in practice for these problems.

2.  **Novel Technical Tool:** The primary technical novelty and contribution is the derivation of a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1). This result is of independent interest and could be leveraged for the analysis of other algorithms for this problem class. The proof technique, which cleverly analyzes the shared singular vector structure of the optimal solution and its gradient, is insightful and overcomes significant challenges that differentiate the trace norm from separable norms like `l1`.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly stating their assumptions and main results, and situating their work within the relevant literature. The proof structure is logical, building up from the error bound to the final convergence result.

4.  **Broad Applicability:** The assumptions on the loss function `f(X) = h(A(X))` are general enough to cover a wide range of important machine learning applications, including matrix completion with squared loss and multi-task learning with logistic loss, which are explicitly discussed.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** While the experiments in Section 5 successfully illustrate the paper's theoretical findings (i.e., the asymptotic linear convergence), they are somewhat limited. They serve as a proof-of-concept rather than a thorough empirical study. There are no comparisons to other state-of-the-art methods or even to accelerated PGM variants (like FISTA), which have better sublinear rates and are often the default choice in practice. It would be valuable to understand the practical regime where the proven asymptotic linear rate leads to superior performance over these strong baselines.

2.  **Omission of Key Proof Details:** Many of the critical steps in the proofs are deferred to the supplementary material (e.g., inequalities (9)-(11) and the proof of Proposition 4.3). Proposition 4.3, which describes the structure of the limiting direction `Q̄`, appears to be a cornerstone of the main technical argument for the error bound. While deferring proofs is necessary due to space constraints, the lack of even a high-level sketch or intuition for this central proposition in the main text makes the core argument difficult to fully assess without the supplement.

3.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic. The paper does not provide much discussion on the factors that influence when this linear convergence phase begins or the magnitude of the convergence constant `ρ`. While a full characterization may be difficult, some discussion on how problem parameters (e.g., properties of `A`, the regularization parameter `τ`, the rank of the solution) affect the rate would add significant practical insight.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Enhancing the Experiments:** To strengthen the paper's impact, I would suggest expanding the experimental section. A comparison between the standard PGM and an accelerated version (FISTA) would be particularly insightful. Plotting the error versus wall-clock time could help demonstrate whether the improved asymptotic rate translates to faster practical convergence compared to a method with a better (but sublinear) global rate guarantee.

*   **Improving the Proof Presentation:** If possible, I recommend the authors include a brief proof sketch or at least some intuition for Proposition 4.3 in the main paper's appendix. Given its centrality to the proof of the error bound, this would make the paper more self-contained and the technical contribution easier to appreciate.

*   **Discussion of Constants:** A brief discussion on what the constants in the linear rate depend on would be a valuable addition. For instance, does the rate degrade as the operator `A` becomes more ill-conditioned? This would help practitioners understand when to expect fast convergence.

*   **Line Search:** The matrix classification experiment uses a backtracking line search, while the theory assumes a step size bounded away from `1/Lf`. It would be helpful to explicitly state that the theoretical result can be extended to cover common line search strategies, if this is indeed the case, perhaps by citing relevant work that connects the two.

### **Decision**

This paper presents a high-quality, technically deep, and significant theoretical contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The establishment of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm and the subsequent proof of linear convergence for PGM under realistic, non-strongly convex settings is a novel and important result. Despite the minor weaknesses in the experimental section and the delegation of some key proofs to the supplement, the strengths of the paper are compelling. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and addresses a fundamental question. I recommend acceptance.
