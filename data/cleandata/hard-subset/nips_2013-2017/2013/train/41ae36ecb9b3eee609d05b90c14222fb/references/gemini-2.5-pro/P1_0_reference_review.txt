### **Summary**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard analysis of PGM guarantees linear convergence only when the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is strongly convex. However, many common loss functions (e.g., square loss in matrix completion) do not satisfy this condition.

The authors' main contribution is to prove that PGM can still achieve an asymptotic linear convergence rate under a weaker and more practical assumption: that the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function (with certain smoothness properties). This covers many important use cases where `f` itself is not strongly convex.

The key technical tool enabling this result is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the optimality conditions. Establishing this error bound is non-trivial because, unlike the l1-norm, the trace norm is non-polyhedral and non-separable, precluding the use of existing proof techniques. The authors develop a novel proof strategy based on analyzing the shared spectral structure of the optimal solution and the corresponding gradient, employing matrix perturbation theory. Using this error bound, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the well-established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a significant and long-standing question in the analysis of first-order methods for machine learning. The gap between the theoretical requirement of strong convexity for linear convergence and its absence in many practical, large-scale problems is a critical issue. This work provides a satisfying positive result, showing that linear convergence can be achieved under much more realistic assumptions. The findings are broadly applicable to a wide range of problems involving trace norm regularization.

2.  **Novel and Strong Technical Contribution:** The core of the paper is the new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm (Theorem 3.1). This result is of independent interest beyond the convergence analysis of PGM. The proof technique is highly original; it cleverly circumvents the difficulties associated with the trace norm by exploiting the specific structure of the optimality conditions (Proposition 4.2) and using tools from matrix perturbation theory. This is a notable departure from and an advance over prior work on error bounds for l1 or group Lasso regularization.

3.  **Excellent Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-motivated, and easy to follow. The authors clearly position their work in the context of existing literature, state their assumptions and main results upfront, and provide a clear and insightful sketch of their proof strategy. This structure effectively communicates the paper's key ideas and contributions.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, as the error bound holds only when an iterate is sufficiently close to the optimal solution set. While the theory is sound, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the practical implications. The initial phase of the algorithm may still be sublinear, and the onset of linear convergence could depend on problem-specific constants. The experimental plots indeed show this behavior (a "knee" before the linear tail). A short discussion on this point would add nuance.

2.  **Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments effectively demonstrate the theoretical linear convergence. However, they could be strengthened to provide more practical insights.
    *   The problem sizes are relatively small (n=40). Demonstrating the effect on slightly larger problems would increase confidence.
    *   A comparison with an accelerated PGM (e.g., FISTA) would be very informative. While FISTA's worst-case rate is O(1/k^2), it is often observed to converge linearly in practice under similar "restricted strong convexity" type conditions. Comparing the practical convergence of PGM and FISTA in this regime would help contextualize the results. Does the non-accelerated PGM become competitive due to a better constant in the linear rate?

3.  **Proof Details in Appendix:** As is common with theory papers in page-limited formats, many crucial proof details (e.g., for Proposition 4.3) are deferred to the supplementary material. This makes a full verification of the technical claims challenging for a reviewer. In the main text, a little more intuition behind the key Proposition 4.3 (the decomposition of `Q_bar`) would be helpful for the reader to better grasp the logic of the proof of the error bound.

### **Detailed Comments**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The theoretical analysis appears sound and rigorously addresses a challenging problem. The claims are well-supported by the detailed proof sketch, and the connection to the established Luo-Tseng framework is appropriate and well-executed.

*   **Clarity:** The writing is exemplary. The motivation in the introduction is compelling, the notation is clear, and the structure of the argument is laid out logically.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While building upon the general framework of error-bound-based convergence analysis, the central contribution—the error bound for the trace norm and its proof—is novel and tackles a known open problem. The paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from prior work on l1-norm and group Lasso.

*   **Significance:** The contribution is highly significant for both the optimization and machine learning communities. It deepens our understanding of the convergence behavior of a fundamental algorithm (PGM) on a widely used class of problems. Researchers and practitioners using trace norm regularization can now be more confident about achieving fast convergence rates even without strong convexity.

### **Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel theoretical contribution to an important problem in machine learning. The strengths—a novel error bound, a strong theoretical result on linear convergence under weak assumptions, and exceptional clarity—far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to the scope of experiments. This work is of high quality and is very likely to have a lasting impact on the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
