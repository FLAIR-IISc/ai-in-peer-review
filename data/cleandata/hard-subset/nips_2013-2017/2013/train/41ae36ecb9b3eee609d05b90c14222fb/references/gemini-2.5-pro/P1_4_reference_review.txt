This paper establishes the asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace norm-regularized optimization problems. The central problem is `min F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`, where `f` is a convex and smooth loss function. While sublinear convergence rates for PGM are well-known, linear convergence typically requires the strong convexity of `f`. This is a restrictive assumption that is not met in many machine learning applications, such as matrix completion with a square loss where the measurement operator is not injective.

The main contribution of this work is to prove linear convergence under a weaker condition: that the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is an arbitrary linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties. This structure for `f` is not necessarily strongly convex, but is common in practice. The key technical innovation is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map. Such error bounds were previously known for problems with polyhedral regularizers like the ℓ1-norm, but the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm epigraph made this an open question. The authors overcome this by exploiting a key structural property of the optimality conditions: an optimal solution and its negative gradient share the same singular vectors. This allows them to use matrix perturbation theory to analyze the local geometry and establish the error bound. With this bound in hand, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993). The paper provides numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification that corroborate the theoretical linear convergence rate.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and appears to be sound. The main claim—that a Lipschitzian error bound holds for trace norm regularization under these conditions—is a significant theoretical result. The proof strategy, while building on the general framework of previous work on error bounds, introduces a novel and clever argument specific to the spectral nature of the trace norm. The authors are careful in laying out their assumptions and proof structure. The experimental results, while not extensive, serve as a clear and effective validation of the theoretical findings, showing the predicted transition from sublinear to linear convergence on a semi-log plot.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly stating the problem, its importance, the limitations of prior work, and the paper's key contributions. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are stated upfront, providing a clear picture of the paper's claims. The proof sketch in Section 4 is particularly helpful for navigating the dense technical details of the main proof. The notation is standard and consistent. The paper is easy to follow for a reader with a background in convex optimization.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the study of PGM convergence is a mature field, this paper addresses a well-defined gap in the literature. Proving a local error bound for the trace norm regularizer is a novel contribution that resolves a known open question. The technique used in the proof, relying on the simultaneous singular value decomposition property (Proposition 4.2) and matrix perturbation arguments, is distinct from the variable-splitting techniques used for ℓ1-type regularizers and is tailored specifically to the structure of the trace norm. This represents a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:** The results are very significant for both the optimization and machine learning communities.
1.  **Theoretical Impact:** It deepens our understanding of the convergence behavior of first-order methods on a fundamentally important class of non-strongly convex problems. The error bound itself is a powerful tool that could potentially be used to analyze other algorithms or properties of the problem.
2.  **Practical Impact:** Linear convergence is the "gold standard" for iterative methods. This result provides theoretical justification for the fast convergence of PGM often observed in practice for problems like matrix completion, even when strong convexity is absent. It assures practitioners that PGM is not just a heuristic but is provably fast in the terminal phase of optimization. The results are likely to be built upon to analyze other matrix norm regularization problems or more complex algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The linear convergence result is asymptotic, holding only once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal solution set (i.e., when `F(X) ≤ ζ` and `||R(X)||F ≤ ε`). The paper does not provide any analysis of the "pre-asymptotic" phase or give any characterization of the size of this local neighborhood where linear convergence is guaranteed. While this is typical for results based on local error bounds, some discussion on how many iterations might be needed to enter this region would strengthen the paper's practical implications.
2.  The analysis is restricted to the basic PGM. A natural and important question is whether this error bound can be used to establish linear convergence for accelerated variants of PGM (e.g., FISTA). It is known that for strongly convex problems, accelerated methods can achieve a better linear rate. A discussion of this possibility is currently missing and would be a valuable addition.
3.  The experiments are primarily illustrative. While they successfully demonstrate the linear convergence phenomenon, they do not compare the performance of PGM against other state-of-the-art solvers for these problems. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation could better contextualize the practical impact of this linear rate.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** It resolves an open question by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization.
*   **Significant result:** It proves the highly desirable linear convergence rate for PGM on a broad and practical class of non-strongly convex problems.
*   **Novel proof technique:** The analysis introduces new ideas based on the spectral properties of the solutions, which could be influential.
*   **High clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The convergence is asymptotic, with no characterization of the pre-asymptotic rate or the size of the linear convergence region.
*   The analysis does not cover accelerated proximal gradient methods, which is a missed opportunity.
*   The experimental validation is limited to a proof-of-concept.

### Recommendation

The strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. The solution to an open theoretical problem with direct implications for a wide range of machine learning applications makes this a top-tier contribution. The originality of the proof and the significance of the result are clear. I strongly recommend acceptance. The weaknesses I have pointed out could be addressed as suggestions for improvement or as clear directions for future work.
