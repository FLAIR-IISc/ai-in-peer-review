- Paper proves linear convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss.
- Core technical innovation: a novel Lipschitzian error bound linking distance to optimal solution with the residual norm via matrix perturbation and shared singular structure.
- Theoretical contribution is significant, extending applicability to common non-strongly convex problems (e.g., matrix completion).
- Presentation is clear, well-motivated, and assumptions/notation are consistent.
- Thorough related work discussion situates the contribution within proximal methods and trace norm literature.
- Empirical validation (matrix completion and classification) supports the theoretical linear convergence and includes illustrative plots.
- Proofs (notably Lemma 4.1 and supporting propositions) are technically dense and would benefit from more intermediate steps for accessibility.
- Numerical experiments are limited: do not systematically explore regularization parameter effects, comparisons to other solvers/accelerations (e.g., FISTA), or varying problem sizes/noise.
- Practical implications are under-discussed; unclear how much faster PGM is in real-world settings versus sublinear methods.
- Heavy reliance on supplementary material for key proofs makes it harder to assess rigor without extra effort.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, well-motivated technical result with supportive experiments and clear writing.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited accessibility due to technical depth, insufficient empirical breadth, and sparse practical discussion.
- Overall assessment: strong accept; important and influential contribution if proofs and experiments are clarified/expanded.
- Reviewer confidence: 9/10.
