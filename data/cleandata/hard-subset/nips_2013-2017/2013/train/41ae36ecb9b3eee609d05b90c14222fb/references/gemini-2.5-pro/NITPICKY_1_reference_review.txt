**Summary:**
This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems. The primary contribution is to establish a linear convergence rate for a class of problems where the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is not necessarily strongly convex. Specifically, the authors consider `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function and `A` is a linear operator. This structure is common in machine learning applications such as matrix completion and multi-task learning. The key technical tool underpinning their analysis is a novel Lipschitzian error bound, which relates the distance to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map. This result is significant as prior work on such error bounds for composite optimization, e.g., for `l1`-norm regularization, relied on separability properties not present in the trace norm. The authors develop a new proof technique based on matrix perturbation theory to handle the non-separable nature of singular values.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper addresses a well-motivated and important theoretical question: can PGM converge linearly on trace norm problems without strong convexity? The affirmative answer provided is a significant contribution, as it offers a theoretical justification for the fast convergence often observed empirically in these settings. The development of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm is a valuable result in its own right and resolves a question that the authors claim was open.
2.  **Novel Technical Approach:** The analysis departs from existing techniques used for separable regularizers like the `l1`-norm. The key observation that an optimal solution `X*` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` can be simultaneously singular-value-decomposed (Proposition 4.2) is insightful and provides a novel avenue for analysis using matrix perturbation theory. This approach appears to be a genuinely new tool for this class of problems.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, despite its promising theoretical contributions, is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its presentation, clarity, and experimental validation. My assessment is that these issues, in aggregate, are too significant to overlook.

1.  **Clarity and Organization:**
    *   **Non-Self-Contained Proofs:** The paper's most critical weakness is that the core technical arguments are not verifiable from the main text alone. The proof of the main convergence result, Theorem 3.2, relies on three key inequalities (9, 10, 11) that are stated without proof and deferred to supplementary material. Similarly, the proofs of Proposition 4.2 (a cornerstone of the analysis) and Proposition 4.3 (essential for the final contradiction argument) are also relegated to the supplement. For a theoretical paper, this makes the central claims impossible to properly review and validate, rendering the main body an extended abstract of the proof rather than a complete scientific argument.
    *   **Grammar and Typos:** There are several distracting grammatical errors and typos. For example, in Section 2.3, "This naturally lead to..." should be "This naturally leads to...". In Section 4, "Given a sequence {X k}k≥0 ∈ R m×n\X" is imprecise; I presume the authors mean `Xk ∈ R m×n\X` for each `k`.
    *   **Inconsistent Formatting and Citations:** The footnote marker "1" in the first paragraph is awkwardly placed. More importantly, several references appear incomplete (e.g., [1] and [2] are missing the year and venue type). This lack of attention to detail is concerning.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Rigor:**
    *   **Proof Details:** The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) is quite dense. In the final step (Section 4.1.2), the paper claims that for a sufficiently small `ε > 0`, several conditions hold. However, it is not immediately obvious that a single `ε` can be chosen to simultaneously satisfy all conditions for all indices `i` across the different cases. This step requires a more careful and explicit argument.
    *   **Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b) ("strongly convex on any convex compact subset") is slightly non-standard. While it implies strict convexity, a brief discussion on why this specific condition is necessary and how it compares to other common assumptions (e.g., restricted strong convexity) would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Missing Figure:** The text in the Matrix Completion section explicitly refers to "Figure 1", but this figure is absent from the paper. This is a major oversight.
    *   **Poor Quality of Figures:** The provided "Figure 2" is of low quality. The y-axes for both subplots are not labeled, making it impossible to interpret what quantity is being plotted (e.g., `log(F(Xk) - v*)`, `||Xk - X*||_F`). The legend is placed inconveniently inside the plot area.
    *   **Lack of Rigor:** For the matrix completion experiment, a fixed step size `α = 1` is used without justification that this satisfies the condition `α < 1/L_f`. The experiments also lack any baseline comparisons, which would help contextualize the performance of the PGM. While the goal is to demonstrate linear convergence, the plots serve only as a weak illustration rather than a rigorous empirical validation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result on the linear convergence of PGM for an important class of non-strongly convex problems.
*   The error bound analysis introduces new techniques that could be of broader interest to the optimization and machine learning communities.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is not self-contained; key proofs are deferred to the supplement, making the core technical claims unverifiable and the paper unsuitable for publication in its current form.
*   There is a significant number of minor but distracting flaws, including grammatical errors, incomplete references, a missing figure, and poorly labeled plots.
*   The experimental section is weak and does not meet the standards expected at NIPS.

**Recommendation:**
Reject.

While the core theoretical ideas in this paper are strong and potentially of high impact, the execution is unfortunately well below the standard required for NIPS. The decision to relegate the proofs of the most critical results to the supplement fundamentally undermines the paper's integrity as a complete piece of work. This major structural issue, combined with the multitude of presentation flaws and a superficial experimental section, leads me to recommend rejection. The work is certainly promising, but it requires a very substantial revision to address these deficiencies.
