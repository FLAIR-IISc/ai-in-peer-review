

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with specific conditions on h. The key contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which the authors claim is the first such result that does not require strong convexity of f. The paper proves that under their assumptions, the PGM converges R-linearly to the optimal solution set, addressing a gap in the literature since previous error bounds for composite minimization applied to polyhedral regularizers like ℓ1-norm but not to trace norm.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a genuine theoretical contribution by resolving the open question of whether a Lipschitzian error bound holds for trace norm regularization. The proof is rigorous and carefully developed, overcoming the challenge of the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm through a novel analysis of singular value structures.

2. **Technical Soundness**: The authors provide a thorough proof of the main error bound (Theorem 3.1) and successfully apply the Luo-Tseng framework to establish linear convergence. The proof strategy, while building on prior work, requires significant technical innovation to handle the trace norm's spectral properties.

3. **Motivation and Justification**: The assumptions are well-motivated with concrete examples from applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning), and the paper clearly explains why previous approaches for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization cannot be directly applied to trace norm.

4. **Numerical Validation**: The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification problems provide empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims of linear convergence, with semi-log plots showing the characteristic linear convergence behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumptions**: The requirement that f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and Lipschitz gradient may be too restrictive for some applications. Many loss functions used in practice (e.g., hinge loss) do not satisfy these conditions, limiting the applicability of the theoretical results.

2. **Limited Experimental Validation**: The numerical experiments are confined to just two problems (matrix completion and classification). While these are standard applications, the paper would be stronger with additional experiments across diverse settings to demonstrate the robustness of the convergence claims.

3. **Lack of Practical Comparison**: The paper does not compare the PGM with alternative methods for trace norm regularization (e.g., alternating minimization, which is common in matrix completion). Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the linear convergence rate translates to practical advantages in terms of computational efficiency.

4. **Step Size Selection**: While the paper assumes a bounded step size (0 < α < α_k < α-bar < 1/L_f), it doesn't provide guidance on how to practically choose α and α-bar for specific applications. This is a significant gap for practitioners.

## Evaluation

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof of the main result. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-supported, though the restrictive assumptions limit the practical scope. The numerical experiments provide reasonable validation but could be strengthened with more diverse examples.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and logical flow from problem setup to theoretical results to experiments. The main contributions are clearly articulated.

**Originality**: This paper makes a genuine contribution by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The technical approach to handle the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm represents a meaningful advance over previous work.

**Significance**: The result has significant theoretical importance for the field of convex optimization, particularly for understanding first-order methods in matrix regularization. However, the practical significance is somewhat limited by the restrictive assumptions on the loss function. The error bound itself could be of independent interest for future work on matrix regularization.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance, but with the following considerations: The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. While the assumptions are restrictive, the paper clearly articulates the limitations and the specific class of problems to which their results apply. The numerical experiments provide reasonable support for the theoretical claims.

The main weaknesses are the limited experimental validation and lack of comparison with alternative methods. However, these are common in theoretical papers, and the paper's primary contribution is theoretical rather than empirical. Given the significance of the theoretical result and the rigorous proof, I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

For future work, the authors could explore relaxing the assumptions on h, provide more extensive numerical experiments across diverse applications, and compare with alternative methods to demonstrate practical advantages of the linear convergence rate.
