## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) for a linear operator A and a strictly convex function h.  The authors demonstrate linear convergence of the PGM under relatively mild assumptions on *h* (smoothness and curvature) without requiring strong convexity of the loss function *f* itself, a significant improvement over existing results.  The core contribution lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to account for the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. They support their theoretical findings with numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established theoretical foundation of PGM convergence, citing key works by Nesterov, Beck & Teboulle, and others.  It addresses a gap in the literature by extending linear convergence results beyond scenarios requiring strong convexity of *f*, a common limitation in applications like matrix completion where the linear operator *A* may be rank-deficient. It correctly positions itself against existing error bound analyses focusing on l1-norm or group LASSO regularization, highlighting the unique challenges posed by the trace norm’s non-polyhedral epigraph and the interdependence of matrix entries.  The authors also draw connections to work on SDP rank reduction and convex multi-view subspace learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The result of establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a valuable contribution, opening the door to wider applicability of PGM in practical machine learning scenarios. The problem addressed—linear convergence for non-strongly convex loss—is a significant one in the field.
* **Technical Depth:** The proof of the error bound is sophisticated, requiring careful application of matrix perturbation theory and demonstrating a deep understanding of the properties of the trace norm. Proposition 4.2 is a key insight.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly motivates the problem and contributions.  The notation is mostly consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The numerical experiments provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings, showcasing linear convergence in practical settings.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of relevant prior work, demonstrating a good understanding of the field.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity:** While technically sound, the proof is dense and challenging to follow in its entirety.  More detailed explanations of key steps (particularly within the proof of Lemma 4.1) would improve accessibility. A more structured presentation of the lemma’s proof, perhaps broken down into sub-claims, would be helpful.
* **Assumptions on *h*:**  While less restrictive than strong convexity, Assumption 2.1(b) regarding *h* being strongly convex on *compact* subsets is a potential limitation. It might be helpful to discuss the practical implications of this assumption and whether it holds for commonly used loss functions without modification.
* **Numerical Experiment Detail:** The numerical experiments lack detailed parameters and comparisons to other solvers.  While linear convergence is demonstrated, a comparison with accelerated gradient methods or other trace norm solvers would strengthen the empirical evaluation. Including convergence plots with a larger number of iterations would be beneficial.  The explanation of the backtracking line search in the classification experiment is brief.
* **Limited Discussion of Constant Factors:** The theoretical results establish linear convergence, but don’t provide tight bounds on the convergence rate.  A discussion of the constants involved and their dependence on problem parameters would be useful for assessing practical performance.
* **Minor Clarifications:** In Section 3, stating explicitly what `Lf` represents (Lipschitz constant of ∇f) at its first mention would improve clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the analysis of PGM for trace norm regularized problems. The proof is technically challenging, but the results are significant and well-supported. While the complexity of the proof and the limited details in the numerical experiments are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant open problem in optimization and machine learning.
* Presents a novel and technically sound solution.
* Provides strong theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.
* Is well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Proof complexity may limit accessibility for some readers.
* Numerical experiments could be more comprehensive and comparative.



**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions addressing the clarity of the proof and providing more details in the numerical experiments. Specifically, the authors should consider:

1.  Breaking down the proof of Lemma 4.1 into smaller, more manageable sub-claims.
2.  Adding a discussion of the practical implications of Assumption 2.1(b).
3.  Providing more detailed parameter settings and comparisons to other solvers in the numerical experiments.
4. Providing more convergence plots with more iterations.
5. Expanding on the description of the backtracking line search used in the matrix classification experiment.
