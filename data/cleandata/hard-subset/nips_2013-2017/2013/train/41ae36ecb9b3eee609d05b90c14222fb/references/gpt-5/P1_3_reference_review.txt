Summary
This paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for convex trace-norm regularized problems of the form minX f(X) + τ||X||*, focusing on the common but non-strongly-convex case where f(X) = h(A(X)) with an arbitrary linear operator A and a smooth strictly convex loss h. The main contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound: for points X in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X, the distance to the optimal solution set X satisfies dist(X, X ) ≤ η||R(X)||F. This error bound is then combined with the Luo-Tseng framework to obtain asymptotic linear convergence of PGM: function values converge Q-linearly to the optimum and iterates converge R-linearly to the solution set, for fixed stepsizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf.

Technically, the key is a spectral structural result: at any optimal solution X̄, the matrices X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same left/right singular vectors, a consequence of the proximal fixed-point equation Sτ(X̄ − ∇f(X̄)) = X̄. This supports a perturbation analysis around X̄, enabling a “restricted invertibility” estimate that rules out directions in which A “loses information” relative to the geometry of the solution set. The assumptions on h (Lipschitz gradient and strong convexity on compact convex subsets) cover common losses (squared and logistic), and the linear operator A is arbitrary (hence f itself is not necessarily strongly convex). Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification exhibit linear convergence once iterates enter a neighborhood of the solution set.

Relation to prior work
- Prior results show sublinear O(1/k) and O(1/k^2) rates for composite problems, and linear rates under strong convexity of f. Error bounds for composite problems with polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group lasso) are known; the nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, and separating variables is not possible. The paper addresses this gap, deriving an error bound for the nuclear norm and leveraging Luo–Tseng to obtain linear rates without strong convexity.
- The approach is distinct from statistical restricted strong convexity analyses and complements KL-inequality based convergence analyses (which often yield local linear rates under a KL exponent). It would help to situate this result relative to KL-based frameworks and to clarify whether the presented error bound can be interpreted as a Luo–Tseng error bound/KL exponent 1/2 condition for this class.

Quality
- The technical development is sound and carefully structured. The simultaneous SVD argument and the construction of the set Xc are elegant, and the contradiction-based proof of the restricted invertibility property is convincing.
- Assumption 2.1(b) (h is strongly convex on compact convex sets) is reasonable and indeed ensures a positive curvature constant σ on bounded level sets; this is the right hypothesis for logistic/squared losses given the boundedness of level sets proved in the paper.
- The convergence theorem is standard given the error bound and descent inequalities, and the proof is consistent with the Luo–Tseng framework.
- Minor issues: Proposition 4.3 is deferred to the supplement; given its central role, a brief sketch in the main text would help. In the experiments, the stated stepsize α=1 for matrix completion may violate α<1/Lf if Lf=1 (sampling operator has unit norm); this should be clarified or adjusted. The logistic-loss experiment uses backtracking, but the theory is stated for fixed stepsizes; a remark on extending the result to line-search variants (which is standard) would strengthen the link between theory and practice.

Clarity
- Overall the paper is well-organized, with a clear statement of assumptions, algorithm, residual map, main theorems, and proof roadmap. The motivation for why ℓ1-style arguments do not carry over is helpful.
- Some presentation issues: inconsistent notation for the regularization parameter (τ vs. μ in experiments); figure numbering and captions appear inconsistent; several references lack years/page info. A schematic illustrating the sets X and Xc, and the role of A, would aid intuition.
- It would be useful to explicitly state how η and ε in the error bound depend on the chosen level set ζ and problem data, and to emphasize the local (asymptotic) nature of linear convergence.

Originality
- The error bound for trace-norm regularization under the stated structure is, to the best of my knowledge, novel and resolves an open question raised by analogy with ℓ1/group-lasso. The spectral perturbation argument and simultaneous SVD insight are nontrivial.
- The combination with Luo–Tseng for linear convergence is standard, but the enabling error bound for a nonpolyhedral norm is the main innovation.

Significance
- Linear convergence guarantees for PGM without strong convexity are of practical and theoretical interest in matrix problems (completion, multitask learning, classification). The result helps explain the commonly observed fast local convergence in practice.
- The scope includes widely used losses and arbitrary measurement operators, broadening applicability beyond restricted injective settings.
- The numerical evidence is illustrative but limited; more comprehensive experiments (e.g., comparison to accelerated PG, ADMM; sensitivity to τ; onset of linear regime; robustness to approximate SVDs) would improve practical significance.

Pros
- Addresses an important gap: linear convergence of PGM for nuclear norm without strong convexity.
- Technically solid with a novel error bound and insightful spectral analysis.
- Assumptions are natural and cover common losses; A can be arbitrary.
- Clear link to a well-established analysis framework (Luo–Tseng).

Cons
- Linear rate is asymptotic/local; no quantification of the neighborhood or constants.
- Experimental section is minimal and contains minor inconsistencies (stepsize, notation, figures).
- Main text omits proofs of key propositions; readers must consult the supplement.
- No discussion of accelerated methods, inexact proximal operators (truncated SVD), or KL-framework connections.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify stepsize conditions in experiments (ensure α<1/Lf or use backtracking) and unify notation (use τ consistently).
- Briefly sketch the proof ideas of Proposition 4.3; add intuition on the restricted invertibility step.
- Discuss extension to backtracking and accelerated variants, and whether the error bound implies local linear rates for FISTA (with/without restart).
- Comment on inexact proximal computations (partial SVD) and whether the analysis tolerates inexactness.
- Improve figure labeling and provide more informative experimental metrics (rate constants, phase where linear convergence starts, comparisons).

Recommendation
A strong theoretical contribution with clear novelty and solid proofs. Despite modest experiments and minor presentation issues, I recommend acceptance after addressing the noted clarifications.
