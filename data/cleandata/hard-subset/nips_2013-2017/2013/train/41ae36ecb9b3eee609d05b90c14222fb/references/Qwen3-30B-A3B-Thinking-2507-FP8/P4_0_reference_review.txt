

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in convex optimization: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieves linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors establish that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties, the PGM converges linearly to the optimal solution set. The key technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which had remained an open question despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization problems.

The paper relates its work to prior NIPS contributions on trace norm regularization, such as those by Recht et al. (2010), Ji and Ye (2009), and Toh and Yun (2010). While prior work established sublinear convergence rates for general convex loss functions, and linear rates under strong convexity assumptions, this paper fills a critical gap by showing linear convergence for a broader class of loss functions commonly used in machine learning (e.g., square loss in matrix completion, logistic loss in classification) where strong convexity fails.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof of the error bound and linear convergence. The authors carefully establish the key lemma (Lemma 4.1) using matrix perturbation theory, which overcomes the challenge that the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph unlike the ℓ1-norm. The proof is detailed and follows a logical structure, with the supplementary material providing additional technical details. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems validate the theoretical results, showing linear convergence in practice once iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, noting that their results require specific assumptions on the loss function form.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and states the main contributions. Key definitions (e.g., proximity operator, residual map) are introduced with sufficient detail. The proof structure is logical, with the main error bound theorem being proved by establishing several intermediate results. The writing is precise and accessible to experts in optimization. The authors provide sufficient mathematical detail for experts to reproduce the results, though some sections (particularly the proof of Lemma 4.1) are quite technical.

**Novelty:** The paper makes a significant novel contribution by resolving an open question about linear convergence for trace norm regularization without strong convexity assumptions. The key insight—observing that optimal solutions and -∇f(X̄) share singular vectors—enables the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound. This approach differs fundamentally from previous work on error bounds for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization, which relied on variable splitting. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior NIPS papers (e.g., Schmidt et al. 2011, Zhang et al. 2013), demonstrating clear differentiation from previous contributions.

**Significance:** The result is highly significant for machine learning applications where trace norm regularization is used (matrix completion, multi-task learning, classification). Many real-world problems use loss functions that are not strongly convex (e.g., square loss in matrix completion with non-injective measurement operators), making the authors' result directly applicable. The linear convergence rate is substantially faster than the sublinear rates previously known, which has practical implications for computational efficiency. The error bound itself is likely to be of independent interest for future work on matrix regularization.

**Strengths:**
- Resolves a long-standing open question in trace norm optimization
- Establishes a novel error bound using matrix perturbation theory
- Proves linear convergence under broader conditions than prior work
- Includes relevant numerical experiments validating the theory
- Well-structured and clearly written with precise mathematical arguments

**Weaknesses:**
- The assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) may exclude some loss functions not expressible in this form
- The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of implementing the proximity operator in practice (though this is standard)
- The error bound constants are not explicitly computed, limiting potential practical application
- The theoretical results are limited to the PGM without acceleration techniques

**Conclusion:** This paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of trace norm regularization. The proof of the error bound is technically sophisticated and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The results are both theoretically sound and practically relevant to machine learning applications. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria, with strong technical content, clear writing, and significant novelty. The numerical experiments provide appropriate validation of the theoretical claims. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it advances the theoretical understanding of a fundamental optimization problem with broad applications in machine learning.
