Summary
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization:
min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||_* where f(X) = h(A(X)).
Under Assumption 2.1 (h has Lipschitz gradient, is strictly convex and strongly convex on any compact convex subset of dom(h)) and nonemptiness of the optimal set, the authors prove a local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear-norm problem:
dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||S_τ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||_F for points in a bounded level set with small residual.
They then invoke the Luo–Tseng framework to obtain asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with step sizes uniformly bounded in (0, 1/L_f). The paper argues this covers common losses (squared, logistic) and arbitrary linear A, even when f is not strongly convex. The core technical ingredient is a spectral structural result: at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) can be simultaneously diagonalized (in a suitable orthonormal basis), enabling a perturbation analysis to establish a restricted invertibility property and the error bound.

Relation to prior work
PGM achieves O(1/k) (or O(1/k^2) with acceleration) for composite convex problems; linear rates are known under strong convexity. Lipschitzian error bounds and linear rates for first-order methods have been established for polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group lasso) and certain block-separable settings (Tseng, Luo–Tseng, Zhang–Jiang–Luo). The paper claims the first linear convergence result for nuclear-norm regularization without assuming strong convexity of f, by proving a tailored error bound for the nuclear norm (whose epigraph is non-polyhedral), thereby filling a gap relative to ℓ1/group-lasso analyses.

Quality
- Theory: The high-level argument (error bound ⇒ linear rate via Luo–Tseng) is standard and appropriate. The main novelty lies in the error bound proof (Theorem 3.1), with a clear roadmap (Lemma 4.1, Propositions 4.2/4.3). The simultaneous SVD property at optimality is plausible from the nuclear-norm subdifferential structure; the restricted invertibility (16) is the crux and is argued via a contradiction/perturbation construction. However, several key steps are deferred to the supplement (e.g., proofs of (9)–(11), Proposition 4.3). Based on the sketch, the approach seems technically sound, but the paper as submitted does not enable full verification.
- Assumptions: Assumption 2.1(b) (“strongly convex on any compact convex subset”) is unusual but holds for squared and logistic loss, and is used only to obtain local curvature (15). The step-size condition for Theorem 3.2 is standard; backtracking typically ensures 0<α_k≤1/L and a positive lower bound locally, but this link is not discussed.
- Experiments: Only small synthetic illustrations (matrix completion/classification) are provided, mainly to show linear slopes in the late phase. There is no comparison to baseline variants (e.g., accelerated PGM), no characterization of the basin of attraction, and no real-data evaluation. Given the theoretical nature, this is acceptable but limited as validation.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized, with a clear path from error bound to convergence. Some clarity issues:
- Residual definition: R(X)=S_τ(X−∇f(X))−X is used, while the algorithm uses S_{τα_k}(X − α_k ∇f(X)). The mismatch should be addressed explicitly (define R_α and state Theorem 3.1 for general α or explain the scaling argument).
- Assumption 2.1(b): It would help to briefly justify it for logistic loss and clarify that the strong convexity modulus is set-dependent.
- Proposition 4.2: The simultaneous SVD deserves a short proof sketch in the main paper (subgradient structure of ||·||_* and choice of bases in the orthogonal complement).
- Minor: Some references lack years; figure numbering and parameter symbols (τ vs µ) are inconsistent.

Originality
The claimed error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under the stated structure, and the ensuing linear convergence without strong convexity, appear novel relative to cited work on ℓ1/group-lasso and standard composite rates. The contribution is primarily theoretical. The paper could strengthen its positioning by more explicitly contrasting with general error-bound/KL-based linear-rate frameworks (and stating why those do/do not directly yield the desired result here).

Significance
Understanding when first-order methods enjoy linear rates beyond strong convexity is important. A tailored error bound for nuclear norm is a meaningful addition that could influence analyses of other spectral regularizers. Practically, the result is asymptotic and local, with no region-of-attraction characterization or computable constants, limiting immediate algorithmic impact. Still, the theoretical advance is of interest to the optimization community working on low-rank learning.

Pros
- Addresses a well-motivated gap: linear convergence for nuclear-norm problems without strong convexity.
- Develops a nontrivial error bound leveraging spectral structure.
- Clear linkage to the Luo–Tseng framework; assumptions cover common losses.
- Clean theoretical statement; illustrative experiments, albeit limited.

Cons
- Several key proofs deferred; some technical steps hard to verify from the main text.
- Residual/stepsize notational mismatch; conditions for backtracking to meet theorem assumptions not discussed.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal; no comparison with established baselines or robustness analyses.
- No guidance on the size of the linear regime or constants affecting practical convergence.

Questions/requests for authors
1) Please align the residual definition with the algorithmic step size (define R_α and restate Theorem 3.1 accordingly, or explain the scaling argument).
2) Provide a brief in-paper proof sketch of Proposition 4.2 from the subdifferential characterization of ||·||_*.
3) Clarify conditions under which backtracking ensures the step-size bounds in Theorem 3.2, including a positive lower bound α.
4) Discuss how large the region of linear convergence may be and whether any computable constants (η, ε) can be estimated from problem data.
5) Add comparisons to accelerated PGM (and possibly other methods) to contextualize practical impact, and fix reference/notation inconsistencies.
