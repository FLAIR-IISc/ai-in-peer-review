### Summary
This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + τ||X||*`. While linear convergence is known for cases where the loss function `f` is strongly convex, many important applications in machine learning, such as matrix completion with a square loss, do not satisfy this condition. The main contribution of this paper is to establish an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM under a much weaker assumption: that the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex but the linear operator `A` can be arbitrary, meaning `f` itself is not necessarily strictly or strongly convex.

The key to this result is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. The authors note that establishing such a bound for the trace norm has been an open problem, as prior techniques used for l1-norm regularization (which has a polyhedral epigraph) do not apply. The authors' main technical innovation is a new analysis based on matrix perturbation theory. They make the crucial observation that an optimal solution and the negative gradient at that solution share the same singular vectors, allowing them to analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimum and thereby derive the desired error bound.

### Strengths
The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and theoretical significance. It tackles a well-known and important open question in the optimization literature for machine learning: can we prove linear convergence for PGM on trace norm problems without the restrictive assumption of strong convexity? The authors answer this in the affirmative for a broad and practical class of loss functions.

1.  **Significant Theoretical Breakthrough:** The development of a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization is a major contribution. Unlike many papers that offer incremental refinements of existing algorithms or theory, this work provides a new, fundamental piece of analysis. The result provides a much-needed theoretical justification for the fast empirical performance of PGM on problems like matrix completion.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Proof Technique:** The approach taken to prove the error bound is highly innovative. The core difficulty is that the trace norm is non-polyhedral and its singular values depend on all matrix entries, preventing the use of standard analysis techniques based on separability. The authors’ key insight regarding the shared singular vector structure between the optimal solution and the gradient is a beautiful and powerful idea. Leveraging this with matrix perturbation theory to control the behavior of iterates near the optimal set is a creative and bold methodological step that could be influential in future analyses of matrix optimization problems.

3.  **High Impact and Relevance:** The problem class addressed is central to many areas of machine learning, including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. By relaxing the condition for linear convergence from strong convexity to a more realistic structural assumption, this work significantly deepens our understanding of one of the most widely used algorithms for these problems. This is a foundational result that others are very likely to build upon.

### Weaknesses
While the theoretical contribution is exceptionally strong, there are minor areas that could be improved. However, in my view, these do not detract from the paper's core value.

1.  **Superficial Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments serve to illustrate the theoretical finding of linear convergence but are not particularly deep. They confirm that the method converges linearly in practice on two standard problems, but they do not offer much beyond this visual confirmation. For instance, there are no comparisons to other methods, nor an exploration of how the convergence rate depends on problem parameters (e.g., rank, condition number, sampling rate). While a more thorough empirical study would be welcome, the paper's primary contribution is theoretical, and the experiments are sufficient as a proof of concept.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The analysis establishes an *asymptotic* linear rate of convergence. The paper does not provide an analysis of the non-asymptotic rate or characterize the constants involved (e.g., `η` in the error bound). This means the theory doesn't tell us how many iterations are needed to enter the linear convergence regime or how steep the convergence will be. This is a common feature of this style of analysis, but it's a practical limitation.

### Recommendation
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It represents a significant and bold step forward in the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The core contribution—the novel Lipschitzian error bound and its creative proof—resolves an important open question and introduces a new analysis technique that is both powerful and elegant. This is precisely the kind of fundamental, high-impact work that NIPS should champion. The weaknesses are minor and largely related to the scope of the experimental section, which is secondary to the paper's theoretical focus. The novelty and significance of the contribution are exceptionally high.
