Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized convex problems of the form minX f(X)+τ‖X‖*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, with Lipschitz gradient and “locally” strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain. The main contribution is an error bound showing that, locally near the optimal set, the distance to the set of optimal solutions is proportional to the proximal-gradient residual ‖Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X‖F. Using the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (R-linear for iterates, Q-linear for objective values) under step sizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf. The result applies to commonly used losses such as squared and logistic loss even when f is not strongly convex (e.g., A has a nontrivial nullspace), addressing a gap in prior theory.

The technical novelty centers on establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization, which is nontrivial because the nuclear norm has a nonpolyhedral epigraph (unlike ℓ1/group LASSO where existing error bounds apply). The key structural observation is that at an optimum X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors; this enables a spectral perturbation analysis to control the structure of nearby points and derive a restricted invertibility inequality needed for the error bound.

This work complements and extends: Beck & Teboulle (FISTA) and other proximal gradient analyses (sublinear rates), Schmidt-Le Roux-Bach (NIPS 2011; linear rates under strong convexity), Tseng and collaborators on error bounds for polyhedral-epigraph regularizers, and applications of PGM/accelerated PGM to nuclear-norm problems (Ma-Goldfarb-Chen; Toh-Yun). To the best of my assessment, linear convergence for nuclear-norm regularized PGM without strong convexity of f was previously open.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorems are clearly stated. The error-bound proof hinges on (i) the simultaneous SVD property derived from optimality conditions, (ii) a decomposition of limiting directions, and (iii) a contradiction via projection onto a suitably aligned subset of the solution set. The use of Luo–Tseng analysis thereafter is standard and appropriate. Some steps are deferred to the supplement (e.g., inequalities (9)–(11), Proposition 4.3), but the outline is coherent and consistent with known tools in convex analysis and matrix perturbation.
- Assumptions: Assumption 2.1(b) (“strongly convex on any compact convex subset”) is satisfied by squared and logistic losses and provides the local curvature (σ) used in inequality (15). The analysis is local: the error bound holds for sufficiently small residual within a fixed level set, which is standard for Luo–Tseng type results.
- Experiments: Two small-scale experiments (matrix completion with square loss; matrix classification with logistic loss) qualitatively illustrate linear rates once iterates enter the local region. They are illustrative rather than comprehensive.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized: setting, algorithm, residual map, main error bound, and convergence theorem are presented clearly. The role of the structural SVD property and its consequences are explained.
- Minor issues:
  - A few references are missing years and have inconsistent formatting.
  - The step size condition in Theorem 3.2 requires αk<1/Lf; in the matrix completion experiment α=1 is used. For the projection operator P, Lf=1, so α=1 equals the boundary; clarifying that the theorem assumes a strict inequality (and that backtracking or α<1 would satisfy it) would avoid confusion.
  - Some proofs rely on the supplement; brief intuition for Proposition 4.3 could help.

Originality
- Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization (nonpolyhedral) and leveraging it to obtain linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f appears novel. The simultaneous SVD insight at the optimum and its exploitation in the error-bound proof are nonstandard compared to ℓ1/group LASSO analyses.

Significance
- The result advances the theory of first-order methods for low-rank learning, covering widely used losses where f lacks global strong convexity (e.g., matrix completion with subsampling). It clarifies why practitioners often observe linear convergence asymptotically and provides conditions that formalize it.
- While the guarantees are local/asymptotic and constants are not explicit, the framework is likely to inform further analyses (e.g., other spectral regularizers, proximal-PL/Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz connections).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Addresses a recognized theoretical gap: linear convergence for nuclear-norm PGM without strong convexity of f.
- Introduces a new error-bound result for a nonpolyhedral regularizer, with a technically interesting proof leveraging spectral structure.
- Clear connection to widely used problems (square/logistic losses with linear operators).
- Formal, rigorous convergence statement using established frameworks; experiments consistent with theory.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- The error bound and linear rate are local; no explicit region of attraction or constants are provided, limiting quantitative predictiveness.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: small-scale, no comparisons to accelerated PGM or alternative solvers; no study of robustness to step-size choices or noise.
- Assumption 2.1(b) excludes nonsmooth/non-strongly convex losses like hinge; scope should be clearly delimited.
- Minor clarity/consistency issues (references, step-size boundary case).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Assumption 2.1(b), provide concrete verification for logistic loss, and discuss whether weaker curvature notions (e.g., restricted secant inequality or proximal-PL) would suffice.
- State explicitly how the constants (η, ε) in the error bound depend on the level set and problem data, and whether a computable bound is feasible.
- Reconcile the step-size condition in theory with practice (α=1 vs. α<1/Lf), and note that backtracking enforces αk<1/Lf.
- Expand experiments: include accelerated PGM, larger problems, and plots of the residual vs. distance to optimum to empirically illustrate the error bound.

Overall assessment
This is a technically solid and novel theoretical contribution establishing asymptotic linear convergence of PGM for a broad class of nuclear-norm regularized problems without strong convexity of f. The scope and assumptions are relevant to common ML applications. The primary limitations are the local nature of the result and modest empirical validation. The work is suitable for NeurIPS given its theoretical advance and relevance.
