This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically when the loss function has a particular structure: f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is a strictly convex function. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under these conditions, a result that extends existing theory by not requiring strong convexity of the overall loss function f. This is a valuable contribution, as many practical loss functions, such as those used in matrix completion, are not strongly convex.

The paper is well-structured. It begins with a thorough introduction that clearly motivates the problem and highlights the gap in existing literature. The subsequent sections define the necessary preliminaries, present the main results (Theorem 3.1 – error bound, Theorem 3.2 – linear convergence), outline the proof strategy, and provide numerical experiments to validate the findings. The presentation is generally clear and concise.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result for the PGM under the specified conditions (f(X) = h(A(X)) without strong convexity of f) is a genuine contribution. This expands the theoretical understanding of PGM's performance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous, building upon established techniques like Luo and Tseng’s framework. The key innovation lies in deriving a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the interdependence of matrix entries.
*   **Significance:**  The result has potential impact in various applications where trace norm regularization is used, especially in scenarios where strong convexity assumptions are not met. Matrix completion and multi-task learning are highlighted as examples.
*   **Clarity:** The writing is generally clear and the mathematical notation is standard. The motivation behind each step and assumption is well-explained.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments provides supporting evidence for the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proof Complexity:** While the proof appears sound, its length and complexity make it difficult to fully verify within the scope of a review. The reliance on several lemmas and propositions adds to this complexity.  A more concise presentation of the critical steps in the proof could improve accessibility.
*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while valuable, are relatively limited. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of problem instances (different matrix sizes, sparsity levels, and alternative loss functions) would strengthen the empirical support.
*   **Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b) requiring strong convexity of *h* on any convex compact subset of *dom(h)* seems potentially restrictive. While justified in the context of the proof, a discussion of its practical implications and the sensitivity of the results to this assumption would be beneficial.  The authors acknowledge the assumption is needed to maintain the smoothness and curvature properties, but further discussion is desired.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper doesn't discuss recent work on accelerated proximal gradient methods for non-strongly convex functions, such as those utilizing variance reduction techniques. While the paper focuses on linear convergence, a comparison with sublinear rates achievable via acceleration would be useful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound result with potential significance in several application areas.
*   The proof strategy is well-motivated and builds upon established theoretical frameworks.
*   The numerical experiments provide initial validation of the theoretical findings.
*   The problem addressed is relevant to the NIPS community.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The complexity of the proof may hinder thorough verification by reviewers.
*   The scope of the numerical experiments is limited.
*   The strong convexity assumption on *h* might limit the applicability of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the theory of proximal gradient methods. The novelty and technical soundness of the result outweigh the weaknesses. While the proof's complexity and limited scope of the numerical experiments warrant consideration, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the comments regarding assumption discussion and potential expansion of numerical results.
