This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. It is well-known that for a general convex loss function `f` with a Lipschitz continuous gradient, PGM achieves a sublinear `O(1/k)` convergence rate, which can be improved to `O(1/k^2)` with acceleration. Linear convergence rates `O(c^k)` are typically only guaranteed when `f` is strongly convex. This paper makes a significant contribution by establishing a linear convergence rate for PGM under a weaker condition. Specifically, it considers loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a smooth, strictly convex function. This class of functions is not necessarily strongly convex (e.g., if the operator `A` is not injective, as is common in matrix completion), but it covers many important machine learning applications, including those with square loss or logistic loss.

The key technical innovation is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound establishes that for any point `X` in a level set of the objective function and sufficiently close to the optimal set `X*`, the distance to the optimal set, `dist(X, X*)`, is bounded by a constant multiple of the norm of a residual map, `‖R(X)‖F`. This residual measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Such error bounds have been instrumental in proving the linear convergence of first-order methods for problems with `ℓ1` and group LASSO regularizers. However, extending this analysis to the trace norm has been an open problem, primarily because the trace norm lacks the separable structure or polyhedral epigraph of the `ℓ1` norm, which were exploited in previous analyses. The authors overcome this challenge by leveraging the spectral structure of the problem. A crucial insight is that for any optimal solution `X̄`, `X̄` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X̄)` can be simultaneously diagonalized by the same singular vectors. This structural property, combined with matrix perturbation theory, allows the authors to analyze the behavior of points near the optimal set and establish the desired error bound. With this bound in hand, the paper uses the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10] to prove the R-linear convergence of the iterates `Xk` and the Q-linear convergence of the objective values `F(Xk)`.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The main theoretical claims—the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and the resulting linear convergence (Theorem 3.2)—appear to be sound. The proof of the error bound is non-trivial and constitutes a complete piece of work. It follows the powerful proof-by-contradiction template from previous work on error bounds but introduces novel and necessary arguments tailored to the spectral properties of the trace norm. The assumptions on the loss function are clearly stated and are satisfied by many common models in machine learning. The authors are careful in their presentation, and the numerical experiments, while simple, provide empirical validation of the theoretical linear convergence rate.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work within the existing literature and highlighting its primary contributions. The problem setup and main results are presented cleanly before diving into the technical proof. The high-level structure of the proof is understandable. However, the core of the proof in Section 4 is extremely dense and challenging to parse. The argument relies on a delicate case analysis of singular values and a contradiction argument whose intuition is not immediately obvious. While technical density is expected for a result of this nature, the readability of this section could be improved, perhaps by providing more intuition for the decomposition of `Q̄` (Proposition 4.3) and the subsequent steps that lead to the contradiction.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While PGM and the Luo-Tseng framework for proving linear convergence are not new, their application to this problem setting is enabled by the paper's main novel contribution: the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first such result and it affirmatively answers an open question in the literature. The technical approach for proving the bound, which relies on the shared singular vector structure of the optimality conditions, is novel and tailored specifically to the challenges posed by the trace norm. This work significantly advances the theory of first-order methods for low-rank matrix problems beyond the standard strong convexity assumption.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and impactful for both theoreticians and practitioners in machine learning and optimization. The paper provides the first theoretical justification for the linear convergence of PGM on a broad class of important problems, like matrix completion and multi-task learning, where strong convexity of the loss function does not hold. This provides a stronger theoretical foundation for using a simple, widely-used algorithm on these problems. The error bound itself is a powerful analytical tool that may find applications in the analysis of other algorithms for trace norm regularization or in statistical analyses of the solutions. The work is likely to be built upon by other researchers studying convergence rates for structured non-smooth optimization problems.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper resolves an open and important theoretical question by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, leading to a proof of linear convergence for PGM without strong convexity.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The technical contributions are substantial and the proofs appear correct, employing novel techniques to handle the non-separable nature of the trace norm.
*   **Impact:** The results are relevant to a wide range of machine learning applications involving low-rank matrix recovery and provide a much stronger theoretical understanding of a popular algorithm's performance.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, making its contributions easy to understand, even if the detailed proofs are complex.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   **Proof Density:** The main proof section is very dense, which may limit its accessibility. Improving the intuitive explanations in this section would strengthen the paper.
*   **Local Convergence:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, as the error bound holds only in a neighborhood of the optimal solution set. The paper does not provide any characterization of this neighborhood or the constants involved, which limits the practical implications of the rate. However, this is a common feature of this type of analysis.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, significant, and technically deep result that advances our understanding of optimization methods for a crucial class of machine learning problems. I recommend acceptance.
