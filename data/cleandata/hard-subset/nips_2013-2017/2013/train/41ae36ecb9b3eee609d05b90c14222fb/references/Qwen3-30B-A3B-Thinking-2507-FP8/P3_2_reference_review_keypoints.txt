- Main result: Proves a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization and uses it to establish linear convergence of the proximal gradient method without strong convexity.

- Key technical insight: Optimal solutions share singular vectors with −∇f(X̄), enabling the error bound despite the trace norm’s non-polyhedral epigraph.

- Novelty: Resolves an open problem by extending linear convergence results to trace-norm regularization where prior work required strong convexity.

- Theoretical soundness: Rigorous, well-structured proofs (Theorem 3.1 and 3.2) with careful handling of the challenging Lemma 4.1 and restricted invertibility property.

- Experimental validation: Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice.

- Restrictive assumptions: Assumption f(X)=h(A(X)) is somewhat limiting and excludes more complex losses; extension to broader loss classes is unclear.

- Lack of practical insight: Paper does not quantify iteration savings or compare empirical iteration counts/rates to sublinear alternatives.

- Proof accessibility: Lemma 4.1(c) is technically dense; readers would benefit from more intuitive explanation of key constructions (e.g., X̂ = X̃k + ǫQ̄).

- Missing constants discussion: The dependence of error-bound constants (η, ε) on problem parameters (dimensions, rank) is not addressed.

- Comparison to ℓ1 work: Correctly contrasts trace-norm difficulty with ℓ1 due to non-polyhedral epigraph; could further explain why trace norm is fundamentally harder.

- Overall assessment: Valuable and significant theoretical contribution relevant to machine learning; recommends accept.

- Suggestion 1: Discuss practical implications of linear vs. sublinear convergence (e.g., iteration count comparisons).

- Suggestion 2: Provide more intuition for the core technical step in Lemma 4.1(c).

- Suggestion 3: Explain how the error-bound constants depend on problem parameters.

- Suggestion 4: Expand the related-work discussion to better articulate why trace-norm analysis is harder than ℓ1.
