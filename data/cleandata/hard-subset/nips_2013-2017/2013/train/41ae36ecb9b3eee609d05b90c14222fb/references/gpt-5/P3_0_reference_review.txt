Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, with f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h has Lipschitz gradient and is strictly/strongly convex on compact subsets of its domain. The main contributions are:
- A local Lipschitzian error bound: dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||F for X in a bounded level set and sufficiently small residual.
- Using the Luo–Tseng framework, this implies asymptotic linear convergence (Q-linear in objective, R-linear in iterates) of the proximal gradient method with fixed step sizes in (0, 1/Lf), without requiring strong convexity of f.
The technical novelty is handling the non-polyhedral nuclear norm by exploiting spectral structure: at an optimum X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular subspaces (Proposition 4.2), enabling a restricted invertibility argument and ultimately the error bound.

This relates to: standard O(1/k), O(1/k^2) convergence of PGM/APG for general composite convex objectives; linear rates under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al. 2011; Nesterov). It complements error bounds established for polyhedral nonsmooth parts (ℓ1, group lasso) by Tseng, Yun, and others, and contributes a first error bound for the trace norm where the epigraph is non-polyhedral. It also connects to the Luo–Tseng error bound analysis.

Quality:
- The high-level proof strategy is sound and aligns with known frameworks: establish a local EB, then obtain linear convergence of PGM. The key restricted invertibility Lemma 4.1(c) is carefully constructed via projections onto a spectrally aligned subset Xc and a contradiction using a direction Q̄ in the nullspace of A.
- Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous singular vector structure of X̄ and −∇f(X̄)) is critical. The argument appears plausible via subgradient structure of the nuclear norm and facial geometry; however, the statement is strong and not standard. Its proof should be made fully self-contained in the main text or at least carefully summarized; currently it is deferred and only sketched, making it hard to validate without the supplement.
- The handling of the three spectral cases (A/B/C) and the shrinkage conditions (25)-(26) is delicate but coherent. The use of h’s local strong convexity on a compact set to obtain (15) is appropriate.
- Minor technical inconsistency: the residual map R(X) is defined using step size 1, yet the algorithm uses variable step sizes αk and shrinkage Sταk. The proof of Theorem 3.2 refers to a bound like ||R(Xk)|| ≤ κ3 ||Xk − Xk+1||, which implicitly assumes a residual with the correct α. This should be cleaned up by defining Rα(X) = Sτα(X − α∇f(X)) − X and using it consistently.
- Numerical experiments are illustrative but limited (small synthetic problems, n=40). They show the expected linear tails but do not test robustness, do not compare against accelerated or alternative methods, nor do they empirically probe the error bound (e.g., correlation between distance and residual). Given the theory focus, this is acceptable but could be strengthened.

Clarity:
- Generally well-organized and readable. The introduction motivates the question (linear rates without strong convexity) and positions the result. Section 2 is clear on assumptions and setup. The proof outline is understandable.
- Notation could be tightened: reconcile τ vs µ; make the residual consistent with α; explicitly state Lf in terms of L and ||A||.
- Some references lack years/pages; please complete bibliographic entries.

Originality:
- The error bound for nuclear-norm regularized problems under general linear A and h with mild curvature is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. Prior linear-rate results for nuclear norm either assume strong convexity (or restricted strong convexity/statistical conditions) or focus on polyhedral regularizers. Leveraging spectral alignment to get EB is an original and interesting technique.
- It would be useful to discuss connections with alternative general frameworks for linear rates such as the Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz property (many such composite objectives are semi-algebraic and satisfy KL with favorable exponents) and with restricted strong convexity results in statistics (e.g., Negahban et al., Agarwal et al.), clarifying differences and advantages.

Significance:
- Proximal methods with nuclear-norm regularization are widely used. Showing asymptotic linear convergence under broad conditions advances theoretical understanding and may inform algorithm design (e.g., step size selection, stopping).
- The spectral-geometry-based EB may inspire analogous results for other spectral regularizers (e.g., spectral k-support norm) or Schatten p-norms; authors hint at this in conclusions.

Pros:
- New Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization with general linear measurements.
- Clean derivation of asymptotic linear convergence for PGM without strong convexity of f.
- Solid use of spectral geometry and Luo–Tseng framework.
- Clear assumptions encompassing common losses (square, logistic).

Cons:
- Critical spectral alignment proposition is nontrivial; proof details are deferred and should be surfaced more.
- Residual/operator and step-size notation inconsistency.
- Experiments are minimal; no comparisons or stress tests.
- Related work could be broadened (KL property; restricted strong convexity literature).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix the residual definition to include α and consistently adapt Theorem 3.1/3.2 statements.
- Move a succinct proof sketch of Proposition 4.2 into the main text, clarifying conditions under which X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, and how facial geometry of the nuclear norm ball is used (cite “Facial Structures of Schatten p-norms” more explicitly).
- Discuss the locality of the EB: how η and ε depend on ζ, L, σ, and ||A||. Any practical guidelines on when the linear regime is entered?
- Expand related work to include KL-based linear convergence results for proximal methods and statistical RSC-based linear rates, differentiating your assumptions.
- Enhance experiments: include larger-scale instances, compare with APG and other solvers (e.g., FISTA, SVT variants), show residual vs distance empirically, and report iteration counts/runtimes.

Questions to authors:
- Can your approach extend to other spectral regularizers (e.g., Schatten p, p>1; max-norm) or structured nuclear norms (overlaps)?
- Is there a way to make η explicit in terms of spectral gaps at the solution (e.g., τ − σi(−Ḡ))?
- Does backtracking (variable αk) preserve the same linear rate constants, and can you cover nonmonotone line search?

Overall: This is a solid theoretical contribution with a novel and technically interesting error bound leading to linear convergence of a widely used algorithm under realistic assumptions. With clarifications and minor fixes, it would be a strong NeurIPS paper. I recommend acceptance. Confidence: high.
