

# Review

This paper addresses an important open question in the optimization of trace norm-regularized problems: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) can achieve linear convergence without requiring the loss function f to be strongly convex. The authors establish a linear convergence rate for PGM when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which resolves a previously open question in the literature.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first linear convergence result for PGM applied to trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f. Prior work on linear convergence for PGM typically required strong convexity of f [16], while sublinear convergence rates were known for more general cases [12, 19]. The authors' error bound result is particularly noteworthy as it overcomes a fundamental challenge: unlike the ℓ1-norm, the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, making standard error bound techniques for sparse regularization inapplicable.

The paper's main strength lies in its novelty. To the best of my knowledge, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization has not been established before, and this is the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f. The approach using matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound is novel and represents a significant technical advance. The paper clearly articulates the gap it fills and demonstrates how it differs from prior work, with a thorough literature review.

The theoretical results are rigorously proven, and the numerical experiments complement the theoretical claims by demonstrating linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and matrix classification problems. The paper's structure is logical, with clear statements of assumptions and results, and the proofs are technically sound.

While the paper is strong in theoretical contribution, it has a few limitations. The scope of the result is limited to problems where f(X) = h(A(X)), and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this form covers many practical applications beyond the examples given. The technical presentation is dense, which might make the paper challenging to follow for some readers, though this is common in theoretical optimization papers.

The paper's significance is substantial for the optimization community working on low-rank matrix recovery. Linear convergence rates are highly desirable in practice, and this result extends the applicability of PGM to a broader class of problems than previously possible. The error bound itself could be valuable for analyzing other first-order methods for trace norm regularization.

In terms of the four criteria:
- Quality: Strong technical contribution with rigorous proofs and supporting experiments.
- Clarity: Well-organized but dense in technical details; could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the trace norm case differs from ℓ1-norm.
- Originality: Highly original; the error bound technique is novel and resolves a long-standing open question.
- Significance: Makes a meaningful contribution to the theory of optimization for low-rank matrix problems.

Pro for acceptance: This is the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm regularization without strong convexity of f, with a novel error bound technique that overcomes a fundamental challenge in the literature. The theoretical contribution is significant and fills a clear gap.

Con for acceptance: The scope is limited to a specific form of loss function, and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why this covers many practical applications.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions, I believe this paper makes a substantial advance in the field. The novelty of the error bound technique and the resolution of a previously open question outweigh the minor limitations in scope. The paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization that has been recognized in the literature [12, 16], and provides a technically sophisticated solution.

I recommend acceptance.
