Summary
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems of the form minX F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, and “locally” strongly convex (strongly convex on any convex compact subset of its domain). The central contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound for this problem class: near the solution set X, the distance dist(X, X) is bounded by a constant times the norm of the fixed-point residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Leveraging this error bound and the Luo–Tseng framework, the paper proves asymptotic linear convergence: objective values converge Q-linearly to the optimum and iterates converge R-linearly to the solution set, under step sizes αk bounded away from 0 and strictly less than 1/Lf.

The technical innovation is establishing the error bound for the nonpolyhedral nuclear norm, which does not admit the standard separable/block-decomposition arguments used for ℓ1 or (group) Lasso. The authors show that any optimal X̄ and −∇f(X̄) can be simultaneously SVD-diagonalized, and then use matrix perturbation arguments and a projection onto a manifold Xc of simultaneously diagonalizable matrices to prove a “restricted invertibility” inequality relating ||X − X̄||F and ||A(X − X̄)||2. This enables the error bound. Numerical experiments on matrix completion (square loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrate linear convergence near the solution.

Relation to prior work
- Proximal gradient and accelerated methods for composite convex objectives are classical (e.g., Nesterov; Beck–Teboulle’s FISTA; and Ma–Goldfarb–Chen; Toh–Yun; Ji–Ye for nuclear norm problems).
- Luo–Tseng’s error bound framework gives linear rates for first-order methods under local error bounds.
- Previous linear convergence results for proximal methods typically required strong convexity of f or polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1, group Lasso). The paper addresses the open case of nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of f, which is highly relevant in matrix completion and related problems.
- NIPS/NeruIPS context includes work on convergence rates of (inexact) proximal gradient (e.g., Schmidt–Le Roux–Bach, NIPS 2011).

Strengths
- Technical novelty: a local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization with f(X) = h(A(X)) and h strictly convex/smooth. To my knowledge, such an EB for the nuclear norm (nonpolyhedral) was not previously available under these assumptions.
- Clear path from EB to linear convergence via a well-established analysis framework; conditions on step sizes are standard.
- Applicability: covers common losses (square, logistic) used in matrix completion, multitask learning, and matrix classification, where f is not strongly convex due to non-injective A.
- The structural insight (simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −∇f(X̄)) is elegant and plausibly useful beyond this result.
- Experiments, while limited, corroborate linear convergence in the neighborhood of the solution.

Weaknesses
- The linear convergence is local/asymptotic: the error bound is proved near the solution set and under small residuals, so no global linear rate or global complexity bound is provided. This should be emphasized more prominently.
- Assumption 2.1(b) (h strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)) is somewhat unusual; while it holds for square loss and for logistic loss on bounded z, a brief discussion clarifying its scope and practical checkability would help.
- Constants in the error bound (η, ε) and in the linear rate are not quantified; some guidance or bounds depending on problem parameters (τ, Lf, ||A||, level-set diameter) would improve practical interpretability.
- Step-size requirement assumes αk < 1/Lf with a strict margin. For the logistic-loss experiment, backtracking is used; a short argument that the backtracking rule ensures ᾱ < 1/Lf (or a reference) would align the practice with the assumptions.
- Clarity/notation: a few minor issues (e.g., missing 1/2 factor in the proximity operator definition; mixing τ and μ for the regularization parameter in experiments; some reference formatting). Some key proof steps are deferred to the supplement; adding a concise sketch would help readers.

Quality
- The results appear technically sound, with a coherent proof strategy grounded in convex analysis and matrix perturbation. The use of optimality conditions for the nuclear norm subdifferential to align singular vectors is correct and standard. The restricted invertibility inequality is the key nontrivial ingredient and is argued carefully via contradiction and projections onto Xc. The derivation of the descent and error relations (9)–(11) is standard for PGM.
- Numerical experiments are illustrative but minimal; the theory does not hinge on them.

Clarity
- The paper is overall well organized and readable; assumptions and main theorems are clearly stated. Minor notation/typo issues should be fixed. A short discussion contrasting the nuclear-norm case with polyhedral regularizers in terms of EB assumptions would improve accessibility.

Originality
- The main contribution (local EB and asymptotic linear convergence for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of f) is novel and addresses a recognized gap relative to ℓ1/group-Lasso cases.

Significance
- The result is significant for theory and practice: PGM is widely used for nuclear norm problems, and linear local convergence under realistic assumptions is valuable. The structural tools (simultaneous SVD at optimality) may inspire further advances (e.g., error bounds for other spectral regularizers).

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify Assumption 2.1(b) with concrete examples and a brief discussion of when it may fail.
- Provide qualitative dependence of η and ε (Theorem 3.1) and the rate constant (Theorem 3.2) on problem data where possible.
- State explicitly that the rate is local and describe how the algorithm transitions from sublinear global behavior to linear local behavior.
- Ensure step-size conditions are satisfied by the backtracking strategy used in experiments; cite a standard linesearch that guarantees αk ≤ 1/Lf with α bounded away from 0.
- Fix minor typos/notation (prox operator factor 1/2; consistent use of τ; figure axes/labels; reference years).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an open and practically relevant theoretical question.
- Strong, nontrivial analysis with clear main theorems.
- Broad applicability to common ML losses and settings.
- Fits NeurIPS topics in optimization for machine learning.

Cons for acceptance
- Local (asymptotic) nature of the rate; no global rate or explicit constants.
- Some reliance on supplementary material for key proofs.
- Experiments are limited in scope and do not compare with alternative methods.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with clear relevance to the NeurIPS community.
