This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. The standard theory guarantees a linear convergence rate `O(c^k)` if the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is strongly convex. However, many important problems in machine learning, such as matrix completion with a square loss, do not satisfy this strong convexity assumption, and for these, only sublinear rates `O(1/k^2)` are typically known for PGM.

The main contribution of this paper is to bridge this gap. The authors prove that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence under a more general set of assumptions. Specifically, they consider loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is an arbitrary linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function (and strongly convex on compact sets). This structure is common in machine learning but does not imply that `f` is strongly convex, especially when `A` has a non-trivial null space.

The key technical innovation is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1). This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set, `dist(X, X)`, to the norm of a residual map, `‖R(X)‖_F`, which measures the violation of the first-order optimality conditions. Establishing such an error bound is known to be challenging for the trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph and the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. The authors overcome this by introducing a novel proof technique that leverages matrix perturbation theory and a crucial observation about the shared singular vector structure of an optimal solution and its corresponding gradient (Proposition 4.2). Once this error bound is established, the linear convergence of PGM (Theorem 3.2) follows from the well-established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in first-order optimization for a widely used class of problems. Establishing linear convergence for non-strongly convex objectives is a significant theoretical advance. The result provides a much stronger theoretical justification for using PGM on problems like matrix completion and matrix classification than was previously available.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The core contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, is highly original and technically deep. The authors correctly identify that prior techniques used for `l1`-norm or group LASSO regularization (based on separability) do not apply here. The proposed proof strategy, which relies on analyzing the spectral decomposition of iterates near the optimal set, is novel and powerful. The argument is intricate but appears to be sound. It successfully resolves what the authors frame as an open problem.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, precisely states the gap in the existing literature, and outlines its contributions and technical approach. The main results are stated upfront, and the proof is structured logically, making a complex technical argument relatively easy to follow. The distinction between the main error bound result and the subsequent convergence proof is clear.

4.  **Completeness:** The work is a complete scientific contribution. It provides a full theoretical analysis, from the foundational error bound to the final convergence rate theorem. Furthermore, the theory is complemented with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification, which empirically demonstrate the predicted linear convergence and validate the theory on practical problems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) and the resulting linear convergence hold once the iterates `X_k` are sufficiently close to the optimal set `X` (i.e., for `F(X_k) ≤ ζ` and `‖R(X_k)‖_F ≤ ǫ`). The paper does not provide any characterization of the constants (`η`, `κ`, etc.) or the size of this "basin of attraction." While this is common for this type of analysis, it leaves open the practical question of how many iterations of sublinear convergence are required before the linear rate kicks in.

2.  **Minor Lack of Detail in Experiments:** The experimental section is a good validation of the theory but could be slightly improved.
    *   The y-axis label "Error in Solution" in Figure 1 is ambiguous. It would be clearer if it was specified whether this is the distance to a known ground truth, distance to the final iterate, or the distance to the optimal set `dist(X_k, X)`.
    *   For the matrix classification experiment, the setup is deferred to another paper [21]. While acceptable, a bit more detail on the problem formulation within the paper would improve its self-containedness.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM on a broad class of trace norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity.
*   Introduces a novel and significant technical result: a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, which is of independent interest.
*   The proof technique is innovative, overcoming limitations of previous methods by using spectral analysis.
*   The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and the theoretical claims are empirically supported.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is asymptotic, and there is no discussion of the non-asymptotic convergence behavior or the constants involved in the convergence rate.
*   The experimental section, while supportive, could benefit from minor clarifications.

### Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of first-order optimization methods for structured problems. The central result—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—is a strong technical achievement that resolves an important open question and directly leads to stronger convergence guarantees for a popular algorithm on a wide class of machine learning problems. The weaknesses are minor and do not detract from the importance of the contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
