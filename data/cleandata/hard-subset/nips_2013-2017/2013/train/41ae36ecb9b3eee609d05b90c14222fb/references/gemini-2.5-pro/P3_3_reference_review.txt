**Summary and Relation to Prior Work**

This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`. It is well-established that for a general convex and smooth `f`, PGM achieves a sublinear `O(1/k)` rate, improvable to `O(1/k^2)` with acceleration. Linear convergence is typically guaranteed only when `f` is strongly convex. However, many important problems in machine learning, such as matrix completion with a square loss, feature a loss function `f` that is convex but not strongly convex (e.g., when the measurement operator is not injective).

This work bridges this gap by proving that PGM can achieve an asymptotic linear rate of convergence under a weaker set of assumptions. Specifically, it considers loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex and smooth, but the linear operator `A` can be arbitrary, meaning `f` itself is not necessarily strictly or strongly convex. The key technical contribution is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map. Establishing such error bounds is a powerful technique for proving linear convergence, as pioneered by Luo and Tseng [10], and has been successfully applied to `l1`-norm and group LASSO problems [22, 23, 25]. However, extending this analysis to the trace norm is highly non-trivial due to its non-polyhedral epigraph and the non-separable nature of singular values, which foils the variable-splitting proof techniques used for the `l1`-norm. The authors overcome this by using a novel argument based on matrix perturbation theory and a key structural property of the optimal solutions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper resolves an important open question regarding the convergence of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity provides a much-needed theoretical justification for the excellent empirical performance of PGM on problems like matrix completion. The central contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm (Theorem 3.1), is novel and of independent interest. It significantly advances the theoretical understanding of this important class of problems.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is deep and rigorous. The main challenge lies in the proof of the error bound (sketched in Section 4), which requires sophisticated tools. The key insight that an optimal solution `X̄` and its corresponding negative gradient `-∇f(X̄)` can be simultaneously diagonalized by the same singular vectors (Proposition 4.2) is elegant and powerful. This allows the authors to analyze the spectral structure of iterates near the solution set and ultimately derive the crucial "restricted invertibility" property (Lemma 4.1(c)), which is the linchpin of the proof. The overall proof strategy, arguing by contradiction based on a sequence violating the error bound, is standard in this area, but its execution for the trace norm case is highly original.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates the contribution within the existing literature. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are stated upfront, followed by a logical and well-organized proof sketch. The authors do a commendable job of explaining why existing proof techniques fail for the trace norm and what new ideas are needed, which greatly enhances the reader's appreciation of the work's novelty.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, meaning it is guaranteed to hold only after the iterates enter a certain neighborhood of the optimal set. While this is a common feature of analyses based on local error bounds, the paper would benefit from a discussion on the "pre-asymptotic" phase. For instance, are there any problem-dependent parameters (e.g., properties of `A`, the value of `τ`, the rank of the optimal solution) that influence how quickly the iterates enter the region of linear convergence? This would provide valuable insight for practitioners.

2.  **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments in Section 5 serve as a good proof-of-concept, visually demonstrating the linear convergence predicted by the theory. However, they could be more comprehensive. The experiments confirm the theory but do not test its limits or provide deeper insights. For example, it would be interesting to see how the convergence rate (i.e., the slope of the line in the semi-log plot) is affected by problem conditioning, the rank of the true solution, or the sampling ratio `θ`. Such an analysis would add significant value by connecting the theoretical constants to practical problem characteristics.

3.  **Proof Sketch Density:** The proof sketch in Section 4 is necessarily dense due to space constraints. While the overall logic is clear, a bit more intuition could be provided in the main text for key steps. For example, a sentence or two explaining the intuition behind why Proposition 4.3 (the decomposition of `Q̄`) holds, or why it leads to the final contradiction, would make the technical core of the paper more accessible without requiring the reader to consult the supplementary material.

4.  **Minor Typo:** In Section 5, the text describes experiments for Matrix Completion, but the associated plots are labeled "Figure 2: Matrix Classification". This appears to be a copy-paste or labeling error.

**Summary of Recommendation**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the optimization literature relevant to machine learning. The results are significant, the technical approach is novel and sophisticated, and the paper is written with outstanding clarity. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for further strengthening the discussion and experiments, rather than any flaws in the core contribution. This work elegantly resolves a long-standing question and is highly likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
