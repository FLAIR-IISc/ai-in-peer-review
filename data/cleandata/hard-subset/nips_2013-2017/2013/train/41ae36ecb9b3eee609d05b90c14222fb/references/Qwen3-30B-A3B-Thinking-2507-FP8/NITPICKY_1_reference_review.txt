

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper addresses the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization problems. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which allows them to prove linear convergence of PGM under conditions that do not require strong convexity of the loss function. The key technical contribution is Proposition 4.2, which establishes that optimal solutions and the negative gradient share singular vectors, enabling the use of matrix perturbation theory. The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the literature.

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a significant gap in the literature regarding linear convergence of first-order methods for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity.
2. The theoretical contribution is non-trivial and fills an important theoretical void.
3. The paper is well-organized with clear mathematical statements and a logical flow.
4. The numerical experiments complement the theoretical results, though they are incomplete in the provided text.

## Weaknesses
Despite the paper's significant theoretical contribution, I identified several serious issues that prevent me from recommending acceptance:

1. **Critical Missing Figures**: The paper references "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" in the numerical experiments section but does not include these figures. The numerical results are a key part of the paper's contribution, and their absence makes it impossible to evaluate the empirical claims.

2. **Technical Inaccuracies**:
   - In Section 2.1, Assumption 2.1(b) states "strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)." This is misleading, as strong convexity typically requires a uniform modulus across the entire domain. The authors should clarify that they mean "strongly convex with modulus depending on the compact subset."
   - In the proof of Theorem 3.1, the authors state "−Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄k‖*" but the explanation is confusing. The correct optimality condition is 0 ∈ ∇f(X̄k) + τ∂‖X̄k‖*, which implies -Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄k‖* (since ∇f(X̄k) = Ḡ), but the explanation should be clearer.
   - In Theorem 3.2, the step size condition is stated as "0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf," which is redundant and confusing. The standard condition is αk ≤ 1/Lf, so the authors should have written "0 < α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ ≤ 1/Lf" (with appropriate constants).

3. **Notational Inconsistencies**:
   - The paper uses "trace norm" and "nuclear norm" interchangeably without consistent terminology.
   - The notation for the projection is inconsistent: "X̄" is used for the optimal solution in some places and for the projection in others.
   - The Frobenius norm is written as "‖·‖F" and "‖·‖_F" inconsistently.
   - The paper uses "k-th iteration" and "k-th iteration" without consistent formatting.

4. **Mathematical Errors**:
   - In the proof of Theorem 3.2, the inequality F(Xk+1)− v∗ ≤ κ2 [ (dist(Xk,X ))2 + ‖Xk+1 −Xk‖2F ] is incorrect. It should be F(Xk+1)− v∗ ≤ κ2(dist(Xk,X))2 + κ3‖Xk+1 −Xk‖2F for some constants κ2, κ3.
   - In Lemma 4.1, the condition "Rk/δk → 0" should be written as "Rk/δk → 0" with a comma for clarity.

5. **Minor Formatting Issues**:
   - The reference list contains extraneous commas at the end of some entries.
   - The paper states "the trace norm is the tightest convex lower bound of the rank function over the set of matrices with spectral norm at most one" which is slightly inaccurate (it's the tightest convex lower bound over the spectral norm ball, not "over the set of matrices with spectral norm at most one").
   - The paper uses "so-called" in academic writing, which is unnecessary and unprofessional.

## Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. However, the presence of critical technical errors, notational inconsistencies, and the absence of the referenced figures (which are essential for evaluating the numerical results) are serious flaws that prevent me from recommending acceptance.

I would recommend rejection because these issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility and completeness. The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but the technical errors and missing figures make it impossible to properly evaluate the work. A revised version that addresses these issues would be worthy of consideration.

## Specific Recommendations for Revision
1. Include the missing figures (Figure 1 and Figure 2) in the final version.
2. Correct the technical errors in the proofs, particularly the step size condition and the inequality in Theorem 3.2.
3. Standardize the notation throughout the paper (consistent use of trace norm/nuclear norm, Frobenius norm, and projection notation).
4. Clarify the meaning of "strongly convex on any convex compact subset" in Assumption 2.1(b).
5. Remove unnecessary phrases like "so-called" from the text.
6. Fix the minor formatting issues in the reference list.

Without these corrections, the paper's technical claims cannot be properly verified, and the contribution is diminished by these avoidable issues.
