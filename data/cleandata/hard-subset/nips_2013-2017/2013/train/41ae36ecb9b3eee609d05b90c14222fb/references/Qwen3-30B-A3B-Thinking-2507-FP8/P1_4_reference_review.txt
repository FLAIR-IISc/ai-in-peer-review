

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖_*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, and A a linear operator. The authors resolve an important open question by proving a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which had remained unresolved despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization. This error bound, established through novel matrix perturbation theory techniques, enables the application of Luo and Tseng's convergence framework to show linear convergence without requiring f to be strongly convex. The paper covers important machine learning applications like matrix completion (with square loss) and matrix classification (with logistic loss), with numerical experiments confirming linear convergence in practice.

The work builds on foundational results in convex optimization (Luo and Tseng, 1992) and trace norm regularization (Recht et al., 2010), but makes a significant technical advance by extending linear convergence guarantees to a broader class of loss functions that do not satisfy strong convexity.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Contribution:** The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which was an open question. This is particularly significant because the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph (unlike ℓ1-norm), making standard approaches based on variable splitting inapplicable.

2. **Rigor and Clarity:** The proofs are exceptionally well-structured and rigorous, with careful attention to technical details. The key Lemma 4.1 and its proof using matrix perturbation theory are elegant and clearly presented. The paper is well-organized with logical flow from problem setup to main results.

3. **Practical Relevance:** The class of loss functions considered (f(X) = h(A(X))) covers numerous important machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.), making the results immediately applicable. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice, validating the theoretical results.

4. **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental gap in the literature—establishing linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f. This is particularly valuable for machine learning applications where strong convexity is often not satisfied (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective measurement operators).

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Applications:** While the paper covers important applications, the class of loss functions f(X) = h(A(X)) is somewhat restrictive. The paper does not consider more general non-convex loss functions that might appear in practice, though this is likely beyond the scope of the current work.

2. **Practical Implementation Details:** The theoretical result requires step sizes bounded away from 0 and 1/L_f. While the numerical experiments use fixed step sizes, the paper does not discuss practical approaches to estimate L_f for large-scale problems, which could be challenging in practice.

3. **Comparison with Related Work:** The paper mentions that existing error bounds for ℓ1-norm regularization rely on polyhedral epigraphs, but could more explicitly compare the nature of their error bound with those for other regularizations. A more detailed discussion of how their error bound differs from previous results would strengthen the contribution.

## Overall Assessment
This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of first-order methods for trace norm-regularized problems. The paper is technically sound, rigorously proven, and addresses an important open question in the field. The results are of clear significance to machine learning researchers working with low-rank matrix problems.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with good organization, original in its approach to establishing the error bound, and significant in its contribution to the field. The authors have carefully considered both the strengths and limitations of their work.

## Recommendation
Accept. This paper makes a clear, important contribution to the theory of convex optimization with applications to machine learning. The resolution of the open question regarding error bounds for trace norm regularization is a significant technical achievement that will be valuable to the broader optimization and machine learning communities.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Resolves an important open question in trace norm regularization (error bounds without strong convexity)
- Establishes linear convergence under weaker conditions than previous work
- Covers important machine learning applications with numerical validation
- Theoretical results are rigorously proven with novel techniques
- Well-written with clear exposition of technical contributions

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- The class of loss functions considered is somewhat restricted (though this is reasonable for a theoretical contribution)
- Practical step size selection could be elaborated upon for broader applicability

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides valuable theoretical insights that will inform future work on optimization for low-rank matrix problems. It is a strong contribution to the NIPS conference.
