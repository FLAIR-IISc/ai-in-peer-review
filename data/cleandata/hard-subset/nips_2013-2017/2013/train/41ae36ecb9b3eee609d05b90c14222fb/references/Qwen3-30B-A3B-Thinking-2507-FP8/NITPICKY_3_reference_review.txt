

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h strictly convex with smoothness properties. The key contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which the authors prove using matrix perturbation theory and the observation that optimal solutions and their gradients share singular vectors. The paper shows that linear convergence holds without requiring strong convexity of f, which is significant for applications like matrix completion where f is not strongly convex.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important open question in optimization for matrix problems, providing a rigorous linear convergence result that extends beyond the standard strong convexity assumption.
2. The proof technique, particularly the use of matrix perturbation theory and the key observation about singular vectors, is novel and well-executed.
3. The numerical experiments for matrix completion and classification demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.
4. The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions and logical progression from preliminaries to main results.

## Weaknesses

### Formatting and Presentation Issues
1. The paper uses inconsistent notation for the trace norm: "‖X‖*" in the introduction versus "‖X‖∗" in the main text (e.g., equation 1). This should be standardized throughout.
2. The section numbering in Section 4 (4.1, 4.1.1, 4.1.2) is inconsistent with standard academic formatting.
3. In the references, several entries have formatting issues (e.g., "Proc. 24th ICML, pages 17–24, , " has an extra comma).
4. The paper states "m ≤ n" without justification in Lemma 4.1(c), which is a significant oversight for a rigorous paper.

### Technical and Mathematical Issues
1. The paper claims to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f, but Assumption 2.1(b) requires h to be strongly convex on convex compact subsets of dom(h). This is not equivalent to strong convexity of f, and the paper fails to clarify how this assumption relates to the strong convexity of f. For instance, in matrix completion with square loss, f(X) = ‖A(X)-b‖²/2, the strong convexity of h does not imply strong convexity of f (as the paper acknowledges), but the paper doesn't fully explore the implications of this assumption.

2. In Lemma 4.1, the authors claim "the sequence {Xk}k≥0 is bounded" but provide no justification. Given that the paper establishes a bounded level set in Section 2.2, this should be explicitly connected.

3. The proof of Theorem 3.1 uses "Rk → 0" without specifying the norm (Frobenius is implied, but this should be stated). Similarly, the paper inconsistently uses "‖Rk‖2F" and "‖Rk‖F" in the same proof.

4. Proposition 4.2 states that X̄ and −Ḡ share singular vectors, but this is not obvious from the optimality conditions. The paper should provide a brief justification or reference to support this claim.

5. The step size condition in Theorem 3.2 is given as 0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf, but Lf (the Lipschitz constant of ∇f) is never defined. This is a critical omission for reproducibility.

6. The numerical experiments for matrix classification use backtracking line search but provide no details on implementation or justification for this choice, which weakens the practical relevance.

### Related Work and Claims
1. The paper claims to be "the first linear convergence result concerning the application of a first-order method to the trace norm-regularized problem (1) that does not require the strong convexity of f." However, Zhang et al. (2013) have established linear convergence for a proximal gradient method under a different set of assumptions. The paper should clarify how their result differs from and extends Zhang et al.

2. The paper cites Luo and Tseng (10) for their convergence framework, but fails to explicitly state how their error bound differs from previous error bounds for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization.

## Recommendation
This paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to optimization theory with clear practical relevance. However, the paper contains several critical technical inconsistencies and formatting errors that undermine its rigor. The inconsistent notation, missing definitions, and unexplained assumptions are particularly problematic for a paper that claims to provide a precise convergence analysis.

While the main results appear to be correct, the paper's presentation is not meticulous enough for NIPS. The minor technical flaws (inconsistent notation, unexplained assumptions, missing definitions) collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be unacceptable in a high-quality NIPS submission.

The paper's significance is strong, but the technical execution falls short of NIPS standards. The authors should address the formatting inconsistencies, clarify the relationship between their assumptions and strong convexity, and provide missing definitions before the paper can be considered for acceptance.

**Recommendation: Reject**
