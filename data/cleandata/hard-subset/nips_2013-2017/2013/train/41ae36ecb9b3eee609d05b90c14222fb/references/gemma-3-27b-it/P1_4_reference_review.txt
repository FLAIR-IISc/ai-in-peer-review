## Peer Review for "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized optimization problems, a common setup in various machine learning applications like matrix completion and low-rank approximation. The authors establish *linear* convergence under fairly general conditions on the loss function *f*, specifically requiring that *f(X) = h(A(X))*, where *h* is strictly convex and *A* is a linear operator. This is a significant result because it extends the known linear convergence guarantees – typically requiring strong convexity of *f* – to a broader class of loss functions where strong convexity doesn't hold, such as in matrix completion with non-full rank measurement matrices.  The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

**Related Work:**

The paper correctly positions its contribution within the existing literature on PGM convergence. It acknowledges the standard sublinear rates (O(1/k) and O(1/k<sup>2</sup>)) and the known linear rates achievable under strong convexity. The authors accurately highlight the gap in the literature - lack of linear convergence results *without* strong convexity of the loss function *f*. They also appropriately cite related work on error bounds for composite optimization, pointing out the limitations of prior approaches based on variable splitting when applied to the trace norm.  The paper builds on Luo and Tseng's framework for analyzing PGM convergence and appropriately references relevant literature on trace norm minimization and proximal algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** The linear convergence result is a valuable contribution. It broadens the applicability of PGM to a wider range of practical problems where strong convexity is not guaranteed, particularly in areas like matrix completion.
* **Novel Error Bound:** The development of a Lipschitzian error bound specific to the trace norm regularization is a crucial technical innovation.  The authors clearly articulate why existing methods from ℓ<sub>1</sub> regularization don’t directly apply and convincingly explain their approach leveraging matrix perturbation theory.
* **Generality:** The condition *f(X) = h(A(X))* is sufficiently general to encompass many common loss functions used in practice, enhancing the paper’s relevance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the key ideas are explained clearly.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence on matrix completion and classification tasks provides further confidence in the theoretical results.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature and clearly position their work within the existing body of knowledge.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity:** While the authors state the core ideas, the proof sketch in the main body lacks the necessary detail for complete verification. It heavily relies on supplementary material and lemmas (like Lemma 4.1) without fully elucidating the steps. This makes it difficult to assess the validity of the technical arguments without a significant time investment in the supplement.
* **Practical Implications of Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b) on *h* (strong convexity on compact subsets) might be restrictive in some applications.  A discussion of how robust the result is to deviations from this assumption, or suggestions for relaxing it, would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Numerical Exploration:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited.  Exploring the impact of different step sizes (α<sub>k</sub>) and the effect of the measurement operator *A* on the convergence rate would be beneficial.
* **Discussion of Constant Factors:** While linear convergence is established, the paper doesn't discuss the constant factor involved in the rate.  Understanding the magnitude of this constant is crucial for assessing the practical performance of the algorithm.
* **Overly Detailed Proposition 4.2 & 4.3:** While technically correct, the presentation of Propositions 4.2 and 4.3 feels somewhat dense and could be streamlined. Their connection to the overall proof could be made more explicit.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with potential impact on a wide range of applications.
* The technical contributions, particularly the error bound, are substantial.
* The empirical validation supports the theoretical claims.
* The paper is well-written and clearly positioned within the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The proof is complex and heavily relies on supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess its correctness.
* The practical implications of the assumptions are not fully explored.
* The numerical experiments could be more extensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and has the potential to be influential. The linear convergence result is a significant advance, and the technical innovations are noteworthy. However, the complexity of the proof and the lack of detailed discussion on practical considerations raise some concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend acceptance contingent upon the following revisions:

1. **Provide a more detailed proof sketch in the main body**, including key steps of Lemma 4.1, to allow for easier verification of the results.
2. **Discuss the practical implications of Assumption 2.1(b)** and address potential scenarios where it might not hold, potentially suggesting ways to relax the assumption.
3. **Expand the numerical experiments** to include an analysis of the step size and the impact of the measurement operator.
4. **Discuss the constant factor associated with the linear convergence rate**.
5. **Streamline Propositions 4.2 and 4.3** to improve readability and highlight their key role in the proof.
