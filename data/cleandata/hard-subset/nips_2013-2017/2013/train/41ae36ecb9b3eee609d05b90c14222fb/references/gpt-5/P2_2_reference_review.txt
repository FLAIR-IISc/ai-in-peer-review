Summary and context
The paper studies the convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for nuclear-norm regularized convex problems of the form minX f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is a linear operator, and h is smooth with Lipschitz gradient and strictly convex (more precisely, strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain). The central technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal solution set to the norm of a fixed-point residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Leveraging this error bound and the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors establish asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (R-linear in iterates, Q-linear in objective) under simple step-size conditions. The crux is a spectral-structure argument showing that optimal solutions X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors; using perturbation analysis and a carefully constructed restricted set Xc aligned with these singular vectors, they prove a “restricted invertibility” inequality that drives the error bound. Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification show linear slopes in semi-log plots near the solution.

Relation to prior work: PGM is known to be sublinearly convergent in general convex settings and linearly convergent under strong convexity of f. Error-bound based linear rates exist for polyhedral nonsmooth parts such as ℓ1 or group LASSO; the nuclear norm is non-polyhedral, and analogous bounds were open. The paper connects to Luo–Tseng (error bounds and FDM analysis) and to proximal methods for nuclear norm minimization (e.g., FISTA variants). The result complements those by providing linear rates without global strong convexity, under a structured f = h∘A. It would be helpful to discuss connections to KL-based convergence analysis (which can also yield local linear rates for semi-algebraic objectives), but the present approach gives a specific, verifiable error bound.

Quality (soundness, completeness)
- The main theorems are well stated. The error bound is local (on a bounded level set and for sufficiently small residual), which is sufficient for asymptotic linear convergence.
- The proof strategy is logically coherent: show asymptotic optimality and boundedness, then establish the restricted invertibility lemma via spectral alignment (Proposition 4.2) and a contradiction argument using projections onto Xc and a perturbation Q̄ in ker(A). This addresses the key difficulty arising from the non-polyhedral nature of the nuclear norm.
- The convergence theorem uses standard descent and residual relations (inequalities (9)-(11)), with details deferred to the supplement. This is acceptable, but the main text should clarify whether these constants persist under line search or variable step sizes (beyond the stated α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf).
- Experiments are small-scale and illustrative; they validate the linear regime but do not stress-test the theory or compare with alternatives.

Potential technical concerns/clarifications:
- The step-size assumption (uniformly bounded below and above by < 1/Lf) is stronger than typical backtracking line search guarantees; the matrix classification experiment uses backtracking but the theory does not show a uniform lower bound on αk. A brief argument or assumption for line search satisfying αk ≥ α > 0 would strengthen the practical relevance.
- The restricted invertibility inequality ‖X − X̄‖F ≤ κ‖A(X − X̄)‖2 cannot hold globally if A has a nontrivial kernel; the proof correctly shows it holds along sequences near X with the right spectral structure. It would help to emphasize the local nature and region of attraction, and to discuss how κ depends on problem data.
- Several key steps (e.g., Proposition 4.3 and the derivation of (9)-(11)) are in the supplement; ensuring those proofs are complete and self-contained is important for reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is overall clearly written and well organized, with a good motivation and precise definitions (prox operator via spectral shrinkage, residual map).
- The spectral alignment insight (Proposition 4.2) and the role of Xc are well explained, which is crucial to understand why the non-polyhedral case can be handled.
- Minor presentation issues: references are incomplete (years/pages missing in many entries); figure numbering appears inconsistent (Figure 1 vs Figure 2 labeling for matrix completion). It would be helpful to more explicitly state that the linear rate is asymptotic/local and to summarize the assumptions needed for Theorem 3.2 right after the theorem (including step-size policy).

Originality
- Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization and deriving linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f is novel and addresses a recognized gap compared to ℓ1/group LASSO. The spectral-structure based argument to circumvent non-polyhedrality is a nice idea.
- The work differs from prior linear-rate results that require strong convexity or restricted strong convexity; it provides a different pathway via error bounds for structured h∘A. A brief discussion comparing to general KL-based analyses would further situate the contribution.

Significance
- The result is theoretically significant: PGM is ubiquitous for nuclear-norm regularized problems, and linear convergence under realistic assumptions can inform algorithm design and stopping rules. The error bound itself may be useful beyond PGM (e.g., for coordinate or stochastic variants).
- Practical impact is moderated by its asymptotic/local nature and unknown constants; nonetheless, the assumptions cover key losses (square, logistic) and arbitrary A, which is broad.

Pros
- Resolves an open question on error bounds for nuclear norm regularization and yields linear convergence without strong convexity.
- Clean, general setting f = h∘A encompassing common losses.
- Clear high-level proof strategy with a key spectral insight.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Cons
- Linear rate is local/asymptotic; constants and region of attraction are not characterized.
- Step-size condition excludes standard backtracking unless a uniform lower bound is proved.
- Some technical details deferred; references and figures need polishing.
- Experiments are minimal and small-scale; no comparison to accelerated PGM/ADMM or discussion of inexact SVDs commonly used in practice.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the applicability to line search by proving or assuming αk ≥ α > 0 under the adopted backtracking.
- Discuss relation to KL framework and how the proposed error bound refines or complements it.
- Provide guidance on detecting entry into the linear regime (e.g., residual thresholds) and comment on constants qualitatively.
- Expand experiments: larger-scale problems, comparisons to accelerated PGM, ADMM, and with inexact/partial SVDs.
- Fix references (complete bibliographic data) and figure labeling.

Recommendation
Lean toward accept for a theory-oriented NeurIPS audience. The paper makes a solid and novel theoretical contribution that fills a meaningful gap, with a clear and rigorous (though technical) analysis. Strengthening the discussion of step-size policies and polishing the presentation would further improve the paper.
