

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper claims to establish linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors introduce a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which they argue is the first such result for this problem. The key technical contribution is the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which they use to establish linear convergence of PGM under certain conditions on the loss function.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions and a logical flow from problem setup to theoretical results. The authors provide a detailed proof of their error bound, which is technically sound. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice, which is consistent with their theoretical claims.

The main strength of the paper is its technical contribution: establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which the authors claim has been an open question. This result is indeed interesting as it extends the applicability of linear convergence guarantees for PGM beyond strongly convex settings. The proof technique using matrix perturbation theory to handle the spectral structure of low-rank matrices is novel and well-executed.

However, I have significant concerns about the novelty and practical significance of the contribution. The paper claims this is the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity of f. However, the authors have missed several key papers that have addressed similar convergence properties for related problems. For instance, Zhang et al. (J. Oper. Res. Soc. China, 2013) established linear convergence for a similar class of problems using different techniques, and their work was not cited. More importantly, the paper fails to establish how this result differs from or improves upon existing work on matrix completion and low-rank matrix recovery, which commonly use trace norm regularization.

The experimental validation is also insufficiently thorough. The numerical experiments are limited to two problems (matrix completion and matrix classification) with only a few parameter settings. The paper doesn't compare against state-of-the-art methods for these problems that might already achieve similar convergence rates. The step size selection (fixed α=1 for matrix completion and backtracking line search for matrix classification) doesn't provide insight into how practical the method would be for larger-scale problems.

Moreover, the paper's claim that their result "covers a wide range of loss functions" is overstated. The authors assume f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, which excludes many loss functions commonly used in practice. For example, the hinge loss used in support vector machines would not satisfy their assumptions, yet it's a standard loss for matrix classification problems.

The paper's significance is also questionable. While linear convergence is theoretically desirable, the paper doesn't demonstrate that this result leads to substantially better performance in practice compared to existing methods. The numerical experiments show convergence behavior consistent with linear convergence, but they don't compare against other methods that might converge at similar rates. Without evidence that this theoretical result translates to improved practical performance, the significance of the contribution is limited.

In summary, while the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theory of trace norm regularization, it falls short in demonstrating novelty relative to existing literature and practical significance. The experimental validation is insufficient, and the claims about the generality of the result are overstated.

**Strengths:**
- Clear and well-structured presentation of technical results
- Rigorous theoretical proof of the error bound
- Numerical experiments consistent with theoretical claims
- Good understanding of relevant background literature (though incomplete)

**Weaknesses:**
- Overstated novelty claims (missed key related work)
- Insufficient experimental validation (limited to two problems with few parameter settings)
- Overstated generality of the result (assumptions exclude common loss functions)
- Lack of comparison against existing methods that might achieve similar convergence rates
- Limited discussion of practical implications

**Recommendation:** Reject.

The paper makes a technical contribution that is interesting but not sufficiently novel or significant to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors have missed important related work, the experimental validation is insufficient, and the claims about the practical impact of the theoretical results are not adequately supported. For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it should demonstrate clear advancement over existing methods with substantial experimental evidence, which this paper does not provide.
