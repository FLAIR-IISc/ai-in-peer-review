- Problem studied: proximal gradient methods for nuclear-norm regularized problems min_X f(X)+τ||X||_* with f(X)=h(A(X)), h differentiable with Lipschitz gradient and local strong convexity on compact sets.  
- Main contribution: a local Lipschitzian error bound dist(X,X*) ≤ η ||S_τ(X−∇f(X))−X|| on bounded level sets with small residual, leading to asymptotic linear convergence of PGM.  
- Convergence result: Q-linear decrease of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates for step sizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/L_f.  
- Core technical idea: exploit spectral alignment at optimality (simultaneous SVD of X* and −∇f(X*)) and a restricted invertibility argument to handle non-polyhedral nuclear norm.  
- Applicability: covers squared and logistic losses via the h∘A structure; synthetic experiments (matrix completion, classification) show linear convergence near optimum.  
- Relation to prior work: extends Luo–Tseng error-bound framework to nuclear norm, distinct from RSC/RIP-based low-rank analyses.  
- Missing contextualization: should better situate relative to KL/partial smoothness/active manifold identification literature.  
- Strength — novelty: first (to reviewer’s knowledge) Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm, addressing non-polyhedral difficulty.  
- Strength — mechanism: simultaneous SVD/shrinkage fixed-point insight is central and elegant.  
- Strength — framework: clean integration with Luo–Tseng yields standard-step-size linear rates.  
- Strength — numerics: experiments align with theoretical predictions of linear phase.  
- Weakness — locality: error bound and rates are asymptotic; paper should emphasize “asymptotic” earlier and characterize the neighborhood where linearity holds.  
- Weakness — step-size assumptions: requiring α_k≥α>0 is nontrivial under backtracking; need explicit conditions guaranteeing this (e.g., bounded level sets).  
- Weakness — restricted invertibility: Lemma 4.1c is strong in presence of null(A); needs geometric interpretation and clarification of κ’s dependence on conditioning/spectral gaps.  
- Weakness — partial smoothness: the alignment suggests active manifold identification; referencing Lewis/Vaiter et al. could simplify exposition and strengthen generality.  
- Weakness — scope of h: the “strongly convex on compact subsets” assumption is unusual; provide examples (e.g., logistic loss with bounded A(X)) to reassure readers.  
- Weakness — related work: add discussion of KL-based linear convergence (Attouch–Bolte–Svaiter, etc.) and RSC-based low-rank rates.  
- Weakness — experiments: synthetic-only and minimal; add a real dataset or baseline comparisons (ALM/ADMM, SVT, Frank–Wolfe) and report identified ranks/subspace stability.  
- Quality: technically sound and original; some key inequalities/constants are deferred to supplement—include succinct sketches for main bounds to improve readability.  
- Clarity: generally well written but Propositions 4.2–4.3 are dense; add higher-level geometric interpretation and a schematic case analysis; fix minor typos (e.g., prox definition).  
- Significance: high theoretical significance for non-polyhedral regularizers and practical justification for observed linear phases in low-rank learning.  
- Pros for acceptance: new important theoretical result, clean linear guarantees beyond strong convexity, methodology extensible to other spectral norms.  
- Cons for acceptance: asymptotic nature, limited empirical evaluation, and incomplete integration with related KL/partial-smoothness/RSC literature.  
- Suggestions: explicitly state asymptotic nature and neighborhood conditions, clarify step-size/backtracking assumptions, link to partial smoothness/identification and KL theory, expand experiments, fix typos, and sketch deferred inequalities.  
- Recommendation: Accept (borderline-strong).
