- Paper studies when proximal gradient method achieves linear convergence for trace-norm regularization without strong convexity, proving a Lipschitzian error bound for losses f(X)=h(A(X)).

- Main theoretical contribution: a novel error bound for trace norm regularization that resolves an open question about non-polyhedral epigraphs.

- Key technical insight: Proposition 4.2 shows optimal X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors, crucial for the analysis.

- Technical approach is sound, using matrix perturbation theory; Lemma 4.1(c) (restricted invertibility) is a notable technical achievement.

- Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification empirically support linear convergence claims.

- Concern: assumption f(X)=h(A(X)) is restrictive (covers square/logistic loss but excludes losses like hinge); authors should discuss limits and extensibility.

- Concern: Theorem 3.1’s error bound uses F(X) ≤ ζ but ζ’s relation to problem parameters and the constant η is unclear and needs explicit characterization.

- Concern: Related work is underdeveloped; should better contextualize against recent non-strongly convex optimization results (e.g., Zhang et al. 2013, Tseng).

- Concern: Practical relevance is unclear — asymptotic linear convergence is shown but constants and the residual threshold where linear behavior begins are not quantified.

- Concern: Proof of Lemma 4.1(c) is technically dense and would benefit from a more intuitive explanation, especially the role of Proposition 4.3’s decomposition.

- Significance: the error bound is an important advance for matrix optimization and applies to problems (like matrix completion) where strong convexity fails.

- Originality: adaptation of established error-bound frameworks to the trace norm is valuable, though the high-level approach follows prior work; comparison to ℓ1-norm bounds should be clarified.

- Recommendation: accept, conditioned on clarifying ζ vs problem parameters, discussing the f(X)=h(A(X)) limitation, expanding related work, and addressing practical implications of the convergence constants.
