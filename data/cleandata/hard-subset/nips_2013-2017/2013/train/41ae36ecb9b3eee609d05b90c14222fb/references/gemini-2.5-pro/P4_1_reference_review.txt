This paper studies the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications such as matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard convergence theory for PGM establishes a sublinear rate for general smooth convex loss functions and a linear rate if the loss function is strongly convex. However, many practical loss functions, like the square loss in matrix completion, are not strongly convex. The main contribution of this paper is to establish a linear rate of convergence for PGM under a weaker condition. Specifically, it considers loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a smooth, strictly convex function that is strongly convex on any compact set. This class of functions is not necessarily strongly convex, yet the authors prove that PGM achieves an R-linear convergence rate for the iterates and a Q-linear rate for the objective values.

The core of the analysis is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound states that for points sufficiently close to the optimal set, the distance to the optimal set is bounded by a constant multiple of the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Such error bounds are known to imply linear convergence for descent methods, following the framework of Luo and Tseng (1993). Previous work had established similar error bounds for problems with l1-norm or group LASSO regularization, often relying on properties like polyhedrality or separability of the regularizer. The trace norm lacks these properties, making the analysis more challenging. The authors develop a new proof technique based on matrix perturbation theory. A key insight is the observation that for an optimal solution `X*`, the matrices `X*` and `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This structural property allows for a detailed analysis of the spectral properties of iterates near the optimum, which is crucial for establishing the error bound. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems, which demonstrate the predicted linear convergence behavior.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization for machine learning: under what conditions do first-order methods converge linearly? By moving beyond the standard strong convexity assumption for an important class of non-smooth problems, the work significantly broadens our theoretical understanding of PGM. The result is directly applicable to popular models like matrix completion with square loss, providing a formal justification for the fast convergence often observed in practice.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The primary contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1), appears to be a new and non-trivial result that resolves what the authors frame as an open question. The proof technique is particularly novel. It cleverly sidesteps the difficulties associated with the non-polyhedral and non-separable nature of the trace norm by exploiting a specific structural property of the optimal solution set (Proposition 4.2) and leveraging matrix perturbation analysis. This approach is a departure from prior techniques used for l1-norm related problems and could potentially be extended to other matrix norm regularization settings.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the arguments are rigorous. The analysis is built upon an established framework (Luo and Tseng), but the central, novel component—the proof of the error bound—is developed carefully. The assumptions are clearly stated and justified for common applications. The logical flow of the proof, presented via a series of lemmas and propositions, is coherent.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work in the context of prior literature and highlighting its unique contribution. The main theorems are presented upfront, giving the reader a clear view of the paper's results. The proof sketch for the error bound in Section 4, while dense, successfully outlines the main steps of the argument.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Proof Sketch:** The proof of the error bound is highly technical. While the main paper provides a sketch, crucial steps rely on propositions (e.g., Prop. 4.2 and 4.3) whose proofs are in the supplementary material. In particular, the derivation of the decomposition of `Q̄` in Proposition 4.3 is central to the contradiction argument but lacks intuition in the main text. Briefly explaining *why* the components of `λ` take on different signs or values in the three cases would enhance the reader's understanding of the proof's mechanics without needing to consult the supplement.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, and the underlying error bound is local (i.e., it holds for `‖R(X)‖F ≤ ǫ`). The paper does not provide any characterization of this local region or the constants involved (`η`, `κ`, `ǫ`), which depend on properties of the problem. While a precise characterization is often difficult, a discussion on how these constants might relate to problem parameters (e.g., the operator `A`, the curvature of `h`, the properties of the optimal solution `X*`) would strengthen the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments effectively illustrate the main theoretical claim—that convergence eventually becomes linear. However, they could be slightly more comprehensive. For instance, showing how the convergence behavior or the onset of the linear phase changes with problem parameters like the rank, the regularization parameter `τ`, or the condition number of the problem could provide further insights.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper provides a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity, a common scenario in machine learning.
*   The core technical result, a new Lipschitzian error bound, is novel and resolves an open question in the analysis of first-order methods.
*   The proof technique, based on matrix perturbation theory and spectral properties of the solution, is innovative and could be of independent interest.
*   The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and places its contributions appropriately within the literature.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The technical proof sketch in the main paper is very dense and may be difficult to fully grasp without the supplementary material, particularly concerning the key Proposition 4.3.
*   The practical implications of the asymptotic and local nature of the main results could be discussed in more detail.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a significant, novel, and technically sound theoretical result. The strengths, particularly the originality and importance of the contribution, substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work advances the state of the art in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for a widely used class of optimization problems.
