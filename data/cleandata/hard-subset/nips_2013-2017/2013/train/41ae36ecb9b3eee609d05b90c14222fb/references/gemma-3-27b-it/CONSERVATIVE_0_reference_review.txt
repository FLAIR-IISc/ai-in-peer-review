This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularization, a common technique for low-rank matrix recovery. The authors aim to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, a significant departure from existing results. They achieve this by deriving a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of solutions near the optimum. The problem setting and potential applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning) are well-motivated.

The paper's strength lies in tackling a relevant and challenging theoretical question. The claim of extending linear convergence guarantees to a broader class of loss functions is potentially impactful, given the prevalence of non-strongly convex losses in practical applications. The technical approach, utilizing a new error bound and matrix perturbation theory, appears sound, and the connection to Luo and Tseng's framework is appropriately made. The inclusion of numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence for matrix completion and classification provides some empirical support for the theoretical findings.

However, several weaknesses raise concerns. First, while the authors acknowledge the difficulty of establishing the error bound, the technical details of the proof (particularly Lemma 4.1 and its supporting propositions) are extremely dense and lack sufficient justification for several crucial steps. The reliance on Proposition 4.2, stating the alignment of left and right singular vectors for optimal solutions and their gradients, feels like a strong assumption that deserves more careful consideration and discussion. It's not immediately clear *why* this property holds in general, and a more detailed explanation or reference would be beneficial. The proof sketch in the main text largely refers the reader to the supplementary material, making it difficult to assess the rigor of the complete argument without significant effort.

Second, the numerical experiments, while present, are somewhat limited in scope. The step size is fixed in the matrix completion experiment and employs a backtracking line search in the classification experiment. A more thorough exploration of different step size strategies and parameter settings would strengthen the empirical validation. The plots showing linear convergence are presented without clear baselines for comparison, making it difficult to gauge the practical performance gain achieved by the proposed analysis. What is the convergence rate achieved with standard PGM implementations without this improved analysis?

Third, the writing style is occasionally convoluted and imprecise. The numerous equations and the density of technical details can make the paper difficult to follow. While rigor is appreciated, clarity should not be sacrificed.

Finally, while the paper builds upon existing PGM analysis, the novelty of the core technical contributions feels incremental. The extension to non-strongly convex losses is valuable, but the reliance on specific structural properties of the problem (particularly the alignment of singular vectors) limits the generality of the results. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important theoretical question regarding the convergence of PGM.
*   Presents a novel error bound for trace norm regularization.
*   Provides some empirical evidence supporting the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The technical proof is dense and lacks sufficient detail/justification in the main text.
*   The key assumption regarding singular vector alignment requires more explanation.
*   The numerical experiments are limited in scope and lack strong baselines.
*   The novelty of the core technical contributions feels incremental.
*   Clarity of writing can be improved.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **reject** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. The paper tackles a worthwhile problem, but the technical presentation needs significant improvement to convince the community of the correctness and significance of the results. The authors must provide a more detailed and accessible explanation of the key technical steps, particularly the justification for Proposition 4.2. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including comparisons to standard PGM implementations and a sensitivity analysis of key parameters, is also required.  The paper, as it stands, does not meet the high bar for originality and technical soundness expected at NIPS.
