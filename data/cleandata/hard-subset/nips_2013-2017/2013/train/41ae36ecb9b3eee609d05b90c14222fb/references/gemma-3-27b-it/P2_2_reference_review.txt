## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) for a linear operator A and a strictly convex function h.  The authors establish a linear convergence rate for the PGM under relatively mild assumptions on h, crucially *without* requiring strong convexity of the loss function f itself. This is a significant contribution, as many practical applications (e.g., matrix completion) involve loss functions that are not strongly convex. The key to their analysis is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems, achieved by leveraging matrix perturbation theory and recognizing a specific structural property of optimal solutions.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental question about the convergence of a widely used algorithm (PGM) for a common regularization technique (trace norm) in a broad range of applications.
* **Novelty:**  The linear convergence result under the given conditions (specifically, without strong convexity of *f*) is a genuine advance over existing literature. Prior works typically relied on strong convexity, which limits their applicability.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous and builds upon well-established techniques (Luo-Tseng framework). The core idea of establishing a new error bound through careful matrix analysis is clever.  The connection to matrix perturbation theory is insightful.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The introduction clearly motivates the problem and positions the contribution.  The assumptions are stated explicitly. The logical flow of the proof outline is easy to follow.
* **Complementary Experiments:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide empirical support for the theoretical results, demonstrating linear convergence in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density of Technical Detail:** While the core ideas are presented well, the paper is quite dense with mathematical notation and technicalities.  This makes it challenging for a reader not deeply familiar with the field to fully grasp all the details.  More intuitive explanations and intermediate steps in the proofs would be helpful.
* **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** While the assumptions are stated, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of their practical implications and limitations. For example, how sensitive are the results to the Lipschitz constant of the gradient of *h*?
* **Experimental Setup Could Be More Thorough:** The experimental section, while helpful, lacks detail.  For example, the choice of step size (backtracking line search in the classification experiment) is mentioned, but not fully justified or compared with fixed step sizes.  More rigorous comparisons and statistical analysis would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Reference to Similar Results:** The paper mentions related work but doesn’t explicitly discuss the differences in assumptions and results with, for instance, work on accelerated proximal gradient methods. 

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the PGM for trace norm regularization. The authors address an important problem, present a novel solution, and support their claims with rigorous analysis and experiments.  While the paper could benefit from greater clarity and more detailed discussion of assumptions and experimental details, these are relatively minor weaknesses.  



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

* Novel and significant theoretical result: Linear convergence without strong convexity.
* Technically sound and well-motivated.
* Empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.
* Addresses a problem relevant to a broad range of applications.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

* Density of technical detail might hinder accessibility.
* Limited discussion of the practical implications of assumptions.
* Experimental setup could be more thorough.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Excellent. The paper is technically sound, the claims are well-supported, and it represents a complete piece of work.
* **Clarity:** Good. While the notation is dense, the overall structure and logical flow are clear. Improvements in readability would be beneficial.
* **Originality:** Excellent. The result is novel and distinguishes itself from existing work.
* **Significance:** Excellent. The result is likely to be influential in the field of optimization and machine learning, particularly for applications involving trace norm regularization.



**Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This paper meets the criteria for acceptance to NIPS and represents a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend it for publication.
