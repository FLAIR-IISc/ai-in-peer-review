- Establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularization without requiring strong convexity by proving a Lipschitzian error bound via spectral/matrix-perturbation analysis.

- Theoretical contribution: first Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, enabling Luo–Tseng framework to prove linear convergence.

- Technical innovation: shows optimal solutions and −∇f share singular vectors (Prop. 4.2) and uses matrix perturbation theory to analyze spectral structure (Lemma 4.1(c)).

- Applicability: framework covers matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification without needing strong convexity.

- Rigor and completeness: careful definitions, clear assumptions, full proofs (with supplements), and experiments that corroborate linear convergence.

- Restrictive assumption: requiring f(X)=h(A(X)) is specific and may exclude many losses; extensions (e.g., adding another convex g) are not discussed.

- Limited numerical experiments: only show asymptotic linear convergence, lack comparisons to alternatives, and do not analyze dependence on rank, dimension, or sampling ratio.

- Clarity issue: insufficient intuitive explanation why ℓ1-norm error bound techniques fail for the trace norm beyond a brief remark about singular-value dependence.

- Related-work positioning: could better compare to prior and recent work on nuclear-norm optimization (e.g., Toh & Yun 2010; Zhang et al. 2013).

- Overall assessment: substantial, novel, and well-supported theoretical advance that resolves an important open question.

- Recommendation: accept—theoretical merits outweigh the noted limitations.

- Future work: investigate extending the error bound to other matrix norms or to problems where f is not of the form h(A(X)).
