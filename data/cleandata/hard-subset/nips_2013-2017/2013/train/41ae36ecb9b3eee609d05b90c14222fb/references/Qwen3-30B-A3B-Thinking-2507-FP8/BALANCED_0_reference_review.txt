

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper studies the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ||X||_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for this problem, which they use to prove linear convergence of PGM without requiring f to be strongly convex (a common assumption in prior work). This resolves an open question regarding whether linear convergence could be established for a broader class of loss functions than those requiring strong convexity.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Quality**: The paper provides a rigorous proof of the key error bound (Theorem 3.1), which is the main contribution. The proof is well-structured and addresses a gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that prior error bounds for composite minimization typically apply only to polyhedral regularizers like ℓ1-norm, not to trace norm.

2. **Theoretical Contribution**: The authors successfully extend linear convergence results to a broader class of problems than previously known. Their result covers important applications like matrix completion (with square loss) and multi-task learning (with logistic loss), as demonstrated in the paper.

3. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the key concepts (optimal solution set properties, residual map, error bound) are well-defined. The numerical experiments complement the theoretical results.

4. **Significance**: The results are significant for both theory and practice. The error bound itself could be of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization, and the linear convergence result provides a stronger theoretical guarantee than the standard O(1/k) rate for non-strongly convex cases.

## Weaknesses

1. **Restrictive Assumption**: The paper's key assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) may be too restrictive for some applications. The authors don't adequately discuss how common this form is in practice or provide examples where it doesn't hold. This limits the applicability of their result.

2. **Lack of Comparative Analysis**: The paper doesn't compare the linear convergence rate with the O(1/k) rate from prior work in terms of practical computational savings. A discussion of how much faster linear convergence is than sublinear would strengthen the significance argument.

3. **Limited Numerical Experiments**: The experiments are limited to two specific problems (matrix completion and matrix classification) and don't compare with other algorithms or provide detailed convergence rate measurements. More comprehensive experiments would better support the theoretical claims.

4. **Missing Discussion of Parameter Dependence**: The error bound constants (η and ε in Theorem 3.1) are not discussed in terms of their dependence on problem parameters. This would be valuable for practitioners to understand how the bound scales with problem size.

5. **Comparison with Related Work**: While the paper correctly notes the novelty of their error bound, it could better address the related work by Zhang et al. (2013) which also studies linear convergence for trace norm problems under different assumptions (though their result applies to a different class of problems).

## Overall Assessment

This is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of convergence rates for trace norm-regularized optimization. The main result fills an important gap in the literature, as the authors correctly identify that linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions was previously an open question.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1) A more thorough discussion of the practical applicability of the f(X) = h(A(X)) assumption
2) Additional numerical experiments comparing the convergence rate to sublinear methods
3) A clearer explanation of the practical implications of linear convergence
4) A more detailed discussion of the error bound constants and their dependence on problem parameters

While the paper is well-written and makes a solid theoretical contribution, the restrictive assumption and limited experimental validation somewhat limit the impact of the results. The authors should address these points in a revision to strengthen the paper's significance for the NeurIPS community.

## Decision Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a novel and technically sound contribution to the field of convex optimization for machine learning. With the suggested revisions addressing the limitations noted above, this paper would be a strong contribution to NeurIPS. The main weakness is the restrictive assumption about the form of f, which should be better justified or discussed. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important open question in the field.
