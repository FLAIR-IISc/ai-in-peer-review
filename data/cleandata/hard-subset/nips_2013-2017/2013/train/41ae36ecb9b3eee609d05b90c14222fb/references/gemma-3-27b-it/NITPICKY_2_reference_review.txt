This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization, a common technique for low-rank matrix recovery. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under conditions where the loss function is not strongly convex, a significant improvement over existing results. They achieve this by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to overcome challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The paper is well-motivated, connecting to a broad range of applications and clearly outlining the limitations of prior work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result for non-strongly convex loss functions is a substantial contribution. Existing literature typically requires strong convexity for achieving linear convergence with PGM.
*   **Technical Depth:** The proof of the error bound appears rigorous and cleverly utilizes matrix perturbation theory to navigate the complexities of the trace norm.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The framework encompasses many real-world problems, including matrix completion and multi-task learning, as explicitly highlighted in the introduction.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and summarizes contributions.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments, although relatively simple, support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical performance of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Density:** The paper relies heavily on mathematical notation, which may hinder readability for readers unfamiliar with the field. While mathematically precise, some sections could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
*   **Assumption 2.1(b):** The assumption that *h* is strongly convex on *any* convex compact subset of dom(*h*) is surprisingly strong. While justified by the applications mentioned, its generality seems questionable and might unnecessarily restrict the applicability of the result. The authors should explicitly discuss the implications of relaxing this assumption.
*   **Lack of Discussion on Step Size Selection:** The theoretical analysis assumes a specific range for the step size αk. However, the practical choice of αk is often crucial for convergence. The paper briefly mentions a backtracking line search in the experiments, but a more detailed discussion of step size selection strategies within the theoretical framework would be valuable.
*   **Limited Experiment Scope:** The numerical experiments are limited to two applications and relatively small problem sizes. A more comprehensive evaluation, including comparisons with state-of-the-art solvers and scalability analysis, would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Minor Formatting Issues:** There are several minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper, such as inconsistent spacing and citation style. Specifically, the reference list is not formatted according to standard NIPS guidelines.
*   **Proposition 4.2 & 4.3 - missing proof:** These are critical parts of the argument but the paper refers to the supplementary material. A complete paper should include the proof for key propositions.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   In Lemma 4.1(c), the authors establish a restricted invertibility property. What are the implications of this property for practical applications? Can it be used to guide feature selection or parameter tuning?
*   The proof of Theorem 3.1 relies on a contradiction argument. Is it possible to derive a constructive error bound that directly relates the distance to the optimal solution set to the residual norm?
*   How sensitive is the linear convergence rate to the choice of the regularization parameter τ? A discussion of the impact of τ on the convergence rate would be helpful.
*   The discussion on the limitations of the splitting approach used in prior work regarding the ℓ1-norm is valid, but could be expanded. Specifically, explain why *each* entry of the matrix depends on *every* singular value more explicitly.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical result with broad implications.
*   The technical analysis is rigorous and innovative.
*   The experimental results provide initial support for the theoretical claims.

**Against:**

*   The strong assumption on the strong convexity of *h* and the lack of a discussion on step size selection are notable weaknesses.
*   The limited scope of the numerical experiments and several minor formatting inconsistencies detract from the overall quality of the presentation.
*   The exclusion of the proofs for Propositions 4.2 and 4.3 detracts from a complete reading of the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite its strengths, I am inclined to **recommend rejection** due to the combination of the strong assumption on *h*, the limited experimental evaluation, the formatting issues and the missing proofs. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, these shortcomings prevent the paper from meeting the high standards for acceptance at NIPS. A significantly revised version, addressing these concerns, could be reconsidered. The paper shows promise, but requires further refinement before it is suitable for publication.
