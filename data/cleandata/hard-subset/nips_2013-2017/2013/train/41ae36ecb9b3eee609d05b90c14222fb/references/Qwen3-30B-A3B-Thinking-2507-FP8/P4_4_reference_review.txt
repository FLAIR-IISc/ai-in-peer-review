

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ||X||_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties. The authors prove a novel Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1) that relates the distance to the optimal solution set with the residual norm of the PGM. This error bound is the key technical contribution, as it resolves an open question regarding whether such bounds exist for trace norm regularization (unlike ℓ1-norm, which has a polyhedral epigraph). Previous linear convergence results for trace norm regularization required strong convexity of f [16], which doesn't hold in many applications like matrix completion. The paper demonstrates that the PGM achieves linear convergence under weaker conditions than previously known.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs. The authors establish a new error bound that is carefully developed and proven through Lemma 4.1 and Proposition 4.2, which is a significant technical contribution. The numerical experiments in Section 5 complement the theoretical results by showing linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and matrix classification problems. The paper properly acknowledges limitations (e.g., the requirement that f takes the specific form f(X) = h(A(X))) and provides clear examples where these assumptions hold (e.g., square loss in matrix completion). The proofs are well-structured and the mathematical arguments are sound, though the proof of Lemma 4.1(c) is quite technical.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and identifies the gap in the literature. The paper carefully defines all necessary mathematical concepts and notation, making it accessible to readers familiar with convex optimization. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are clearly stated and proven. The numerical experiments are well-described with appropriate metrics. Some of the technical details in Section 4 may be challenging for non-specialists, but the paper provides sufficient context for readers to follow the main arguments.

**Originality**: This work makes a significant contribution by establishing the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f. The authors correctly identify that prior work on error bounds applies to ℓ1-norm regularization [22, 23, 25] but not to trace norm, which has a non-polyhedral epigraph [18]. The key insight that optimal solutions X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors (Proposition 4.2) is novel and enables the authors to overcome technical challenges. This work clearly differs from previous NIPS papers on trace norm regularization [6, 9, 19, 20] by establishing a linear convergence rate under weaker conditions.

**Significance**: The results have direct implications for machine learning applications involving trace norm regularization, including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. The linear convergence result provides valuable theoretical guarantees for practitioners, as it explains why PGM often performs well in practice for these problems. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is potentially of independent interest for future research on matrix norm regularization. The numerical experiments demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical findings.

**Strengths**:
- Establishes the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f.
- Provides a novel Lipschitzian error bound that resolves an open question in the literature.
- Careful theoretical development with rigorous proofs.
- Complemented by numerical experiments showing linear convergence in practice.
- Clear comparison with related work and proper identification of the technical gap.

**Weaknesses**:
- The assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex may exclude some interesting applications.
- The paper does not discuss how to efficiently compute the Lipschitz constant Lf for the specific loss functions considered (e.g., logistic loss in matrix classification).
- The numerical experiments, while supportive, are limited to two specific applications.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance**:
- Pro: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that addresses a clear gap in the literature, with rigorous proofs and practical validation. The results have direct relevance to many machine learning applications.
- Con: The scope of the results is limited to a specific class of loss functions (f(X) = h(A(X))), though this class covers many common applications.

Overall, this paper makes a high-quality theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization with clear significance for machine learning. The results are technically sound, well-justified, and properly contextualized within the existing literature. The paper meets all four criteria for a strong NIPS submission.
