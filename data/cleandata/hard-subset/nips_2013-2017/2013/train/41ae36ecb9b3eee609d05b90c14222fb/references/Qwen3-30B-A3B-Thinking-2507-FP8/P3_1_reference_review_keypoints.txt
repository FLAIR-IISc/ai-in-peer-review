- Main contribution: establishes the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization and uses it to prove linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) without strong convexity.

- Problem scope: focuses on loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth; this assumption enables the main results.

- Novel technical insight: optimal solutions and their negative gradients share singular vectors, allowing use of matrix perturbation theory to derive the error bound.

- Novelty vs prior work: advances beyond prior error bounds that required polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ₁-norm) which do not apply to trace norm.

- Practical relevance: results apply to common matrix learning tasks (matrix completion, multi-task learning) where the loss often has the assumed form.

- Theoretical rigor: proofs are mathematically careful and well-motivated, with appropriate literature context.

- Empirical evidence: provides numerical experiments showing linear convergence in matrix completion and matrix classification settings.

- Limited experiments: validation is restricted to two problems and lacks broader testing across problem structures, parameter settings, or competing methods.

- Restrictive assumption: the f(X)=h(A(X)) form may be too narrow and the paper doesn't fully justify its generality across applications.

- Computational practicality omitted: no discussion of proximal operator cost (e.g., SVD shrinkage complexity) or how linear rates translate to runtime for large matrices.

- Comparison gaps: could better compare to work on Schatten p-norms and further discuss how non-polyhedral trace norm affects convergence relative to other matrix norms.

- Overall assessment and recommendation: significant, novel theoretical contribution with minor empirical and scope limitations; reviewer recommends acceptance.
