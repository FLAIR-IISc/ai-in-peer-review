This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning. The central contribution is proving that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate for a broad class of problems where the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is not necessarily strongly convex. This is a significant result, as many important applications, such as matrix completion with a square loss, fall into this category. The analysis hinges on a novel technical result: a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem, which relates the distance to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map.

**Quality:**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous piece of theoretical work. The main claims are well-supported by a detailed proof structure.

The core of the paper is the derivation of a Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1) for the trace norm problem. The authors correctly identify that prior techniques used for `l1`-norm or group LASSO regularization, which rely on separability, do not apply here due to the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. The proposed proof strategy is novel and elegant. The key insight (Proposition 4.2), that an optimal solution `X̄` and the corresponding gradient component `-∇f(X̄)` can be simultaneously singular-value-decomposed, is a powerful observation that unlocks the analysis. This allows the authors to leverage matrix perturbation theory to control the spectral structure of iterates near the optimal set, which is a sophisticated and appropriate tool for this problem.

Once the error bound is established, its application within the Luo and Tseng framework to prove linear convergence (Theorem 3.2) is standard but correctly executed. The assumptions made on the objective function—specifically that `f(X) = h(A(X))` where `h` is strictly convex and smooth—are reasonable and cover a wide range of important loss functions like square loss and logistic loss, as justified by the authors. The authors are careful to state that the linear convergence is asymptotic, which is an honest representation of what the theory proves (the error bound holds locally near the solution set).

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work within the existing literature and articulating the central research question: can PGM converge linearly without strong convexity? The main results are stated upfront in Section 3, followed by the proof sketch, which greatly aids readability. The structure of the proof, while highly technical, is broken down into manageable lemmas and propositions.

However, the proof of the error bound itself (Section 4 and 4.1) is extremely dense. While this is perhaps unavoidable given the topic's complexity, the intuition behind some key steps could be further elaborated. For instance, the decomposition of the direction `Q̄` in Proposition 4.3 is central to the contradiction argument, but its proof is deferred to the supplementary material. A more intuitive explanation of why `Q̄` must have this structure in the main text would strengthen the paper's clarity for a broader audience. Similarly, the final contradiction argument in Section 4.1.2, involving the three cases, is intricate and could benefit from a higher-level summary of the logic.

**Originality:**

The work is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first result establishing a linear convergence rate for the standard PGM on this class of trace norm regularized problems without assuming strong convexity. Previous work either required strong convexity for linear rates or settled for sublinear rates.

The most original component is the proof of the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm. The authors correctly state that this was an open question. Their approach, based on analyzing the shared spectral structure of the optimal solution and its gradient, is a novel technique that overcomes the limitations of methods used for other non-smooth regularizers. This contribution is substantial and of independent interest beyond the specific application to PGM convergence. The paper does an excellent job of referencing related work on error bounds and clearly distinguishing its contribution.

**Significance:**

The results presented in this paper are highly significant for both the optimization and machine learning communities.
1.  **Practical Relevance:** PGM is a workhorse algorithm for many large-scale machine learning problems involving trace norm regularization. This paper provides a much stronger theoretical justification for its excellent empirical performance than was previously available, showing that the fast convergence observed in practice is not an anomaly but a predictable theoretical property.
2.  **Theoretical Advancement:** The Lipschitzian error bound is a fundamental result that deepens our understanding of the geometry of the trace norm regularized objective function near its solution set. This tool could potentially be used to analyze the convergence of other algorithms or to develop new, faster ones.
3.  **Broad Applicability:** The result applies to a wide range of models, including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification, making it immediately relevant to practitioners and researchers in these areas.

**Strengths:**
*   **Major Theoretical Contribution:** Establishes linear convergence of PGM for a broad and practical class of non-strongly convex problems.
*   **Novel Technical Tool:** Introduces and proves a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, resolving an open question.
*   **Elegant Proof Technique:** The use of simultaneous singular value decomposition is a key, insightful step in the proof.
*   **High Clarity and Strong Motivation:** The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and clearly explains the importance of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Proof Density:** The core proof in Section 4 is very technical and could be made more accessible with additional intuition.
*   **Local Nature of Convergence:** The linear convergence is asymptotic. While the theory is sound, a discussion on the size of the region where this linear convergence kicks in would be valuable from a practical standpoint, though likely very difficult to analyze.
*   **Limited Experiments:** The numerical experiments serve as a good proof of concept, visually confirming the theoretical linear convergence. However, they are conducted on relatively small-scale problems (`n=40`). Including experiments on larger, more challenging datasets and comparing the convergence behavior to other state-of-the-art solvers would further strengthen the paper's practical impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of first-order optimization methods in machine learning. It solves an important open problem with a novel and technically impressive proof. Despite the density of the theoretical sections, the paper is well-motivated and the significance of the results is clear. It advances the state of the art and the core ideas are likely to be built upon by future research. I strongly recommend acceptance.
