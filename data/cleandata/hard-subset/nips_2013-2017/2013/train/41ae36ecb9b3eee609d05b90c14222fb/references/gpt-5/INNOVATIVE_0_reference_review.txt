Summary
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for nuclear-norm–regularized problems min_X f(X)+τ||X||_* with f(X)=h(A(X)), where A is linear and h has Lipschitz gradient and is strictly/locally strongly convex on compact convex subsets. The main result is an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM without requiring strong convexity of f itself (A can be rank-deficient), covering square and logistic losses. The key technical contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization: dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||S_τ(X−∇f(X)) − X|| for small residual and within a bounded level set. With this bound, the Luo–Tseng framework yields Q-linear convergence in objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates under standard step-size conditions.

The proof strategy departs from polyhedral arguments used for ℓ1/group-LASSO error bounds. It hinges on the observation that at optimality X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors (from subdifferential structure of the nuclear norm), enabling a spectral alignment argument and a “restricted invertibility” inequality that links ||X−P_X*||_F to ||A(X−P_X*)||_2. Numerical experiments on small-scale matrix completion and matrix classification illustrate linear convergence once near the solution set.

Relation to prior work
- Prior error bounds and linear convergence for composite problems were known for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group LASSO) and more general error-bound/KL frameworks. For nuclear norm, the existence of a comparable local error bound had remained open. This paper fills that gap and shows how it leads to linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f.
- The paper correctly leverages the Luo–Tseng analysis and distinguishes its contribution as establishing the new error bound for the nuclear norm. It would benefit from positioning relative to general KL-based linear-rate results and to works on linear rates under restricted strong convexity/PL-type conditions, clarifying why those do not directly cover the setting with arbitrary linear A and non-strongly convex f.

Strengths
- Novelty: A clear, nontrivial theoretical advance—the local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization—resolving a longstanding gap relative to ℓ1/group LASSO. This is the key enabling step; once established, linear convergence follows from a well-known framework.
- Generality: Applies to widely used losses (square, logistic) via mild assumptions on h (Lipschitz gradient; local strong convexity on bounded sets), and arbitrary linear A, even when f is not strongly convex.
- Technical insight: Elegant use of spectral structure at optimality (simultaneous SVD of X* and −∇f(X*)) and a careful perturbation/splitting argument to construct the restricted invertibility property.
- Clarity of theory: The core statements (error bound, convergence theorem) are clean; the proof roadmap is well organized.

Weaknesses
- Locality and constants: The error bound and resulting linear rate are local (require small residuals and bounded level sets). Constants (η, ε, ρ) are not characterized, limiting practical guidance.
- Empirics: Experiments are small-scale and primarily illustrative; no baselines, no study of robustness (noise, different ranks), no exploration of constants/regions of linearity, and no real datasets. Results still support the theory but are limited.
- Scope of discussion: Limited comparison to KL-based linear convergence results, restricted strong convexity/PL conditions, or inexact PGM analyses; these connections would contextualize the contribution further.
- Minor clarity issues: Some notation inconsistencies (τ vs μ in experiments), a few typos, missing years in references, and reliance on the supplement for key technical lemmas.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you relate your error bound to the KL property (e.g., KL exponent 1/2) for the nuclear norm? Does your analysis imply a KL exponent locally, or vice versa?
- Do your results extend to accelerated PGM with restart (global sublinear, local linear after restart)? Any formal statement possible under your error bound?
- What about inexact proximal mappings (e.g., truncated SVD), which are common in large-scale nuclear-norm problems? Can a perturbed error bound yield linear convergence with controlled inexactness?
- Can you comment on extending the approach to other spectral regularizers (e.g., Schatten p, p∈(1,2), spectral k-support) or to composite norms?
- Provide guidance on the size of the attraction region (in terms of level-set radius or residual threshold), even if conservative.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a genuinely new and broadly relevant theoretical tool (error bound) for nuclear-norm problems.
- Delivers a crisp conceptual message: linear rates for PGM without strong convexity via structure.
- Likely to spur follow-up work (e.g., accelerated/inexact/stochastic variants, other spectral norms).

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical section is minimal; practical implications (constants, robustness, scalability) are underexplored.
- The linear rate is local; the paper could be clearer about the practical radius of linearity.
- Limited comparison to alternative modern theoretical frameworks (KL/PL).

Quality: Strong on theory, proofs appear careful and logically structured; minor polish needed.  
Clarity: Generally clear; could improve exposition around assumptions, constants, and related work.  
Originality: High; the nuclear-norm error bound is new and nontrivial.  
Significance: High for optimization and ML communities using low-rank models.

Recommendation: Accept. The core idea is novel and impactful despite modest experiments; the theoretical contribution stands on its own and opens a useful direction. Confidence: High.
