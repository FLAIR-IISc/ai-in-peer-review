Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for nuclear-norm regularized problems minX f(X)+τ||X||*, focusing on the common, non-strongly convex case f(X)=h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is strictly convex, L-smooth, and strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain. The main technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound that connects distance to the optimal solution set to the residual of the proximal fixed-point map: dist(X, X*) ≤ η||Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X|| for points in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual. This resolves an open question for trace norm regularization, where the epigraph is nonpolyhedral and prior error-bound proofs for l1/group-lasso do not carry over. Leveraging this error bound and the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors prove asymptotic linear convergence (Q-linear in objective, R-linear in iterates) for PGM with step sizes bounded away from 0 and less than 1/Lf. The proof relies on a structural property at optimality: X* and −∇f(X*) share left/right singular vectors, which enables a matrix perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” relation between deviations in X and in A(X). Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification illustrate linear convergence once iterates are sufficiently close to the solution set.

This sits well within the literature on first-order methods for composite convex optimization. Prior results give O(1/k)–O(1/k^2) rates for general convex objectives and linear convergence under strong convexity. Linear convergence via error bounds is known for polyhedral regularizers (l1, group lasso) but not for nuclear norm due to nonpolyhedral geometry. The paper provides the missing error bound and thus extends linear convergence guarantees to a broad class of loss functions used in low-rank learning without requiring strong convexity of f.

Quality:
The technical development appears sound and carefully reasoned. The error bound is the key novelty; the proof strategy is convincing: (i) establish spectral alignment at optimality and describe the optimal set structure; (ii) use perturbation arguments to derive a restricted invertibility inequality linking ||X−projX*(X)||F to ||A(X−projX*(X))||; (iii) combine with smoothness/curvature of h and the optimality conditions to obtain the local error bound; (iv) apply the Luo–Tseng machinery to get linear convergence of PGM. The assumptions on h are clearly stated and cover common losses (square, logistic). The step-size requirement is standard; the backtracking used in experiments is compatible as it typically generates αk ∈ (α, 1/Lf) after finitely many steps under smoothness. Some technical details (e.g., Proposition 4.3) are deferred to the supplement; from what is presented, the logic is coherent.

The experiments are modest but consistent with the theory: they show linear decrease in objective and solution error asymptotically. They do not validate constants or compare methods, but that is acceptable for a theory-focused contribution.

Clarity:
Overall the paper is well organized: problem setup, PGM/residual map, main theorems, and proof sketch are clear. The motivation for why the l1-style decomposition does not carry over to the nuclear norm is helpful. The exposition of the key structural observation (simultaneous SVD at optimum) and how it drives the analysis is a strength, though it could be emphasized earlier. A few issues could be improved: several proofs are in the supplement; the role and dependence of the constants (η, ε, κ) are not discussed; some references are missing years; figure numbering/text mismatch (Figure 1/2). Providing a brief intuitive explanation of the “restricted invertibility” inequality and a discussion of the neighborhood in which the error bound holds would aid readability.

Originality:
High. The Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under the proposed assumptions is, to my knowledge, new and addresses a recognized gap compared with polyhedral regularizers. The use of optimality-induced spectral alignment to circumvent the nonpolyhedral difficulty is insightful. The linear convergence result itself follows a known template (Luo–Tseng), and prior works have established linear rates under error bounds or strong convexity; the novelty here lies in proving the error bound in the nuclear-norm case.

Significance:
The result is theoretically significant for optimization in machine learning: many applications use nuclear-norm regularization with non-strongly convex f (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A, logistic loss). Establishing conditions under which standard PGM enjoys linear convergence strengthens the theoretical foundation and can influence algorithmic choices. While the result is asymptotic and constants are not explicit, it closes a gap in the convergence theory and is likely to be cited and built upon (e.g., for other spectral regularizers or to derive complexity bounds under additional structure).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Asymptotic/local nature: The theorem guarantees linear convergence once sufficiently close to the solution; no explicit characterization of the neighborhood or constants is provided. A discussion of how η and ε depend on A, τ, and spectral properties at the solution would increase practical value.
- Assumptions on h: “Strongly convex on any compact convex subset” is somewhat unusual. Clarify its necessity, and whether weaker conditions (e.g., KL property with exponent 1/2) would suffice; relate to known KL-based linear convergence results for convex semi-algebraic functions.
- Experiments: Add comparisons to FISTA/ADMM and report wall-clock time, residual norms, and possibly an empirical check of the error bound (residual vs. distance to an approximate solution set). Include larger-scale runs.
- Step-size/backtracking: State conditions under which backtracking guarantees αk bounded away from 0 in practice, aligning with Theorem 3.2.
- Minor: Fix reference years and figure labels; provide a concise statement and proof sketch of Proposition 4.2 in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves a nontrivial open problem: error bound and linear convergence for nuclear-norm PGM without strong convexity.
- Technically solid and elegant spectral argument; broadly applicable to common losses and arbitrary A.
- Clear positioning within literature; likely to impact subsequent theoretical and algorithmic work.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Asymptotic result with unspecified constants; limited practical guidance.
- Experiments are minimal and lack comparisons.
- Some technical details relegated to supplement; clarity could be improved in places.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is novel and significant despite modest empirical evaluation and asymptotic nature.
