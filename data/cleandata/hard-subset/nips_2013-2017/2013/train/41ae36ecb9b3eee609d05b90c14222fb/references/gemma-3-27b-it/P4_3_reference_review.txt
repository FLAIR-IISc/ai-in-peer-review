This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A being a linear operator and h being strictly convex. The authors establish linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions on h, without requiring strong convexity of f, which is a significant contribution.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the gap in existing literature regarding linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions. The notation is consistent throughout, and the mathematical derivations appear sound. The authors carefully present their assumptions and the key technical lemma, which forms the basis for their convergence analysis. The inclusion of numerical experiments is a positive aspect, demonstrating the practical applicability of the theoretical results. The related work section is comprehensive, appropriately citing relevant publications. 

The main strength of the paper lies in establishing a new error bound for trace norm regularization and using it to prove linear convergence of the PGM in a broader context than previously known. This result expands the applicability of the PGM to problems where the loss function is not strongly convex, which is common in many machine learning applications (e.g., matrix completion). The careful treatment of matrix perturbation theory and the insightful observation regarding the singular vectors of optimal solutions are crucial for achieving this result.  The numerical experiments provide further validation of the theoretical findings, showing linear convergence in practice.

However, some aspects could be improved. While the assumptions are stated clearly, a more detailed discussion of their practical implications and the limitations they impose would be helpful. For instance, what classes of operators A and functions h are commonly encountered in practice that satisfy the given assumptions? The connection between the constant η in Theorem 3.1 and the parameters of the problem (e.g., Lipschitz constant L, properties of A) is not fully explored.  The numerical experiments are somewhat limited.  While they demonstrate linear convergence, a comparison with other algorithms (e.g., accelerated PGM, other first-order methods) would strengthen the results. The discussion of the step size selection in the matrix classification experiment mentions a backtracking line search but lacks details on its implementation.  Finally, while the paper cites [10] as the framework for the convergence analysis, a more explicit connection to the conditions required by that framework would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The result demonstrating linear convergence without strong convexity is a significant contribution.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The mathematical analysis appears rigorous, and the key lemmas and theorems are well-justified.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow.
* **Significance:** The results have implications for a wide range of applications involving trace norm regularization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited practical discussion:** The discussion of the assumptions and their practical implications is somewhat lacking.
* **Limited numerical evaluation:** The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive and include comparisons with other algorithms.
* **Missing implementation details:** Details regarding the backtracking line search in the matrix classification example are missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While there are a few areas for improvement, the novelty, soundness, and potential impact of the results outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
