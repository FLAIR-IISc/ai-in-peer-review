- Main contribution: Proves a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization (min_X f(X)+τ‖X‖* with f(X)=h(A(X))) and uses it to show linear convergence of proximal gradient without strong convexity.

- Theoretical significance: First linear convergence result for PGM on trace-norm problems without requiring strong convexity, important for tasks like matrix completion.

- Technical sophistication: Employs matrix perturbation theory to establish a restricted invertibility property and leverages shared singular-vector structure between optima and gradients.

- Clarity and organization: Paper is well-written, with clear definitions, logical flow, and useful supplementary technical material.

- Experimental validation: Numerical results on matrix completion and classification exhibit the predicted linear convergence behavior.

- Limited scope of loss functions: Analysis is restricted to f(X)=h(A(X)), leaving unclear applicability to more general or composite loss structures.

- Global assumption in error bound: Theorem 3.1 requires F(X) ≤ ζ (for some ζ ≥ v*); the relation to the initial iterate and practical restrictiveness need clarification.

- Comparison to related work: The paper should contextualize its bound quantitatively by comparing constants to known ℓ1-norm error bounds.

- Numerical experiments limited: Only two problem types tested; more experiments (e.g., multi-task learning or other trace-norm problems) would strengthen generality claims.

- Step-size discussion missing: Analysis assumes fixed step sizes (bounded by 1/L_f); authors should discuss extension to adaptive schemes like backtracking.

- Overall assessment: Strong, novel, and technically sound contribution that resolves an important open question; limitations are notable but do not undermine the main result.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—requested revisions: (1) expand scope discussion beyond f=h(A(X)), (2) clarify practical implications of the global F(X)≤ζ assumption, (3) compare error-bound constants to ℓ1 results, (4) add at least one more numerical experiment.
