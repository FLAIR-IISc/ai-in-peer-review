Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for convex optimization with trace/nuclear norm regularization, focusing on problems of the form min_X f(X)+τ||X||_* where f(X)=h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth and strictly convex (and strongly convex on any compact convex subset of dom(h)). The main contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound that upper-bounds the distance to the optimal solution set by the norm of a natural proximal residual, and the resulting proof (via the Luo–Tseng framework) of asymptotic linear convergence of PGM without assuming strong convexity of f. This covers common losses (squared, logistic) and settings where A has a nontrivial nullspace (e.g., matrix completion), where f is not strongly convex.

Technically, the key novelty is a spectral structural property of optimal solutions: at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors. This enables a perturbation argument that circumvents the lack of polyhedral structure of the nuclear norm and yields the restricted invertibility inequality required to derive the error bound. The results connect to prior linearisations of PGM under strong convexity, and to error bounds previously established for ℓ1 and (group) LASSO regularizers (polyhedral cases). To my knowledge, a comparable local error bound for the nuclear norm under such general f was not previously established; most existing linear-rate results either assume strong convexity of f, RIP-type conditions, or appeal to general KL/EB frameworks without exploiting the spectral structure.

Quality:
- The theoretical development is careful and appears sound. The main theorem (error bound) is proved via contradiction, relying on Lemma 4.1 and two propositions that encode the spectral alignment and a decomposition of the limiting descent direction. The use of Luo–Tseng’s framework to translate the error bound into linear rates for both objective values (Q-linear) and iterates (R-linear) is standard and appropriate.
- Assumption 2.1(b) (h is strongly convex on any compact convex subset) is reasonable and holds for squared and logistic losses (the latter has a positive curvature lower bound on compact sets). The boundedness of relevant level sets is established, which is crucial for local strong convexity constants and for the local error bound to apply.
- The convergence result is asymptotic/local, as expected given the absence of global strong convexity; this is made explicit.
- The experiments are limited but consistent with the theory, showing linear trends once near the solution. However, they are relatively small-scale and do not quantify the neighborhood of linear convergence or constants, and provide no comparisons.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized: assumptions, algorithm, residual map, error bound, and convergence results are presented clearly. The role of each technical lemma/proposition is easy to follow.
- Some notation and editorial issues should be addressed: figure numbering/mislabelling (two “Figure 2”), minor typos, and incomplete reference metadata (missing years). It would also help to give more intuition around Proposition 4.2 (e.g., explicitly connecting to subdifferentials of the nuclear norm) and to briefly summarize what parts of the proof are deferred to the supplement.
- The assumptions (especially 2.1(b)) would benefit from examples and a short discussion of when they fail (e.g., non-smooth h, unbounded level sets).

Originality:
- The main contribution—establishing a local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularized problems under broad smooth losses and using it to prove linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity—is, to my knowledge, novel and nontrivial. Prior error bounds focused on polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1/group LASSO) or required other structural conditions. The spectral alignment argument and perturbation-based restricted invertibility are insightful.
- The paper could better contextualize the result relative to general KL/error-bound frameworks for composite convex problems that also yield local linear convergence of proximal methods (e.g., KL with exponent 1/2 for semi-algebraic functions, which includes nuclear norm). Positioning this work as providing sharper, structurally grounded conditions and constants specific to the nuclear norm would strengthen the originality claim.

Significance:
- The result advances our theoretical understanding of first-order methods with nuclear-norm regularization, a core tool in low-rank learning problems. The error bound itself is likely useful beyond PGM (e.g., for coordinate, block, or proximal Newton-type methods, or for analyzing ADMM).
- The scope includes widely used losses and operators A, making the result broadly applicable in machine learning (matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification).
- Practical impact would be improved by discussing how to estimate or bound the constants (η, ε, ρ) in practice, and by demonstrating linear convergence on larger-scale problems and comparisons with accelerated PGM and competing solvers.

Pros:
- Clear, well-motivated problem; important setting in ML.
- Novel local error bound for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity.
- Elegant spectral argument enabling the analysis.
- Rigorous translation to linear convergence of PGM; assumptions are natural for common losses.
- Experiments support asymptotic linear behavior.

Cons:
- Linear convergence is local; no guidance on the size of the basin or constants; limited practical implications discussed.
- Experimental section is small-scale, lacks baselines and ablations, and does not quantify constants or entry into the linear regime.
- Missing discussion of connections to general KL/EB literature and whether their conditions imply or are implied by the present assumptions.
- Minor clarity issues (figures, references, typos).

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a brief comparison to KL-based linear convergence results, clarifying what this paper adds (e.g., explicit residual-based EB, structural constants, weaker conditions on f).
- Provide some guidance on estimating Lf and ensuring αk bounds with backtracking (show αk bounded away from 0 in practice).
- Include larger-scale experiments and comparisons with accelerated PGM and other nuclear-norm solvers; report iteration and time to reach a target accuracy and illustrate the onset of linear regime.
- Tighten exposition around Proposition 4.2 (cite subdifferential characterization of the nuclear norm leading to shared singular vectors) and ensure all key proof steps not in the supplement are sufficiently sketched.
- Fix figure numbering and complete reference metadata.

Overall recommendation:
This is a solid theoretical contribution with a nontrivial and broadly relevant result. Despite modest experiments, the paper meets NeurIPS standards in quality, originality, and significance; with the suggested clarifications and expanded empirical evaluation, it would be even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
