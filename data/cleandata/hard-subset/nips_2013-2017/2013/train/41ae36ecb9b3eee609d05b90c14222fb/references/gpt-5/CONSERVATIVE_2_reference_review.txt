Summary
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is continuously differentiable, Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex and “locally strongly convex” (strongly convex on any convex compact subset of its domain). The main technical contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear-norm regularized problem: in a bounded level set and near optimality, dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F, where R(X) = proxτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Leveraging Luo–Tseng’s framework, the authors deduce that proximal gradient with fixed step size αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf) achieves asymptotic Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates, without assuming strong convexity of f. The technical novelty is an analysis of the spectral structure near the solution set: at an optimum X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, which enables a decomposition argument and a restricted invertibility property. The paper includes small-scale synthetic experiments for matrix completion (quadratic loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrating apparent linear convergence near the solution.

Relation to prior work
Error bounds and linear convergence for proximal gradient under polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group lasso) are known; the nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, so previous proofs do not transfer. The paper builds on Luo–Tseng’s error bound machinery and on subdifferential characterizations of the nuclear norm. There is also a body of work establishing linear rates under strong convexity or quadratic growth, and, in statistics, under restricted strong convexity for low-rank models; the authors should position their result more carefully against those frameworks and against general KL/PL-type results that also imply local linear rates for broad classes of semialgebraic objectives.

Quality
- The high-level argument is standard and sound: prove a local error bound then invoke Luo–Tseng to get linear rates. The main novelty is the error bound for a nonpolyhedral regularizer via spectral alignment and perturbation.
- However, several crucial steps are deferred to the supplement (e.g., inequalities (9)–(11), Proposition 4.3). Given the delicacy of spectral arguments (e.g., multiplicities in the spectrum, non-uniqueness of singular vectors), more detail in the main text would inspire confidence.
- The assumptions are tailored to yield only asymptotic/local linear convergence with constants depending on a compact set around X*. This is reasonable but limits practical guarantees; the paper should make this explicit and discuss the size of the basin.
- The step-size condition αk < 1/Lf is standard but not always practical without a good Lf; the experiments use backtracking, but the theory assumes αk bounded away from 0 and 1/Lf. Please clarify whether the backtracking scheme ensures the required bounds.
- Experiments are purely illustrative: small n, synthetic data, no comparisons (e.g., FISTA, SVT/ADMM), and no investigation of pre-asymptotic regimes or robustness. They do not validate the theoretical constants or range of linear behavior.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The motivation for going beyond strong convexity is clear.
- There are some notational inconsistencies and minor issues: τ vs µ for the regularization parameter in experiments; prox definition (3) seems to miss a 1/2 factor; figure numbering/labels are confusing; step-size choice α=1 for matrix completion is not justified relative to 1/Lf. Proof sketches occasionally rely on “see the supplementary material” for nontrivial steps.
- A clearer statement of Assumption 2.1(b) (and how σ is chosen in (15)) would help.

Originality
- Establishing a Luo–Tseng type error bound for nuclear-norm regularization is novel and nontrivial, given the nonpolyhedral geometry. The simultaneous SVD characterization at optimality is classical in spectral function analysis, but its use in deriving an error bound in this setting appears new.
- The paper should discuss connections/differences to: (i) KL/PL-based linear convergence results for proximal methods on tame/semialgebraic functions; (ii) restricted strong convexity/quadratic growth frameworks yielding linear rates (e.g., for matrix sensing/completion under RIP-like conditions). This would better situate the contribution.

Significance
- The result tightens our theoretical understanding of a widely used algorithm for a central regularizer in ML. That said, the rate is local/asymptotic with unknown constants, and does not change algorithm design or yield improved practical performance guarantees. Thus, the impact is primarily theoretical.
- Without stronger empirical validation or sharper, more computable constants, the practical significance appears modest.

Pros
- Addresses an important, widely used setting (nuclear norm regularization).
- Nontrivial theoretical advance: local error bound for a nonpolyhedral regularizer and resulting linear rate without strong convexity of f.
- Clear high-level structure; builds on established analysis framework.

Cons
- Key proofs deferred; handling of spectral multiplicities and stability deserves more detail.
- Only asymptotic/local linear rate; constants are not quantified.
- Experiments are minimal, synthetic, and lack baselines; no study of basin of attraction or pre-asymptotics.
- Limited discussion of related frameworks (KL/PL, RSC, quadratic growth) that can also imply linear rates.

Questions for authors
1) Can you characterize or bound the constants η, ε in Theorem 3.1 and κ, σ in Lemma 4.1 in terms of problem data (A, τ, local curvature of h)? Any guidance for practitioners?
2) Do your arguments extend to accelerated PG (e.g., FISTA) or to proximal Newton variants? To stochastic/variance-reduced settings?
3) How do you handle non-unique singular vectors at X̄ (e.g., repeated singular values) in Proposition 4.2 and the perturbation steps?
4) Can you compare to KL-based analyses that often yield local linear rates for nuclear-norm regularized objectives?
5) Please clarify the backtracking rule and verify it enforces 0<α≤αk≤ᾱ<1/Lf eventually.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting but primarily asymptotic and the empirical validation is too limited for NeurIPS. Strengthening the proofs in the main text, situating the work within broader linear-convergence frameworks, and providing more substantial experiments/comparisons would improve the case for acceptance. Confidence: high.
