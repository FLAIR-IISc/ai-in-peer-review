### **Summary**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. While PGM is known to converge linearly when the smooth component `f` is strongly convex, many important applications in machine learning, such as matrix completion with a square loss, do not satisfy this condition. The authors address this gap by establishing an asymptotic linear convergence rate for a broad class of problems where `f(X)` has the composite structure `h(A(X))`, with `h` being a strictly convex and smooth function and `A` a linear operator. This structure for `f` is not necessarily strongly convex.

The core technical contribution is the derivation of a novel local Lipschitzian error bound for this class of trace norm regularized problems. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, a key condition for proving linear convergence of first-order methods. The authors highlight that deriving such a bound for the trace norm is challenging due to its non-polyhedral nature and the interdependence of matrix entries, which prevents the use of techniques developed for `l1` or group LASSO regularization. Their analysis overcomes this by leveraging a crucial structural property of the optimal solution—that an optimal matrix `X̄` and the corresponding gradient `-∇f(X̄)` share the same singular vectors. This insight, combined with matrix perturbation theory, allows for the establishment of the error bound. Using this bound, the paper then applies the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng to prove R-linear convergence of the iterates and Q-linear convergence of the objective values. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and practically relevant question: under what weaker-than-strong-convexity conditions does a workhorse algorithm like PGM converge linearly? Given the ubiquity of trace norm regularization in machine learning, this result provides a much deeper understanding of the algorithm's performance on common problems like matrix completion and multi-task learning. Proving linear convergence in these settings is a significant step forward from the standard sublinear rate guarantees.

2.  **Novelty and Technical Depth:** The primary contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1)—appears to be novel and resolves a question that the authors convincingly argue was open. The analytical technique is sophisticated. The observation about the shared singular vectors of the optimal solution and the negative gradient is a key insight that unlocks the analysis. The subsequent use of matrix perturbation theory to handle the non-separability of the trace norm is technically impressive and distinguishes this work from prior analyses of error bounds for other regularizers (e.g., `l1`-norm).

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work in the context of existing literature and stating the contributions upfront. The problem setup, assumptions, and main results are presented precisely. The authors do a good job of outlining the proof strategy, explaining why new techniques are needed and providing high-level intuition before diving into the technical details.

4.  **Rigorous Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical framework is sound and builds upon the well-established error bound analysis of Luo and Tseng. The assumptions made on the loss function `f(X) = h(A(X))` are reasonable and are shown to hold for standard loss functions like square loss and logistic loss, making the results broadly applicable.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The established error bound is local, holding only in a neighborhood of the optimal solution set (i.e., when `F(X) ≤ ζ` and `‖R(X)‖_F ≤ ǫ`). Consequently, the proven linear convergence is asymptotic. While the paper's abstract mentions an "asymptotic linear rate," the main body could be more explicit about this limitation and discuss its practical implications. For instance, the length of the initial sublinear convergence phase is not characterized.

2.  **Clarity of the Proof Sketch:** While the overall paper is clear, the proof sketch of the main result in Section 4 is extremely dense and relies heavily on propositions whose proofs are deferred to the supplementary material. In particular, the proof of the crucial "Restricted Invertibility" property (Lemma 4.1(c)) and the decomposition of `Q̄` (Proposition 4.3) are central to the novelty of the paper. Providing more intuition in the main text about how the spectral analysis and the case-by-case argument lead to the contradiction would significantly improve the paper's self-containedness and allow readers to better appreciate the technical argument without immediately resorting to the supplement.

3.  **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** The numerical experiments effectively illustrate and validate the theoretical linear convergence. However, they are somewhat limited in scope. The paper does not compare PGM's performance against other specialized state-of-the-art solvers for matrix completion or classification. While this is a theoretical paper, such a comparison could have helped contextualize the practical importance of achieving a linear rate.

### **Constructive Suggestions for Improvement**

*   In Section 4, consider adding a short paragraph to provide more intuition for the proof of Proposition 4.3. A brief explanation of what the three cases (A, B, C) for the singular values represent and how they help characterize the limit matrix `Q̄` would make the logic much easier to follow.
*   To better connect the theory with the experiments, I suggest adding plots of the residual norm `‖R(Xk)‖_F` versus iteration count. Since the error bound directly links `dist(Xk, X*)` to `‖R(Xk)‖_F`, showing that the residual also converges linearly would provide a more direct and compelling visualization of the paper's core theoretical mechanism at play.
*   It would be beneficial to explicitly state that the linear convergence is an asymptotic property in the discussion of Theorem 3.2 and briefly comment on this. This is not a major flaw, as it is typical for such results, but acknowledging it directly would make the claims more precise.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**
*   The paper provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM on trace norm regularized problems without assuming strong convexity of the smooth term, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The core technical result, a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, is of independent interest and is derived using a clever and non-trivial analysis based on the spectral properties of the solution.
*   The problem is highly relevant to the NeurIPS community, and the results enhance our understanding of a widely used optimization algorithm.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and rigorously executed.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The proof of the main technical result is highly condensed in the main paper, making a full assessment of its correctness difficult without extensive study of the supplement.
*   The convergence result is asymptotic, a detail that could be emphasized more clearly.
*   The experimental section is purely illustrative and lacks comparisons to other methods.

### **Decision**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a strong, novel, and significant contribution to the optimization literature relevant to machine learning. The strengths, particularly the novelty and importance of the theoretical results, substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses related to presentation and experimental scope. The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS and should be accepted.

**Recommendation: Accept**
