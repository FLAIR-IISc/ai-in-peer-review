## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm-regularized optimization problems, a common framework in areas like matrix completion and low-rank learning. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under fairly general conditions on the loss function, specifically `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strongly convex and `A` is a linear operator. This is a significant result because it extends the known linear convergence guarantees, which typically require strong convexity of `f` itself, to a wider class of problems commonly encountered in machine learning. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored for trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges stemming from the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established theoretical analysis of PGM, referencing works like Beck & Teboulle (2009), Nesterov (2013) and Luo & Tseng (1992). It correctly identifies the gap in the literature regarding linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors accurately position their work relative to existing error bound results for composite optimization (e.g., for ℓ1 regularization) and highlight the difficulties in applying those approaches directly to the trace norm due to its unique properties.  They appropriately cite relevant work in trace norm minimization (Recht et al., 2009; Koltchinskii et al., 2011) and applications like matrix completion (Fazel et al., 2001) and multi-task learning (Argyriou et al., 2008).

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:**  The linear convergence guarantee without strong convexity of the loss function is a valuable contribution to the theory of PGM and expands its applicability.
* **Novel Error Bound:** The derivation of a Lipschitzian error bound specifically for trace norm regularization is a major achievement, addressing a previously open problem. The matrix perturbation theory approach is clever and well-motivated.
* **Generality:** The result applies to a broad class of loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), making it relevant to numerous applications.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The notation is consistent, and the core ideas are clearly explained.
* **Supporting Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification provide empirical support for the theoretical results and demonstrate the linear convergence in practice.
* **Detailed Proof Outline:** The paper provides a solid proof outline, acknowledging the detailed proofs are in supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on h:** While the assumption of strong convexity on *compact* subsets of `dom(h)` is reasonable, it could be clarified how this impacts practical scenarios where the optimal solution might not lie on such a subset. More discussion on the practical implications of Assumption 2.1(b) would be beneficial.
* **Step Size Selection:** The theoretical convergence result relies on a carefully chosen step size.  While the authors mention a backtracking line search in the experiments, the paper doesn’t provide practical guidance on how to choose an appropriate step size in applications.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper demonstrates the convergence rate, it doesn’t discuss the computational cost of each iteration, particularly the proximity operator calculation. Understanding the overall runtime complexity is crucial for practical applications.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are helpful, they only cover two specific applications. Expanding the empirical evaluation to a wider range of problems and datasets would strengthen the claims.
* **Proposition 4.2 & 4.3 - Clarity:** The explanation and intuition behind Propositions 4.2 and 4.3, while technically sound, are dense. More intuitive explanations would improve readability.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical result with broad implications.
* The technical contributions (error bound, analysis framework) are novel and well-justified.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
* The work addresses an important open question in the field of optimization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The assumptions on the loss function, while reasonable, could be further clarified.
* The lack of detailed discussion on step-size selection and computational complexity limits the practical applicability of the results.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. The theoretical results are strong, the technical approach is innovative, and the empirical validation is encouraging. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  Addressing the concerns regarding step-size selection and expanding the empirical evaluation in a future version would further enhance the impact of this paper.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10)

**Confidence:** High
