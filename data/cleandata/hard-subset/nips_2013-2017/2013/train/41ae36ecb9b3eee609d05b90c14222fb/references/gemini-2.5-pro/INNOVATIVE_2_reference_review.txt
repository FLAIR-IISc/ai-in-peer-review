**Summary**

This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning. The standard theory guarantees a linear convergence rate only when the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is strongly convex. However, many important applications, such as matrix completion with a square loss, do not satisfy this condition. The main contribution of this paper is to prove that PGM still achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence for a broad class of problems of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex but the overall function `f` is not necessarily so. The core of their analysis is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, which relates the distance to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map. This result resolves what the authors claim is an open question, extending previous error bound results from `l1`-type regularizers (with polyhedral structure) to the non-polyhedral trace norm.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novelty and the importance of its contribution. Establishing a linear convergence rate for PGM on this class of non-strongly convex problems is a significant theoretical breakthrough. It provides a rigorous explanation for the fast convergence often observed in practice for methods applied to problems like matrix completion. This is not an incremental improvement; it fundamentally expands our understanding of one of the most common optimization frameworks for a widely used regularizer.

2.  **Novel Technical Approach:** The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is highly non-trivial and introduces a creative analytical technique. Unlike prior work on `l1` or group LASSO norms, which could exploit separability, the authors here tackle the global nature of the trace norm's singular values. Their key insight—using matrix perturbation theory to analyze the shared spectral structure of an optimal solution `X̄` and the gradient term `-∇f(X̄)` (Proposition 4.2)—is a bold and effective way to overcome this challenge. This analytical tool could be of independent interest and potentially applicable to other matrix norm problems.

3.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The authors clearly motivate the problem, situate their contribution within the existing literature, and present their main theorems before diving into the proof. The proof sketch is logical and follows the established Luo-Tseng framework, while clearly delineating where their new contributions (namely, the proof of the error bound in Lemma 4.1) lie. The technical arguments are dense but appear sound.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments, while illustrative of the theoretical claims, are somewhat limited. They serve as a proof-of-concept, showing that linear convergence indeed occurs on synthetic matrix completion and classification tasks. However, they do not provide a comprehensive empirical study. There are no comparisons against other state-of-the-art solvers, nor is there an investigation into how factors like problem conditioning or the structure of the operator `A` affect the convergence constant.

**Critique and Recommendation**

This paper presents a foundational theoretical result. The central question it addresses—whether PGM converges linearly for trace norm regularization without strong convexity—is of great importance to the optimization and machine learning communities. The authors answer this question affirmatively by developing a novel and technically sophisticated error bound.

While the experimental section could be more thorough, this is a minor weakness in the context of a paper whose main contribution is clearly theoretical. The novelty of the result and the elegance of the proof technique are outstanding. This work provides the missing theoretical justification for a widely observed phenomenon and introduces new analytical tools that could spur further research. It is a significant step forward, not an incremental one, and represents precisely the kind of bold, high-impact research that NIPS should champion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first proof of linear convergence for PGM on a wide and practical class of non-strongly convex trace norm regularized problems.
*   Resolves a known open problem in the optimization literature.
*   Introduces a novel and powerful proof technique for analyzing the trace norm that may be of broader use.
*   The paper is technically deep, well-written, and addresses a significant problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The empirical evaluation is illustrative rather than exhaustive.

In my view, the profound theoretical contribution of this paper far outweighs the limited scope of the experiments. I strongly recommend acceptance.
