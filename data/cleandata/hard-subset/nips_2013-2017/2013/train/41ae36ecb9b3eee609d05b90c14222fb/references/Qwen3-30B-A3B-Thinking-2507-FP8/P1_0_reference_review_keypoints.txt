- Problem addressed: conditions for proximal gradient method (PGM) to achieve linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity.  
- Main result: novel Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1) yielding linear convergence of PGM (Theorem 3.2) under mild assumptions.  
- Problem class: handles min_X f(X)+τ‖X‖_* with f(X)=h(A(X)), A linear, h strictly convex and smooth (covers square loss, logistic loss).  
- Technical novelty: uses matrix perturbation theory and simultaneous singular vector structure to handle non-polyhedral trace-norm epigraph.  
- Builds on prior work (Luo & Tseng) but resolves an open question specific to trace-norm regularization.  
- Quality: rigorous, well-constructed proofs with reasonable, well-justified assumptions and sufficient supplementary detail.  
- Originality: first to provide linear convergence for PGM in trace-norm setting without strong convexity; methodological novelty in error-bound derivation.  
- Significance: important theoretical and practical implications for matrix completion, multi-task learning, and other trace-norm applications.  
- Clarity: well-written, logically organized, with clear connection between error bound and convergence and supportive numerical experiments.  
- Weakness — experiments: empirical validation limited to two applications; needs more diverse/larger-scale experiments and parameter-sensitivity analysis.  
- Weakness — assumptions: f(X)=h(A(X)) with strict convexity/smoothness excludes some structured losses; limitations should be discussed/clarified.  
- Weakness — accessibility: key Proposition 4.3 proof relegated to supplementary material, reducing accessibility of an important component.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — substantial theoretical advance, technically sound, original, and significant for ML/optimization.  
- Pros summary: resolves open question, first linear convergence result without strong convexity, novel error-bound technique, rigorous proofs, experiments support theory.  
- Cons summary: limited experimental scope, possibly restrictive assumptions, important proof placed in supplement.
