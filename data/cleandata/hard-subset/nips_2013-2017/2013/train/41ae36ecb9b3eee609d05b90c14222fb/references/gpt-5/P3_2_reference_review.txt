Summary and contribution
This paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for nuclear-norm regularized convex problems min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where the loss f has the structured form f(X) = h(A(X)). The key assumptions are that A is linear, h is continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex, and “locally strongly convex” on any convex compact subset of its domain, and the optimal solution set X is nonempty. Under these conditions, the authors establish a local Lipschitzian error bound of Luo–Tseng type: dist(X, X) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F for points in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual, where R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. This error bound, combined with standard descent and residual inequalities for PGM, yields asymptotic linear convergence: Q-linear in objective values and R-linear in iterates for fixed step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf). The central technical novelty is the error bound for the nuclear norm case, which is nonpolyhedral and has resisted direct extensions of existing L1/group-LASSO analyses. The proof leverages spectral structure at optimality: optimal solutions X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors (via properties of the nuclear-norm proximal mapping), enabling a perturbation argument and a “restricted invertibility” inequality that controls distances via A(X − X̄).

Relation to prior work
- Sublinear rates O(1/k) and O(1/k^2) for PGM and its acceleration in composite convex settings are standard; linear rates typically require strong convexity or restricted strong convexity. Existing linear-rate results under error bounds have covered polyhedral nonsmooth terms (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) or PLQ functions. The nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, and its subdifferential geometry couples all entries through singular vectors, precluding separability-based arguments. This work appears to be the first to prove a Luo–Tseng type error bound for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f, thereby extending linear convergence guarantees to common low-rank learning losses (squared and logistic).
- The approach is consistent with the general error-bound framework (Luo & Tseng), Schmidt et al. (inexact PG), and Tseng/Yun for separable nonsmooth terms, but departs technically in handling spectral functions.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The main theorems are plausible and technically careful. The fixed-point characterization via the proximal mapping and the residual map is standard; inequalities (9)–(11) are routine for PGM and are deferred to the supplement.
- The error-bound proof is the key novelty. The construction of the set Xc of optimizers that share singular vectors with −∇f(X̄), and the projection argument using a contradiction with sequences having Rk/δk → 0, are convincing and well-motivated. Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous diagonalization of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) in the basis induced by X̄ − ∇f(X̄)) is consistent with the spectral form of the nuclear-norm proximal operator. The three-case decomposition of singular values and the consequent control of perturbations (Proposition 4.3) are the right tools to mimic separability in a spectral basis.
- Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity on any compact convex subset) is unusual but sufficient for a local modulus σ on a bounded set Z containing the iterates; it holds for squared and logistic losses on bounded regions. The resulting linear convergence is asymptotic/local, as expected.
- One subtle step is the “restricted invertibility” inequality (16). Intuitively, it asserts that A is injective on the relevant tangent directions to the optimal set; the proof by contradiction (constructing a descent contradiction within Xc along a direction in ker(A)) is elegant and avoids imposing additional nondegeneracy/strict complementarity assumptions. The argument depends critically on the spectral alignment at optimality.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The motivation for using error bounds and the need for new ideas beyond ℓ1/group-LASSO are clear. The proof roadmap is explicit.
- Several technical details are deferred to the supplement (derivation of (9)–(11), proof of Proposition 4.3); given space constraints, this is acceptable, but the main text could benefit from a brief sketch of Proposition 4.3.
- Minor issues: mismatch of figure numbering (matrix completion text mentions Figure 1 but the included caption says Figure 2); inconsistent notation in experiments (µ vs τ); clarify that backtracking ensures αk ≤ 1/Lf and eventually bounded away from zero.

Originality
- The main novelty is the Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization and the resulting linear convergence of vanilla PGM without strong convexity of f. This addresses a recognized gap: nonpolyhedral nonsmooth terms with spectral coupling. To my knowledge (given the cited literature), this is new and nontrivial.

Significance
- The result is of clear interest to the optimization and machine learning communities: it justifies linear convergence for widely used nuclear-norm problems (matrix completion, multitask learning, matrix classification) under realistic assumptions on h and regardless of injectivity of A.
- Practically, the result is asymptotic and local; constants (η, neighborhood size ε, contraction factor) are not quantified in problem primitives, limiting direct algorithmic tuning. Nonetheless, establishing the existence of such a regime is valuable, and the spectral proof technique may be reusable for other spectral regularizers.

Empirical evaluation
- The experiments are illustrative (synthetic completion and classification), showing linear decay in semi-log plots once near the solution. They are minimal: no real datasets, runtime/accuracy tradeoffs, or comparisons to alternatives (e.g., accelerated methods, ADMM, SVT). For a primarily theoretical paper, this is acceptable, but strengthening the empirical section would broaden impact.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the authors comment on accelerated PGM (FISTA) under the same error bound? With restarts, linear rates typically follow; without restarts, nonmonotone behavior complicates analysis.
- How do η and the neighborhood radius in Theorem 3.1 depend on spectral properties at the optimum (e.g., gaps around τ and nonzero singular values)? Any interpretable conditions that enlarge the linear regime?
- Many practical solvers use inexact prox via truncated SVDs. Does the error bound extend to inexact PGM with summable proximal errors?
- Clarify that standard backtracking produces αk ∈ [αmin, 1/Lf], satisfying Theorem 3.2’s step-size assumptions.

Pros
- Novel and technically solid error bound for nuclear-norm problems.
- Extends linear-rate guarantees of PGM to common losses without strong convexity.
- Clear positioning within the Luo–Tseng framework; proof uses insightful spectral arguments.

Cons
- Asymptotic/local result with unspecified constants; limited practical guidance on when linear regime is reached.
- Experimental section is minimal; minor clarity/notation issues.
- Some key technical proofs are only in the supplement.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is original and relevant to NeurIPS, with sound methodology and clear implications. Improving clarity around assumptions/constants and modestly strengthening the experiments would further enhance the paper.
