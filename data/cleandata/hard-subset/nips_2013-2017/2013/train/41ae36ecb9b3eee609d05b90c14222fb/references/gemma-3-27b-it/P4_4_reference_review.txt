This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically when the loss function has a particular structure – f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex. The authors aim to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the overall loss function *f*, a common limitation in prior work. They derive a novel Lipschitzian error bound relating the distance to the optimal solution to the residual norm, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to overcome challenges arising from the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical gap in the theoretical understanding of PGM convergence for widely used loss functions (e.g., in matrix completion) where strong convexity doesn't hold. The problem statement is clear, and the connection to existing work is thoroughly established. The core contribution – the error bound and subsequent linear convergence result – is significant. The presentation is generally clear and follows a logical structure. The authors acknowledge limitations and discuss potential future directions.  The inclusion of numerical experiments bolstering the theoretical findings is a definite strength. 

However, the paper's density and technicality pose challenges.  While the authors strive for clarity, the numerous definitions, assumptions, and intermediate lemmas require significant effort from the reader to follow.  The proofs, while seemingly correct, are highly condensed and rely heavily on citing supplementary material, making independent verification difficult during a review. The connection between the assumptions (particularly on *h* and *A*) and concrete applications is somewhat implicit; elaborating on how these assumptions translate in practice would be beneficial. While Proposition 4.2 is a key technical insight, its motivation and implications are not immediately obvious and could benefit from further explanation. The numerical experiments, while encouraging, are limited in scope and lack detailed comparisons against alternative algorithms. The discussion of the backtracking line search in the matrix classification experiment feels somewhat brief given its importance in obtaining results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result for non-strongly convex loss functions is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The error bound and convergence analysis appear rigorously developed.
*   **Relevance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to various machine learning applications.
*   **Completeness:** The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis and is supported by numerical experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is dense and technically challenging, hindering readability.
*   **Proof Verification:** Reliance on supplementary material makes verifying proofs during review difficult.
*   **Practical Implications:** The connection between assumptions and real-world applications could be more explicit.
*   **Experimental Scope:** The numerical experiments are limited and lack extensive comparisons.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with potential impact on the field.
*   The technical analysis appears sound and well-motivated.
*   The paper addresses an important open problem in the analysis of PGM for trace norm regularization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The density and technicality of the paper may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
*   The reliance on supplementary material for key proofs hinders independent verification.
*   The limited scope of the numerical experiments prevents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The core theoretical contributions of this paper are strong and address an important open problem. However, the authors should prioritize improving the clarity and accessibility of the paper. Specifically, they should consider expanding the explanations of key concepts (e.g., Proposition 4.2) and providing more details within the main manuscript rather than exclusively relying on the supplementary material. A more extensive experimental evaluation, including comparisons to state-of-the-art algorithms, would also strengthen the paper.
