

# Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ||X||_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and A a linear operator. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for this problem, which is the key technical contribution. This error bound allows them to prove linear convergence of PGM under conditions that do not require f to be strongly convex, significantly extending previous results that required strong convexity. The paper makes a crucial observation that for optimal solutions X̄, both X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors, which enables the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound. The authors validate their theoretical results with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof of the main result. The authors carefully develop the key lemma (Lemma 4.1) and establish the error bound (Theorem 3.1) through a well-structured argument. The proof of the error bound is non-trivial and addresses a previously open question in the literature. The authors properly cite related work, including the prior results on error bounds for ℓ₁-norm regularization and the limitations of those approaches for trace norm regularization.

The numerical experiments complement the theoretical results well, showing linear convergence in practice for both matrix completion and matrix classification problems. The experimental setup is reasonable and directly tests the theoretical claims. The paper is careful to state its assumptions clearly and discusses the limitations of the results.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by clearly explaining the limitations of previous work and positioning the contribution. The technical sections are well-structured, with clear definitions and step-by-step development of the key concepts. The use of mathematical notation is consistent and appropriate for the field. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The figures illustrating the numerical experiments are clear and effectively support the theoretical claims.

## Originality
This paper makes a significant original contribution by resolving an open question about error bounds for trace norm regularization. Prior work on error bounds for composite minimization focused on cases where the non-smooth part has polyhedral epigraph (like ℓ₁-norm), but the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph. The authors' key insight about the shared singular vectors of optimal solutions and the gradient is novel and directly addresses this gap. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization, which required strong convexity of f. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature, citing relevant NIPS papers and other key references.

## Significance
The results have substantial significance for machine learning applications where trace norm regularization is used, including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. The paper provides theoretical guarantees that can guide practitioners in choosing appropriate algorithms for these problems. The linear convergence rate established here is faster than the sublinear rates previously known for this problem under weaker conditions. The error bound itself could be of independent interest for future work on matrix norm regularization. The paper advances the state of the art in optimization for matrix problems by establishing a convergence guarantee that applies to a broader class of loss functions than previously known.

## Strengths
- Resolves an important open question about error bounds for trace norm regularization
- Establishes linear convergence of PGM without requiring strong convexity of f
- Provides a novel technical insight about shared singular vectors of optimal solutions
- The proof is rigorous and well-structured
- Numerical experiments support the theoretical claims
- Clear writing and organization

## Weaknesses
- The assumption f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex might not cover all loss functions used in practice (though it covers many important cases)
- The numerical experiments are limited to two specific problems, though this is reasonable given the scope
- The paper doesn't compare the computational complexity of PGM with other methods for trace norm regularization, though this might be out of scope

## Recommendation
This paper makes a substantial contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning, particularly for problems involving trace norm regularization. The technical contribution is significant, the proof is rigorous, and the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The paper advances the state of the art by establishing linear convergence under weaker conditions than previously known, which has important implications for many machine learning applications.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's of high quality, clearly written, original, and significant. It addresses an important problem in a novel way that will be of interest to the machine learning community. The authors have carefully positioned their work within the literature and provided a rigorous proof of their claims.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS.

## Specific Comments
The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the Lipschitz constant of ∇f relates to the choice of step size in practice, though this may be minor. The numerical experiments could include a comparison with other methods (like accelerated proximal gradient or alternating minimization) to better contextualize the computational efficiency, but this is not essential for the main theoretical contribution.

The paper is well-written and makes a clear, important contribution to the field. It would be valuable to the NIPS community as it provides theoretical guarantees that can guide the use of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization in a wide range of applications.
