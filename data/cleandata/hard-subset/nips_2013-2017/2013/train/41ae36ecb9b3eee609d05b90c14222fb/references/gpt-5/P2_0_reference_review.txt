Summary
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization: minimize F(X)=f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, Lipschitz-gradient, and “locally strongly convex” on any compact convex subset of its domain. The main contributions are:
- A local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear-norm regularized problem: dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F whenever F(X) ≤ ζ and the residual ||R(X)||F is small, where R(X)=proxτ(X−∇f(X))−X.
- Using the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors derive asymptotic linear convergence (Q-linear in objective and R-linear in iterates) of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with step sizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf, without requiring strong convexity of f.
The technical novelty is an analysis that circumvents the non-polyhedral epigraph of the nuclear norm. The key observation is that an optimal solution X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), enabling a perturbation-based argument to establish a “restricted invertibility” inequality that links distances in X-space to differences in A(X)-space. Numerical experiments on synthetic matrix completion and matrix classification with logistic loss illustrate linear convergence in the late phase.

Relation to prior work
- Prior linear-rate results for composite problems using error bounds were known for polyhedral nonsmooth parts like ℓ1 and (group) LASSO regularization (Tseng, Yun; Zhang et al.). Those analyses rely on separability and polyhedral structure; neither holds for the nuclear norm.
- Luo–Tseng provide the general EB-to-linear-rate framework adopted here.
- Standard PGM rates for smooth+convex are O(1/k) or O(1/k^2) with acceleration, and O(c^k) with strong convexity. Here, f typically is not strongly convex due to A’s nullspace (e.g., matrix completion).
- To my knowledge, the local EB and consequent linear rate for nuclear-norm regularization under the stated composite structure (h(A(X))) fill a clear gap.

Quality
The main theorems are technically sound and carefully reasoned. The central Lemma 4.1 (asymptotic optimality, boundedness of images, and the restricted invertibility property) is the heavy lift; the contradiction argument via projections onto Xc (the set of optima sharing singular vectors) is convincing and well-motivated by Proposition 4.2 and the decomposition in Proposition 4.3. The translation from EB to rate via Luo–Tseng is standard and correctly applied.

Assumptions are reasonable for common losses (square, logistic). The linear rate is asymptotic/local (requires being sufficiently close to X*, via small residual), which matches what EB-based analyses typically deliver. The step-size condition 0<α<αk<ᾱ<1/Lf is standard; backtracking can ensure it in practice.

The experiments are illustrative (semi-log plots show linear slopes) but small-scale and synthetic; they serve to corroborate the asymptotic behavior rather than benchmark performance.

Clarity
Overall, the paper is clearly written and well-organized. The high-level intuition (spectral alignment at optimality enabling perturbation control) is explained upfront, which helps the reader navigate the technical proofs. Some suggestions:
- Assumption 2.1(b) (“strongly convex on any convex compact subset”) could be discussed more: provide concrete sufficient conditions (e.g., C2 with uniformly PD Hessian on compacts) and clarify which popular losses satisfy it.
- Discuss how the constants (η, ε) in the EB and the eventual contraction factor depend on problem data (L, ||A||, τ) to the extent possible, even qualitatively.
- Explicitly state that backtracking yields a stepsize sequence satisfying the required bounds and monotonic decrease, aligning the theory and experiments.
- Briefly compare with KL-based analyses (e.g., KL exponent 1/2 implies linear rates) and explain why the present EB is preferable/necessary for this setting.

Originality
High. Establishing a Lipschitzian EB for nuclear-norm regularization without polyhedral structure is nontrivial. The simultaneous SVD property at optimality and the projection argument onto Xc leading to restricted invertibility appear novel in this context. The result generalizes beyond square loss to a broad class f(X)=h(A(X)).

Significance
Moderate to high theoretical significance. Nuclear-norm regularization is ubiquitous; showing that standard PGM enjoys local linear rates under mild and practice-relevant conditions improves our understanding and can inform algorithm design (e.g., restarts, stopping rules based on residuals). The EB itself may be useful beyond PGM (e.g., for proximal Newton, coordinate variants, or proving linear rates for restarted accelerations).

Weaknesses and limitations
- The linear rate is local/asymptotic; no explicit radius of the basin of linear convergence or computable constants are provided. This limits predictive power on when linear behavior kicks in.
- The optimal set can be non-singleton; while the analysis addresses this by working with X*, convergence of iterates is only R-linear to some point in X*, and no identification properties (e.g., rank identification) are discussed.
- Experiments are minimal: small n, synthetic data, no comparison to alternative solvers (accelerated PGM, SVT, ADMM) or to cases where f is strongly convex to highlight the distinction.
- Practical scalability issues (full SVD per iteration) are not addressed; although orthogonal to the theory, a comment on using partial SVDs and how the EB might interact with inexact prox would be valuable.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and technically solid EB for nuclear-norm regularization.
- Clear, broadly applicable linear convergence result without strong convexity, covering important losses.
- Well-motivated proofs with interesting spectral insights likely to be reusable.
- Topic is relevant to NeurIPS audiences working on optimization for ML.

Cons for acceptance
- Local nature of the result; constants not explicit.
- Experimental validation is limited and does not explore boundaries of the assumptions.
- Some assumptions (local strong convexity of h) could be discussed more deeply.

Recommendations to authors
- Expand discussion on the scope of Assumption 2.1(b) with concrete examples and counterexamples.
- Comment on extensions to inexact prox (e.g., truncated SVD) and whether the EB can be combined with inexact PGM analyses.
- Provide at least one larger-scale experiment and a comparison to an accelerated method to contextualize the practical impact.

Overall recommendation: Accept (borderline to clear). The contribution is primarily theoretical but addresses a recognized open question with elegant techniques, and the result is pertinent to widely used methods in machine learning optimization.

Reviewer confidence: High.
