## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized optimization problems, a common setup in areas like matrix completion and low-rank learning. The authors establish linear convergence under relatively weak conditions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function *f*.  Their key contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this problem class, which allows them to leverage existing convergence analysis frameworks (Luo & Tseng) to prove the linear convergence result. The work addresses a gap in the literature, as previous linear convergence guarantees often relied on stronger assumptions about the loss function.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a genuine advance.  Many practical applications involve loss functions that are not strongly convex, making this result more broadly applicable than existing theorems.
* **Novel Error Bound:** The derived Lipschitzian error bound appears to be the core technical innovation. The authors correctly identify the challenge posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and their approach leveraging matrix perturbation theory seems insightful.
* **Well-Motivated Problem:** Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone technique in numerous machine learning tasks, making the problem relevant and impactful.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing work.  The notation is reasonably clear.
* **Supporting Experiments:** The inclusion of numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provides empirical evidence supporting the theoretical findings. While not exhaustive, they demonstrate the practical relevance of the result.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** While generally clear, the proof of the error bound (and related lemmas) is quite dense and requires a solid background in matrix analysis and optimization.  More intuitive explanations of the key steps could benefit a broader audience.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments, while helpful, are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring the impact of different parameter settings, different datasets, and potentially comparing performance against other solvers would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Reliance on Luo & Tseng Framework:** While leveraging existing results is not inherently a weakness, the paper's convergence analysis *heavily* relies on the framework from Luo & Tseng. A more self-contained derivation, even if more complex, would add to the paper’s independence.
* **Discussion of Practical Implications:** The paper doesn't delve deeply into the practical implications of the linear convergence rate. How does this translate to speedups in real-world applications?  A more detailed discussion of this would be valuable.



**Addressing the Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proof appears rigorous (though detailed examination is beyond the scope of this review). The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis. The work is complete and addresses a well-defined problem.  The authors acknowledge potential limitations.
* **Clarity:**  Generally clear, but the technical details in the proof require significant mathematical maturity. Improved intuition for key steps would enhance clarity.
* **Originality:** The work presents a novel result – linear convergence without strong convexity – and a new error bound. The paper adequately references related work, though a more thorough comparison with existing convergence analyses for proximal gradient methods (even those with strong convexity assumptions) could be beneficial.
* **Significance:** The result is significant. The broader applicability of linear convergence guarantees makes this work valuable to researchers and practitioners working with trace norm regularization. The development of the error bound could also have implications beyond this specific problem.

**Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

**Pro Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant open problem in optimization and machine learning.
* Provides a novel and technically sound solution.
* Includes empirical validation to support the theoretical results.
* The result has the potential to impact a wide range of applications.

**Con Against Acceptance:**

* The technical details are dense and may be inaccessible to some readers.
* The experimental validation is somewhat limited.
* Heavily relies on existing results (Luo & Tseng).




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field. The theoretical result is significant, the technical approach is well-justified, and the presentation is generally clear. While some areas could be improved (e.g., expanding the experiments, providing more intuitive explanations), these are relatively minor weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**  **Accept**. I recommend this paper for acceptance to NIPS. It is a high-quality piece of research that addresses an important open problem and advances the state of the art.
