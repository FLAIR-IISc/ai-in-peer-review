Summary
The paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems min_X f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth, strictly convex, and with Lipschitz gradient. The main theoretical contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound of the Luo–Tseng type for this composite problem class, despite the nonpolyhedral epigraph of the nuclear norm. Leveraging this error bound, the authors show that PGM attains asymptotic linear convergence (Q-linear for the objective, R-linear for iterates) under standard step-size constraints. The technical core is a “restricted invertibility” property near the optimal solution set, obtained using spectral-structure arguments: at an optimum, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) admit simultaneous SVD with shared singular vectors, which enables a perturbation analysis to control distances to the solution set via the residual map. Short numerical experiments on matrix completion (square loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) qualitatively illustrate linear convergence in the tail.

Relation to prior work
- Linear rates for PGM without strong convexity are known under error bounds for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group LASSO) or for separable settings (Tseng & Yun 2009; Tseng 2010; Zhang–Jiang–Luo 2013). The present work targets the nuclear norm (nonpolyhedral), where such error bounds have been open.
- The convergence proof follows the Luo–Tseng framework: establish an error bound relating distance to the solution set and a computable residual, then derive linear rates.
- There is related literature giving linear rates for low-rank problems under restricted strong convexity/identifiability assumptions on A (e.g., Negahban–Wainwright-style RSC) and KL-based analyses (Attouch–Bolte–Svaiter; Bolte et al.), which the paper does not discuss.

Strengths
- Addresses a real theoretical gap: a Luo–Tseng-type error bound for the nuclear-norm regularized class with general linear operators A, without assuming strong convexity of f.
- Technical novelty: the simultaneous SVD characterization at the optimum and the ensuing perturbation argument for “restricted invertibility” are interesting and potentially reusable.
- Assumptions on h cover square and logistic losses, and A need not be injective, so the results apply to common ML models (matrix completion/classification).
- The convergence statement is clean and standard within the Luo–Tseng framework, and matches observed linear tails in practice.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The linear rate is only asymptotic and local: the error bound holds when the residual is below ε and within a fixed level set. There is no characterization of how/when iterates enter this region, nor computable constants (η, ε), limiting practical guidance.
- Step-size conditions in Theorem 3.2 require αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) with ᾱ<1/Lf. In experiments, a fixed α=1 is used for matrix completion; for the usual sampling operator, Lf=1, so α=1 violates the assumption. For logistic loss, backtracking is used but the theory still assumes a uniform ᾱ<1/Lf. This mismatch should be reconciled (e.g., by proving the same result for standard backtracking rules with an upper cap).
- Relation to broader theory is incomplete. The paper should discuss whether KL-based analyses (many convex semi-algebraic objectives, including nuclear norm, enjoy the KL property with exponent 1/2 at nondegenerate minimizers) would also imply linear rates and how the present error bound compares (stronger/weaker, different constants/assumptions). Likewise, how does this compare to linear rates under RSC for low-rank recovery?
- The “restricted invertibility” inequality ||X−X̄||F ≤ κ||A(X−X̄)|| is strong given arbitrary A with nullspace. The proof outline is intricate; much is deferred to the supplement. Given the centrality of Proposition 4.2/4.3, more intuition and checks (e.g., edge cases with rank deficiency or multiplicities in singular values) would improve confidence.
- Experiments are minimal: small-scale synthetic problems, no comparison to established baselines (accelerated PGM with/without restart, Frank–Wolfe/CG, proximal quasi-Newton), no study of dependence on conditioning or size, and no test of the neighborhood size where linearity sets in. Plots are qualitative and lack error bars or rigorous slope estimates.
- Minor clarity/consistency issues: τ vs μ in experiments; several references lack years; figures lack axis labels/units; the assumption “h strongly convex on any convex compact subset” should be clarified (it is automatic for C^2 strictly convex with continuous positive-definite Hessian; otherwise, give sufficient conditions and typical constants).

Quality
Technically ambitious with a nontrivial proof strategy. However, key arguments are condensed and deferred. The theoretical contribution appears sound but would benefit from tighter integration with existing general frameworks (KL, RSC) and from clarifying the step-size and neighborhood issues.

Clarity
Generally well organized and readable for an expert. Some notational slips and missing details in main text; figures and experimental section need polishing. A brief intuitive explanation of why simultaneous SVD enables the error bound would help.

Originality
Moderately high: error bounds for nuclear norm under this general setting are, to my knowledge, not standard. However, the linear convergence conclusion itself follows a well-trodden path once the error bound is in place.

Significance
The result explains and formalizes observed linear tails of PGM for nuclear-norm problems without strong convexity and could influence subsequent analyses for other spectral regularizers. Practical impact is limited by locality and lack of quantitative constants.

Suggestions for improvement
- Reconcile step-size assumptions with backtracking; provide a theorem covering standard backtracking rules or clarify how to enforce ᾱ<1/Lf in practice.
- Discuss KL-based linear convergence and RSC-based results; position your assumptions and conclusions relative to these.
- Provide intuition and a sanity check example for Proposition 4.2/4.3; handle multiplicities carefully.
- Quantify or estimate η, ε (even conservatively) for common losses/operators, or give a diagnostic to detect entry into the linear regime.
- Expand experiments: larger scales, comparisons to baselines, and empirical estimation of convergence factors vs problem parameters.

Pros
- Addresses an open theoretical question for nuclear-norm regularization.
- Broad applicability to common ML losses with arbitrary linear A.
- Clear asymptotic linear-rate statement via a standard framework.

Cons
- Local result with nonconstructive constants; limited practical guidance.
- Step-size mismatch with experiments; lack of backtracking theory.
- Sparse empirical validation; no comparisons.
- Limited discussion of related general convergence frameworks.

Recommendation: Borderline reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting but, given NIPS standards, the empirical section is too weak, the assumptions/relations to broader theory need clearer positioning, and practical implications are underdeveloped. Strengthening the above points would make this a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
