## NIPS Review: On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form min<sub>X</sub> F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||<sub>*</sub>.  The authors establish *linear* convergence of PGM under relatively weak assumptions on the loss function *f*, specifically that *f(X) = h(A(X))*, where *A* is a linear operator and *h* is strictly convex with suitable smoothness and curvature properties. This is a significant result as it doesn’t require strong convexity of *f* itself, a condition often absent in practical applications like matrix completion.  The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.  The paper strengthens the theoretical foundation for using PGM in a wider range of applications.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:**  Trace norm regularization is a fundamental technique in low-rank matrix recovery and numerous applications. Understanding its convergence properties is crucial.
* **Significant Result:** Establishing linear convergence *without* strong convexity of the loss function is a substantial advance.  Existing results typically rely on strong convexity, which limits their applicability.
* **Novel Technical Contribution:** The Lipschitzian error bound is a key innovation. The authors convincingly articulate why existing error bound techniques from ℓ1-regularization don't directly transfer and cleverly leverage matrix perturbation theory to address the unique challenges posed by the trace norm.
* **Broad Applicability:** The result applies to a wide range of loss functions commonly encountered in machine learning (square loss, logistic loss, etc.).
* **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. The introduction motivates the problem effectively, and the technical sections are logically presented.
* **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provides supportive evidence for the theoretical findings.
* **Thorough Related Work:**  The paper demonstrates a good awareness of existing literature on proximal gradient methods, error bounds, and trace norm regularization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity:** While logically structured, the proof is dense and requires significant mathematical background to fully grasp.  A more detailed breakdown of some of the key lemmas and propositions in the supplementary material might improve accessibility.
* **Experimental Scope:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited. While they demonstrate linear convergence, exploring different parameter settings, problem sizes, and the impact of the step size choice more systematically would strengthen the empirical validation.  A comparison to other algorithms (e.g., accelerated gradient methods) would also be valuable.
* **Assumptions on *h*:**  Assumption 2.1(b) requires strong convexity of *h* on *compact* subsets of its domain.  While reasonable, a discussion of the implications if this condition is slightly relaxed, or how it relates to the choice of *h* in specific applications, could be beneficial.
* **Practical Step Size Selection:** While the theoretical result assumes a specific step size constraint (0 < α < α<sub>k</sub> < ᾱ < 1/Lf), the paper doesn't provide clear guidance on how to determine the Lipschitz constant *L<sub>f</sub>* in practice. The matrix classification experiment mentions backtracking line search, but more detail here would be useful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality (9/10):** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported.  The error bound is a significant theoretical result. The use of matrix perturbation theory is clever and appropriate. The only minor deduction is due to the density of the proof.
* **Clarity (8/10):**  The paper is generally well-written, but the mathematical notation can be challenging.  A bit more exposition in the proof sections would enhance clarity.
* **Originality (9/10):** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The error bound is also a significant original contribution.
* **Significance (9/10):** The result has the potential to impact a wide range of applications that rely on trace norm regularization.  The broader applicability of PGM due to this result is valuable.  

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Establishes a fundamental theoretical result on the convergence of PGM for a widely used regularization technique.
* Overcomes a significant limitation of existing theory by removing the strong convexity requirement.
* Contains a novel technical contribution (Lipschitzian error bound) that could be valuable in other contexts.
* Supported by empirical evidence.

**Against:**

* Proofs are dense and potentially inaccessible to some readers.
* Experimental validation could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the theory of trace norm regularization and optimization. The strengths of the paper far outweigh the minor weaknesses.  I believe it will be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
