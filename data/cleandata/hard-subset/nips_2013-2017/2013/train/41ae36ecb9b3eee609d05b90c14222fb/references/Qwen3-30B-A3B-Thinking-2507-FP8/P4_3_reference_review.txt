

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization for matrix completion and related problems: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) can achieve linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring the loss function to be strongly convex. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables them to prove linear convergence of the PGM under a general class of loss functions f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties. This resolves an open question in the literature, as prior error bounds for composite optimization with non-smooth regularizers (e.g., for ℓ1-norm) relied on polyhedral epigraphs, which do not apply to trace norm regularization.

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. The key technical innovation is the use of matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimal solution set, overcoming the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. This approach differs fundamentally from previous work on error bounds for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization, which relied on variable splitting. The authors prove that for problems where f(X) = h(A(X)) with h satisfying Assumptions 2.1-2.2, the PGM converges R-linearly to the optimal solution set.

The paper is technically sound and rigorously developed. The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is carefully constructed, with Lemma 4.1 establishing the critical restricted invertibility property (16) through a novel decomposition of the matrix Q̄ (Proposition 4.3). The linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) is then derived by applying the Luo-Tseng framework to the error bound. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems validate the theoretical findings, showing linear convergence in practice for both square and logistic loss functions.

Strengths:
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question about linear convergence for trace norm regularization without strong convexity assumptions.
- The proof technique using matrix perturbation theory is novel and addresses the specific challenges of trace norm regularization.
- The paper provides clear theoretical results with well-defined assumptions, making the contribution precise and verifiable.
- The numerical experiments complement the theory by demonstrating linear convergence in practical settings.
- The writing is clear, well-organized, and mathematically rigorous, with sufficient detail for experts to follow the proofs.

Weaknesses:
- The paper does not explicitly compare the computational cost of the PGM with the step size selection strategy (backtracking line search) used in the numerical experiments with other first-order methods for trace norm regularization.
- While the theoretical results are strong, the paper does not quantify the practical improvement in convergence rates compared to the standard O(1/k) sublinear rate, which would help practitioners understand the magnitude of the improvement.
- The discussion of related work could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting the error bound technique with prior approaches for other non-polyhedral regularizers.

Originality: The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature. Prior to this work, linear convergence for PGM in trace norm regularization was only known for strongly convex f [16], while sublinear rates were established for general convex f [19]. The authors' error bound technique is novel for trace norm regularization, as the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm prevents direct application of existing error bound methods [22, 23, 25]. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from previous work by explicitly stating this open question and resolving it.

Significance: The results are highly significant for machine learning applications where trace norm regularization is commonly used (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). Many practical loss functions (square loss, logistic loss) fall into the authors' framework but are not strongly convex. The paper provides a stronger theoretical foundation for using PGM in these settings, potentially enabling faster optimization in practice. The error bound itself may be useful for analyzing other first-order methods for trace norm regularization.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The authors are careful to address the limitations of their approach (e.g., the requirement that f(X) = h(A(X)) rather than general convex f). The numerical experiments provide empirical validation of the theoretical results.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical exposition is clear, with appropriate definitions and logical flow from problem setup to main results. The supplementary material provides additional details for the proofs, which is helpful for expert readers.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization for trace norm regularization. The resolution of the open question about linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions is both novel and impactful for the machine learning community. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the substantial theoretical contribution.

Pro: Clear resolution of an open question in trace norm optimization; novel proof technique using matrix perturbation theory; strong theoretical results with rigorous proofs; empirical validation of theoretical claims.

Con: Limited discussion of computational cost of step size selection; no quantification of practical convergence rate improvement; opportunity for more explicit comparison with alternative optimization methods.

This paper should be accepted as it makes a substantial contribution to the theory of convex optimization with applications to machine learning. The results are both novel and significant, and the paper is of high technical quality.
