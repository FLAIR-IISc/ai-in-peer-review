- Main result: Proves linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity, for losses f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth.

- Key technical contribution: Establishes a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization (Theorem 3.1), resolving an open question in the literature.

- Proof technique: Novel use of matrix perturbation theory and analysis of spectral structure (Lemma 4.1, Proposition 4.2) rather than polyhedral splitting used for ℓ₁-type problems.

- Theoretical framework: Builds on Luo–Tseng convergence analysis and carefully states applicable assumptions (Assumptions 2.1–2.2).

- Applicability: Results apply to common ML tasks like matrix completion and matrix classification where f has the form h(A(X)).

- Empirical validation: Numerical experiments (Sections 5, Figures 1–2) show linear convergence in practice for the tested problems.

- Originality: First linear-convergence result for trace-norm regularization that avoids strong convexity; extends error-bound techniques to non-polyhedral regularizers.

- Significance: Removes a major barrier for first-order methods in low-rank matrix problems and suggests broader utility of the error-bound approach.

- Clarity and presentation: Well-written, well-organized, and balances technical depth with readability.

- Weaknesses: Restriction to losses of the form h(A(X)) and limited empirical scope (only two applications) limit generality and practical validation.

- Recommendation: Strong accept (NIPS) — technically sound, original, significant, and likely to stimulate further research.
