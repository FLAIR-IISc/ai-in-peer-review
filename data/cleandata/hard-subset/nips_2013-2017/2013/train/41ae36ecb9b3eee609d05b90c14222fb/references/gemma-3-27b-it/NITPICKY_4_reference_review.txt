This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization, specifically aiming to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The problem setting is well-motivated, with numerous applications cited. The authors demonstrate that under certain conditions on the loss function (f(X) = h(A(X)) where h is strictly convex), linear convergence can be achieved. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to overcome challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** Establishing faster convergence rates for PGM, especially without strong convexity assumptions, is a significant contribution. The widespread use of trace norm regularization makes this result potentially impactful.
* **Novelty:** The Lipschitzian error bound is a key novel element, addressing a previously open question. The use of matrix perturbation theory in this context appears insightful.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of existing work on PGM, trace norm regularization, and related error bounds.
* **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivation. The theoretical framework is generally well-structured.
* **Numerical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments strengthens the theoretical findings and demonstrates practical applicability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Excessive Technicality & Density:** While rigor is appreciated, the presentation is *extremely* dense and, at times, obscures the main ideas. The notation is heavy, and the flow could be improved. Several sentences are unnecessarily complex and could be simplified.
* **Lack of Intuition:** The “crucial observation” regarding the shared singular vectors of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) (Proposition 4.2) is presented without sufficient intuitive explanation. Why is this observation important *before* stating it is crucial? The reader is left to infer its significance.
* **Step Size Assumptions:** Theorem 3.2 requires a rather restrictive condition on the step size (0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf). This isn’t unreasonable, but the practical implications aren't discussed. Is this condition easily satisfied in practice? What guidance can be provided for selecting appropriate αk?
* **Numerical Experiment Details:** The numerical experiments lack crucial details. For matrix completion, what is the rank of the ground truth matrix? How is the sampling performed? What specific algorithm is used for solving the subproblems? For matrix classification, details regarding the Wishart matrix generation are missing. The choice of parameters (e.g., µ = 1) is not justified. The plots should include error bars.
* **Minor Technical Issues:**
    * In Section 1, the sentence "Recall that the trace norm of a matrix is defined as the sum of its singular values." is oddly placed and interrupts the flow.
    * In Assumption 2.1(b), specifying “strictly convex” is redundant given “continuously differentiable with Lipschitz–continuous gradient."
    * Equation (21) defines γk as a norm, but it is used later as a scalar value in expressions. This requires careful justification.
    * The phrase "Despite its apparent similarity to ℓ1–norm regularization" in Section 1 feels unsubstantiated.
* **Proof Organization:** The lengthy proof of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 4.1 are relegated to the supplementary material, which, while acceptable, hinders accessibility. A more condensed overview of the key ideas in the main body would be beneficial. The frequent references to “supplementary material” disrupt the reading experience.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant theoretical question with a positive result.
* The novelty of the Lipschitzian error bound is substantial.
* The numerical experiments provide some support for the theory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The extremely dense and technical writing style makes the paper difficult to follow.
* The lack of intuition and justification for key claims weakens the overall impact.
* The limited details regarding the numerical experiments reduce confidence in the practical relevance of the results.
* Numerous minor technical issues and stylistic flaws detract from the overall quality of the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential significance of the results, I am inclined to **reject** this paper. While the technical contributions are valuable, the paper suffers from significant clarity and presentation issues. The density of the writing, lack of intuitive explanations, and insufficient details in the numerical experiments outweigh the strengths. A major rewrite, focusing on clarity and providing more context and justification for the key ideas, is needed to make this work accessible and convincing to a broader audience. The paper feels like it's written *for* experts, rather than *explaining* complex ideas to other knowledgeable researchers.
