- Summary: Proves linear convergence of Proximal Gradient Method for trace-norm regularized problems under weak assumptions (f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strictly convex/smooth), via a novel Lipschitzian error bound using matrix perturbation theory.  
- Important problem: Addresses convergence for widely used trace-norm regularization in low-rank recovery.  
- Significant result: Establishes linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f.  
- Novel technical contribution: Develops a tailored Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm overcoming non-polyhedral challenges.  
- Broad applicability: Results cover common losses (square, logistic, etc.).  
- Presentation: Paper is well-written, structured, and motivates the problem effectively.  
- Empirical validation: Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification support theoretical claims.  
- Related work: Thorough literature awareness on PGM, error bounds, and trace-norm methods.  
- Proof complexity: Proofs are dense and require substantial mathematical background; supplementary breakdown would help.  
- Experimental scope: Experiments are limited; need wider parameter sweeps, sizes, step-size studies, and comparisons to other algorithms.  
- Assumptions on h: Assumption of strong convexity on compact subsets needs discussion regarding relaxation and practical implications.  
- Step-size guidance: Paper lacks practical guidance for determining Lf and step-size selection beyond mentioning backtracking.  
- Quality: 9/10 — technically sound with a strong error-bound contribution.  
- Clarity: 8/10 — generally clear but mathematically dense in places.  
- Originality: 9/10 — novel linear convergence result without strong convexity.  
- Significance: 9/10 — potentially impactful for many trace-norm applications.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Fundamental theoretical advance, removes strong convexity requirement, introduces reusable error-bound technique, and includes supporting experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Proof density and limited experimental validation.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong Accept.
