

# Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses a fundamental theoretical question: under what conditions can the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieve linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors establish a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables them to prove linear convergence for a broad class of loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties.

## Strengths

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question about whether a Lipschitzian error bound holds for trace norm regularization. Unlike the ℓ₁-norm case (where such bounds are known), the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, making the analysis substantially more challenging. The key insight (Proposition 4.2) that optimal solutions X* and -∇f(X*) share the same singular vectors is elegant and crucial for their analysis.

The technical approach is sound, with a careful development of the error bound using matrix perturbation theory. The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) (restricted invertibility) is particularly impressive, overcoming the main difficulty that the trace norm depends on all matrix entries simultaneously. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide empirical support for the linear convergence claim.

## Weaknesses and Concerns

1. **Restricted Assumptions**: The paper's key result requires f(X) = h(A(X)), which covers square loss and logistic loss but excludes many common loss functions like hinge loss. The authors should explicitly discuss the limitations of their framework and whether their techniques can be extended to more general loss functions.

2. **Vague Error Bound Conditions**: The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is stated to hold for F(X) ≤ ζ, but the paper doesn't clarify how ζ relates to problem parameters. For practical implementation, it's crucial to know the relationship between the objective value and the constant η. A more explicit characterization would strengthen the result.

3. **Limited Related Work Context**: While the paper correctly cites the need for a trace norm error bound, it doesn't adequately contextualize their contribution relative to recent advances in non-strongly convex optimization. The work of Zhang et al. (NIPS 2013) on "On the Linear Convergence of a Proximal Gradient Method for a Class of Nonsmooth Convex Minimization Problems" and Tseng's work on error bounds should be more thoroughly discussed.

4. **Theoretical vs. Practical Gap**: The paper establishes asymptotic linear convergence, but doesn't address the practical implications of the constants in the convergence rate. The numerical experiments show linear convergence, but the paper doesn't quantify the "large enough" threshold for the residual norm where linear convergence kicks in, which is important for practitioners.

5. **Proof Clarity**: The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) is technically dense. While the mathematics is sound, the argument could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why the restricted invertibility property holds, particularly why the decomposition in Proposition 4.3 leads to the desired contradiction.

## Significance and Originality

The paper's main contribution is a novel error bound for trace norm regularization, which is indeed a significant theoretical advance. This resolves an open question that has been mentioned in several prior works (e.g., [18] references the non-polyhedral epigraph issue). The result extends beyond previous work that required strong convexity, making it applicable to many important machine learning problems like matrix completion where strong convexity fails.

However, the originality is somewhat limited by the fact that the error bound approach itself is not new (Luo and Tseng [10] developed the framework), but the authors successfully adapt it to the trace norm case. The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how their error bound differs from those for ℓ₁-norm regularization.

## Recommendation

This is a technically strong paper with a novel theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The main limitation is the restrictive assumption on the loss function structure. While the results are valuable, they are applicable to a narrower class of problems than the title suggests.

I recommend **acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:
1. Clarify the relationship between ζ and problem parameters in Theorem 3.1
2. Discuss the limitations of the f(X) = h(A(X)) assumption more thoroughly
3. Expand the related work section to include recent advances in non-strongly convex optimization
4. Provide more explicit discussion of the practical implications of the constants in the convergence rate

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of trace norm regularization, and the error bound they establish is likely to be valuable for future work in matrix optimization. The numerical experiments effectively support the theoretical claims, though additional experiments on more diverse problem instances would strengthen the practical relevance.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, the paper establishes a fundamental result that will likely be cited in future work on matrix optimization.
