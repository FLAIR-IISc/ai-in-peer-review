

# Review: On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization

This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm-regularized optimization problems. The authors establish a linear convergence rate for the PGM when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with appropriate smoothness properties. This is significant because previous linear convergence results for PGM in this context required the loss function to be strongly convex, which is not satisfied in many machine learning applications like matrix completion. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, resolving an open question that had remained unanswered despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. It builds upon Luo and Tseng's framework for error bounds [10] and addresses a gap in the literature regarding trace norm regularization. Previous works on error bounds for composite minimization typically focus on cases with polyhedral epigraphs (such as ℓ1-norm) [22, 23, 25], but the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, making the analysis fundamentally different. The authors correctly identify this gap and provide a novel solution through their key insight about the singular vector structure of optimal solutions.

The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors establish a rigorous proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which is the main contribution of the paper. They carefully address the challenges of trace norm regularization by making the crucial observation that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors (Proposition 4.2). The proof is logically structured, with Lemma 4.1 establishing the key properties needed for Theorem 3.1. The linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) is derived as a straightforward application of the error bound to the Luo-Tseng framework. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems provide empirical validation of the theoretical results, showing linear convergence in practice.

The paper excels in originality. The authors explicitly state that their linear convergence result is the first for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a meaningful advance over previous work, as the authors demonstrate that their conditions cover many practical loss functions used in machine learning (square loss, logistic loss, etc.) where strong convexity does not hold. The development of the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization represents a significant technical contribution that could be of independent interest beyond the specific problem considered.

The significance of this work is substantial. The trace norm regularization problem appears in numerous machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification, etc.), and the linear convergence rate established here is superior to the sublinear rates previously known. The authors provide clear justification for their assumptions through examples from matrix completion and multi-task learning. The paper advances the state of the art in optimization for machine learning by providing a more general convergence guarantee for an important class of problems.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and resolution of an important open problem in the literature
- Rigorous theoretical development with a well-structured proof
- Appropriate numerical experiments supporting the theoretical claims
- Careful justification of assumptions through practical examples
- Clear contribution statement that precisely identifies how this work differs from previous results

Weaknesses:
- The numerical experiments could have included a comparison with alternative optimization methods to better demonstrate the practical advantages of the linear convergence rate.
- The paper does not discuss the sensitivity of the convergence rate to the choice of step size parameters (α, ᾱ), which is important for practical implementation.
- The paper could have provided more discussion about the computational cost of the proximal operator (though this is somewhat standard knowledge in the field).
- The proof of Proposition 4.3 is referenced to supplementary material, which may make the main paper less self-contained.

Overall, this paper makes a significant technical contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The main result (Theorem 3.2) is novel, technically sound, and addresses an important open question. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides a clear explanation of the key insights. The numerical experiments effectively support the theoretical claims. The work has clear significance for practitioners working with trace norm regularization, as it provides a more general convergence guarantee that applies to a wider range of loss functions than previous results.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound, clearly written, novel, and significant. The authors have made a genuine contribution to the field by resolving an open problem that had remained unanswered despite the apparent similarity to other well-studied cases. This work will be valuable to researchers working on optimization for machine learning problems involving matrix regularization.

Pro: Resolves a significant open question in optimization for machine learning; provides the first linear convergence result for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function; technically sound with rigorous proofs; well-organized and clearly written.

Con: Numerical experiments could have included comparisons with alternative methods; some details of the proof are referenced to supplementary material; does not discuss sensitivity to step size parameters.
