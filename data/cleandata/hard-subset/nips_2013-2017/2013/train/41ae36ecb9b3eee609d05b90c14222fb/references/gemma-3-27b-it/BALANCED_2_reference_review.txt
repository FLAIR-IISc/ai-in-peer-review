This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically when the loss function has a particular structure: f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is a strictly convex function. The authors establish linear convergence under these conditions, a significant result as it relaxes the strong convexity requirement typically needed for linear convergence of PGM. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and addresses an important open question in the field.

**Summary of Related Work:** The introduction effectively contextualizes the problem within a broad range of applications and summarizes existing work on trace norm regularization and PGM. The authors accurately highlight the limitations of prior linear convergence results, which typically require strong convexity of the loss function. The referencing is thorough and relevant, citing key works in optimization, matrix completion, and related areas. The connection to previous error bound results for ℓ1-norm and group LASSO regularization is also well-articulated, highlighting the novelty of addressing the challenges posed by the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The main contribution – establishing linear convergence without strong convexity – is significant and fills a gap in the existing literature. This broadens the applicability of PGM to a wider class of problems.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proof strategy, based on a new Lipschitzian error bound, appears rigorous and well-motivated. The careful analysis using matrix perturbation theory to address the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm is commendable.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the core ideas are clearly explained. The assumptions are stated explicitly, and their relevance to the applications discussed is highlighted.
* **Completeness:** The paper provides a comprehensive analysis, including a theoretical result (Theorem 3.1 & 3.2), its proof, and numerical experiments demonstrating the practical performance of the method.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification provide supporting evidence for the theoretical results and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the PGM.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** While the authors justify their assumptions, Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity on any convex compact subset of dom(h)) could be considered strong. While often satisfied in practice, it would be valuable to discuss the sensitivity of the results to weaker convexity assumptions.
* **Step Size Selection:** The paper mentions using a backtracking line search in the numerical experiments for the logistic loss case due to the difficulty in obtaining a good lower bound on the Lipschitz constant. A more detailed discussion of practical step size selection strategies, especially in cases where computing the Lipschitz constant is challenging, would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Proof:** While rigorous, the proof is quite dense and requires a strong background in optimization and matrix analysis. Adding more intuitive explanations or intermediate steps could make it more accessible to a broader audience.
* **Limited Numerical Scope:** While the results are promising, the numerical experiments are limited to two specific problem settings. Exploring the performance of the algorithm on a wider range of problems and datasets would strengthen the empirical validation.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with broad implications for the application of PGM to trace norm regularized problems.
* The technical analysis is rigorous and the proof strategy is well-motivated.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a comprehensive analysis.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The strong convexity assumption in Assumption 2.1(b) could limit the applicability of the results.
* The proof is complex and might be difficult for some readers to follow.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization. The theoretical result is novel and significant, the technical analysis is rigorous, and the presentation is clear and well-organized. While the strong convexity assumption and the complexity of the proof are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should consider:

* Discussing the sensitivity of the results to weaker convexity assumptions than strong convexity on any compact set.
* Providing a more detailed discussion of practical step size selection strategies.
* Potentially adding more intuitive explanations to the proof.
* Considering adding a brief discussion on the computational cost of the proposed method.
