

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the optimization of trace norm regularized problems by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound that enables proving linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The key novelty is resolving an open question about whether such an error bound exists for trace norm regularization, which has remained unresolved despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization.

The authors prove that when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties, the PGM exhibits linear convergence. This is particularly significant because many important machine learning problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning) use loss functions that fit this form but are not strongly convex. The paper's main technical contribution is the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which the authors establish through a novel approach using matrix perturbation theory and an important observation about the singular vectors of optimal solutions (Proposition 4.2).

The paper elegantly addresses a fundamental gap in the literature. Prior work on error bounds for composite minimization typically focused on polyhedral epigraphs (like ℓ1-norm) or group LASSO, but the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph presented unique challenges. The authors overcome this by recognizing that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors, enabling them to analyze the spectral structure near the optimal solution set. This insight is both elegant and powerful, resolving the open question in the affirmative.

The theoretical contribution is substantial and well-supported. The proofs are technically sound, and the authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous approaches. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provide empirical validation, showing linear convergence in practice as predicted by the theory.

This paper excels in novelty and significance, which aligns perfectly with the reviewer's priorities. The error bound itself is a valuable contribution that could be used in future work on matrix regularization. The linear convergence result significantly broadens the class of problems to which linear convergence guarantees apply, making it highly relevant to machine learning practitioners.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with the main contributions clearly articulated. The authors effectively position their work against previous literature, particularly contrasting their approach with the splitting techniques used for ℓ1-norm regularization. The writing is accessible to optimization and machine learning researchers.

The only minor limitation is that the paper focuses on the theoretical aspects without extensive discussion of how the error bound might inform practical implementation choices (e.g., adaptive step sizes). However, this is a minor point given the paper's primary contribution is theoretical.

This paper represents a substantial advance in the theory of trace norm regularization. It resolves a long-standing open question that has been unsolved despite the apparent similarity between trace norm and ℓ1-norm regularization. The authors' approach using matrix perturbation theory is elegant and novel, and their error bound has the potential to be widely applicable in future work.

Given the emphasis on novelty and bold theoretical contributions over methodological perfection, this paper is an excellent fit for NIPS. The theoretical contribution is significant, the approach is innovative, and the results have clear implications for a wide range of machine learning problems. The paper meets all the criteria for high-quality NIPS work, particularly in terms of originality and significance.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field that advances our understanding of optimization for matrix regularization problems.
