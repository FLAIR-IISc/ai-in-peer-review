Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems of the form min_X f(X) + τ||X||_* with f(X) = h(A(X)). Unlike classical linear convergence results that require strong convexity of f, the authors establish an asymptotic linear convergence rate under a weaker structure: h is continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex, and strongly convex on compact convex subsets of its domain; A is arbitrary linear. This covers widely used losses (squared, logistic) even when A is not injective so f is not strongly convex. The key technical result is a local Lipschitzian error bound: dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||S_τ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||_F for iterates in a bounded level set and small residual. They then invoke the Luo–Tseng framework to derive linear convergence of both iterates (R-linear) and objective values (Q-linear) for fixed stepsizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/L.

Prior work on PGM rates includes O(1/k) and O(1/k^2) for general convex/smooth (Beck–Teboulle; Nesterov/accelerated variants), and linear rates under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al. NIPS 2011). Error bounds are known for polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO; Tseng and coauthors) and more generally for certain separable regularizers. For the nuclear norm, the non-polyhedral epigraph has made such EB analyses nontrivial. The authors’ main novelty is an EB tailored to the nuclear norm via new spectral arguments, including a structural proposition that optimal X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors through the fixed-point/shrinkage characterization, and a restricted invertibility property enabling the EB.

Strengths:
- Technical novelty: Establishes a local Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, previously open in contrast to ℓ1/group norms. The spectral alignment argument (Proposition 4.2) and the perturbation-based decomposition (Proposition 4.3) are insightful.
- Clear scope and assumptions: The h(A(X)) setting matches common ML applications (matrix completion, matrix classification/logistic), where f is not strongly convex due to A’s nullspace.
- Solid convergence conclusion: Asymptotic linear rate for PGM with fixed stepsizes < 1/L; conditions are standard and verifiable.
- Empirical illustrations on synthetic matrix completion and classification corroborate the expected local linear behavior of objective and iterates.

Weaknesses:
- Local (asymptotic) result: The EB and hence linear rate are local, requiring bounded level sets and sufficiently small residual. While standard, the paper could be clearer about the basin of attraction and how practical step-size rules ensure entering it.
- Stepsize/backtracking: The theorem assumes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) with ᾱ < 1/L. The experiments use backtracking but the theory does not explicitly cover backtracking; a brief argument ensuring bounded αk in practice would help.
- Limited experimental validation: Small-scale, synthetic data only; no comparison with accelerated PG, restarted variants, or alternative solvers; no empirical check of the EB (residual vs. distance). Figures are under-described (y-axes units, missing figure numbering consistency) and notation in experiments (µ vs τ) drifts.
- Relation to broader theory: The paper does not discuss connections to the Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz/EB frameworks that also yield linear rates for semi-algebraic convex objectives. Positioning the contribution relative to KL-based linear rates (and why the present EB is sharper/novel for nuclear norm) would strengthen the claims.

Quality:
- The analysis appears technically sound and follows a well-established scheme (EB + Luo–Tseng). The key propositions are plausible given the shrinkage fixed-point and are carefully integrated. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; ensuring those steps (especially Proposition 4.3) are transparent would be important.
- The assumptions on h are reasonable for squared and logistic losses. Boundedness of level sets is justified via compactness of the optimal set.

Clarity:
- Overall well organized with preliminaries, algorithm, residual map, and main theorems stated clearly. Proof sketch is detailed enough to follow the main ideas. Some notation could be streamlined (consistent τ vs µ; careful figure references). Explicitly stating that the linear rate is local would improve clarity.

Originality:
- The EB for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity and the accompanying spectral/perturbation arguments are original and address an open gap relative to ℓ1/group regularizers. The overall convergence framework is standard.

Significance:
- Establishing linear convergence for a widely used class of nuclear-norm problems (beyond strong convexity) is useful for both theory and practice, informing algorithm design and stopping criteria. Impact is moderate-to-strong on the optimization-for-ML community; broader empirical validation could enhance practical significance.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and elegant error bound for a non-polyhedral regularizer.
- Extends linear convergence guarantees to important losses lacking strong convexity in X.
- Clear linkage to an established analysis framework; results likely to be cited.

Cons for acceptance:
- Local nature and practical basin-of-attraction issues not deeply explored.
- Limited experiments and lack of comparisons with accelerated/restarted PGM.
- Missing discussion of KL-based results and backtracking guarantee details.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a short section relating your EB to KL inequalities and existing linear-rate results for composite convex problems; clarify the novelty for nuclear norm.
- State conditions under which standard backtracking ensures αk bounded away from 0 and below 1/L, preserving the theorem’s conclusions.
- Provide empirical evidence on the EB (scatter of dist(X, X*) vs ||R(X)||) and compare with ℓ1 cases; include comparisons with FISTA (with/without restart) to show practical behavior.
- Clarify the dependence of constants (η, ϵ, κi) on problem data and step sizes; discuss how close one needs to be for linear convergence to manifest.
- Fix minor presentation issues: consistent notation (τ/µ), figure numbering/captions, axes labels.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution (a new EB for nuclear norm and resulting linear convergence) is novel and relevant to NeurIPS; strengthening the positioning and empirical section would make it a clear accept.
