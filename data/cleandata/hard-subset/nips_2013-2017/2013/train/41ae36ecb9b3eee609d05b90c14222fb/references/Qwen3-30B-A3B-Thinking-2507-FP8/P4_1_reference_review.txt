

This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h strictly convex with certain smoothness properties. Previous results required strong convexity of f, which doesn't hold in important applications like matrix completion. The authors' key contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1), resolving an open question about whether such a bound exists for the non-polyhedral trace norm (unlike ℓ1-norm regularization).

The paper correctly situates its contribution within prior work, noting that existing error bounds for composite minimization rely on polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ1-norm), which don't apply to trace norm. The authors develop a novel proof technique using matrix perturbation theory and the observation that optimal solutions X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), enabling analysis of spectral structure near the optimal set. This approach overcomes the challenge of the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph, which prevented standard variable-splitting techniques used for ℓ1-norm.

The technical development is rigorous and sound. The proof of Theorem 3.1 is detailed, with Lemma 4.1 establishing the crucial restricted invertibility property (16) through careful analysis of singular value decompositions. Theorem 3.2 on linear convergence follows from applying the Luo-Tseng framework with the new error bound. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification demonstrate linear convergence as predicted by the theory, with clear semi-log plots showing the linear rate once iterates approach the optimal set.

Strengths: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by establishing linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions, which is applicable to many machine learning problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). The error bound for trace norm regularization is novel and could be of independent interest. The theoretical contribution is well-supported by both rigorous proofs and empirical validation.

Weaknesses: The specific form f(X) = h(A(X)) might exclude some trace norm-regularized problems, though the authors convincingly argue it covers common cases (square loss, logistic loss). The paper doesn't compare PGM's empirical performance against other methods for trace norm regularization (e.g., accelerated methods or alternating direction methods), which would strengthen the practical significance.

Novelty: This is the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity of f. The error bound for trace norm regularization is the key novel contribution, as prior work focused on polyhedral cases.

Significance: The result has immediate practical relevance for machine learning applications involving low-rank matrix recovery. Linear convergence is a substantial improvement over the standard O(1/k) rate, which is important for large-scale problems. The error bound itself could facilitate analysis of other optimization methods for trace norm problems.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate technical depth. The motivation, proofs, and numerical results are logically presented, and the supplementary material provides necessary details for reproducibility.

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization methods for trace norm-regularized problems. The novelty of the error bound and the linear convergence result without strong convexity requirements are significant, and the paper is well-supported by both theory and experiments.

Pro-acceptance: Novel error bound for trace norm regularization; first linear convergence result without strong convexity; applicable to important machine learning problems; rigorous proofs; supported by numerical experiments.

Con-acceptance: Specific loss function form might limit applicability; lacks empirical comparison to alternative methods.

This paper is clearly within NIPS scope as it addresses theoretical convergence properties of an important optimization method for machine learning applications. The contribution represents a meaningful advance in the field, with both theoretical novelty and practical significance.
