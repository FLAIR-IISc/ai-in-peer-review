Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is strictly convex, C1 with Lipschitz gradient and locally strongly convex on compact subsets of its domain. The authors show that the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) with stepsizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf) enjoys asymptotic linear convergence: objective values converge Q-linearly and iterates R-linearly to the optimal set, even though f need not be strongly convex (because A may be rank-deficient). The key technical contribution is a local Lipschitzian error bound of Luo–Tseng type: dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F for all X in a bounded level set with sufficiently small proximal residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Establishing this error bound for nuclear-norm regularization was open; prior error bounds handled polyhedral nonsmooth parts (ℓ1, group lasso). The proof hinges on a structural property: at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors, enabling a perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” estimate. Numerical experiments on matrix completion (square loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrate asymptotic linear behavior.

This work builds on and extends the Luo–Tseng framework (Ann. OR, 1994) and related error bounds for structured convex problems (e.g., Tseng and Yun, 2009; Zhang–Jiang–Luo, 2013). It complements acceleration-based sublinear guarantees for proximal methods (Beck–Teboulle; Ji–Ye; Toh–Yun) and differs from strong-convexity-based linear rates by covering important ML losses where f is not strongly convex due to A. It is also related in spirit to KL-based linear convergence results, but the paper provides a tailored error bound for the nuclear norm.

Strengths
- Clear, focused theoretical contribution: a new local error bound for nuclear-norm regularization and its consequence—linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f.
- Broad applicability: covers widely used losses (square, logistic) and general linear operators A.
- Technically nontrivial insight: simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) and careful perturbation arguments to overcome non-polyhedrality of the nuclear norm.
- Solid integration with established frameworks (Luo–Tseng), producing clean convergence theorems.
- Simple, relevant experiments illustrate the asymptotic linear regime on two canonical tasks.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope of linear rate is local/asymptotic and requires bounded level sets and small residuals; the constants (η, ε, rates) are not quantified or easily estimable, limiting practical guidance.
- Experimental section is minimal. No comparisons to accelerated PGM or alternative solvers, no wall-clock/runtime, and limited discussion of how “solution error” is measured (e.g., distance to the optimal set, to ground truth, or relative to KKT residual).
- Assumption on stepsizes (bounded away from 0 and < 1/Lf): in practice, backtracking is used, but the paper should clarify that standard backtracking ensures a positive lower bound on αk (or specify conditions under which it does), to make the theorem directly applicable.
- Relation to KL-based linear convergence for composite convex problems (Attouch–Bolte and subsequent work) is not discussed. The authors should explain whether their error bound implies a KL exponent of 1/2, or how their result compares/contrasts with KL-based linear rates for semi-algebraic objectives that include the nuclear norm.
- Some key technical statements are deferred to the supplement (e.g., Proposition 4.3, inequalities (9)–(11)), making it harder to assess completeness within the main text.

Quality
- The theoretical claims are well-motivated and appear sound given the structure of the proofs. The main novelty—Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm—is carefully argued via spectral structure and perturbation. The convergence theorem follows standard lines once the error bound is in place.
- Proof details of auxiliary propositions are relegated to the supplement; bringing concise versions of the most novel steps (e.g., proof sketch of Proposition 4.3) into the main text would strengthen the paper.
- Experiments are correct in spirit but light; adding quantitative rate estimates, runtime, and comparisons would improve empirical support.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-written and organized. The setup, assumptions, and algorithm are clearly presented. The residual map and its role are well explained.
- Minor notation inconsistencies: τ vs μ for regularization in experiments; proxτ vs proxταk; clarify Lf in terms of L and ‖A‖ (Lf = L‖A‖2).
- Please define precisely how “error in solution” is computed in figures and whether the distance to X* is estimated or computed via ground truth.

Originality
- Establishing a Luo–Tseng-type error bound for nuclear-norm regularization is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The simultaneous SVD observation at optimality is known at a high level via subdifferential characterizations, but its deployment here to prove a quantitative error bound is novel.
- The convergence theorem per se is a consequence of the error bound plus standard machinery, as acknowledged.

Significance
- The result improves theoretical understanding of a widely used method (PGM) in important applications (matrix completion, multitask/classification with low-rank structure), under realistic non-strongly convex settings. It may inform the analysis of other first-order methods and regularizers.
- Practical impact is moderate in the short term due to local nature and unknown constants, but the conceptual contribution is valuable.

Questions for the authors
- Can you comment on the relationship between your error bound and the KL property? Does your setting imply a KL exponent of 1/2, yielding linear convergence via KL analysis as well?
- Under standard Armijo/backtracking rules, can you guarantee αk ≥ αmin > 0, ensuring your stepsize assumption?
- Could your approach extend to Schatten p-norms (p ≥ 1) or other spectral regularizers (e.g., spectral k-support), and what obstacles arise?
- In the classification experiment, how is “solution error” computed without access to X*? Is it KKT residual or distance to an estimated limit?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and technically interesting error bound for nuclear-norm regularization.
- Fills a gap: linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity in a widely used setting.
- Clear exposition and applicability to common ML losses.

Cons against acceptance
- Empirical validation is minimal; no quantitative rate constants or runtime comparisons.
- Limited discussion of connections to KL-based linear rates and related contemporary theory.
- Asymptotic/local nature; practical guidance on when the linear regime starts is not provided.

Minor comments/typos
- Ensure consistent notation for the regularization parameter (τ vs μ).
- Specify Lf explicitly as L‖A‖2.
- Some equation references and braces appear mismatched in the draft; a careful proofread is recommended.

Overall recommendation: accept (borderline to clear accept) for a solid theoretical contribution that resolves a natural open question with relevance to ML optimization. Confidence: moderate.
