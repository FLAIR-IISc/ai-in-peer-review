### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. This formulation is prevalent in many machine learning applications, including matrix completion and multi-task learning. While it is well-known that PGM exhibits sublinear convergence for general smooth convex `f` and linear convergence for strongly convex `f`, many practical loss functions (e.g., the square loss `‖A(X)−b‖^2` with a non-injective operator `A`) are not strongly convex. This paper bridges this gap by proving that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence under a more general structural assumption: that the loss function has the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function.

The core of the paper's contribution is the derivation of a novel local Lipschitzian error bound for this class of trace norm regularized problems. This bound establishes that, for points sufficiently close to the optimal set `X*`, the distance to the set, `dist(X, X*)`, is bounded by a constant multiple of the norm of a residual map, `‖R(X)‖F`, which measures the violation of the first-order optimality condition. The authors note that proving such an error bound for the trace norm is challenging because, unlike the `ℓ1`-norm or group LASSO penalties, its epigraph is non-polyhedral and its structure is not separable. To overcome this, the authors introduce a novel analysis technique based on matrix perturbation theory. The key insight is that an optimal solution `X*` and the negative gradient at that point, `-∇f(X*)`, share the same singular vectors. This structural property allows for a detailed analysis of the spectral properties of points near the optimum, leading to the desired error bound. Building on this result and the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993), the paper proves the R-linear convergence of the PGM iterates and the Q-linear convergence of the objective function values. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks, which demonstrate the predicted linear convergence behavior.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous, albeit complex, theoretical analysis. The assumptions on the objective function are clearly stated and are shown to be satisfied by important problems in machine learning. The authors are careful in their presentation, building the argument logically from the novel error bound to the final convergence result. The proof strategy, while drawing inspiration from prior work on error bounds, is adapted in a non-trivial way to handle the specific challenges of the trace norm. The inclusion of numerical experiments provides empirical validation for the theoretical claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work within the existing literature and highlighting the specific open question it addresses. The structure of the paper is logical, with the main results presented upfront in Section 3, followed by a detailed sketch of the main proof in Section 4. The authors do a good job of explaining why existing techniques for `ℓ1`-norm regularization do not apply and what new ideas are needed. While the proof itself is highly technical and dense, this is inherent to the topic. The overall narrative and the significance of each step are communicated effectively.

**Originality:** The primary contribution—the establishment of a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization under the `h(A(X))` structural assumption—is novel and significant. The paper convincingly argues that this problem was open and that its solution requires new analytical tools beyond those used for `ℓ1`-type regularizers. The use of matrix perturbation theory and the exploitation of the shared singular vector structure (Proposition 4.2) is a creative and original approach to the problem. While the subsequent application of the Luo and Tseng framework to prove linear convergence is a known technique, the originality lies in enabling this framework by proving the key error bound, which is the main technical hurdle. The related work is adequately cited and distinguished.

**Significance:** The results are of high significance to both the optimization and machine learning communities. Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone of low-rank matrix recovery, and the Proximal Gradient Method is a workhorse algorithm for solving the resulting optimization problems. This work provides a formal theoretical justification for the fast convergence of PGM that is often observed in practice, even when the strong convexity assumption does not hold. This improved theoretical understanding is valuable and could guide future algorithm design and analysis. The techniques developed to prove the error bound may also be of independent interest and could potentially be extended to other matrix norm regularization problems. Researchers and practitioners working on large-scale matrix problems are likely to use and build upon these ideas.

A minor weakness is that the convergence result is asymptotic in nature, and the analysis does not provide explicit rates or a characterization of the region where linear convergence begins. However, this is a common feature of analyses based on local error bounds and does not detract significantly from the paper's core contribution.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Solves an important open problem:** Establishes linear convergence of PGM for a broad and practical class of trace norm regularized problems that lack strong convexity.
2.  **Novel technical contribution:** Introduces a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, with a proof technique based on matrix spectral analysis that is original and of independent interest.
3.  **High potential impact:** The result provides a stronger theoretical foundation for a widely used algorithm on a fundamental class of problems in machine learning.
4.  **Excellent presentation:** The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and rigorously executed.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
1.  **Technical density:** The proof of the error bound is highly technical, which may limit its accessibility. Key details are deferred to supplementary material.
2.  **Asymptotic nature of the result:** The analysis guarantees linear convergence locally but does not characterize the pre-asymptotic behavior or the size of the constants involved.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant, novel, and technically sound contribution to an important area of research. The strengths substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses.
