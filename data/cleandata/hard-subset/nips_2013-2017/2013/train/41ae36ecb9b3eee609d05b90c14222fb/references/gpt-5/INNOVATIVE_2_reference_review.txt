Summary
This paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm–regularized problems of the form min_X f(X)+τ‖X‖*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), with A linear and h strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient and locally strongly convex on compact sets. The main technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear-norm–regularized objective: in a bounded level set and near optimality, dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X‖F, where Sτ is the singular-value shrinkage operator and X* is the optimal set. Leveraging this error bound, and the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors prove asymptotic linear convergence of PGM without requiring strong convexity of f (which generally fails when A is not injective, e.g., matrix completion or logistic models with linear features). The key new idea is a spectral structure result: at an optimal solution X̄, the matrices X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), enabling matrix perturbation arguments and a restricted invertibility property that underpins the error bound. The paper includes small synthetic experiments for matrix completion (squared loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) indicating linear convergence near the optimum.

Relation to prior work
PGM is known to achieve O(1/k) (and O(1/k^2) with acceleration) for smooth+convex composite problems; linear rates typically require strong convexity of f (e.g., [Schmidt et al. 2011]). Error bounds enabling linear rates exist for ℓ1 and group LASSO penalties with polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., Tseng and coauthors), but whether analogous results hold for the nuclear norm (non-polyhedral epigraph) has been open. This paper addresses exactly that gap by proving a local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm and deriving linear rates for PGM under broad composite losses (including square and logistic). The analysis is in the spirit of Luo–Tseng error bounds but requires new spectral tools unique to the trace norm. To my knowledge, this specific error bound for nuclear-norm regularization and the consequent linear-rate result without strong convexity are new and significant.

Strengths
- Novelty: Establishes a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, resolving an open question in the composite optimization literature and enabling linear convergence without strong convexity of f. The simultaneous SVD observation at optimality is elegant and leveraged effectively.
- Breadth: Applies to a wide family of practically relevant losses via f(X)=h(A(X)), encompassing square and logistic losses with arbitrary linear operators A.
- Technical depth and correctness: The proof strategy is careful, with a clear chain from the error bound to linear convergence via Luo–Tseng. The restricted invertibility property and the decomposition of perturbations (Proposition 4.3) are nontrivial and appear sound.
- Clarity and organization: The paper is well structured; the residual map viewpoint makes the result intuitive. Assumptions are stated cleanly.
- Potential impact: Error bounds for nuclear-norm problems are a foundational tool; this result may inform convergence analyses of other first-order methods, variants with line search, and possibly accelerated schemes.

Weaknesses and questions
- Local/asymptotic nature: The linear rate is asymptotic (requires ‖R(X)‖F ≤ ε). It would be helpful to quantify when the algorithm enters the region where the error bound holds (iteration complexity to a neighborhood) and how constants depend on data (A, τ, loss parameters).
- Step size and line search: The theorem requires αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0,1/Lf). In experiments, a backtracking line search is used for logistic loss. Please clarify conditions under which backtracking ensures a positive lower bound α>0 uniformly (needed by the proof), and whether monotone backtracking satisfies the assumptions.
- Assumption on h: “Strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)” is slightly unusual. While it holds for square and logistic losses on bounded sets, a brief discussion or examples delineating when it fails (e.g., hinge-like losses) would help practitioners.
- Experiments: Empirically, the results are illustrative but limited (small synthetic problems, no baselines, no study of constants or neighborhood size). While I appreciate that the contribution is primarily theoretical, adding:
  - comparisons to accelerated PGM (to see if acceleration + error bound yields observable linear rates),
  - sensitivity to τ and sampling ratio,
  - and a check with inexact SVD proximal steps,
  would strengthen the empirical story.
- Scope of the error bound: The bound is local to a level set and for small residuals. A short comparison to KL-based analyses (which often yield local linear rates under error bounds/Łojasiewicz properties) would help position the contribution, and clarify if the current bound implies a KL exponent of 1/2 for this class.

Quality
Technically solid and carefully argued. Some assumptions and constants could be discussed more concretely, and the experimental section is minimal but acceptable for a theory-focused paper.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. Suggestions:
- Provide an intuitive explanation of Proposition 4.2 via the subdifferential of the nuclear norm to guide readers less familiar with spectral subdifferentials.
- Summarize in the main text (not only supplementary) the derivation of inequalities (9)–(11) and how α, ᾱ enter κ1–κ3.
- State explicitly that the result yields R-linear convergence of iterates and Q-linear of the objective, and define the neighborhood more concretely.

Originality
High. The error bound for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity and its exploitation for PGM linear rates is, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial.

Significance
High for the optimization/theory community and relevant for ML practitioners using low-rank models. This should become a reference for convergence analyses of nuclear-norm problems.

Pros for acceptance
- Resolves a notable open question: EB for nuclear-norm regularization.
- Elegant spectral-structure–based analysis; broadly applicable assumptions.
- Clear pathway to linear convergence without strong convexity of f.
- Potentially enables further advances (e.g., for other Schatten norms, variants of PGM).

Cons against acceptance
- Linear rate is local; neighborhood size and constants not quantified.
- Limited empirical validation; no comparisons or exploration of practical step-size schemes under theory.
- Some assumptions (uniform lower bound on step sizes, local strong convexity of h on the relevant set) could be discussed more explicitly for common setups.

Recommendation
Accept (clear theoretical novelty and significance outweigh the minor empirical limitations).

Confidence
High.
