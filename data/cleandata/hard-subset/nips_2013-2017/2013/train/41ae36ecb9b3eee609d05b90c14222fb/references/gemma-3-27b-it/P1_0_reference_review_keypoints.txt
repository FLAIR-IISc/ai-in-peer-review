- Shows linear convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss f.
- Establishes a novel Lipschitzian error bound specifically for trace-norm regularization.
- Uses matrix perturbation theory and a special property of optimal solutions (Prop. 4.2) to handle singular-value interdependence.
- Overcomes challenges from the trace norm’s non-polyhedral epigraph.
- Extends applicability to real problems like matrix completion where strong convexity often fails.
- Builds on Luo & Tseng (2010) and relates to ℓ1/group LASSO error-bound results, highlighting unique trace-norm difficulties.
- Numerical experiments (matrix completion and classification) corroborate the theoretical linear convergence.
- Novelty: removes a common strong-convexity requirement, broadening theoretical applicability.
- Technical contribution: development and proof of a tailored Lipschitzian error bound.
- Theoretical rigor: clear assumptions and well-structured, thorough proofs.
- Practical validation: experiments support theory, though not exhaustive.
- Writing: generally clear and well-organized with good notation.
- Proof complexity: key proofs (Lemma 4.1, Props. 4.2/4.3) are dense and need more intuitive explanation.
- Limited experiments: more datasets, parameter sweeps, solver comparisons, and step-size analysis are needed.
- Assumptions: practical implications of Assumptions 2.1 and 2.2 should be clarified with examples.
- Computational cost: missing discussion of per-iteration complexity and proximity-operator cost.
- Minor clarity issues: some derivations (e.g., decomposition of Q̄ in Prop. 4.3) require more explicit definitions.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a significant theoretical gap, substantial technical work, strong potential impact, supported by experiments.
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility of proofs, limited empirical validation, and omission of computational-cost discussion.
- Recommendation: improve intuitive exposition of key proofs, expand numerical experiments/comparisons, discuss computational complexity, and clarify practical conditions for assumptions.
