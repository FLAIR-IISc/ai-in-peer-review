

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper establishes a linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors prove a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which is the key technical contribution. The main result (Theorem 3.1) establishes that the distance to the optimal solution set is bounded by the residual norm of the PGM, enabling the application of Luo and Tseng's framework for proving linear convergence. The paper covers important applications like matrix completion and matrix classification, with supporting numerical experiments.

## Strengths

**Technical Contribution and Originality (High):** The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature. Prior work on linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization required strong convexity of the loss function (e.g., [16, 19]), which is not satisfied in many important applications like matrix completion. The authors' key insight—that optimal solutions share singular vectors with -∇f(X̄)—enables them to establish a Lipschitzian error bound, resolving an open question noted in [18]. This is a genuine technical advance, as the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph makes standard approaches for ℓ1-norm regularization (e.g., [22, 23]) inapplicable.

**Theoretical Soundness (High):** The proofs are rigorous and well-structured. The authors carefully establish the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and then apply it to prove linear convergence (Theorem 3.2). The proof of Lemma 4.1, particularly the restricted invertibility property (16), is the most challenging part and is handled with sufficient detail. The paper properly references related work and clearly explains how their approach differs from previous error bound analyses.

**Experimental Validation (Medium):** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification support the theoretical claims, showing linear convergence in practice. While limited to two applications, they demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

## Weaknesses

**Restrictive Assumptions (Significant):** The assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) (Assumption 2.1) is somewhat restrictive. While it covers square loss and logistic loss, it excludes more complex loss functions that might be of interest in practice. The paper would be strengthened by discussing the limitations of this assumption and whether the approach could be extended to broader loss classes.

**Lack of Practical Insight (Moderate):** The paper doesn't quantify the practical advantage of linear convergence over sublinear convergence. For example, how many iterations are saved in practice? The numerical experiments show linear convergence but don't provide a comparison to the theoretical rates (e.g., O(1/k) vs. linear convergence) or to alternative methods.

**Proof Complexity (Moderate):** The proof of Lemma 4.1(c) is quite technical and may be challenging to follow for readers. While referencing supplementary material is acceptable, a more intuitive explanation of the key steps (particularly the construction of X̂ = X̃k + ǫQ̄) would improve accessibility.

**Missing Discussion of Constants (Moderate):** The error bound in Theorem 3.1 depends on constants η and ε. The paper doesn't discuss how these constants behave with respect to problem parameters (e.g., matrix dimensions, rank), which would be valuable for practitioners.

## Comparison to Related Work

The paper correctly identifies the gap in the literature: while error bounds exist for ℓ1-norm regularization [22, 23], the trace norm case was open due to its non-polyhedral epigraph [18]. The authors' approach using matrix perturbation theory to analyze spectral structure is novel and appropriately contrasts with the grouping techniques used for ℓ1. However, the paper could better elaborate on why the trace norm case is fundamentally harder than ℓ1, beyond just noting the non-polyhedral epigraph.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. The linear convergence result under weaker assumptions than prior work is significant for machine learning applications where strong convexity is often not satisfied. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a clear resolution to an open problem.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper is a strong contribution to the field, addressing a meaningful gap in the literature with a technically sound proof. While the assumptions are somewhat restrictive, the result is general enough to cover many important machine learning applications. The theoretical contribution is substantial and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Justification:** The paper advances the state of the art in optimization for structured matrix problems, which is directly relevant to NIPS. The main contribution (the error bound for trace norm regularization) is novel, the proofs are rigorous, and the results are supported by numerical experiments. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions relative to prior work. While there are some limitations (mainly the restrictive assumption on the loss function), these do not undermine the significance of the theoretical result.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
1. Discuss the practical implications of the linear convergence rate more explicitly (e.g., iteration count comparison).
2. Provide more intuition for the key step in the proof of Lemma 4.1(c).
3. Briefly address how the constants in the error bound depend on problem parameters.
4. Consider extending the discussion of related work to better explain why the trace norm case is harder than ℓ1.
