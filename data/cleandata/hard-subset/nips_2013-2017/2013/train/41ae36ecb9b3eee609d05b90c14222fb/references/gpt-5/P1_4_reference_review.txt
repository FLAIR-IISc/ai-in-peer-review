Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM/ISTA) for the convex composite problem minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where ||·||* is the nuclear norm and f(X)=h(A(X)) with linear A and strictly convex, smooth h. The main result is an asymptotic Lipschitzian error bound: for points X in a bounded level set and with small residual R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X, the distance to the optimal set X* is bounded by η||R(X)||F. This error bound, together with the Luo–Tseng analysis framework, yields R-linear convergence of the iterates and Q-linear convergence of objective values for PGM with stepsizes bounded away from zero and strictly below 1/Lf, without requiring f to be strongly convex in X.

The novelty is establishing an error bound for trace-norm regularization, extending prior results that covered polyhedral nonsmooth terms (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) or specific group-structured settings. The technical crux is a spectral structure at optimality: any optimal X* and −∇f(X*) admit a simultaneous SVD (Proposition 4.2). This enables a matrix perturbation argument and a “restricted invertibility” inequality that links distances in X-space to distances in observation space A(X). The paper situates its contribution with references to Luo–Tseng error bounds, proximal gradient rates for strongly convex loss, and prior nuclear-norm optimization methods. Numerical illustrations on matrix completion (squared loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) exhibit linear convergence in the tail.

Quality:
- Theoretical soundness: The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is local (on bounded level sets and for small residual) and, paired with standard descent and residual control inequalities, implies linear rates (Theorem 3.2). The assumptions on h (C1, Lipschitz gradient, strict convexity, and strong convexity on compact convex subsets) are satisfied by squared and logistic losses on bounded domains; the boundedness of the relevant domain is obtained via bounded level sets of F. The spectral arguments (Propositions 4.2 and 4.3) are plausible and well-motivated; key technical steps are deferred to the supplement, which is reasonable for space, but the main text presents the proof architecture clearly.
- Empirics are illustrative rather than exhaustive. They corroborate the asymptotic linear phase but do not attempt to measure constants, neighborhood sizes, or compare with alternatives.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized, with a clear narrative: define residual map, state and prove a local error bound, then invoke the Luo–Tseng framework. The role of each assumption is explained. Some notation could be streamlined (µ vs τ in experiments) and a few inequalities and proofs are deferred; brief intuition for Proposition 4.2 via the subdifferential of ||·||* would help readers.
- Minor detail: Theorem 3.2 requires αk<1/Lf, but the matrix completion experiment uses α=1; this may equal 1/Lf for that A, but the strict inequality is not met—please clarify.

Originality:
- To my knowledge, this is the first Lipschitzian error bound tailored to nuclear-norm regularization, enabling linear convergence of plain PGM without assuming strong convexity of f. Prior error bounds for ℓ1/group-LASSO rely on polyhedral geometry and separability, which do not transfer to the trace norm; the simultaneous SVD approach is an original and insightful workaround. The work is distinct from general-purpose KL-based analyses by directly proving a problem-specific error bound with explicit structural use of the nuclear norm.

Significance:
- The result explains and justifies the widely observed linear phase of ISTA on nuclear-norm models and broadens the class of losses beyond strongly convex ones. This has practical implications for matrix completion/classification and related tasks where A is not injective. The error bound technique may be reusable for other Schatten norms or spectral regularizers, as the authors suggest.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean and nontrivial main theorem (local error bound) for a widely used regularizer with nonpolyhedral epigraph.
- Clear linkage to a standard convergence framework; conditions are practical (fixed stepsize with known Lipschitz constant or backtracking).
- Broad applicability: covers squared and logistic losses with linear measurement operators.
- Empirical illustrations on two tasks confirm the theory qualitatively.

Weaknesses / concerns:
- Local nature: the error bound holds near the solution set and on bounded sublevel sets, so the result is asymptotic. Some discussion of how large the neighborhood can be (e.g., in terms of problem constants) would be valuable.
- Constants and computability: No guidance on estimating η, ε, or the contraction factor in practice; this limits quantitative predictive power.
- Experiments are small-scale and qualitative: no comparisons to FISTA/accelerated variants, no wall-clock timing, no sensitivity to conditioning or noise, and no exploration of cases with multiple optimal solutions.
- Minor mismatch in stepsize condition in the first experiment (α=1 vs α<1/Lf).
- Some proofs rely on supplementary material; including a brief derivation of Proposition 4.2 from the subgradient characterization of the nuclear norm would improve self-containment.

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide bounds or estimates for η and ε in Theorem 3.1 in terms of Lf, ||A||, and curvature parameters of h?
- Does the error bound extend to accelerated PGM (FISTA) to show eventual linear rates for the function values?
- How sensitive is the restricted invertibility (16) to degeneracy when X* is not unique? Any pathological cases?
- Could your approach extend to other spectral regularizers, e.g., Schatten p-norms with p∈(1,2], or to constraints (e.g., affine constraints) in addition to the regularizer?

Minor comments:
- Unify notation: τ is the regularization parameter in the theory, but µ is used in experiments.
- Some references are missing years or have incomplete bibliographic details.
- Clarify that the linear rates are asymptotic (after entering a neighborhood), which is consistent with the figures.

Reproducibility:
- Algorithmic settings are described; datasets are synthetic with clear generation procedures. Including seeds and code would further enhance reproducibility, but sufficient detail is present to replicate qualitatively.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10)
This is a solid theoretical contribution that resolves an open question about error bounds and linear convergence for nuclear-norm regularization under broad, practically relevant losses. While the empirical section could be stronger and constants are not quantified, the core idea is novel and significant for the optimization and ML communities. Confidence: high.
