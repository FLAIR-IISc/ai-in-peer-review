

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization for machine learning: under what conditions can the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieve linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems, without requiring the loss function to be strongly convex. The authors establish a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which they use to prove linear convergence of the PGM under relatively mild assumptions. Specifically, they consider problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*} where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A a linear operator and h strictly convex with certain smoothness properties. This covers common loss functions like square loss in matrix completion and logistic loss in classification.

The paper builds on previous work by Luo and Tseng (2000) who developed a general framework for analyzing convergence rates, but extends it to the trace norm setting, which had remained an open question. Prior work on error bounds for composite optimization typically handled cases like ℓ1-norm regularization with polyhedral epigraphs, but the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph posed new challenges. The authors overcome this by leveraging matrix perturbation theory and the key observation that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof of the main error bound (Theorem 3.1) and linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2). The proofs are well-constructed, with careful attention to the nuances of trace norm regularization. The assumptions (2.1 and 2.2) are reasonable and well-justified with examples from machine learning applications. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for verification, though some parts (like Proposition 4.3) could be more accessible.

**Originality:** This is a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an open question in the literature. While error bounds for ℓ1-norm regularization were known, the trace norm case had remained unresolved despite its apparent similarity. The authors' approach using matrix perturbation theory and simultaneous singular value decomposition is novel and addresses a key technical challenge that previous methods couldn't handle. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on error bounds for composite optimization.

**Significance:** The results have important practical implications for machine learning applications using trace norm regularization (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). The linear convergence guarantee is valuable for practitioners as it provides theoretical assurances about faster convergence compared to the standard O(1/k) rate. The error bound itself could be of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear motivation in the introduction and proper contextualization of the problem. The main results are presented with appropriate technical detail, and the numerical experiments effectively complement the theoretical results. The connection between the error bound and the linear convergence result is clearly explained.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Experimental Scope:** While the numerical experiments for matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice, they are limited to two specific applications. More diverse experiments on different loss functions or larger-scale problems would strengthen the practical significance. The authors could also provide more insight into how the convergence rate changes with different problem parameters.

**Assumption Restrictiveness:** The assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth is reasonable for many applications, but excludes some important cases. For instance, the paper doesn't address loss functions that aren't expressible in this form (e.g., certain structured losses). The authors should more explicitly discuss the limitations of their assumptions and whether their framework could be extended to broader classes of loss functions.

**Supplementary Material:** The proof of Proposition 4.3, which is crucial for the main result, is relegated to supplementary material. While this is common practice, the proof is sufficiently important to be included in the main text for better accessibility.

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a significant advance in understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The paper addresses a well-motivated open question with a novel technical approach, and the results are of clear significance to the machine learning and optimization communities.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission. It is technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant. The minor weaknesses noted (limited experiments, assumption restrictiveness) are common in theoretical papers and do not detract from the paper's overall contribution.

## Pro/Con Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Resolves an important open question in trace norm optimization
- Provides the first linear convergence result for PGM without strong convexity assumptions
- Develops a novel error bound technique using matrix perturbation theory
- Theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous proofs
- Numerical experiments confirm the theoretical findings

**Con:**
- Limited experimental scope (only two applications tested)
- Assumptions may exclude some practical loss functions
- Proof of key proposition in supplementary material (reducing accessibility)

## Final Recommendation

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of first-order methods for non-strongly convex optimization with trace norm regularization. The results are important for both theoretical optimization and practical machine learning applications. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the field.

The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the paper's significant theoretical contribution and would be easily addressed in the camera-ready version through expanded experiments or more detailed discussion of the assumptions.
