This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems, specifically when the loss function is of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is a strictly convex function. The authors establish linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions on h, without requiring strong convexity of f itself—a significant improvement over existing results. The core of the analysis lies in deriving a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, highlights the gap in existing literature, and clearly states the contributions. The notation is consistent throughout, and the authors provide sufficient details to understand the technical arguments. The organization, with detailed preliminaries, main results, and a proof sketch, facilitates comprehension. The inclusion of numerical experiments strengthens the validation of the theoretical findings.

The primary strength of the work lies in its theoretical novelty. Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity on the loss function is a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of PGM to a wider range of machine learning problems like matrix completion, where strong convexity is often not present. The careful derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound, addressing the difficulties posed by the trace norm, is a substantial technical achievement. The connection to Luo and Tseng's framework provides a solid foundation for the convergence analysis. The numerical experiments demonstrate that the theoretical findings align with practical performance.

However, there are some areas for improvement. While the paper claims to be the first linear convergence result for trace norm regularization without strong convexity, it would benefit from a more detailed comparison to related work, specifically highlighting how this result differs from existing accelerated gradient methods with sublinear convergence rates. The discussion surrounding Assumption 2.1 could be more elaborate. Providing examples where this assumption fails, or conditions for its validity in practical scenarios, would strengthen the argument. The numerical experiments, while helpful, are somewhat limited in scope. Extending the experiments to more diverse datasets and loss functions would provide greater confidence in the robustness of the findings. The connection between the step size parameter and the convergence rate could be further elucidated. While αk is constrained, a more precise discussion of optimal step size selection would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant theoretical advance.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound is rigorous and well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical concepts.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to impact the design and analysis of algorithms for a variety of applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical validation could be more extensive.
*   **Lack of Detailed Comparison:** A more detailed comparison to related work would strengthen the claim of novelty.
*   **Assumption Elaboration:** Further discussion around Assumption 2.1 is needed for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The technical details are sound, the presentation is clear, and the results are potentially impactful. While the numerical experiments could be more comprehensive and a more detailed comparison to related work would be beneficial, the strengths of the paper outweigh these minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
