Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization with trace (nuclear) norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient and strictly convex, with local strong convexity on compact sets. The main result is an asymptotic linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to this problem without requiring strong convexity of f. The key technical contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound: within a bounded level set and for sufficiently small residual, dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F, where R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X is the fixed-point residual map and X* is the optimal set. Given this error bound, the Luo–Tseng framework yields Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates under standard step-size conditions (0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf).

The proof overcomes a known difficulty: unlike ℓ1 or group lasso (polyhedral) regularizers, the nuclear norm has a nonpolyhedral epigraph, and classical separable-variable arguments do not apply. The authors establish that any optimal solution X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same left/right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), enabling a spectral-structure analysis via matrix perturbation theory. This yields a “restricted invertibility” property linking distances in the decision variable to distances in the measurement space, which is pivotal for the error bound. The results cover widely used losses such as squared and logistic losses used in matrix completion/classification and multi-task learning, aligning with numerous prior works on nuclear-norm models (e.g., Ma et al. 2011; Ji & Ye 2009; Toh & Yun 2010) and on error bounds for other regularizers (Tseng and co-authors; Luo & Tseng). It extends linear-convergence guarantees known for strongly convex f (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011) to a broader class where f may not be globally strongly convex (e.g., when A is not injective).

Quality (technical soundness):
- The analysis is grounded in the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework and standard PGM descent properties. The new ingredient—the local error bound for the nuclear-norm regularized problem—appears technically solid. The spectral alignment at optimality follows from the subdifferential structure of the nuclear norm and facial geometry (cited work on Schatten norms). The contradiction-based proof of restricted invertibility is careful and uses projections onto a spectrally aligned optimal subset Xc and an SVD-based decomposition of limiting directions.
- Assumptions are clearly stated. The requirement that h is strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h) ensures a positive curvature parameter σ on the bounded level set, which is reasonable for square loss and logistic loss (the latter is strictly convex and has Hessian bounded away from zero on compact domains).
- The convergence result is asymptotic/local (linear rate holds once iterates are sufficiently close), which is consistent with the local error-bound nature. The step-size condition αk < 1/Lf is standard; Lf = L‖A‖2 follows from the chain rule and is implicitly used.
- Some proofs are deferred to the supplement (e.g., inequalities (9)–(11), details of Propositions 4.2 and 4.3). Given the centrality of Proposition 4.2, including more of its argument in the main text would improve verifiability.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized and clearly written. The setup, residual map, and algorithm are standard and well motivated. The flow from error bound to linear convergence is clear.
- Minor issues: define Lf explicitly as L‖A‖2 when first used; unify notation for the proximity operator with/without step sizes (proxτ vs proxταk); ensure all references include complete bibliographic details. A brief intuitive explanation of Proposition 4.2 (via the subdifferential of ‖·‖*) would aid readability.

Originality:
- Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization and deriving linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f is, to the best of my knowledge, novel relative to prior error-bound results for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group lasso) and prior convergence analyses for nuclear-norm problems (which did not show linear rates without strong convexity). This is a meaningful theoretical advance.

Significance:
- Nuclear norm regularization is widely used in machine learning and signal processing. The error bound and resulting linear convergence guarantee provide theoretical justification for the often-observed linear phase of PGM in practice, even when f is not strongly convex due to non-injective A (as in matrix completion). The techniques may be extensible to other spectral regularizers. While the rate is local, this is standard for error-bound-based analyses and still practically valuable.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated problem and contribution.
- Novel error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under broad, practically relevant assumptions.
- Clean application of Luo–Tseng framework to obtain linear rates.
- Insightful spectral-structure argument (simultaneous SVD at optimality).
- Empirical illustrations for squared and logistic losses showing linear convergence behavior.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Local nature of the error bound (requires small residual and bounded level set) implies only asymptotic linear convergence; making the basin of attraction more explicit or providing sufficient conditions for entering it would strengthen the practical message.
- The matrix completion experiment uses a fixed step size α = 1. For a sampling operator P, Lf = 1, so the stated theorem requires α < 1/Lf = 1; the experiment slightly violates this assumption. Clarify this point or set α = 0.9 to align with the theory.
- Numerical experiments are small-scale and synthetic; adding larger-scale tests and/or comparisons to accelerated methods would improve empirical support.
- Provide more detail in the main text for Proposition 4.2 and its proof or a succinct lemma based on the subdifferential of the nuclear norm to make the spectral alignment self-contained.
- Clarify and illustrate Assumption 2.1(b) for logistic loss (e.g., explain how bounded level sets yield a positive curvature σ).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and common setting in ML optimization; fills a gap in theory (error bounds and linear convergence for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity).
- Solid analysis with clear assumptions and implications.
- Likely to influence subsequent work on first-order methods for low-rank learning.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The main rate guarantee is local/asymptotic, with constants unspecified; practical implications could be further substantiated.
- Some proofs are deferred and key propositions could be elaborated in the main text.
- Empirical section is limited and contains a minor inconsistency with step-size assumptions.

Overall, the paper is a technically sound and original contribution on the convergence theory of proximal methods for nuclear-norm regularization, relevant to the NeurIPS community working on optimization for ML.
