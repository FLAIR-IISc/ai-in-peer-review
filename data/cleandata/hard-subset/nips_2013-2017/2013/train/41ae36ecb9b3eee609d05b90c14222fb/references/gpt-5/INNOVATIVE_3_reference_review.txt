Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the composite optimization problem min f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, and “locally strongly convex” (strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain). The main contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization: dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X‖F for points in a level set with sufficiently small residual. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with constant step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf) without requiring f to be strongly convex. The key technical insight is that any optimal solution X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors; this enables a careful perturbation analysis to control the spectral structure of nearby points and establish a “restricted invertibility” type inequality that substitutes for strong convexity in the analysis.

This advances prior work in two ways. First, linear rates for PGM in trace-norm regularization previously required strong convexity of f; here f generally fails to be strongly convex unless A is injective, yet linear rates are shown. Second, existing error bounds for composite problems were largely for polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO), exploiting separability; the nuclear norm is non-polyhedral and couples all entries through singular values, so those arguments do not carry over. The spectral alignment observation and subsequent analysis appear new and nontrivial. The empirical section demonstrates linear convergence on matrix completion (square loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss), fitting the stated assumptions.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The high-level proof strategy is standard (error bound + Luo–Tseng), but the core error bound for the nuclear norm is novel and relies on careful spectral arguments. The assumptions on h (smooth, Lipschitz gradient, and strongly convex on compact sets) are reasonable for square and logistic losses. The convergence result is asymptotic/local, consistent with the local nature of the error bound. Some proofs are deferred to supplementary material; the main steps are clearly stated.
- Empirical support: The experiments are small-scale but appropriate sanity checks illustrating linear convergence once iterates enter the basin where the error bound applies. They are sufficient for a theory paper but could be expanded.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized: clear statement of assumptions, definitions, residual map, and main theorems. The role of Proposition 4.2 (spectral alignment) and Lemma 4.1 (restricted invertibility) in enabling the error bound is well motivated. Minor clarity issues: some figures/references appear misnumbered; some references lack years; notation occasionally switches between τ and μ.

Originality:
High. Establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization without relying on polyhedrality/separability is a significant conceptual step, and the spectral alignment observation at optimality is a neat idea. To my knowledge (and as argued by the authors), this is the first linear convergence result of PGM for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.

Significance:
Conceptually important and likely to influence subsequent analyses of first-order methods for non-polyhedral regularizers (other Schatten norms, spectral functions). Practically, it justifies the widely observed linear convergence behavior of PGM for trace-norm problems and provides conditions under which it holds. While the rate is only local/asymptotic and constants are not quantified, this is typical for error-bound-based results and still valuable.

Strengths:
- Novel Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm; resolves an open question.
- Linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f; applies to square and logistic losses with arbitrary linear operators A.
- Elegant spectral argument (simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −∇f(X̄)) and careful perturbation analysis.
- Clean, broadly applicable result and simple algorithmic requirements (fixed steps < 1/L or backtracking).

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Local/asymptotic nature: The result requires small residual and bounded level sets. It would help to discuss conditions ensuring that PGM enters this region and any dependence on initialization; any global complexity bound to reach the local regime would strengthen the practical message.
- Assumption 2.1(b): “strongly convex on any compact convex subset” is natural for square loss and often holds for logistic on bounded sets, but a brief discussion clarifying when σ>0 can be ensured (e.g., via bounded iterates) and how σ relates to L would improve readability. Could weaker conditions (e.g., a local PL/EB condition on h∘A) suffice?
- Step sizes: The theorem assumes αk < 1/Lf; experiments use backtracking. Please clarify that the line search ensures this and state the rule used.
- Constants: The analysis is nonconstructive re: η, ε, and κ’s. Any qualitative dependence (e.g., on spectral gap of −∇f(X̄) or rank structure) would be insightful.
- Experiments: Limited in scope; adding larger-scale runs, comparisons to accelerated PGM, and plots showing when the linear regime begins would strengthen the empirical section.
- Minor editorial issues: fix figure numbering and incomplete references; ensure consistent notation (τ vs μ).

Questions to authors:
- Is the simultaneous SVD property stable under perturbations beyond optimality, and could it yield identification of the active singular subspace (i.e., finite identification of left/right singular vectors) for PGM?
- Can your error bound be extended to other spectral regularizers (e.g., Schatten p-norms, spectral k-support norms)?
- Does an accelerated PGM enjoy a similar asymptotic linear rate under your EB (as in the ℓ1 case), or only O(1/k2) globally?
- Can the assumptions be relaxed to a local quadratic growth/PL condition on F without invoking properties of h?

Recommendation:
Accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept (a Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm) and leverages it to obtain the first asymptotic linear-rate result for PGM in a widely used setting without strong convexity. Despite modest experiments and local nature, the theoretical advance is substantial and likely to spur further work.

Confidence: High.
