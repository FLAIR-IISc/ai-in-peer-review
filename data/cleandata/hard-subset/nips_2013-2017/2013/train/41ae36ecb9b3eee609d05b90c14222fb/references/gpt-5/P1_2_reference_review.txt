Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving nuclear norm–regularized problems of the form minX f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is a linear operator, and h is strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient and “locally” strongly convex (strongly convex on any compact convex subset of dom(h)). The main contributions are:
- A new local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm–regularized problem: dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F for points in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual, where R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X is the residual map and Sτ is the matrix shrinkage operator.
- Using this error bound and the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors prove asymptotic linear convergence of PGM (Q-linear for objective values and R-linear for iterates) for any step size αk bounded away from 0 and 1/Lf, without requiring f to be strongly convex.
- Key technical insight: at an optimum X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2). This permits a spectral-structure–aware perturbation analysis leading to a “restricted invertibility” inequality relating distances in X-space and A(X)-space, which in turn yields the error bound.

The results generalize prior linear convergence guarantees for PGM beyond the classical strongly convex setting (e.g., Schmidt et al. 2011) and beyond cases where the nonsmooth part has a polyhedral epigraph (e.g., ℓ1/group-Lasso error bounds by Tseng and collaborators). To my knowledge, a local linear rate for nuclear norm regularization via an error bound (independent of restricted strong convexity of f) has not been previously shown. The analysis is also related in spirit to Luo–Tseng error bound theory and to identification/partial smoothness results for structured nonsmooth problems, though the paper’s approach is tailored to the spectral structure of the nuclear norm.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The proof strategy is careful and nontrivial. Establishing a local error bound for a nonpolyhedral regularizer is challenging, and the authors’ use of simultaneous SVD at optimality plus matrix perturbation arguments is well-motivated. The contradiction argument that yields the “restricted invertibility” inequality (16) appears coherent: assuming the inequality fails yields a direction Q̄ in the nullspace of A along which one can move within the identified optimal face Xc to produce a closer point, contradicting the projection optimality. The fixed-point characterization via shrinkage (Eq. 23) is used effectively to handle cases depending on thresholding at τ.
- Some technical steps (e.g., Proposition 4.3, derivation of constants κ1–κ3, and handling singular value multiplicities) are deferred to the supplement; their correctness is essential. The assumption “h strongly convex on compact convex subsets” is reasonable for smooth strictly convex losses (e.g., square, logistic) and is used to obtain curvature on bounded level sets.
- The linear rate is asymptotic/local (requires small residual and bounded level set), which is consistent with error-bound-based analyses.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The motivation, assumptions, algorithm, and residual map are clearly presented. The role of the main lemma and the structure of the contradiction proof are explained.
- Minor issues: unify notation for the regularization parameter (τ in theory, µ=1 in experiments); state explicitly that the linear rate is local/asymptotic and the constants are not quantified; briefly discuss handling of singular-value multiplicities in Proposition 4.2 (simultaneous diagonalization claims can be subtle when eigenspaces are not unique).
- A short high-level comparison to alternative frameworks (e.g., KL property/partial smoothness yielding local linear rates) would further situate the result.

Originality:
- The main novelty is an error bound for nuclear norm regularization and its use to show asymptotic linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f. Prior linear rates either require strong convexity (or restricted strong convexity/statistical assumptions) or polyhedral regularizers. The spectral-alignment insight and the way it circumvents the nonpolyhedral difficulty are original and interesting.

Significance:
- The result advances our understanding of first-order methods for low-rank learning by providing a general (model-agnostic) asymptotic linear rate under mild structural assumptions on f (composition with a linear map and smooth, strictly convex h). This is relevant to numerous ML tasks (matrix completion, multitask learning, matrix classification).
- Practically, the result is local and does not deliver explicit constants, but it justifies the widely observed linear tail behavior of PGM on such problems. It could spur further work on sharper constants, globalization strategies (e.g., restart), and extensions to other spectral regularizers.

Pros:
- Clear, nontrivial theoretical contribution: first local error bound for nuclear norm regularization and consequent linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f.
- Broad applicability: square and logistic losses, arbitrary linear operators A, non-unique optimal sets allowed.
- Clean integration with Luo–Tseng framework; proof ideas are insightful and may be reusable.

Cons:
- The linear rate is asymptotic/local, with unspecified constants; no discussion of magnitude or detectability of the linear regime.
- Some technical pieces moved to the supplement; a concise sketch of Proposition 4.3’s proof would help.
- Experiments are illustrative only (two small setups, no baselines, no runtime or wall-clock comparisons); step-size α=1 equals 1/Lf in matrix completion (the theory requires <1/Lf).
- The simultaneous SVD property at optimality should acknowledge potential degeneracies (repeated singular values) and clarify the role of faces/partial smoothness; related literature could be cited.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Explicitly state the locality of the error bound and linear rate, and discuss how one might check entry into the linear regime (e.g., via a residual threshold).
- Clarify Proposition 4.2 in the presence of multiplicities; connect to the known subdifferential structure of ||·||* and partial smoothness/identification, with citations.
- Tighten experimental section: include larger-scale runs, report iteration and time to reach a target accuracy, and compare with accelerated PGM (with/without restart) and ADMM to contextualize practical impact.
- Fix minor notation inconsistencies and provide the missing years in references.

Recommendation: Accept (7/10). Strong theoretical contribution addressing a recognized gap, with clear relevance to ML optimization. While empirical validation is light and the rate is local, the main insights are novel and valuable.

Confidence: 4/5.
