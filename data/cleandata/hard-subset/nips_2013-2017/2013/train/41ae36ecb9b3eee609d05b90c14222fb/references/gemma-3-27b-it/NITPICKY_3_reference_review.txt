This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems, specifically focusing on achieving linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under the assumption that the loss function has a specific structure – f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex. The key contribution lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the motivation, contributions, and technical details. The mathematical derivations appear rigorous, and the connection to existing work on PGM convergence is well-established. The inclusion of numerical experiments to support the theoretical results is a positive aspect.

However, despite the novel contributions, a significant number of minor flaws and an overabundance of detail detract from the overall impact. The presentation, while clear, borders on overly verbose, particularly in the introduction and the preliminary sections. The length of the paper feels excessive considering the core technical contribution. Several citations are missing page numbers (e.g., [17], [20]). The frequent reiteration of the problem statement and methodology feels repetitive.

More critically, the assumption that f(X) = h(A(X)) feels overly restrictive. While the authors acknowledge its applicability to matrix completion and multi-task learning, the generalizability to other relevant applications isn’t thoroughly discussed. The strength of the convergence result hinges on this assumption, and a more comprehensive discussion of its limitations is needed. The numerical experiments, while providing some support, are limited in scope. The parameter settings (e.g., fixed step size α = 1) are not adequately justified. A more thorough sensitivity analysis, varying parameters and comparing performance on different datasets, would strengthen the empirical validation.

The proof of Theorem 3.1 is intricate and relies heavily on Proposition 4.2 and Lemma 4.1. While the authors claim the proof closely follows [22], the level of detail is overwhelming, and the numerous sub-sections and propositions within the proof make it difficult to follow the overall argument. A more concise and streamlined presentation of the proof would significantly improve readability. Certain parts of the proof, particularly the justification for specific steps in Lemma 4.1, are insufficiently detailed and require further clarification.

The discussion of related work is thorough but somewhat uncritical. The authors highlight the differences between their work and previous error bounds for composite function minimization. However, they don't fully engage with the potential limitations of those existing approaches in the context of trace norm regularization.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The linear convergence result for trace norm regularization without strong convexity is a valuable contribution.
*   Technical soundness: The mathematical analysis appears rigorous and well-supported.
*   Potential impact: The results could have implications for the development of more efficient algorithms for low-rank matrix recovery.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Overly restrictive assumption: The requirement that f(X) = h(A(X)) limits the generalizability of the results.
*   Excessive detail and verbosity: The paper is too long and contains unnecessary repetition.
*   Limited numerical validation: The experimental setup is basic and lacks a thorough sensitivity analysis.
*   Minor flaws: Missing page numbers in citations, inconsistencies in formatting, and lack of clarity in certain parts of the proof.



**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents a worthwhile theoretical result, the numerous minor flaws, the overly restrictive assumption, and the verbose presentation significantly detract from its overall quality. I recommend **rejection**. Addressing the concerns regarding conciseness, justification of assumptions, and providing a more comprehensive experimental evaluation could significantly improve the paper. A more careful review of formatting and citations is also necessary. The paper isn’t *bad*, it’s simply not polished enough for NIPS. It feels more like a preliminary draft than a finished submission.
