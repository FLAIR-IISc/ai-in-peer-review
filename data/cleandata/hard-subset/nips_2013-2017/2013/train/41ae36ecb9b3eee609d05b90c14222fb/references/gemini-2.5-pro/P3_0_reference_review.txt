### Summary

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min h(A(X)) + τ||X||*`. While it is known that PGM converges linearly for strongly convex objectives, many important problems in machine learning (e.g., matrix completion with square loss) do not satisfy this condition, as the loss function `f(X) = h(A(X))` is often not strongly convex. The main contribution of this work is to establish, for the first time, an *asymptotic linear convergence rate* for PGM on this class of problems, requiring only that `h` is strictly convex and smooth, not that `f` itself is strongly convex.

The key technical innovation is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a fixed-point residual map. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm has been an open problem, as prior techniques for l1-norm and group LASSO regularization, which rely on the polyhedral structure of the epigraph or separability, do not apply here. The authors overcome this challenge by leveraging a crucial structural property of the optimal solutions: an optimal matrix `X*` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows them to use matrix perturbation theory to analyze iterates near the optimal set and ultimately establish the error bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from this bound by applying the well-established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10]. Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification support the theoretical findings.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental question about the convergence of one of the most widely used algorithms (PGM) for one of the most important non-smooth regularizers (the trace norm). Proving linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function is a significant theoretical advance. This result provides a much-needed theoretical justification for the fast convergence often observed in practice for problems like matrix completion.

2.  **Novel and Insightful Technical Contribution:** The primary contribution, the proof of the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm (Theorem 3.1), is highly novel. The authors correctly identify the technical barriers that prevented a straightforward extension of existing results for l1-type norms. The key observation about the simultaneous singular value decomposition of an optimal solution and its corresponding gradient (Proposition 4.2) is elegant and powerful. It provides a new analytical tool for problems involving the trace norm, which could be of independent interest and inspire further research.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears to be rigorous and correct. The proof structure, while building upon the general framework of Luo and Tseng [10], contains a substantial and non-trivial core argument specific to the trace norm. The logic is clearly laid out, proceeding from the contradiction argument to the detailed spectral analysis.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation, situates the work within the existing literature, explicitly states its main contributions, and provides a comprehensible (though dense) proof sketch of the main result. The distinction between the strong convexity of `h` and the lack thereof for `f` is well-explained and motivates the entire paper.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, and the error bound only holds in a neighborhood of the optimal set (`F(X) ≤ ζ`, `‖R(X)‖F ≤ ε`). While this is a common feature of such analyses, the paper could benefit from a discussion of the practical implications. Is the pre-asymptotic phase significant? The experiments show a "knee" in the convergence plot, after which linear convergence takes over. Can anything be said, even heuristically, about when this regime is reached? Acknowledging this limitation more explicitly and discussing its practical relevance would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Dependence on Problem Constants:** The convergence rate depends on constants (`η`, `κ1`, `κ4`, etc.) which in turn depend on properties of the function `h` (`σ`, `L`), the operator `A`, and the optimal set `X`. The analysis does not provide any insight into how the convergence rate `ρ = κ4 / (κ1 + κ4)` behaves as a function of these underlying problem parameters (e.g., matrix dimensions, rank, condition number of `A` restricted to a relevant subspace). While a full analysis might be beyond the scope of this paper, some discussion or experimental investigation into this would make the results more complete. For example, how does the slope of the linear convergence in Figure 1 change with the rank `r` or the sampling ratio `θ`?

3.  **Clarity of the Proof Sketch:** The proof sketch in Section 4, particularly the decomposition in Proposition 4.3, is very condensed. While page limits necessitate deferring details to the supplement, a bit more intuition in the main text would be helpful. For instance, what is the geometric intuition behind the decomposition of `Q̄`? And what do the three cases (A, B, C) for the singular values physically represent? (e.g., they correspond to singular values of `X* - ∇f(X*)` being less than, equal to, or greater than `τ`). Adding a sentence or two of explanation could significantly improve readability for non-specialists in error bound analysis.

4.  **Comparison with Accelerated Methods:** The paper analyzes the standard PGM. A natural question is how this result compares to accelerated methods like FISTA, which have an optimal sublinear rate of O(1/k^2). While FISTA's worst-case rate is sublinear, it is often much faster in practice, especially in the initial stages. The paper would be strengthened by including a comparison to FISTA in the numerical experiments to show the point at which the linear rate of standard PGM overtakes the sublinear rate of the accelerated variant.

### Overall Evaluation

This is an excellent theoretical paper that resolves an important open question in optimization and machine learning. The results are significant, the technical approach is novel and powerful, and the paper is written with great clarity. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to providing more practical context and intuition around the powerful theoretical results. This work significantly advances our understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization and is a clear-cut accept for NIPS.
