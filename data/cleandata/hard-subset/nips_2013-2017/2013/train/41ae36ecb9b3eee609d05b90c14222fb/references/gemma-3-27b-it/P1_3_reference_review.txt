## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) when applied to trace norm regularized optimization problems, a common setup in numerous machine learning applications like matrix completion and low-rank approximation. The authors establish a *linear* convergence rate under fairly general conditions on the loss function, specifically requiring only a smoothness and curvature property on *h* in f(X) = h(A(X)), and crucially *not* strong convexity of *f* itself. This is a significant contribution as existing linear convergence guarantees typically rely on strong convexity.  The core technical innovation lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, circumventing difficulties arising from the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the inability to split variable analysis as is common in ℓ1 regularization. The results are supported by numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence in matrix completion and classification tasks.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The result of establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a notable advancement. It broadens the applicability of PGM to a wider range of problems where strong convexity assumptions are unrealistic.
* **Novel Error Bound:** The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization is a key technical achievement.  The authors clearly articulate the difficulties compared to ℓ1 regularization and present a convincing approach leveraging matrix perturbation theory.
* **Broad Applicability:** The paper’s theoretical results cover a wide range of loss functions commonly used in applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning, as explicitly demonstrated in the introduction and assumptions.
* **Supporting Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments provide empirical evidence supporting the theoretical findings, showing linear convergence in practice.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the work is clearly presented, and the assumptions are explicitly stated.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper does a good job of relating its contributions to existing work on proximal gradient methods, error bounds, and trace norm regularization.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Complexity:** The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and supporting Lemma 4.1 are quite dense and rely on numerous propositions and a fair amount of technical machinery. While the authors promise details in the supplementary material, a more streamlined presentation of the core ideas in the main body would improve accessibility.
* **Limited Numerical Experiment Detail:** The experimental section is somewhat brief. While the plots show linear convergence, more details regarding the experimental setup (e.g., specific parameters used, computational resources, and alternative methods used for comparison) would strengthen the results. Comparing with other solvers, beyond demonstrating convergence, would be valuable.
* **Constant Dependence:** The analysis derives a linear convergence rate, but the constants involved (κ1, κ2, κ3, etc.) are left unspecified.  Understanding the dependence of these constants on problem parameters (e.g., condition number, regularization parameter) would be useful for practical application of the results.
* **Step Size Assumption:** Theorem 3.2 requires a fairly specific condition on the step size (0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf).  Discussion on practical strategies for choosing a step size that satisfies these constraints, especially given that *Lf* might be difficult to estimate in practice, would be helpful.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the presented analysis. The theoretical result is a genuine contribution, though the complexity of the proof requires careful scrutiny (likely best assessed in the supplementary material).  The completeness feels good, given the scope of the result.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally clear, but the dense mathematical arguments in the proof section could benefit from further simplification or explanation. 
* **Originality:** The work presents a novel and original result, bridging a gap in the understanding of PGM convergence for trace norm regularization.  The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior literature.
* **Significance:** The result is significant as it expands the applicability of the PGM to a broader class of problems and provides a more nuanced understanding of its convergence behavior.  This is likely to be useful for both researchers and practitioners working with trace norm regularization.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution with broad implications.
* The technical novelty of the error bound is impressive.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is generally well-written and clearly motivated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proof may make it difficult for some readers to fully grasp the technical details.
* The experimental section could be more comprehensive.
* The lack of explicit constant dependence and step-size guidance could limit the practical impact.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The theoretical contribution is substantial and the results are well-supported. While the proof's complexity and the limited detail in the experimental section are minor drawbacks, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality and importance of the work. I recommend acceptance to NIPS, pending a thorough review of the supplementary material to verify the correctness and completeness of the proof.  I suggest the authors consider streamlining the proof presentation and expanding the experimental section for the camera-ready version.
