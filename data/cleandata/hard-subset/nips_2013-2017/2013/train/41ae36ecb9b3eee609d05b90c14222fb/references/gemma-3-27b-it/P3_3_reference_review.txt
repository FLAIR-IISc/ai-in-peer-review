## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) when applied to trace norm regularized problems, a common setting in areas like matrix completion and low-rank learning. The authors establish *linear* convergence under relatively weak assumptions – specifically, they do *not* require strong convexity of the loss function *f* itself, a significant improvement over existing results. They achieve this by proving a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, linking the distance to the optimal solution with the residual norm. The paper clearly articulates the gap in current literature, presents a technically sound analysis, and demonstrates the result with empirical validation.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a noteworthy advancement.  Existing work relies heavily on this stronger assumption, limiting applicability to many practical machine learning problems where loss functions are often non-strongly convex (e.g., square loss in matrix completion with rank deficiency).
* **Novel Error Bound:**  The derived Lipschitzian error bound is the core technical innovation and appears to be a substantial contribution.  The authors correctly identify the difficulty of extending existing error bound techniques (developed for ℓ1 or group LASSO) to the trace norm, due to its non-polyhedral epigraph and the interdependency of matrix entries.  Their use of matrix perturbation theory based on the shared singular structure of optimal solutions is a clever approach.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction motivates the problem effectively, and the assumptions are clearly stated.  Mathematical notation is generally clear and consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification, while not exhaustive, provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical findings and illustrate the linear convergence behavior in practice. The inclusion of plots is helpful.
* **Thorough Related Work Discussion:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of related work in proximal gradient methods, trace norm regularization, and error bounds, accurately positioning their contribution within the broader field.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:**  While generally clear, the proofs (particularly of Lemma 4.1 and its supporting propositions) are quite dense and require significant expertise in matrix analysis and optimization to fully follow.  More detailed explanations and intermediate steps within the proofs would enhance readability for a broader audience.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat basic.  The impact of different regularization parameters (τ) on the convergence rate isn't explored in detail. A more systematic comparison against other solvers or acceleration techniques (e.g., FISTA) would strengthen the empirical evaluation.  The experiments also don’t investigate the performance on different data dimensions (m, n) or levels of noise/missingness.
* **Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the theory is strong, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the *practical* implications of the linear convergence result. What does this mean for solving real-world problems? How much faster can we expect the PGM to converge compared to sublinear methods in practice?
* **Proof Structure:** The reliance on a significant amount of material being relegated to the supplementary material, while common, makes it harder for the reader to assess the full rigor of the argument without substantial effort. 



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical result addressing an important open question in the optimization of trace norm regularized problems.
* The technical approach is novel and well-motivated, demonstrating a deep understanding of the underlying mathematical structures.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented (despite some density in the proofs).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical depth of the proofs may limit accessibility to a wider audience.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The practical implications of the result could be discussed in more detail.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a **strong accept**. The theoretical contribution is substantial, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated the linear convergence of the PGM under realistic conditions.  While the technical density and limited scope of the experiments are minor weaknesses, they do not outweigh the significance of the result.  Addressing the suggestions for improvement (particularly providing more detailed explanations within the proofs and expanding the empirical evaluation) would further strengthen the paper.  This work is likely to be highly influential in the field of trace norm regularization and will be of great interest to researchers and practitioners working on low-rank learning and related problems.

**Confidence:** 9/10.
