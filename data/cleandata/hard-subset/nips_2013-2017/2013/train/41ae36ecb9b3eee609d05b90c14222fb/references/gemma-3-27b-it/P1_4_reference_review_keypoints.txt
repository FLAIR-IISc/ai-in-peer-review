- Paper proves linear convergence of Proximal Gradient Method for trace-norm regularization when f(X)=h(A(X)), extending results beyond strong convexity.  
- Key technical contribution: a novel Lipschitzian error bound tailored to trace-norm regularization using matrix perturbation theory.  
- Result is significant for practical problems (e.g., matrix completion) where measurement operators prevent strong convexity.  
- Builds on and correctly situates within prior PGM and trace-norm literature, leveraging Luo and Tseng’s framework.  
- The condition f(X)=h(A(X)) is sufficiently general to cover many common loss functions.  
- Paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and uses consistent notation.  
- Numerical experiments demonstrate linear convergence on matrix completion and classification tasks.  
- Related work discussion is thorough and accurately highlights prior limitations and gaps.  
- Main-body proof sketch lacks sufficient detail and relies heavily on supplementary material and lemmas.  
- Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity on compact subsets) may be restrictive; robustness or relaxations are not discussed.  
- Numerical exploration is limited; effects of step sizes and the measurement operator on convergence are not fully investigated.  
- The paper establishes linear rate but does not discuss the magnitude of the constant factor affecting practical performance.  
- Propositions 4.2 and 4.3 are technically dense and could be streamlined with clearer connections to the main proof.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical result, substantial technical contribution, supportive experiments, and clear exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: complex proofs dependent on supplements, insufficient discussion of practical assumptions, and limited experiments.  
- Overall assessment: valuable and potentially influential contribution, but concerns about proof accessibility and practical discussion remain.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions, including more detailed proof sketch, discussion of Assumption 2.1(b), expanded experiments, discussion of the convergence constant, and streamlining Propositions 4.2–4.3.
