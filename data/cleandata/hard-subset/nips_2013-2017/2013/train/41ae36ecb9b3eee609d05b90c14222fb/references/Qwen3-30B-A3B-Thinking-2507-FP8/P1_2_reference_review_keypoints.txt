- Summary: Shows linear convergence of proximal gradient for trace-norm regularization under weaker conditions (f(X)=h(A(X)) with h smooth/curved) by proving a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm.  
- Strength 1 (Technical Contribution): Presents a novel error bound for trace-norm regularization using matrix perturbation theory and singular-vector structure.  
- Strength 2 (Rigorous Analysis): Provides careful, well-structured proofs and situates the approach relative to prior ℓ₁/group LASSO error-bound work.  
- Strength 3 (Empirical Validation): Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification display the predicted linear convergence in practice.  
- Strength 4 (Applicability): The convergence conditions cover many ML problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning) where f is not strongly convex but of the form h(A(X)).  
- Weakness 1 (Assumption Scope): Requiring h to be strictly convex and satisfy specific smoothness/curvature may be restrictive and needs discussion on relaxations or necessity.  
- Weakness 2 (Practical Implementation): Does not provide guidance on computing the Lipschitz constant Lf or choosing step sizes for PGM in practice.  
- Weakness 3 (Limited Experiments): Empirical validation is narrow (only two problem types) and lacks variation in sizes, ranks, and data distributions.  
- Weakness 4 (Relation to Recent Work): Could better connect to recent non-convex low-rank methods and adaptive step-size approaches.  
- Assessment — Quality: Technically sound with rigorous proofs and supporting experiments.  
- Assessment — Clarity: Well-organized and clear but some technical proof details may be hard for non-experts.  
- Assessment — Originality: Resolves an open question by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm.  
- Assessment — Significance: Results meaningfully extend theoretical understanding of first-order methods for low-rank problems and relax strong-convexity requirements.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel theoretical result, rigorous analysis, broad applicability, and empirical support.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Restrictive assumptions, limited numerical scope, and lacking practical parameter-selection guidance.
