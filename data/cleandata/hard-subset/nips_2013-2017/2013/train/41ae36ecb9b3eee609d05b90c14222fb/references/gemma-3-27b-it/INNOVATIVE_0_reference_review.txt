This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems, specifically demonstrating linear convergence under conditions weaker than strong convexity of the loss function. This is a notable contribution, as existing linear convergence results typically require strong convexity. The authors cleverly leverage matrix perturbation theory and establish a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, which is the key to their analysis. The connection to existing work on error bounds (ℓ1 regularization, group LASSO) is clearly articulated, and the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm are well-explained. The paper is well-structured and the mathematical exposition is generally clear.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The primary strength of this work is its novel contribution to the convergence analysis of PGM. Establishing linear convergence *without* strong convexity is a significant advancement, broadening the applicability of the method.
*   **Technical Depth:** The proof strategy, employing a novel Lipschitzian error bound and matrix perturbation theory, is sophisticated and demonstrates a strong technical understanding of the problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The theoretical results appear sound, and the assumptions are clearly stated. The connection to existing results (Luo-Tseng framework) is well-established.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The established results are broadly applicable to a variety of problems where trace norm regularization is used, including matrix completion and multi-task learning, as highlighted in the introduction and preliminary sections.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments, though not exhaustive, provides some initial validation of the theoretical findings. The observed linear convergence in the experiments is encouraging.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited. While they demonstrate linear convergence in certain scenarios, a more extensive evaluation across different problem sizes, noise levels, and operator properties would strengthen the empirical validation. The choice of step size in the matrix classification experiments using backtracking line search isn’t sufficiently justified.
*   **Assumptions on *h*:** Assumption 2.1, requiring strict convexity of *h* on the domain, is a reasonable starting point, but its practical implications deserve further discussion. How sensitive are the results to violations of this assumption?
*   **Proof Complexity:** While rigorous, the proof is quite lengthy and dense. Making it more accessible would enhance the paper's impact. More explanation could be given for key propositions (e.g. 4.2) before referencing them in the proof.
*   **Limited Discussion of Constant Factors:** The convergence rate is given as linear, but the constant factors influencing the convergence speed are not explicitly discussed. These factors can be critical in practice.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result that significantly advances our understanding of the convergence properties of PGM for trace norm regularization.
*   The relaxation of the strong convexity requirement opens up new avenues for applying PGM to a wider range of machine learning problems.
*   The technical contributions (Lipschitzian error bound, matrix perturbation theory application) are substantial.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured, despite the technical complexity.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental validation is limited and could be strengthened.
*   The assumptions on the function *h* might be restrictive in some applications.
*   The proof's complexity could hinder broader adoption of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution. While the experimental validation could be more comprehensive, the novelty and rigor of the analysis outweigh this weakness. The authors have addressed a challenging problem and provided a valuable result that is likely to be influential in the field. I believe the paper is currently acceptable for NIPS, given its high degree of novelty and theoretical importance. Minor revisions to improve the clarity of the proof and expand the experimental evaluation would further strengthen the paper. I rate this as a **Strong Accept**.
