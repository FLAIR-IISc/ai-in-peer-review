

# Review: On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization

This paper addresses an important open question in optimization for trace norm-regularized problems: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) can achieve linear convergence without requiring the loss function to be strongly convex. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables them to prove linear convergence of PGM under the assumption that the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h satisfying certain smoothness and convexity properties. This is significant because in many applications (e.g., matrix completion), the loss function is not strongly convex, yet the authors show linear convergence is still achievable.

The main technical contribution is the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which resolves an open question about whether a Lipschitzian error bound exists for trace norm regularization (unlike ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization). The key insight is recognizing that at optimal solutions, X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors, allowing the authors to use matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound. The proof is thorough and carefully addresses the specific challenges of trace norm regularization compared to other norms.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper makes a clear and significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question in the literature. The error bound for trace norm regularization is novel and fills an important gap.
2. The proof is technically sound and well-structured, with careful attention to the specific challenges of trace norm regularization (e.g., non-polyhedral epigraph).
3. The paper is well-written with clear notation and good organization. The motivation for the work is well-explained, highlighting the practical relevance in applications like matrix completion.
4. The numerical experiments complement the theoretical results, demonstrating linear convergence in practice for both matrix completion and matrix classification problems.
5. The connection to Luo and Tseng's convergence analysis framework is well-motivated and properly applied.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify the practical scope of the assumptions. While the authors show that common loss functions (square loss, logistic loss) fit their framework, it's unclear how broadly applicable these assumptions are. For example, the restriction that f must take the form h(A(X)) might exclude some important applications.
2. The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope (only two problems). More diverse experiments across different applications would better demonstrate the practical significance of the theoretical results.
3. The paper doesn't explicitly compare the practical convergence rates of PGM under their assumptions to other methods (e.g., alternating direction methods or accelerated variants) beyond the theoretical rates.
4. The relationship between the error bound established and previous error bounds for other norms (like ℓ1) could be more clearly articulated, particularly regarding the differences in assumptions and implications.

**Overall assessment:**
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the optimization literature for trace norm-regularized problems. The main result (Theorem 3.1) is novel, well-justified, and addresses a well-motivated open question. The technical proof is rigorous and carefully addresses the specific challenges of trace norm regularization. The numerical experiments provide reasonable support for the theoretical results.

The paper meets the quality standard with a technically sound contribution that advances the state of the art. The clarity is good, with the paper being well-organized and clearly written. The originality is strong, as the error bound for trace norm regularization has been an open question. The significance is high for the optimization community and for machine learning practitioners working with trace norm regularization.

**Recommendation:**
Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an important open question in optimization for trace norm-regularized problems. The results are of broad interest to the NIPS community, particularly to researchers working on convex optimization methods for machine learning. While the numerical experiments could be expanded, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper is well-written and makes a clear, valuable contribution to the literature.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. In the introduction, more clearly articulate the practical scope of the assumptions (f(X) = h(A(X))) and discuss potential limitations in real-world applications.
2. Expand the numerical experiments to include more diverse applications or larger-scale problems.
3. Include a brief discussion comparing the practical convergence rates of PGM under these assumptions to other methods for trace norm regularization.
4. More explicitly contrast the error bound for trace norm with those for other norms (like ℓ1), highlighting the key differences in assumptions and implications.
