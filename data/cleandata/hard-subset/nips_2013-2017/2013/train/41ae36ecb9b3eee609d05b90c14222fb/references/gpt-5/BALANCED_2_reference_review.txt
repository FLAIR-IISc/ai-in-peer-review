Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for convex objectives with a nuclear-norm regularizer, F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with a linear operator A and a smooth strictly convex function h. The key result is an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM without assuming strong convexity of f. The authors establish a local Lipschitzian error bound: dist(X, X*) ≤ η||Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||F for points in a bounded level set and with small residual. Plugging this error bound into the Luo-Tseng framework [10] yields Q-linear convergence in objective values and R-linear convergence in iterates for step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) with ᾱ < 1/Lf. The analysis hinges on a structural observation at the optimum: X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors (via the fixed-point characterization with singular-value shrinkage), enabling an SVD-based perturbation argument to prove a “restricted invertibility” property despite potentially non-injective A. The result covers common losses (squared, logistic) and explains the fast local behavior observed in practice. Prior linear-rate results for nonsmooth composite problems relied on polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) [22,23,25] or strong convexity [16]; the nuclear norm is non-polyhedral [18], and linear rates for PGM without strong convexity were open for this setting. The empirical section shows linear decay (semi-log plots) on small matrix completion and matrix classification problems.

Strengths
- Novel theoretical contribution: a local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under broad losses f(X) = h(A(X)), resolving a recognized gap versus ℓ1/group norms.
- Careful and insightful analysis: the simultaneous SVD of X* and −∇f(X*) and the ensuing perturbation argument are elegant and technically nontrivial; the “restricted invertibility” near the solution set is particularly interesting.
- Broad applicability: covers widely used losses (square, logistic) and arbitrary linear A, where f need not be strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion).
- Clear convergence statement: R-linear iterates and Q-linear objective values once in the local region; integrates cleanly with the Luo–Tseng framework.
- Empirical corroboration: experiments illustrate the predicted local linear behavior for two representative tasks.

Weaknesses
- Local (asymptotic) nature: the error bound requires small residuals and bounded level sets; there is no explicit global complexity to enter the linear regime or identification guarantees (e.g., finite-time manifold identification).
- Assumption 2.1(b): “h strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)” is essentially a local strong convexity assumption; while satisfied by standard smooth strictly convex losses (e.g., squared and logistic), the paper could better discuss scope and provide explicit curvature constants as a function of the level set.
- Practical constants: the proof yields existence of η, ε, κ, σ, but no estimates or guidance on their magnitudes, making it hard to predict rates or regions where linear convergence kicks in.
- Experiments are limited in scale and breadth: n = 40, no comparison to accelerated PGM, restarted schemes, or alternative solvers; no wall-clock or SVD cost analysis; no study of sensitivity to τ, noise, or rank.
- Minor clarity issues: step-size choice in matrix completion uses α = 1, which violates the stated assumption αk < 1/Lf if Lf = 1 (as it is for a projection sampling operator). Some notational inconsistencies (τ vs μ) and several reference entries appear incomplete.

Quality
The technical development appears sound and well-motivated. The central lemmas (asymptotic optimality, boundedness, and the restricted invertibility) plausibly follow from the structure induced by proximal fixed points and SVD perturbations; however, several key steps are deferred to the supplement (e.g., inequalities (9)–(11), Propositions 4.2 and 4.3). The theorem statements are precise, but the constants are abstract. Experiments support the claims qualitatively.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable given the technical depth. The high-level proof roadmap is clear, and the role of each lemma in the Luo–Tseng framework is explicit. Clarity would benefit from fixing minor inconsistencies (step size, τ/μ) and from a more explicit discussion of Assumption 2.1(b), constants, and the locality of the result.

Originality
High. Providing a nuclear-norm error bound without strong convexity of f and leveraging spectral alignment for the analysis are, to my knowledge, new in this precise form relative to prior work on ℓ1/group LASSO error bounds and standard strong-convexity-based linear rates.

Significance
Solid. PGM is a workhorse for nuclear-norm problems; explaining and guaranteeing its linear local behavior under broad conditions is practically and theoretically valuable. The result may inspire analogous analyses for other unitarily invariant regularizers.

Constructive suggestions
- Discuss Assumption 2.1(b) more concretely: for square and logistic losses, provide explicit curvature lower bounds σ on the relevant compact set Z (e.g., depending on bounds on A(X) along the level set).
- Clarify the step-size conditions in experiments: for matrix completion, either choose α < 1/Lf explicitly or explain why α = 1 is safe (e.g., exact Lf and using ≤ in practice).
- Provide guidance on constants and region of linear convergence: even qualitative dependence on ||A||, L, τ, and the level-set diameter would improve interpretability.
- Extend experiments: larger n, partial/truncated SVDs, wall-clock plots; compare with FISTA and restart strategies; test sensitivity to τ, rank, sampling ratio, and noise.
- Related work: briefly connect to identification/partial smoothness perspectives and error-bound-based linear convergence in composite optimization more broadly; ensure references are complete with years/pages.

Arguments for acceptance
- New and technically strong contribution (error bound and linear convergence) for a widely used non-polyhedral regularizer.
- Applicable to common ML problems; clarifies observed practice.
- Proof ideas (simultaneous SVD, restricted invertibility) are insightful and likely reusable.

Arguments against acceptance
- Linear convergence is only local; no global iteration complexity to reach the linear regime.
- Experimental section is limited and lacks comparisons and scalability analysis.
- Some clarity and rigor gaps in the presentation (deferred proofs, step-size mismatch).

Recommendation
Accept (borderline strong). The theoretical contribution is novel and relevant, the analysis is nontrivial and well-motivated, and the scope covers important applications. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying assumptions/constants would further improve the paper.
