Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems of the form minX F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, with f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is smooth, strictly convex, and “locally” strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain. This covers common losses such as squared and logistic loss composed with linear measurements A. While classical results guarantee sublinear O(1/k) or O(1/k^2) convergence for first-order methods, and linear rates when f is strongly convex, the present setting allows A to be rank-deficient so f need not be strongly convex in X. The main contribution is an asymptotic linear convergence result for PGM without requiring strong convexity of f, established via a new local Lipschitzian error bound: dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F for points in a level set with small residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Combining this error bound with the Luo–Tseng framework yields Q-linear convergence of the objective and R-linear convergence of the iterates for step sizes αk bounded away from 0 and strictly below 1/Lf.

The error bound is nontrivial because the nuclear norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, unlike the ℓ1 or group LASSO cases where such bounds are known (Tseng, Zhang et al.). The key structural insight is that at any optimal solution X̄, the matrices X̄, −∇f(X̄), and X̄ − ∇f(X̄) share the same left and right singular vectors (a consequence of the fixed-point characterization X̄ = Sτ(X̄ − ∇f(X̄))). This spectral alignment enables a refined perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” inequality along sequences approaching the solution set, which in turn implies the desired error bound.

Numerical experiments on synthetic matrix completion (squared loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrate linear convergence in the asymptotic regime.

Quality
- The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The simultaneous SVD observation at optimality is correct via the proximal fixed-point relation: if X̄ = Sτ(X̄ − Ḡ), then X̄ − Ḡ, X̄, and −Ḡ share singular vectors.
- The Luo–Tseng error-bound-based analysis is standard and appropriate once the new bound is established.
- Some proof details (e.g., the decomposition in Proposition 4.3 and parts of Theorem 3.2) are deferred to the supplement. Given the centrality of the error bound, having a bit more of its proof in the main text would improve verifiability.
- Assumptions are reasonable for common losses: logistic and squared loss satisfy the “strongly convex on any compact subset” premise, ensuring a positive curvature parameter on bounded level sets.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear and well organized. The optimization model and assumptions are stated early, the residual map is defined, and the overall proof roadmap is explicit.
- Minor clarity issues: inconsistent notation for the regularization parameter (τ vs μ) across sections; figure numbering/text mismatches (e.g., “Figure 1 shows ...” but only Figure 2 is labeled); occasional typos.
- The role and necessity of Assumption 2.1(b) could be emphasized: it provides a uniform lower curvature bound σ on the relevant compact set, which is crucial in inequality (15).

Originality
- The Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under the stated composite structure appears novel and addresses a gap compared to known results for separable polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1/group LASSO).
- The spectral-structure-based perturbation argument (using simultaneous SVD and projection onto Xc) is a nice, problem-specific idea not directly available for separable norms.

Significance
- Error bounds are a key tool for establishing linear convergence of first-order methods. Extending them to nuclear-norm regularization is of broad interest for low-rank learning problems.
- The result is asymptotic and local (small residual in a level set), which is standard in this literature but still practically meaningful.
- The experiments are limited in scope (small n, synthetic, only PGM) but consistent with the theory. Broader empirical validation (e.g., larger-scale problems, comparisons to accelerated or restarted variants, and reporting of residuals) would strengthen the impact.

Pros
- Novel error bound for trace-norm regularization with non-polyhedral geometry.
- Conceptually clean use of proximal fixed-point structure and Luo–Tseng framework.
- Covers common losses (square/logistic) without requiring f to be strongly convex in X.
- Clear asymptotic linear convergence guarantees for both iterates and objective.

Cons
- Asymptotic/Local result; no global linear rate or rate constants that reflect problem geometry (e.g., dependence on A and τ).
- Some technical details relegated to the supplement; core steps (Proposition 4.3) could use more intuition in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation is minimal (small synthetic problems; no comparisons; limited diagnostics).
- Notation inconsistencies and figure/labeling issues.

Questions and suggestions
- Please clarify whether the error bound constants (η, ε) can be expressed in terms of problem parameters (e.g., σ from (15), ‖A‖, L) to give some sense of conditioning.
- Discuss connections to partial smoothness/active manifold identification for the nuclear norm and how your error bound relates to KL-exponent-based linear rates in forward–backward splitting (this could position your contribution relative to broader convergence frameworks).
- Consider adding a brief proof sketch of Proposition 4.3 in the main paper to convey the key idea behind the A/B/C case analysis and the construction of Q̄.
- Improve experiments: include larger-scale instances; report residual norms ‖R(Xk)‖ and distances to the solution set proxy; compare with accelerated PG (with and without restart) to highlight when your guarantee translates into practical speedups; provide runtime/SVD counts.
- Fix notation (use τ consistently), ensure figure numbering matches text, and proofread for minor typos.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution—a new Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization and the ensuing linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity—is technically interesting and relevant to the NeurIPS community working on optimization for low-rank models. The proof ideas are elegant and tailored to the non-polyhedral geometry. The main weaknesses are local/asymptotic scope and limited empirical validation, but these do not detract from the core theoretical value. Strengthening clarity and experiments would further improve the paper.
