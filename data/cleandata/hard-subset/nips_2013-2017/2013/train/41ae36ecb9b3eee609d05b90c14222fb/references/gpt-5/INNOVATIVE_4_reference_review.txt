Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = h(A(X)) + τ||X||*, where A is linear and h is strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient and “local” strong convexity (strongly convex on any compact convex subset of dom h). The main contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal set to the proximal-gradient residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence (Q-linear in objective, R-linear in iterates) of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with fixed step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf), without assuming strong convexity of f = h ∘ A (A may be rank-deficient).

This addresses a notable gap: prior linear-rate results for PGM typically rely on strong convexity (or variants like restricted strong convexity), or on polyhedral regularizers where error bounds are known (e.g., ℓ1, group lasso). For nuclear norm, the epigraph is nonpolyhedral, and the existing “group-splitting” techniques used for ℓ1/group-lasso error bounds do not port. The paper’s key technical insight is a spectral alignment property at optimality (X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors), used with matrix perturbation to control local spectral structure and establish the error bound.

The work thus connects the nonpolyhedral nuclear norm case to the Luo–Tseng error bound machinery and extends linear convergence guarantees of first-order methods to a broader class of widely used losses (square, logistic) composed with arbitrary linear operators.

Strengths:
- Novelty: A new Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm-regularized problems. To my knowledge, this is the first such result enabling linear rates for PGM without strong convexity of f in this setting.
- Conceptual contribution: The simultaneous SVD characterization at optimality (Prop. 4.2) and its use in the error-bound proof are elegant and may be of independent interest.
- Generality: Covers f = h ∘ A with arbitrary linear A (not injective), and h including square and logistic losses. The error bound and rate statements are local but apply under mild, practical conditions (bounded level sets).
- Theoretical clarity: The route from the error bound to linear convergence via Luo–Tseng is standard and clean; the residual map is natural and computable.
- Experiments: While simple, the semi-log plots illustrate the predicted linear regime near the solution for both square and logistic losses.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Only asymptotic/local linear rates: The constants (η, ε) in the error bound and the contraction factors are unspecified and may depend on unknown problem quantities. A discussion on how they scale with A and h would help.
- Practical computation: The analysis assumes exact proximal steps (singular value thresholding). In large-scale regimes, partial/inexact SVDs are used. Can the error bound and linear rate extend to inexact PGM (e.g., Schmidt et al., 2011) with quantified accuracy requirements?
- Positioning vs. broader theory: It would be useful to relate the result to general KL-based convergence rate theory for semi-algebraic convex functions and to restricted strong convexity (RSC) frameworks. When RSC holds for h ∘ A, can one obtain global linear rates? How does your EB compare/contrast with KL exponents in this problem?
- Acceleration: Do analogous linear-rate statements hold for accelerated schemes (e.g., FISTA with restart), perhaps under the same EB? Even a brief discussion would increase practical impact.
- Assumptions on h: “Strongly convex on any convex compact subset” is somewhat unusual; please clarify sufficient conditions (e.g., twice continuously differentiable with uniformly positive definite Hessian on bounded sets) and how they hold for logistic loss given unbounded domain (relying on bounded level sets).
- Scope of the spectral alignment: The simultaneous SVD at optimality is key; a brief intuitive explanation in the main text (beyond a proposition) would improve readability. Are there counterexamples if h is merely convex but not strictly convex?
- Experiments: Limited scale and no comparisons (e.g., to methods exploiting RSC or to accelerated variants). While I appreciate that the contribution is theoretical, a small ablation on inexact prox or varying condition numbers of A would strengthen empirical support.
- Presentation: Some references lack years; minor notation inconsistencies (e.g., τ vs. μ in experiments; αk subscripts); ensure prox operator notation is consistent.

Quality: The technical core appears sound, with a carefully constructed proof architecture and reasonable assumptions for common ML losses. Some details are deferred to the supplement; given the importance of Prop. 4.2 and Prop. 4.3, ensuring these are fully rigorous is essential.

Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The motivation for why nuclear norm evades prior polyhedral EB techniques is clear, and the residual-based EB is well explained. Minor editorial fixes suggested above.

Originality: High. Establishing a Lipschitzian EB for nuclear norm and deriving linear convergence without strong convexity fills a genuine gap.

Significance: The result is timely and relevant to a large body of work using nuclear norm regularization. The proof techniques may seed further advances for other nonpolyhedral regularizers (e.g., spectral functions, Schatten norms).

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, novel theoretical contribution (new error bound) with broad applicability.
- Advances understanding of convergence rates in a central problem class without strong convexity.
- Clean integration with established Luo–Tseng framework; plausible to extend.

Cons for acceptance:
- Asymptotic/local nature; constants and practical implications not quantified.
- Limited experimental validation; no treatment of inexact prox or accelerated variants.
- Positioning vs. KL/RSC literature could be strengthened.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper introduces a significant new concept (a Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm-regularized problems) and leverages it to obtain linear convergence of a widely used method beyond the strong convexity regime. Despite modest experiments and local rates, the theoretical advance is substantive and likely to influence subsequent work.

Reviewer confidence: High.
