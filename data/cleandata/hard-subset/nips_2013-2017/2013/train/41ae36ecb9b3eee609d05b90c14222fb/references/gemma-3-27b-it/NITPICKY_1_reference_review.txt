This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization, specifically aiming to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under the assumption that the loss function can be expressed as h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with suitable smoothness and curvature properties. They derive a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure near the optimal solution. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical gap in the theoretical understanding of PGM's convergence in machine learning contexts where loss functions are often non-strongly convex.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result for trace norm regularization *without* strong convexity is a significant contribution. Prior results typically rely on strong convexity assumptions.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound and the subsequent linear convergence proof appear technically sound, drawing on established analysis frameworks (Luo & Tseng) and matrix perturbation theory.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The result covers a wide range of loss functions commonly used in applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning.
*   **Clear Presentation (mostly):** The introduction clearly articulates the problem and contributions. The mathematical notation is generally standard.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Excessive Detail in Introduction:** While thorough, the introduction lists an overwhelming number of applications. This feels somewhat gratuitous and detracts from the core message.
*   **Ambiguous Assumption 2.1(b):** The statement that h being strongly convex on *any* convex compact subset of dom(h) implies strict convexity on dom(h) is not universally true and requires more precise justification. This subtle point undermines the theoretical foundation.
*   **Insufficient Justification of Proposition 4.2:** The crucial Proposition 4.2, which forms the basis for the error bound, is stated without sufficient intuition or explanation. Why does this specific singular value decomposition structure hold? A more detailed explanation, perhaps with a sketch of the proof, is crucial for understanding the core innovation. The claim of simultaneuos SVD seems quite strong.
*   **Overly Convoluted Proof Structure:** The proof of Lemma 4.1 and Theorem 3.1 is dense and relies heavily on numerous sub-claims (Propositions 4.2 and 4.3) presented without full proofs in the main manuscript (referred to as "supplementary material"). The supplementary material *must* be meticulously reviewed to ensure its correctness; currently, this is not feasible during the review process.
*   **Numerical Experiments Limited:** The numerical experiments are rather basic. While they demonstrate the expected linear convergence, they lack a comprehensive comparison with alternative algorithms or a sensitivity analysis to different parameter settings. The choice of step size α = 1 seems arbitrary and could be improved with a more systematic line search procedure.
*   **Formatting Issues:** Minor formatting inconsistencies are present throughout the manuscript (e.g., inconsistent spacing, line breaks). Specifically, the use of \pmb for bolding in some equations but not others is jarring.
*   **Redundancy:** Some statements are repeated unnecessarily, adding to the overall length of the paper.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   In Assumption 2.1(b), could the authors provide a more rigorous argument for why strong convexity on *any* convex compact subset implies strict convexity on the domain?
*   The justification for Proposition 4.2 is extremely thin. A more detailed explanation or a proof sketch is essential.
*   What is the computational cost of verifying the condition ‖R(X)‖F ≤ ǫ in practice? Is this computationally feasible for large-scale problems?
*   The paper claims that the result is the *first* linear convergence result without strong convexity. Is this claim truly exhaustive? Are there related works that explore different conditions for linear convergence in similar settings?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution is significant and addresses an important open problem.
*   The mathematical analysis appears rigorous (pending a thorough review of the supplementary material).
*   The result has potential implications for a wide range of machine learning applications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws, including ambiguous assumptions, insufficient justification of key claims, a convoluted proof structure, limited numerical experiments, and formatting inconsistencies.
*   The lack of a complete proof in the main manuscript (relying heavily on supplementary material) hinders a comprehensive assessment of the technical validity.
*   The overall presentation, while generally clear, is overly dense and lacks sufficient intuition.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea and theoretical contribution are valuable, the numerous technical shortcomings and the reliance on extensive supplementary material prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. The paper demonstrates a high level of technical expertise but falls short of the polish and clarity expected for a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit in the future. Specifically, the authors *must* provide a more detailed justification for Proposition 4.2, clarify the ambiguous assumption 2.1(b), and improve the clarity and completeness of the proof. A more comprehensive set of numerical experiments would also significantly strengthen the paper. The lack of attention to detail (formatting, redundancy) is also concerning.
