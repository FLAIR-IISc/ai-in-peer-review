Summary and contributions
The paper studies the proximal gradient method (PGM) for nuclear-norm (trace-norm) regularized problems of the form minX f(X) + τ||X||*, where the smooth loss f factors as f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h convex, C1 with Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex, and “locally strongly convex” on any compact convex subset of its domain. The main technical result is a local Lipschitz error bound that links the distance to the optimal solution set to the residual norm ||Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||F. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework, the authors show that PGM with a fixed stepsize bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf converges R-linearly in iterates and Q-linearly in objective values, despite f possibly not being strongly convex (e.g., when A is not injective). The proof hinges on a spectral-alignment property at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors) and a “restricted invertibility” inequality that controls directions near the optimal set. Numerical illustrations on small matrix completion and matrix classification problems show an asymptotic linear phase.

Relation to prior work
- For composite convex problems with polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO), Luo–Tseng-type error bounds and linear convergence of first-order methods are known (Tseng & Yun; Zhang–Jiang–Luo). However, the nuclear norm is non-polyhedral, and existing separable arguments do not apply.
- The paper fills this gap by proving an error bound tailored to the nuclear norm via spectral analysis. The simultaneous SVD property is consistent with the subdifferential characterization of ||·||* and has appeared implicitly in nuclear-norm optimality conditions, but its use to derive a local error bound and linear convergence seems novel in this context.
- There is a broader KL/Lojasiewicz-based literature giving local linear rates for proximal algorithms under KL exponents, but those results typically require additional structure to certify the exponent; for nuclear norm, sharp exponents are subtle. The present work offers a concrete, verifiable pathway under widely used h (square/logistic).

Quality and technical soundness
- The main results (Theorem 3.1 error bound; Theorem 3.2 linear rate) are well-motivated and technically careful. The structure of the proof (Lemma 4.1 with asymptotic optimality, boundedness, and restricted invertibility) is coherent. The contradiction argument using projections onto a carefully chosen subset Xc and a perturbation along a limit direction Q̄ is elegant.
- The spectral alignment (Proposition 4.2) follows from subdifferential calculus for the trace norm; the decomposition into cases (A/B/C) and use of matrix shrinkage are consistent with standard matrix perturbation arguments.
- Some crucial proofs are deferred to the supplement (e.g., Proposition 4.3, constants κ1–κ3 in Theorem 3.2), which is acceptable but makes it harder to fully audit the arguments in the main text. It would help to bring a concise proof sketch of the most novel steps (e.g., the restricted invertibility property) into the main paper.
- The assumptions on h (C1 with L-Lipschitz gradient and locally strongly convex on compacts) are reasonable and cover square and logistic losses; the analysis correctly observes that f need not be strongly convex due to A. The linear rate is asymptotic/local, which is clearly stated.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The motivation for moving from residual norm to distance via an error bound is clear. The algorithm and residual map are standard, and the connection to Luo–Tseng is explicit.
- Notational consistency is mostly good; however, the experiments switch to µ for the regularization parameter while the theory uses τ—please unify. A few references lack years, and some formatting is off; please fix.
- Minor point: The stepsize condition in Theorem 3.2 requires αk < 1/Lf, yet the matrix completion experiment uses α = 1. For the projection sampling operator, Lf = 1; thus α = 1 violates the strict inequality. Either clarify that numerically the method still behaves well with α = 1, or set α = 0.99 to be consistent with the theorem.

Originality
- The core novelty is the Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization without assuming strong convexity of f, obtained via spectral structure near the optimal set. To my knowledge, such a bound has not been established in prior convex composite analyses for nuclear norm. This is a meaningful extension of error-bound techniques beyond polyhedral regularizers.

Significance
- PGM is a workhorse for nuclear-norm regularized learning; a clean, verifiable condition ensuring asymptotic linear rates without strong convexity of f is valuable theoretically and practically. The result can inform stopping criteria, stepsize choices, and could extend to other algorithms (e.g., coordinate descent, proximal Newton, ADMM) that benefit from local error bounds.
- The paper’s constants are implicit, and the linear regime is local; nonetheless, the result advances understanding and could spur follow-up work on computable constants and global-to-local strategies (e.g., restart schemes).

Experiments
- The experiments are small but sufficient to illustrate the predicted linear phase. It would strengthen the empirical section to:
  - Report Lf and stepsize used (and verify αk < 1/Lf or explain deviations).
  - Include a comparison to accelerated PGM with restart (which often exhibits local linear rates).
  - Show when the iterates enter the linear regime (e.g., via residual norm threshold) and discuss sensitivity to τ.

Pros
- Addresses an open question: linear convergence of PGM for nuclear-norm problems without strong convexity of f.
- Solid, technically novel error-bound proof based on spectral structure.
- Broadly applicable assumptions (square/logistic loss).
- Clear linkage to Luo–Tseng framework; results are crisp (Q-linear in objective, R-linear in iterates).

Cons
- Asymptotic/local result with implicit constants; practical guidance on detecting/ensuring the linear regime is limited.
- Some proof details relegated to the supplement; key steps could be summarized in main text.
- Experiments are small-scale and have minor inconsistencies with theory (stepsize).

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you comment on whether the error-bound constant η can be bounded in terms of problem data (e.g., curvature of h on Z and properties of A at X̄), and whether this yields a computable threshold for entering the linear regime?
- Does a similar argument extend to other unitarily invariant norms (e.g., Schatten p, mixed norms), or to composite penalties like trace + ℓ1?
- Could the analysis be adapted to accelerated PGM with restart to obtain linear rates under the same assumptions?
- Please clarify the strict αk < 1/Lf condition versus α = 1 in the matrix completion experiment.

Minor issues
- Unify notation for the regularization parameter. Add missing years in references and fix formatting.
- A brief discussion connecting your error bound to KL/Lojasiewicz perspectives would broaden context.

Recommendation
This is a strong theoretical paper with a novel and useful contribution to first-order methods for low-rank learning. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the clarity/experiment issues noted above. Confidence: high.
