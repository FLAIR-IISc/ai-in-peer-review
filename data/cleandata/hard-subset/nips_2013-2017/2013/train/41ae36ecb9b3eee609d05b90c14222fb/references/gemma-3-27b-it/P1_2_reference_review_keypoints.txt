- Paper proves linear convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of f.
- Core contribution: a novel Lipschitzian error bound using matrix perturbation theory to handle the trace norm’s non-polyhedral epigraph.
- Supports theory with numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification.
- Extends prior PGM convergence results to settings where the linear operator A may be rank-deficient.
- Correctly contrasts trace-norm challenges with existing l1/group LASSO error bound analyses.
- Novel and significant: linear convergence without strong convexity broadens PGM applicability in ML.
- Technical depth: sophisticated proof leveraging matrix perturbation; Proposition 4.2 is a key insight.
- Presentation: generally clear and well-structured with mostly consistent notation.
- Empirical validation: experiments demonstrate linear convergence in practice.
- Related work coverage: thorough and well-situated within the literature.
- Proof complexity: dense and hard to follow; Lemma 4.1 needs more structured, detailed exposition.
- Assumption on h: Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity on compact subsets) may limit applicability and needs discussion.
- Numerical experiments lack detailed parameters, comparisons to other solvers, and longer convergence plots.
- Limited discussion of constant factors: convergence rate constants and dependence on problem parameters are not tightly characterized.
- Minor clarity issue: explicitly define Lf (Lipschitz constant of ∇f) at first mention.
- Overall assessment: strong, significant theoretical contribution; minor weaknesses do not outweigh merits.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses important open problem, novel technically sound results, good theory and empirical support, well-written.
- Arguments against acceptance: proof accessibility concerns and insufficiently comprehensive experiments.
- Recommendation: Accept with Minor Revisions.
- Requested revisions: break down Lemma 4.1 proof into sub-claims; discuss practical implications of Assumption 2.1(b); provide detailed experiment parameters and solver comparisons; add longer convergence plots; expand description of backtracking line search.
