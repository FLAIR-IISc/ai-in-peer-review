Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex optimization with nuclear-norm regularization:
min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||_* where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h has Lipschitz gradient and is strictly convex with local curvature (strong convexity on compact subsets of dom(h)). The main contribution is an asymptotic linear convergence result for the proximal gradient method (PGM) when f need not be strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with underdetermined A). The proof hinges on establishing a local Lipschitzian error bound of Luo–Tseng type: dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||R(X)||_F for points with small residual R(X) = S_τ(X − ∇f(X)) − X. With this error bound and standard descent properties of PGM, the paper shows Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates, for fixed step sizes α_k ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/L_f).

Prior work has shown linear convergence of PGM under strong convexity of f (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011) and error bounds for other nonsmooth regularizers (ℓ1, group lasso). The open question has been whether an error bound holds for nuclear norm regularization, whose epigraph is nonpolyhedral. The paper resolves this by exploiting a structural property: at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors; this allows a matrix-perturbation-based restricted invertibility estimate that substitutes for strong convexity. The work fits the Luo–Tseng error bound framework and complements results in proximal first-order optimization.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: Establishes a Luo–Tseng-type error bound for nuclear norm regularization, which has been elusive due to nonpolyhedral geometry. The spectral-alignment argument (Proposition 4.2) and the construction of X_c are insightful.
- Generality: Covers many loss functions used in practice (squared loss, logistic loss), even when f is not strongly convex due to a non-injective A.
- Clean consequence: Once the error bound is in place, the linear rate for PGM follows with standard arguments. The result (Theorem 3.2) is easy to apply in practice with fixed step sizes below 1/L_f.
- Solid and careful analysis: The key lemma provides asymptotic optimality, boundedness, and a restricted invertibility inequality; the contradiction proof is well structured.
- Clarity of the main idea: The role of spectral structure at the solution is clearly articulated and seems of independent interest.

Weaknesses
- Scope of linear rate: The result is local/asymptotic (requires small residual and bounded level set). This is clearly stated but limits practical guarantees when far from the solution.
- Assumption 2.1(b): “Strongly convex on any convex compact subset” is somewhat unusual; the paper could better explain this assumption and give explicit constants (σ) for canonical h (e.g., logistic loss) and how they enter the rate constants.
- Residual map/step-size mismatch: The error bound is stated for R(X) defined with τ and unit step, while the algorithm uses prox_{α_k τ}(X − α_k ∇f(X)). The connection (inequality (11)) is given later, but the exposition would benefit from defining a step-size–aware residual R_α and stating the error bound equivalently for it.
- Experiments are minimal: Only synthetic tasks; no comparisons to FISTA/accelerated methods or alternative solvers (SVT, ADMM/ALM), and no real datasets. Moreover, in the matrix completion experiment, α = 1 is used even though Theorem 3.2 requires α_k < 1/L_f. For sampling operators, L_f = 1, so α should be strictly less than 1 to match the theory.
- Related work coverage: It would be helpful to relate the error-bound approach to the KL/PL frameworks that also yield linear rates for composite problems under local sharpness (e.g., KL exponent 1/2), and to partially smooth regularizers; while your contribution is the first, to my knowledge, to prove a Luo–Tseng error bound tailored to the nuclear norm, discussing these connections would strengthen positioning.

Quality
The analysis appears technically sound, with clear identification of the main bottleneck (nonpolyhedral geometry) and a credible resolution via spectral alignment and perturbation theory. Proof sketches are convincing; details are deferred appropriately. Some constants’ dependencies (η, ε, κ_i) are not explicit; a discussion of how they scale with A, τ, L, σ would improve reproducibility and interpretability.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized, with a logical flow from problem setup to error bound to convergence. A few presentational issues:
- Align the definition of the residual with step size.
- Clarify Assumption 2.1(b) with examples and bounds.
- Fix minor inconsistencies: mixing τ and μ for the regularization parameter, figure numbering (refers to Figure 1/2 inconsistently), and incomplete years in the references.

Originality
High. The core error-bound result for nuclear norm regularization, via simultaneous SVD at optimality and matrix perturbation, appears novel and addresses a gap relative to ℓ1/group-lasso cases.

Significance
Moderate to high. Proximal methods are standard for nuclear-norm problems; a broadly applicable linear-rate guarantee without strong convexity is valuable theoretically and practically (e.g., for stopping criteria and algorithm design). The result is asymptotic, which tempers immediate impact, but the technique may extend to other matrix norms or structured penalties.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves an open technical question (error bound for nuclear norm) with a neat spectral argument.
- Delivers a generally applicable linear convergence guarantee for a widely used method.
- Solid, careful analysis; results likely to be cited and built upon.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experiments are too limited; no baselines, no real data, and a step-size choice that does not strictly meet the theorem’s conditions.
- Some exposition gaps (residual definition vs. algorithm, unusual curvature assumption) and missing connections to related frameworks (KL/PL, partial smoothness).

Suggestions for improvement
- Define R_α(X) = prox_{ατ}(X − α∇f(X)) − X and restate the error bound in this metric, or explicitly show equivalence and constants’ dependence on α ∈ (0, 1/L_f).
- Elaborate Assumption 2.1(b): provide examples (square/logistic), explicit σ(Z) on compact sets, and discuss how σ impacts the rate.
- Provide a discussion of how η depends on A (e.g., through ||A|| and restricted invertibility κ), τ, and the local curvature of h.
- Expand experiments: add FISTA and ADMM baselines, report iteration and time, include at least one real dataset (matrix completion/classification), and ensure step sizes satisfy theoretical conditions. Show when the linear regime “kicks in.”
- Improve related work section: discuss KL-based linear rates and partially smooth regularizers, and clarify how your error bound compares/relates.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is novel and of interest to the NeurIPS optimization community, but the empirical section and some clarity issues should be strengthened.
