## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) and A is a linear operator.  The authors establish linear convergence *without* requiring strong convexity of the loss function f, a significant improvement over existing results.  Their key contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this type of problem, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to account for the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the dependencies between matrix entries. The results cover a wide range of applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The paper is well-motivated, clearly presented, and includes numerical validation of the theoretical results.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Theoretical Result:** The linear convergence guarantee without strong convexity is a substantial contribution to the field.  It expands the applicability of PGM to a wider class of problems commonly encountered in machine learning where strong convexity is often absent.
* **Novel Error Bound:** The derived Lipschitzian error bound is the core of the result and tackles a challenging problem—establishing such a bound for the trace norm, which differs significantly from the well-studied ℓ1-norm. The use of matrix perturbation theory is a clever and effective approach.
* **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the assumptions are explicitly stated, and the proof structure is logical. 
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the existing literature, correctly positioning their work within the context of prior research on proximal algorithms, trace norm regularization, and convergence analysis. They thoroughly acknowledge relevant papers.
* **Supporting Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification lend further support to the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence in practice.  The use of backtracking line search in the matrix classification example acknowledges the practical challenges of finding appropriate step sizes.
* **Strong Justification of Assumptions:** The authors explicitly mention how their assumptions apply to common application scenarios, bolstering the relevance of their work.



**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Proof:** While the presentation is clear, the technical details of the proof (especially Lemma 4.1 and the associated propositions in the Appendix) are dense and may be difficult to fully grasp without significant effort. While this is often the case with theoretical papers, a more intuitive explanation of the key steps could benefit the reader.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the numerical experiments are helpful, the paper doesn’t extensively discuss the practical implications of the linear convergence rate.  For example, how much faster is PGM compared to methods with sublinear convergence in realistic settings, considering factors like constant factors and the Lipschitz constant?
* **Step-Size Sensitivity:**  The assumption that the step size αk is bounded away from 1/Lf (0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf) is fairly restrictive and potentially difficult to satisfy in practice. The authors acknowledge the use of backtracking line search in one example but don’t thoroughly analyze the impact of step size selection on the convergence rate.
* **Proposition 4.2 - Assumption on Optimal Solution Set:** The strong assumption that the optimal solution set can be written in the form Xc = { X ∈ X : X = Ū [Diag(σ(X)) 0] V̄ T } seems potentially restrictive.  While the authors provide justification, it's worth exploring if the results hold under weaker assumptions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advancement in the understanding of PGM convergence for trace norm regularization.
* The results are applicable to a wide range of important machine learning problems.
* The paper is well-written, thoroughly researched, and includes supporting numerical validation.
* The technical novelty and rigor of the proof are commendable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proof may limit accessibility for some readers.
* The practical implications and step size sensitivity could be further discussed.
* The assumption inherent in Proposition 4.2, while justifiable, might benefit from further investigation regarding its necessity.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The theoretical result is significant, the presentation is excellent, and the work is well-motivated and supported. While there are a few minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I **strongly recommend acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points regarding practical implications, step size sensitivity, and potentially providing more intuition behind the complex proof steps.
