This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm-regularized problems, specifically when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is a strictly convex function. The authors establish linear convergence under certain smoothness and curvature assumptions on *h*, without requiring strong convexity of *f* itself. This is a notable contribution, as existing linear convergence results often rely on strong convexity. The paper provides a clear presentation, a well-motivated problem statement, and a technically sound analysis.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result for the specified class of loss functions is a genuine contribution. It expands the applicability of PGM to a broader range of problems where strong convexity is not guaranteed.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof relies on a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The use of matrix perturbation theory appears well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the assumptions are well-defined. The notation is consistent.
*   **Significance:** The results are relevant to a variety of applications including matrix completion, matrix classification, and multi-task learning, as highlighted in the introduction. Improving convergence guarantees for PGM is a valuable contribution.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments demonstrate the linear convergence behavior on both matrix completion and classification tasks, bolstering the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Error Bound Complexity:** While the Lipschitzian error bound is a key result, its practical application may be limited. The constants involved (η, ǫ) are likely difficult to estimate in real-world scenarios. A discussion of the practical implications of these constants would be helpful.
*   **Assumption 2.1(b):** The assumption that *h* is strongly convex on *any* convex compact subset of dom(*h*) seems strong. It might be more realistic to require strong convexity on a specific, bounded subset relevant to the problem at hand. A justification or discussion of the necessity of this assumption would be beneficial.
*   **Step Size Selection:** The paper mentions a backtracking line search for the logistic loss case, implying that finding an appropriate step size is non-trivial. A more detailed discussion of step size selection strategies and their impact on convergence would enhance the practical value of the work.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The numerical experiments primarily focus on demonstrating linear convergence. A comparison with other optimization methods (e.g., accelerated PGM, alternative methods) in terms of runtime and solution accuracy would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
*   **Proof Length & Detail:** The proofs are referred to the supplementary material. While this is acceptable, making key lemmas and insights more directly accessible within the main body would improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with significant implications for trace norm regularization.
*   The technical challenges overcome in establishing the error bound are substantial.
*   The experimental validation supports the theoretical findings.
*   The work aligns well with the NIPS focus on machine learning and optimization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the error bound and the strong convexity assumption require further consideration.
*   The limited comparison with other methods weakens the assessment of practical performance.
*   The reliance on supplementary material for core proof details makes it harder to fully assess the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The theoretical result is novel and significant. The technical analysis is rigorous and the experimental validation is encouraging. While there are some weaknesses regarding practical considerations and comparison with other methods, they do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by:

1.  Discussing the practical implications of the constants in the error bound and providing guidance on how they might be estimated.
2.  Providing a justification for the strong convexity assumption on any convex compact subset of dom(h).
3.  Expanding the discussion on step size selection strategies.
4.  Including a comparison with alternative optimization methods in the numerical experiments.
5.  Consider summarizing key lemmas and insights from the supplementary material within the main text.
