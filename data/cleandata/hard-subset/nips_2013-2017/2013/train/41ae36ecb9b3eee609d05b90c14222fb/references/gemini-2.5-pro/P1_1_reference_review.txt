### Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`. While PGM is known to have a sublinear convergence rate for general smooth convex `f`, and a linear rate for strongly convex `f`, many important machine learning problems (e.g., matrix completion with square loss) involve a loss function `f` that is convex but not strongly convex. This paper bridges this theoretical gap by establishing a linear convergence rate for PGM under a more general set of conditions. Specifically, it assumes that the loss function has the composite structure `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties.

The key technical contribution is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for this class of trace norm regularized problems. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set, `dist(X, X*)`, to the norm of a residual map, `||R(X)||`, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. The authors argue that establishing such a bound for the trace norm is non-trivial compared to the `l1`-norm, due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm ball and the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. Their proof technique leverages matrix perturbation theory and a novel characterization of the spectral structure of the optimal solutions. Using this error bound, the paper then applies the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng to prove the R-linear convergence of the iterates and Q-linear convergence of the objective function values. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks, which demonstrate the predicted linear convergence behavior.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance:** The paper addresses a very important and relevant problem. Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone of modern machine learning for finding low-rank solutions, and the proximal gradient method is one of the most widely used algorithms for solving such problems. The lack of a linear convergence guarantee for many practical, non-strongly-convex cases was a significant gap in the literature. This work provides a strong theoretical justification for the excellent empirical performance of PGM often observed in practice.

2.  **Strong Originality and Technical Depth:** The primary contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1)—is highly novel and resolves what the authors convincingly argue was an open question. The proof strategy is creative, moving beyond existing techniques for `l1` or group LASSO regularization that rely on separability. The observation that an optimal solution `X*` and the corresponding gradient `-∇f(X*)` share singular vectors (Proposition 4.2) is a crucial insight that enables the use of matrix perturbation theory. This represents a substantial technical achievement.

3.  **Excellent Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, despite its technical density. The introduction provides clear motivation and situates the work within the existing literature. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are stated upfront in Section 3, followed by a detailed proof sketch in Section 4. This structure makes the paper's contributions immediately accessible. The notation is consistent and the arguments are laid out logically.

4.  **Soundness and Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears to be rigorous and correct. The assumptions on the function `h` (Assumption 2.1) are reasonable and hold for many common loss functions like square loss and logistic loss, making the result broadly applicable. The application of the Luo-Tseng framework is standard but appropriate once the key error bound is established. The numerical experiments, while simple, effectively corroborate the theoretical claims by clearly showing a linear convergence phase.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments serve as a proof-of-concept for the theory but are not comprehensive. They demonstrate that linear convergence occurs, but do not compare the performance of PGM against other state-of-the-art solvers for trace norm problems. While the paper's focus is theoretical, a slightly broader experimental section could have strengthened its practical implications.

2.  **Dependence on Supplementary Material:** The proof of the main error bound relies on several key propositions (e.g., Proposition 4.3), whose proofs are deferred to the supplementary material. This is standard practice, but it means that a full verification of the paper's central technical claims requires studying a separate document. The proof sketch in the main body is, however, quite detailed and provides good intuition.

3.  **Clarity on Constants:** The linear convergence rate depends on constants (e.g., `κ1`, `κ4` in the proof of Theorem 3.2) which in turn depend on properties of the function `h` and operator `A`. The paper does not discuss how these constants might behave in practice (e.g., their dependence on problem conditioning or dimensions), which could affect the *actual* speed of convergence. This is a minor point, as the primary goal is to establish the existence of a linear rate, not to characterize it fully.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Solves a long-standing theoretical problem regarding the convergence rate of a fundamental algorithm (PGM) for a fundamental problem class (trace norm regularization).
*   The core technical contribution, a new Lipschitzian error bound, is novel, technically deep, and of independent interest.
*   The results are significant for the machine learning and optimization communities, providing a better understanding of why PGM works so well on problems like matrix completion.
*   The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and logically structured.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The experimental validation is illustrative rather than exhaustive.
*   The full technical details of the main proof are in the supplement, making the main paper a high-level sketch in its most crucial section. (This is a very weak argument, as this is common practice).

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent theoretical paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on optimization for machine learning. It elegantly proves the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for a broad and important class of trace norm regularized problems, a result that was previously unavailable. The technical machinery developed, particularly the novel error bound, is impressive and could inspire further research. The paper is of high quality, clearly written, and will be of great interest to the NIPS audience. I strongly recommend acceptance.
