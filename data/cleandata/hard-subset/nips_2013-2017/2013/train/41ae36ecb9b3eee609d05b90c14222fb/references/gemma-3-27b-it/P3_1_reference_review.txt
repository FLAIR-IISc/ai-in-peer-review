## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) for a linear operator A and a strictly convex function h. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under fairly general conditions on *h* (smoothness and curvature) without requiring strong convexity of *f*, a notable improvement over existing results.  The key contribution lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound specifically for trace norm regularization, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the interdependence of its singular values. The paper validates its findings with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established theoretical foundation of PGM for composite optimization (Beck & Teboulle, 2009). It addresses a gap in the literature by extending linear convergence results beyond cases requiring strong convexity of the loss function *f*. Prior work (Luo & Tseng, 2010) provided a powerful framework for convergence analysis but relied on Lipschitzian error bounds that were not previously available for trace norm regularization. The authors correctly position their work in relation to existing bounds for ℓ1 and group LASSO regularization (e.g., Tibshirani & Bien, 2008), highlighting the unique difficulties posed by the trace norm. The numerical experiments connect to the practical applications of trace norm minimization in areas like matrix completion (Recht et al., 2010) and low-rank matrix learning (Koltchinskii et al., 2009).

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity of *f* is a significant contribution, expanding the applicability of PGM to a broader range of machine learning problems.
* **Technical Depth:** The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound is the core technical achievement. The authors cleverly leverage matrix perturbation theory and properties of the optimal solution set to overcome the inherent difficulties of trace norm regularization. Proposition 4.2 regarding the shared singular vectors is particularly insightful.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a clear and rigorous analysis, grounded in convex optimization theory.  The proof structure is well-defined, and the lemmas and theorems are stated precisely.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide supporting evidence for the theoretical results. The observed linear convergence in both matrix completion and classification scenarios is encouraging.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The introduction motivates the problem effectively, and the technical sections are presented in a comprehensible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions on *h*:** While the conditions on *h* (smoothness and curvature) are reasonable, a more detailed discussion of their practical implications and how easily they are satisfied in common machine learning scenarios would be beneficial.  Examples of loss functions *not* meeting these conditions would also be helpful for readers.
* **Step Size Selection:** The theoretical result relies on a specific (and somewhat restrictive) condition on the step size αk. While practical line search methods can be used (as demonstrated in the experiments), a more detailed discussion of robust step size selection strategies would enhance the paper’s practical relevance.
* **Numerical Experiments – Limited Scope:** While the numerical results are positive, the experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  Exploring the impact of different problem sizes, regularization parameters (τ), and dimensionality would strengthen the empirical validation. It would also be useful to compare performance against other solvers specifically designed for trace norm minimization.
* **Proof Length and Detail:** The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is quite involved and relies heavily on Proposition 4.2 and the subsequent lemma.  While technically sound, it could be made more accessible by providing more intuitive explanations of the key steps. The full proofs in the supplementary material are crucial but could benefit from even greater clarity.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant theoretical advance:**  The paper addresses an important open problem in the convergence analysis of PGM.
* **Strong technical contribution:** The derived Lipschitzian error bound is a novel and valuable result.
* **Potential impact:** The relaxed assumptions broaden the applicability of PGM to real-world problems.
* **Sound empirical validation:** The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Assumptions on *h* could be limiting:** The practical implications of these assumptions need to be better clarified.
* **Step size requirements:**  The restrictive step size condition adds complexity to practical implementation.
* **Limited scope of numerical experiments:** More extensive empirical validation would be desirable.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization and machine learning. The theoretical results are significant, the technical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical validation is supportive. While the paper has some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from its overall quality.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Specifically, I recommend the authors:

1. Provide a more detailed discussion of the assumptions on *h* and their practical implications.
2. Discuss more robust step size selection strategies beyond the theoretical requirements.
3. Expand the numerical experiments to include a wider range of problem settings and a comparison to alternative solvers.
