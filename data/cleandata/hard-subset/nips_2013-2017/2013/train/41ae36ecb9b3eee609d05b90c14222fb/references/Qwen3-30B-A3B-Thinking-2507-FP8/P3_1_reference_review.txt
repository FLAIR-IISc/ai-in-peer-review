

# Peer Review: "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental question in optimization for trace norm-regularized problems: under what conditions can we establish linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors establish a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularization problem, which enables them to prove linear convergence of PGM for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties. This resolves an open question in the literature, as prior error bounds for composite optimization typically required polyhedral epigraphs (e.g., ℓ₁-norm), which don't apply to trace norm.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which is non-polyhedral (unlike ℓ₁-norm). This is a substantial advance over prior work [22, 23, 25] that only addressed polyhedral regularizations. The key insight—that optimal solutions and their negative gradients share singular vectors—enables the authors to use matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound, which is both novel and technically sophisticated.

**Practical Relevance:** The result is highly relevant to numerous machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.) where the loss function often takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) but is not strongly convex (e.g., square loss in matrix completion). The authors provide numerical evidence of linear convergence in these settings, which matches their theoretical claims.

**Rigor and Completeness:** The paper is mathematically rigorous, with careful proof construction and well-motivated technical lemmas. The authors properly contextualize their work within the existing literature, acknowledging that the polyhedral epigraph assumption for prior error bounds doesn't apply to trace norm.

## Weaknesses

**Restrictive Assumption on Loss Function:** The authors assume f(X) = h(A(X)), which, while covering many practical cases, may be too restrictive. For example, in matrix completion with missing data, the loss function is indeed of this form, but the paper doesn't discuss how common this form is across other applications. It's unclear whether the results extend to more general loss functions where f(X) is not expressible as h(A(X)). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide sufficient justification for why this form is sufficiently general for most applications.

**Limited Numerical Experiments:** The empirical validation is limited to two problems (matrix completion and matrix classification). While these are standard test cases, the paper would be strengthened by additional experiments on different problem structures, varying regularization parameters, or comparisons with alternative methods that might achieve linear convergence under different assumptions.

**Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of the proximal operator or how the linear convergence rate translates to practical computational efficiency. For instance, the matrix shrinkage operation (required for the proximal operator) has O(mn min(m,n)) complexity, which could impact the practical relevance of the linear convergence result for very large matrices.

**Comparison with Related Work:** While the paper cites relevant literature, it could better contrast its error bound with other approaches for matrix norm regularization. For example, the paper could discuss how their result compares to recent work on error bounds for Schatten p-norms [18, 24] or how the non-polyhedral nature of trace norm specifically affects convergence rates.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing the first Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables linear convergence results without strong convexity assumptions. The technical novelty is significant, and the results address an important open question in the field. The paper is well-written, rigorously proven, and supported by empirical evidence.

The main limitations are the restrictive assumption on the loss function form and limited experimental validation. However, these do not undermine the core theoretical contribution, which is both novel and important for the field. The authors have made a substantial step toward understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization, which has broad applications in machine learning.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, novel, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the assumptions and experimental scope could be expanded, the core contribution is substantial and will be of interest to the NeurIPS community. The results provide a theoretical foundation for the empirical success of proximal gradient methods in many matrix learning problems, which is highly relevant to the machine learning community.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the understanding of first-order methods for non-strongly convex optimization with matrix regularizers, which is a timely topic given the widespread use of trace norm regularization in modern machine learning. The technical depth and novelty of the error bound result make this a strong candidate for NeurIPS.
