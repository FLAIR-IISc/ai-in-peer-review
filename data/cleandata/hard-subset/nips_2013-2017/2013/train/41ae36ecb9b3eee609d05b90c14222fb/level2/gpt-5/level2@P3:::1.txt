Summary and contributions:
The paper studies composite convex optimization of the form minX f(X) + τ‖X‖*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth and strictly convex on compact subsets of its domain. The main results are:
- A new local Lipschitzian error bound for trace/nuclear-norm regularized problems: dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F for points in a bounded level set and with sufficiently small residual. This fills a gap relative to known error bounds for polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO).
- Using the Luo–Tseng framework, an asymptotic linear convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) under constant stepsizes 0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf, without assuming strong convexity of f. The objective converges Q-linearly to v*, and the iterates converge R-linearly to the solution set.
A key technical ingredient is a structural result: at any optimal solution X̄, both X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors. This enables a careful spectral perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” inequality linking Frobenius distance to the solution set with the residual on the A-image.

Relation to prior work:
- Standard PGM rates for smooth + nonsmooth convex objectives are O(1/k) (ISTA) and O(1/k^2) (FISTA) [Beck & Teboulle 2009]. Linear rates are available when f is strongly convex [Schmidt, Le Roux, Bach 2011].
- Error bounds and linear convergence for composite problems with polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group LASSO) or separable structures have been studied by Tseng and co-authors [Tseng 2010; Tseng & Yun 2009; Zhang, Jiang, Luo 2013].
- For nuclear norm regularization, widely used algorithms (SVT, APG) have strong practical performance [Ma et al. 2011; Toh & Yun 2010], but linear convergence without strong convexity and an accompanying error bound were previously open due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the nuclear norm.
The paper positions itself clearly within this literature and extends the Luo–Tseng error-bound-based analysis to the nuclear norm case.

Strengths:
- Conceptual advance: establishes a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, overcoming non-polyhedral difficulties via spectral alignment and perturbation arguments. This appears novel and technically nontrivial.
- Broad applicability: assumptions on h cover square and logistic losses used in matrix completion/classification and multi-task learning, even when A is not injective (hence f is not strongly convex).
- Clear convergence statement: asymptotic linear rate for PGM under standard stepsize conditions, with a clean separation between objective Q-linear and iterate R-linear convergence.
- The theory uses standard and well-accepted frameworks (proximity operator/shrinkage, Luo–Tseng analysis) and connects to practical residual measures implementable in algorithms.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Essential:
- Scope and locality of the error bound: Theorem 3.1 holds for small residuals within a bounded level set. Clarify how small “ε” must be in terms of problem data, and whether one can provide any computable or verifiable criterion during optimization to ensure entry into the linear regime (e.g., a threshold on the residual).
- Stepsize and backtracking: The experiments use backtracking for logistic loss. Provide a brief argument that standard backtracking ensures α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf eventually, so that Theorem 3.2 applies asymptotically, or state precise conditions under which it does.

Optional but valuable:
- Quantitative constants: While exact constants (η, κ, σ) may be conservative, offering bounds or dependencies on L, ‖A‖, τ would improve interpretability and could guide parameter tuning.
- Inexact proximal computations: Practical nuclear-norm prox often uses truncated SVDs. Discuss whether the linear rate is robust to inexact prox steps (e.g., summable errors or relative accuracy), perhaps by leveraging known analyses for inexact proximal-gradient methods.
- Acceleration: Comment on whether the error bound plus Luo–Tseng framework yields linear convergence for accelerated variants (or if momentum disrupts the analysis), and whether restarting schemes recover linear rates.
- Experiments: The numerical section confirms asymptotic linear behavior but is limited. Adding comparisons with APG and ADMM-like methods and reporting iteration counts/time to reach fixed tolerance across problem sizes and sampling rates would strengthen empirical support.

Quality:
The analysis is sound and well-grounded. The spectral alignment proposition and the restricted invertibility argument are the critical technical steps; the main proof is careful, with some details deferred to the supplement. The convergence theorem follows established templates once the error bound is in place. Assumptions are explicit and justified for common losses.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well-structured. Notation is heavy but standard. Suggestions: briefly restate or summarize the key inequalities (9)–(11) in the main text (currently referenced to the supplement), and provide an intuitive discussion of Proposition 4.2’s spectral alignment to aid readers less familiar with proximal fixed-point conditions.

Originality:
High. The Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under the stated composite structure appears new, and the resulting linear convergence without strong convexity of f addresses a recognized gap in first-order method theory for matrix problems.

Significance:
Moderate to high. PGM is ubiquitous for nuclear-norm problems; guaranteeing linear rates under realistic losses without strong convexity improves theoretical understanding and may inform algorithm design. The error bound itself can have further impact, potentially extending to other non-polyhedral regularizers.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and technically solid error bound for a widely used non-polyhedral regularizer.
- Clear, general convergence guarantee with practical relevance (square and logistic losses).
- Bridges an open gap between practice and theory (linear rates without strong convexity).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Linear convergence is asymptotic/local with unspecified constants, limiting quantitative guidance.
- Experimental validation is minimal and lacks comparisons to competing methods.
- No discussion of inexact proximal computations commonly used in large-scale settings.

Overall, the work makes a meaningful theoretical contribution with reasonable assumptions and clear impact on a core class of problems. Strengthening the discussion on stepsize/backtracking, constants, and practical robustness would further improve the paper.
