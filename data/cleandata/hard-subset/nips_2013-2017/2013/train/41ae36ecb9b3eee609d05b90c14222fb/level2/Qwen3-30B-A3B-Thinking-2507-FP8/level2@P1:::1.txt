

This paper addresses an important theoretical question in optimization for machine learning: establishing linear convergence rates for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors prove a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables them to establish linear convergence for a broad class of loss functions (those expressible as f(X) = h(A(X)) where h satisfies certain smoothness and curvature properties). The key insight is recognizing that optimal solutions and their gradients share the same singular vectors, enabling the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature. Previous work on linear convergence for trace norm regularization either required strong convexity of the loss function [16] or was limited to specific cases [19]. The authors properly contextualize their work by noting that the trace norm regularization problem has been widely studied in machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.), yet the question of linear convergence without strong convexity had remained open despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization, which had established error bounds [22, 23, 25]. The authors' novel approach to establishing the error bound for trace norm regularization is particularly impressive given the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph.

The quality of the paper is excellent. The theoretical results are technically sound, with a rigorous proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and the subsequent linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2). The proof is well-structured and builds on the framework of Luo and Tseng [10], while introducing new technical insights. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems provide strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims, showing linear convergence in practice as predicted by the theory.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and positions the work within the broader literature. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout. The proof of Lemma 4.1, which establishes the crucial restricted invertibility property, is particularly elegant. The authors carefully address the challenges of working with trace norm regularization (the non-polyhedral epigraph and the interdependence of singular values) and provide a compelling solution.

The originality of the work is substantial. The error bound for trace norm regularization is novel and appears to be the first such result for this class of problems that doesn't require strong convexity. The authors' observation about the shared singular vectors between optimal solutions and gradients is a key technical insight that enables their approach. This is not merely a minor extension of previous work but represents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace norm regularization.

The significance of the results is high. Many machine learning applications use trace norm regularization (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.), and the linear convergence rate established here represents a substantial improvement over the standard sublinear O(1/k) rate. The error bound itself may be of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization. The authors correctly note that their result applies to a wide range of loss functions used in practice, including square loss (matrix completion) and logistic loss (matrix classification).

One minor limitation is that the paper does not explicitly discuss the computational cost of verifying the assumptions in practice, particularly the strong convexity of h on compact subsets of its domain. However, this is a minor point given that the authors provide concrete examples where the assumptions hold (matrix completion, multi-task learning with square or logistic loss).

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, addressing a fundamental question in optimization with clear implications for machine learning applications. The theoretical contribution is significant, the results are well-supported by both theory and experiments, and the writing is clear and precise.

Overall, this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of optimization for machine learning. The linear convergence result without strong convexity assumptions fills an important theoretical gap and has practical implications for a wide range of applications. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution that advances our understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a solid theoretical advance with clear significance for machine learning applications, and the results are carefully established with appropriate technical rigor.
