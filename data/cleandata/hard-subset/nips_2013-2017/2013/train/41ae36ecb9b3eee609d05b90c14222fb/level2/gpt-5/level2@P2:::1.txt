Summary
The paper studies composite convex optimization problems of the form F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex, and “locally” strongly convex on any convex compact subset of its domain. The main contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal solution set X to the proximal-gradient residual ||R(X)||F = ||Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||F in a neighborhood of X and within a fixed level set. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence (Q-linear in objective values, R-linear in iterates) of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with stepsizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf, without assuming strong convexity of f. A key technical tool is a spectral-structure result (Proposition 4.2): at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors, enabling a matrix-perturbation argument to establish a restricted invertibility property and hence the error bound. Experiments on matrix completion (squared loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrate linear behavior near the solution.

Relation to prior work: PGM guarantees sublinear rates in general and linear rates with strong convexity. Prior error bounds for composite problems are known for polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1) and group Lasso; the nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral, and analogous bounds had been open. The paper builds on Tseng/Luo error-bound analyses and proximal gradient convergence under such bounds (e.g., Tseng and Yun; Zhang et al., 2012). To my knowledge, a linear rate for PGM with nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of f is new. It would be useful to also position the result vis-à-vis KL-based convergence theory (Attouch–Bolte et al.), which can give linear rates under certain KL exponents for semi-algebraic objectives.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The assumptions on h ensure L-smoothness and a positive curvature modulus on bounded subsets, which is used to derive a uniform σ on a compact level set (inequality (15)). The spectral alignment at optimality follows from the nuclear-norm subdifferential structure; the “simultaneous SVD” claim appears consistent with UV^T + W form and orthogonality constraints. The restricted invertibility property (16) is the crux; the contradiction-based proof via projections onto Xc and careful perturbation of singular values is plausible and thoughtfully executed. The derivation of the three key inequalities (9)–(11) for PGM is standard.
- Scope/limitations: The error bound is local (requires ||R(X)||F ≤ ε and a bounded level set), so linear convergence is asymptotic. Constants (η, ε, κi) are not quantified, which limits practical iteration-complexity estimates. The result is shown for fixed stepsizes in (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf); the experiments use backtracking, but the theory assumes a positive lower bound on stepsizes—worth clarifying for line search.
- Experiments: They are illustrative rather than exhaustive (n = 40, low rank, synthetic data). They demonstrate linear slopes near optimality in both objective and solution error, consistent with theory. No timing comparisons or baselines are reported; that is acceptable given the theoretical focus but limits empirical impact.

Clarity
The paper is well organized, with clear problem setup, assumptions, and a transparent link from the error bound to linear convergence via Luo–Tseng. The proof outline is detailed and the key lemmas are well motivated. A few suggestions:
- Precisely state Proposition 4.2 with the standard nuclear-norm subdifferential characterization and clarify “simultaneous SVD” (i.e., block-diagonal structure in the basis of singular vectors of X̄).
- Explicitly articulate how the line-search ensures αk ∈ [α, ᾱ] eventually (or adapt the theorem to backtracking).
- Provide a short discussion contrasting the proposed error bound with KL-based analyses (and why the present bound is sharper/stronger for PGM).
- Minor issues: some references lack years; figure numbering seems inconsistent (text mentions Figure 1 for matrix completion; the classification figure is labeled “Figure 2”).

Originality
The local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under the stated structural assumptions and the ensuing linear rate for PGM (without strong convexity of f) appear novel. Prior works handled polyhedral or group sparse regularizers; extending to the nonpolyhedral nuclear norm with a new spectral argument is a substantive technical advance.

Significance
The result strengthens our understanding of first-order methods for low-rank regularization, a central tool in machine learning. The error bound is of independent interest and could inform restart policies, termination criteria, and analyses of other algorithms (e.g., coordinate descent, proximal Newton, or ADMM) for nuclear-norm problems. The asymptotic nature and lack of explicit constants limit immediate practical guidance, but the theoretical message—linear rates are achievable widely beyond strong convexity—is impactful.

Constructive suggestions
- Quantify or at least discuss dependencies of η and ε on problem data (A, τ, L, level-set size), and whether sharper, possibly global, bounds are attainable under additional conditions (e.g., restricted strong convexity/weak decomposability).
- Discuss extensions to accelerated PGM with restarts; under the same error bound, a restarted scheme can enjoy linear rates in objective.
- Explore broader empirical validation: larger n, varying ranks, noisy observations, different A (e.g., design matrices with correlated rows), and sensitivity to τ.
- Clarify practical detection of the linear regime (via residual magnitude) and how backtracking ensures stepsizes bounded away from 0.

Arguments for acceptance
- Resolves an open question: establishes a Lipschitzian error bound and linear PGM rate for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- Technically solid and original spectral perturbation argument; likely useful beyond this specific setting.
- Clear exposition and alignment with well-known convergence frameworks.
- Broad applicability to common losses (square, logistic) via f = h ∘ A.

Arguments against acceptance
- Asymptotic/local result with unspecified constants; limited practical complexity guidance.
- Experimental section is minimal and small-scale; no comparisons to alternative methods or step-size policies.
- Limited discussion of related general frameworks (e.g., KL property) and how this result fits therein.

Overall: A strong theoretical contribution that advances the analysis of first-order methods for trace-norm regularized problems. Despite modest empirical scope, I lean toward acceptance.
