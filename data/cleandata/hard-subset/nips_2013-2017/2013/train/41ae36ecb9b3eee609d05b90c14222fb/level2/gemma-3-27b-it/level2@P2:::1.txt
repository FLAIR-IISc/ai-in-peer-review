This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically aiming to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The problem setup is well-motivated with numerous applications cited, and the authors correctly identify the gap in existing literature – previous linear convergence results necessitate strong convexity, which is often not present in practical machine learning scenarios.

The paper's main contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem. This bound is then leveraged, using results from Luo and Tseng, to prove the linear convergence of the PGM. The authors acknowledge the difficulty in extending existing error bound techniques (developed for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO) to the trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph and the interdependence of matrix entries. Their approach, based on observing shared singular vectors between the optimal solution and its negative gradient, is a novel and insightful idea.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a significant contribution. The approach using a Lipschitzian error bound and leveraging the singular vector structure is innovative.
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The proofs appear to be well-structured and the use of existing results (Luo & Tseng) is appropriate.  The authors carefully state assumptions and delineate their applicability.
* **Motivation & Relevance:** The problem is well-motivated by real-world applications, and the gap in existing literature is clearly articulated.
* **Experimental Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification provides empirical support for the theoretical results. The observation of linear convergence in the experiments adds confidence.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations of key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The proof is quite dense and requires a strong background in convex optimization and matrix analysis to fully appreciate. While rigor is good, some parts could benefit from more intuitive explanations.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While they demonstrate linear convergence, exploring the impact of different parameters (e.g., regularization parameter, step size) and problem sizes would strengthen the empirical validation. The connection between the theoretical assumptions and the experimental setup could also be clearer (e.g., justifying how the assumptions on 'h' are satisfied in the matrix completion experiment).
* **Proposition 4.2 & 4.3 – Lack of Intuition:** While the mathematical derivations in these propositions seem correct, the intuition behind why these specific properties hold for the optimal solution set and how they enable the error bound is not fully explained.  A more intuitive explanation of the significance of shared singular vectors would be helpful.
* **Potential for Generalization Discussion:** The conclusion briefly mentions generalizing to other matrix norms. Expanding on the potential challenges and opportunities in extending the results to other norms (e.g., nuclear norm with different weights) would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound contribution to the understanding of PGM convergence for trace norm regularization.
* The established linear convergence result is a significant advancement over existing literature, particularly its applicability to non-strongly convex loss functions.
* The numerical experiments provide empirical support for the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The technical density of the proof may make it inaccessible to a broader audience.
* The limited scope of the experiments.
* The lack of deeper intuition surrounding key propositions (4.2 & 4.3).




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The weaknesses are primarily related to the technical density and the limited scope of experimental validation. However, these do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work.  I believe the paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, contingent on the authors considering the suggestions for improving the clarity and intuition of the technical arguments and potentially expanding the experimental evaluation.



**Rating:** Accept with minor revisions.
