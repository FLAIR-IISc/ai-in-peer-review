This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems, demonstrating linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a significant contribution, as existing linear convergence results typically rely on strong convexity assumptions. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a relevant problem in machine learning with broad applicability. 

The core contribution, a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, is well-presented and appears to be the key enabler for the linear convergence result. The authors acknowledge the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and successfully overcome them using a clever observation regarding the singular vectors of optimal solutions and matrix perturbation theory. The connection to existing work, particularly Luo and Tseng’s framework, is clearly articulated. 

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous analysis and the novelty of the result. The proof strategy is sound, and the numerical experiments, while limited, support the theoretical findings by demonstrating linear convergence in matrix completion and classification tasks.  The inclusion of numerical experiments, even basic ones, adds credibility to the theoretical claims.

However, there are areas for improvement. While the paper clearly states its assumptions, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of Assumption 2.1 (specifically regarding h) would be valuable.  For example, what classes of loss functions commonly used in machine learning automatically satisfy this assumption, and when might it be problematic?  Providing more concrete examples would enhance clarity. 

The discussion of related work, while adequate, could be expanded. For instance, the paper mentions work on ℓ1-norm regularization, but doesn’t delve into the differences in the challenges faced when establishing error bounds for the trace norm versus the ℓ1-norm beyond stating the non-polyhedral epigraph.  A more detailed comparison would further highlight the novelty of the contribution. 

The numerical experiments are relatively simple. Expanding these experiments to include larger datasets, different regularization parameters, and more complex loss functions would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the PGM’s performance and robustness. Investigating the sensitivity of the convergence rate to the step size parameter α could also be insightful.  Furthermore, a comparison against alternative algorithms (e.g., accelerated PGM, alternative optimization algorithms) would strengthen the results.

The notation is generally clear, but the frequent use of "X̄" and similar barred variables could be slightly confusing. Consistent use of a more descriptive naming convention might improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novelty: The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant contribution.
*   Soundness: The analysis is rigorous and well-motivated.
*   Relevance: The problem addressed is important in machine learning.
*   Clarity: The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited numerical experiments.
*   Discussion of assumptions could be more detailed.
*   Related work section could be expanded.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. While the numerical experiments could be more extensive and the discussion of assumptions more detailed, the core result is significant and well-supported. The reviewer recommends acceptance, with minor revisions to address the identified weaknesses. Specifically, the authors should consider expanding the numerical experiments, providing more concrete examples relating to Assumption 2.1, and expanding the related work discussion.
