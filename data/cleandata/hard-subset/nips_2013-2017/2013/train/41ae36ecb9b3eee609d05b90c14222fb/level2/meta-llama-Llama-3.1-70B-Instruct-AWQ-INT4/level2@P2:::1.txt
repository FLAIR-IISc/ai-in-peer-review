The paper "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization" presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, specifically in the context of trace norm regularization. The authors provide a rigorous analysis of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving a class of convex optimization problems with trace norm regularization. They establish a linear convergence rate for the PGM without assuming strong convexity of the loss function, which is a common assumption in many existing works.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, including the motivation and background. The technical sections are dense but well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the PGM, including a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem.

The main contribution of the paper is the establishment of a linear convergence rate for the PGM, which is a significant improvement over the existing sublinear convergence rate. The authors achieve this by introducing a new Lipschitzian error bound, which is a key technical tool in the proof. The bound is established without assuming strong convexity of the loss function, which makes the result more general and applicable to a broader range of problems.

The paper also includes numerical experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the PGM in practice. The experiments show that the PGM converges linearly, which is consistent with the theoretical results. However, the experiments are limited to two specific problems, and it would be beneficial to see more extensive numerical results to further validate the theoretical findings.

One potential criticism of the paper is that the analysis is focused on a specific class of problems, and it is not clear how the results generalize to more general settings. Additionally, the paper assumes that the loss function is of a specific form, which may not be the case in all applications. However, the authors provide a clear discussion of the assumptions and limitations of their results, which is helpful for readers to understand the context and potential applications of the work.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of optimization, and the authors provide a new perspective on the analysis of the PGM for trace norm regularization. The paper builds on existing works, but the authors provide a clear and concise discussion of the related literature, which helps to situate their contribution in the broader context of optimization research.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the PGM for trace norm regularization. The main contribution of the paper is the establishment of a linear convergence rate for the PGM, which is a significant improvement over existing results. The paper is a valuable contribution to the field of optimization, and it has the potential to impact a broad range of applications in machine learning and signal processing.
