This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + tau*||X||_*`. This class of problems is fundamental to many machine learning tasks, including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. While PGM is known to have a sublinear rate of convergence for general smooth and convex `f`, and a linear rate for strongly convex `f`, many practical loss functions are not strongly convex. The main contribution of this paper is to prove that PGM still achieves a linear rate of convergence for a large and important class of non-strongly convex loss functions, specifically those of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function.

The key to this result is the development of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm is non-trivial. Unlike `l1`-norm regularization, where similar bounds have been shown using separability arguments, the trace norm is non-separable. The authors overcome this challenge by identifying and exploiting a key structural property of the optimal solution set: at an optimum `X*`, the matrix `X*` and the negative gradient `-grad f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows the authors to use tools from matrix perturbation theory to control the spectral structure of points near the optimum and thereby establish the desired error bound. With this bound in hand, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and timely question. PGM is a workhorse algorithm for trace norm problems, and its convergence behavior is of great interest. Showing linear convergence under weaker-than-strong-convexity assumptions substantially improves our theoretical understanding of this algorithm's practical performance on common problems like matrix completion with a square loss, which this work covers.

2.  **Originality:** The main technical contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1), is novel and resolves an open question. The proof technique is innovative, moving beyond the standard separability-based arguments used for `l1`-norm problems. The observation about the shared singular vectors at the optimum (Proposition 4.2) is a key insight that enables the analysis. This result could be of independent interest to the optimization community.

3.  **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The arguments are laid out logically, building from the structural properties of the optimal set to the error bound and finally to the linear convergence result. The theoretical claims appear well-supported by the proof sketches provided. The authors are careful to state their assumptions clearly.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the contribution within the existing literature. The main results are stated clearly in Section 3, and the proof outline provides a good roadmap for the technical arguments in Section 4. The numerical experiments, while simple, provide a clear illustration of the paper's theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  The proof of the error bound in Section 4 is quite technical and dense, which is understandable given the space constraints of a conference paper. However, the reliance on the supplementary material for key parts of the proof (e.g., for Proposition 4.3) makes a full verification of the result from the main text challenging.

2.  The assumptions on the function `h` (Assumption 2.1) are central to the result. In particular, the assumption that `h` is "strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)" is slightly non-standard. While this condition is satisfied by common loss functions, it might be helpful for the authors to briefly comment on the generality of this assumption and how it relates to more standard notions of convexity or curvature.

3.  The numerical experiments in Section 5 successfully demonstrate the linear convergence predicted by the theory. To further strengthen this section, it would be interesting to investigate how the convergence rate depends on problem parameters. For example, does the constant factor in the linear rate depend on the rank of the solution, the properties of the operator `A`, or the regularization parameter `tau`? While a full theoretical characterization may be beyond the scope of this work, an empirical investigation could provide valuable insights.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper provides a significant and novel theoretical result, establishing for the first time the linear convergence of PGM for a broad class of non-strongly convex trace norm regularized problems.
*   The core technical contribution—a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm—is of independent interest and relies on an elegant and novel proof technique.
*   The work addresses a practically relevant problem and improves our understanding of a widely used algorithm.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The technical proofs are complex and require careful verification, with some key steps deferred to the supplementary material.
*   The experimental section, while illustrative, could be more comprehensive.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the analysis of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The strengths, particularly the significance and originality of the main result, substantially outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
