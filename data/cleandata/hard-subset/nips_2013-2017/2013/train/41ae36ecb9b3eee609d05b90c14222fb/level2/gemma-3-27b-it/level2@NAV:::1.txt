## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems, a common setup in many machine learning applications like matrix completion and low-rank learning. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function – a significant improvement over existing results. This is achieved by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which may be of independent interest.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing sublinear convergence guarantees for PGM and highlighting the frequent occurrence of non-strongly convex loss functions in practice. The contributions are clearly stated. The proof technique, leveraging a novel Lipschitzian error bound and building upon the framework of Luo and Tseng [10], appears sound and the arguments are presented with reasonable detail. The authors acknowledge the difficulties in extending prior error bound approaches (developed for ℓ1-norm regularization) to the trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph, and their solution via matrix perturbation theory is a notable achievement.

A strength of the paper lies in its theoretical rigor and careful analysis. The inclusion of numerical experiments on both matrix completion and classification tasks, while brief, provides empirical support for the theoretical findings. The experiments demonstrate linear convergence in practice, reinforcing the contribution. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the assumptions made on the linear operator *A*.  Specifically, the smoothness and curvature properties of *h* (Assumption 2.1) are crucial, but their verification in real-world applications isn’t fully addressed. Furthermore, while the paper mentions the step-size selection, more detail on this aspect – particularly for the non-strongly convex case and the experiments – would be valuable. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant advancement.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:**  The proof appears rigorous and builds upon a well-established framework.
*   **Potential Impact:**  The result has implications for a wide range of applications leveraging trace norm regularization.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Practical Considerations:** Discussion of the applicability of the assumptions in practical settings could be expanded.
*   **Experimental Detail:** More details regarding the step-size selection in the experiments would be beneficial.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. While some practical aspects could be further explored, the novelty and rigor of the work warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
