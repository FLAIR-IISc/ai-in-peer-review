This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + τ||X||*`. This problem class is of central importance in machine learning, with applications in matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. The standard convergence analysis for PGM establishes a sublinear O(1/k^2) rate for accelerated variants when `f` is convex and smooth, and a linear rate if `f` is strongly convex. The key contribution of this paper is to show that PGM converges at a linear rate even when `f` is not strongly convex. The authors establish this result for a large and practical class of loss functions `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function (like the square or logistic loss) and `A` is an arbitrary linear operator. Since `A` can have a non-trivial nullspace, `f` itself is not necessarily strongly or even strictly convex.

The core of the theoretical analysis is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. The authors note that establishing such an error bound for the trace norm was an open question, as previous techniques for related problems (e.g., L1-norm regularization) rely on separability properties that the trace norm lacks. The proof of this bound is the paper's main technical achievement, leveraging a key structural property of the optimal solutions and employing matrix perturbation theory. With this error bound in hand, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10].

### Strengths

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question about the performance of a widely used algorithm (PGM) on an important class of problems. The main result—linear convergence without strong convexity—is a significant theoretical improvement over existing analyses. It provides a formal justification for the fast convergence often observed in practice for problems like matrix completion, where the loss function is typically not strongly convex.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The primary contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, is highly original and technically deep. The proof technique is novel; it circumvents the lack of separability by exploiting the shared singular vector structure between an optimal solution `X*` and the corresponding gradient term `-∇f(X*)`. This insight allows the authors to use spectral analysis and matrix perturbation theory in a clever way, which constitutes a new approach for analyzing this class of problems. This result is of independent interest and could be useful for analyzing other algorithms.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical claims are well-supported. The argument is carefully constructed, building upon a known framework but providing the crucial missing piece. The proof outline for the main error bound theorem is presented clearly in the main text, giving the reader a good high-level understanding of the intricate steps involved.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly situating the work in the context of prior art and stating the contributions upfront. The problem setup, assumptions, and main theorems are presented with precision.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Discussion of Constants and Asymptotic Nature:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic. The analysis shows that for some `k >= K`, the objective values converge Q-linearly. It would be beneficial to include a discussion on what the constants defining the convergence rate and the threshold `K` depend on. For instance, how do properties of the linear operator `A` (e.g., its singular values), the curvature of `h`, and the regularization parameter `τ` affect the rate? While an exact characterization may be difficult, a qualitative discussion would significantly enhance the practical implications of the result.

2.  **Relation to Restricted Strong Convexity:** The "restricted invertibility" property established in Lemma 4.1(c) is a key condition that essentially states that the operator `A` is injective over certain directions related to the optimal set. This condition is reminiscent of concepts like Restricted Strong Convexity or the Restricted Isometry Property used in other contexts to prove linear convergence for non-strongly convex problems. A brief discussion connecting or contrasting the paper's condition to these more widely known concepts would be very illuminating and would help place the contribution in a broader theoretical context.

3.  **Experimental Validation:** The experiments provide good visual confirmation of the linear convergence claim. However, they could be strengthened. For instance, it would be interesting to see a comparison with a problem instance where the paper's assumptions are violated (if a simple one exists) to demonstrate sublinear convergence, thereby highlighting the necessity of the conditions. Also, a minor but important point: the caption for Figure 1 is missing, and the plot is incorrectly referred to as "Figure 2" in the caption title. This should be corrected.

4.  **Proof Details in Main Paper:** A significant portion of the proof is, understandably, deferred to the supplementary material. However, Proposition 4.3 is a central piece of the contradiction argument in the proof of the error bound. The main text could benefit from a sentence or two providing more intuition on how the decomposition of `Q̄` and the properties of `λ` are derived from the classification of singular values (Cases A, B, C). This would make the core argument more self-contained.

### Conclusion

This is an excellent paper that makes a substantial and novel contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The authors prove a significant and long-sought-after result: the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularization without assuming strong convexity. The key technical tool, a new Lipschitzian error bound, is a major contribution in its own right. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and the results are of high importance to both researchers and practitioners. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed with small revisions. I strongly recommend acceptance.
