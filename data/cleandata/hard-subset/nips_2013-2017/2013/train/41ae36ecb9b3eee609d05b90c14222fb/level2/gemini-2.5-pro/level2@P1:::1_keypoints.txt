- Main contribution: Proves linear convergence of proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems f(X)=h(A(X)) without strong convexity.  
- Key technical tool: Introduces a novel Lipschitzian error bound relating distance to optimum with a residual measuring fixed-point violation.  
- Structural insight: Shows optimal X* and -∇f(X*) share singular vectors, enabling matrix perturbation arguments despite trace-norm non-separability.  
- Proof strategy: Uses the error bound plus the Luo–Tseng analysis framework to derive linear convergence.  
- Significance: Addresses an important practical gap—explains PGM’s linear behavior for common non-strongly convex losses (e.g., matrix completion with square loss).  
- Originality: Theorem 3.1 (error bound) and the shared-singular-vector observation (Proposition 4.2) are novel and of independent interest.  
- Technical quality: Arguments are logically presented and assumptions stated clearly; theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and the proof outline and experiments effectively communicate results.  
- Weakness: Main proof is technical and dense, with crucial steps deferred to supplementary material, complicating full verification.  
- Weakness: Assumption that h is “strongly convex on any convex compact subset of dom(h)” is non-standard and could use more discussion about generality.  
- Weakness: Experiments are simple; more empirical study of how rates depend on rank, operator A, or τ would strengthen results.  
- Acceptance arguments: Significant, novel theoretical result with an elegant proof technique and practical relevance; recommendation to accept.  
- Rejection arguments: Complexity of proofs (reliance on supplement) and limited experiments are the main concerns.
