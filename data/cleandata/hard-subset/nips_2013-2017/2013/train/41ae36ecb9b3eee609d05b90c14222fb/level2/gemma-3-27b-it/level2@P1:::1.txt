This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized problems of the form min X {F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗}, where f is a convex smooth loss function. The authors demonstrate linear convergence of the PGM under fairly general conditions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of f. This is a significant result as many practical loss functions, such as those arising in matrix completion, are not strongly convex. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important open problem. The connection to applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning is well-explained. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing results, which typically require strong convexity, and clearly articulate their contribution. The proof relies on a novel error bound, and the authors acknowledge the challenges in establishing this bound due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The result of linear convergence without strong convexity is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically sound. The authors provide a clear outline of the proof strategy and highlight the key technical lemmas and propositions.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The notation is clear, and the exposition is logical.
*   **Significance:** The result has potential impact in applications where the loss function is not strongly convex, such as matrix completion and related areas.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough review of relevant literature, correctly positioning their work within the existing body of knowledge.
*   **Numerical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments to support the theoretical results is a strong positive. The results demonstrate linear convergence in practice.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proof Complexity:** While the authors provide an outline, the detailed proof (especially for Lemma 4.1 and Propositions 4.2 & 4.3) is relegated to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult for the reviewer to fully assess the correctness and originality of the technical details without significant effort. While it's common to move details to the supplement, the core technical novelty resides there.
*   **Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b), requiring strong convexity of *h* on any convex compact subset of its domain, seems a little strong. It would be valuable to discuss the implications of relaxing this assumption.
*   **Step Size Selection:** The theoretical results rely on specific conditions on the step size αk. In practice, determining appropriate step sizes can be challenging. A discussion on practical step size selection strategies would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Numerical Experiments:** While the numerical experiments are encouraging, they are limited to two specific problems. Expanding the experimental evaluation to other applications or datasets would strengthen the claims. The description of the experiments is also slightly brief.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result with potential practical impact.
*   The authors have provided a clear and well-written exposition of their work.
*   The numerical experiments provide supporting evidence for the theoretical findings.
*   The paper addresses an important open problem in the field of optimization.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The detailed technical arguments are primarily contained in the supplementary material, making a full assessment challenging.
*   The assumptions, while reasonable, might be overly restrictive.
*   The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field and should be accepted. While the complexity of the proof and the reliance on the supplementary material are drawbacks, the novelty and significance of the result outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions:

1.  **Encourage authors to provide more key details of the proof in the main text**, particularly for the central lemmas and propositions, even if it means increasing the paper's length slightly.
2.  **Discuss the implications of relaxing Assumption 2.1(b)**.
3.  **Include a brief discussion on practical step size selection strategies** for the PGM.
4.  **Consider expanding the numerical experiments** to other applications or datasets.
