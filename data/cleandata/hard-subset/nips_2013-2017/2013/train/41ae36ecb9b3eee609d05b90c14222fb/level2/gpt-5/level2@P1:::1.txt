Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization with trace-norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex, and has Lipschitz gradient; additionally, h is “locally strongly convex” in the sense of being strongly convex on any compact convex subset of its domain. The main result is a Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal solution set to the proximal residual ‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F. Using the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework, this yields asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) with fixed step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf), without assuming strong convexity of f.

This addresses a gap between known sublinear rates O(1/k) (or O(1/k^2) with acceleration) for general convex composite problems and linear rates that previously required strong convexity of f. Error bounds and linear rates had been established for problems with polyhedral or separable nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) via Tseng and Luo–Tseng analyses. For the trace norm, whose epigraph is non-polyhedral and whose singular-value coupling blocks variable-splitting arguments, analogous bounds were open. The paper overcomes this by exploiting a spectral alignment property at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors), together with matrix perturbation arguments and a restricted invertibility inequality on the optimal set’s manifold, to prove the desired error bound. Numerical illustrations on matrix completion (least squares) and matrix classification (logistic loss) show linear convergence once sufficiently close to the solution set.

Strengths:
- Novel and nontrivial theory: A local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization under a broad and natural class of losses (f = h∘A) appears new and of independent interest.
- Clear connection to established frameworks: The paper integrates the new bound into the Luo–Tseng analysis to deliver clean linear rate statements (Q-linear for values, R-linear for iterates).
- Technical insight: The simultaneous SVD property of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) (Proposition 4.2) and the restricted invertibility inequality are well-motivated and address the chief obstacle vs. ℓ1/group LASSO.
- Applicability: Covers square and logistic losses and general linear sensing operators A, including non-injective A (where f is not strongly convex).
- Empirical support: Experiments, though small-scale, are consistent with the theory and include both least squares and logistic losses.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Locality and practicality of the bound: The error bound is local (requires F(X) ≤ ζ and ‖R(X)‖F ≤ ε). While standard, it would help to discuss the size of the neighborhood (i.e., how ε depends on problem data) or provide practical diagnostics for when linear convergence should kick in.
- Step-size assumptions vs. experiments: The theory requires αk < 1/Lf. In the matrix completion experiments, α = 1 is used. For the projection sampling operator, Lf = 1, so α = 1 sits on the boundary (the theorem assumes ᾱ < 1/Lf). Please clarify whether the proof tolerates α = 1 in this special case, or adjust the experiments (e.g., α = 0.9).
- Scope of results for variants: It would be valuable to comment on whether the error bound directly implies linear convergence for variants: (i) constant step with backtracking, (ii) inexact proximal steps (cf. Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011), and (iii) proximal acceleration with restarts. A short discussion would increase practical impact.
- Comparison and scale in experiments: The experiments are limited to n = 40 and do not compare against accelerated PGM (which often performs best in practice), proximal quasi-Newton, or ADMM methods commonly used for nuclear-norm problems. Adding even brief comparisons or larger n would strengthen the empirical section.
- Assumption 2.1(b): “Strongly convex on any convex compact subset” is less standard. It is used to yield a positive curvature σ on the compact set Z containing the iterates. Clarifying how σ depends on Z, and whether Z can be chosen uniformly for the level set {F ≤ ζ}, would improve readability.
- Notational consistency: Experiments use µ as the regularization parameter while the paper uses τ elsewhere; consider unifying.

Quality:
The technical development is careful and leverages appropriate tools from convex analysis, variational analysis, and matrix perturbation/SVD theory. The main lemmas (asymptotic optimality, bounded iterates/curvature, and restricted invertibility) are plausible and appropriately support Theorem 3.1. The linkage to Luo–Tseng is standard and sound. Some proofs are deferred to the supplement; for a NIPS paper, the level of detail provided in the main text seems reasonable.

Clarity:
The paper is well-organized, with a clear statement of assumptions, algorithm, residual map, and main results. The role of each ingredient in the error bound proof is well-motivated. Suggestions:
- Explicitly summarize in the main text how the constants (η, ε) in the error bound depend on problem data and αk ranges.
- Highlight the spectral alignment proposition earlier as the key structural fact and give intuition.
- Unify notation for the regularization parameter and for operators’ norms (e.g., define Lf explicitly as L||A||^2 when f = h∘A and ∇h is L-Lipschitz).

Originality:
High. To my knowledge, this is the first linear-rate result for PGM on nuclear-norm regularized problems without strong convexity of f, obtained via a new error bound tailored to the non-polyhedral trace norm. The alignment-based analysis is a novel twist compared to separable norms.

Significance:
Moderate to high. Linear convergence guarantees for widely used methods in matrix learning are of broad interest. The error bound technique may inspire extensions to other spectral regularizers (e.g., Schatten-p norms, spectral k-support norms) and to algorithm variants. The locality of the result is standard; still, providing explicit guidance on the basin of linear convergence would enhance practical value.

Specific suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the step-size condition and reconcile with α = 1 used in experiments; consider adding a brief experiment with α just below 1/Lf and one with backtracking to illustrate robustness.
- Discuss applicability to inexact proximal computations and line search; a short corollary or remark would be helpful.
- Add a brief comparison to accelerated PGM and/or ADMM on at least one task, and report iteration and time.
- Provide a short discussion tying the error bound to Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz-type analyses and how the present approach differs/strengthens them for nuclear norm.
- Include a note on estimating or bounding η and ε in practice, even conservatively, and simple diagnostics (e.g., monitoring residual vs. distance decrease).
- Minor: unify τ/µ notation; state explicitly Lf in terms of L and ||A||.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and technically solid error bound for trace-norm regularization.
- First linear-rate result for PGM without strong convexity of f in this setting.
- Clear relevance to widely used problems (matrix completion/classification).
- Proof techniques (spectral alignment and restricted invertibility) are insightful and likely reusable.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical section is limited in scale and comparisons; step-size condition needs clarification.
- Local nature of the result is not quantified; guidance for practitioners could be elaborated.
- Some proof details are deferred; a bit more intuition in the main text would aid accessibility.

Overall, I find the contribution original, technically sound, and relevant; with the suggested clarifications and minor experimental enhancements, it would make a strong NIPS paper.
