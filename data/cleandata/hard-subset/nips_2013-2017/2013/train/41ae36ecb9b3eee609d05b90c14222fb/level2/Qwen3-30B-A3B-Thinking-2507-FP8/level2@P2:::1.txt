

This paper addresses an important open question in the optimization of trace norm-regularized problems: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieves linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions on the loss function. The authors establish a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which they use to prove linear convergence of the PGM for a broad class of loss functions (those of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h satisfying certain smoothness and convexity properties). 

**Strengths:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question about linear convergence of first-order methods for trace norm regularization without strong convexity. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is a key technical advance that appears to be novel, as the authors properly contextualize it against prior work on error bounds for ℓ1-norm and group LASSO regularization. The proof techniques are sophisticated, particularly the use of matrix perturbation theory to establish the restricted invertibility property (Lemma 4.1(c)). The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification successfully demonstrate the linear convergence behavior predicted by the theory, with semi-log plots clearly showing the linear convergence phase once the iterates are close to the optimal solution set.

**Weaknesses:** While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper could better contextualize the significance of their error bound relative to existing error bounds. The authors note that prior error bounds exist for ℓ1-norm and group LASSO regularization, but they don't explicitly compare the constants or the conditions under which their bound holds. This would help readers understand the precise improvement over prior work. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational implications of their results. For instance, the Lipschitz constant of ∇f (which affects the step size in PGM) is not discussed in the context of the specific applications considered, which might impact practical implementation. 

The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope (only two problems), and the paper doesn't compare the convergence rate of PGM with other methods (e.g., accelerated PGM or ADMM) for the same problems. While the authors note that they use backtracking line search for the logistic loss case, they don't quantify the computational overhead this introduces compared to fixed step sizes.

**Major Concerns:** The most significant issue is that the paper doesn't clearly establish the conditions under which the linear convergence rate is achieved in practice. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) requires that the iterates satisfy F(X) ≤ ζ and ‖R(X)‖F ≤ ǫ, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to determine these constants for specific applications. Without this, the practical utility of the linear convergence guarantee is limited. The authors could strengthen their contribution by discussing how to estimate these constants or providing empirical evidence that the iterates typically enter the region where the linear convergence rate applies.

**Constructive Suggestions:** 
1. Expand the discussion of the error bound's practical implications, including how to estimate the constants η and ǫ for specific applications.
2. Add a comparison with other first-order methods (e.g., accelerated PGM) in the numerical experiments to better demonstrate the practical advantages of the linear convergence rate.
3. Clarify the relationship between the error bound and the step size requirements in the convergence proof, particularly how the choice of step size affects the region where linear convergence is guaranteed.
4. Include a brief discussion of computational complexity to help readers understand the trade-offs between the theoretical guarantees and practical implementation.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization for trace norm regularization. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important open question. While the numerical experiments are somewhat limited, the theoretical results are significant and likely to be valuable to researchers working on matrix optimization problems in machine learning. The paper meets the criteria for high-quality NIPS research in terms of technical contribution, novelty, and significance.

The main weakness is the limited discussion of the practical conditions required to achieve linear convergence, but this is a common challenge in convergence rate analysis. The paper is technically rigorous, makes a clear contribution to the field, and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors could address the minor concerns in a revised version.
