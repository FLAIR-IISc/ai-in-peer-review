- Main contribution: Proves a local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularized problems and uses it to obtain asymptotic linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) without strong convexity of f.  
- Technical novelty: Shows that any optimal X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors, enabling spectral perturbation and a “restricted invertibility” inequality.  
- Error-bound detail: dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F holds on bounded level sets for sufficiently small residuals.  
- Convergence result: Under constant stepsizes 0<αk<ᾱ<1/Lf, objective converges Q-linearly and iterates converge R-linearly to the solution set.  
- Applicability: Assumptions cover square and logistic losses and non-injective A, so results apply to matrix completion, classification, and multi-task learning.  
- Relation to prior work: Extends Luo–Tseng error-bound framework from polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group LASSO) to the non-polyhedral nuclear norm.  
- Practical context: Addresses an open gap where nuclear-norm algorithms empirically converge fast but lacked linear-rate theory without strong convexity.  
- Strength: Novel, nontrivial technical advance overcoming non-polyhedral difficulties via spectral alignment.  
- Strength: Uses standard, well-accepted frameworks and provides implementable residual measures for algorithms.  
- Essential weakness: Error bound is local — need clarification on how small the residual ε must be and how to verify entry into the linear regime during optimization.  
- Essential weakness: Experiments use backtracking; the paper should argue that standard backtracking ensures the required asymptotic stepsize bounds (α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf).  
- Optional suggestion: Provide quantitative dependency of constants (η, κ, σ) on L, ‖A‖, τ to improve interpretability and tuning guidance.  
- Optional suggestion: Discuss robustness to inexact proximal computations (e.g., truncated SVDs) and whether summable/relative errors preserve linear rates.  
- Optional suggestion: Comment on accelerated methods and whether restarting or other schemes can recover linear rates under the error bound.  
- Optional suggestion: Strengthen experiments with comparisons to APG/ADMM, and report iteration/time to fixed tolerance across sizes and sampling rates.  
- Quality: Analysis is sound; key proofs are careful though some details are deferred to the supplement.  
- Clarity: Well-written and structured; suggest restating key inequalities from the supplement and giving intuitive discussion of the spectral alignment proposition.  
- Originality: High — the Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm in this composite setting appears new.  
- Significance: Moderate–high — improves theoretical understanding of ubiquitous PGM methods for nuclear-norm problems and may impact algorithm design.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, technically solid error bound and general convergence guarantees for common losses without strong convexity.  
- Rejection arguments: Results are asymptotic/local with unspecified constants, experiments are limited and lack comparisons, and no discussion of inexact prox for large-scale use.
