- Problem & main result: Presents a local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularized convex problems that yields asymptotic linear convergence of fixed-step proximal gradient without requiring strong convexity of f.  
- Techniques: Leverages a spectral alignment property between X̄ and −∇f(X̄), matrix perturbation arguments, and a restricted invertibility inequality on the optimal manifold.  
- Relation to prior work: Fills the gap between known sublinear rates for general composite problems and linear rates previously obtained only under strong convexity or for polyhedral/separable nonsmooth terms.  
- Strength: Novel, nontrivial theory — the error bound for the trace norm under h∘A losses appears new and of independent interest.  
- Strength: Integrates the new bound into the Luo–Tseng framework to obtain clear Q-linear (values) and R-linear (iterates) convergence statements.  
- Strength: Technical insight — the simultaneous SVD property and restricted invertibility address the main obstacle versus ℓ1/group LASSO analyses.  
- Strength: Applicability — covers squared and logistic losses and general linear sensing operators, including non-injective A.  
- Strength: Empirical support — small-scale experiments for matrix completion and logistic classification align with the theory.  
- Weakness: Locality — the error bound is local (requires F(X) ≤ ζ and small residual); the size of the basin (ε dependence on data) is not clarified.  
- Weakness: Step-size mismatch — theory needs αk < 1/Lf, but experiments use α = 1 (on the boundary); clarification or adjusted experiments needed.  
- Weakness: Variants left unclear — does the error bound imply linear rates for backtracking, inexact prox, or accelerated methods with restarts?  
- Weakness: Limited experiments — small n (n=40) and no comparisons to accelerated PGM, proximal quasi-Newton, or ADMM; larger-scale and comparative results would help.  
- Weakness: Assumption 2.1(b) (“strongly convex on any compact convex subset”) is nonstandard; dependence of curvature σ on the compact set Z should be clarified.  
- Weakness: Notational inconsistency — regularization parameter denoted µ in experiments vs τ in theory; unify notation.  
- Quality: Technical development is careful and plausible; main lemmas support Theorem 3.1 and linkage to Luo–Tseng is sound, though some proofs are deferred to the supplement.  
- Clarity: Paper is well-organized; recommended to summarize how constants (η, ε) depend on problem data, highlight the spectral alignment earlier, and define Lf explicitly.  
- Originality: High — likely the first linear-rate result for PGM with nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.  
- Significance: Moderate–high — linear convergence guarantees for matrix learning are broadly relevant and techniques may extend to other spectral regularizers, though locality limits immediate practical impact.  
- Specific improvement: Clarify step-size condition and reconcile with experiments; add experiments with α slightly below 1/Lf and with backtracking.  
- Specific improvement: Comment on or extend the theory to inexact proximal steps and line search; possibly add brief corollaries or remarks.  
- Specific improvement: Add at least one larger-scale comparative experiment (accelerated PGM/ADMM) and report iteration/time.  
- Specific improvement: Discuss relations to Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz analyses and provide guidance for estimating η and ε or practical diagnostics (monitor residual vs distance).  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel, technically solid error bound; first linear-rate for PGM without strong convexity; relevant to common matrix learning tasks; techniques are reusable.  
- Rejection arguments: Limited empirical validation and comparisons; step-size and locality issues need clarification; some proof details are deferred and key constants aren’t quantified.  
- Overall verdict: Original and technically sound work that would benefit from clarifications on step-size, locality, and expanded experiments before final acceptance.
