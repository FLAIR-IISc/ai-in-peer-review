**Summary**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for convex optimization problems involving a smooth loss term and a trace norm regularizer. While PGM is known to have a sublinear convergence rate in general, and a linear rate under a strong convexity assumption on the loss function, this work establishes that linear convergence can be achieved under a weaker set of conditions. Specifically, the paper shows that if the loss function has the composite form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function and `A` is a linear operator, then PGM converges R-linearly. This setting is highly relevant as it includes many important machine learning problems, such as matrix completion with the square loss, where the overall loss function `f` is not strongly convex.

The key technical contribution enabling this result is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance of an iterate to the optimal solution set with the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. The proof of this error bound is non-trivial; it departs from existing techniques for `l1`-norm or group LASSO regularizers due to the non-polyhedral and non-separable nature of the trace norm. The analysis instead leverages the spectral structure of the optimal solutions and uses matrix perturbation theory to control the behavior of iterates near the solution set. With this error bound established, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the analytical framework of Luo and Tseng. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks, which demonstrate the predicted linear convergence behavior.

**Quality**

The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs. The overall argument is sound, proceeding logically from the central technical contribution—the novel error bound (Theorem 3.1)—to the main result on the linear convergence of PGM (Theorem 3.2). The proof of the error bound is particularly involved and appears to be correct, successfully navigating the challenges posed by the spectral properties of the trace norm. The work represents a complete and solid piece of theoretical research.

**Clarity**

The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by situating the problem within the existing literature, clearly stating the gap being addressed, and summarizing the main contributions. The main results are presented precisely, and the subsequent proof outline (Section 4) effectively conveys the key ideas and challenges of the technical argument without getting lost in details, which are appropriately deferred to the supplementary material. The notation is standard and consistent.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to more explicitly highlight that the established linear convergence is asymptotic. The error bound in Theorem 3.1 holds locally (when `‖R(X)‖_F ≤ ǫ`), which implies the linear convergence phase begins once iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. While this is implicitly understood and observed in the experiments, stating this limitation more directly in the main theoretical section could further improve clarity.

**Originality**

The work is highly original and novel.
1.  It provides, to the best of this reviewer's knowledge, the first linear convergence result for the standard PGM on this class of trace norm regularized problems without requiring the strong convexity of the loss function `f`. This significantly relaxes the conditions under which fast convergence can be guaranteed.
2.  The main technical tool, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, resolves what the paper identifies as an open question.
3.  The proof technique for this error bound is particularly innovative. It moves beyond the variable-splitting approaches used for separable regularizers (like the `l1`-norm) and introduces a new analysis based on the shared singular vector structure of the optimal solution and its gradient, using tools from matrix perturbation theory. This approach is novel and tailored specifically to the properties of the trace norm.

**Significance**

The results presented are of high significance for the machine learning and optimization communities.
*   Trace norm regularization is a fundamental technique for a wide range of problems involving low-rank matrix estimation, including collaborative filtering, multi-task learning, and system identification.
*   The proximal gradient method is a workhorse algorithm for solving these problems due to its simplicity and scalability.
*   This work provides a much-improved theoretical understanding of the practical performance of PGM, explaining the fast convergence often observed in practice even when the problem is not strongly convex.
*   The established error bound is a significant result in its own right and could serve as a tool for analyzing other algorithms or for developing new, faster methods for this class of problems.

The work substantially advances the state of the art in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for non-strongly convex, structured optimization problems.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong, but the following suggestions might help to further improve it:
*   **Experimental Comparisons:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the linear convergence. Their value could be enhanced by including a comparison with an accelerated variant of PGM (e.g., FISTA). While the analysis applies to the standard PGM, showing how the asymptotic linear rate compares to the sublinear `O(1/k^2)` rate of an accelerated method on a semi-log plot would provide a more complete picture of the practical implications. This would help readers understand when the asymptotic linear phase begins to dominate.
*   **Intuition for Proof:** In the proof sketch for Lemma 4.1, the decomposition of `Q̄` in Proposition 4.3 is a critical step. While the full proof is in the supplement, adding a few sentences in the main text to provide more intuition behind the three cases for the singular values (A, B, C) could make this key part of the argument more accessible.

These suggestions are intended to enhance the paper's impact and are not essential for acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a significant and practical problem: the convergence rate of PGM for trace norm regularization.
2.  Provides a novel and strong theoretical result—linear convergence without strong convexity—that improves upon the existing state of the art.
3.  The main technical contribution, a new Lipschitzian error bound, is of independent interest and resolves a known open problem.
4.  The proof methodology is innovative and tailored to the unique structure of the trace norm.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
This reviewer finds no significant flaws in the paper that would argue against its acceptance. The identified areas for improvement are minor and relate to enhancing clarity and the scope of the experiments.
