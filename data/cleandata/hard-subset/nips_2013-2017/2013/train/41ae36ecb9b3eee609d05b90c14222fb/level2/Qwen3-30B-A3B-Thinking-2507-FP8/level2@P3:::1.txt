

This paper establishes a linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, and A a linear operator. The key contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1) that establishes the relationship between the residual norm ‖R(X)‖_F and the distance to the optimal solution set, which enables the linear convergence proof without requiring strong convexity of f.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by resolving an open question regarding linear convergence for PGM on trace norm problems without strong convexity assumptions. Previous work had established linear convergence under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al. 2011), while sublinear convergence was known for general convex loss functions (e.g., Toh and Yun 2010). The authors' approach using matrix perturbation theory to handle the spectral structure of trace norm regularization is novel and addresses a gap noted in prior work (Luo and Tseng 1992; Tseng 2009), which had established similar error bounds for ℓ1-norm but not for trace norm.

The technical development is rigorous and well-structured. The proof of Theorem 3.1 relies on a key lemma (Lemma 4.1) that establishes asymptotic optimality, bounded iterates, and restricted invertibility properties. The proof technique, particularly the use of simultaneous singular value decomposition for X̄ and -Ḡ (Proposition 4.2), provides valuable insight into the structure of optimal solutions for trace norm problems. The authors also provide numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification that demonstrate the linear convergence behavior, consistent with their theoretical claims.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the prevalence of trace norm regularization in machine learning applications and the theoretical gap that this work addresses. The technical sections are carefully developed, and the notation is consistent throughout. The paper also properly contextualizes its contributions relative to prior NIPS work on similar problems (e.g., Ji and Ye 2009, Tomioka and Aihara 2007).

The significance of the work is substantial. Trace norm regularization is widely used in machine learning applications including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and classification. Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions broadens the applicability of the PGM to important practical problems like matrix completion where the loss function is not strongly convex. The error bound itself may be of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization.

One minor weakness is that the paper could better contextualize the error bound relative to existing results for other regularizers. While the authors note the difference between trace norm and ℓ1-norm regularization, a more detailed comparison with error bounds for other matrix norms would strengthen the argument for the novelty of this contribution. Additionally, the numerical experiments could provide more detail on parameter selection and computational efficiency.

The theoretical claims are well-supported by the detailed proofs, and the numerical results confirm the linear convergence behavior in practice. The paper addresses the key question of whether linear convergence can be established for a broader class of loss functions than those requiring strong convexity. The proof technique using matrix perturbation theory to handle the spectral structure represents a valuable technical contribution.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria: it is technically sound, well-written, original, and significant. The linear convergence result is a notable advancement in the understanding of first-order methods for trace norm-regularized problems, with implications for both theoretical optimization and practical machine learning applications. The error bound established in Theorem 3.1 is likely to be useful for future work on matrix optimization problems.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a clear and significant advance in the understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. The paper is well-motivated, technically rigorous, and well-presented. The results are important for both the optimization and machine learning communities.
