- Main claim: Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) attains R-linear convergence for trace-norm regularized problems of the form f(X)=h(A(X)) without requiring strong convexity.  
- Relevance: Result directly applies to practical problems like matrix completion where loss is not strongly convex.  
- Key technical contribution: A new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularized objectives, proved via matrix perturbation theory and spectral analysis.  
- Resolution of open problem: The error bound settles a known gap in the literature about linear convergence for trace-norm regularization.  
- Significance: Establishes a major theoretical advance explaining PGM’s strong empirical performance under weaker assumptions.  
- Originality: First to prove linear convergence for this problem class without strong convexity; novel proof technique handling spectral properties.  
- Quality: Technically rigorous proofs, strong connection to Luo–Tseng framework, and supportive numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification.  
- Clarity: Manuscript is well-written, well-motivated, and clearly organized with outlined proof strategy.  
- Weakness: Convergence guarantee is asymptotic; theory does not quantify the duration of the initial sublinear phase.  
- Acceptance arguments: Novel and significant theoretical result, independently interesting error bound, technically excellent and empirically validated.  
- No substantive arguments against acceptance; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
