This paper studies the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularization problems. While PGM is known to converge sublinearly for general convex loss functions and linearly under a strong convexity assumption, this work proves that linear convergence can be achieved without strong convexity. The main claim is that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex and smooth but the overall function f is not necessarily so, PGM converges at an R-linear rate. This result is highly relevant to applications like matrix completion, where the loss is often not strongly convex. The key technical tool enabling this analysis is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized objective, a significant contribution that resolves a known open question in the literature.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning optimization. Establishing linear convergence for a standard algorithm under weaker, more practical assumptions is a major theoretical advance. The result provides a strong theoretical justification for the excellent empirical performance of PGM on problems like matrix completion.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. It appears to be the first to establish linear convergence for this problem class without strong convexity. The proof technique, particularly the development of the error bound using matrix perturbation theory to handle the spectral properties of the trace norm, is novel and overcomes limitations of prior work on L1 and group LASSO norms.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The proofs are detailed, and the connection to the established Luo-Tseng framework for convergence analysis is expertly handled. The theoretical claims are further validated with clear and supportive numerical experiments on both matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.
*   **Clarity:** The manuscript is exceptionally well-written and organized. It does an excellent job of motivating the problem, positioning the contribution against prior art, and clearly outlining the proof strategy before delving into technical details.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper is very strong, with only minor points for consideration. The established linear convergence is asymptotic, and the theory does not characterize the length of the initial sublinear phase. While this is a minor point, a brief comment on this could add further context.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and significant theoretical result for a widely used algorithm on a fundamental class of problems.
2.  The key technical contribution—a new error bound for trace norm regularization—is of independent interest.
3.  The paper is technically excellent, clearly written, and well-supported by experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
None.

This is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
