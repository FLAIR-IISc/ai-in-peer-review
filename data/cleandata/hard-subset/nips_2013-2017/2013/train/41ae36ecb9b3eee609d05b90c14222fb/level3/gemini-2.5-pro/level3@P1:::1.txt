This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems of the form `min f(X) + τ||X||*`. It is well-known that PGM converges sublinearly for general convex loss functions `f` and linearly if `f` is strongly convex. The primary contribution of this work is to show that a linear convergence rate can be achieved even when `f` is not strongly convex, under the structural assumption that `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a smooth function that is strongly convex on any compact set. This setting covers many important applications, such as matrix completion with a square loss, where the overall loss function is not strongly convex.

The core of the analysis is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized objective. This bound provides a local estimate of the distance from any point `X` to the optimal solution set in terms of a computable residual. Establishing this error bound is challenging because the trace norm's epigraph is not polyhedral, precluding analysis techniques used for l1-norm or group LASSO problems. The authors overcome this by using matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimum. This error bound is the key ingredient that allows the application of the general framework of Luo and Tseng to prove the linear convergence of PGM. This work therefore extends an important line of research on error-bound-based convergence analysis from vector-valued problems to the matrix setting of trace norm regularization.

### Strengths
*   **Theoretical Significance:** The paper provides an affirmative answer to the important question of whether PGM can converge linearly for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This result provides stronger theoretical guarantees for a widely used algorithm on a practical and important class of problems.
*   **Technical Novelty:** The main technical result, a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, is a non-trivial and valuable contribution. The proof technique, which analyzes the spectral properties of the iterates and the gradient, is novel for this type of analysis and successfully circumvents the difficulties posed by the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The motivation is well-established, and the authors do a good job of outlining the high-level proof strategy before diving into technical details, making the paper accessible despite its technical depth.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement
*   **Perceived Incrementality:** While technically sound, the work builds heavily upon the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng. The paper could be strengthened by more explicitly delineating the novel contributions (the matrix analysis for the error bound) from the parts of the proof that are adapted from this existing framework. This would help better frame the originality and impact of the contribution.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments demonstrate the linear convergence predicted by the theory. However, they could provide deeper insight into the behavior of the algorithm. It would be highly beneficial to include simulations that explore the boundaries of the theory, for instance, by showing how the convergence rate changes as problem parameters related to the assumptions are varied. A discussion on scenarios where the theory might fail, but linear convergence is still empirically observed, would also be a valuable addition.
*   **Clarity of Proof Details:**
    *   The proof of Theorem 3.2 introduces constants κ1 through κ4. It would improve the clarity and completeness of the analysis to specify how these constants depend on the step-size bounds `α` and `ᾱ`.
    *   The sequence of inequalities leading to (18) is slightly confusing. The final inequality appears to follow more directly from the convexity of `f` and the subgradient optimality condition for `X̄k`, as seen in related works like Tseng [22]. Clarifying this step would make the proof easier to follow.

### Proposed Changes
1.  **Refine Positioning:** In the discussion, please elaborate further on the specific technical challenges posed by the trace norm that are not present in l1/group LASSO problems, and how your analysis uniquely addresses them. This will better highlight the novelty beyond the direct application of an existing framework.
2.  **Enhance Experiments:** Consider adding experiments that test the sensitivity of the convergence rate to problem properties (e.g., conditioning of `A`). An investigation into cases where theoretical assumptions are relaxed or fail would provide valuable practical insights.
3.  **Expand Discussion:** A brief discussion on the potential to extend this error bound analysis to other popular algorithms, such as the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM), would be a welcome addition and would broaden the paper's scope and impact.
4.  **Minor Proof Clarifications:** Please clarify the dependencies of the constants in the proof of Theorem 3.2 and consider revising the derivation leading to inequality (18) for improved readability.

In conclusion, this is a strong technical paper presenting a significant theoretical result for a popular class of optimization problems in machine learning. The primary weaknesses are a perception of the contribution as a technical extension and an opportunity for more insightful empirical validation. Addressing the suggestions above would substantially strengthen the paper.
