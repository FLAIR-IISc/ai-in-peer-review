This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for minimizing a composite objective `F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`, where `f` is a smooth convex function and `||.||*` is the trace (nuclear) norm. While PGM is known to have a sublinear `O(1/k)` convergence rate in general, and a linear rate when `f` is strongly convex, this work establishes an asymptotic linear convergence rate under weaker conditions. Specifically, it assumes `f` has the form `h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex but the overall function `f` is not necessarily strongly convex (e.g., if the linear operator `A` has a non-trivial nullspace).

The proof strategy follows the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng (1993), which requires an error bound that relates the distance to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map. The main technical contribution of this paper is the establishment of such a local Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. The key insight enabling this proof is a novel observation that an optimal solution `X*` and the negative gradient at that point, `-∇f(X*)`, can be simultaneously diagonalized by the same singular vectors. This allows the authors to reduce the matrix-level analysis to a vector-level analysis of singular values, overcoming difficulties that arise because the trace norm is non-polyhedral and non-separable.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Addresses a Key Theoretical Question:** The paper provides a positive answer to whether linear convergence of PGM can be established for trace norm problems without the strong convexity of the loss function. This extends a line of analysis previously applied to L1 and group-LASSO penalties to the important case of the trace norm.
2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** The core of the paper is the proof of a new local error bound for trace norm regularization. The observation about the simultaneous singular value decomposition of the optimal solution and the gradient is elegant and is the key technical tool that makes the analysis tractable. This is a clever and non-trivial contribution.
3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical arguments appear to be sound and the derivations are carefully laid out.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Moderate Originality:** While the technical trick for proving the error bound is new, the overall approach of using a Luo-Tseng style error bound to show linear convergence is a standard technique. The work can be seen as an extension of the analyses in Tseng (2010) and Zhang et al. (2013) to the trace norm setting.
2.  **Limited Practical Significance:** The impact of the result is primarily theoretical. The established linear convergence is asymptotic, meaning it only applies once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. In practice, the initial sublinear phase may dominate the optimization process, and accelerated methods with global `O(1/k^2)` rates may still be superior. The paper does not propose a new algorithm or provide experiments that demonstrate a practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally readable, but the organization could be improved. The main result on linear convergence (Theorem 3.2) is stated, but its proof immediately dives into the need for the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which is then proven in a dense technical section. The paper might be easier to follow if it first presented Theorem 3.2 and showed how the linear rate is derived *assuming* the error bound holds. The more involved proof of the error bound (Section 4) could then follow. This would better separate the main idea of the convergence analysis from the technical machinery required for the error bound itself.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**
*   The proof sketch of Theorem 3.2 relies on three inequalities (9, 10, 11). Inequality (11), `‖R(X^k)‖_F ≤ κ_3‖X^k −X^{k+1}‖_F`, is stated without proof in the main paper, with a reference to the supplementary material. This inequality is a key step, as it connects the residual to the progress of the iterates. It appears this exact type of bound was already established in Lemma 4 of Suvrit Sra, "A new metric on the positive definite cone with applications to algorithms," NIPS 2012. The authors should clarify the relationship to this prior work and provide a clear justification for this inequality in their context.
*   The asymptotic nature of the result raises questions about its practical relevance. Can the authors provide any intuition or analysis on the size of the region where linear convergence takes hold? The numerical experiments demonstrate the phenomenon but do not provide a comparison against standard accelerated proximal gradient methods, which would be a valuable benchmark.
*   The key assumption is that `f(X) = h(A(X))` where `h` is strictly convex. While this covers important cases like squared and logistic loss, could the authors comment on whether their analysis framework could be extended to more general smooth convex functions `f` that satisfy some other regularity condition (e.g., a quadratic growth condition) instead of strong or strict convexity?

**Summary of the Review:**
The paper presents a solid, technically sound theoretical result on the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace norm regularization, extending prior work to settings without strong convexity. The main novelty lies in the proof of a local error bound, which is enabled by an elegant observation about simultaneous diagonalization. However, the originality is somewhat tempered by the use of a standard analytical framework, and the practical significance is limited by the asymptotic nature of the result. Overall, this is a moderately interesting theoretical contribution that will be of interest to the optimization and theory communities within NIPS.
