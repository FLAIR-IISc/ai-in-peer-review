This paper studies the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace-norm regularized risk minimization problems. The authors aim to establish linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, a common limitation in previous work (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, 2009; Nesterov, 2013). They achieve this by following the framework of Luo & Tseng (2010), and crucially, introduce a new observation allowing simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, reducing the problem to a more tractable vector case.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a relevant problem in machine learning with applications in areas like matrix completion and low-rank approximation. The extension of linear convergence results beyond strong convexity is a significant potential contribution. The connection to existing work, particularly Luo & Tseng’s framework, is clearly stated. The authors correctly point out the difficulties in extending existing error bound techniques (developed for ℓ1 regularization) to the trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph and the interdependent nature of its singular values.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The overall approach appears correct, and the derivation seems rigorous. The authors leverage existing theory effectively and propose a novel approach to handle the specific challenges posed by the trace norm.
* **Novelty:** The simultaneous diagonalization observation is a key novelty. While building upon existing frameworks, it represents a non-trivial technical contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and readable. The authors provide sufficient context and justification for their assumptions. The numerical experiments help to illustrate the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Originality:** While the simultaneous diagonalization is new, the overall methodology heavily relies on the established Luo & Tseng framework. The paper feels incremental rather than a paradigm shift.
* **Significance:** The results are primarily theoretical. While linear convergence is desirable, its asymptotic nature and the specific conditions required may limit its practical impact. The experiments, while helpful, don’t demonstrate significant improvements over existing solvers.
* **Justification Gap:** As noted in the reviewer’s initial assessment, the justification for the inequality |R_k| <= κ |X_k - X_{k+1}| (Appendix, line 085) is missing a clear explanation of why the choice of κ3 (related to α < 1) guarantees the stated bound. This is a crucial step in the error bound proof and needs further elaboration.
* **Presentation:** The paper would benefit from a restructuring. The current presentation buries the main result (linear convergence) behind detailed technical proofs. Presenting the high-level result and then delving into the technical details would improve readability and impact. Specifically, stating Theorem 3.2 upfront, then presenting the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and *then* the proof, would provide a clearer narrative.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Section 4.1.2:** The discussion of Cases A, B, and C feels somewhat disjointed. A clearer explanation of how the choice of ǫ guarantees the satisfaction of equations (25) and (26) in each case would be beneficial.
* **Notation:** While generally clear, the notation could be slightly more consistent. For example, the use of both 'X̄' and 'X̃k' to represent related concepts is potentially confusing.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a relevant theoretical question and provides a positive answer.
* The technical contribution of the simultaneous diagonalization is valuable.
* The results are rigorously proven and supported by numerical experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The originality is moderate, and the contribution feels incremental.
* The practical significance is limited due to the asymptotic nature of the convergence result.
* The missing justification in the appendix raises a concern about the completeness of the proof.
* The presentation could be improved to enhance clarity and impact.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of the PGM for trace-norm regularized problems. However, the incremental nature of the work and the limitations in practical impact temper enthusiasm. The missing justification in the appendix needs to be addressed. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors providing a clear justification for the inequality in Appendix line 085 and restructuring the paper to prioritize the presentation of the main result. A stronger emphasis on the limitations of the asymptotic convergence and potential avenues for future research would also be beneficial.
