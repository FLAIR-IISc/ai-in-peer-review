## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper presents a significant theoretical result: proving linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems under relatively weak assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a valuable contribution, as many machine learning applications, such as matrix completion, naturally involve non-strongly convex loss functions. The problem addressed is important and well-motivated, with clear connections to a wide range of applications.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors build upon Tseng’s Lipschitzian error bound technique [20] and successfully extend it to the nuclear (trace) norm setting, addressing a previously open question. The proof appears technically sound, and the authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature. The inclusion of numerical experiments further strengthens the validity of the theoretical findings, showing linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and classification tasks.

However, a more detailed comparison to the vector l1-norm case, as requested, would be beneficial. While the authors acknowledge the difficulties in directly translating l1 techniques due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the interdependence of singular values, a more explicit analogy – outlining *precisely* where the approaches diverge and why – would enhance understanding. Specifically, discussing how the matrix perturbation theory approach overcomes the challenges of splitting variables (unlike the l1 case) would be insightful.  It's not immediately clear to what extent the trace-norm result is a straightforward extension versus a genuinely novel contribution; explicitly stating this would be helpful. The corollary relating to the l1-norm case should be explicitly stated.

Regarding minor points, clarifying whether the operator *P* in line 337 is a sampling or a mask operator is necessary. The broken reference on line 337 should be fixed. Briefly recalling the definitions of Q- and R-linear convergence for reader clarity would improve accessibility. Relating the residual *R(X)* defined in (6) to Tseng’s original definition (46) would aid interpretation. Finally, expanding the discussion/conclusions justifying the technical Lemma 3.2 would be beneficial, as its importance is stated but not fully elaborated.

The title could be improved by removing the word "structured," as the core contribution lies in the loss function's properties (the linear operator *A*) rather than the regularizer itself.

**Overall:**

**Strengths:** Technically sound, clear writing, addresses an important problem, novel extension of existing techniques.
**Weaknesses:**  Limited detailed comparison to the l1-norm case, minor clarity issues.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** The paper presents a significant theoretical advance with practical relevance. The technical soundness and clarity of presentation are commendable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** The lack of a more detailed comparison to the l1-norm case slightly diminishes the paper's impact.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
