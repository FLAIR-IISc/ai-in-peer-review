## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems of the form min<sub>X</sub> f(X) + τ||X||<sub>*</sub>, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is a linear operator, and h is strongly convex on compact sets. The main contribution is a proof of linear convergence under relatively weak assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the overall loss function f. This generalizes previous linear convergence results (Tseng [20], Tseng & Yun, Zhang et al.) which typically relied on stronger conditions. 

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical need for faster convergence guarantees in applications like matrix completion and low-rank learning. The core technical contribution lies in establishing a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, which is then leveraged using the framework of Luo and Tseng [10] to demonstrate linear convergence. The presentation is generally clear, and the proofs appear logically sound, though dense. 

However, the paper feels primarily as a technical extension of existing work. While the generalization to non-strongly convex functions is valuable, the practical impact isn’t immediately clear without more extensive empirical validation. The numerical experiments presented are limited and don’t fully demonstrate the benefits of the theoretical result in realistic scenarios. The claim of linear convergence is supported, but a more comprehensive evaluation across different problem settings and parameter regimes would strengthen the argument.

**Strengths:**

*   Establishes linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization under weaker assumptions than prior work.
*   The derived Lipschitzian error bound is a potentially useful result in its own right.
*   Clear and generally well-structured presentation of the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Perceived as a primarily technical contribution lacking sufficient empirical validation.
*   Limited numerical experiments; a semilog plot of objective vs. iterations for a wider range of parameters is needed to convincingly demonstrate linear convergence.
*   The outline of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely mirrors Tseng’s work; the authors should more explicitly cite and differentiate their contributions.
*   The dependencies of constants κ<sub>1</sub>,…,κ<sub>4</sub> on α̲ and ᾱ are unclear and should be clarified.
*   The sequence of inequalities before (13) is confusing and the justification is not entirely clear.

**Requests addressed:**

The authors have included a simulation demonstrating linear convergence for cases where the loss is not strongly convex, and discussed scenarios where the theory might fail. They also briefly mention the potential extension to ADMM, which is a good starting point, but a more detailed discussion of the challenges and potential approaches would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution, but its impact is limited by the lack of comprehensive empirical validation. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on addressing the points raised regarding the dependencies of the constants, clarifying the inequalities before (13), and providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the theory and potential extensions to ADMM. A more extensive numerical evaluation would also significantly strengthen the paper.
