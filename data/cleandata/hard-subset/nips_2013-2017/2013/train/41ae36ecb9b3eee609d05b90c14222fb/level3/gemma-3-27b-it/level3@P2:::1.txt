This paper presents a theoretical result establishing the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace-norm regularized problems of the form min_X F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is a linear operator, and h is strongly convex with a Lipschitz continuous gradient. This extends previous linear convergence results, typically requiring strong convexity of *f* itself, to a broader class of problems. The core contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace-norm regularized problem, enabling the application of Luo and Tseng’s convergence framework.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the motivation, problem setup, main results, and proof outline. It correctly positions the work within the existing literature on proximal gradient methods and trace-norm regularization, citing relevant papers such as those by Beck and Teboulle, Recht and Fazel, and Tseng. The mathematical notation is generally clear and the proofs appear technically sound, although some steps could benefit from further clarification (see weaknesses below).  The connection to prior work on error bounds (Tseng [20], Tseng & Yun, Zhang et al.) is acknowledged, but could be more thoroughly discussed with a precise differentiation of the current contribution’s novelty.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The result extends the applicability of linear convergence analysis to trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function *f*. This is a valuable theoretical extension.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears to be technically rigorous, with a well-defined problem setup and a detailed proof outline. The use of a Lipschitzian error bound as a key ingredient is a clever approach.
*   **Relevance:** Trace-norm regularization is a widely used technique in various machine learning applications, including matrix completion and low-rank matrix learning, making the theoretical result potentially impactful.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a clear exposition of the main ideas and results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The primary weakness is the lack of substantial empirical validation. While the authors include numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification, these are limited in scope.  Critically, they do not demonstrate linear convergence for a regime where the strong convexity assumption on *h* is *not* met, yet the PGM still appears to converge linearly in practice. A semilog plot of objective value versus iterations for such a case is strongly recommended.
*   **Overlapping Proof Structure:** The outline of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 4.1 closely resembles the approach in Tseng’s work [20]. While citing Tseng, the authors should more clearly articulate how their proof differs and builds upon the existing literature, highlighting the specific technical challenges overcome in adapting the analysis to the trace norm.
*   **Dependency of Constants:** The dependencies of the constants κ1 through κ4 in Theorem 3.2 on the step size parameters α and ᾱ are not explicitly stated. Clarifying these dependencies would improve the practical applicability of the results.
*   **Minor Technical Issue:** The sequence of inequalities preceding equation (13) is somewhat unclear. The final inequality stated relies on convexity and -Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X||* (as noted in [20, p.289]), not the intermediate step presented.
*   **Limited Discussion of Assumptions:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of scenarios where the theoretical assumptions (particularly the strong convexity of *h* on compact sets) are violated, but empirical linear convergence is still observed. Investigating the robustness of the result to deviations from the assumptions is crucial.  

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and novel theoretical result extending the applicability of linear convergence analysis to a relevant class of problems.
*   The use of a Lipschitzian error bound is a clever approach to overcome the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents the main ideas and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of substantial empirical validation limits the practical impact of the theoretical result.  The omission of an example showing linear convergence when strong convexity is not met is a significant oversight.
*   The close resemblance to existing work (Tseng [20]) without sufficient differentiation of the contributions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but its impact is limited by the lack of comprehensive empirical validation and a more detailed discussion of its connection to related work. While the theoretical result is sound, the limited experimental evidence raises concerns about its practical relevance.

**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject.** I recommend rejection in its current form. However, I would be willing to reconsider if the authors address the following concerns:

1.  **Provide a numerical example demonstrating linear convergence of the PGM even when the strong convexity assumption on *h* is violated.** A semilog plot of objective value versus iterations would be sufficient.
2.  **More clearly differentiate the technical contributions of this work from those of Tseng [20] and other related papers.**
3.  **Clarify the dependencies of the constants κ1 through κ4 on the step size parameters α and ᾱ.**
4.  **Briefly outline how the analysis might extend to the ADMM algorithm.**  (Mentioning this would show awareness of a commonly used alternative.)
