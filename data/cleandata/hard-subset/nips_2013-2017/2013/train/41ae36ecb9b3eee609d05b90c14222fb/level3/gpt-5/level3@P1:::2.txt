This paper studies composite convex problems of the form minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth, strictly convex, and Lipschitz gradient on its domain. The main result proves that the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) achieves asymptotic linear convergence: objective values converge Q-linearly to the optimum and iterates converge R-linearly to the solution set, under a constant step size bounded above by 1/Lf (or via backtracking ensuring such bounds). Crucially, this linear rate holds without strong convexity of f, thereby covering important cases like square loss with non-injective A (matrix completion) and logistic loss. The proof hinges on establishing a local Lipschitzian error bound linking the distance to the optimal solution set to the residual norm ||Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X||F, and then invoking the Luo–Tseng framework.

Relation to prior work: Previous rate guarantees for PGM in nuclear-norm regularization were sublinear O(1/k)–O(1/k^2) in general, with linear convergence known only under strong convexity. Lipschitzian error bounds enabling linear rates were developed for polyhedral or group-separable nonsmooth terms (e.g., ℓ1 and group LASSO) by Tseng and coauthors [22,25] and leveraged by Luo–Tseng [10]. Whether analogous bounds hold for the nuclear norm had been open due to its non-polyhedral epigraph. This paper resolves that gap by deriving an error bound for the nuclear norm case and showing how the spectral structure of optimal points enables the analysis. The result complements algorithmic advances for nuclear-norm problems (e.g., accelerated PG [8,19], fixed-point/Bregman methods [11]) by providing asymptotic linear rates for the basic PGM under general, practically relevant losses.

Technical contributions: The error bound (Theorem 3.1) asserts dist(X,X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F for points in a level set and with sufficiently small residual, where R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X. The key new ingredient is Proposition 4.2: at optimality, X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors; this enables a spectral perturbation analysis of points near the optimal set and a “restricted invertibility” inequality tying ||X−ΠX*||F to ||A(X−ΠX*)||2. Combining this with the curvature of h on compact sets yields the bound. With this in place, the standard Luo–Tseng descent and error-bound machinery gives Q-linear decrease in function values and R-linear convergence of iterates (Theorem 3.2). Experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification illustrate linear slopes in semi-log plots once iterates enter the local region.

Quality: The assumptions on h (smooth, strictly convex, Lipschitz gradient; strongly convex on compact sets) and the A-linear structure cover widely used losses. The analysis is carefully written, with a plausible proof strategy that overcomes the non-polyhedral difficulty by exploiting simultaneous SVD at optimality and matrix perturbation arguments. The linear rate is local/asymptotic via a residual threshold, consistent with error-bound based analyses. The step-size conditions are standard and practically handled by backtracking. The empirical results support the theory qualitatively.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a succinct setup and an intuitive residual-based optimality measure. It would help readers to:
- Include a short paragraph comparing the residual R(X) here to the residual maps used by Tseng and Luo–Tseng (e.g., [10,22]) to clarify the connection.
- Briefly recall the definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence earlier (you already do so near Theorem 3.2; consider moving forward).
- Provide more intuition for the key technical step (restricted invertibility in Lemma 4.1(c)), e.g., a sketch explaining why simultaneous SVD plus curvature of h yield the bound.
- Ensure consistent notation for the regularization parameter (τ vs. μ in experiments).

Originality: High. Extending Lipschitzian error bounds from ℓ1/group norms to the nuclear norm is nontrivial due to non-polyhedral geometry and coupling through singular values. The spectral-structure argument appears novel in this context. As the authors note, this yields (to my knowledge) the first linear-rate result for first-order methods on trace-norm problems without strong convexity of f.

Significance: High. Nuclear-norm regularization is ubiquitous in ML and signal processing. Establishing linear rates under natural loss structures improves theoretical understanding and guides practical algorithm design. The error-bound technique and the simultaneous-SVD insight may be reused for other spectral regularizers.

Suggestions and questions:
- ℓ1 analogy and corollary: Please add a subsection comparing your argument to the ℓ1 case [22,25]. Emphasize why variable-splitting arguments fail for the nuclear norm and how simultaneous SVD replaces separability. Also note that the ℓ1 result follows as a special case by restricting to diagonal matrices (identifying the nuclear norm with ℓ1 on the spectrum); making this an explicit corollary would help position the novelty.
- Scope of the local region: Can you comment on how one might estimate the constants (η, ε) or the neighborhood where the bound holds in practice? Any dependence on A’s conditioning seen in experiments?
- Accelerated methods: While the focus is PGM, can the error-bound framework yield linear rates for proximal gradient with Nesterov acceleration in the local regime (e.g., restart heuristics)? A brief discussion would broaden impact.
- Experiments: Consider adding comparisons to an accelerated variant (e.g., FISTA) and to alternative solvers, and reporting wall-clock time or SVD ranks, to complement iteration-based plots.

Minor issues:
- Clarify P as a masking operator: In matrix completion, “sampling” is random in data generation; but in the optimization problem P is fixed. Please state this explicitly.
- Fix any broken reference around line ~337 in the submitted PDF.
- Relate R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X (your eq. (7)) to prior residual definitions for composite problems to aid interpretation.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open question on error bounds and linear rates for nuclear-norm regularization.
- General, practically relevant loss structure; broad applicability.
- Clean, well-executed analysis with clear takeaways; supportive experiments.

Cons for acceptance:
- Linear rate is asymptotic/local; constants not quantified.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and lacks baselines.

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution of clear relevance to the NIPS community, with novel analysis and practical implications.
