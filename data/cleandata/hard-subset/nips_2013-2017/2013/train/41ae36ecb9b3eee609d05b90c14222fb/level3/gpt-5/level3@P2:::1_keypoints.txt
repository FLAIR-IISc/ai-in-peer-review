- Problem setting: composite convex optimization with trace-norm regularizer F(X)=f(X)+τ||X||*, f(X)=h(A(X)), h has Lipschitz gradient and is strictly/strongly convex on compact convex subsets.
- Main contribution: a Lipschitzian local error bound for R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X that implies asymptotic linear convergence of proximal gradient method (PGM) for fixed step sizes 0<α≤αk≤ᾱ<1/Lf.
- Novelty: result requires no global strong convexity of f, covering cases like square loss with non-injective A and logistic loss.
- Key technical idea: spectral alignment at optimality (optimal X and −∇f(X) share singular vectors) plus matrix-perturbation arguments to get restricted invertibility and the local error bound.
- Empirics: semi-log plots for matrix completion and classification show qualitative linear trends but the experiments are light.

- Relation to prior work: builds on Luo–Tseng error-bound framework and extends error-bound based linear rates from polyhedral/separable regularizers to the non-polyhedral trace norm using new spectral tools.
- Positioning: clarifies relation to prox-gradient linear rates under strong convexity and inexact variants, emphasizing that no strong convexity of f is assumed here.

- Technical soundness: proofs and architecture are sound; Proposition 4.2 (spectral alignment) is the main novel ingredient leading to Theorem 3.2 via standard Luo–Tseng arguments.
- Scope and limitations: convergence rate is local/asymptotic with constants depending on local curvature of h and A; step-size condition requires α bounds and backtracking implications need discussion.
- Empirics quality: experiments support the theory qualitatively but need stronger ablations.

- Clarity: generally well written and organized; proof ideas are clear.
- Request for clarification: make dependence of κ1…κ4 on α, ᾱ, and Lf explicit or give tight inequalities to interpret the contraction factor.
- Minor correction: fix or remove the confusing chain of inequalities before eq. (13) and cite Tseng (2009) for the subgradient relation.
- Explicitness: state that η, ε (error bound) and σ (Lemma 4.1) depend on a bounded level set {F≤ζ}.

- Originality: extending error-bound linear convergence to the trace norm is nontrivial; the spectral alignment and perturbation argument appear new.
- Attribution: overall proof strategy mirrors Tseng/Luo–Tseng—improve attribution and clearly delineate which lemmas/results are borrowed versus new.

- Significance: practically relevant for nuclear-norm problems as it formalizes observed linear PGM behavior without strong convexity.
- Potential impact: the error bound could extend to other first-order splitting methods, inexact proximal steps, stochastic gradients, and inform restart rules.

- Empirical suggestions: add controlled simulations where f is not strongly convex and cases violating assumptions to illustrate when theory applies or fails.
- Discuss failure modes: iterates outside the local region, step sizes not bounded away from zero (aggressive backtracking), or unbounded level sets.
- Backtracking clarity: confirm accepted αk in experiments satisfy αk≤1/Lf and become bounded below by some α>0 after finite iterations.
- ADMM extension: outline how the error bound plus existing ADMM analyses could yield local linear convergence and state necessary extra assumptions (e.g., full column rank in splitting, penalty choices).
- Constants/rates: provide an explicit contraction factor in (12) in terms of σ, ||A||, L, and step-size bounds, or give a computable lower bound.

- Pros: establishes asymptotic linear PGM convergence for nuclear-norm without strong convexity; introduces a novel spectral argument; clear links to common losses and good exposition.
- Cons: incremental extension of established framework; thin empirical evaluation; attribution and some proof steps need tightening; limited practical guidance on constants and step sizes.

- Overall assessment: solid theoretical contribution addressing an open question; recommend acceptance conditional on improved attribution, clarified constants and step-size conditions, stronger empirical validation (non-strongly-convex cases and failure modes), and a brief ADMM discussion.
