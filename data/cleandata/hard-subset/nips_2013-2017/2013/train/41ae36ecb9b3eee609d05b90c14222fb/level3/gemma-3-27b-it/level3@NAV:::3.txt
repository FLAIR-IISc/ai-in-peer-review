## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized risk minimization, a problem prevalent in various machine learning applications like matrix completion and low-rank representation. The authors aim to establish linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function – a significant departure from existing results. They achieve this by building upon the framework of Luo and Tseng, leveraging a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace-norm regularized problem. This bound is established through a clever observation concerning the simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, effectively reducing the problem to a vector case. 

The paper is technically sound and builds logically upon established concepts in convex optimization and proximal algorithms. The approach of utilizing the Luo-Tseng framework is appropriate and well-executed. The key technical contribution, the simultaneous diagonalization observation, is interesting and appears to be novel, enabling the crucial error bound. However, it's important to note that the initial claim regarding the error bound, specifically |R_k| <= κ |X_k - X_{k+1}|, was already proven in Sra (NIPS 2012, Lemma 4), and this connection should be acknowledged more explicitly.

While the analysis appears correct, the presentation could be improved. The paper currently buries the main results – the linear convergence guarantee – within a dense theoretical development. A more effective structure would be to state the key convergence result upfront, followed by a high-level outline of the approach, and then delve into the technical details, particularly the local error bound. Furthermore, the justification for the inequality in Appendix line 85 – specifically, why choosing κ3 based on α < 1 implies R(X^k) <= κ3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F – requires further elaboration. 

The originality of the work is moderate. While the simultaneous diagonalization is a valuable contribution, the overall strategy largely follows established approaches. The significance of the paper is primarily theoretical; the asymptotic nature of the convergence and the lack of discussion regarding practical implications (e.g., choosing appropriate parameters) limit its immediate impact on algorithmic development.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and insightful observation regarding simultaneous diagonalization.
*   Technically sound analysis leading to a linear convergence result under weak assumptions.
*   Addresses an important open problem in the convergence analysis of proximal algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution, building heavily on existing work.
*   Limited practical relevance due to the asymptotic nature of the convergence.
*   Presentation could be improved for better clarity and impact.
*   Missing acknowledgement of prior work (Sra, 2012) on the key error bound.



**Overall assessment:** This paper presents a solid theoretical contribution, but its impact is somewhat limited by its incremental nature and lack of clear practical implications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the presentation concerns and acknowledge related work.
