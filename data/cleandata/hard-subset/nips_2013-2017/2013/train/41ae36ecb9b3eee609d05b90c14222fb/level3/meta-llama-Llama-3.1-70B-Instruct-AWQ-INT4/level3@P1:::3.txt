This paper, "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization," presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and convex optimization. The authors investigate the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving a class of trace norm-regularized problems, which is a popular approach in machine learning.

The paper's main contribution is the establishment of a linear convergence rate for the PGM without requiring strong convexity of the objective function. This is a notable achievement, as previous results on linear convergence of the PGM relied on strong convexity assumptions. The authors achieve this by introducing a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which may be of independent interest.

The technical soundness of the paper is solid, with a clear and well-organized presentation of the results. The authors follow the framework developed by Luo and Tseng, which provides a powerful tool for analyzing the convergence of the PGM. The key technical novelty of the paper lies in the observation that allows for the simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, reducing the problem to the vector case. This insight enables the authors to establish a local error bound, which is crucial for proving the asymptotic linear convergence of the PGM.

However, the reviewer notes that there is a gap in the appendix, where the authors need to justify why the chosen κ3 (e.g., α < 1) implies R(X^k) <= κ3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F. This gap should be addressed to ensure the completeness of the proof.

In terms of clarity, the paper is readable, but the reviewer suggests that the authors should state the main claims upfront and defer the technical local error bound proof. This would improve the paper's readability and make it easier for non-experts to follow the main ideas.

The originality of the paper is moderate, as the simultaneous diagonalization technique is new and interesting, but most of the approach follows existing work. The significance of the paper is mainly theoretical, with limited algorithmic impact. The asymptotic nature of the result weakens its practical significance, as it may not provide immediate benefits for practitioners.

Overall, the reviewer assesses the paper as making an incremental contribution, which is moderately interesting from a theoretical perspective. While the paper does not break new ground in terms of algorithmic impact, it provides a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization and machine learning.
