

This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss function. The key contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound that resolves an open question about applying Tseng's technique to nuclear norm regularization. The result applies to important ML and signal processing applications (matrix completion, classification) with loss functions like square loss and logistic loss, without requiring strong convexity.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant theoretical contribution by extending Tseng's framework to the trace norm case. The main proof appears correct, and the paper properly situates itself within the literature. However, several improvements would strengthen the work. First, the title should be revised to remove "structured" since the primary novelty concerns the loss side (linear operator A), not the regularizer. Second, the paper lacks a clear comparison with the l1-norm case (e.g., [20,22]), which is critical to understanding why l1 techniques don't directly translate to trace norm. The authors should explicitly state how the l1 result follows as a corollary and clarify the perceived novelty versus expected extension.

Minor issues include: clarifying whether P in Section 5 is a sampling or mask operator (sampling implies randomness but P is fixed); fixing the broken reference at line 337; briefly defining Q- and R-linear convergence; relating residual R(X) to Tseng's original definition (46); and adding more discussion to justify Lemma 3.2.

Strengths: Addresses an important open problem, technically rigorous, clear writing, significant contribution to error bounds for trace-norm problems. Weaknesses: Insufficient l1-norm comparison, title issue, minor clarifications needed.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution with clear significance to the field. The minor issues are easily fixable, and the main result represents an important advance in understanding first-order methods for trace-norm problems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
