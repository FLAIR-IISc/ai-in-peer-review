This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems, specifically focusing on the case where the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with h strongly convex on compact sets and Lipschitz continuous. The main claim is the establishment of linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the overall loss function f, a significant extension of prior work that typically relied on strong convexity. The authors generalize existing “error-bound” based linear convergence results (Tseng, Tseng & Yun, Zhang et al.) from lasso/group lasso to the trace norm.

The paper is technically sound and the proofs, while dense, are generally clear. The contribution lies in extending the known linear convergence results to a broader class of problems, namely those with a composite structure involving a linear operator and a strongly convex function. This is a non-trivial extension, though perhaps incremental. The presentation is good, and the inclusion of numerical experiments is a positive step.

**Strengths:**

*   **Linear Convergence Guarantee:** The primary strength is establishing linear convergence of the PGM for trace norm regularization under relatively weak assumptions. This provides a strong theoretical justification for using the PGM in practical applications.
*   **Extension of Existing Work:** The paper successfully generalizes previously known results from sparse regularization to trace norm regularization, addressing a gap in the literature.
*   **Clear Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, building upon the framework of Luo and Tseng, to derive the convergence result.
*   **Numerical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments, although limited, provides some empirical support for the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While technically sound, the paper feels largely like a technical extension of existing work rather than a breakthrough result. The core ideas are built heavily on previous findings, and the novelty lies in adapting those ideas to the trace norm setting.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope and don't fully demonstrate the robustness of the theoretical results across a diverse range of problem instances. A more comprehensive empirical evaluation would strengthen the paper. Specifically, the simulations are constrained to cases where linear convergence *is* observed; more evidence is needed on scenarios approaching the limits of the theory.
*   **Theoretical Assumptions vs. Empirical Observation:** The paper does not adequately discuss scenarios where the theoretical assumptions (e.g., strong convexity on compact sets) might fail, but empirical linear convergence is still observed. Exploring this disconnect would be valuable.
*   **ADMM Extension:** While requested, an outline of how the analysis might extend to ADMM is missing, hindering the potential for broader impact.

**Specific Points & Requests:**

*   **Tseng Citation:** The outlines of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely mirror Tseng’s work. The authors should more explicitly cite and differentiate their contribution from Tseng’s original paper (Tseng [20]). A clearer discussion of the differences and the specific adaptations made for the trace norm case is necessary.
*   **Constants κ1-κ4:** The dependencies of the constants κ1,…,κ4 on the choices of α̲ and ᾱ are unclear. Clarifying these dependencies is crucial for practical implementation and understanding the impact of step size selection.
*   **Inequality before (13):** The sequence of inequalities before equation (13) is confusing. The final inequality follows from convexity and -Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X||_* (see p.289 in [20]) rather than the intermediate step shown. This should be corrected for clarity.
*   **Simulation Discussion:** The added simulation demonstrating linear convergence is helpful, but the paper would benefit from a discussion of *when* the theory fails. For example, what happens as the stability level decreases? What conditions lead to a breakdown of linear convergence?
*   **ADMM Extension Outline:** The authors should provide a brief outline of how the analysis might be extended to the ADMM algorithm. This would enhance the practical relevance of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a technically sound and rigorous analysis.
*   It extends existing results on linear convergence to a new setting.
*   The numerical results provide some support for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and lacks significant novelty.
*   The empirical validation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the robustness of the theory.
*   The paper doesn't address potential limitations of the theory or explore extensions to other algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid technical contribution, but addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the empirical validation and discussion of limitations, would significantly enhance its impact and value. The clarifications requested regarding the citations and constants are also important for ensuring the accuracy and clarity of the paper.
