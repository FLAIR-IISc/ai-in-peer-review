### Summary

This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving optimization problems involving the sum of a smooth convex loss function and a trace norm regularizer. While PGM is known to have a sublinear O(1/k) convergence rate in this setting, the authors prove that the rate is in fact linear under certain assumptions on the loss function. Specifically, they require the loss to be of the composite form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function. Crucially, this result does not require the overall loss function `f` to be strongly convex, a condition that is often violated in important machine learning applications such as matrix completion with an undersampled operator `A`.

The key technical contribution enabling this result is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This error bound relates the distance from any point `X` to the optimal set with the norm of a residual map, `||R(X)||_F`, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such a bound has been an open question, as prior techniques developed for l1-norm and group LASSO regularization, which rely on the separability of variables, do not apply to the trace norm due to the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. The authors overcome this by using matrix perturbation theory to analyze the spectral structure of points near the optimal set. With this error bound in hand, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the well-established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10].

### Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and practical problem. Trace norm regularization is a cornerstone of low-rank matrix recovery methods used in collaborative filtering, multi-task learning, and system identification. The loss functions used in these domains (e.g., squared loss for matrix completion) are typically not strongly convex. This work provides the first linear convergence guarantee for the standard, widely-used PGM in this setting, significantly strengthening our theoretical understanding of its excellent empirical performance.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The primary contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm (Theorem 3.1), is novel and technically sound. It affirmatively answers an open question regarding the extension of the Luo-Tseng error bound analysis framework. The proof strategy is elegant, identifying that an optimal solution `X*` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share common singular vectors (Proposition 4.2) and leveraging this structural property to control the behavior of iterates near the optimal set. This is a clever departure from the variable-splitting techniques used for separable regularizers like the l1-norm. The overall proof appears correct and complete.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides a clear motivation, situates the work within the existing literature, states its main contributions upfront, and presents a logical and readable proof outline. The numerical experiments, while simple, effectively corroborate the theoretical findings.

### Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)

1.  **Comparison with l1-norm Regularization:** The paper's novelty would be more apparent with a more explicit comparison to the analogous results for l1-norm regularization (e.g., [22, 23]). The introduction correctly notes that l1-based proof techniques fail for the trace norm. However, this crucial point could be elaborated. The authors could add a short discussion explaining precisely *why* the non-separability of singular values breaks the existing analysis and how their SVD-based approach circumvents this. This would help the reader better appreciate the technical challenge and the innovation of the proposed solution.

2.  **Novelty in Context:** Following the point above, it would be helpful to explicitly state that the l1-norm error bound is a special case of the trace norm bound (when applied to diagonal matrices). Framing the result as a non-trivial generalization of the vector case would better position the paper's contribution.

3.  **Intuition for Technical Lemmas:** The proof of the main error bound relies heavily on Lemma 4.1, particularly the "Restricted Invertibility" property. While the proof is provided, the paper would benefit from more high-level intuition about this lemma. A brief paragraph explaining why this property is central, how it relates to similar concepts in compressed sensing (like the RIP), and how the proof strategy (arguing by contradiction on the properties of the limiting direction `Q̄`) establishes it would greatly enhance the paper's accessibility and impact.

### Minor Issues

*   **Title:** The title is clear, but if the authors are considering alternatives, one that highlights the composite structure of the loss function (e.g., "...for Composite Loss Functions") might be more descriptive of the key assumption enabling the result.
*   **Definitions:** For reader convenience, it would be helpful to briefly recall the definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence in the main text, rather than just citing them. (The paper does this, but it could be made slightly more self-contained).
*   **Residual Map:** To better connect with prior work, the authors could briefly mention how their residual map `R(X)` in Eq. (7) relates to the general proximal residual defined in the Luo-Tseng framework [10].
*   **Experiments:** In the matrix completion experiment, please clarify if the sampling operator `P` corresponds to a single random mask fixed for all iterations, or if it is related to a random sampling process.
*   **Reference:** There appears to be a broken or incorrect reference in the manuscript (reviewer note mentioned line 337). Please double-check all citations.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents a significant theoretical result for a widely-used optimization model in machine learning. The technical contributions are novel and the paper is clearly written. Addressing the suggestions above, particularly by strengthening the comparison to the l1-norm case, would further improve an already excellent paper. I recommend acceptance.
