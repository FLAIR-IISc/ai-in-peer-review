This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems. This class of problems is of significant interest in machine learning, with applications in matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. While PGM is known to have a sublinear O(1/k^2) convergence rate for general convex smooth loss functions and a linear rate for strongly convex ones, many practical loss functions (e.g., the square loss in matrix completion) are not strongly convex. This paper aims to bridge this gap by establishing a linear convergence rate for PGM without assuming strong convexity.

The main contribution is to show that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where h is strictly convex and smooth, PGM converges with an asymptotic linear rate. The authors achieve this by following the well-established analytical framework of Luo and Tseng (1993), which connects linear convergence to the existence of a local Lipschitzian error bound. The core technical novelty of the paper lies in proving such an error bound for the trace norm regularizer. The proof leverages a key structural property of the optimal solution set: an optimal solution X* and the corresponding gradient component -∇f(X*) can be simultaneously diagonalized by the same singular vectors. This insight allows the authors to analyze the spectral properties of iterates near the optimal set and establish the required error bound, which states that the distance to the optimal set is locally bounded by the norm of the proximal residual map.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a relevant and open theoretical question: under what weaker-than-strong-convexity conditions does PGM converge linearly for trace norm regularization? Proving a linear rate for this important class of problems is a valuable contribution to the optimization and machine learning literature.
2.  **Novel Proof Technique:** The establishment of the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm is non-trivial. Unlike the l1-norm, the trace norm is not polyhedral and its structure is more complex. The authors' observation about the simultaneous singular value decomposition (Proposition 4.2) is a clever and elegant tool that effectively handles this complexity, forming the technical heart of the paper.
3.  **Soundness:** The overall theoretical development appears to be correct and follows a logical progression from the error bound to the final linear convergence result.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Originality:** The paper's originality is moderate. While the proof of the error bound is novel, the overall approach of using the Luo and Tseng framework to establish linear convergence is a standard technique that has been applied to other problems (e.g., LASSO, group LASSO). Furthermore, some of the intermediate results appear in prior work. For instance, the inequality ||R(X^k)||_F ≤ κ3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F (Equation 11) is central to the final convergence proof. A similar result was established in Lemma 4 of "A new perspective on fast-gradient methods" by Suvrit Sra (NIPS 2012), which should be cited and discussed. The main novelty is thus confined to the specific derivation of the error bound for the trace norm setting.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is generally readable, but its structure could be improved to better highlight the main contributions. The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is highly technical and occupies a significant portion of the main text. A more reader-friendly organization would be to first state the main linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) and sketch its proof, assuming the error bound holds. This would immediately show the reader the main result and how the pieces fit together. The detailed and involved proof of the error bound could then follow in a separate section or be deferred to the supplementary material.
3.  **Significance:** The significance of the result is primarily theoretical. The linear convergence is asymptotic, meaning it is only guaranteed to occur once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal solution set. In practice, an algorithm might spend a substantial number of iterations in a sublinear convergence phase before this linear rate kicks in. For practitioners who may terminate the algorithm early based on a loose tolerance, the benefit of this asymptotic linear rate might not be realized. The paper provides an analysis of an existing algorithm rather than proposing a new, more efficient one, which limits its direct algorithmic impact.
4.  **Technical Gap:** There is a minor gap in the proof of Theorem 3.2. The authors state that inequality (11), ||R(X^k)||_F ≤ κ3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F, can be shown to hold. However, the conditions under which this holds (e.g., related to the step size αk) are not explicitly derived. As noted above, related results exist but typically require specific conditions on the step size (e.g., α < 1 in Sra, 2012). A more detailed justification in the supplementary material would strengthen the paper's technical completeness.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper provides a new, non-trivial theoretical result on the linear convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization, extending analysis beyond the standard strong convexity assumption. The proof of the key error bound is novel and technically interesting.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The contribution is somewhat incremental, as it applies a known analytical framework to a new problem class. The practical significance is limited by the asymptotic nature of the result. There are also minor issues with citation of related work and a small gap in the proof derivation that should be addressed.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical paper with a moderately interesting contribution. While the practical impact may be limited, the new error bound and the associated proof technique are valuable for the optimization theory community.
