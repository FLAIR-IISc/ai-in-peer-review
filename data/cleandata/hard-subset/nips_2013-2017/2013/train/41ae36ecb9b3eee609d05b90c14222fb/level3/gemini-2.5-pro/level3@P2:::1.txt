This paper establishes the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace norm regularized optimization problems. The specific problem class is `min f(X) + τ||X||*`, where the loss function `f(X)` has the structure `h(A(X))`. Here, `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a smooth function that is strongly convex on any compact subset of its domain. Crucially, the overall loss function `f(X)` is not required to be strongly convex, which is a significant relaxation of the standard conditions for linear convergence. This result is applicable to many important problems in machine learning, such as matrix completion and multi-task learning. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance from a point to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map measuring the violation of the optimality conditions. This result extends the powerful error-bound-based convergence analysis framework, developed by Luo and Tseng [10] and previously applied to l1-norm and group LASSO problems [22, 23, 25], to the non-polyhedral, non-separable trace norm case.

### Strengths

1.  **Important Theoretical Result:** The paper provides the first proof of linear convergence for the standard PGM on this class of trace norm problems without assuming strong convexity of the loss function. This fills a significant gap in the literature, as many practical problems (e.g., matrix completion where the measurement operator `A` has a non-trivial nullspace) fall into this category. It formalizes the observation that PGM is often fast in practice even when standard theory only guarantees sublinear rates.

2.  **Technical Novelty:** The main technical hurdle is deriving a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, which is non-trivial due to the non-separable and non-polyhedral nature of the norm. The authors overcome this with a clever analysis based on matrix perturbation theory. The key insight, encapsulated in Proposition 4.2, is that an optimal solution `X*` and the corresponding gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This structural property allows the authors to analyze the spectral properties of points near the optimal set and ultimately establish the error bound.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clearly laid out, the main results are stated upfront, and the proof structure is explained before diving into technical details. The authors do a good job of outlining how their proof strategy builds upon, yet significantly departs from, prior work on l1-norm regularization.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Contextualization and Originality:** While the technical derivation of the error bound is novel, the overarching framework for using this bound to prove linear convergence (Theorem 3.2) closely follows the analysis of Luo and Tseng [10, 22]. The paper would be strengthened by more explicitly delineating which parts of the proof are a direct application of this existing framework and which required fundamentally new ideas. For example, the statement "The structure of our proof of Theorem 3.1 largely follows that laid out in [22, Section 6]" is helpful, but a more detailed comparison highlighting the specific challenges posed by the trace norm would better emphasize the paper's unique contributions.

2.  **Clarity in Proofs:** There are a few minor points in the proofs that could be clarified:
    *   In the proof sketch of Theorem 3.2, the dependence of the constants κ1, κ2, κ3 on the step size bounds `α` and `ᾱ` should be made explicit to make the result more self-contained and transparent.
    *   The derivation leading to inequality (18) can be made more direct. The inequality `τ||X̄k||* ≤ 〈Ḡ,X k +Rk − X̄k〉+ τ‖Xk +Rk‖*` follows directly from the subgradient inequality `-Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄k‖*` and the convexity of the trace norm. Citing a standard reference for this step (e.g., Tseng [22], p. 289) might streamline the argument.

3.  **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments confirm the paper's theoretical predictions but could be more insightful. The main theoretical achievement is proving linear convergence when `f` is *not* strongly convex. A more compelling experiment would be to design a problem instance (e.g., a matrix completion problem with a highly under-determined operator `A`) where strong convexity clearly fails, and show that PGM still converges linearly. This would provide a more direct and powerful validation of the theory's practical relevance.

### Questions and Further Suggestions

*   It would be valuable to include a discussion on the boundaries of the established theory. For instance, are there practical scenarios where the assumptions of Theorem 3.1 are violated, but linear convergence is still observed? An experiment that explores this, perhaps by tuning problem parameters to different "stability levels," could provide significant insight.
*   Given the prominence of other first-order methods, a discussion on whether this analysis could be extended to algorithms like ADMM would be a welcome addition and would broaden the paper's impact.

### Summary of Arguments

*   **Arguments for acceptance:**
    *   Provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM on a broad and important class of trace norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity.
    *   The core technical contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, is novel and non-trivial.
    *   The paper is well-written, and the result is significant for the machine learning and optimization communities.

*   **Arguments against acceptance:**
    *   The contribution could be perceived as a technical, albeit challenging, extension of an existing analysis framework to a new setting.
    *   The empirical evaluation, while supportive, could be designed to more forcefully demonstrate the practical implications of the main theoretical claim (i.e., linear convergence in the absence of strong convexity).

Overall, this is a strong paper with a valuable theoretical contribution. The technical novelty in deriving the error bound for the trace norm is significant, and the result is of high interest. The weaknesses are minor and can be addressed. I recommend acceptance.
