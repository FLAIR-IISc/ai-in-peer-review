- Main contribution: Proves linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss.  
- Significance: Addresses important settings (e.g., matrix completion) where loss functions are not strongly convex.  
- Writing quality: Exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making technical details accessible.  
- Proof correctness: Proof appears correct, extending Tseng’s Lipschitzian error bound technique to the non-trivial nuclear (trace) norm case.  
- Novelty: Resolves an open question by successfully generalizing Tseng’s approach to the trace norm.  
- Context and motivation: Effectively motivated and well-situated within low-rank recovery literature, with solid connections to prior work [10,22,23,25].  
- Empirical support: Numerical experiments for matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice.  
- l1-norm comparison needed: Must provide a clear comparison to vector l1-norm results and explain why l1 techniques don’t directly translate to the trace norm.  
- l1 as corollary: Authors should state that the l1 result should follow as a corollary and explain exactly how the proof generalizes or what modifications are needed.  
- Emphasize non-triviality: More strongly articulate why the extension isn’t a trivial analogue despite superficial similarities.  
- Title suggestion: Remove “structured”; prefer a title emphasizing convergence under weaker assumptions on the loss/operator A.  
- Clarify operator P: State explicitly whether P is a sampling/mask operator to remove ambiguity.  
- Fix broken reference: Correct the broken reference on line 337.  
- Definitions: Add brief recalls of Q- and R-linear convergence for readers unfamiliar with them.  
- Connect residuals: Relate the residual R(X) in (6) to Tseng’s original definition (46) to improve intuition and linkage to foundational work.  
- Justify Lemma 3.2: Provide more discussion on why Lemma 3.2 is necessary, what challenges it overcomes, and its role in the overall proof.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Strong theoretical result, clear presentation, novel extension of Tseng’s technique, practical relevance, and supportive experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient elaboration on the l1-norm connection and several minor technical issues that need fixing.  
- Recommendation: Strong paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the l1 comparison and listed technical points.
