

This paper addresses an important question in optimization for machine learning: whether the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems (min_X f(X) + τ‖X‖_*) can achieve linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function f. Previous work established linear convergence only when f is strongly convex [16], but in many applications (e.g., matrix completion), f is not strongly convex. The authors show that for f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h strictly convex with certain smoothness properties, PGM achieves linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions on f. Their key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, which resolves an open question about applying Tseng's error bound framework [10] to nuclear norm regularization. This extends previous results for ℓ1-norm [22,23,25] where the polyhedral epigraph structure enabled simpler analysis, but nuclear norm lacks this structure.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The main proof is carefully constructed, with Lemma 4.1 establishing the crucial restricted invertibility property needed for the error bound. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification demonstrate linear convergence in practice, matching the theoretical results. The paper properly situates itself within the literature, citing relevant work on trace-norm regularization [6,9,20] and error bounds [10,22,25]. The authors correctly note that their result does not require the strong convexity of f, which is significant because many common loss functions (square loss, logistic loss) are not strongly convex in matrix completion and classification tasks.

The paper's main contribution is the Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, which is of independent interest. However, I have some concerns regarding novelty and presentation. The authors should clarify why the l1-norm result (e.g., [22]) does not directly extend to trace norm, as the nuclear norm is the matrix analog of ℓ1-norm. While the paper mentions the non-polyhedral epigraph of nuclear norm [18], it should explicitly discuss why the standard splitting approach for ℓ1-norm (which works because variables from different groups don't interfere) fails for nuclear norm. The authors should also clarify that the l1-norm case should follow as a special case of their result (when m=1 or n=1), which would strengthen the novelty claim.

The title "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization" is misleading as it suggests the contribution is about the regularization structure, whereas the key novelty is about the loss function structure (the linear operator A). I recommend changing the title to something like "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace-Norm Regularized Problems with Structured Loss Functions" to better reflect the contribution.

Minor issues that should be addressed: 
1. The paper uses "P" as a sampling operator (line 200) without clarifying whether it's a fixed mask operator or a random sampling operator. In matrix completion, P is typically a fixed mask, not random, so this should be clarified.
2. Reference [20] is broken in the text (line 337).
3. The paper should briefly define Q- and R-linear convergence (standard terms in optimization) for readers unfamiliar with the distinction.
4. The residual map R(X) in (7) should be explicitly related to Tseng's original definition (46) in [10] for better interpretation.
5. Section 3 should add more discussion justifying why Lemma 3.2 (the error bound) is the key insight, rather than just stating it.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The result is significant because it establishes linear convergence for a wider class of loss functions than previously known, which has immediate practical implications for matrix completion and other applications. The technical contribution of the error bound is novel and well-justified. The paper meets the quality criterion with sound theoretical results and empirical validation. It is clearly written and well-organized, meeting the clarity criterion. The novelty is clear relative to previous work on error bounds for structured convex optimization, though the relationship to the ℓ1-norm case needs further clarification to fully establish novelty.

Pro acceptance: Significant theoretical contribution resolving an open question; important for practical applications; well-written with sound proofs; strong numerical validation.
Con rejection: Title misrepresents contribution; need to better clarify novelty relative to ℓ1-norm case; minor technical issues in presentation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the title, the comparison to ℓ1-norm, and the minor presentation issues noted above. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with these minor clarifications, it will be an important reference for future work on optimization for low-rank matrix problems.
