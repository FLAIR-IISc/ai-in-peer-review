## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper presents a compelling result establishing the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems under relatively weak assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a significant contribution, as many practical machine learning applications involve loss functions that lack strong convexity (e.g., matrix completion). The problem addressed is important and frequently encountered in areas like low-rank matrix recovery, collaborative filtering, and dimensionality reduction. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors carefully lay out the problem, assumptions, and theoretical framework, making the core ideas accessible. The proof appears correct, building upon Tseng’s Lipschitzian error bound technique [20] and successfully extending it to the nuclear (trace) norm setting, addressing a noted open question. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of related work and correctly position their contribution within the existing literature. 

However, a more direct comparison with the vector l1-norm case (as highlighted in [20, 22]) would strengthen the paper. While the authors explain the difficulties in directly translating l1 techniques due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm, elaborating on *why* the splitting approach used for l1-norm regularization isn't applicable to the trace norm (beyond the singular value dependency) would be beneficial.  Specifically, discussing how the inherent interdependence of matrix entries impacts the error bound derivation would be insightful.  The result for the l1-norm *should* follow as a corollary, and explicitly stating this, along with a brief discussion of the novelty (or lack thereof) of extending the result, would be valuable.

The title could be improved. While “structured” is a common term, the main contribution lies in the properties of the loss function and the linear operator A, rather than the trace norm regularizer itself. A title like “Linear Convergence of PGM for Trace Norm Regularization with Non-Strongly Convex Loss” would be more precise.

Minor points: The paper refers to operator P at line 337, but it’s unclear whether this is a sampling operator (implying randomness) or a fixed mask. Clarification is needed. Also, reference [337] is broken.  The paper would benefit from briefly recalling the definitions of Q- and R-linear convergence for reader clarity. Finally, explicitly relating the residual R(X) defined in (6) to Tseng’s original definition (46) would aid interpretation. Expanding the discussion/conclusions regarding the technical Lemma 3.2 would also justify its importance and highlight the novel aspects of its proof.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant theoretical result with practical implications.
*   Well-written, clear, and technically sound.
*   Addresses an important open problem.
*   Strong connection to existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Missing a more in-depth comparison to the l1-norm case.
*   Minor issues with clarity/precision in the title and notation.



**Overall Assessment:**  Strong Accept. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the theory of proximal algorithms and is of high quality. Addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen an already excellent manuscript.
