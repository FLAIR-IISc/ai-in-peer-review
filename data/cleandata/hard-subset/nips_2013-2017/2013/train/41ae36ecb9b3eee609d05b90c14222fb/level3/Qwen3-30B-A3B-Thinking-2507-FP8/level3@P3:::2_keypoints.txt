- Main contribution: Proves linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss.
- Key technical result: Establishes a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, resolving an open question about applying Tseng's error-bound technique to nuclear-norm problems.
- Methodological advance: Extends Tseng's framework to handle the trace norm's non-polyhedral epigraph by exploiting shared singular vectors between solutions and gradients.
- Assumptions: Shows linear convergence when f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, covering common losses used in practice.
- Empirical support: Numerical experiments (matrix completion and classification) demonstrate linear convergence consistent with theory.
- Quality: Proofs are carefully constructed and technically sound, particularly Theorem 3.1; limitations are acknowledged and justified.
- Clarity issues to fix: Relate residual map R(X) to Tseng's original definition, recall Q- vs R-linear convergence, and clarify notation "P" in experiments.
- Originality: Novel extension of existing error-bound techniques to the trace-norm case; nontrivial due to non-polyhedral structure.
- Missing discussion: Paper does not clearly relate its trace-norm results to established ℓ1-norm results or show ℓ1 as a corollary.
- Significance: Results apply to many ML problems (matrix completion, multi-task learning) and to common losses (square, logistic) without strong convexity.
- Title suggestion: Remove "structured" since the main contribution concerns the loss/operator side rather than the regularizer.
- Technical suggestion: Explain why ℓ1-norm techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm, emphasizing epigraph differences.
- Expository suggestion: Add intuition and necessity for the proof of Lemma 3.2 to aid reader understanding.
- Minor fixes: Correct the broken reference at line 337 and clarify whether "P" is a sampling or fixed mask operator in experiments.
- Strengths: Resolves an important open theoretical question with rigorous proofs and supportive experiments; writing is clear and organized.
- Weaknesses: Insufficient discussion of connections to ℓ1-norm literature, a slightly imprecise title, and minor technical/notation ambiguities.
- Acceptance case: Significant theoretical contribution with practical implications and nontrivial technical work warrants acceptance.
- Rejection caveat: Novelty is an extension of existing techniques and the relation to ℓ1 results could be better articulated, though this is not decisive.
- Recommendation: With minor revisions addressing clarity and related-work connections, the paper is recommended for acceptance.
