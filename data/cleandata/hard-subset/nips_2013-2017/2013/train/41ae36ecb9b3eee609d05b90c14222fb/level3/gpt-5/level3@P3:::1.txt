Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies proximal gradient (PG) for trace-norm regularized problems min_X f(X)+τ||X||_* with f(X)=h(A(X)), A linear, and h C1 with Lipschitz gradient and (importantly) strongly convex on any compact set of its domain. The main technical result is a local Lipschitzian error bound that upper-bounds the distance to the optimal set by the fixed-point residual of the PG optimality map. Leveraging Luo–Tseng’s framework, the paper then establishes Q-linear convergence of the objective values and R-linear convergence of the iterates for PG with fixed stepsizes αk∈(α̲,ᾱ)<1/Lf. The analysis extends error-bound-based linear-rate results that were previously available for lasso, group lasso, or polyhedral regularizers (Tseng; Tseng & Yun; Zhang et al.) to the non-polyhedral trace norm. The key novel ingredient is a spectral alignment property at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors), enabling a restricted invertibility bound via matrix perturbation arguments.

Strengths
- Establishes linear convergence of PG for a broad class of smooth composite losses with trace-norm regularization without requiring global strong convexity of f in X. This is theoretically significant and practically relevant (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A).
- The error bound for the trace norm is novel and of independent interest; the spectral alignment plus perturbation analysis is a nontrivial extension beyond polyhedral settings.
- Proofs are, for the most part, clear and carefully organized; the connection to Luo–Tseng’s framework is well-executed.

Weaknesses
- The contribution is primarily a technical extension of an established error-bound framework; the overall conceptual novelty is moderate.
- Empirical validation is limited; more targeted numerical evidence illustrating the error-bound regime and linear slopes under different “stability” conditions would strengthen the paper.
- Some dependencies of constants and connections to prior proofs (Tseng; Tseng & Yun) are not fully spelled out.

Quality
Technically sound. The main claims follow from the stated assumptions. The new error bound appears correct and the linear-rate argument is standard once the bound holds. A few proof steps need clarification (see below), and the dependence of constants on α̲,ᾱ and Lf should be made explicit.

Clarity
Overall clear. Notation is consistent; the spectral arguments are well-motivated. Minor places are confusing (see “Minor technical issue”).

Originality
Moderate. The core novelty is extending error-bound analyses to a non-polyhedral regularizer via a spectral alignment lemma and perturbation tools. The linear-rate conclusion for PG itself follows known templates.

Significance
Useful to the community working on low-rank learning and first-order methods. The result can inform step-size policies and algorithm selection in practice and may serve as a building block for analyzing related algorithms (e.g., ADMM).

Detailed comments and requests
- Numerical example request: Please add a simple synthetic experiment where f is not strongly convex in X (e.g., matrix completion with square loss and a highly rank-deficient sampling operator A) to demonstrate linear convergence across a range of “stability levels.” Two concrete suggestions:
  1) Vary sampling ratio θ to control restricted strong convexity/RSC on the tangent space; show semilog plots of F(Xk)−F* and ||Xk−Xk+1||F for several θ, with fixed stepsizes αk in (α̲,ᾱ).
  2) Create cases with different spectral gaps at the solution (e.g., vary singular values of X*) to illustrate how the slope depends on the local error-bound modulus. Please include semilog objective-vs-iterations plots and report the estimated linear rate.
- When does the theory fail but linear PG is observed? Please discuss:
  - Non-smooth or non-C1 h (e.g., hinge, absolute loss) where Lipschitz gradient fails, or h not strongly convex on compacts (piecewise-linear losses), invalidating the key inequality (15). Practitioners may still observe linear slopes due to local polyhedrality or problem structure, but the current proof does not apply.
  - Degenerate spectra at the threshold (multiple singular values equal τ at X̄−Ḡ) can make the spectral alignment non-unique and may deteriorate the restricted invertibility constant, causing the EB modulus to blow up. Empirically, PG can exhibit linear behavior until iterates enter a degenerate neighborhood, after which the rate becomes less predictable.
  - Unbounded level sets or stepsize choices that break monotonicity can violate the compactness needed to secure a uniform strong convexity parameter on the relevant set.
  Please articulate these failure modes and relate them to the constants in the EB.
- Extension to ADMM: It would be valuable to outline how your error bound (metric subregularity of the KKT/residual map) can be used to prove local linear convergence of ADMM for the same class. A standard route is: show calmness/EB of the KKT mapping for the split trace-norm problem; combine with contractive properties of ADMM updates to obtain a linear rate. Please discuss which parts carry over verbatim (e.g., your EB for the nonsmooth part) and what additional assumptions on penalty parameters or full-row-rank constraints are needed. Citing early linear-rate ADMM under EB/RSC-type conditions (e.g., works by Deng & Yin; Hong & Luo; He & Yuan) would help situate the extension.
- Dependencies of κ1,…,κ4 on α̲,ᾱ:
  - κ1: From the standard PG descent, one can take κ1 ≥ 1/(2ᾱ) − Lf/2 (for fixed stepsizes with αk≤ᾱ<1/Lf).
  - κ3: Since R(Xk)=Sτ(Xk−∇f(Xk))−Xk while the iterate displacement is Xk+1−Xk=Sταk(Xk−αk∇f(Xk))−Xk, one can bound ||R(Xk)||F by a Lipschitz-in-α scaling of ||Xk+1−Xk||F; an explicit bound of the form κ3 ≤ 1 + ᾱLf (or a small variation) should be provable. Please provide the exact derivation you use.
  - κ2 and κ4: These depend on the EB modulus η and the local Lipschitz constants of ∇h on the compact set Z, hence ultimately on Lf, α̲, ᾱ, and the level-set radius. Please make these dependencies explicit (constants and radii), as they are important for interpreting observed slopes.
- Proof overlap/citation: The structure of the Theorem 3.1 → Theorem 3.2 argument and parts of the inequality chaining closely mirror Tseng’s error-bound analysis (Tseng [20]; Tseng & Yun; Zhang et al.). Please (i) cite the exact lemmas/steps you adapt (e.g., how you specialize Luo–Tseng’s Theorem to your EB), and (ii) sharply delineate your new components (Prop. 4.2 spectral alignment; the non-polyhedral EB proof).
- Minor technical issue: The sequence of inequalities leading to (18) is confusing. The final inequality follows directly from convexity and −Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X̄k||_* (cf. Tseng [20], p.289). Please simplify this derivation or replace it with the standard subgradient inequality to avoid suggesting a stronger intermediate step than needed.
- Exposition/notation:
  - Around (7) vs algorithm (5), clarify the mismatch between the residual map using α=1 and the algorithm using αk∈(α̲,ᾱ). Explicitly show how you relate ||R(Xk)||F to ||Xk+1−Xk||F.
  - In Section 2.2, the compactness of X is crucial; briefly recap why A(X) is constant over X and why that implies boundedness when combined with the level-set description.

Pros
- First linear-rate result for PG with trace-norm regularization without global strong convexity of f.
- New, nontrivial error bound for a non-polyhedral regularizer using spectral techniques.
- Clear, modular analysis and useful practical implications.

Cons
- Primarily a technical extension of known EB-based linear-rate machinery.
- Limited empirical evidence tied to the theory; missing experiments probing EB/stability.
- Some constants and proof dependencies could be made more transparent; overlap with prior proofs should be better acknowledged.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The technical advance (error bound for trace norm) is meaningful and will likely be used by others. Strengthen the paper by adding the requested semilog experiments under varying stability levels, clarifying constants and proof lineage, discussing failure modes where linear behavior is still seen empirically, and outlining an ADMM extension under the same EB.
