- Main claim: Proximal gradient method (PGM) has linear convergence for trace-norm regularized problems f(X)=h(A(X))+τ||X||* when h is smooth and strongly convex on compacts, without requiring global strong convexity of f.  
- Significance: Result is important for practical problems (e.g., matrix completion) where A is not injective and f is not strongly convex.  
- Technical approach: Establishes a new Lipschitzian error bound linking distance to the optimal set with the norm of a residual map.  
- Novelty: Extends error-bound techniques from ℓ1/group-lasso to the trace norm using matrix perturbation theory to handle spectral dependence.  
- Strength — theoretical impact: Provides a stronger theoretical foundation for using PGM on trace-norm regularized problems under realistic assumptions.  
- Strength — technical contribution: Nontrivial adaptation of existing frameworks with novel spectral perturbation arguments.  
- Strength — exposition: Paper is well-written, proofs mostly clear, and numerical experiments corroborate theory (including semi-log plots).  
- Weakness — originality: Work is largely a technical extension of established error-bound-based analyses, so conceptual novelty is limited.  
- Weakness — experiments: Empirical evaluation is limited and does not probe the boundaries or necessity of the theoretical assumptions.  
- Suggestion — attribution: Explicitly state that the high-level proof strategy is adapted from Tseng [22] and delineate which steps are novel for the trace norm.  
- Suggestion — constants: Clarify how convergence constants (κ1…κ4) depend on step-size bounds α̲ and ᾱ, either in text or an appendix.  
- Suggestion — proof clarity: Simplify the inequality before (18) by invoking the subgradient optimality condition directly to improve readability.  
- Suggestion — explore limits: Add simulations where h is not strongly convex (e.g., ||z||^p with 1<p<2) to test when linear convergence still occurs and assess tightness of bounds.  
- Suggestion — broader impact: Discuss how the established error bound could extend analysis to other algorithms (e.g., ADMM) to broaden the paper’s significance.
