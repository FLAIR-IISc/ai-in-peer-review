Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proves that the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) attains an asymptotic linear convergence rate for trace-norm regularized problems minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is smooth, strictly convex on compact sets, and has Lipschitz gradient. Crucially, the result removes the usual strong-convexity assumption on f. The analysis hinges on establishing a Lipschitzian error bound (LEB) that relates the distance to the optimal set to the proximal residual ||Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X||F. With this LEB, the Luo–Tseng framework yields Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates. The proof develops new spectral-structure arguments showing that, at optimality, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, enabling a perturbation analysis that overcomes the non-polyhedral nature of the nuclear norm.

This extends Tseng’s LEB program from polyhedral regularizers (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) to the nuclear norm, addressing an open question raised in, e.g., Tseng [20] and subsequent work [22, 25]. The result has direct implications for ML and signal processing problems such as matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification, and trace regression. The paper is clearly written, careful, and, to the best of my reading, the main proof is correct.

Strengths
- Important result: linear convergence of a widely used first-order method for a broad class of nuclear-norm problems without strong convexity of f.
- Technical novelty: a new LEB for the trace-norm case via spectral alignment and perturbation arguments; answers an open question about extending Tseng’s technique to the nuclear norm.
- Generality: covers common losses (square, logistic) and arbitrary linear operators A (not necessarily injective), which captures practical ML sensing/design matrices.
- Clear exposition: the algorithmic setup, residual mapping, and proof structure are well organized; numerical illustrations, though modest, are consistent with the theory.

Weaknesses and requests for clarification/improvement
- Analogy to ℓ1 case and why ℓ1 techniques do not carry over:
  • Please add a dedicated subsection comparing your argument to the ℓ1 and group LASSO analyses (e.g., Tseng [20], Tseng–Yun [22]). Make explicit which steps rely on polyhedrality and separability in the ℓ1 case and how your spectral alignment substitutes for group-wise decoupling.
  • It would be helpful to include an intuitive figure/table contrasting “coordinate splitting” (ℓ1) vs “spectral coupling” (trace norm).
- ℓ1 as a corollary:
  • Since ℓ1 is a special case of the nuclear norm (e.g., vector problems embedded as diagonal matrices, or via an equivalent separable regularizer when A and h act componentwise), the ℓ1 LEB and linear convergence should follow as a corollary of your theorem. Please state this corollary explicitly and explain any conditions needed for the reduction.
  • Discuss the novelty versus expected extension: while practitioners might anticipate an extension, your proof resolves the non-polyhedral barrier; emphasize precisely where the additional work lies.
- Residual mapping and connection to Tseng:
  • Clarify that R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X is Tseng’s residual with stepsize α=1 and g(X)=τ||X||*. If using αk≠1 in practice, define Rα(X)=Sτα(X−α∇f(X))−X and state the simple equivalence and constants used in the LEB and convergence proofs.
  • Explicitly relate your R(·) to Tseng’s definition (his Eq. (46)), aiding readers to map constants/assumptions between the two analyses.
- Q- and R-linear convergence:
  • Briefly recall definitions inline when first used: Q-linear (F(Xk+1)−v*) ≤ ρ(F(Xk)−v*), ρ∈(0,1); R-linear dist(Xk,X*) ≤ Cρk. You do provide definitions; consider moving them earlier or restating near Theorem 3.2 for reader convenience.
- Assumptions on A and “structured” terminology:
  • The main new contribution hinges on f through A and h (loss-side structure) rather than on additional structure in the nuclear norm itself. If your title or abstract uses “structured,” consider revising, e.g., “Linear Convergence of Proximal Gradient for Trace-Norm Regularization without Strong Convexity.”
- Interpretation of the LEB:
  • Add a short discussion connecting your LEB to quadratic growth/restricted strong convexity surrogates, and to the Luo–Tseng EB condition, to better position the result among modern EB/PL conditions.
- Lemma 3.2 (Section 3) justification and intuition:
  • Provide more intuition and a brief proof sketch for the technical lemma (currently Theorem 3.2 in the draft): how (9)–(11), Theorem 3.1, and the monotonicity combine to yield Q-linear decrease of values and R-linear convergence of iterates; clarify dependence of constants on α, ᾱ, Lf.
- Minor issues:
  • Clarify P as sampling vs mask in Section 5: please state that P is the fixed masking operator induced by a randomly drawn set of entries; randomness is only in data generation, not during optimization.
  • Fix the broken reference at line 337.
  • Relate R(X) in (7) to Tseng’s residual (46) explicitly, including stepsize dependence, as noted above.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main arguments (Proposition 4.2, the restricted invertibility Lemma 4.1(c), and the contradiction setup) appear correct. The reliance on spectral alignment and perturbation is convincing and addresses the non-polyhedral difficulty.
- Empirics: Limited in scope but consistent; the semilog plots show apparent linear regimes near the solution.

Clarity
- Generally excellent. The proof roadmap and notation are careful. The requested additions (ℓ1 comparison, residual mapping alignment, brief intuition for the main convergence lemma) would make the paper even more accessible.

Originality
- Strong. Extends Tseng’s LEB technique to a non-polyhedral regularizer via new spectral arguments; addresses a known gap between ℓ1/group LASSO and trace norm.

Significance
- High. Linear-rate guarantees for popular nuclear-norm problems without strong convexity should influence both theory and practice in ML/signal processing.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline strong). The contribution is solid; the requested clarifications are straightforward.

Pros
- First linear-rate result for PGM on trace-norm problems without strong convexity.
- Resolves an open question about applying LEBs to the nuclear norm.
- Clear, careful proofs; broadly applicable assumptions.

Cons
- Comparison to ℓ1/group LASSO can be made more explicit.
- Limited empirical scope.
- Minor presentation issues (residual mapping, notation, references) to fix.
