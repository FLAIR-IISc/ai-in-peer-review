

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f is a convex smooth loss function. The authors establish linear convergence for a broad class of loss functions f(X) = h(A(X)) where h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties, without requiring strong convexity of f. This extends previous results that typically required strong convexity for linear convergence rates. The main technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which the authors prove using a key observation about the simultaneous singular value decomposition of optimal solutions and their gradients.

The paper builds upon the well-established framework of Luo and Tseng (1992) for analyzing convergence rates of proximal methods. It extends their approach to the trace norm case, where the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph had previously prevented such error bounds. The authors cleverly overcome this by observing that optimal solutions X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors, enabling them to use matrix perturbation theory to establish the required error bound. This technical insight appears novel and is of independent interest beyond the specific convergence result.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound overall, with a correct proof of the main error bound and convergence result. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification support the theoretical findings by demonstrating linear convergence in practice. However, I identified a gap in the argument for the inequality ‖R(X^k)‖_F ≤ κ_3‖X^k - X^{k+1}‖_F (line 085 of the appendix). The justification for choosing κ_3 (particularly the dependence on α < 1) is not sufficiently detailed, which slightly weakens the completeness of the proof.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though it could be improved by stating the main contributions more prominently at the beginning. The technical details of the error bound proof are quite dense, and it would be helpful to present the key intuition before diving into the formal arguments. The authors could also clarify the relationship between their work and Sra's NIPS 2012 paper (Lemma 4), which already established a similar inequality for the residual map.

**Originality:** The paper makes a moderate original contribution. The simultaneous singular value decomposition observation is novel and clever, but the overall approach (using error bounds to establish linear convergence) follows the standard framework of Luo and Tseng. The authors appropriately cite related work, but the novelty is somewhat limited to the specific technical details of applying this framework to trace norm regularization.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is interesting but primarily of academic significance. The asymptotic nature of the linear convergence (which only applies when iterates are close to the optimal set) limits the immediate practical impact. While the result applies to important machine learning problems like matrix completion, the paper does not provide new algorithmic insights or practical improvements beyond confirming the theoretical convergence rate.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance:**
- *For acceptance:* The paper makes a technically sound contribution to understanding convergence rates of first-order methods for trace norm regularization. The error bound is novel and of independent interest, and the proof technique is clever. The numerical results support the theoretical claims.
- *Against acceptance:* The contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The significance is limited to theoretical understanding, with minimal algorithmic or practical implications. The paper could be clearer about the main contributions and the relationship to existing work.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of proximal gradient methods for trace norm regularization. While the result is not revolutionary, it fills an important gap in the literature by establishing linear convergence without strong convexity assumptions. The paper is well-written and the technical content is sound, though it could be improved with better organization and clearer presentation of the main contributions.

I recommend acceptance as a theoretical contribution, though it represents an incremental step in the field. The paper is suitable for NIPS as it makes a clear, technically sound contribution to understanding convergence properties of optimization methods in machine learning. The significance is somewhat limited, but the theoretical novelty is sufficient to warrant acceptance.
