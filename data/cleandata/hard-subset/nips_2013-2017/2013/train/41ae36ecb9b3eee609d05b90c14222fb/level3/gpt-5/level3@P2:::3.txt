Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies proximal gradient methods (PGM) for convex risk minimization with trace/nuclear-norm regularization. The main contribution is to show asymptotic linear convergence of PGM for a large class of composite losses f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h has Lipschitz-continuous gradient and is strictly convex (indeed strongly convex on compact subsets), without assuming strong convexity of f itself. The proof follows the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework: the authors establish a local Lipschitzian error bound relating the distance to the optimum set to the residual ‖proxτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X‖F, and then use standard descent properties of PGM to deduce linear convergence of both iterates (R-linear) and objective values (Q-linear) with fixed stepsizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf).

The key technical novelty is an error bound for trace-norm regularization, which has been open due to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the nuclear norm (unlike ℓ1/group-LASSO). The proof draws on a new structural observation: at an optimal point X̄, the matrices X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share the same left/right singular vectors; this allows a reduction to a vector-like argument via simultaneous SVD and matrix perturbation analysis. This extends prior error bounds for ℓ1 and group norms (e.g., Tseng and coauthors) to the nuclear norm. The convergence proof then parallels established analyses (Luo–Tseng; Zhang–Jiang–Luo), and the empirical section shows semilog-linear convergence near the solution for matrix completion and classification.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important theoretical question: linear rates for nuclear-norm regularized problems without strong convexity of f.
- The main technical ingredient (Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm case) is novel and of independent interest; the simultaneous diagonalization observation is elegant.
- Analysis is careful overall and leverages a well-known, robust framework.
- Assumptions cover widely used losses (squared, logistic) with linear operators, so the result applies broadly.
- Numerical experiments are consistent with the theory (linear phase near optimum).

Weaknesses:
- Originality is moderate: beyond the new structural lemma/error bound, the remainder closely follows Luo–Tseng’s framework; the linear-rate result is asymptotic/local.
- Limited algorithmic impact: no new method or practical acceleration; constants in the error bound are not quantified, and the result is asymptotic, which weakens practical significance.
- A technical gap: the inequality ‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F (Theorem 3.2, inequality (11)) needs more justification. This relation (for appropriate κ3 depending on stepsizes) has been established before (e.g., S. Sra, NIPS 2012, Lemma 4); the current appendix (line 085) should either give a correct, detailed argument (e.g., via firm nonexpansiveness and the relation between proxτ and proxταk) or cite existing results.
- Presentation can be improved: the paper jumps quickly into technicalities; the main claim and high-level proof plan could be stated more prominently, with the lengthy error-bound proof deferred.

Quality:
- Technical correctness appears solid overall. The error-bound proof is involved but well structured; the “restricted invertibility” property and use of matrix perturbation theory are convincing.
- Please fix the gap for inequality (11). One way is to invoke nonexpansiveness/firm nonexpansiveness of the proximal operator and relate R(Xk) to Xk+1 − Xk while controlling the mismatch in thresholds (τ vs ταk) using that αk ∈ (α, ᾱ). Alternatively, cite Sra (NIPS 2012, Lemma 4), where a similar inequality is proven for proximal-gradient with nuclear norm.
- Experiments are small-scale but sufficient to illustrate asymptotic linear behavior.

Clarity:
- Generally readable, with helpful preliminaries. However:
  - State the main contributions and conditions up front (assumptions on h and A, nature of linear convergence—local/asymptotic).
  - Move the long error-bound proof to an appendix and keep a high-level sketch in the main text. Then present the linear-rate derivation (via Luo–Tseng) immediately after stating the error bound.
  - Clarify the dependence of constants (η, ε) in the error bound on problem data (A, Lipschitz constants, level set).
  - Define R(X) and relate it to the PGM update earlier, highlighting differences between τ and ταk.

Originality:
- Moderate. The extension of error bounds to the nuclear norm is novel and valuable; the simultaneous SVD observation is interesting. The remainder follows established frameworks.

Significance:
- Theoretical significance is reasonable: it resolves an open question about local error bounds and explains the often-observed linear phase of PGM in nuclear-norm problems.
- Practical impact is limited by the asymptotic/local nature and unspecified constants; it does not change algorithmic practice.

Questions/suggestions for authors:
- Provide a complete proof (or a precise citation) for inequality (11). If you adapt Sra (NIPS 2012, Lemma 4), please explain how the constants depend on α, ᾱ, and Lf and the mismatch between τ and ταk.
- Can you bound/estimate the neighborhood size (ε) for which the error bound holds, or discuss how it depends on the spectral gap in the singular values at the optimum?
- Comment on whether similar arguments extend to other nonpolyhedral spectral regularizers (e.g., Schatten p-norms for p ≠ 1).
- In the experiments, report the stepsizes used (fixed vs line search) and illustrate the transition from sublinear to linear phases.

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves a nontrivial theoretical question (error bound and linear convergence without strong convexity of f) for nuclear norm.
- Clean structural insight (simultaneous diagonalization) likely reusable.
- Solid, careful analysis consistent with empirical behavior.

Cons for acceptance:
- Incremental beyond the new lemma/error bound; largely follows existing frameworks.
- Asymptotic/local nature and lack of constants reduce practical impact.
- One proof step appears under-justified; needs a fix or citation.

Overall recommendation:
- Borderline. I lean toward weak reject based on limited novelty/significance beyond the technical lemma and the asymptotic scope, but I would be supportive if the community values this theoretical closure and the authors fix the noted gap and improve presentation.

Reviewer confidence: High on the methodological assessment; medium on the breadth of related spectral error-bound literature.
