## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace-norm regularized problems of the form min<sub>X</sub> f(X) + τ||X||<sub>*</sub>, where f(X) = h(A(X)) and h is strongly convex on compact sets. The main claim is the establishment of linear convergence under these conditions, extending prior results that typically require strong convexity of *f* itself. This is a valuable extension as many practical applications, like matrix completion, do not naturally exhibit strong convexity in the loss function. 

The paper builds upon existing error-bound based linear convergence results (Tseng [20], Tseng & Yun, Zhang et al.) and successfully generalizes them to the trace norm setting. The technical contribution lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace-norm regularized problem, cleverly utilizing matrix perturbation theory to address the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.  The proof appears generally clear and well-structured, benefiting from a solid foundation in convex optimization. 

However, despite the technical soundness, the paper feels somewhat limited in its empirical validation. While the theoretical results are interesting, the practical impact remains unclear without more extensive experiments. The included numerical examples, although demonstrating linear convergence in specific scenarios, are insufficient to fully support the broader claims.  Specifically, the authors should include a simulation where the loss is *not* strongly convex but PG still converges linearly for different stability levels.  Crucially, they need to discuss *when* the theory fails – what conditions break down the linear convergence guarantee?  This discussion is vital for practitioners to understand the limitations of the result.

Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a discussion of how this analysis might extend to the more commonly used Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM).  ADMM is often preferred in large-scale problems, and exploring the convergence properties in that context would significantly increase the paper's relevance. 

There's a noticeable overlap in the outlines of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 with Tseng’s work; the authors need to more explicitly cite and differentiate their contributions from the original. The dependence of constants κ<sub>1</sub>–κ<sub>4</sub> on ᾱ and α̲ also requires clarification. Finally, the sequence of inequalities preceding (13) is indeed confusing, and the justification provided is correct, but should be more clearly presented within the text.

**Arguments for acceptance:** Technically sound, extends existing theory, provides a potentially useful error bound.
**Arguments against acceptance:** Limited empirical validation, lack of discussion on limitations and extensions (ADMM).

**Overall:** I recommend acceptance with major revisions. The paper presents a solid theoretical contribution, but needs stronger empirical support and a more thorough discussion of its practical implications and limitations. Addressing the reviewer's comments, particularly concerning empirical validation and the extension to ADMM, will significantly strengthen the paper.
