- Paper studies whether proximal gradient method (PGM) achieves linear convergence for trace-norm regularized problems without strong convexity of f.  
- Main theoretical result: PGM attains linear convergence when f(X)=h(A(X)) with A linear and h strictly convex/smooth, removing strong convexity requirement on f.  
- Key technical contribution: a new Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm regularization, enabling application of Tseng’s error bound framework to the trace norm.  
- Proofs are technically sound and carefully constructed; Lemma 4.1 provides a crucial restricted invertibility property.  
- Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification validate the theoretical linear convergence claims.  
- Paper situates itself well in related work and correctly notes significance for common loss functions (e.g., square, logistic) used in matrix problems.  
- Contribution is of independent interest for optimization theory and practical low-rank problems.  
- Concern: novelty relative to ℓ1-norm results is unclear—authors should explain why ℓ1 techniques do not directly extend to the nuclear norm.  
- Suggestion: clarify that the ℓ1-norm case is a special case of the result (e.g., when m=1 or n=1) to strengthen novelty claims.  
- Title is misleading; recommend reframing to emphasize the structured loss (linear operator A) rather than the regularizer.  
- Minor presentation issues to fix: clarify the sampling operator P (fixed mask vs random), fix broken reference [20], and define Q-/R-linear convergence.  
- Additional clarifications requested: relate residual map R(X) to Tseng’s original definition and expand discussion in Section 3 on why Lemma 3.2 is the key insight.  
- Overall assessment: strong, well-written paper with significant theoretical contribution and solid experiments; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing novelty comparison, title, and presentation issues.
