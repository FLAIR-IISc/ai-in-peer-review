This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized optimization problems of the form min F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||∗. The main claim is that for a broad class of loss functions f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is a linear operator and h is a smooth function that is strongly convex on any compact set, the PGM converges at a linear rate. This is a significant result because it does not require the overall loss function `f` to be strongly convex, a condition often violated in practical applications such as matrix completion where the operator A is not injective.

The analysis is based on establishing a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace-norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map. This work extends a line of research that has successfully used error bounds to prove the linear convergence of first-order methods for other problems, such as the lasso and group lasso (e.g., Luo & Tseng [10], Tseng [22], Zhang et al. [25]). The key technical challenge, which this paper addresses, is extending these ideas from vector norms with polyhedral epigraphs (like the l1-norm) to the trace norm, whose structure is more complex due to its dependence on singular values.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Result:** The paper establishes the linear convergence of the widely used proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularization under realistic assumptions that do not include strong convexity of the loss function `f`. This is a valuable contribution to the optimization and machine learning communities, providing a stronger theoretical foundation for using PGM on problems like matrix completion and multi-task learning.

2.  **Novel Technical Contribution:** While building upon the error-bound framework of Luo and Tseng, the extension to the trace norm is technically non-trivial. The authors correctly identify that existing proof techniques for l1-type regularizers do not directly apply. Their analysis, which leverages matrix perturbation theory to handle the spectral properties of the iterates, is a key and novel part of the contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and the proofs are mostly clear and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem and situating their work within the existing literature. The numerical experiments in Section 5, which include the requested semi-log plots, effectively corroborate the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Incremental Originality:** The primary weakness is that the work can be viewed as a technical extension of an established analysis framework to a new problem class. While the extension is challenging and valuable, the core conceptual approach (using an error bound to show linear convergence) is not new.

2.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments confirm that linear convergence occurs in practice, which is good. However, they do not explore the boundaries of the theory or provide deeper insights into when the assumptions are critical. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough empirical investigation.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Relation to Prior Work:** The proof structure for the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and the key lemma (Lemma 4.1, not 3.2) closely follows the template laid out in Tseng [22]. While adapting existing proof techniques is standard practice, the authors should be more explicit in their attribution. I suggest adding a sentence that clearly states that the high-level proof strategy is adapted from Tseng [22], and then carefully delineating which steps required novel arguments specific to the trace norm. This would better highlight the paper's unique technical contributions.

2.  **Dependency of Constants:** In the proof of Theorem 3.2, the constants κ1, κ2, κ3, κ4 determine the final convergence rate. The paper states that they depend on the step-size bounds α̲ and ᾱ, but does not elaborate. For completeness and to give the reader a better sense of how the step-size choice affects the convergence rate, it would be helpful to briefly state these dependencies in the main text or an appendix.

3.  **Minor Proof Clarification:** The sequence of inequalities just before (18) could be made clearer. The inequality τ‖X̄k‖∗ ≤ 〈Ḡ, Xk + Rk − X̄k〉 + τ‖Xk + Rk‖∗ follows directly from the subgradient optimality condition -Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X̄k‖∗ (see e.g., p.289 in Tseng [22]) and the convexity of the trace norm. Stating this directly would be more straightforward than the intermediate steps presented. This is a minor point but would improve the proof's readability.

4.  **Exploring the Limits of the Theory:** The paper's impact would be significantly enhanced by a discussion of scenarios where the theoretical assumptions are not met, yet linear convergence might still be observed. I strongly recommend adding a simulation where the function `h` is *not* strongly convex on the relevant compact set (e.g., using a loss like ||z||^p with 1 < p < 2) but PGM still exhibits linear convergence. It would be insightful to investigate this for different problem "stability" levels (e.g., by varying how close the problem is to being ill-conditioned) and discuss why the current theoretical tools fail to capture this behavior. This would provide valuable insights into the tightness of the derived bounds.

5.  **Extension to Other Algorithms:** The error bound established in this paper is a powerful tool that could potentially be used to analyze other algorithms. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on how the analysis might be extended to other popular methods for this problem class, such as the Alternating Direction Method of Multipliers (ADMM). This would broaden the significance of the paper's core contribution (the error bound) beyond just the PGM.

In summary, this is a strong, technically sound paper that makes a solid contribution to the literature on first-order methods. The main weakness is its incremental nature. Addressing the points above, particularly by adding a discussion on the limits of the theory and potential extensions to other algorithms, would substantially improve the paper and solidify its impact.
