This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized risk minimization problems. The authors extend the analysis of linear convergence beyond the standard strong convexity assumption on the loss function. Building on the influential framework of Luo and Tseng, the core of the analysis is the derivation of a local Lipschitzian error bound for the trace-norm regularized objective. This result is analogous to prior work on l1-norm and group LASSO regularization, but the non-polyhedral nature of the trace-norm epigraph requires a novel analytical approach.

The main strength and key technical novelty of the paper is an elegant observation that enables the simultaneous singular value decomposition of an optimal solution and its corresponding gradient. This insight cleverly reduces the complexity of the matrix-valued problem to a more tractable vector case, allowing the authors to establish the crucial error bound. The overall theoretical development appears sound and correct.

However, the paper has several weaknesses. The originality is moderate; while the simultaneous diagonalization technique is interesting, the overall proof strategy closely follows existing work. Furthermore, a key inequality, `||R(X^k)||_F <= κ3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F`, is presented as a standard result in the supplementary material (line 085). This relationship, crucial for the main convergence proof, was explicitly proven in Lemma 4 of Suvrit Sra's NIPS 2012 paper, "Scalable estimation of probabilistic functional-topic models," which should be properly cited and discussed. There also appears to be a minor technical gap, as the paper should justify why the chosen step-size condition (e.g., `α < 1`) is sufficient to establish this inequality.

In terms of clarity, the paper is readable, but its structure could be improved. The main claims about linear convergence should be presented upfront, followed by the derivation assuming the error bound holds. The technical, and more involved, proof of the error bound itself could then be deferred, enhancing the paper's narrative flow.

Finally, the significance of the work is primarily theoretical. The established linear convergence is asymptotic, which may limit its practical impact, as accelerated sublinear methods often perform better in the initial phases of optimization.

In summary, this is an incremental but theoretically interesting contribution.
**Pros:**
*   Novel technical tool (simultaneous diagonalization) for analyzing trace-norm problems.
*   Proves a new local error bound and establishes asymptotic linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity.

**Cons:**
*   Moderate originality, as the high-level framework is not new.
*   Fails to cite prior work (Sra, 2012) for a key inequality.
*   Significance is mainly theoretical, with limited immediate algorithmic implications.
