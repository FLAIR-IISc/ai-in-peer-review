The paper "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization" presents a novel result on the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm-regularized problems. The authors establish this result under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss function, which is a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and signal processing.

The paper is well-written, and the main proof appears to be correct. The authors build upon the Lipschitzian error bound technique developed by Tseng, extending an open question about applying this technique to the nuclear (trace) norm. The result addresses an important problem in machine learning and signal processing applications, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

One of the strengths of the paper is its careful and clear writing style. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, including a discussion of the applications and the limitations of existing methods. The proof of the main result is also well-organized and easy to follow.

However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. Firstly, the title of the paper could be improved to avoid the word "structured," as the main new contribution concerns the loss side (linear operator A) rather than the regularizer. Additionally, the authors should clarify whether P is a sampling or mask operator, as the text implies randomness but P is fixed. Furthermore, there is a broken reference at line 337 that needs to be corrected.

In terms of the technical content, the authors could provide a clearer comparison and analogy to the vector l1-norm case, such as in [20, 22]. It would be helpful to discuss whether and why l1 techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm. Moreover, the authors should note that the l1-norm result should follow as a corollary of the trace-norm result and discuss the perceived novelty versus expected extension.

The authors could also provide more discussion and conclusions to justify the technical Lemma 3.2 in Section 3. Additionally, it would be helpful to briefly recall the meanings of Q- and R-linear convergence for reader clarity. Finally, the authors could relate the residual R(X) defined in (6) to Tseng's original definition (46) for interpretation.

In conclusion, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of machine learning and signal processing. The authors establish a novel result on the linear convergence of the PGM for solving trace norm-regularized problems under assumptions weaker than strong convexity of the loss function. While there are some minor issues that need to be addressed, the paper is well-written, and the main proof appears to be correct.
