This paper presents a compelling result: the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized problems under relatively weak assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a significant contribution, as many machine learning applications utilize loss functions that aren't strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion). The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized, making the technical details accessible. The proof appears correct, building logically on Tseng’s Lipschitzian error bound technique and successfully extending it to the nuclear (trace) norm case – a non-trivial extension acknowledged as an open question.

The paper effectively motivates the problem and situates it within the broader context of low-rank matrix recovery and its applications. The authors appropriately highlight the limitations of existing linear convergence results, which typically rely on strong convexity. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the connection to prior work, particularly [10, 22, 23, 25], is well-established. The numerical experiments further support the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence in practice for matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.

However, the reviewer’s request for a clear comparison to the vector l1-norm case (e.g., [20,22]) and a discussion of why l1 techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm is crucial and deserves further elaboration. While the authors briefly touch on the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the difficulty of splitting variables, a more detailed explanation would be beneficial. Specifically, it would be useful to explicitly state that the l1-norm result *should* follow as a corollary of the trace-norm result – highlighting the generality of their approach. Discussing *how* the proof generalizes, and what specific modifications (if any) are needed to obtain the l1-norm result, would strengthen the paper and underscore the novelty of their contribution. The fact that this isn’t a trivial extension, despite superficial similarity, needs to be more strongly articulated.

The suggestion to change the title to avoid the word “structured” is well-taken. The core contribution lies in the analysis of the loss function (specifically, its relationship to the linear operator A) rather than characteristics of the trace norm regularizer itself. A title focusing on the convergence properties under weaker assumptions would be more accurate and representative of the paper's content.

Regarding the minor issues: clarifying whether P is a sampling or mask operator is important. While a fixed operator is assumed, explicitly stating its nature removes ambiguity. The broken reference on line 337 should be corrected. A brief recall of Q- and R- linear convergence definitions would aid readers unfamiliar with these concepts. Relating the residual R(X) defined in (6) to Tseng’s original definition (46) would enhance intuition and allow readers to easily connect to the foundational work. Finally, adding more discussion or conclusions justifying the technical Lemma 3.2 in Section 3 would strengthen the argument and demonstrate its importance within the broader framework. Why is this lemma necessary? What specific challenges does it overcome?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Result:** The linear convergence guarantee under weak assumptions is a significant advancement.
*   **Clear and Rigorous Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Novel Extension:** Successfully extends Tseng’s technique to the trace norm, resolving an open question.
*   **Practical Relevance:** Applicable to a wide range of machine learning problems.
*   **Supporting Numerical Experiments:** Demonstrates the theoretical results in practice.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Insufficient Elaboration on l1-norm Connection:** The comparison to the vector l1-norm case needs more detail to fully appreciate the novelty.
*   **Minor Technical Issues:** The minor issues (title, reference, definitions, Lemma 3.2 justification) detract slightly from the overall polish.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field of optimization and machine learning. The theoretical results are significant, the presentation is excellent, and the numerical experiments support the claims. Addressing the reviewer’s concerns regarding the l1-norm comparison and resolving the minor technical issues would further enhance the paper’s impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
