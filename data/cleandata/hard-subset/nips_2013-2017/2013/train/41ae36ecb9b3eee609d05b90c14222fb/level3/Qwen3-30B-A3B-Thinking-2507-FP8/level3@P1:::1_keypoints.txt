- Establishes linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method on trace norm-regularized problems f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strongly convex and Lipschitz gradient.  
- Main technical contribution: a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization that permits proving linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f.  
- Extends prior error-bound-based linear convergence results (ℓ1 and group LASSO) to the trace norm setting.  
- Resolves an open question by proving a Lipschitzian error bound despite the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm.  
- Key technical insight: optimal solutions X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), enabling matrix perturbation theory use.  
- Empirical validation includes matrix completion and classification experiments but is insufficient to verify linear rates.  
- Missing required semilog plots of objective vs iterations to explicitly demonstrate the claimed linear convergence.  
- Strengths: sound, well-structured theoretical development; novel and well-motivated error bound; clear relation to prior literature.  
- Proofs are mostly clear and rigorous, though some steps could be clarified (e.g., inequalities before equation 13).  
- Weakness: contribution is primarily a technical extension of existing frameworks rather than a fundamentally new approach.  
- Paper closely follows Tseng’s framework (Theorem 3.1, Lemma 3.2), needing clearer differentiation from prior work.  
- Fails to discuss cases where assumptions do not hold but empirical linear convergence still occurs.  
- Does not address requested extension of analysis to ADMM.  
- Convergence-rate constants (κ1, κ2, κ3) are not clearly related to step-size parameters α and ᾱ, reducing practical usefulness.  
- Evaluation summary: Quality—technically sound but weak empirical validation; Clarity—generally good but needs technical clarifications; Originality—novel error bound but overall incremental; Significance—advances theory but limited practical impact without stronger experiments.  
- Arguments for acceptance: establishes linear convergence under weaker assumptions, provides a novel error bound, and is theoretically sound and well-contextualized.  
- Arguments against acceptance: mainly a technical extension, insufficient empirical evidence (missing semilog), lacks discussion of assumption failures and ADMM extension, and needs clarification of some proof steps.  
- Recommendation: valuable theoretical contribution but authors should add requested numerical evidence and clarify relation to prior work before final acceptance.
