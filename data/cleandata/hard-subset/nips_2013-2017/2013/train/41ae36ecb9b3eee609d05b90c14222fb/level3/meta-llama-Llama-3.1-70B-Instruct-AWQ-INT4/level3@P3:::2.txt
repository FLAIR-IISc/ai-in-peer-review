This paper proposes a novel analysis of the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving a class of trace norm-regularized problems. The authors provide a new Lipschitzian error bound for the aforementioned problems, which may be of independent interest.

The main contribution of this paper is the establishment of linear convergence of the PGM for a large class of loss functions, without any strong convexity assumption on the loss function. The authors prove that when the loss function f takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is an arbitrary linear operator and h is strictly convex with certain smoothness and curvature properties, the PGM for solving the trace norm-regularized problem has an asymptotic linear rate of convergence.

The paper is carefully and clearly written, and the main proof appears correct. The authors build on Tseng's Lipschitzian error bound technique and extend an open question about applying that technique to the nuclear (trace) norm.

One of the strengths of the paper is that it addresses an important problem for machine learning and signal processing applications. The authors provide numerical experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of the PGM on two problems: matrix completion and matrix classification. The results show that both the objective values and the solutions converge linearly.

However, there are some minor issues that need to be addressed. For example, the authors should clarify whether P is a sampling or mask operator, as sampling implies randomness but P is fixed. Additionally, there is a broken reference at line 337.

The authors should also consider adding more discussion and conclusions to justify the technical Lemma 3.2 in Section 3. Furthermore, it would be helpful to briefly recall the meanings of Q- and R-linear convergence for reader clarity.

Moreover, the authors should relate the residual R(X) defined in (6) to Tseng's original definition (46) for interpretation. It would also be beneficial to include a clear comparison/analogy to the vector l1-norm case (e.g., [20,22]) and discuss whether/why l1 techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm.

Finally, the authors should consider changing the title to avoid the word "structured," since the main new contribution concerns the loss side (linear operator A) rather than the regularizer.

In terms of novelty, the paper provides a new error bound for trace norm-regularized problems, which is a significant contribution to the field. However, it is worth noting that the l1-norm result should follow as a corollary of the trace-norm result, and the authors should discuss the perceived novelty vs. expected extension.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the results are significant. With some minor revisions, the paper has the potential to make a substantial contribution to the field of machine learning and signal processing.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper provides a novel analysis of the linear convergence of the PGM for solving a class of trace norm-regularized problems.
* The authors establish a new Lipschitzian error bound for the aforementioned problems, which may be of independent interest.
* The paper addresses an important problem for machine learning and signal processing applications.
