This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. While PGM is known to have a sublinear O(1/k^2) convergence rate for this problem class, linear convergence is typically only guaranteed if the smooth part of the objective, `f(X)`, is strongly convex. The main contribution of this work is to establish an asymptotic linear convergence rate for PGM under a weaker condition, namely when the loss function has the structure `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is a strictly convex function but `A` can be an arbitrary linear operator, meaning `f` is not necessarily strongly convex. This setting covers important applications like matrix completion with a square loss.

The authors' analysis follows the powerful framework developed by Luo and Tseng (1993), which requires establishing a local Lipschitzian error bound. The core technical result of the paper is a new error bound for the trace norm regularization problem, which relates the distance to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map. The proof of this bound is non-trivial due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm ball and relies on a key new insight: an optimal solution `X*` and the negative gradient at that point, `-∇f(X*)`, share the same set of singular vectors. This allows for a reduction of the matrix analysis to a more manageable vector-based argument using matrix perturbation theory.

### Strengths

*   **Theoretical Novelty:** The paper addresses an important theoretical question by extending the conditions for linear convergence of PGM to non-strongly convex objectives in the context of trace norm regularization. This is a valuable contribution to the optimization theory literature for machine learning.
*   **Key Technical Insight:** The main technical tool, the proof of the Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1), is based on an elegant and novel observation about the simultaneous singular value decomposition of the optimal solution and its corresponding gradient (Proposition 4.2). This technique for handling the spectral properties of the trace norm is interesting and could be of independent value.
*   **Relevance:** The problem class considered is highly relevant to machine learning, with applications in matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification, as correctly identified by the authors. Weakening the strong convexity assumption makes the analysis applicable to a broader and more realistic set of problems.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

*   **Originality in Context:** The overall analysis strategy relies heavily on the established Luo and Tseng framework. The primary novelty lies in proving that the required error bound holds for this specific problem class. While this is a significant step, it is important to place all components of the proof in the context of prior work. Specifically, inequality (11) in Theorem 3.2, `‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk −Xk+1‖F`, seems to be a known property of proximal gradient steps. For instance, Lemma 4 in Sra (NIPS 2012, "Scalable Frank-Wolfe...") establishes a very similar bound. The authors should clarify the novelty of this part of their derivation or cite the relevant literature.
*   **Significance and Practical Impact:** The contribution is primarily of a theoretical nature. The linear convergence result is asymptotic, meaning it is only guaranteed to hold once the iterates are in a neighborhood of the optimal solution set. In practice, PGM is often terminated based on modest stopping criteria, and may not reach this asymptotic regime. The experiments show linear convergence, but it is unclear if this provides a practical speedup over accelerated sublinear methods in terms of wall-clock time for achieving moderate accuracy. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the practical implications of this asymptotic result.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** While the paper is readable, its structure could be improved to better highlight the main message. The primary result is the linear convergence of PGM (Theorem 3.2), but the paper first focuses heavily on the technical machinery of the error bound (Theorem 3.1). A more intuitive presentation might state the main convergence result upfront in Section 3 and present its proof, citing the error bound as a key lemma. The subsequent sections could then be dedicated to the more involved proof of the error bound itself. This would make the paper's main takeaway more accessible.
*   **Gap in Proof:** There appears to be a minor gap in the proof of Theorem 3.2. The paper asserts that inequality (11) holds and refers to the supplementary material for details. Could the authors provide a more direct justification for this inequality in the main text, perhaps by briefly explaining how the choice of step size `α_k` guarantees this relationship? This would make the proof more self-contained and convincing.

### Summary

Overall, this is a solid theoretical paper that makes an interesting contribution to our understanding of the convergence behavior of PGM for trace norm regularized problems. The primary strength is the novel technique used to prove a local error bound, which in turn enables the application of existing analytical frameworks to show linear convergence without strong convexity.

However, the contribution feels somewhat incremental, as it builds directly on the Luo-Tseng framework, and its practical significance is limited by the asymptotic nature of the result. Questions regarding the originality of some intermediate steps in the proof should also be addressed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a relevant theoretical problem for machine learning.
*   Contains a novel and elegant proof technique for establishing an error bound for trace norm regularization.
*   Extends linear convergence guarantees to a broader class of non-strongly convex problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Contribution is mainly theoretical with limited direct algorithmic or practical impact.
*   The overall proof structure follows a well-known template.
*   Clarity could be improved, and some steps in the proof need better justification or citation.

Given the novel technical insight and the theoretical importance of the result, the paper is a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community. I lean towards acceptance, provided the authors can address the points raised above, particularly regarding the relationship to prior work and the justification for all steps in their proof.
