

This paper addresses the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized optimization problems, specifically establishing linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The work builds on the well-known result that PGM achieves sublinear convergence for such problems, and extends it by showing that for a broad class of loss functions (those of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth), PGM exhibits asymptotic linear convergence.

The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularized problems, which the authors establish by proving that optimal solutions X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same singular vectors. This observation enables a novel approach to analyzing the spectral structure of points near the optimal solution set, circumventing the difficulties encountered when trying to extend existing error bounds for ℓ1-norm regularization to the trace norm case. The authors then apply the convergence framework developed by Luo and Tseng to establish the linear convergence rate.

The paper is technically sound overall, with clear exposition of the main ideas and sufficient mathematical rigor. However, I note a minor gap in the proof of Theorem 3.2 where the constant κ3 in equation (11) (R(Xk) ≤ κ3‖Xk - Xk+1‖F) requires justification for the choice of step size α < 1/Lf. While this is likely a technical detail that can be resolved, it deserves explicit clarification in the main text.

The paper demonstrates good clarity in its presentation, though it could benefit from stating the main contribution more prominently at the beginning, rather than burying it in the introduction. The proof of the error bound is highly technical and might be better presented as a separate section after establishing the main convergence result, allowing readers to first understand the significance of the linear convergence result before delving into the technical details.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a moderate contribution to the field. While the approach follows the established framework of Luo and Tseng, the key insight about simultaneous diagonalization of matrices (Proposition 4.2) is novel and interesting. This represents a meaningful extension beyond the existing literature, particularly since the paper resolves an open question regarding error bounds for trace-norm regularization that has remained unanswered despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization.

The significance of the work is primarily theoretical. The linear convergence result is an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of optimization methods for low-rank matrix problems, but the asymptotic nature of the convergence means it has limited immediate algorithmic impact. The authors demonstrate this theoretically with numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification problems, showing linear convergence in the asymptotic regime as expected from the theory.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work, particularly the recent papers by Sra (NIPS 2012) on proximal gradient methods and the work by Zhang et al. (J. Oper. Res. Soc. China, 2013) on linear convergence for nonsmooth convex minimization. The authors appropriately cite these works and position their contribution within the existing literature.

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. A novel technical insight (simultaneous diagonalization) that enables the error bound proof
2. Clear theoretical development of the convergence result
3. Good experimental validation of the theoretical findings
4. Appropriate connections to related work

The main weaknesses include:
1. The minor gap in justifying the constant κ3
2. The slightly convoluted presentation of the main result before the technical details
3. Limited practical significance due to the asymptotic nature of the convergence

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding the convergence properties of first-order methods for trace-norm regularized problems. While the results are incremental in the sense that they build on existing frameworks, the specific technical insight about the spectral structure of optimal solutions represents a meaningful advance in the field. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the minor gap in the proof and improving the presentation of the main result.

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization methods for trace-norm regularized problems. It would be a suitable contribution to NIPS as a solid theoretical paper that makes a clear, incremental advance in the field. The results are significant for researchers working on low-rank matrix problems and optimization theory, though the practical impact is limited to the theoretical understanding rather than immediate algorithmic improvements.
