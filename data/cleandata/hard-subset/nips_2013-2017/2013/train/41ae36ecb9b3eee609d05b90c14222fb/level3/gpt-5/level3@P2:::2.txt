Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex minimization with a smooth convex loss composed with a linear operator and a trace/nuclear-norm regularizer: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖*, with f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is continuously differentiable with Lipschitz gradient, strictly convex, and strongly convex on compact convex subsets of its domain. The main result establishes an asymptotic linear convergence rate for the standard proximal gradient method (PGM) without assuming f is strongly convex. The key technical ingredient is a new local Lipschitzian error bound of the form dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖R(X)‖F, where R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X is the residual map defined via the matrix shrinkage operator Sτ and X* is the optimal solution set.

This addresses an open question left by the rich literature on composite minimization where linear rates are known under strong convexity (e.g., for LASSO-like problems) or where error bounds were available for polyhedral nonsmooth parts such as ℓ1 and (group) LASSO. In contrast, the nuclear norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, and variable-splitting arguments used in the ℓ1/group-LASSO analyses do not carry over. The paper overcomes this by exploiting a spectral alignment property at optimality (the solution and negative gradient share singular vectors) and matrix perturbation arguments to establish a restricted invertibility relation that yields the desired error bound. With this bound in place, the Luo–Tseng analysis framework implies Q-linear decrease of F(Xk) and R-linear convergence of iterates.

The result directly covers commonly used losses in ML, including least squares and logistic loss, and it explains the linear convergence observed empirically for trace-norm regularization even when f is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion when A has a nontrivial nullspace). The paper complements prior works on PGM and accelerated variants for nuclear-norm problems (e.g., Ji & Ye; Toh & Yun) by tightening the rate beyond the standard sublinear guarantees under weaker assumptions than strong convexity.

Strengths:
- Novel and useful: Establishes linear convergence of PGM for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f, via a new local Lipschitzian error bound. This fills a notable gap compared to ℓ1/group-LASSO where polyhedrality is key.
- Solid analysis: The proof is technically careful. The simultaneous SVD property (Proposition 4.2) and the construction underpinning Lemma 4.1(c) are insightful and appear correct.
- Clear connection to established frameworks: Leverages the Luo–Tseng error bound approach; the structure of the analysis is transparent.
- Practical relevance: Assumptions cover square and logistic losses; experiments on matrix completion and classification display linear convergence in practice, aligning with theory.
- Well-written and organized; the main assumptions and the scope are clearly delineated.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- ℓ1 analogy and novelty positioning: Please more explicitly compare with the ℓ1/group-LASSO error bounds (e.g., Tseng, Tseng–Yun, Zhang–Jiang–Luo), clarifying why those techniques fail for the nuclear norm (non-polyhedral epigraph; lack of separability) and how your spectral alignment circumvents this. Also clarify whether the ℓ1 case can be recovered as a corollary of your result (e.g., via diagonal matrices) and what, if anything, is new in that limit.
- Scope and locality of the error bound: Theorem 3.1 is local (small residual, bounded level set). Please discuss the basin of attraction, how constants (η, ε) depend on the level ζ, and whether one can ensure global-linear after a finite number of iterations under standard descent/backtracking.
- Residual mapping: Relate your residual R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X to the residual mappings used in the Luo–Tseng/Tseng analyses, and explain the role of step size (your definition corresponds to unit step; in the algorithm you use αk and Sταk). A short remark on scaling would aid interpretation.
- Clarify the role of h’s “strong convexity on compact sets”: Provide intuition/examples and explain why this suffices (together with the restricted invertibility) to derive (15) and feed the error bound.
- Experiments: While illustrative, they are small-scale; consider reporting the estimated linear rate constants and how they relate to theoretical quantities (Lf, αk) and to θ. Also briefly discuss accelerated PGM: does the same error bound imply linear asymptotic rates for FISTA-type schemes?
- Title wording: If the title or abstract uses the term “structured” to refer to the regularizer, consider rewording; the novelty is on handling the composition f = h ∘ A and the non-polyhedral nuclear norm via spectral structure rather than new regularizer structure. (If your current title is “On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization,” it is already appropriate.)
- Minor clarity: In the matrix completion section, clarify whether P is a deterministic masking operator or a random sampling operator; in the optimization model it is fixed, but the text uses “sampling,” which suggests randomness in data generation only.

Quality:
- Technical soundness appears high. Assumptions are clearly stated; the main lemmas are justified; the reduction to Luo–Tseng is standard once the error bound is proved. I did not find gaps in the main argument; providing a bit more intuition for Lemma 4.1(c) would help readers.

Clarity:
- Generally very clear and well organized. Consider briefly recalling the definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence in the main text (you do in Theorem 3.2, but an earlier reminder would help non-experts).
- Please fix a broken/missing reference around line 337 and ensure all references include years/pages. Also ensure consistent notation for the residual (equation numbers: your R is defined in (7), but later you refer back to (6) for prox/shrinkage).
- Minor: briefly restate the proximity operator and shrinkage definition in the appendix for completeness.

Originality:
- High. The error bound for nuclear-norm regularization (non-polyhedral) under h ∘ A is new and addresses an open question. The simultaneous SVD/spectral perturbation route is a fresh angle relative to the separability-based ℓ1 analyses.

Significance:
- High. Nuclear-norm regularization is ubiquitous in ML; linear rates without strong convexity are both theoretically and practically important. The error bound technique may be reusable for other spectral regularizers.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Resolves an open problem by providing a new error bound and linear rate for a widely used regularizer under weak assumptions.
- Rigorous analysis with clear exposition and supporting experiments.
- Potentially broad impact on first-order methods for spectral regularization.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is modest; no exploration of accelerated methods or larger-scale problems.
- The locality of the error bound and dependence of constants are not fully elucidated.
- The paper could better position itself relative to ℓ1/group-LASSO error-bound literature and clarify the novelty boundary.

Minor comments and typos:
- Clarify whether P is a sampling or masking operator; specify that it is fixed during optimization though generated randomly in experiments.
- Broken reference at line 337; several references are missing years/page numbers—please correct.
- Please explicitly tie your residual mapping to Tseng/Luo–Tseng residuals and note the role of αk scaling.
- Provide a short intuition paragraph for Lemma 4.1 (especially part (c)) in the main text; currently the technical development is dense.
- Consider reporting constants or bounds for Lf in the logistic-loss experiment, or justify the chosen backtracking parameters.

Overall, this is a strong and timely contribution. With the clarifications above, it would make a valuable addition to the program.
