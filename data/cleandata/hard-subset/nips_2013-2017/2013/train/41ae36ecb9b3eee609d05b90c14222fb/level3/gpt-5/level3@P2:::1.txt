Summary
This paper studies composite convex optimization with a trace-norm regularizer: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h has Lipschitz gradient and is strictly (indeed, strongly) convex on any convex compact subset of its domain. The main contribution is a Lipschitzian error bound for the residual map R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X near the solution set, which implies that the proximal gradient method (PGM) converges linearly (asymptotically) under fixed step sizes bounded away from zero and below 1/L. Notably, the result does not assume global strong convexity of f, covering important cases such as square loss with non-injective A and logistic loss. The key technical idea is to exploit a spectral alignment at optimality (the optimal X and −∇f(X) share singular vectors), enabling a perturbation-based analysis to prove a local error bound for trace-norm regularization. Empirically, the authors show linear trends in semi-log plots for matrix completion and matrix classification.

Relation to prior work
The analysis follows the Luo–Tseng error-bound framework for establishing linear convergence of first-order methods. Previous error bounds yielding linear convergence were known for polyhedral or separable regularizers (lasso, group lasso) via Tseng and Tseng–Yun, and for some structured nonsmooth problems (e.g., Zhang et al.). The present work extends that line to the non-polyhedral trace norm, where separability arguments fail and new spectral tools are required. The positioning relative to prox-gradient linear rates under strong convexity and inexact variants (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011) is clear: here no strong convexity of f is assumed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The statements and the proof architecture are sound. The new ingredient is the spectral alignment (Proposition 4.2) and the use of matrix perturbation ideas to obtain restricted invertibility and the local error bound. The linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) then follows standardly from Luo–Tseng plus descent properties of PGM.
- Scope and limitations: The rate is asymptotic and local; constants depend on local curvature of h over a compact level set and on A. The step-size condition requires 0 < α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf. Backtracking implementations should be discussed vis-à-vis these bounds.
- Empirics: The experiments qualitatively support asymptotic linear convergence, but the evaluation is light and could be strengthened by the suggested ablations.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The proof ideas are clearly motivated, and the structure of the error bound proof is transparent.
- Please clarify the dependence of constants κ1,…,κ4 in Theorem 3.2 on α, ᾱ, and Lf (ideally give explicit expressions or at least tight inequalities). This helps interpret the contraction factor in (12).
- Minor technical point: the chain of inequalities leading to the analogue of (18) (just before your equation (13) in the current draft) is confusing. The final inequality follows from convexity and the subgradient relation −Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X̄||* (cf. Tseng, 2009, Math. Programming, p. 289). Please either remove the intermediate step or correct it.
- Please state explicitly that the constants η, ε in the error bound and σ in Lemma 4.1 depend on a bounded level set {F ≤ ζ}.

Originality
- The extension of error-bound-based linear convergence from polyhedral/separable nonsmooth regularizers to the non-polyhedral trace norm is nontrivial. The spectral alignment and ensuing perturbation argument are, to my knowledge, new in this context.
- At the same time, the global proof strategy closely mirrors the Tseng/Luo–Tseng framework. Please improve attribution throughout the error-bound and rate derivations, and delineate precisely what is borrowed versus new. For example, highlight that Theorem 3.1’s structure follows Tseng’s approach but requires new analysis to overcome non-separability and non-polyhedrality; cite the exact lemmas and propositions from Tseng and Tseng–Yun that you instantiate.

Significance
- The result is practically relevant: PGM is the workhorse for nuclear-norm problems, and asymptotic linear rates without strong convexity formalize common empirical observations.
- The error bound may enable similar rates for other first-order splitting methods and for variants with inexact proximal steps or stochastic gradients, and may inform restart rules.

Requests and suggestions
- Empirical validation: Include a controlled simulation where f is not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with varying sampling ratios or conditioning of A) and demonstrate linear convergence across different “stability levels.” Also show a case where assumptions are violated (e.g., hinge loss or a setting where h lacks the required local curvature) and discuss the observed behavior and why the theory does not apply.
- Discuss when the theory may fail despite apparent linear behavior, e.g., if the iterates have not yet entered the local region where the error bound holds, or if step sizes are not bounded away from zero (aggressive backtracking), or if the level set is unbounded.
- Step sizes: For the matrix-classification experiment with backtracking, please clarify that the accepted αk satisfy αk ≤ 1/Lf and are bounded below by some α > 0 after a finite number of iterations, ensuring the conditions of Theorem 3.2.
- ADMM extension: The established error bound suggests that operator-splitting methods such as ADMM, applied to a split formulation of the same problem, may also enjoy local linear convergence under standard regularity (metric subregularity/strong monotonicity of the primal-dual KKT map). Please outline how your error bound plus existing ADMM analyses could yield such results, and what additional assumptions (e.g., full column rank in the splitting, appropriate penalty parameter) would be needed.
- Constants and rates: If possible, provide an explicit contraction factor in (12) in terms of σ (local curvature), ||A||, L, and step-size bounds, or at least a computable lower bound.

Pros
- Establishes asymptotic linear convergence of PGM for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity of f.
- Introduces a novel spectral argument to obtain a local error bound for the trace norm.
- Clear connection to widely used losses (square, logistic) and common applications.
- Generally clear exposition and proofs.

Cons
- Primarily a technical extension of an established framework; broader methodological impact is incremental.
- Empirical section is thin and could better illustrate the scope and limits of the theory.
- Some attributions to Tseng/Luo–Tseng could be more precise; a few proof steps need clarification.
- Practical guidance on constants and step-size conditions is limited.

Overall assessment
A solid theoretical contribution that resolves a natural open question about linear convergence of PGM for trace-norm regularization under mild structural assumptions on f. While the framework is established, the extension to the non-polyhedral trace norm is technically interesting and relevant. I lean toward acceptance if the authors strengthen attribution and clarity, provide the requested empirical illustration of non-strongly-convex cases (and discussion of failure modes), clarify constants and step-size conditions, and add a brief discussion on ADMM extensions.
