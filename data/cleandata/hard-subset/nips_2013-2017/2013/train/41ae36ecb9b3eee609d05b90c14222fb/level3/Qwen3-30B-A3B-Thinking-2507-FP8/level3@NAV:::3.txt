

This paper investigates the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The authors extend Luo and Tseng's framework by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, which enables asymptotic linear convergence. The key technical contribution is a novel observation allowing simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, reducing the problem to the vector case—a significant departure from existing approaches that rely on variable splitting for ℓ₁-norm regularization.

The paper is technically sound overall, with correct theoretical derivations. However, the reviewer notes that the inequality |R_k| ≤ κ |X_k - X_{k+1}| was already established in Sra (NIPS 2012, Lemma 4), which weakens the novelty claim. Additionally, the paper fails to justify why the chosen κ₃ (e.g., α < 1) implies R(Xᵏ) ≤ κ₃ ‖Xᵏ - Xᵏ⁺¹‖_F in Appendix line 085, creating a gap in the technical argument.

The paper is well-written but could improve clarity by stating the main convergence result upfront and deferring the detailed error bound proof. While the simultaneous diagonalization observation is novel and interesting, the overall approach follows established frameworks, resulting in moderate originality. The significance is primarily theoretical, as the asymptotic nature of the convergence result limits practical algorithmic impact. The numerical experiments support the theoretical claims but don't demonstrate substantial computational advantages.

In summary, this is a technically correct but incremental contribution. The error bound for trace-norm regularization is valuable, but the paper's reliance on prior frameworks and lack of practical implications reduce its impact. The authors should address the justification gap in the appendix and better highlight the novelty of their diagonalization approach. While the theoretical result is sound, the modest contribution and limited practical significance make it a marginal acceptance for NIPS. The paper would benefit from more explicit discussion of how their approach differs from existing work, particularly Sra's earlier result.
