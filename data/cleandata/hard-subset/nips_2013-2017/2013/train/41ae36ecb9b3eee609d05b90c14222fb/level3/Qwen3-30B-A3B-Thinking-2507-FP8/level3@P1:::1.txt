

This paper establishes a linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is a linear operator, and h is strongly convex with Lipschitz gradient. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which enables the authors to prove linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of the loss function f. This extends prior error-bound-based linear convergence results (Tseng [20], Tseng & Yun, Zhang et al.) from ℓ1-norm and group LASSO regularization to the trace norm setting.

The paper makes an important theoretical contribution by resolving a previously open question about whether a Lipschitzian error bound exists for trace norm regularization. Unlike the ℓ1-norm, the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, making standard splitting techniques inapplicable. The authors overcome this by proving that optimal solutions X̄ and -∇f(X̄) share the same left and right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2), allowing them to use matrix perturbation theory to establish the error bound. This is a non-trivial technical advance that could be of independent interest.

The paper demonstrates the theoretical results through two numerical experiments: matrix completion and matrix classification. However, the empirical validation is insufficient as requested by reviewers. The authors fail to include a semilog plot of the objective value versus iterations showing linear convergence, which would have clearly demonstrated the theoretical claims. The provided figures show convergence behavior but don't explicitly verify the linear rate as required.

The paper has several strengths: the theoretical development is sound and well-structured, the error bound is novel and well-motivated, and the paper properly relates to previous work in NIPS and elsewhere. The proofs are mostly clear and technically rigorous, though some steps could be clarified (e.g., the sequence of inequalities before equation 13).

However, the paper has significant weaknesses. The contribution appears to be primarily a technical extension of existing work rather than a fundamentally new approach. The outline of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely mirrors Tseng's framework [10], and the paper would benefit from more precise differentiation from this prior work. The paper does not adequately discuss when the theoretical assumptions fail but empirical linear convergence still occurs, which would have strengthened the theoretical contribution. The paper also does not address the request to outline how the analysis might extend to ADMM.

The constants κ1, κ2, κ3 in the convergence rate analysis are not clearly explained in terms of the step size parameters α and ᾱ, which would have made the theoretical results more practically useful. The presentation of the proof has minor technical issues, such as the confusing sequence of inequalities before equation 13, which could be clarified by referencing the convexity argument from [20].

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis, but the empirical validation is insufficient.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written, but some technical steps could be clarified, particularly regarding the constants in the convergence rate.
- Originality: The error bound for trace norm regularization is novel, but the overall contribution is primarily a technical extension of existing error-bound frameworks.
- Significance: The results advance the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization, but the practical significance is somewhat limited without stronger empirical validation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Establishes a linear convergence rate for PGM under weaker assumptions than prior work
- Provides a novel error bound for trace norm regularization that may be of independent interest
- The theoretical contribution is sound and well-motivated
- The paper properly contextualizes its work within the literature

Arguments against acceptance:
- Contribution appears to be primarily a technical extension rather than a fundamentally new approach
- Insufficient empirical validation (missing semilog plot showing linear convergence)
- Fails to address reviewer requests for discussion of when assumptions fail but convergence still occurs
- Inadequate discussion of extension to ADMM as requested
- Some technical steps in the proofs could be clarified

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of first-order methods for trace norm regularization, but its impact is somewhat limited by the lack of comprehensive empirical validation and insufficient discussion of practical implications. The authors should address the reviewer requests before final acceptance, particularly by providing the requested numerical evidence and clarifying the relationship to prior work. The paper would benefit from stronger empirical validation and a more thorough discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results.
