This paper investigates the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, a common task in machine learning applications like matrix completion and low-rank matrix recovery. The authors demonstrate linear convergence *without* requiring strong convexity of the loss function, a significant extension of existing results. The core of their approach lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, which, combined with the powerful analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (2010), yields the desired convergence guarantee. A key technical contribution is an observation allowing for the simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, simplifying the analysis.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of existing literature on proximal gradient methods, convex optimization, and matrix completion/regularization. It correctly positions itself within this landscape, citing relevant works such as Beck and Teboulle (2009) on iterative shrinkage-thresholding, Recht et al. (2010) on nuclear norm minimization, and Koltchinskii et al. (2011) on optimal rates for matrix completion. The connection to Luo and Tseng’s framework is clearly articulated.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technically Sound:** The paper appears largely technically sound. The proof structure seems logical, and the derivations appear correct. The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of the relevant mathematical tools and techniques.
*   **Novelty:** The simultaneous diagonalization observation is a genuinely interesting and novel technical contribution. While the overall approach builds on existing work, this insight simplifies the analysis considerably.
*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The extension of linear convergence guarantees to non-strongly convex loss functions is a valuable theoretical result.
*   **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The paper is generally well-written and follows a logical structure. The notation is mostly consistent, and the explanations are reasonably clear.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental Contribution:** While the result is valuable, it feels somewhat incremental. The framework largely follows established methods, and the practical impact of purely asymptotic linear convergence is limited.
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper could benefit from a reorganization. The current presentation buries the main results (linear convergence) behind the technical details of the local error bound. I suggest stating the key convergence claim upfront, followed by a high-level overview of the approach, *then* delving into the technical details of the error bound.
*   **Missing Justification:** In the appendix (line 85), the authors state α < 1 implies R(X^k) <= κ3 ||X^k - X^{k+1}||_F. This requires justification. The choice of α and its relationship to κ3 needs to be explicitly explained.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the numerical experiments demonstrate the linear convergence behavior, they are relatively limited in scope and do not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm's performance in various settings.
*   **Related Work Oversight:** The reviewer notes a relevant result by Suvrit Sra at NIPS 2012 (Lemma 4) which demonstrates the inequality |R_k| <= κ |X_k - X_{k+1}|. The authors should acknowledge and discuss this prior work.

**Specific Questions/Suggestions:**

*   Please provide a justification for the statement on line 85 of the appendix regarding the relationship between α, κ3, and the residual norm.
*   Discuss the connection to Sra’s work (NIPS 2012) and acknowledge the prior establishment of a similar inequality.
*   Consider reorganizing the paper to prioritize the presentation of the main linear convergence result.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a sound theoretical result that extends the known convergence guarantees for the PGM to a broader class of problems.
*   The technical novelty of the simultaneous diagonalization argument is valuable.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The contribution is somewhat incremental, and the practical impact of the purely asymptotic convergence result may be limited.
*   The clarity of presentation could be improved by prioritizing the main results.
*   The lack of a thorough empirical evaluation raises concerns about the practical relevance of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The theoretical contribution is valuable, and the paper is technically sound. However, the incremental nature of the work, coupled with the minor clarity issues and the limited empirical validation, prevents me from giving it a stronger recommendation. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly the justification in the appendix and discussion of Sra’s work, would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors should consider carefully whether the benefits of the result outweigh the limitations in its practical applicability.
