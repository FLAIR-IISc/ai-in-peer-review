This paper presents a significant result: the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, under conditions notably weaker than strong convexity of the loss function. This is a valuable contribution, as these problems arise frequently in machine learning applications like matrix completion and classification. The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical result and empirical validation. The proofs appear technically sound and build upon established techniques.

The core idea revolves around establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, which allows application of the convergence analysis framework developed by Luo and Tseng [10]. This addresses an open question concerning the applicability of such bounds to the trace norm, which differs fundamentally from the more commonly studied ℓ1-norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph. The authors successfully navigate this difficulty via a clever analysis of the spectral structure of solutions close to the optimum.

A key strength of the work is its clear connection to prior research. The paper carefully positions its contribution within the existing literature on proximal gradient methods, convex optimization, and low-rank matrix recovery. The detailed discussion of related work is comprehensive and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the field. The empirical results provided in Sections 5, on both matrix completion and matrix classification, provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical relevance of the method.  The observation of linear convergence in practice reinforces the theoretical guarantees.

However, the paper could benefit from a more explicit and detailed analogy to the well-established results for the vector ℓ1-norm. While the authors mention the difference in epigraphs, a more direct comparison outlining *why* existing techniques for ℓ1-norm regularization (e.g., those leveraging group sparsity or variable splitting) do not readily translate to the trace norm case would strengthen the argument for the novelty of their approach. It would be beneficial to explicitly state the ℓ1-norm result as a corollary of their trace-norm result, and discuss whether the generalization is straightforward or requires substantial modifications. This would better highlight the contribution’s significance.

The title, "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization," could be improved. The primary innovation isn’t inherent to the trace norm itself, but rather the handling of the loss function *through* a linear operator A. A title like "Linear Convergence of PGM for Composite Problems with Trace Norm Regularization" or similar might be more accurate.

Several minor points could further improve the clarity of the paper. The notation ‘P’ is used in the matrix completion section (line 337). It would be helpful to clarify whether this represents a sampling operator (implying a random process) or a fixed linear operator. Furthermore, reference [337] appears to be incomplete. The paper would benefit from briefly recalling the definitions of Q-linear and R-linear convergence for readers less familiar with these terms. Finally, clarifying the relationship between the residual *R(X)* defined in equation (7) and Tseng’s original definition (46 in [20]) would aid in interpreting the practical implications of the error bound. The justification for the technical Lemma 3.2, before its statement, could also be slightly more expansive.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:**  The paper establishes a new linear convergence result for trace norm regularization without strong convexity assumptions, filling a gap in the existing literature.
*   **Significance:** The result has direct relevance to a broad range of machine learning and signal processing applications.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-supported.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments corroborate the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection to existing ℓ1-norm results, while acknowledged, could be made more explicit and insightful.
*   Minor clarity issues regarding notation and definitions.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of convex optimization and its application to machine learning.  The theoretical results are significant and the empirical evidence is convincing. While there are some minor areas for improvement, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points outlined above.
