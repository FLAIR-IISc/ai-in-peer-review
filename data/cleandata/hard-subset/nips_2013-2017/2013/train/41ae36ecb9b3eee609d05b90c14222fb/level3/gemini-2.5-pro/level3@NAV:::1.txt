This paper addresses the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized optimization problems. The main contribution is a proof of linear convergence for problems where the loss function has the form f(X) = h(A(X)), with h being strongly convex on any compact set and having a Lipschitz gradient, but without requiring f itself to be strongly convex. This work builds upon and generalizes the line of "error-bound" based convergence analysis previously applied to lasso and group lasso problems by Tseng, Tseng & Yun, and Zhang et al. The key technical ingredient is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace-norm regularized objective, which is of independent interest.

The primary strength of the paper is its rigorous theoretical result. Establishing linear convergence for this important class of problems, which includes applications like matrix completion, is a valuable contribution. The proofs are technically non-trivial and generally well-presented and clear.

However, the contribution appears to be a technical extension of an existing and powerful analysis framework to a new problem setting, which slightly limits its originality. The main weakness is the lack of comprehensive empirical validation. While the experiments confirm the theory, they do not explore its boundaries or limitations.

To strengthen the manuscript for the final version, I recommend the following:
1.  **Differentiating from Prior Work:** The proof outlines for Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely follow Tseng [20]. The authors should more explicitly cite and discuss how their analysis adapts the original arguments for the unique challenges of the trace norm.
2.  **Clarity on Constants:** Please clarify the dependency of the convergence rate constants κ1,…,κ4 on the step-size bounds α̲ and ᾱ.
3.  **Proof Detail:** The sequence of inequalities before Eq. (13) is slightly confusing. The result appears to follow more directly from convexity and the subgradient condition -Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X||_* as in [20, p.289].
4.  **Empirical and Theoretical Scope:** The paper would be significantly improved by including a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex, demonstrating linear convergence for different stability levels. This could be shown effectively with a semilog plot of the objective versus iterations. Importantly, a discussion on when the theoretical assumptions might fail while linear convergence is still empirically observed would be very insightful. Finally, a brief discussion on how this analysis could be extended to other relevant algorithms like ADMM would broaden the paper's impact.
