This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of convex optimization by establishing the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving a class of trace norm-regularized problems. The authors' main claim is that the PGM converges linearly for a large class of loss functions, which is a substantial extension of previous results.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, including the background and motivation. They also provide a clear explanation of the main result and its significance.

The technical contribution of the paper is non-trivial and appears to be primarily a technical extension of existing work. However, the authors' use of a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm-regularized problems is a key innovation that enables them to establish the linear convergence result.

One of the strengths of the paper is that it establishes the linear convergence of the PGM for a wide range of loss functions, including square loss and logistic loss. This makes the result applicable to many settings in machine learning.

However, one of the weaknesses of the paper is that it lacks sufficient empirical validation. While the authors provide some numerical experiments, they are limited to two specific problems (matrix completion and matrix classification), and more extensive experiments would be needed to fully demonstrate the effectiveness of the PGM.

Another weakness is that the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the constants in the error bound depend on the choice of the regularization parameter. This could be an important consideration in practice, and the authors should provide more guidance on how to choose the regularization parameter to achieve good performance.

In terms of originality, the paper is not entirely new, as it builds on previous work by Tseng and others. However, the authors' use of a new Lipschitzian error bound and their extension of the linear convergence result to a wider range of loss functions are significant contributions.

Overall, I would rate the paper as follows:

* Quality: 8/10 (the paper is technically sound, but could benefit from more empirical validation and a clearer explanation of the constants in the error bound)
* Clarity: 9/10 (the paper is well-written and clearly organized, but could benefit from more detailed explanations of some of the technical points)
* Originality: 7/10 (the paper builds on previous work, but makes significant contributions through the use of a new Lipschitzian error bound and the extension of the linear convergence result to a wider range of loss functions)
* Significance: 8/10 (the paper establishes a significant result that is applicable to many settings in machine learning, but could benefit from more extensive empirical validation)

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should provide more empirical validation and a clearer explanation of the constants in the error bound. They should also consider providing more guidance on how to choose the regularization parameter to achieve good performance.
