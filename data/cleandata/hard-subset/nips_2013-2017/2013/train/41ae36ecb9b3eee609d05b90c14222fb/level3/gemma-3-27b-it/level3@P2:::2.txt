This paper presents a compelling theoretical result: the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems, under assumptions significantly weaker than strong convexity of the loss function. This is a notable contribution, as PGM is a widely used algorithm in machine learning and related fields, and establishing tighter convergence guarantees is crucial for practical applications. The problem addressed – recovering low-rank matrices – is central to numerous applications, including matrix completion, collaborative filtering, and subspace learning.

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, carefully outlining prior work and motivating their contribution. The technical details are presented with precision, and the proof structure is logical and easy to follow. The inclusion of numerical experiments further strengthens the paper, demonstrating the practical relevance of the theoretical findings. The authors appropriately reference relevant literature, demonstrating awareness of the state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Result:** The linear convergence guarantee under weak assumptions is a significant theoretical advancement.
*   **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is remarkably well-written and easy to understand, even for readers not intimately familiar with the specific details of proximal gradient methods.
*   **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem of trace norm regularization is highly relevant to a wide range of machine learning applications.
*   **Rigorous Proof:** The mathematical proof appears technically sound and is presented with sufficient detail.
*   **Supporting Experiments:** The numerical experiments provide empirical validation of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Novelty Relative to l1-Norm Results:** While the authors acknowledge related work on l1-norm regularization (e.g., [20, 22]), the paper would benefit from a more explicit comparison. The connection between the trace norm and the l1-norm is fundamental, and a clearer discussion of *why* existing l1-norm techniques don’t directly translate to the trace norm case would enhance the understanding of the paper’s contribution. Specifically, a statement about whether the l1-norm results can be directly obtained as a corollary would be beneficial. While the difficulty stemming from the non-polyhedral epigraph is mentioned, a more detailed discussion regarding the inability to split variables as in the l1-norm case would strengthen the argument for the novelty.
*   **Title:** The title, “On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization,” uses the term "structured." This is somewhat misleading, as the core contribution lies in the properties of the *loss function* (specifically the linear operator A) rather than the trace norm regularizer itself. A more descriptive title, such as "Linear Convergence of PGM for Trace Norm Regularized Problems with Linear Operators," might be more accurate.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The notation 'P' at line 337 is ambiguous—is it a sampling operator (implying randomness) or a fixed mask? This requires clarification. The reference at line 337 is broken. A brief recall of the definitions of Q- and R-linear convergence would improve readability. Finally, relating the residual R(X) defined in equation (7) to Tseng’s original definition (46) in [20] would aid in interpretation.
* **Justification of Lemma 3.2:** Section 3 presents a key technical lemma, but a more extensive discussion of its implications and how it bridges the gap between the error bound and the linear convergence rate would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental problem in optimization and machine learning.
*   The theoretical result is significant and likely to have a substantial impact.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly presented.
*   The numerical experiments provide strong support for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The novelty relative to existing results on l1-norm regularization could be more clearly articulated.
*   Minor clarity issues detract slightly from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical result is significant, the paper is well-written, and the numerical experiments are convincing. While the authors should address the concerns regarding the novelty relative to l1-norm results and the minor clarity issues, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The revisions should focus on expanding the discussion of the connection to l1-norm regularization, clarifying the notation, fixing the broken reference, providing definitions for Q- and R-linear convergence, and offering further insights into the role of Lemma 3.2. These enhancements would elevate the paper from excellent to outstanding.
