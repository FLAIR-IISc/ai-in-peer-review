

This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is strongly convex on compact sets with Lipschitz gradient. The main contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, which extends prior error-bound based linear convergence results from ℓ1/group LASSO to trace norm. The authors prove that under their assumptions, PGM converges R-linearly to the optimal solution set.

The paper's strengths include a technically sound proof framework, clear presentation of the main results, and meaningful extension of prior work. The error bound for trace-norm regularization is novel and could be of independent interest. The numerical experiments in Section 5 provide semi-log plots showing linear convergence for matrix completion and classification problems, which partially addresses the empirical validation concern.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that need addressing. First, the proof of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 3.2 closely mirrors Tseng's framework without sufficient differentiation; the authors should explicitly cite and clarify how their approach differs from [20]. Second, the dependencies of constants κ1,...,κ4 on the step size bounds α̲ and ᾱ are unclear and need clarification. Third, the sequence of inequalities before (13) is confusing; the final inequality follows from convexity and -Ḡ ∈ τ∂‖X‖_* rather than the intermediate step shown. 

Most critically, the paper lacks sufficient discussion of when the theoretical assumptions (e.g., strong convexity of h) fail but empirical linear convergence still occurs, which is requested in the key points. The authors should add a simulation where the loss is not strongly convex but PGM still converges linearly (for different stability levels) and discuss the failure cases of the theory. Additionally, the paper should outline how the analysis might extend to ADMM, as requested.

While the technical contribution is non-trivial, the paper appears to be primarily a technical extension of existing work without sufficient empirical validation or discussion of theoretical limitations. The authors should address these points to strengthen the paper's contribution and significance.
