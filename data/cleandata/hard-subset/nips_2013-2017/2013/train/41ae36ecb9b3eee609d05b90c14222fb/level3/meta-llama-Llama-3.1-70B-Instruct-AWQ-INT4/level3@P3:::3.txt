This paper studies the linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized risk minimization. The authors extend the linear convergence result beyond strong convexity by following Luo and Tseng's framework and proving a local error bound. The key technical novelty is an observation allowing simultaneous diagonalization of matrices, reducing the problem to the vector case.

The paper's main contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem. This bound is established without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, which is a common assumption in the literature. The authors also provide a comprehensive proof of the error bound, which is based on a careful analysis of the spectral properties of the matrices involved.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and the technical sections are well-organized and easy to understand. The proof of the error bound is detailed and thorough, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the key steps and insights.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the result is mainly theoretical, with limited algorithmic impact. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that a future direction is to study error bounds for more general matrix norm-regularized problems and their implications on the convergence rates of first-order methods.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is moderate. While the simultaneous diagonalization observation is new and interesting, most of the approach follows existing work. The authors build on the framework developed by Luo and Tseng, and the proof of the error bound is based on a careful analysis of the spectral properties of the matrices involved.

The significance of the paper is mainly theoretical, as it provides a new insight into the convergence properties of the PGM for trace norm-regularized problems. However, the result may have limited practical significance, as the asymptotic nature of the convergence result may not provide a clear advantage over existing methods in practice.

Overall, I would assess the paper as making an incremental contribution to the field. The result is technically sound and well-presented, but its significance and originality are moderate.
