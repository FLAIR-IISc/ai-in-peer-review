This paper studies proximal gradient methods for trace-norm regularized risk minimization minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where f(X)=h(A(X)), A is linear, and h is strictly convex with Lipschitz gradient and exhibits curvature (strong convexity on compact subsets). The main technical contribution is a Lipschitzian local error bound: near the solution set X, the distance dist(X,X) is upper bounded by a constant times the residual ||R(X)||F, where R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X. Leveraging this bound and the Luo–Tseng framework, the paper proves asymptotic linear convergence of proximal gradient (Q-linear in objective, R-linear in iterates) under step sizes bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf, without assuming strong convexity of f.

Relation to prior work: Sublinear rates for (accelerated) proximal gradient are classical (Nesterov), and linear rates are known under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011). Local error bounds and their role in linear convergence were developed by Luo & Tseng and subsequently specialized to polyhedral and group-structured regularizers (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO; Tseng, Tseng–Yun, Zhang–Jiang–Luo). The novelty here is establishing a comparable bound for the nonpolyhedral trace norm, a case left open despite its similarity to ℓ1. The authors’ key observation is that at optimality X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, allowing a reduction to a diagonal (singular-value) analysis via matrix perturbation, which in turn yields the desired error bound and hence linear rates for many commonly used losses (square, logistic) even when A is not injective.

Quality: The results appear technically sound and the main argument is coherent: (i) prove a local Lipschitz error bound on a suitable level set, (ii) verify descent and residual relations for proximal gradient with fixed step sizes, and (iii) invoke the Luo–Tseng mechanism to obtain Q-linear decrease in values and R-linear convergence of iterates. The spectral alignment at optimality and its careful use to control nearby perturbations are well executed.

That said, I identified a gap that should be fixed. The inequality ‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk − Xk+1‖F (Theorem 3.2, eq. (11)) needs a transparent justification; the appendix (line 085) currently asserts it with an appeal to step sizes (e.g., α<1) but lacks a derivation. This relation follows from standard proximal-gradient mapping properties; see, e.g., S. Sra, NIPS 2012, Lemma 4, or provide a self-contained proof by defining the proximal-gradient mapping Gα(X)=(1/α)(X−proxατ||·||*(X−α∇f(X))) and relating R(X) (defined with α=1) to X−X+ via Lipschitz continuity of the shrinkage operator in both its argument and threshold, along with bounds on αk∈[α,ᾱ]. Clarifying constants and assumptions here would strengthen the presentation.

Clarity: The paper is readable but could be better organized for a NIPS audience:
- State the main claims (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) and their scope early in the introduction. Emphasize “asymptotic” and the locality of the error bound (residual ≤ ε on a bounded level set).
- Move most of the technical proof of the error bound to the appendix, keeping a high-level proof roadmap in the main text.
- Unify the definition of the residual/proximal-gradient mapping with the step sizes used in the algorithm, or explicitly relate the two.
- Minor expository suggestions: summarize Assumption 2.1(b)’s implication (strict convexity, Lipschitz gradient) when first used; clearly define the basin of attraction where the bound holds; tighten the discussion around Proposition 4.2.

Originality: Moderate. The overarching proof technique closely follows established templates (error bounds + Luo–Tseng). The key novelty is the simultaneous singular-vector alignment at optimality and the resulting perturbation analysis for the trace norm, which seems new and resolves a previously open (nonpolyhedral) case. The linear-rate result without strong convexity of f for trace-norm regularization is, to my knowledge, not previously shown.

Significance: Primarily theoretical. The error bound itself may be useful beyond this paper (e.g., for analyzing other first-order schemes, or stopping criteria). The linear rate is asymptotic and local; in practice, global complexity remains sublinear, and accelerated methods may dominate away from the solution. The experiments are small-scale but consistent with the theory; they illustrate linear tails but do not quantify the neighborhood where the bound becomes active or compare with alternatives (e.g., accelerated PG, SVT variants).

Pros for acceptance:
- Resolves an open question by establishing a local Lipschitz error bound for trace-norm regularization.
- Clean, broadly applicable linear-rate result for a widely used class of losses without strong convexity.
- Sound analysis leveraging a neat spectral alignment insight that may have independent interest.

Cons against acceptance:
- Incremental at a high level; the overall framework is standard and the linear rate is asymptotic/local.
- Experimental section is limited; lacks diagnostics of the error bound (e.g., plotting dist vs residual) or comparisons to accelerated methods/other solvers.
- A key inequality linking residual to iterate difference is asserted but not properly derived; relies on known results without citation or proof.
- Clarity could be improved; main claims and scope should be front-loaded, and technical details deferred.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Provide a precise proof (or citation) for ‖R(Xk)‖F ≤ κ3‖Xk−Xk+1‖F. One route: define the proximal-gradient mapping with αk and show ||R(Xk)||F ≤ c1||Xk−proxτ(Xk−∇f(Xk))||F ≤ c2||Xk−Xk+1||F using Lipschitzness of Sμ in both μ and its argument and αk∈[α,ᾱ]⊂(0,1/Lf).
- Clarify the locality in Theorem 3.1: specify how ε and η depend on the level set bound ζ, and discuss how backtracking step sizes satisfy the assumptions in practice.
- Consider adding experiments that (i) plot dist(Xk,X) vs ||R(Xk)||F to empirically validate the error bound, (ii) compare to accelerated PG and to methods tailored for nuclear norm (e.g., SVT/ALM), and (iii) test sensitivity to non-injective A.
- Minor: fix small typos, standardize notation (e.g., consistently denote Lipschitz constants Lh vs Lf), and explicitly state that the simultaneous singular-vector property (Proposition 4.2) hinges on optimality conditions.

Overall assessment: A solid, mostly well-executed theoretical contribution that extends a known linear-convergence template to a nonpolyhedral setting via a new spectral observation. The contribution is moderate in originality and practical impact. With the clarity fixes and the missing inequality addressed, I would view it more favorably; as it stands, I lean toward a borderline reject, primarily due to the incremental nature and limited empirical validation.
