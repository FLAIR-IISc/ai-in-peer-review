- Proves linear convergence of proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm regularized problems f(X)+τ||X||* where f(X)=h(A(X)) without requiring global strong convexity, covering tasks like matrix completion and multi-task learning.

- Main technical contribution: a new Lipschitzian error bound linking distance to the optimal set with a residual measuring optimality violation, extending error-bound analysis to the non-polyhedral, non-separable trace norm.

- First theoretical guarantee of linear convergence for standard PGM on this class without strong convexity, explaining empirically fast PGM behavior in practical underdetermined settings.

- Technical novelty: uses matrix perturbation theory; key structural insight (Prop. 4.2) that an optimal X* and -∇f(X*) share singular vectors, enabling spectral analysis near the optimum.

- Paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear motivation, upfront statement of results, and a readable proof roadmap.

- Context/originality concern: the overall convergence framework closely follows Luo & Tseng’s analysis; the paper should more explicitly separate which proof elements are novel versus direct applications.

- Proof clarity issues: make explicit how constants κ1, κ2, κ3 depend on step-size bounds α and ᾱ, and simplify/cite the step leading to inequality (18).

- Empirical validation is limited: experiments should include instances where f is clearly not strongly convex (e.g., highly underdetermined A) to directly demonstrate the claimed linear convergence.

- Suggest exploring the boundaries of the theory (cases violating assumptions but showing linear rates) via experiments that vary problem “stability.”

- Suggest extending or discussing applicability of the analysis to other first-order methods (e.g., ADMM) to broaden impact.

- Arguments for acceptance: first linear-convergence proof without strong convexity, novel and non-trivial error bound, and clear exposition.

- Arguments against acceptance: may be viewed as a technical extension of existing frameworks and empirical results could be more compelling.

- Overall recommendation: strong paper with significant technical novelty and minor addressable weaknesses; recommend acceptance.
