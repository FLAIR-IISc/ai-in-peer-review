

# Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for trace-norm regularized problems. The authors establish linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for a class of trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, which resolves an open question about applying Tseng's error bound technique to nuclear norm problems.

The main ideas build upon Tseng's framework [20] but extend it to handle the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The authors demonstrate that when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, the PGM exhibits linear convergence. This addresses an important gap in the literature, as many machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.) use loss functions that are not strongly convex yet benefit from linear convergence guarantees.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with a carefully constructed proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1), which is the paper's main contribution. The proof correctly extends Tseng's approach to the trace norm case, overcoming the challenge that the trace norm's spectral structure cannot be decomposed into independent components as with the ℓ1-norm. The numerical experiments in Section 5 provide good empirical support for the theoretical results, showing linear convergence in practice for both matrix completion and matrix classification problems. The paper properly acknowledges the limitations of the assumptions and carefully justifies them in the context of machine learning applications.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem setup to main results. The authors provide sufficient background on the proximal gradient method, trace norm regularization, and error bounds. However, some minor clarifications would improve readability: (1) The residual map R(X) defined in (6) should be explicitly related to Tseng's original definition (46) for better interpretation; (2) The distinction between Q-linear and R-linear convergence should be briefly recalled for reader clarity; (3) The notation "P" in the matrix completion experiments (Section 5) should be clarified as a fixed mask operator rather than implying randomness.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by extending Tseng's error bound technique to the trace norm case. While the ℓ1-norm case is well-studied [20,22], the trace norm case has remained open due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph. The authors' key insight—that optimal solutions and gradients share singular vectors—enables the proof of the error bound. The l1-norm result should follow as a corollary of the trace-norm result, but the paper does not explicitly discuss this connection. The authors should clarify the relationship between their result and the existing ℓ1-norm results to better position their contribution.

**Significance**: This work addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning and signal processing where trace-norm regularization is widely used. The linear convergence result applies to many important loss functions (square loss, logistic loss) without requiring strong convexity, which is a significant improvement over prior results. This has immediate implications for practical applications where matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification are used.

**Specific Suggestions**:
1. The title should be revised to remove "structured" since the main contribution concerns the loss side (linear operator A) rather than the regularizer.
2. The authors should explicitly discuss why the l1-norm techniques do not directly translate to the trace norm case, highlighting the fundamental differences between the ℓ1-norm's polyhedral epigraph and the trace norm's non-polyhedral structure.
3. The proof of Lemma 3.2 in Section 3 would benefit from more discussion of its intuition and necessity for the overall proof.
4. The broken reference at line 337 should be corrected.
5. The authors should clarify whether "P" in the matrix completion experiments is a sampling or mask operator (the paper currently uses it as a fixed operator, not implying randomness).

**Strengths**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis that resolves an important open question, with a careful proof that appears correct. The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings. The writing is clear and well-organized.

**Weaknesses**: The relationship between the trace-norm result and the existing l1-norm results is not sufficiently discussed. The title could be more precise. Some minor technical details (like the definition of P) need clarification.

**Arguments for Acceptance**: This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that resolves an open question in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for trace-norm regularized problems. The results are applicable to many important machine learning applications and represent a meaningful extension of Tseng's error bound technique.

**Arguments against Acceptance**: The novelty is somewhat limited in that the result is an extension of existing techniques to a new setting, and the relationship to the l1-norm case could be better articulated. However, the technical challenge of extending to the trace norm is non-trivial and the result is valuable.

This paper makes a strong contribution to the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for matrix optimization problems. With minor revisions to clarify the relationship to the ℓ1-norm case, improve the title, and address the minor technical points, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The results are both theoretically sound and practically relevant to important machine learning problems. I recommend acceptance.
