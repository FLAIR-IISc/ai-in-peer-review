This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized optimization problems, specifically minimizing `F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*`, where `f(X) = h(A(X))` with a linear operator `A` and a strongly convex function `h`. The core contribution is establishing linear convergence of the PGM *without* requiring strong convexity of `f` itself, a significant extension of prior work (e.g., Tseng [20], Tseng & Yun, Zhang et al.).  This is achieved via a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem, which the authors argue is of independent interest.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of convex optimization theory and prior results on PGM convergence. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing linear convergence guarantees (typically requiring strong convexity) in common machine learning applications like matrix completion where the loss function may not be strongly convex. The proof strategy leverages matrix perturbation theory to address the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm, a key distinction from analyses of ℓ1 regularization.

A major strength of the paper is the rigorous theoretical analysis and the extension of known results to a wider class of problems. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the relevant literature and carefully articulate the assumptions required for their convergence result. The proposed error bound appears to be a valuable technical contribution. 

However, the paper suffers from a perceived lack of thorough empirical validation. While the authors present simulation results for matrix completion and matrix classification, these experiments primarily *demonstrate* convergence, but do not systematically investigate the relationship between the theoretical assumptions (specifically, the smoothness and curvature conditions on `h`) and the observed convergence rate. Specifically, the authors should present a simulation illustrating linear convergence with a loss function which is *not* strongly convex, and include plots showcasing convergence for varying levels of stability (e.g., different regularization parameters or noise levels). 

Furthermore, the relationship to existing work, particularly Tseng [20], could be made more explicit. The outlines of Theorem 3.1 and Lemma 4.1 bear a striking resemblance to Tseng’s approach; the authors should provide a more detailed comparison, emphasizing precisely how their contributions differentiate from the original work and offer new insights.  Clarifying how the constants κ1 through κ4 in Theorem 3.2 depend on the chosen step sizes α̲ and ᾱ would also strengthen the analysis.

A minor technical point: the sequence of inequalities preceding equation (13) appears to contain a non-obvious step. The stated derivation seems to overlook the use of convexity and the optimality condition –Ḡ ∈ τ∂||X||* [Rockafellar & Wets, 20].

Finally, the authors should briefly discuss scenarios where the theoretical assumptions might be violated (e.g., `h` is not strongly convex on compact subsets) but empirical linear convergence is still observed. Exploring this discrepancy could reveal valuable insights into the robustness of the proposed method.  Moreover, briefly outlining how the analysis might be extended to the alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM), a common algorithm for solving such problems, would further enhance the paper's impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contribution extending prior work on PGM convergence.
*   Addresses a practical limitation of existing theory by removing the strong convexity requirement.
*   Rigorous proof and clear presentation of the main result.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited empirical validation beyond demonstrating convergence.
*   Insufficiently detailed comparison with related work (Tseng [20] in particular).
*   Lack of discussion regarding the robustness of the theory to violations of assumptions and extension to ADMM.
*   Minor technical issues in the proof outlining.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The theoretical contribution is solid and potentially valuable. However, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation and the need for further clarification regarding related work and robustness limit its impact. Addressing the reviewer’s comments regarding simulations, comparison with existing work, and discussion of the theory’s limitations would significantly strengthen the paper.
