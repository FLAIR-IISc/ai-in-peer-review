Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies proximal gradient methods for convex risk minimization with a trace-norm (nuclear norm) regularizer, F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with a linear A and a strictly convex, smooth h that is strongly convex on compact sets. The main contribution is a Lipschitzian local error bound of the form dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F in a neighborhood, where R(X) = proxτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X is the residual map, and a consequent asymptotic linear convergence rate for the proximal gradient method under fixed step sizes αk ∈ (α, ᾱ) ⊂ (0, 1/Lf). The analysis follows the Luo–Tseng framework for error bounds and linear rates, extending prior results known for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group Lasso) to the nonpolyhedral trace norm.

The key technical idea is an observation (Proposition 4.2) that at any optimal solution X̄, the matrices X̄ and −∇f(X̄) admit simultaneous SVDs (shared left/right singular vectors). This allows the authors to reduce local spectral perturbation arguments to the vector case, enabling the desired error bound.

The work relates to: Luo and Tseng’s error-bound-based linear convergence theory; error bounds for ℓ1/group Lasso (Tseng, Tseng–Yun, Zhang–Jiang–Luo); and matrix shrinkage methods (Toh–Yun; Ma–Goldfarb–Chen). The inequality linking the residual to the step difference, ||Rk|| ≤ κ||Xk − Xk+1||, is standard in proximal-gradient analyses and was proved in Sra, NIPS 2012 (Lemma 4) for a closely related setting.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: Establishes a local error bound for trace-norm regularization without strong convexity of f, and uses it to prove asymptotic linear convergence of PGM. The simultaneous-diagonalization observation is neat and effectively reduces the matrix analysis to a vector one.
- Soundness: The main line of argument (local error bound + Luo–Tseng framework) is standard and appears correct. The perturbation/structure lemmas are carefully set up.
- Scope: The assumptions on h (smooth, strictly convex, strongly convex on compact sets) cover commonly used losses (squared, logistic) and linear operators A with nontrivial nullspaces.
- Experiments: Plots illustrate linear decay in a neighborhood on matrix completion and matrix classification with logistic loss.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Gap in the appendix (line 085): The paper claims there is κ3 > 0 such that ||R(Xk)||F ≤ κ3||Xk − Xk+1||F (equation (11)). Given R(X) = proxτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X, while Xk+1 = proxταk(Xk − αk∇f(Xk)), this bound needs justification that accounts for the different step sizes αk and the scaling of the proximal operator. A short proof (e.g., via firm nonexpansiveness and Lipschitz continuity of ∇f with α ∈ (0, 1/Lf), or via a proximal-gradient mapping equivalence) should be added, or explicitly cite S. Sra, NIPS 2012, Lemma 4, which proves an inequality of the form ||Rk|| ≤ κ||Xk − Xk+1|| under similar conditions. Please make κ3’s dependence on (α, ᾱ, Lf) explicit.
- Attribution: The inequality ||Rk|| ≤ κ||Xk − Xk+1|| has prior art (Sra, NIPS 2012, Lemma 4). The paper should cite it where (11) is stated/derived.
- Asymptotic nature: The linear rate holds locally; the paper does not quantify the size of the neighborhood (ε in Theorem 3.1), nor does it provide explicit rate constants (ρ in (12)). This limits practical guidance.
- Algorithmic significance: The result does not alter the algorithm or yield improved global rates; practical step-size selection and stopping criteria are unchanged. The experiments are illustrative but limited and do not compare against accelerated methods (e.g., FISTA) or alternative solvers.
- Clarity/organization: The main claims (local error bound and linear convergence theorem) could be stated prominently in the introduction, followed by an outline of the proof strategy, with technical details (Lemma 4.1, Propositions 4.2/4.3) deferred. This would improve readability. Some constants (η, ε, κ1–κ4) could be summarized with their dependencies on problem/algorithm parameters.

Quality
- Overall technically solid, with a clear path from the local error bound to the linear-rate conclusion. The identified gap around κ3 is fixable by adding a standard lemma/citation. The simultaneous-diagonalization argument appears correct and well-motivated.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but the narrative would benefit from foregrounding the two main results and postponing technicalities. Explicitly state what “asymptotic” means in the theorem, the dependence of rate constants on α, ᾱ, Lf, and ζ, and where the local error bound is valid.

Originality
- Moderate. The proof framework is largely Luo–Tseng’s, and the residual-vs-step inequality is known. The novelty lies in adapting the framework to the trace norm via the simultaneous-diagonalization insight and a careful perturbation analysis.

Significance
- Primarily theoretical. It advances understanding of when PGM enjoys linear rates without strong convexity for matrix problems. Algorithmic impact is limited because the rate is local and constants are not quantified, and the practical behavior often already appears linear near the solution in many applications.

Specific suggestions
- Add a lemma or citation justifying (11): for αk ∈ [α, ᾱ] ⊂ (0, 1/Lf), show ||R(Xk)||F ≤ κ3||Xk − Xk+1||F, with κ3 explicit. Refer to Sra (NIPS 2012, Lemma 4) and adapt the proof to the nuclear norm and variable step sizes.
- Clarify the dependence of η and ε in Theorem 3.1 on ζ, α, ᾱ, Lf, τ, and properties of A and h.
- Move the proof of the local error bound to an appendix; in the main text, present the linear-rate derivation first (using Luo–Tseng) once the error bound is stated.
- Discuss when the local regime is entered in practice (e.g., after backtracking stabilizes), and provide guidance on detecting it via the residual norm.
- Expand experiments: compare PGM to accelerated PGM (FISTA) and possibly proximal quasi-Newton; report iteration counts, wall-clock times, and ranks of iterates.

Pros
- Correct and careful extension of Luo–Tseng error-bound theory to the trace norm.
- Insightful spectral argument (simultaneous diagonalization).
- Applicable to common losses beyond strong convexity.

Cons
- Key inequality (residual vs. iterate difference) is known; missing explicit justification/citation.
- Asymptotic/local result with unquantified constants; limited practical impact.
- Experiments are illustrative but not comprehensive; no comparisons to strong baselines.

Overall assessment
A solid, incremental theoretical contribution: the local error bound for trace-norm regularization and the resulting asymptotic linear convergence of PGM are of interest to the theory community. The technical novelty is moderate but genuine (simultaneous diagonalization). Addressing the small gap around inequality (11), clarifying constants and scope, and improving presentation would strengthen the paper. I view it as moderately interesting theoretically, with limited algorithmic significance.
