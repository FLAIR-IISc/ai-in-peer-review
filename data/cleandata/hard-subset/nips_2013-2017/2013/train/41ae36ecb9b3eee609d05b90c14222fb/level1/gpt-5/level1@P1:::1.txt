Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies minimizing a smooth convex loss with trace (nuclear) norm regularization via the proximal gradient method (PGM). While PGM typically enjoys sublinear rates O(1/k) or O(1/k^2), linear convergence is known under strong convexity of the smooth part. The authors prove that for a broad class of structured losses f(X)=h(A(X)), with A linear and h strictly convex, C1 with Lipschitz gradient, and strongly convex on compact subsets of dom(h), the standard PGM achieves asymptotic linear convergence without requiring f to be strongly convex (A may be rank-deficient). The central technical contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear-norm–regularized composite problem: dist(X, X*) ≤ η‖Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X‖F for X in a level set and with small residual. This enables application of the Luo-Tseng framework to obtain Q-linear decrease in objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates.

The result addresses an open question: error bounds were established for composite problems with polyhedral nonsmooth terms (ℓ1, group LASSO), but the nuclear norm is nonpolyhedral. The authors overcome the lack of separability by exploiting a structural property at optimality: an optimal solution X* and −∇f(X*) share the same left/right singular vectors. This spectral alignment allows a careful perturbation analysis to establish a restricted invertibility property and the desired error bound. The paper relates to and builds on work on PGM/ISTA/FISTA (Beck & Teboulle; Ma et al.; Toh & Yun), error bounds (Tseng; Luo & Tseng), and low-rank learning.

Quality:
The technical development is careful and largely self-contained. The core lemmas (asymptotic optimality, boundedness, restricted invertibility) and the spectral propositions are clearly stated, with some proofs deferred to the supplement (reasonable for NIPS). The use of the residual map and its link to the optimality fixed point is standard and appropriate. The theorem assumes step sizes bounded away from zero and upper-bounded by 1/Lf, and the analysis yields asymptotic linear rates (via local error bounds). Numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification corroborate linear convergence when near optimality.

Clarity:
The paper is well organized, with clear motivation, setup, and definitions (prox operator, shrinkage). The assumptions on h are explicit and justified via examples (squared and logistic losses). The proof sketch of the main theorem is cogent; the key technical difficulty and its resolution via simultaneous SVD are well explained. Minor notational/formatting issues exist, but overall readability is good for the target audience.

Originality:
The paper presents, to my knowledge, the first linear convergence result for PGM applied to nuclear-norm regularized problems without strong convexity of f, under broad and practically relevant structure. The Lipschitzian error bound for the nuclear norm composite is novel and of independent interest.

Significance:
Error bounds underpin linear convergence of many first-order methods. Establishing such a bound for the nuclear norm broadens our theoretical understanding and has practical implications (e.g., step-size and restart strategies, local rate guarantees). The setting encompasses important applications such as matrix completion and low-rank classification. This work is likely to be cited and built upon (e.g., for inexact prox, stochastic variants, or other matrix norms).

Strengths:
- Establishes an important local linear convergence result without strong convexity for a widely used regularizer.
- Introduces a new error bound technique overcoming nonpolyhedral challenges via spectral alignment.
- Uses a general and influential analysis framework (Luo-Tseng), making the result broadly interpretable.
- Assumptions on h cover common losses; the result applies to many problems in ML.
- Empirical evidence consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The linear rate is asymptotic and local; the constants (η, ε, contraction factor) are not quantified. A discussion on how they depend on problem data (A, τ, L) would enhance practical insight, even qualitatively.
- The step-size condition requires αk ∈ [α, ᾱ] with ᾱ < 1/Lf. While backtracking is used in experiments, the paper should clarify whether the standard backtracking guarantees a uniform positive lower bound α in practice under Lipschitz ∇f, or specify conditions under which it does.
- Practical implementations often compute prox approximately via partial SVDs. Discuss robustness of the error bound and linear rate to inexact prox (e.g., citing Schmidt et al. on inexact PGM).
- Experiments are small-scale and synthetic. Adding at least one real dataset or larger-scale runs, and a comparison with accelerated PGM (to show the transition to local linear behavior and potential benefits of restarts) would strengthen the empirical section.
- The class of losses excludes nonsmooth choices (e.g., hinge). A brief note on whether similar arguments might extend to smoothings or other surrogates would be useful.

Minor comments:
- Ensure all key proofs referenced in the main text are available in the supplement (notably Proposition 4.3). Consider adding a short intuitive explanation of why X* and −∇f(X*) share singular vectors via subdifferential characterization of the nuclear norm.
- Clarify the uniqueness of z̄ and compactness of X (the argument is sound; a short explicit statement helps).
- Typographical and formatting nits: equation numbering consistency; check references formatting and dates; ensure all cited results (e.g., [11, Theorem 3]) match statements used.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and technically solid contribution solving a recognized gap (error bound and linear rate for nuclear-norm composite).
- Broad applicability to ML problems; likely impact on theory and practice.
- Clear exposition and reasonable empirical validation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Asymptotic/local nature with unquantified constants; limited empirical evaluation.
- Some reliance on supplemental proofs for crucial steps.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The paper makes a significant and original theoretical contribution with clear relevance to NIPS, and the presentation is solid. The suggestions above are primarily to improve clarity and practical guidance.
