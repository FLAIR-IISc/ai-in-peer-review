- Main contribution: Shows Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) attains linear convergence for trace-norm regularized problems f(X)=h(A(X)) when h is strictly convex, without requiring strong convexity of f.  
- Improvement over prior art: Strengthens the known sublinear O(1/k^2) rate for general convex functions and extends linear rates beyond the strongly convex case.  
- Key technical novelty: Proves a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace-norm regularization, overcoming non-separability challenges.  
- Proof technique: Uses matrix perturbation theory to handle the trace norm’s non-polyhedral, non-separable structure.  
- Significance: The result is theoretically important and directly relevant to matrix completion, multi-task learning, and other applications using trace norm.  
- Originality and quality: The error bound and analysis are highly original, rigorous, and resolve an open question.  
- Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and provides a clear roadmap for the proof.  
- Weakness: Convergence guarantee is asymptotic and only holds once iterates are sufficiently close to the optimum; the paper lacks characterization of that neighborhood.  
- Weakness: Numerical experiments are limited to small-scale problems and do not fully demonstrate large-scale practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel significant theoretical result, elegant technical contribution of independent interest, and high-quality exposition.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Practical implications are not fully validated on large-scale settings.  
- Overall: Strengths substantially outweigh minor weaknesses; represents a fundamental advance in convergence analysis for structured first-order methods.
