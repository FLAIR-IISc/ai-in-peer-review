Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization of the form minX f(X) + τ||X||*, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth and strictly convex. While proximal gradient methods (PGM) are known to achieve sublinear rates, linear rates typically require strong convexity of f, which is often absent in applications like matrix completion. The authors prove that for a broad class of losses (square, logistic) and arbitrary linear operators A, PGM enjoys asymptotic linear convergence without assuming strong convexity of f. The key technical contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound relating distance to the optimal set to the residual norm ||Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X||F. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, this error bound yields Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates for step sizes bounded away from 0 and less than 1/Lf.

Prior work has established linear convergence for PGM in strongly convex settings (e.g., Schmidt et al. NIPS 2011) and error bounds for polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group lasso). For nuclear norm regularization, existing results were sublinear (e.g., Ji & Ye 2009; Toh & Yun 2010). Error bounds for nuclear norm had remained open due to its nonpolyhedral epigraph. The paper overcomes this by proving a spectral alignment property at optimality (X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors) and using matrix perturbation arguments to control nearby iterates, leading to a restricted invertibility inequality and the desired error bound.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The main theorems are carefully stated. The error bound is local: for points in a bounded level set with sufficiently small residual, dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F. The proof strategy (contradiction via constructing a direction in Null(A) that would decrease the objective unless alignment holds) is convincing and addresses the nonpolyhedral difficulty. The convergence result follows appropriately from Luo–Tseng’s framework, with standard descent and residual bounds. The assumptions on h (C1 gradient, Lipschitz gradient, strict convexity, and strong convexity on compact subsets) are sufficient to obtain positive curvature on bounded sets encountered by the iterates.
- Completeness: The core arguments (Proposition 4.2 and the decomposition in Proposition 4.3) are central; much is relegated to the supplement, but the main ideas are traceable. The step-size conditions are standard; backtracking can ensure a uniform lower bound eventually.
- Empirics: The experiments are minimal and only illustrative (small synthetic matrix completion and classification), showing linear trends in semilog plots near the solution. There is no comparison to accelerated PGM or alternative solvers, no exploration of scalability, noise, or parameter sensitivity.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The role of Assumption 2.1(b) is clearly explained: because level sets are bounded (due to compactness of the optimal set), the iterates stay in a compact subset where h exhibits a uniform curvature σ > 0. The residual map and its interpretation are clear. Some parts of the proof (spectral alignment, cases A/B/C) are highly technical; adding more intuition (e.g., identification of an “active rank manifold” and why Null(A) directions cannot persist near optimality) would help a broader NeurIPS audience.

Originality:
The local Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization is novel and addresses a recognized gap versus ℓ1/group-lasso. The linear convergence guarantee of PGM without strong convexity for this class of problems appears to be the first of its kind. The use of spectral alignment to circumvent nonpolyhedral geometry is original in this context.

Significance:
The result has clear implications: it justifies the commonly observed fast local convergence of PGM in trace-norm problems used in matrix completion, multitask learning, and classification. The error bound is of independent interest and may inform analyses of other first-order schemes (e.g., coordinate or accelerated variants), and connects to broader themes of metric subregularity and identification. While the linear rate is asymptotic/local, this is often the regime of practical importance.

Strengths:
- Resolves an open question by establishing a local error bound for nuclear norm regularization.
- Clean and general statement covering many practical losses and arbitrary linear operators A.
- Sound application of the Luo–Tseng framework; clear delineation of R- vs Q-linear convergence.
- Insightful spectral alignment property at optimality enabling the analysis.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Only asymptotic linear convergence: clarify the neighborhood size and provide explicit dependencies/estimates on η, ε, and the linear rate constant in terms of problem data (Lf, τ, ||A||).
- Limited empirical support: include comparisons to accelerated PGM (with and without restarts), ADMM/ALM-based solvers, and larger-scale instances (e.g., n in the hundreds/thousands). Report empirical rate constants and robustness to noise and mis-specified τ.
- Discuss relation to the KL (Kurdyka–Łojasiewicz) framework (e.g., Attouch–Bolte–Svaiter) and partly smooth/identification results, which also imply local linear rates under error-bound-like conditions for semi-algebraic functions such as the nuclear norm.
- Clarify the step-size condition under backtracking (guarantee αk ≥ α > 0 eventually) and whether the linear rate holds once the algorithm enters the attraction region.
- Expand the discussion on non-uniqueness of solutions (optimal set is a compact face). The algorithm converges to some X*; a brief note on identification of the active singular subspaces would enrich the geometric picture.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and technically nontrivial theoretical contribution with broad applicability.
- Fills a gap in convergence theory for a widely used regularizer.
- Solid, careful analysis built on well-established frameworks.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental validation is limited and does not demonstrate practical impact beyond illustrative plots.
- The linear rate is local and constants are not quantified, limiting guidance for practitioners.
- Heavy reliance on supplemental proofs may hinder accessibility.

Overall recommendation:
Accept. The theoretical contribution is significant and timely for the NIPS community, and the limitations are primarily in empirical evaluation and exposition, which can be addressed in revisions.
