- Main contribution: Proves linear convergence of proximal gradient method for trace-norm problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss.
- Novel technical result: Derives a Lipschitzian error bound that enables the convergence analysis.
- Context & positioning: Builds on prior trace-norm and PGM work (Beck & Teboulle, Nesterov, Recht et al.) and connects to Luo & Tseng’s framework.
- Technical soundness: Analysis appears rigorous and grounded in convex optimization theory.
- Presentation: Paper is generally well-written with clear motivation and explicit assumptions.
- Empirical validation: Numerical experiments (matrix completion, classification) demonstrate linear convergence in practice.
- Broader impact: The Lipschitzian error bound may be useful beyond the studied problem.
- Assumptions concern: Requirement that f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strictly convex on its domain may still be restrictive for common losses.
- Step-size practicality: Theorem 3.2’s requirement on carefully chosen αk (< 1/Lf) may be impractical; no robust step-size strategy provided.
- Limited experiments: Empirical study is narrow; more datasets, parameter sweeps, and metrics (e.g., reconstruction error) are needed.
- Proof accessibility: Key proof steps (notably Lemma 4.1) are deferred to the supplement, reducing clarity for the reader.
- Practical implications: Paper lacks discussion on how results affect algorithm design or parameter tuning in practice.
- Accept arguments: Novel, theoretically sound result widening PGM applicability; empirical support and independently useful error bound.
- Reject arguments: Potentially limiting assumptions, impractical step-size requirement, limited experiments, and unclear supplemental proofs.
- Overall verdict: Solid, significant contribution; recommend acceptance with revisions.
- Requested revisions: Clarify how common losses satisfy assumptions, analyze sensitivity to step-size deviations, expand experiments and metrics, and move key proof parts into the main text.
