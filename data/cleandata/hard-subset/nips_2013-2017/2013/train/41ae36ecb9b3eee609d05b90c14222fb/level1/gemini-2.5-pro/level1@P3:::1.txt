### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, which are prevalent in machine learning applications like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The standard convergence theory for PGM establishes a sublinear rate for general smooth convex loss functions and a linear rate only when the loss is strongly convex. Many practical problems, such as matrix completion with a square loss, do not satisfy this strong convexity assumption, yet PGM often exhibits fast convergence in practice.

The main contribution of this paper is to bridge this gap between theory and practice. The authors prove that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate for a broad class of problems of the form `min_X h(A(X)) + τ||X||_*`, where `h` is a strictly convex and smooth function and `A` is an arbitrary linear operator. This result holds even when the composite function `f(X) = h(A(X))` is not strongly convex. The key technical innovation is the derivation of a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of trace norm regularized problems. This bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set to the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. This error bound is of independent interest and overcomes significant technical challenges, as prior techniques for establishing such bounds (e.g., for l1-norm or group LASSO regularization) are not applicable to the trace norm due to its non-polyhedral epigraph and the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. The authors then leverage the powerful analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10] to show that this error bound implies the linear convergence of PGM. Numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification tasks corroborate the theoretical findings.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The main theoretical result, the Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1), is novel and the proof is rigorous, albeit highly technical. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing proof techniques and develop a new approach based on analyzing the spectral properties of matrices near the optimal set. The key insight regarding the shared singular vector structure between an optimal solution `X*` and the gradient `-∇f(X*)` (Proposition 4.2) is elegant and powerful. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical analysis and the experimental results, which clearly demonstrate the predicted linear convergence regime. The work is a complete and significant contribution to the optimization theory underpinning many machine learning models.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, situates the work within the existing literature, and articulates the main contributions. The structure of the argument is logical: the problem is defined, the main results (error bound and linear convergence) are stated upfront, and then the proof of the central error bound is detailed. This makes the paper easy to follow despite the technical depth of the material. The notation is consistent and standard.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the general approach of using an error bound to prove linear convergence follows the framework of Luo and Tseng [10, 22], establishing such a bound for the trace norm regularization problem was an open and challenging question. Previous works had established similar bounds for norms with simpler, typically separable or polyhedral, structures. The authors' method for tackling the non-separable and non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm is genuinely novel. The use of matrix perturbation theory and the careful analysis of the singular value decomposition in this context represents a significant conceptual advance. This work is a substantial and non-incremental improvement over prior art in convergence analysis for first-order methods.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for both the optimization and machine learning communities. The PGM is a workhorse algorithm for many large-scale problems involving trace norm regularization. This paper provides the first formal justification for the linear convergence that is often observed empirically in non-strongly convex settings. This deeper understanding can guide algorithmic design and parameter tuning. Furthermore, the novel error bound and the associated proof techniques could potentially be extended to analyze other matrix norm regularization problems or other algorithms, opening up new avenues for future research. Researchers and practitioners working on low-rank matrix recovery problems are very likely to build upon or use the ideas presented here.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions:**

1.  **Scope of Assumptions:** The analysis hinges on the assumption that `f(X) = h(A(X))` where `h` is strongly convex on any compact set (implying strict convexity). While this covers many important loss functions (square, logistic), it would be valuable to include a brief discussion on the necessity of this assumption. For instance, what happens if `h` is merely convex but not strictly so? Does the result break down, and if so, can a counterexample be constructed? This would help delineate the precise boundaries of the theory.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of Convergence:** The results establish *asymptotic* linear convergence. The error bound holds when the residual is small enough (`||R(X)||_F ≤ ε`), and the linear rate kicks in after some number of iterations `K`. The experimental plots (e.g., Figure 1) clearly show an initial sublinear phase before the convergence becomes linear. While this is typical for this type of analysis, the authors could be more explicit about this "local" or "asymptotic" nature in the abstract and introduction for greater precision.

3.  **Proof Intuition:** The proof of the error bound is complex and relies heavily on Proposition 4.3, whose proof is in the supplementary material. A bit more intuition in the main paper about what the three cases (A, B, C) for the singular values physically represent—e.g., singular values that are shrunk to zero, shrunk but non-zero, or at the threshold `τ`—could further enhance the reader's understanding of the proof's mechanics without adding significant length.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper solves a well-known and important theoretical problem regarding the convergence of a widely used algorithm on a popular class of machine learning models.
*   **Pro:** The primary technical contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—is highly novel, technically deep, and overcomes limitations of previous analysis techniques.
*   **Pro:** The results have high impact, providing a theoretical explanation for empirical observations and potentially enabling further research into the convergence of first-order methods for related problems.
*   **Pro:** The paper is very clearly written and presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   None of significance. The identified weaknesses are minor and relate to clarifying the scope and providing additional intuition, which can be easily addressed.

**Recommendation:** I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It is a landmark contribution to the theory of first-order optimization methods for structured problems in machine learning.
