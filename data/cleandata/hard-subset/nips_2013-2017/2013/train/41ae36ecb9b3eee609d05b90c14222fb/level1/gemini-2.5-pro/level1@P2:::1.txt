### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. While PGM is known to have a sublinear convergence rate in general and a linear rate under the strong convexity of the loss function `f`, the authors tackle the common and important scenario where `f` is not strongly convex. The main contribution is to prove that PGM still achieves an asymptotic linear rate of convergence for a broad class of loss functions, specifically those of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex, smooth function (e.g., square loss or logistic loss).

The key technical innovation enabling this result is the development of a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization. This bound relates the distance from a point `X` to the optimal solution set with the norm of a residual map, `‖R(X)‖F = ‖prox(X - ∇f(X)) - X‖F`, which measures the violation of the fixed-point optimality condition. Proving such a bound for the trace norm is non-trivial, as prior techniques for L1 or group Lasso regularization, which rely on separability, do not apply. The authors overcome this by making a crucial observation about the spectral structure of the optimal solutions: an optimal matrix `X*` and its negative gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows them to use matrix perturbation theory to analyze points near the optimal set and establish the required error bound. With this bound in hand, the linear convergence of PGM follows from the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993). The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification tasks.

### Evaluation

#### Quality
The paper is of high technical quality. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The proof of the main result (Theorem 3.2) relies on the novel error bound established in Theorem 3.1. The proof of this error bound is intricate and constitutes the core technical contribution of the work. It correctly identifies the main difficulty in extending previous error bound results (i.e., the non-separability of the trace norm) and proposes a clever solution based on the spectral properties of the optimality conditions. The assumptions made on the loss function (`f(X) = h(A(X))`, with Assumption 2.1 on `h`) are reasonable and cover many important applications in machine learning, such as matrix completion with square loss and multi-task learning with logistic loss. The work appears complete, presenting the theoretical derivation, clear statements of the main results, and empirical validation demonstrating the predicted linear convergence.

#### Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly stating the problem, summarizing the state-of-the-art convergence rates for PGM, and positioning the paper's contribution within this context. The authors do a commendable job of explaining *why* their problem is difficult and how their approach is novel. The structure of the paper is logical, first presenting the main results (the error bound and the linear convergence theorem) and then delving into the detailed proof of the error bound. The notation is standard and clearly defined. While the proof itself (Section 4) is dense and technically demanding, this is inherent to the nature of the topic. The authors guide the reader through the main steps of the proof by contradiction, making the logic as accessible as possible.

#### Originality
The work is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to establish a linear convergence rate for a first-order method on trace norm regularized problems *without* assuming strong convexity of the loss function. This is a significant departure from previous analyses. The central technical contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization—appears to be a novel result that resolves an open question in the field. The proof technique, which leverages the shared singular vector structure between an optimal solution and its gradient, is a genuinely new idea for this class of problems and is tailored specifically to overcome the challenges posed by the trace norm. This is not merely a combination of existing techniques but a new analysis founded on a key structural insight. Related work is adequately cited and distinguished.

#### Significance
The results presented in this paper are of high significance to the machine learning and optimization communities.
1.  **Theoretical Importance:** Trace norm regularization is a fundamental tool for inducing low-rank structures, and PGM is a standard algorithm for solving the resulting optimization problems. This paper provides a much deeper understanding of the algorithm's performance, showing that the fast convergence rates observed in practice are not an anomaly but are theoretically grounded even in non-strongly convex settings.
2.  **Practical Relevance:** The analysis covers widely used models like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The confirmation of linear convergence provides stronger performance guarantees for practitioners using PGM on these problems.
3.  **Future Impact:** The development of the error bound for the trace norm is a significant contribution in its own right. It is a powerful analytical tool that could be used to analyze other algorithms or extended to different matrix norm regularizers. This work is likely to inspire further research into non-asymptotic rates and the analysis of first-order methods under conditions weaker than strong convexity.

### Arguments for Acceptance
*   **Pro:** The paper solves a long-standing and important theoretical question regarding the convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization.
*   **Pro:** It introduces a novel and significant technical result—a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm—which is of independent interest.
*   **Pro:** The proof technique is original and based on a deep structural insight into the problem's optimality conditions.
*   **Pro:** The results are highly significant for both theory and practice in machine learning, covering popular models like matrix completion.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and well-motivated.

### Arguments against Acceptance
*   **Con (minor):** The established convergence is asymptotic. The analysis does not provide an explicit characterization of the "linear convergence region" or the constants involved in the rate, which could limit direct practical application for parameter tuning. However, for a theoretical contribution of this nature, establishing the existence of a linear rate is the primary and most important achievement.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to our understanding of first-order optimization methods in machine learning. The strengths vastly outweigh the minor weaknesses. It is technically sound, highly original, and of great significance. I strongly recommend acceptance.
