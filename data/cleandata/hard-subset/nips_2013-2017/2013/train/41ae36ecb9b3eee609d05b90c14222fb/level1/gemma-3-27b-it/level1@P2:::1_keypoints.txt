- Main contribution: Proves linear convergence of proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss.  
- Technical core: Introduces a new Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1) derived via matrix perturbation analysis; uses it to obtain linear convergence (Theorem 3.2).  
- Motivation and relevance: Well-motivated for matrix completion and low-rank learning, addressing a practical and widely applicable problem.  
- Literature positioning: Strong connection to prior work (Beck & Teboulle, Nesterov, Luo & Tseng) and situates contributions within established convex optimization theory.  
- Theoretical clarity: Key results are stated precisely and supported by rigorous proofs.  
- Empirical support: Numerical experiments show linear convergence in matrix completion and classification, but are brief.  
- Novelty strength: The removal of a strong convexity requirement is a significant and original advance.  
- Technical soundness: Proofs appear rigorous and leverage established techniques; Proposition 4.2 is a crucial, well-justified component.  
- Presentation quality: Paper is generally well-written, organized, and uses consistent notation.  
- Practical significance: Results can improve algorithmic efficiency for many low-rank machine learning tasks.  
- Proof complexity (weakness): Proofs are dense and may be hard to follow for readers not versed in matrix perturbation or advanced convex analysis.  
- Limited experiments (weakness): Empirical evaluation lacks breadth (varying sizes, noise levels, and algorithm comparisons like accelerated PGM or ADMM); step-size choice details are sparse.  
- Strongness of assumptions (weakness): Assumption 2.1(b) (strong convexity of h on compact sets) may be restrictive and worth relaxing or discussing.  
- Interpretation gap (weakness): The constant η in the error bound is not clearly interpreted or tied to problem parameters.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions; request more proof details (esp. Lemma 4.1), expanded experiments and comparisons, discussion/relaxation of Assumption 2.1(b), and elaboration on the constant η.
