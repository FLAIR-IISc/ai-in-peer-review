This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for minimizing a convex smooth loss function with trace norm regularization. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under fairly general conditions, notably *without* requiring strong convexity of the loss function – a significant improvement over existing results. The core contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which is established through careful matrix perturbation analysis.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical issue in numerous machine learning applications (matrix completion, low-rank learning, etc.). The problem statement is clearly articulated, and the authors effectively relate their work to prior research, citing relevant papers on PGM, trace norm regularization, and convergence analysis. The connection to existing literature (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, Nesterov, Luo & Tseng) is particularly strong, positioning the work within a well-established theoretical framework. The theoretical results – the error bound (Theorem 3.1) and the linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) – are stated precisely and are the key contributions of the paper. The numerical experiments, while relatively brief, provide some empirical support for the theoretical findings, demonstrating linear convergence in matrix completion and matrix classification scenarios.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant contribution, expanding the applicability of PGM to a broader class of problems.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous, building on established techniques in convex optimization and utilizing a clever error bound derived from matrix perturbation theory. The use of Proposition 4.2 appears crucial and well-justified.
*   **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the notation is consistent.
*   **Significance:** The result has practical implications for many machine learning tasks involving low-rank structures. Improved convergence guarantees can lead to faster algorithms and more efficient solutions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Proof Complexity:** While rigorous, the proof is dense and relies on multiple lemmas and propositions. A reader unfamiliar with matrix perturbation theory or advanced convex analysis might find it challenging to follow all the details. More intermediate steps and explanations within the proof itself would improve readability.
*   **Numerical Experiments Limited:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While they demonstrate convergence, exploring different problem sizes, noise levels, and comparing with other algorithms (e.g., accelerated PGM, alternating direction method of multipliers (ADMM)) would strengthen the empirical validation. A more thorough experimental evaluation would add credibility. The step size selection in the matrix classification experiment using a backtracking line search isn't fully explained (just mentioned).
*   **Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b) – the strong convexity of *h* on compact subsets – feels a bit strong. It might be worthwhile to investigate whether the results can be extended to broader classes of functions.
*   **Error Bound Interpretation:** The meaning of the constant 'η' in the error bound (Theorem 3.1) isn’t fully clear, beyond stating it exists. Its dependence on various problem parameters could be elaborated on.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with potential impact on a wide range of machine learning applications.
*   The technical contributions are significant and build upon a solid foundation of existing research.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The proof is complex and may be difficult for some readers to follow.
*   The numerical experiments are limited and could be more comprehensive.
*   The strong convexity assumption on *h* might limit the broader applicability of the results.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical result is significant, and the paper demonstrates a deep understanding of the relevant techniques. While the proof complexity and limited numerical experiments are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. The paper pushes the theoretical boundaries of PGM for trace norm regularization and addresses an important open question.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. I recommend accepting the paper with a request for the authors to:

1.  Provide more detailed explanations and intermediate steps within the proofs, particularly for Lemma 4.1.
2.  Expand the numerical experiments to include comparisons with other algorithms and explore the sensitivity of the results to different problem parameters.
3.  Discuss the implications of Assumption 2.1(b) and potentially investigate whether the results can be extended to broader classes of functions.
4. Elaborate on the constant 'η' in the error bound.
