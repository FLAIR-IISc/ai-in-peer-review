This paper presents a significant result: establishing linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm regularization without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a notable contribution, as existing linear convergence results typically rely on strong convexity assumptions which are often not met in practical machine learning applications like matrix completion. The problem is well-motivated, clearly stated, and the result addresses a key limitation of previously known convergence rates.

The paper is well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the gap in existing literature. The key ideas – a novel Lipschitzian error bound – are clearly presented. The related work section is appropriate and acknowledges relevant contributions.  The notation is generally clear, though some familiarity with matrix optimization is assumed.

The technical contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1), is the core of the paper. The proof strategy appears sound, leveraging matrix perturbation theory to overcome the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the inability to use variable splitting techniques common in ℓ1-regularization analysis.  The use of Luo and Tseng’s framework is appropriate given the established error bound. The authors acknowledge the core difficulty of establishing this bound and provide a clear rationale for their approach.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. While the high-level proof sketch is provided, the detailed proofs, especially for Lemma 4.1 and Propositions 4.2 & 4.3, are relegated to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult for the reviewer to fully assess the correctness and subtlety of the technical arguments. The supplementary material *should* be self-contained, allowing for complete verification of the claims.  A more detailed presentation of at least the critical steps in the main body of the paper would significantly enhance its accessibility.

The numerical experiments, while demonstrating the linear convergence in practice, are somewhat limited. The experiments primarily showcase the method working on matrix completion and matrix classification – both scenarios where the theoretical result applies. However, it would be valuable to include experiments that explicitly test the performance when the strong convexity assumption *is* violated, perhaps comparing against methods that rely on strong convexity. Furthermore, the parameter tuning process isn’t fully described, specifically for the backtracking line search used in the matrix classification experiment. While the empirical results *support* the theoretical findings, they don't offer extensive validation across a wider range of problem instances.

The paper’s clarity could be improved in a few places. For example, the motivation behind Assumption 2.1(b) being “strictly convex on dom(h)” is not fully explained – linking this to the necessary smoothness for the analysis would be helpful.  Also, the connection between the residual map *R* and the distance to the optimal set is stated but could be elaborated on a bit more intuitively.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant advancement.
*   **Significance:** The result is applicable to a broad range of problems in machine learning, including matrix completion and related low-rank approximation tasks.
*   **Soundness:** The overall approach appears technically sound, building on established results and utilizing appropriate tools from matrix analysis.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The authors provide a formal error bound and utilize a well-established convergence analysis framework.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Detail in Main Text:** The detailed proofs are in the supplementary material, hindering complete verification.
*   **Limited Numerical Validation:** The experiments, while positive, are not extensive and lack comparisons in specific settings where strong convexity is absent.
*   **Clarity Issues:** Some minor points could be clarified to improve accessibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, the paper presents a valuable and original contribution to the field. The theoretical result is significant and well-motivated. The authors have addressed a long-standing question regarding the convergence rates of PGM for trace norm regularization. I recommend **acceptance** with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding the detailed proofs being confined to the supplementary material. Specifically, I suggest including a more detailed sketch of the proof of Lemma 4.1 within the main text or providing significantly more detailed explanations and justification within the supplementary document to ensure its self-contained nature.  Minor clarifications as suggested above would also be beneficial.
