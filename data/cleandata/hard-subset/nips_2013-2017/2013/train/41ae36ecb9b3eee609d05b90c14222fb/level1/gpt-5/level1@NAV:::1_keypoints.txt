- Main contribution: proves proximal gradient method (PGM) attains asymptotic linear convergence for min_X f(X)+τ||X||* without strong convexity of f.  
- Core technical result: a local Lipschitzian error bound dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||R(X)||_F for points in a bounded level set with small residual R(X)=S_τ(X−∇f(X))−X.  
- Convergence claims: Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates for stepsizes α_k < 1/L_f using the Luo–Tseng framework.  
- Structural insight: at optimality X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, enabling a perturbation argument to overcome the nuclear norm’s non-polyhedrality.  
- Scope: results apply to common losses used in low-rank learning (square and logistic losses) and thus cover matrix completion and matrix classification.  
- Empirics: semi-log plots show linear trends near the solution, supporting the asymptotic linear claim.  
- Relation to prior work: closes a gap where classical PGM rates are sublinear and linear rates typically required strong convexity or polyhedral regularizers.  
- Novelty: the error bound and ensuing linear-rate result for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity appear new and original.  
- Technical quality: analysis is careful and nontrivial; the restricted invertibility lemma and spectral alignment (Proposition 4.2) are compelling.  
- Practical concern 1: theorem requires α_k < 1/L_f, but the matrix completion experiment uses α = 1 while L_f = 1, violating the strict inequality—needs adjustment or justification.  
- Practical concern 2: backtracking-line-search needs to be shown to produce α_k uniformly bounded away from zero to satisfy assumptions.  
- Clarity issues: Section 4 is dense and would benefit from earlier intuition about shared singular vectors; also figure numbering/text mismatches and missing axis labels hinder readability.  
- Significance: high, since PGM is widely used for low-rank problems and the error bound has independent value for analyzing other matrix norms.  
- Suggested context: compare and contrast with KL-based analyses and restricted strong convexity results to better delimit the scope.  
- Pros for acceptance: novel error bound and linear rate, solid assumptions and analysis, broad applicability and insightful structural lemmas.  
- Cons for acceptance: results are local/asymptotic with limited finite-iteration guidance, minor theoretical/expository gaps (stepsize/backtracking), and small, illustrative experiments without baselines or robustness tests.
