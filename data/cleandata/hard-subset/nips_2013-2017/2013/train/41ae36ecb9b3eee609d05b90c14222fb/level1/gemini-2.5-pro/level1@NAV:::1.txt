This paper addresses the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for trace norm regularized optimization problems, a formulation central to many machine learning tasks like matrix completion and multi-task learning. The authors show that for a broad class of loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex but `f` itself is not necessarily strongly convex, PGM achieves a linear convergence rate. This significantly improves upon the established sublinear `O(1/k^2)` rate for general convex functions and extends the known linear rate from the restrictive case of strongly convex objectives. The key technical contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This is a non-trivial extension of similar bounds for L1 or group LASSO norms (e.g., Tseng and Yun, 2009; Luo and Tseng, 1993), as the trace norm's non-separability requires a new analytical approach based on matrix perturbation theory.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** This is a strong theoretical contribution with direct relevance to a popular class of algorithms and problems. Establishing linear convergence without strong convexity for PGM on trace norm problems is an important and impactful result that advances our understanding of first-order optimization methods.
*   **Originality and Quality:** The main technical result, the Lipschitzian error bound, is highly original. The proof technique overcomes the significant challenge posed by the non-polyhedral and non-separable nature of the trace norm, which had left this question open. The mathematical analysis is rigorous, complete, and technically sound.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem, contextualizes its contribution within the existing literature, and provides a clear roadmap for its complex proof structure.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The established convergence is asymptotic, meaning it holds once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal set. While the experiments demonstrate this behavior, a theoretical or empirical characterization of this "close enough" region would be a valuable addition.
*   The numerical experiments, while effectively illustrating the theoretical claims, are conducted on relatively small-scale problems. Demonstrating the practical implications on larger-scale settings would further strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides a novel and significant theoretical result (linear convergence without strong convexity) for a widely used algorithm (PGM) on an important problem class.
2.  The core technical contribution, the error bound, is a substantial and elegant piece of analysis that resolves an open question and could be of independent interest.
3.  The paper is of high quality and is clearly written, making a complex topic accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The practical implications are not fully explored on large-scale problems.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a fundamental advance in the convergence analysis of first-order methods for structured optimization.
