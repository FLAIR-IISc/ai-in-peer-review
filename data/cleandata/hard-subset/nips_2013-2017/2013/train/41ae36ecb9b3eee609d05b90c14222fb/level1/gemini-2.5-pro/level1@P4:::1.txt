This paper studies the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for solving trace norm-regularized optimization problems of the form `min F(X) = f(X) + τ‖X‖∗`. This formulation is central to many machine learning tasks involving low-rank matrix recovery, such as matrix completion and multi-task learning. Prior work has established that PGM achieves a sublinear convergence rate for general convex and smooth loss functions `f`, and a linear rate if `f` is strongly convex. However, many loss functions of practical interest, such as the square loss in matrix completion, are not strongly convex. The main contribution of this work is to prove that PGM can still achieve a linear convergence rate for a large class of these problems, without requiring strong convexity of `f`.

The analysis applies to loss functions with the structure `f(X) = h(A(X))`, where `A` is a linear operator and `h` is a strictly convex function with certain smoothness properties. The key technical result underpinning the convergence analysis is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this class of problems. This error bound relates the distance from an iterate to the optimal solution set with the norm of a residual map, which measures the violation of the problem's fixed-point optimality condition. Establishing such an error bound for the trace norm has been an open problem, as prior techniques used for `ℓ1`-norm or group LASSO regularization do not apply due to the non-polyhedral nature of the trace norm's epigraph and the global dependence of singular values on all matrix entries. The authors overcome this by exploiting a crucial structural property of the optimal solution: at an optimum `X̄`, the matrix `X̄` and the negative gradient `-∇f(X̄)` share the same singular vectors. This insight, combined with matrix perturbation theory, allows for the derivation of the error bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from this bound by applying the established analysis framework of Luo and Tseng (1993).

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental question about the performance of a widely used algorithm (PGM) on a very important class of problems (trace norm regularization). Proving linear convergence without the restrictive assumption of strong convexity is a significant theoretical advance. It provides a formal justification for the fast empirical convergence rates often observed in practice for methods like PGM on problems like matrix completion.

2.  **Originality and Technical Contribution:** The primary contribution, the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, is highly novel and constitutes a substantial technical achievement. The proof technique is innovative, moving beyond the variable-splitting approaches used for separable norms. The key insight regarding the shared singular vector structure at optimality (Proposition 4.2) is elegant and powerful, providing a new analytical tool for this problem class.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly states the problem, reviews the relevant literature, and precisely articulates the paper's contributions. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are presented upfront, followed by a clear and logical exposition of the proof. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the technical challenges and motivating their novel approach.

4.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears to be rigorous and correct. The argument is carefully constructed, building upon a central lemma (Lemma 4.1) concerning the properties of sequences that might violate the error bound. The use of the Luo and Tseng framework to connect the error bound to the linear convergence of PGM is standard and appropriate. The provided numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification effectively support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the predicted linear convergence behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Scope of Assumptions:** The analysis is restricted to loss functions of the form `f(X) = h(A(X))` where `h` satisfies Assumption 2.1 (differentiability and strong convexity on compact sets). While this structure covers many important applications, including square loss and logistic loss, a brief discussion of cases where this assumption might not hold could strengthen the paper by clarifying the boundaries of the result.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic, meaning it is guaranteed to hold once the iterates are sufficiently close to the optimal solution set. The error bound in Theorem 3.1 holds under the condition `‖R(X)‖F ≤ ǫ`, but the analysis does not provide a characterization of the constant `ǫ` or the region where linear convergence begins. This is a common limitation in this style of analysis, but it means the global convergence rate remains sublinear, with the linear phase kicking in later.

3.  **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** Key components of the proof, such as the derivations for inequalities (9)-(11) and the proof of the critical Proposition 4.3, are deferred to supplementary material. While necessary due to space constraints, this makes a complete verification of the paper's technical claims from the main text alone challenging.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Solves an important and open theoretical problem regarding the convergence of PGM for trace norm regularization.
    *   Presents a novel and significant technical contribution in the form of a Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm, with an innovative proof technique.
    *   The result has direct implications for understanding the practical efficiency of first-order methods in many machine learning applications.
    *   The paper is written with exceptional clarity and is well-organized.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The theoretical result is asymptotic in nature, without a characterization of the pre-asymptotic phase.
    *   The assumptions on the loss function, while covering many cases, are not completely general.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial and original contribution to the optimization literature for machine learning. The weaknesses are minor and typical for theoretical work of this kind. The paper's strengths in significance, originality, and clarity are outstanding. It resolves an important open question and provides valuable theoretical insight into a widely used class of algorithms and problems.
