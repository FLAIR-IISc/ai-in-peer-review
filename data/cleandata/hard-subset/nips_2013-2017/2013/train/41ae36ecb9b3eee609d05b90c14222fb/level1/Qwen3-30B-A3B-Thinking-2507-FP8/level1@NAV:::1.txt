

This paper investigates the convergence properties of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems, establishing linear convergence for a broad class of loss functions without requiring strong convexity. The authors prove a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which is the key technical contribution enabling their linear convergence result. This addresses an open question noted in prior work (e.g., [18]) about whether such error bounds exist for trace norm regularization, which differs fundamentally from polyhedral norms like ℓ1 due to its non-polyhedral epigraph.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured. The proof of the error bound cleverly leverages matrix perturbation theory and the observation that optimal solutions and gradients share singular vectors, which overcomes the technical challenges of analyzing trace norm regularization. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification problems effectively demonstrate linear convergence behavior in practice, supporting the theoretical claims. The writing is clear and the organization logical, making the technical content accessible.

The work is highly original. While linear convergence of PGM is known for strongly convex problems, this is the first result establishing linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity assumptions. The error bound technique itself could be valuable for other matrix norm regularization problems, making the contribution potentially impactful beyond the specific problem considered.

The significance is strong: trace norm regularization is widely used in machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.), and establishing linear convergence for a broader class of loss functions significantly enhances the theoretical understanding of PGM for these problems. The assumption f(X) = h(A(X)) is restrictive but covers many common loss functions (square loss, logistic loss), and the authors provide clear justification for this class.

Strengths: Novel technical contribution (error bound), rigorous proof, strong theoretical and empirical validation, significant implications for machine learning applications.

Weaknesses: The assumption f(X) = h(A(X)) might exclude some practical loss functions, though the authors justify its relevance well.

The paper makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of first-order methods for trace norm-regularized problems, addressing an important open question with a clean and elegant solution. It meets all quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
