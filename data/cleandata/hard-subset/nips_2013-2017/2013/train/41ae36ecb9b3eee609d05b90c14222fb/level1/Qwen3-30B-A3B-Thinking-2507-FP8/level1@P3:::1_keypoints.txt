1. Proves linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity.  
2. Assumes the loss has the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth.  
3. Main technical novelty: a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization via matrix perturbation theory.  
4. Key insight: simultaneous singular value decomposition (Proposition 4.2) and singular vector alignment enable the error bound despite non-polyhedral epigraph.  
5. Extends Luo & Tseng’s convergence framework to trace norm where prior bounds covered only ℓ1/group LASSO.  
6. Connects to important applications such as matrix completion and multi-task learning.  
7. Strength: first linear convergence result for PGM on trace-norm problems without strong convexity assumptions.  
8. Strength: rigorous, technically sound proofs (notably Theorem 3.1 and Proposition 4.2).  
9. Strength: assumptions are reasonable for many ML applications and numerical experiments support theory.  
10. Strength: clear organization and exposition linking the error bound to convergence.  
11. Weakness: result restricted to losses of the form f(X)=h(A(X)), excluding some practical losses.  
12. Weakness: numerical experiments are limited to two applications with fixed parameters.  
13. Weakness: lacks empirical or computational comparison to alternative trace-norm methods (e.g., alternating minimization, Bregman).  
14. Weakness: matrix perturbation arguments (Section 4.1) are technically dense and could use more intuitive explanation.  
15. Quality: technically sound work with supporting experiments.  
16. Clarity: generally well-written though some proofs (e.g., Lemma 4.1) require careful reading.  
17. Originality: novel solution to an open problem via singular vector alignment.  
18. Significance: important implications for low-rank matrix recovery and trace-norm applications; error bound useful for other analyses.  
19. Arguments for acceptance: resolves open question, provides first linear guarantee, error bound of independent interest, empirical support, well-motivated assumptions.  
20. Arguments against acceptance: limited loss-function scope, limited experiments, insufficient contextualization of computational advantages versus other methods.  
21. Overall recommendation: strong contribution; reviewer recommends acceptance.
