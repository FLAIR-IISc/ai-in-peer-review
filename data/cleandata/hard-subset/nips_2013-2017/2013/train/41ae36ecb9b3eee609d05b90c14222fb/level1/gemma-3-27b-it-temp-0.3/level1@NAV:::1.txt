## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for minimizing a convex smooth loss function with trace norm regularization. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function – a significant improvement over existing results. This is achieved through a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which is presented as a contribution of independent interest.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the prevalence of trace norm regularization in various machine learning applications (matrix completion, low-rank approximation, etc.) and the limitations of existing sublinear convergence guarantees for PGM. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, referencing key papers on PGM, trace norm minimization, and convergence analysis (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, Recht & Fazel, Luo & Tseng). The introduction effectively highlights the gap in knowledge addressed by the paper. 

A key strength lies in the derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound, overcoming challenges related to the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm and the inability to directly apply existing techniques from ℓ1-regularization. The proof, while detailed and relying on matrix perturbation theory, appears sound. The inclusion of numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence on matrix completion and classification tasks further strengthens the claims. 

However, the paper suffers from some clarity issues. The notation is dense, and the presentation could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the key steps in the proof of the error bound. While the authors mention the challenges in obtaining a good Lipschitz constant for the logistic loss in the numerical experiments and resort to a backtracking line search, a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of this choice would be beneficial.  Additionally, the connection between the derived error bound and the specific properties of the linear operator *A* could be more explicitly discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and significant theoretical result: linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity.
*   Well-motivated problem with broad applicability.
*   Sound mathematical analysis and a potentially useful Lipschitzian error bound.
*   Supporting numerical experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense notation and occasionally challenging presentation.
*   Limited discussion of practical considerations in the numerical experiments.
*   Could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the error bound’s connection to the problem structure.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the presentation could be improved for clarity, the theoretical results are strong and the paper addresses an important open question. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve readability and provide more practical insights.
