

# Peer Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of convex optimization for machine learning. The authors establish that the proximal gradient method (PGM) achieves linear convergence for trace norm-regularized problems without requiring strong convexity of the loss function, addressing a key open question in the literature. Specifically, they show that when the loss function takes the form f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth, the PGM converges linearly. The main technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, which they prove using matrix perturbation theory and a novel observation about the singular vectors of optimal solutions.

The paper builds on prior work in several important ways. It extends the convergence analysis framework developed by Luo and Tseng (2002) to trace norm regularization, where previous error bounds were only established for ℓ1-norm or group LASSO regularization (Tseng 2008; Tseng & Yun 2009). The authors correctly note that the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, making the standard splitting approach for error bounds inapplicable. Their approach using simultaneous singular value decomposition of optimal solutions (Proposition 4.2) is a key insight that resolves this challenge. The paper also connects to important applications like matrix completion (Recht et al. 2010) and multi-task learning (Argyriou et al. 2008), where trace norm regularization is commonly used.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical contribution:** The paper provides the first linear convergence result for PGM on trace norm-regularized problems without strong convexity assumptions. This is a substantial advance over the standard O(1/k) sublinear rate.

2. **Rigorous proof:** The proof of the error bound (Theorem 3.1) is technically sound, with careful handling of the matrix perturbation arguments. The key Proposition 4.2 is well-motivated and crucial to the proof.

3. **Practical relevance:** The assumptions are reasonable for many machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning), and the numerical experiments confirm the theoretical claims.

4. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with proper mathematical notation and logical flow. The connection between the error bound and convergence analysis is well-explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited scope of assumptions:** The paper's result applies only to loss functions of the specific form f(X) = h(A(X)). While this covers important cases (square loss, logistic loss), it excludes some practical applications where the loss function may not be expressible in this form. The authors could better clarify the boundaries of their result.

2. **Numerical experiments:** The experiments are limited to two specific applications (matrix completion and matrix classification) with fixed parameters. More comprehensive experiments across different problem sizes, ranks, and noise levels would strengthen the empirical validation.

3. **Comparison to existing methods:** The paper doesn't compare the PGM's convergence rate to alternative approaches for trace norm regularization (e.g., alternating minimization, Bregman methods). A discussion of computational complexity versus other methods would be valuable.

4. **Technical accessibility:** While the proof is rigorous, the matrix perturbation arguments in Section 4.1 may be challenging for readers not specializing in matrix optimization. A more intuitive explanation of why the singular vector alignment (Proposition 4.2) enables the error bound would improve accessibility.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with strong theoretical contributions. The proof is thorough, and the numerical experiments support the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge the specific assumptions required for their result.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The mathematical presentation is clear, though some sections (particularly the proof of Lemma 4.1) may require careful reading. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** This is a novel contribution that resolves an open question in the optimization literature. While error bounds for ℓ1-norm regularization exist, the trace norm case had remained open despite apparent similarity. The authors' approach using singular vector alignment is a significant technical advance.

**Significance:** The result has important implications for machine learning applications involving low-rank matrix recovery. Many practical problems use trace norm regularization without strong convexity assumptions, and the linear convergence guarantee provides theoretical justification for the empirical success of PGM in these settings. The error bound itself is likely to be useful for analyzing other optimization methods for matrix problems.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Resolves an important open question in convex optimization for machine learning
- Provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM on trace norm problems without strong convexity
- The error bound is of independent interest for matrix optimization
- Theoretical results are supported by empirical validation
- The assumptions are well-motivated by common machine learning applications

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited scope of the loss function assumption
- Numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope
- Could better contextualize the computational advantages over other methods

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of optimization methods for trace norm regularization. While the scope of the assumptions is somewhat limited, the paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The result has important implications for practitioners using trace norm regularization in machine learning applications. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful open question. I recommend acceptance.

The paper stands out from previous NIPS work (e.g., Schmidt et al. 2011 on proximal-gradient methods, Toh & Yun 2010 on accelerated methods) by providing the first linear convergence result for trace norm regularization without strong convexity, making it a valuable addition to the literature.
