

The paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems. The authors prove that for loss functions of the form f(X) = h(A(X)) (where A is a linear operator and h is strictly convex with certain smoothness properties), the PGM exhibits linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f. This addresses a significant open question, as previous linear convergence results required strong convexity of the loss function. The key technical contribution is a new Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization, established through matrix perturbation theory and a novel observation about the singular vectors of optimal solutions.

The paper relates well to previous work, noting that while error bounds exist for ℓ1-norm [23] and group LASSO [22, 25], the non-polyhedral epigraph of trace norm made the question open. The authors correctly identify this as a significant gap in the literature on first-order methods for matrix regularization.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a rigorous proof of the Lipschitzian error bound (Theorem 3.1), which is the core novelty. The proof overcomes challenges specific to trace norm by leveraging matrix perturbation theory and the observation that optimal solutions share singular vectors with -∇f(X̄).
2. The linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) follows from standard techniques applied to the new error bound, demonstrating a clean application of the framework developed by Luo and Tseng [10].
3. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification support the theoretical claims, showing linear convergence in practice as predicted by the theory.
4. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with consistent notation and good explanations of key concepts.

Weaknesses:
1. The loss function form f(X) = h(A(X)) is somewhat restricted, though the authors justify it covers many applications (square loss, logistic loss). The paper could better address why this form is general enough and discuss potential limitations for other loss functions.
2. The numerical experiments are limited to two problems; more extensive testing across different applications would strengthen the significance.
3. The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the proximal operator for large-scale problems, though this might be beyond the scope of the theoretical contribution.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs. The error bound is carefully established, and the convergence analysis follows from standard techniques. The numerical experiments appropriately complement the theoretical results.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with a logical structure. Key concepts are well-explained, and mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The supplementary material provides necessary details for the proof.

Originality: The Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization appears to be novel, as the authors correctly note this question remained open despite the apparent similarity to ℓ1-norm regularization. The technical approach using matrix perturbation theory is innovative.

Significance: The result has important implications for machine learning applications using trace norm regularization (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). The linear convergence rate without strong convexity assumptions significantly broadens the applicability of PGM, making it more relevant to real-world problems where strong convexity is often not satisfied (e.g., matrix completion with square loss).

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the understanding of convergence rates for first-order methods on trace norm-regularized problems. It resolves an important open question and provides a new technical tool (the error bound) that may be applicable to other matrix regularization problems. The results are significant for both theoretical optimization and practical machine learning applications.

The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in understanding convergence rates for first-order methods on trace norm-regularized problems, which is directly relevant to many machine learning applications. The theoretical contribution is significant, and the numerical experiments provide appropriate validation.
