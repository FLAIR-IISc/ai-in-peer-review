Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies composite convex optimization of the form minX f(X)+τ||X||*, where ||·||* is the nuclear norm and f(X)=h(A(X)) with A linear and h smooth and strictly convex (locally strongly convex on compacts). While proximal gradient methods (PGM) for such problems are known to converge sublinearly (O(1/k) or O(1/k^2) with acceleration) unless f is strongly convex, the authors prove an asymptotic linear convergence rate for a broad class of losses without assuming strong convexity of f. The key technical contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound that relates the distance to the optimal solution set to the proximal optimality residual ||Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X||F. Combining this error bound with the Luo–Tseng error-bound-based framework yields Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates for fixed step sizes 0<αk<1/Lf bounded away from 0 and 1/Lf.

The analysis addresses a longstanding gap: error bounds were known for polyhedral nonsmooth parts (e.g., ℓ1, group LASSO) but not for the nonpolyhedral nuclear norm. The proof uses spectral structure at optimality: optimal X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, enabling a refined perturbation argument and a “restricted invertibility” inequality that controls ||X−X̄||F by ||A(X−X̄)||2. Numerical experiments on matrix completion (squared loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrate linear trends once iterates are near optimal.

This extends prior NIPS/optimization literature: accelerated proximal methods for nuclear-norm problems (e.g., Ji & Ye 2009; Toh & Yun 2010) give O(1/k^2); Schmidt et al. (NIPS’11) analyze linear rates under strong convexity. Luo–Tseng and subsequent works provide EB-based linear rates for polyhedral regularizers. To my knowledge, a linear-rate result for PGM with nuclear norm regularization absent strong convexity of f was open.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The main theorems are plausible and carefully argued. The error bound (Theorem 3.1) is local (requires small residual and bounded level set), which is standard in EB-based analyses. The derivation of the spectral alignment (Proposition 4.2) and the decomposition of perturbations (Proposition 4.3) appears correct and addresses the nonpolyhedral challenge. The linear rate theorem (Theorem 3.2) follows cleanly from the EB and standard descent inequalities with fixed step sizes.
- Assumptions: h being C1 with Lipschitz gradient and locally strongly convex on compacts (hence strictly convex) holds for squared and logistic losses; f need not be strongly convex because A may be rank-deficient (e.g., matrix completion). The optimal solution set is assumed nonempty. Step sizes must satisfy 0<α<αk<ᾱ<1/Lf; this is realistic for fixed-step PGM or backtracking that ensures αk≤1/Lf and, in practice, αk bounded away from 0.
- Completeness: The core proofs are present; some technical details are deferred to the supplement. Experiments are small-scale but adequate to illustrate the asymptotic phenomenon.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized: motivation, definition of residual map, statement of the EB and rate theorems, and a detailed proof roadmap. The discussion of why nuclear norm differs from ℓ1 (nonpolyhedral epigraph, global coupling via singular values) is helpful. A few minor issues: figures are referenced sparsely; clarify Figure numbering and axes; unify notation (τ vs µ appears in experiments). Consider a brief intuitive explanation of Proposition 4.2 and the geometric meaning of the restricted invertibility inequality.

Originality
- The Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear-norm-regularized problems and the resulting linear convergence of PGM without strong convexity of f is, to my knowledge, new and addresses a recognized gap. The spectral-perturbation approach is novel relative to prior EB results that rely on polyhedral decomposability.

Significance
- The result is important for theory and practice. Nuclear norm regularization is ubiquitous in ML (matrix completion, multi-task learning, classification). Establishing linear rates under realistic losses justifies observed fast convergence and may inform algorithm design and stopping criteria. The EB technique may be reusable for other spectral regularizers.

Strengths
- Resolves an open question: linear convergence of PGM for nuclear norm without strong convexity.
- Clear conditions covering widely used losses (square, logistic) and arbitrary linear A.
- Careful and nontrivial analysis overcoming nonpolyhedral difficulties via spectral structure.
- Clean linkage to the Luo–Tseng EB framework; explicit rate form (Q-linear for values, R-linear for iterates).
- Experiments corroborate asymptotic linear behavior.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope is asymptotic/local: the EB requires small residual and bounded level set; discuss practical identification of the linear regime and sensitivity to conditioning.
- Step-size requirement: Theorem 3.2 assumes αk uniformly bounded in (0,1/Lf). Provide a corollary for standard backtracking (which typically enforces αk∈(0,1/Lf]) and argue αk does not vanish.
- Inexact proximal steps: Practical nuclear-norm PGMs use truncated/partial SVDs. Discuss whether the linear rate extends to inexact proximal with summable errors or relative accuracy (cf. Schmidt et al. 2011).
- Constants: No guidance on the EB constant or linear factor; any dependence on spectral gap or rank would be valuable.
- Experiments: Small n and limited baselines. Consider larger-scale problems, explicit residual plots, and comparison to accelerated PGM, ALM/ADMM, and methods with added quadratic regularization (to contrast strong vs local strong convexity).

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and significant theoretical contribution addressing a widely used model.
- Sound analysis with clear assumptions and general applicability.
- Bridges important gap between ℓ1/group-LASSO EB results and nuclear norm.
- Likely to be cited and built upon by the community.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and breadth.
- No treatment of inexact proximal computations common in large-scale settings.
- Only asymptotic linearity; limited discussion of pre-asymptotic behavior and constants.

Overall recommendation
Strong accept. The paper makes a clear, original, and technically solid contribution by establishing a local Lipschitzian error bound and consequent linear convergence of PGM for nuclear norm regularization without strong convexity of the loss. With minor clarifications and expanded empirical support, it will be a valuable addition to NIPS.
