- Main contribution: proves linear convergence of proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace-norm-regularized problems without strong convexity by establishing a Lipschitzian error bound.  
- Technical novelty: introduces a new error bound using matrix perturbation theory and the observation that optimal solutions and gradients share singular vectors.  
- Problem setting: applies to f(X)=h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth; relevant to matrix completion and multi-task learning.  
- Resolution of open question: extends error-bound results from ℓ1/group LASSO to trace norm despite its non-polyhedral epigraph.  
- Relation to prior work: correctly situates against O(1/k) and O(1/k^2) PGM rates and prior linear rates requiring strong convexity.  
- Theoretical quality: rigorous, novel proofs (Lemma 4.1, Theorem 3.1) that fill a significant gap in the literature.  
- Empirical support: numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification demonstrate observed linear convergence.  
- Originality: first linear convergence result for PGM with trace-norm regularization absent strong convexity.  
- Significance: broadens applicability of linear convergence guarantees to many ML problems; error bound of independent interest.  
- Clarity: well-structured, clearly written, and logically presented with good motivation.  
- Weakness — examples: needs more concrete examples and clearer conditions on h.  
- Weakness — experiments: empirical evaluation is limited; should compare to other algorithms (e.g., accelerated PGM) and explore practical speedups.  
- Weakness — exposition: proof of Lemma 4.1(c) is dense and would benefit from a more intuitive explanation.  
- Arguments for acceptance: resolves an important open problem, provides novel technical contributions, and is theoretically sound with supporting experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited experiments and insufficient discussion of practical implications of the linear rate.  
- Overall recommendation: recommend acceptance — strong, novel, and broadly relevant theoretical contribution.
