This paper studies composite nuclear-norm regularization minX f(X)+τ||X||*, with f(X)=h(A(X)), and proves that the proximal gradient method (PGM) attains asymptotic linear convergence without strong convexity of f. The core contribution is a new local Lipschitzian error bound dist(X, X*) ≤ η||R(X)||F for points in a bounded level set with small residual R(X)=Sτ(X−∇f(X))−X. Leveraging the Luo–Tseng framework, the authors derive Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates for bounded-away-from-zero stepsizes αk<1/Lf. The proof hinges on a structural result: at optimality X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share singular vectors, enabling a perturbation argument that bypasses the lack of polyhedrality of the nuclear norm. The results cover square and logistic losses appearing in matrix completion and matrix classification. Empirically, semi-log plots confirm linear trends in the neighborhood of the solution.

Relation to prior work: Classical PGM rates are sublinear (Beck–Teboulle; Nesterov), and linear rates typically require strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011). Error bounds for composite problems with polyhedral regularizers (ℓ1, group LASSO) exist (Tseng and others). This work closes a gap for trace/nuclear norm, whose epigraph is non-polyhedral; related nuclear-norm algorithms (Toh–Yun; Ma–Goldfarb–Chen) do not establish linear PGM rates under comparable assumptions. The analysis also interfaces with the Luo–Tseng error-bound theory.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically nontrivial. The restricted invertibility lemma and spectral alignment (Proposition 4.2) are compelling. Two concerns: (i) The theorem requires αk<1/Lf; the matrix completion experiment uses α=1 where Lf=1 for sampling operators, violating strict inequality—please adjust or justify. (ii) The backtracking scheme should be shown to produce αk uniformly bounded away from zero to meet the assumptions.

Clarity: Generally well organized, but Section 4 is dense; moving the intuition about shared singular vectors earlier would help. Some figure numbering/text mismatches and missing axis labels hinder readability.

Originality: High—the error bound for nuclear-norm regularization without strong convexity and the resulting linear rate appear new.

Significance: High—PGM is widely used for low-rank learning; the error bound is of independent interest and may inform analyses of other matrix norms. Comparing with KL-based analyses or restricted strong convexity (e.g., for matrix completion) would further contextualize the scope.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel error bound and linear-rate result for a widely used setting
- Solid, careful analysis with clear assumptions
- Broad applicability (square/logistic losses); insightful structural lemmas

Cons for acceptance:
- Local/asymptotic nature of the rate; limited finite-iteration guidance
- Minor theoretical/expository gaps (stepsize conditions; clarity in Section 4)
- Experiments are small-scale and largely illustrative; no baselines or robustness tests
