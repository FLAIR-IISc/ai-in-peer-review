## Review of "On the Linear Convergence of the Proximal Gradient Method for Trace Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the convergence rate of the Proximal Gradient Method (PGM) for minimizing a convex smooth loss function with trace norm regularization. The authors demonstrate linear convergence for a broad class of loss functions, a significant advancement over existing sublinear or strongly-convex-requiring results. The core contribution lies in establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which is also presented as a potentially valuable independent result.

The paper is well-motivated, framing the problem within the context of numerous applications in machine learning where low-rank matrix recovery is crucial (matrix completion, multi-task learning, etc.). The authors clearly articulate the gap in existing literature – the lack of linear convergence guarantees for PGM without strong convexity assumptions on the loss function. They correctly position their work concerning prior results like those by Beck & Teboulle (2009) and Nesterov (2013), highlighting how their approach overcomes limitations present in those studies.

A key strength is the technical rigor of the proof, utilizing a clever matrix perturbation argument to circumvent the challenges posed by the non-polyhedral epigraph of the trace norm. The inclusion of numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence on matrix completion and classification tasks further strengthens the validity of the theoretical findings. The experiments are well-designed and complement the theory nicely.

However, the paper’s clarity suffers slightly from a dense writing style and a large number of technical details, making it challenging to follow for readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of convex optimization and matrix analysis. While thorough, streamlining the presentation, particularly within the proof of the error bound, would enhance readability.  Additionally, while related work is mentioned, expanding the discussion around existing error bound techniques (e.g., those by Tseng (2001)) and explicitly contrasting their limitations with the authors' approach would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The linear convergence result without strong convexity is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous and builds upon established techniques in a creative way.
*   **Significance:**  The results have practical implications for applications relying on trace norm regularization.
*   **Empirical Validation:** Numerical results support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:**  The dense writing style can hinder understanding.
*   **Related Work:**  Could benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing error bound techniques.



**Overall:** I recommend acceptance.  The paper presents a valuable theoretical result with practical implications, despite minor issues with clarity. Addressing the comments regarding writing style and related work would further enhance the quality of the contribution.
