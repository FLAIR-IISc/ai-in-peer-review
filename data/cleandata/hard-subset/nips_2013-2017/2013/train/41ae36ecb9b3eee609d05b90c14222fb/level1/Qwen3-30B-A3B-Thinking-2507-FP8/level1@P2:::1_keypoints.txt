- Establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method for trace-norm regularized problems min_X {f(X)+τ‖X‖_*} with f(X)=h(A(X)), via a novel Lipschitzian error bound removing the need for strong convexity.  
- Resolves an open question: linear convergence achievable without strong convexity, extending prior results that gave only O(1/k) or required strong convexity.  
- Main technical contribution (Theorem 3.1): a new error bound proved using an observation on singular vectors of optimal solutions (Proposition 4.2) and matrix perturbation theory to handle the trace norm’s non-polyhedrality.  
- Proof is rigorous, well-structured, and appropriately builds on the convergence framework of prior work ([10]).  
- Numerical experiments (matrix completion and matrix classification) empirically demonstrate the predicted linear convergence.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with early articulation of contributions and consistent notation.  
- Properly contextualizes the work relative to prior ℓ1 error-bound results and explains why the trace-norm case is harder.  
- Originality is strong: identifies a literature gap and provides a novel approach; the singular-vector insight is particularly valuable.  
- Significance is substantial: result applies to common ML tasks (e.g., square or logistic loss with nontrivial nullspace) and strengthens theoretical guarantees.  
- The error bound itself could be of independent interest for other trace-norm optimization problems.  
- Strengths: resolves an important open question, provides novel and rigorous analysis, applies to common loss functions, supported by experiments, and is well-written.  
- Weaknesses: technical depth may challenge readers without matrix-analysis background; the class f(X)=h(A(X)) excludes some cases; limited discussion on practical estimation of convergence rates from the bound.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant, novel theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs and practical relevance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility concerns due to technical complexity and a somewhat restricted problem class.  
- Overall recommendation: high-quality, original, and significant work that warrants acceptance.
