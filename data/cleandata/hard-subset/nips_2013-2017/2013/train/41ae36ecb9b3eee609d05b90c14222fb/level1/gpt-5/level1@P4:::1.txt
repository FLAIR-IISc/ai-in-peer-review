Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies composite convex optimization with trace (nuclear) norm regularization: minimize F(X) = f(X) + τ||X||*, focusing on proximal gradient methods (PGM). While standard results guarantee sublinear rates (O(1/k) or O(1/k^2) with acceleration) and linear rates under strong convexity of f, many machine learning losses are not strongly convex (e.g., matrix completion with non-injective A). The main contribution is to show that for losses of the form f(X) = h(A(X)), where A is linear and h is continuously differentiable, Lipschitz gradient, and strictly/locally strongly convex on compact subsets of its domain, the standard PGM achieves asymptotic linear convergence without requiring strong convexity of f.

The key technical tool is a new local Lipschitzian error bound: within a bounded level set and for sufficiently small residual, dist(X, X*) ≤ η ||R(X)||F, where R(X) = Sτ(X − ∇f(X)) − X is the proximal residual (with matrix shrinkage Sτ). With this error bound, the Luo–Tseng framework yields Q-linear convergence of objective values and R-linear convergence of iterates under fixed step sizes 0 < α ≤ αk ≤ ᾱ < 1/Lf. The error bound is nontrivial because the trace norm has a non-polyhedral epigraph, unlike ℓ1 or group LASSO where existing error bounds apply. The proof hinges on a structural property: at any optimum X̄, X̄ and −∇f(X̄) share left/right singular vectors (Proposition 4.2). This enables a perturbation analysis and a “restricted invertibility” relation between distances in variable space and measurement space (Lemma 4.1(c)). Numerical experiments on matrix completion (squared loss) and matrix classification (logistic loss) illustrate linear convergence in the asymptotic regime.

This work complements classical PGM/ISTA/FISTA analyses (e.g., Beck & Teboulle; Ji & Ye; Toh & Yun; Ma et al.) and linear convergence under strong convexity (e.g., Schmidt et al., NIPS 2011), as well as error bound results for polyhedral nonsmooth terms (Tseng; Zhang et al.). It addresses an open question of whether an analogous error bound holds for nuclear norm regularization.

Quality (technical soundness):
- Assumptions: h has Lipschitz gradient and is strongly convex on convex compact subsets of its open domain; A is arbitrary linear; the solution set is nonempty. These are reasonable for common losses (square, logistic) when the iterates remain in bounded level sets, which the paper establishes.
- Main theorems: The local error bound (Theorem 3.1) is precisely stated (local in residual and level set). The linear convergence result (Theorem 3.2) follows from standard descent and residual bounds plus the error bound, with constants depending on α, ᾱ, Lf.
- Proof techniques: The decomposition showing simultaneous SVD of X̄ and −∇f(X̄) and the subsequent perturbation argument (Propositions 4.2, 4.3) are technically careful and tailored to the nonpolyhedral nature of the nuclear norm. The restricted invertibility bound (Lemma 4.1(c)) is the critical step linking distances through A.
- Some derivations (e.g., inequalities (9)–(11), details of Propositions 4.2 and 4.3) are deferred to supplementary material; the main text provides sufficient outline to assess plausibility.
- Scope: The rate is asymptotic (requires small residual), as is typical for error-bound-based analyses. The step size condition (αk bounded away from 0 and below 1/Lf) is standard; backtracking can enforce αk < 1/Lf, and a uniform lower bound can be ensured on bounded level sets, but this could be stated explicitly.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized: motivation, preliminaries, algorithm and residual, main results, proof sketch, and experiments.
- Notation is consistent, and key definitions (proximal map, residual, error bound) are clear. The role of Assumption 2.1(b) and bounded level sets in ensuring local strong convexity of h along the iterates could be highlighted earlier.
- Minor suggestions: clarify the dependence of constants in Theorem 3.1 on the level set bound ζ; explicitly discuss that the linear rate is local/asymptotic and whether global linear convergence can occur under additional conditions (e.g., restricted strong convexity).

Originality:
- The Lipschitzian error bound for nuclear norm regularization under the stated composition structure appears novel. Prior error bounds predominantly covered polyhedral nonsmooth terms or group penalties; the nonpolyhedral trace norm required new spectral arguments.
- Establishing linear rates for PGM without strong convexity of f for these problems is a meaningful theoretical advance.

Significance:
- PGM is a standard tool for nuclear norm regularization in machine learning; improved (asymptotic) rates under realistic loss structures are valuable for theory and may inform practice (e.g., step-size policies, stopping criteria).
- While the result is local and does not provide explicit rate constants, it broadens understanding of when linear convergence can be expected in trace-norm problems.
- The numerical section is illustrative rather than comprehensive; it confirms the asymptotic behavior but does not quantify constants or compare against alternative methods.

Pros:
- Clear, rigorous theoretical contribution: a new error bound for nuclear norm regularization leading to linear convergence without strong convexity of f.
- Addresses a recognized gap relative to ℓ1/group penalties; technique leverages spectral structure in an elegant way.
- Assumptions cover widely used losses (squared, logistic) and arbitrary linear operators A.

Cons:
- Linear convergence is local and asymptotic; no explicit constants or non-asymptotic complexity bounds are provided.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and scope; no comparisons with accelerated PGM/ADMM/ALM or discussion of practical gains.
- The step-size conditions and backtracking strategy are not fully connected to the theory (e.g., ensuring a uniform lower bound on αk on bounded level sets).
- Extensions to accelerated variants, stochastic/inexact proximal steps, or other spectral regularizers are not addressed.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents a technically solid and original theoretical result establishing asymptotic linear convergence of PGM for a broad class of trace-norm–regularized problems via a new error bound. The work is relevant to NIPS audiences interested in optimization for machine learning and advances understanding beyond strong-convexity regimes. The main weaknesses are the asymptotic nature of the result and modest experimental validation; these do not undermine the core theoretical contribution but suggest possible improvements in exposition and empirical context.
