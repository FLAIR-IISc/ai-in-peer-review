This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the proximal gradient method (PGM) applied to trace norm regularized problems, demonstrating linear convergence under relatively mild assumptions – specifically, without requiring strong convexity of the loss function. This is a notable contribution, as existing linear convergence results typically rely on strong convexity. The authors establish a novel Lipschitzian error bound for the problem, which is itself a potentially valuable result.

**Summary of Related Work & Context:** The paper builds upon a significant body of work on trace norm minimization, motivated by applications in low-rank matrix recovery (matrix completion, subspace learning, etc.). It correctly positions itself within the landscape of existing PGM analyses, highlighting the limitations of prior work requiring strong convexity. The cited papers (e.g., Beck & Teboulle, Nesterov, Recht et al.) are relevant and well-placed, demonstrating a solid understanding of the field. The connection to Luo & Tseng’s framework for convergence analysis is also clearly articulated. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The primary strength is the establishment of linear convergence *without* strong convexity. This expands the applicability of the PGM to a wider range of problems, particularly in machine learning where strongly convex loss functions are not always present.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis appears rigorous, building upon a solid foundation of convex optimization theory. The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound is a significant technical accomplishment.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and highlights the contribution. The assumptions are stated explicitly.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of numerical experiments (matrix completion and matrix classification) provides some support for the theoretical findings. The demonstration of linear convergence in practice is encouraging.
*   **Potential for Independent Interest:** The Lipschitzian error bound derived in the paper could be useful in other contexts beyond the specific problem addressed here.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** While avoiding strong convexity is a major strength, the assumptions on the loss function *f(X) = h(A(X))* (specifically, the properties of *h*) are still relatively restrictive. The requirement for strict convexity of *h* on the domain might limit practical applicability.  A more detailed discussion of how commonly used loss functions satisfy these assumptions would be beneficial.
*   **Step Size:** The theoretical guarantee in Theorem 3.2 requires a carefully chosen step size αk satisfying 0 < α < αk < ᾱ < 1/Lf.  While not uncommon in theoretical analyses, this is potentially impractical to implement directly. A practical strategy for choosing αk in real-world applications isn't sufficiently addressed.  A comment on how robust the convergence is to less-than-optimal step size choices would be helpful.
*   **Numerical Experiments – Limited Scope:** The numerical experiments, while present, are somewhat limited in scope. More extensive testing on different datasets and with varying problem parameters would strengthen the empirical validation.  The experiments only show convergence behavior; reporting reconstruction error or other relevant metrics would provide a more complete evaluation.
*   **Proof Details:** While the paper provides a high-level overview of the proof, some of the critical steps (particularly within the proof of Lemma 4.1) are relegated to the supplementary material. This makes it difficult for the reader to fully assess the technical details without a significant time investment.
* **Limited discussion of practical implications:** The paper focuses heavily on theoretical analysis. A brief discussion of how this theoretical result might impact practical algorithm design or parameter tuning would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result that advances our understanding of the convergence properties of the PGM.
*   The removal of the strong convexity requirement is a significant contribution that broadens the applicability of the method.
*   The empirical results provide some support for the theoretical findings.
*   The Lipschitzian error bound may be of independent interest.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The assumptions on the loss function, while less restrictive than strong convexity, might still be limiting in practice.
*   The impracticality of the required step size in Theorem 3.2.
*   The limited scope of the numerical experiments.
*   Lack of clarity in some key proof steps due to reliance on supplementary material.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The theoretical result is novel and significant. While there are some weaknesses, particularly concerning the assumptions and practical implementation of the step size, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with a request for the authors to:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of how common loss functions satisfy their assumptions.
2.  Discuss the sensitivity of the convergence rate to deviations from the optimal step size.
3.  Expand the numerical experiments to include more datasets and relevant performance metrics.
4.  Consider including key parts of the proof from the supplement directly into the main manuscript for improved clarity.
