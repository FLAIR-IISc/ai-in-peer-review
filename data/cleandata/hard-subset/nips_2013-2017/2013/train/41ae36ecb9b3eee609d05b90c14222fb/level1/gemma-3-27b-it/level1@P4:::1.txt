This paper investigates the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for minimizing a convex smooth loss function with trace norm regularization. The authors demonstrate linear convergence under relatively weak assumptions, specifically without requiring strong convexity of the loss function – a notable improvement over existing results. The core contribution lies in establishing a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm-regularized problem, which is leveraged to prove the linear convergence rate using a well-established analysis framework.

The paper is well-structured, starting with a clear introduction outlining the problem and its relevance. The motivation is well-presented, and the authors effectively position their work within the existing literature. The mathematical notation is standard and generally clear, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility, although some steps are deferred to the supplementary material. The presentation of the main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) is concise and well-defined. The inclusion of numerical experiments provides empirical validation of the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The result of establishing linear convergence without strong convexity is a significant contribution, extending the applicability of the PGM to a broader range of problems.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous and builds upon established techniques in convex optimization. The derivation of the Lipschitzian error bound is a key technical achievement.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical nature of the material. The motivation and implications of the results are clearly articulated.
*   **Significance:** The results have practical implications for various applications, including matrix completion and related machine learning tasks. The linear convergence rate can lead to significant computational savings compared to sublinear rates.
* **Empirical Validation**: The inclusion of numerical experiments to support the theoretical claims is a strong point.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** Assumption 2.1(b), requiring strong convexity of *h* on any convex compact subset of its domain, while reasonable, might be restrictive in some applications.  A discussion of the implications of relaxing this assumption could be beneficial.
*   **Complexity of Proof:** The full proof, particularly of the error bound (Theorem 3.1), is detailed and potentially challenging for readers unfamiliar with the intricacies of matrix perturbation theory and related concepts. While deferred to the supplementary material, a more intuitive explanation of the key steps within the main body could enhance understanding.
*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments are relatively limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to include a wider range of problem instances and comparing the PGM’s performance against other state-of-the-art algorithms would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Lack of discussion on practical implications of step size selection:** The paper mentions the need for a proper step size selection but doesn’t delve into strategies for determining appropriate values in practical scenarios, particularly when dealing with non-strongly convex functions.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result with significant implications for the optimization community.
*   The authors clearly demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and position their work effectively.
*   The empirical results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
*   The paper addresses a challenging problem and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical complexity of the proof might limit its accessibility to a wider audience.
*   The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive.
*   The assumption 2.1(b) could be restrictive in some applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of the PGM for trace norm regularization. The demonstrated linear convergence rate without strong convexity is a valuable result with practical implications. While the technical details are complex and the numerical experiments could be more extensive, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, such as providing a more intuitive explanation of the error bound and expanding the numerical experiments.
