This paper studies the convergence rate of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for solving trace norm regularized optimization problems of the form `min f(X) + τ ||X||_*`. This formulation is central to many machine learning tasks, including matrix completion, multi-task learning, and matrix classification. While PGM is known to have a sublinear O(1/k^2) convergence rate (with acceleration), a linear rate was previously only established under the strong assumption that the loss function `f` is strongly convex. However, many common loss functions, such as the square loss `f(X) = ||A(X) - b||^2` for an underdetermined linear operator `A`, are not strongly convex.

The main contribution of this paper is to prove that PGM achieves an asymptotic linear convergence rate for this problem under a much weaker set of assumptions. Specifically, it is assumed that `f(X)` has the structure `h(A(X))`, where `h` is strictly convex and smooth, but `A` is an arbitrary linear operator. This covers many important non-strongly convex cases. The key technical innovation is the derivation of a new Lipschitzian error bound for the trace norm regularized problem. This bound relates the distance from a point `X` to the optimal set to the norm of a residual map that measures the violation of the first-order optimality conditions. Establishing such a bound for the trace norm is non-trivial, as prior techniques for `l1` or group Lasso norms, which rely on separability, do not apply. The authors overcome this by identifying and exploiting a key structural property of the optimal solutions: an optimal matrix `X*` and the corresponding gradient `-∇f(X*)` share the same singular vectors. This insight allows the use of matrix perturbation theory to analyze points near the optimum and establish the desired error bound. The linear convergence of PGM then follows from this error bound via the analysis framework of Luo and Tseng [10].

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and well-motivated problem. The discrepancy between the sublinear theoretical convergence rates and the often-observed linear convergence of PGM in practice for problems like matrix completion has been a notable gap in the literature. This work provides a strong theoretical explanation for this phenomenon, extending linear convergence guarantees to a broad and practical class of problems without requiring strong convexity.
*   **Originality:** The core technical contribution—the Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization (Theorem 3.1)—is highly original and resolves what the authors convincingly argue was an open question. The proof strategy is novel and clever. Unlike previous error bound analyses for separable norms, the authors develop a new approach based on the shared singular vector space of the optimal solution and the negative gradient. This is a sophisticated and insightful use of matrix analysis that is well-suited to the non-separable nature of the trace norm.
*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The assumptions are stated precisely, and the theoretical claims are well-supported by a detailed proof outline. The argument, which hinges on proving the "restricted invertibility" property (Lemma 4.1c) by contradiction, is rigorous. The main results (Theorems 3.1 and 3.2) are substantial contributions to the optimization literature for machine learning. The provided numerical experiments on matrix completion and classification, while not exhaustive, effectively illustrate and support the main theoretical finding of linear convergence.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation and a concise review of related work that perfectly frames the paper's contribution. The main results are stated upfront in Section 3, followed by a detailed sketch of the proof in Section 4. This structure makes the paper easy to follow despite the technical depth of the material. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the key difficulties (e.g., non-separability of trace norm) and their high-level ideas for overcoming them.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The linear convergence result is asymptotic. The analysis does not provide a characterization of the convergence rate constant or the size of the region in which linear convergence takes hold. Understanding how these quantities depend on problem parameters (e.g., the operator `A`, the function `h`, the regularization parameter `τ`, and properties of the optimal solution) would be of great practical interest but is beyond the scope of the current analysis.
*   **Reliance on Supplementary Material:** Due to space constraints, several key steps of the proof are deferred to the supplementary material (e.g., the proofs of inequalities (9)-(11) and the crucial Proposition 4.3). While this is understandable, it makes a full and independent verification of the results from the main paper difficult. The proof of Proposition 4.3, which describes the structure of the limiting direction `Q̄`, seems particularly central to the argument.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the linear convergence tail predicted by the theory. However, their scope is somewhat limited. A comparison with cases where the theory's assumptions are violated would have been insightful. Furthermore, exploring how the onset of the linear phase is affected by problem conditioning or the sampling ratio `θ` would have added further value.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first linear convergence guarantee for PGM on trace norm regularized problems without strong convexity, a significant theoretical advancement.
*   Resolves an open problem by establishing a novel Lipschitzian error bound for trace norm regularization.
*   The proof technique is original and could inspire further research into error bounds for other matrix norm regularization problems.
*   The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The results are highly relevant to the NIPS community, as they concern the analysis of a fundamental algorithm applied to a widely used model.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is asymptotic, providing limited practical guidance on the actual convergence speed.
*   The experiments are illustrative rather than exhaustive.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental and elegant contribution to the theory of first-order methods in machine learning. The strengths, particularly the significance and originality of the theoretical results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper bridges a critical gap between theory and practice and is likely to be highly influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
