

This paper establishes linear convergence of the proximal gradient method (PGM) for trace norm-regularized problems of the form min_X {f(X) + τ‖X‖_*}, where f(X) = h(A(X)) with h strictly convex and smooth. The key contribution is a novel Lipschitzian error bound for this problem, resolving an open question about whether linear convergence can be achieved without strong convexity assumptions on f. This extends prior work by [19] (which established sublinear convergence rates O(1/k) for general convex f) and [16] (which required strong convexity).

The paper demonstrates exceptional technical quality. The authors establish the error bound (Theorem 3.1) through a clever observation about the singular vectors of optimal solutions (Proposition 4.2), enabling the application of matrix perturbation theory to overcome the non-polyhedral nature of trace norm regularization. The proof is rigorous, well-structured, and properly references the convergence analysis framework of [10]. The numerical experiments on matrix completion and matrix classification clearly demonstrate linear convergence in practice, supporting the theoretical claims.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The main contributions are articulated early in the introduction, and the technical arguments flow logically from problem setup to proof. The authors properly contextualize their work relative to prior NIPS papers, noting that while error bounds exist for ℓ1-norm regularization [23], the trace norm case remained open due to its non-polyhedral epigraph. The notation is consistent and the paper provides sufficient background for readers familiar with convex optimization.

The originality is strong and well-justified. The authors correctly identify the gap in the literature and provide a novel approach to the problem. The key insight about singular vectors of optimal solutions (Proposition 4.2) is particularly valuable, as it enables the proof where previous methods for ℓ1-norm regularization fail. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior work, noting that their result applies to a broader class of loss functions than strong convexity would allow.

The significance is substantial. Many machine learning applications (matrix completion, multi-task learning, matrix classification) use trace norm regularization with loss functions that are not strongly convex (e.g., square loss when A has a non-trivial nullspace). The linear convergence result provides a stronger theoretical guarantee for these applications. The error bound itself could be of independent interest for other optimization problems involving trace norm regularization.

Strengths:
- Resolves an important open question about linear convergence without strong convexity
- Provides a novel error bound with rigorous technical proof
- Applies to common loss functions (square loss, logistic loss) used in machine learning
- Supported by numerical experiments demonstrating linear convergence
- Well-written with clear exposition of main ideas

Weaknesses:
- Technical depth of the proof may challenge readers without strong matrix analysis background
- The class of loss functions considered (f(X) = h(A(X))) excludes some potentially interesting cases
- Limited discussion of how the error bound could be practically applied to estimate convergence rates

Arguments for acceptance:
- Makes a significant theoretical contribution to convex optimization for machine learning
- The result is novel and fills an important gap in the literature
- Proof is rigorous and claims are well-supported
- Has immediate practical implications for many machine learning applications

Arguments against acceptance:
- Technical complexity might limit accessibility to some readers
- The restricted class of loss functions (though broad enough for most applications)

Overall, this paper is of high quality, original, and significant. It makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of optimization methods for machine learning problems. The result is both theoretically interesting and practically relevant, and the proof is technically sound. The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and should be accepted. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field, and their work will likely influence future research on optimization methods for structured machine learning problems.
