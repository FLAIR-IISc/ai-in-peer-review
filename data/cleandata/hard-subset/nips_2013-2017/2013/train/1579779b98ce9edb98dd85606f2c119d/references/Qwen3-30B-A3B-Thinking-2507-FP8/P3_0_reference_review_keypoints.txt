- Provides the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, showing ε-Hannan consistent selection leads to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.

- Extends the connection between regret minimization and Nash equilibrium from matrix games to extensive-form simultaneous move games via induction on tree depth.

- Addresses an important theoretical gap given widespread empirical use of MCTS in simultaneous-move domains.

- Theoretical novelty and rigor: well-structured proof and substantial contribution to the literature.

- Clear theoretical framework: precise definitions of required properties (Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) and linkage to online learning theory.

- Practical relevance: gives theoretical justification for MCTS variants used in real simultaneous-move applications.

- Empirical validation: experiments (including worst-case games) show proposed algorithms (RMM, Exp3M) converge to approximate equilibria with competitive rates.

- Well-organized presentation with careful definitions, notation, and logical flow connecting theory and experiments.

- Scope limitation: results restricted to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, excluding imperfect-information and more general EFGs.

- No finite-time convergence bounds; only asymptotic convergence is proved, limiting practical theoretical guarantees.

- Insufficient empirical comparisons: claims of comparable convergence rates lack quantitative benchmarks against state-of-the-art methods.

- Ambiguity in terminology: "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" is used without a clear definition for approximate equilibria across subgames.

- Weak connection to UCT: paper cites UCT’s failure to converge but doesn't explicitly show why UCT fails the required Hannan consistency or exploration properties.

- Error-bound clarity: Theorem 4.11's error bound (D² + δ) needs clearer derivation and explanation of inductive error accumulation.

- Practical implication gap: paper doesn't fully discuss how parameters (e.g., exploration γ) affect real-world convergence trade-offs.

- Empirical paradox unexplained: mean propagation (RMM) converges slower than sample propagation (RM) despite theory requiring mean propagation.

- Suggest clarifying the subgame-perfect definition for approximate equilibria explicitly in the main text.

- Suggest explaining why UCT fails to satisfy Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration to strengthen theoretical claims.

- Suggest detailing how the D² + δ error bound arises from the inductive proof and how errors accumulate across depths.

- Suggest adding quantitative empirical comparisons (e.g., iterations to exploitability < 0.01) against state-of-the-art simultaneous-move methods.

- Suggest discussing practical choices between regret matching and Exp3 based on theoretical and empirical trade-offs.

- Suggest addressing the mean vs. sample propagation discrepancy and explaining its implications for algorithm design.

- Overall assessment: significant, rigorous theoretical contribution that fills a key gap, despite scope and empirical limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept — contribution and rigor justify publication, though improvements and clarifications are needed.
