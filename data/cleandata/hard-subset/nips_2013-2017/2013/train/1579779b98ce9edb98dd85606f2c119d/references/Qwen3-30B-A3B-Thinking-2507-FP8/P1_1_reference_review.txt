

# Peer Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this class of games, proving that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (ensuring each action is attempted infinitely often), it converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper makes a novel connection between online learning theory (specifically Hannan consistency) and MCTS, extending previous work on MCTS in sequential games to simultaneous move settings. The authors empirically validate their theoretical claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the underlying selection procedures, demonstrating comparable convergence rates to existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games.

The paper builds on prior work in game theory (backward induction for simultaneous move games [7, 8, 9]), MCTS in sequential games [14], and online learning algorithms [15, 16], but makes a crucial contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which had been an open problem. Previous work on MCTS for simultaneous move games [10, 11, 12, 17] lacked theoretical justification for convergence to Nash equilibrium, with Shafiei et al. [17] even showing that UCT (a popular selection policy) does not converge to Nash equilibrium in simple simultaneous move games.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is novel and well-motivated.

2. **Rigorous Proof**: The proof is carefully structured, starting with matrix games and building up to extensive-form games through induction. The authors address important technical challenges, including bounded error in repeated matrix games and the composition of games at different tree levels.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The experimental section provides strong support for the theoretical claims. The authors compare their approach with previous MCTS variants, analyzing both the influence of propagating mean values versus current samples and the convergence rates on worst-case games.

4. **Clear Problem Statement**: The paper clearly identifies the gap in the literature and positions its contribution within the broader context of game theory and MCTS research.

5. **Honest Assessment of Limitations**: The authors correctly note that their analysis does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, acknowledging this as a direction for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications**: While the paper establishes convergence guarantees, it doesn't deeply explore the practical implications of the theoretical results. For instance, how do the convergence rates of the Hannan-consistent algorithms compare to UCT in real-world applications where time constraints are tight?

2. **Some Ambiguity in Definitions**: The paper could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of why Hannan consistency is the appropriate property for MCTS convergence. The connection between online learning and game-theoretic convergence could be made more explicit.

3. **Limited Comparison with Alternative Methods**: While the paper mentions that their approach explains the convergence of existing MCTS algorithms, it doesn't provide a direct comparison of convergence rates between their Hannan-consistent algorithms and the best-performing UCT variants in practice.

4. **Supplementary Material Reference**: The paper references supplementary material for omitted proofs, but the review process doesn't have access to this material. This is acceptable, but the main paper should ideally contain the most critical proofs.

5. **Potential Overstatement in Conjecture**: The authors conjecture that "many of these algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium and that our formal analysis could be extended to include them." This is a bit speculative without some preliminary evidence or a more nuanced discussion.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The proofs are carefully constructed and the experiments are well-designed. The authors are careful to distinguish between what they prove (convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium) and what they don't (finite-time convergence bounds). The paper is a complete contribution rather than a position paper, with a clear problem statement, theoretical analysis, and empirical validation.

## Clarity

The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The definitions are clear, and the mathematical notation is consistent. The figures (Figures 1-3) effectively illustrate the key concepts and results. The main weakness in clarity is the somewhat abrupt transition from matrix games to extensive-form games; a brief intuitive explanation of why the induction step works would be helpful. The paper would benefit from a short discussion of the intuition behind using Hannan consistency as the selection criterion.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While MCTS has been widely used in sequential games, and Hannan consistency has been studied in matrix games, the connection between the two in the context of MCTS for simultaneous move games is novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly from [17] which showed UCT doesn't converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games. The connection to online learning algorithms (regret matching and Exp3) is a novel application of these techniques to MCTS.

## Significance

This paper is highly significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretically, it provides a foundation for understanding when MCTS will converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games. Practically, it justifies the use of MCTS as an approximation algorithm for this class of games, which includes applications in security domains, general game playing, and other areas mentioned in the introduction. The results are likely to influence future work on MCTS for games with simultaneous moves, and the authors correctly identify finite-time convergence bounds as an important direction for future research.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution that addresses an important open problem in the MCTS literature. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides both theoretical and empirical validation of the claims. The results are clearly important for the field and advance the state of the art in understanding MCTS convergence properties.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro (Accept):**
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Novel connection between online learning (Hannan consistency) and MCTS
- Rigorous proof structure with careful handling of technical challenges
- Empirical results supporting theoretical claims
- Clear contribution to a well-defined gap in the literature

**Con (Reject):**
- Limited discussion of practical implications of theoretical results
- Some ambiguity in explaining why Hannan consistency is the right property to use
- Limited comparison with the best-performing MCTS variants in practice
- The conjecture about extending the analysis to other algorithms is somewhat speculative

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory and MCTS. The paper is suitable for publication at NeurIPS.

## Minor Suggestions for Improvement

1. Consider adding a brief intuitive explanation of why Hannan consistency is the appropriate property for MCTS convergence (beyond the technical definition).

2. In the experimental section, include a comparison of the convergence rates of the Hannan-consistent algorithms versus the best-performing UCT variants in real-world applications.

3. Clarify the relationship between the theoretical convergence rate and the practical convergence rate observed in experiments.

4. Consider discussing the computational overhead of using Hannan-consistent algorithms versus simpler selection policies like UCT.

5. Add a brief discussion of how the results might generalize to games with imperfect information, as this is a common extension of simultaneous move games.
