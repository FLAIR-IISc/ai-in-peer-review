This paper tackles a challenging and important problem: understanding the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games. While MCTS is widely used in practice, theoretical guarantees are lacking, particularly outside of the well-studied sequential game setting. The authors make a significant step towards addressing this gap by providing convergence guarantees for a general template of MCTS algorithms utilizing -Hannan consistent regret minimizing procedures. The core idea of leveraging existing theoretical results for matrix game solving within an MCTS framework is clever and well-executed.

The paper’s strengths lie in its novelty and theoretical rigor. The convergence proof, even with the simplifying assumption of complete tree building, is a substantial contribution. The connection to -Hannan consistency is insightful and provides a clear path for justifying the use of various exploration strategies. The empirical evaluation, though not exhaustive, lends further support to the theoretical findings and demonstrates comparable performance with existing algorithms. The acknowledgement of the limitations – specifically, the difficulty of extending the analysis to algorithms like UCB – is also appreciated.

However, the paper is not without its weaknesses. The reliance on the complete tree-building assumption, while acknowledged, feels somewhat restrictive. While the authors argue it simplifies the analysis and doesn't fundamentally change the outcome, it diminishes the immediate practical impact of the theorem. It's unclear how easily the results translate to the iterative, tree-growth process characteristic of MCTS. Furthermore, the empirical evaluation is limited in scope. Comparing to more recent and sophisticated MCTS algorithms designed specifically for simultaneous moves beyond those cited would strengthen the claims. The exploration of the implications of the proposed template for existing algorithms (as hinted at in the conclusion) feels superficial. A more detailed analysis, even if speculative, would be valuable. The presentation, while generally clear, could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the mathematical formalism, particularly for readers less familiar with online learning theory. The formatting of some equations is also slightly awkward.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper introduces a new theoretical framework for analyzing MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a largely unexplored area.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The convergence proof is a significant achievement, providing a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of MCTS in this setting.
*   **Potential Impact:** The results could guide the development of more robust and theoretically grounded MCTS algorithms for a wide range of applications.
*   **Soundness:** The approach of connecting MCTS to regret minimization in matrix games is logically sound and well-justified.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Simplifying Assumption:** The reliance on complete tree building limits the immediate practical applicability of the results.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are somewhat limited and could be strengthened by comparing to more recent algorithms.
*   **Superficial Exploration of Implications:** The discussion of extending the analysis to existing algorithms lacks depth.
*   **Presentation:** The mathematical formalism could be more accessible to a wider audience.

**Overall:**

Despite the minor flaws, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty and theoretical rigor outweigh the limitations. The convergence guarantee, even under the simplifying assumption, is a valuable result and opens up new avenues for research. I am willing to overlook the incomplete validation and suggest **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the clarity of the mathematical notation and a more thorough discussion of the potential for extending the results to existing MCTS algorithms. The authors have identified a crucial gap in the literature and taken a bold step towards filling it. This work is likely to stimulate further research in this area and has the potential to significantly impact the development of more effective MCTS algorithms for simultaneous-move games.
