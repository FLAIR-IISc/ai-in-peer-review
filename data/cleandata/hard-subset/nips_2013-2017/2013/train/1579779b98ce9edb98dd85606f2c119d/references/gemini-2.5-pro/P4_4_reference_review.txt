### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS variants are commonly used for this game class in practice, a formal proof of their convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been an open problem. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games.

The main contribution is a formal proof that if the action selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the resulting SM-MCTS algorithm's average strategy converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is inductive on the depth of the game tree. It begins by leveraging known results from online learning, showing that two ε-Hannan consistent players converge to an approximate Nash Equilibrium in a single matrix game. This result is then extended to matrix games where payoffs are noisy estimates with a bounded error. This extension is crucial for the inductive step, as the values of subgames in MCTS are estimated from simulations and are inherently noisy. By modeling the game tree as a stack of matrix games where subgame values are payoffs with bounded error, the authors prove convergence for the entire extensive-form game.

The paper provides concrete instantiations of this framework using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. It also includes an empirical evaluation on randomly generated and worst-case games, comparing the convergence speed of different MCTS variants, including a version that propagates mean values (as required by the proof) versus the standard approach of propagating the most recent sample value.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The primary claim—that MCTS based on ε-Hannan consistent selection converges to a subgame perfect ε'-NE—is well-supported by a rigorous, multi-stage proof. The theoretical analysis is the core strength of the paper. The authors are careful in their definitions (e.g., ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) and build their argument logically from established results in repeated games to the more complex extensive-form setting. The modeling of subgame value estimation as a matrix game with bounded error is an effective and key insight. The empirical section provides a reasonable validation of the theory, confirming that the proposed algorithms do converge in practice and exploring the performance impact of the assumptions made for the proof.

A minor weakness is the disconnect between the variant proven to converge (which propagates the mean of rewards) and the variant that is more common in practice and performs slightly better empirically (propagating the single sample). While the authors conjecture their proof could be extended, the current result applies to a slightly suboptimal algorithm. Additionally, the derivation of the final error bound in Theorem 4.11 is explained very briefly in the main text, with the details deferred to the appendix. A more intuitive sketch of how the error accumulates across levels of the game tree would have improved the self-containedness of the main paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation and background, defines the necessary concepts precisely, and presents the main algorithm template. The structure of the formal analysis in Section 4 is particularly clear, progressively building from simple repeated matrix games to the full extensive-form setting, which makes the complex proof easier to follow. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly. Figures are legible and support the conclusions drawn in the text. Algorithm 1 provides an unambiguous description of the general SM-MCTS framework.

**Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is highly original. While MCTS, Regret Matching, and Exp3 are all existing techniques, this is, to the best of my knowledge, the first work to formally prove the convergence of an MCTS-based algorithm to a Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move games. Previous work has either used MCTS heuristically in this domain or has shown non-convergence for specific variants like UCB-based selection. This paper bridges an important theoretical gap between the practice of game-playing AI and the theory of online learning and algorithmic game theory. The synthesis of these fields and the specific proof technique are novel and insightful.

**Significance:** The results are significant. They provide a firm theoretical justification for using MCTS as a principled approximation algorithm for a broad and important class of games. This work gives practitioners confidence that algorithms based on regret minimization within MCTS are not merely heuristics but are guaranteed to approach an optimal solution. Researchers are likely to build on this work, for instance by seeking to tighten the convergence bounds, proving convergence for other MCTS variants (like the one propagating single samples), or extending the analysis to more complex game classes, such as those with imperfect information. The connection established between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is a valuable contribution that may inspire new algorithmic designs.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, solving an important open problem and providing a strong theoretical foundation for a practical class of algorithms.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, well-structured, and appears correct. The proof technique of modeling subgame estimations as games with bounded error is elegant.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow, making a complex theoretical contribution accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Theory-Practice Gap:** The proof formally applies to a variant of MCTS that propagates mean values, which the authors' own experiments show is slightly slower than the standard variant. While the authors conjecture the proof can be extended, this is not shown.
2.  **Lack of Convergence Rate:** The analysis is asymptotic and does not provide finite-time bounds on the convergence rate, limiting its practical predictive power. (Note: The authors acknowledge this as future work, so it is a minor point).
