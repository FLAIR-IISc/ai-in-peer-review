

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a theoretically interesting gap in the literature: convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors propose a general template for SM-MCTS using -Hannan consistent selection functions, and prove that such algorithms converge to subgame perfect -Nash equilibria. They empirically evaluate their approach using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection procedures.

The paper makes a valid contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a specific class of games. The authors correctly identify that while MCTS variants are widely used in practice for simultaneous move games, their theoretical properties remain poorly understood. The formal analysis extends existing results on Hannan consistency from repeated matrix games to multi-stage games, which is a technically sound approach.

However, I have significant concerns about the novelty, significance, and empirical validation of this work. The theoretical contribution is largely incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core insight—applying established Hannan consistent algorithms (regret matching and Exp3) to the MCTS framework—builds directly on well-known results in online learning and game theory. The authors cite [15, 16] as establishing the Hannan consistency of these algorithms, and their extension to multi-stage games follows standard inductive reasoning. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its contribution from existing work on MCTS convergence in sequential games, where similar theoretical analyses have been performed.

The empirical evaluation is notably weak. The authors only test on small, randomly generated games with limited depth (max depth 2 in the worst-case analysis), and do not compare against state-of-the-art MCTS variants that are actually used in practice. Figure 3 shows convergence to "minimal achievable values" that correspond to the exploitability of the optimal strategy with uniform exploration (γ=0.05), suggesting their algorithms aren't significantly better than simple exploration methods. The paper claims "empirical speed of convergence ... comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms," but fails to provide any meaningful comparison to these methods.

The most concerning aspect is the paper's overstatement of novelty. The authors claim this is "the first formal analysis of convergence of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves," but this is inaccurate. The paper [20] (cited in the paper) already provides a convergence analysis for a similar MCTS approach in simultaneous move games. The authors also fail to properly contextualize their work against existing theoretical results on MCTS convergence in sequential games (e.g., [14, 21]).

The paper's claim that "many of these algorithms also converge to -Nash equilibrium" without rigorous proof is particularly troubling. The authors merely conjecture that their analysis could be extended to include other MCTS variants, but provide no evidence to support this claim. This is a classic case of presenting a theoretical framework without sufficient empirical validation.

The theoretical results are also limited in practical utility. The convergence guarantee is asymptotic, with no finite-time bounds provided. The convergence rate depends on the depth of the game tree (D² in Theorem 4.11), which makes the guarantee less useful for practical applications where finite-time performance matters.

While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a specific class of games, it fails to demonstrate significant advancement over existing work. The theoretical extension is straightforward, the empirical validation is insufficient, and the paper overstates its novelty. The results do not provide meaningful guidance for practitioners, as the proposed methods converge slower than standard MCTS implementations (as shown in Figure 2).

Strengths:
- Correctly identifies an important theoretical gap in MCTS analysis
- Provides technically sound (though incremental) theoretical results
- Good formalization of the game-theoretic framework

Weaknesses:
- Overstates novelty without proper contextualization
- Insufficient empirical validation (limited to small games, no comparison to practical methods)
- No finite-time convergence bounds
- Theoretical results have limited practical utility
- Fails to address why their approach is superior to existing MCTS variants

I cannot recommend acceptance. While the paper is technically sound, it makes only a minor theoretical contribution that does not advance the state of the art significantly. The empirical evidence is insufficient to support the claims of practical relevance, and the paper's overstatement of novelty undermines its credibility. The work would be better suited for a more specialized game theory or theoretical computer science venue rather than NIPS, where the emphasis is on impactful contributions to machine learning.
