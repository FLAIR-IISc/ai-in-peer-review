

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper provides the first formal convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors develop a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures all actions are attempted infinitely often), the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper empirically validates these theoretical results using regret matching and Exp3 as selection procedures, demonstrating convergence rates comparable to existing MCTS algorithms for these games.

The work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as previous MCTS convergence guarantees were limited to sequential games [14, 20], while simultaneous move games are prevalent in practical applications [10, 11, 12, 13, 17]. The authors connect their analysis to regret minimization theory [15, 16] and build upon the established framework of Hannan consistency.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: This is the first paper to provide formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a significant gap in the literature. The induction-based proof technique is elegant and well-structured, progressing from leaf nodes up through the game tree.

2. **Generality and Flexibility**: The proposed template is general enough to accommodate any ε-Hannan consistent selection procedure, making the results widely applicable to various existing and future MCTS variants.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The empirical analysis effectively supports the theoretical claims. The comparison between mean propagation versus current sample value (Figures 2-3) provides valuable insights into implementation details that impact convergence speed.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with precise definitions, logical flow of arguments, and clear connections between theoretical results and empirical validation. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings.

5. **Appropriate Scope**: The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature that has been noted in prior work [17], making it highly relevant to the NIPS community interested in algorithmic game theory and reinforcement learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The empirical evaluation is restricted to small games (depth ≤ 2, branching factor ≤ 2) and randomly generated instances. Larger, more complex games that would better represent practical applications are not evaluated.

2. **Lack of Finite-Time Bounds**: While the paper proves convergence, it does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, which would be valuable for practitioners. The empirical analysis shows convergence rates but doesn't explicitly relate them to the theoretical guarantees.

3. **Missing Practical Considerations**: The paper doesn't discuss implementation challenges, such as computational complexity of maintaining regret tables or the impact of exploration parameter γ on convergence speed.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The comparison with existing MCTS algorithms is limited to the empirical section. A more thorough comparison with recent algorithms designed specifically for simultaneous move games would strengthen the paper.

5. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The authors don't thoroughly discuss potential limitations of their approach, such as how game structure might affect convergence rates or potential failure cases.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs are well-structured and the theoretical results are supported by empirical validation. The authors carefully address both the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly in the empirical analysis. The paper is a complete piece of work, not a position paper.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The definitions and theorems are clearly presented, and the connection between theoretical results and empirical analysis is well-explained. The use of figures (Figures 2 and 3) to illustrate key findings is helpful.

**Originality**: This is a highly original contribution that bridges a significant gap in the literature. While MCTS has been widely applied to simultaneous move games [10, 11, 12, 13, 17], the lack of theoretical guarantees was a known limitation. The paper is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on MCTS, which have primarily focused on sequential games.

**Significance**: The results have important implications for the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a broad class of games. By providing convergence guarantees, the paper justifies the use of MCTS as an approximation algorithm for simultaneous move games from a game-theoretic perspective. This work is likely to be influential for researchers working on game theory, reinforcement learning, and search algorithms.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant, the analysis is sound, and the empirical results support the claims. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and provides a solid foundation for future work on MCTS in simultaneous move games.

## Specific Comments

1. The paper states that "the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on our template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms" but doesn't provide a direct comparison with specific algorithms. Including a table comparing convergence rates with specific algorithms would strengthen this claim.

2. The conjecture that "many existing MCTS algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium" would be more convincing with a brief explanation of why these algorithms might be covered by the analysis.

3. The worst-case analysis is limited to depth 2 games. It would be valuable to discuss how the worst-case behavior might scale with larger games.

4. The proof of Theorem 4.11 relies on error accumulation across levels but doesn't provide a detailed explanation of how this accumulation works. A more detailed explanation would improve the clarity of the proof.

5. The paper mentions that "the value of the game" is used in the theoretical guarantees, but doesn't explicitly connect this to the exploitability measure used in the empirical analysis.

## Conclusion

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The work is well-executed, with sound theory and empirical validation. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS as it addresses a meaningful gap in the literature and provides valuable theoretical insights for practitioners working with MCTS in game-theoretic settings.
