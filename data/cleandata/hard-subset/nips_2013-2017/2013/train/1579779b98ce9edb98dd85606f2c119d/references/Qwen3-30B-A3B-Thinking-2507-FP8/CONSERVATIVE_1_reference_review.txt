

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper proposes a formal convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors present a general MCTS template using -Hannan consistent selection functions (like regret matching and Exp3), proving that such algorithms converge to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They also provide empirical validation on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios.

The paper's main strength lies in its technical contribution: establishing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a gap the authors correctly identify in the literature. The theoretical framework builds on established results from online learning (Hannan consistency), which lends credibility to the approach. The proofs appear sound, with well-structured induction arguments and clear definitions. The paper also addresses a genuine limitation in the MCTS literature, as the authors note "no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves."

However, as a reviewer who prioritizes practical significance alongside theoretical rigor, I have significant concerns about the paper's contribution to the field. The most troubling aspect is the contradiction between the theoretical claim and empirical results: the authors claim that mean propagation (RMM) is necessary for convergence, but their Figure 2 shows RMM converges *slightly slower* than RM (current sample value propagation) in all tested scenarios. This directly contradicts their theoretical foundation and raises questions about the practical utility of their formal analysis. If the mean propagation variant is theoretically required but empirically inferior, the theoretical contribution loses practical relevance.

The empirical evaluation is limited and fails to address the paper's most important question: why should practitioners care about these convergence guarantees? The authors test on randomly generated games with small branching factors and limited depth (max depth 2 in worst-case analysis), which bears little resemblance to real-world applications where MCTS is typically used. For instance, they don't evaluate on games like Goofspiel (cited in the introduction) or other real-world simultaneous move games where MCTS has been successfully applied. Without evidence that their theoretical guarantees translate to better practical performance in meaningful scenarios, the contribution appears academic rather than impactful.

Furthermore, the paper makes an unsubstantiated claim that "no convergence guarantees are known" for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While it's true that previous MCTS variants lacked formal guarantees, the Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) algorithm has long provided convergence guarantees for similar game classes, with variants specifically designed for simultaneous move games. The authors should engage more deeply with this established literature to clarify how their work differs from and improves upon CFR-based approaches.

The paper also fails to address a critical practical question: why would a practitioner switch from the empirically superior RM (current sample value) to the theoretically justified but empirically slower RMM? The authors acknowledge that "in practice, we set wh,i = xh,i−maxi′∈A1(h) xh,i′" for numerical stability, but don't explain why this practical adjustment doesn't invalidate their theoretical claims.

The significance of this work is therefore questionable. While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, it doesn't provide actionable insights for practitioners. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the theoretical guarantees translate to improved performance in real-world applications, which is the primary concern for the NeurIPS community. The empirical evidence actually undermines the practical value of the theoretical contribution by showing that the theoretically "correct" approach (mean propagation) performs worse than the empirically successful approach (current sample value).

In summary, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, but the practical significance is limited due to the empirical contradiction between theory and practice, the lack of real-world validation, and failure to demonstrate how the theoretical results improve upon existing approaches. For NeurIPS, where practical impact is as important as theoretical rigor, this paper falls short of the expected standard. The theoretical analysis is interesting but not sufficiently compelling to justify its publication in a venue that prioritizes both theoretical and practical significance.

I recommend rejection. While the paper is well-written and the theoretical contribution is valid, the disconnect between theory and practice, coupled with the limited empirical evaluation, prevents it from making a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the empirical evaluation with real-world applications and address the critical contradiction between their theoretical claims and empirical results before this work would be suitable for NeurIPS.
