Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) online learning algorithm (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) as the local action selector at each node. The main theoretical result shows almost-sure convergence of the induced SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with an approximation bound that grows with game depth D (Theorem 4.11: roughly O(D^2 ε) + δ). The analysis proceeds by: (i) establishing convergence in repeated matrix games under HC play; (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff error; and (iii) propagating bounded errors up the game tree via induction, provided backups use mean returns and selection ensures persistent exploration. Experiments on random games and small worst-case instances compare regret matching and Exp3 variants with current-sample versus mean backups, showing convergence in practice and only a modest slowdown when using mean backups.

Relation to prior work
- UCT performs well in many domains but can fail to converge to NE in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). Backward induction with LP or double oracle can solve these games but is expensive and needs evaluation functions.
- Hannan-consistent algorithms (regret matching, Exp3) guarantee no-regret in matrix games; the paper lifts these guarantees to stacked matrix games via MCTS with exploration and mean backups.
- To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence result for MCTS to (approximate) NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs, filling a gap between practice (SM-MCTS used in e.g., Tron, Goofspiel) and theory.

Strengths
- Novelty: Establishes a clear, general convergence theorem for SM-MCTS using a broad family of HC selectors. This is a meaningful conceptual contribution, bridging no-regret learning and MCTS in simultaneous-move trees.
- Generality: The template is agnostic to the specific HC algorithm (RM, Exp3, others), requiring only guaranteed exploration. The analysis covers stochastic payoffs via bounded error and proves subgame-perfect convergence.
- Conceptual clarity: The “repeated game with bounded error” abstraction is a useful device to carry regret guarantees through the tree and should be reusable beyond this specific setting.
- Practical relevance: Empirical results indicate that the theoretically-required mean backups do not dramatically harm convergence, and that both RM and Exp3 variants converge on random and worst-case small games.

Weaknesses
- Bounds and rates: The asymptotic result has a loose dependence on depth (O(D^2)) and does not provide finite-sample rates. This limits practical guidance, especially in deeper games.
- Mean backups requirement: The proof critically relies on backing up mean returns, which is not standard in many MCTS implementations and can slow learning; the paper shows small slowdowns empirically but does not analyze the gap theoretically or reconcile with more commonly used sample backups.
- Experimental scope: Experiments are restricted to random small trees and exhaustive search over tiny worst-case instances; there is no evaluation on larger benchmark domains or comparison against strong baselines beyond RM/Exp3 variants (e.g., bespoke solvers or domain-specific SM pruning).
- Assumptions: Guaranteed exploration is handled via an exploration wrapper that does not update internal statistics during exploratory steps; while technically convenient, this can be practically suboptimal and may further slow convergence.
- Presentation: Some pseudocode and notation (e.g., RetVal, Update definitions, and consistency of average vs empirical frequencies) could be clearer. Most proofs are deferred to the appendix, and key lemmas (e.g., error compounding) could benefit from more intuition in the main text.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level; the proof strategy is standard in regret-based game analysis and appears carefully adapted to the simultaneous-move, perfect-information setting. However, the tightness of constants, depth dependence, and lack of finite-time bounds leave room for improvement. Experiments support the asymptotic claims but are limited in scope.

Clarity
Generally readable and well organized. Some parts (Algorithm 1’s return/update semantics, precise role of mean backups, the exploration wrapper) would benefit from clearer exposition and explicit discussion of practical trade-offs.

Originality
High. To my knowledge, this is the first convergence guarantee for MCTS to (approximate) NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs using general HC selectors. The “repeated game with bounded error” framing and subgame-perfect guarantee are noteworthy.

Significance
Moderate to high. The results provide theoretical foundations for a widely used heuristic (SM-MCTS) in a class of games of practical interest. Despite limitations (asymptotic, depth dependence, mean backups), the work is likely to influence subsequent theory and practice, and suggests pathways to more general settings.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide any finite-time convergence statements (even loose) to guide practice.
- Tighten the dependence on depth if possible (e.g., O(D) rather than O(D^2)), or clarify why D^2 is intrinsic.
- Analyze when sample backups can be used without losing convergence, or propose bias-corrected estimators that recover similar guarantees.
- Expand experiments to larger, structured domains (e.g., deeper Goofspiel variants) and compare to specialized solvers (e.g., alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning, double oracle) where feasible.
- Clarify Algorithm 1’s RetVal/Update semantics and the exploration wrapper’s effect on learning speed.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning). The paper makes a clear, novel, and broadly applicable theoretical contribution that opens a useful direction. While the bounds are asymptotic and the experiments modest, the conceptual advance outweighs these limitations.

Confidence
High.
