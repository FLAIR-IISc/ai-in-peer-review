Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”), a class that includes domains such as Goofspiel and pursuit–evasion. It proposes a general Simultaneous-Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) template instantiated with any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) selection algorithm that also guarantees sufficient exploration (e.g., regret matching, Exp3). The main theoretical result shows that, if nodes back up mean returns, SM-MCTS with such a selection rule almost surely converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE). The approximation error grows with game depth; the paper informally claims convergence to ε-NE and formally states a bound scaling with D (reported as “(D^2+δ)–NE,” though the exact dependence on ε and constants deserves clarification). The proof proceeds by: (i) establishing that HC learners in repeated matrix games converge (in empirical frequencies) to approximate NE; (ii) extending to “repeated games with bounded error”; and (iii) an induction over tree depth, propagating bounded errors from leaves upward. Empirically, the paper compares mean-backup vs. sample-backup variants of RM and Exp3 on random and small worst-case games, observing that mean backups are slightly slower but still converge; all variants approach the exploration-constrained optimum.

This work complements prior uses of MCTS in simultaneous move games (e.g., Tron, general game playing) where UCT does not have convergence guarantees and can fail to reach NE in simple simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). It leverages classic online learning results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) on HC algorithms in matrix games and relates to alpha–beta variants for simultaneous moves and double-oracle pruning for exact/deterministic search. Compared to CFR-style regret minimization for imperfect-information EFGs (e.g., Zinkevich et al., 2007), this work specifically targets perfect-information simultaneous-move games and provides MCTS-style convergence guarantees under HC selection.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The high-level proof strategy—reducing node-wise play to repeated matrix games, accommodating bounded payoff errors via mean backups, and inducting over depth—is coherent. The lemmas about convergence under ε–HC (with and without bounded error) are standard in spirit; the transition from observed noisy payoffs to bounds against the true matrix game is plausible.
- The guaranteed exploration modification A* and the claim that it preserves ε–HC up to an additive γ is reasonable, and the requirement that each joint action be sampled infinitely often is clearly stated.
- The necessity of mean backups to control bias/variance and enable the bounded-error argument is appropriate; the empirical section supports that sample backups remain effective in practice, though they are outside the formal proof.
- Some details need tightening for rigor and reproducibility:
  - Theorem 4.11’s bound is written as “(D^2 + δ)–NE” but omits the dependence on ε and constants from earlier lemmas (e.g., the 4(c+1) factor). The precise constant and scaling should be stated explicitly.
  - It is stated that RM and Exp3 “generally” guarantee exploration; in practice RM requires explicit mixing (as done in Section 3.1), so the text should be consistent about when guaranteed exploration holds natively vs. via γ-mixing.
  - Lemma 4.5 (empirical vs average strategy equivalence) is plausible (LLN with bounded martingale differences), but a more explicit statement of its conditions would help.
  - The formal analysis temporarily assumes the entire tree is built and argues this occurs eventually with probability 1; this reduction should be stated precisely (e.g., via Borel–Cantelli given guaranteed exploration).

Clarity
- The paper is mostly well organized: definitions, algorithm template, two instantiations, theory, and experiments. The “mean vs sample backup” design choice is clearly highlighted.
- There are some notation/typo issues:
  - Ensure the final convergence bound includes ε and explicit constants; currently ambiguous.
  - Algorithm 1 would benefit from clearer pseudocode, especially for node expansion, bookkeeping of Xh and Xh,ij, and the definition of RetVal and Update to disambiguate per-joint-action statistics.
  - The RM update uses x(h,i′,j) = Xh,ij; clarify what estimator Xh,ij holds when (i′,j) has few samples.
  - Several references lack years/pages; add complete citations. Cite CFR for context on regret minimization in EFGs.
- Figures are summarized but not shown; ensure axes, parameters, and confidence measures are clear in the final version.

Originality
- To the best of available evidence, this is the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games under HC selection, yielding subgame-perfect approximate NE. The reduction to repeated games with bounded error for depth-wise induction is a novel and useful combination.
- The algorithmic instantiations (RM, Exp3) are standard, but their use within SM-MCTS under mean backups with proven convergence is new for this setting.

Significance
- The result gives theoretical justification for a widely used heuristic (MCTS) in a non-trivial game class where UCT lacks guarantees. This is valuable for researchers building agents in simultaneous-move domains.
- The bound is asymptotic and potentially loose (growing with D, seemingly quadratically, and with constants from exploration/error), and no finite-time rates are provided. Thus, practical impact on algorithm selection/tuning is limited at this stage.
- Experiments demonstrate qualitative convergence and compare backup choices, but are small scale; stronger empirical validation on established benchmarks would increase significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Provides the first convergence proof of SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE under widely used HC selection rules with guaranteed exploration.
- Clear reduction from EFGs to repeated matrix games with bounded error; conceptually clean and likely extensible.
- Empirical results, while modest, align with theory and offer practical guidance on mean vs sample backups.

Cons against acceptance
- Final bound in Theorem 4.11 is ambiguously stated and likely loose; constants and ε-dependence need to be explicit.
- No finite-sample convergence rates; purely asymptotic guarantees.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and baselines; claims of comparability to other SM-MCTS variants are not thoroughly substantiated.
- Some clarity and citation gaps (e.g., exploration in RM, relation to CFR literature, incomplete references).

Suggestions for improvement
- State the precise convergence bound in Theorem 4.11, including ε and constants propagated from Lemma 4.9 and exploration γ; provide a brief derivation sketch.
- Clarify the exploration properties of RM vs Exp3 and consistently apply γ-mixing where needed.
- Detail the data structures (per-node joint-action statistics) and the Update/RetVal procedures to remove ambiguity; discuss the bias/variance trade-offs of mean vs sample backups.
- Add experiments on recognized benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) with larger depths/branching, sensitivity to γ, and comparisons to other SM-MCTS baselines and to exact/backward-induction methods where feasible.
- Include or cite finite-time regret-to-exploitability links if possible, or discuss obstacles to deriving rates.
- Complete and expand related work, including CFR and more recent NeurIPS/AAAI work on MCTS in simultaneous-move or multiagent settings.
