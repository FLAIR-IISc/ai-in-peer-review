

# Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. While MCTS variants like UCT have been widely used in practice for such games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), no convergence guarantees to Nash equilibria were previously known. The authors present a general MCTS template that uses any ε-Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as the selection function. They prove that under this framework, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper complements its theoretical analysis with empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3 as selection functions, demonstrating convergence rates comparable to existing methods.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a rigorous formal proof of convergence that proceeds in a logical, inductive manner, starting with simple matrix games and building to the full extensive-form game setting. The proof structure is sound, with clear definitions of key concepts (ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) and careful handling of the bounded error introduced by the MCTS process. The authors are appropriately cautious about the limitations of their analysis - they acknowledge that their framework does not cover all existing MCTS variants (like UCT) but conjecture that those could be shown to converge as well.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors systematically compare their mean-propagation variants (RMM, Exp3M) against current-sample variants (RM, Exp3) across different game depths, branching factors, and worst-case scenarios. The results consistently support their theoretical claims, showing that the mean-propagation variants converge to approximate Nash equilibria, with the difference between mean and current-sample propagation being small but consistent (mean-propagation converges slightly slower, as predicted by theory).

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The authors effectively bridge the gap between game theory and MCTS by providing sufficient background on both domains. The notation is well-defined and consistent throughout, with the necessary mathematical concepts (Hannan consistency, regret minimization) carefully explained. The algorithm descriptions are precise and accompanied by helpful figures (Figure 1 showing the game structure, Figure 2 and 3 presenting empirical results). The transition from matrix games to extensive-form games is particularly well-explained, making the complex induction argument accessible.

The only minor clarity issue is in Section 4.3, where the formal definition of a "repeated game with error" could be slightly more intuitive. However, this is a minor point given the overall clarity of the paper.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution. While MCTS has been applied to simultaneous move games in practice (e.g., [10, 11, 12]), this is the first paper to provide formal convergence guarantees for such algorithms. The key insight - that ε-Hannan consistent selection functions can guarantee convergence - is novel and elegantly connects two active research areas: online learning (regret minimization) and Monte Carlo tree search.

The paper distinguishes itself from prior work by focusing specifically on simultaneous move games, which have different properties than sequential games. Previous convergence results for MCTS (e.g., for sequential games [21]) do not apply here. The authors appropriately cite related work on backward induction [7-9] and UCT [14] but clearly identify the gap in convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides the first theoretical foundation for the widespread empirical use of MCTS in simultaneous move games. This bridges a critical gap between theory and practice in algorithmic game theory and AI. The results will be valuable to both researchers developing new MCTS algorithms and practitioners applying these algorithms to real-world problems (e.g., security games, general game-playing).

The paper also opens several promising research directions: finite-time convergence bounds, generalization to imperfect information games, and extension to non-zero-sum games. The authors' conjecture that many existing MCTS variants also converge to ε-Nash equilibria (Section 1) suggests this work could have broader implications for the field.

## Weaknesses

The paper is largely strong, but there are a few minor weaknesses:

1. The empirical evaluation could include more games beyond the "worst-case" examples. While the worst-case analysis is valuable, a broader set of benchmarks would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. The paper mentions that "most HC algorithms... guarantee exploration without any modification," but it doesn't explicitly state why this is true for RM and Exp3. A brief explanation would improve the paper's completeness.

3. The connection to NIPS specifically could be strengthened. While the paper addresses a topic relevant to ML (MCTS is used in reinforcement learning), it might be helpful to explicitly discuss how this contributes to ML theory or practice.

## Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The theoretical results are novel, the empirical evaluation supports the claims, and the significance of the work is clear. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

The authors have successfully bridged the gap between the theoretical guarantees of regret minimization and the practical application of MCTS, providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this important class of games. This work should be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working on algorithmic game theory, reinforcement learning, and decision-making under uncertainty.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to a practically important problem, with sound analysis and empirical validation. It meets all the NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is well-written and provides a clear path forward for future research.

## Pro/Con Summary

**Pro:**
- First formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with careful handling of technical details
- Thorough empirical evaluation supporting theoretical claims
- Clear writing and well-structured presentation
- Significant implications for both theory and practice

**Con:**
- Minor opportunity to expand empirical evaluation with more game types
- Slightly more explicit explanation of why RM/Exp3 guarantee exploration would be helpful
- Limited explicit connection to ML/NIPS community beyond game theory

This paper represents a substantial advance in understanding MCTS convergence properties and should be accepted for publication at NIPS.
