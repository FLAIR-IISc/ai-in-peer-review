Summary
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses any no-regret (ε-Hannan consistent) selection algorithm at each node. The key theoretical result shows that, assuming guaranteed exploration and mean-value backpropagation, the average strategies induced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds bottom-up: (i) no-regret dynamics in a single matrix game approach value; (ii) extend to repeated matrix games with bounded observation error; (iii) inductively propagate bounded errors up the game tree to obtain a depth-dependent approximation bound. Empirically, the paper instantiates the template with regret matching and Exp3, and compares backpropagating current samples vs means, showing convergence on random and small worst-case games, with mean backprop converging slightly slower.

Relation to prior work
- UCT/MCTS has strong empirical success in sequential perfect-information games, but its behavior with simultaneous moves is poorly understood; prior work (Shafiei et al.) gives a negative example for UCT even in a one-shot game. This paper targets that gap by giving the first convergence guarantees for MCTS variants in simultaneous-move, perfect-information, zero-sum settings.
- Exact solvers (backward induction with α–β pruning, double oracle) exist but require domain evaluation or LP/DO subroutines and are costly at depth; the proposed approach is simulation-based and learning-theoretic.
- The analysis leverages classical no-regret/online learning results (Hannan consistency, regret matching, Exp3) and their convergence implications in zero-sum games, adapted to the stacked structure with bounded-error propagation.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: A unifying template showing that “MCTS + no-regret selection + guaranteed exploration + mean backprop” converges to subgame-perfect ε-NE in simultaneous-move games. To my knowledge, this is the first such formal guarantee for this class.
- Solid theoretical scaffold: clean reduction from leaf-level no-regret dynamics to depthwise error accumulation via repeated games with bounded error. The “guaranteed exploration” modification and the distinction between empirical frequencies vs average strategies are handled carefully (martingale SLLN).
- Practical relevance: Instantiations with regret matching and Exp3 are natural and widely used. Empirical results, although small-scale, are consistent with the theory and highlight a practical trade-off (mean vs sample backprop).
- Subgame perfection: The analysis yields subgame-perfect guarantees, not just root-level convergence.

Weaknesses and questions
- Depth dependence and constants: The main theorem states a bound of roughly O(D^2) times ε (the notation “( D2 + δ )” in Theorem 4.11 is ambiguous—please clarify whether this is ε·D^2 or 2(D+1)(ε+…) etc.). The constants stack from Lemma 4.9 (4(c+1)) and the induction; a precise, explicit bound would greatly improve readability.
- Mean backprop requirement: The proof requires backpropagating averages instead of instantaneous samples. This is nonstandard in MCTS practice. It would help to (i) clarify whether there’s a proof route for sample backprop under additional conditions, or (ii) give stronger empirical evidence that mean backprop does not harm performance significantly at scale.
- “Guaranteed exploration” modification A*: Exploration steps do not update the learner in Def. 4.3. This may be overly conservative and slow learning. Can the results be extended to the standard “mixture with uniform” where updates still occur? Empirically, how sensitive are convergence and exploitability to γ?
- Completeness of tree expansion: The analysis assumes the tree eventually becomes complete with probability 1. A short lemma sketch (or reference) showing that exploration suffices to expand all nodes in finite branching games would be helpful.
- Empirics are limited: Experiments are on random utilities and exhaustive tiny games; no results on larger or more structured simultaneous-move domains (e.g., Tron variants, Goofspiel instances with realistic sizes). Also, the worst-case search uses only 100 runs; error bars and statistical tests are missing.
- Scope: The setting is perfect information, zero-sum. Discussion on whether the approach could extend to general-sum or imperfect-information EFGs (e.g., relation to CFR) would broaden impact.

Quality
- The theoretical claims are well-motivated and (modulo the notational ambiguity on constants) appear sound. The structure—matrix games → bounded-error repeated games → stacked games—is compelling.
- Empirical section is honest but modest; it supports convergence qualitatively, but does not probe scalability, parameter sensitivity, or comparisons to other simultaneous-move MCTS variants beyond citing consistency.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Suggestions:
  - Fix notation in Theorem 4.11 to explicitly show the ε, D, and constant factors.
  - Emphasize early that mean backprop is required for the proofs, and consistently denote the SM-MCTS-Mean variant.
  - Some references are incomplete; clean bibliography formatting.

Originality
- High. The convergence result for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games under no-regret selection is, to my knowledge, new and opens a clean bridge between online learning and search in this setting.

Significance
- Significant for researchers working on MCTS and game-theoretic planning in simultaneous-move domains. It provides a principled way to design convergent SM-MCTS variants and a framework that could be extended (finite-time rates, broader game classes).

Pros
- First convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE.
- General template applicable with standard no-regret learners (RM, Exp3).
- Clean theoretical decomposition; aligns with practice.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee.

Cons
- Ambiguity and likely looseness in the final depth-dependent bound; no finite-time rates.
- Requires mean backprop and explicit exploration; practical implications not fully explored.
- Limited empirical validation and scale.

Questions for authors
- Please clarify the exact bound in Theorem 4.11 (explicit constants and ε dependence).
- Can you allow updates during exploration without breaking the proof?
- Any insight on extending to imperfect-information or general-sum simultaneous-move games?

Recommendation
Accept (leaning positive for novelty and conceptual contribution). While the experiments are limited and constants are loose, the paper opens a new, principled direction for SM-MCTS with formal guarantees, which I value highly.

Confidence
High in the assessment of novelty and main ideas; moderate in fine-grained technical constants pending clarification.
