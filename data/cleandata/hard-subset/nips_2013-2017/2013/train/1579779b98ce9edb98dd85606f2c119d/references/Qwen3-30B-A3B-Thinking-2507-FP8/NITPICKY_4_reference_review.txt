

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for MCTS algorithms that uses any -Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure as the action selection function, and prove that such algorithms converge to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The theoretical analysis is complemented by empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection procedures.

## Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a significant gap in the literature. The theoretical framework is well-structured, building from matrix games to extensive-form games through induction. The empirical evaluation on randomly generated games provides useful insights into the practical performance of the proposed methods. The connection between regret minimization in online learning and MCTS convergence is conceptually sound.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inconsistencies in Algorithm Description**: 
   Section 3.1 describes the regret matching update rule as "∀i′ ∈ A1(h), rh[i′]← rh[i′] + (x(h, i′, j)− u1)." This is incorrect - the regret update should be based on the difference between the payoff of the chosen action and the payoff of the alternative action, not a fixed value u1. The correct update should involve the payoff of the specific action taken, not the utility returned from the recursion.

2. **Notation and Definition Errors**: 
   - In Definition 4.1, there's an extra comma: "lim supt→∞, r(t) ≤" should be "lim sup_{t→∞} r(t) ≤"
   - In Definition 4.8, the error parameter is inconsistently denoted as "c" in the definition but "η" in the condition: "|aij(t)− aij | < η"
   - Theorem 4.11 states "subgame perfect (D2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium" where D is "maximal depth" - D² is unclear and likely a typo for D times some constant.

3. **Missing Figures and Incomplete References**: 
   The paper references "Figure 2" in Section 5.1, but no figure is provided in the text. Additionally, the first reference is incomplete (missing year, pages), and the reference list contains inconsistent formatting.

4. **Overstated Claims**: 
   The paper claims "no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general EFGs," which is inaccurate. While the specific analysis presented here is novel, there is prior work on MCTS convergence in similar settings (e.g., [17] mentioned in the paper discusses convergence properties for specific cases).

5. **Formatting and Clarity Issues**: 
   The paper uses inconsistent notation for strategies (σ̂1(t) vs σ̄1(t)) without clearly explaining the difference. The transition from matrix games to extensive-form games in Section 4.4 is not sufficiently detailed for a rigorous proof. The statement "the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on our template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms" lacks specific comparisons to prior work.

6. **Minor but Significant Technical Oversight**: 
   The paper claims "we disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree" (Section 4), but fails to justify why the incremental tree construction doesn't affect the convergence guarantees. This is a critical assumption that requires more careful justification.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting contribution to the convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous move games, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notation errors, and overstated claims significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The minor methodological flaws identified (particularly in the algorithm description and theoretical justification) are substantial enough to question the validity of the main results. 

The paper's main contribution is valuable, but the presentation contains too many technical errors and inconsistencies for it to be accepted in its current form. The authors should carefully revise the algorithm descriptions, fix all notation inconsistencies, provide proper justification for the tree construction assumption, and temper their claims about prior work.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but critical technical flaws that compromise the paper's technical soundness. While the theoretical framework is promising, the execution contains too many errors to be considered a complete and rigorous contribution. The paper would benefit from a thorough technical review to fix these issues before submission.
