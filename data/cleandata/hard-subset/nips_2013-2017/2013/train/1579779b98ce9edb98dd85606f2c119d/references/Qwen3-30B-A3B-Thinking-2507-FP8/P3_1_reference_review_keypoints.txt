- Paper proves convergence of a general MCTS template to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in two-player zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games using ε-Hannan consistent selection.
- Empirical validation uses Regret Matching and Exp3, showing the RMM variant (mean propagation) converges to ε-Nash.
- Theoretical contribution: first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games linking Hannan consistency to MCTS.
- Proof is rigorous and elegant, using induction on depth and online learning results to handle repeated matrix-game errors.
- Definitions and formalism are clear (game model, MCTS variants, Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration).
- Practical insight: using mean of previous samples (RMM) rather than current sample (RM) is necessary for theoretical guarantees.
- Experiments are designed to test the theoretical claim and compare RMM with standard RM.
- Scope is limited to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, reducing broader applicability to real-world imperfect-information domains.
- No finite-time convergence bounds are provided; only asymptotic convergence is shown.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: small games (depth 2, branching 2) with random utilities, lacking larger or more realistic benchmarks.
- Missing comparisons to other algorithms (double-oracle, CFR variants) and other MCTS variants for simultaneous-move games (e.g., UCT comparisons).
- Insufficient discussion of practical limitations, including the impact of exploration parameter γ on convergence and scalability to larger games.
- Methodological simplification: analysis assumes the complete tree is built before convergence, which may not hold in incremental tree growth in practice.
- Reinforces the necessity of mean propagation (RMM) over current-sample RM for theoretical convergence.
- Related work is adequate but could better connect to recent advances in imperfect-information game solving (e.g., CFR).
- Empirical claims (e.g., relative difficulty of games across methods) lack deeper analysis or evidence.
- Significance is limited by narrow problem class; extension discussions to imperfect-information games are lacking.
- Section 4.4 should clarify how exploration parameter γ affects convergence rate and bounds.
- Section 5.2 needs more detailed analysis explaining why certain games are harder for methods.
- Conclusion should discuss potential extensions to imperfect-information games alongside finite-time bounds.
- Figures need improvements (e.g., error bars for variance across runs) to better show result reliability.
- Include a comparison of convergence behavior with UCT on the same games to contextualize contributions.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, novel theoretical contribution, well-written, but limited by scope and empirical breadth.
- Recommended revisions: compare with other simultaneous-move methods, test larger/challenging games, discuss extensions to imperfect-information games, and pursue finite-time convergence bounds.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
