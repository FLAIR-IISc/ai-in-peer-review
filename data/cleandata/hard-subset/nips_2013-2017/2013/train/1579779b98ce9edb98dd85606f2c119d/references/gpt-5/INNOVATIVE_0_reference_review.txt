Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where, at each state, a selection function is any Hannan-consistent algorithm for matrix games (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3), with guaranteed exploration. The main theoretical result proves eventual convergence to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium: if the selection algorithm is ε–Hannan consistent and actions are explored infinitely often, then the average strategies induced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-NE, where D is the game depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The proof proceeds by (i) analyzing repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to “repeated matrix games with bounded error,” and (iii) propagating these bounds inductively up the tree. A technical condition required by the analysis is to back up mean returns rather than the latest sample. Empirically, the authors compare RM/Exp3 with and without mean backups on random/worst-case small games; all variants converge, with mean backups slightly slower.

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual advance: To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The idea to instantiate SM-MCTS with any ε–Hannan-consistent bandit/online-learning algorithm and to analyze convergence via bounded-error repeated games is elegant and broadly applicable.
- Theoretical contribution: Clear decomposition of the argument: (a) approximate no-regret dynamics imply approach to NE in matrix games; (b) robustness of no-regret convergence to bounded payoff errors; (c) inductive composition over depth with explicit accumulation of error, yielding subgame-perfect guarantees. The “repeated games with bounded error” lemma is a useful technical tool beyond this setting.
- Generality: The template covers multiple algorithms (RM, Exp3) and suggests that other regret-minimization–based selectors should also inherit convergence, which can catalyze further work on principled search in simultaneous-move domains.
- Empirical sanity checks: Although small-scale, the experiments corroborate asymptotic convergence, contrast mean vs sample backups, and probe worst-case instances.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical gap due to mean backups: The main theorem requires backing up the mean value Xh/nh to control error propagation. Standard MCTS backs up the latest sample. While experiments show both variants converge, the proof does not cover the sample-backup variant. Can the analysis be extended using martingale arguments or importance-weighted/unbiased estimators to recover guarantees for standard backups (perhaps with additional assumptions)?
- Asymptotic only; no rates: The results are eventual convergence without finite-time bounds. Given the field’s interest in sample efficiency, can you derive horizon- and regret-dependent rates (e.g., polynomial in D, 1/ε, and regret rates of the selector)? Even a coarse rate would strengthen the practical message.
- Accumulated approximation: The (ε D^2 + δ) bound appears loose. The proof sketch mentions “players can gain 2d by deviating” and summing across levels. Is the D^2 dependence necessary, or can it be tightened to O(D ε) or better with a sharper induction (e.g., contracting bias using mixing or variance reduction)?
- Scope limitations: The setting is perfect information; simultaneous moves but no hidden information. While the class is motivated and includes interesting games, exact solutions are polynomial via backward induction; thus the main significance is theoretical understanding of search dynamics, not computational tractability. It would help to clarify when SM-MCTS is preferable in practice (e.g., large branching, limited evaluation functions), and to include at least one medium-scale domain (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) to demonstrate practical viability.
- Exploration policy: The guarantees rely on guaranteed exploration (e.g., uniform γ-mixing). In practice, fixed exploration induces a floor on exploitability (observed in experiments). Can you discuss decaying exploration schedules that preserve infinite visitation while allowing asymptotic removal of this floor? Does the proof go through with γt → 0 if ∑t γt = ∞?
- Clarity/notation: The presentation is dense; a compact theorem box (assumptions, statement) for the main result, and an intuition figure for the error-propagation induction, would improve readability. Also, ensure consistent notation for empirical vs average strategies and for backups.

Relation to prior work
- The paper is well-situated with respect to UCT, RM, Exp3, and simultaneous-move pruning (alpha-beta, double oracle). It would be useful to contrast more explicitly with CFR/MCCFR lines: while CFR addresses imperfect information and guarantees convergence in normal-form reach-weighted regrets, here the novelty is online search with simultaneous moves and bandit feedback at nodes; clarifying these distinctions will help readers situate contributions.

Pros for acceptance
- First convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games.
- Clean, modular theoretical contribution likely to influence follow-up work (e.g., search with no-regret selectors, extensions to richer EFGs).
- Empirical results consistent with theory; actionable insights (mean backups vs sample; exploration effects).

Cons for acceptance
- Asymptotic-only guarantees with loose dependence on depth; limited empirical validation on small synthetic games.
- Mean-backup requirement departs from common MCTS practice; lack of coverage for standard variant.

Suggestions for improvement
- Attempt finite-time bounds leveraging known rates for RM/Exp3 and a refined error-propagation analysis.
- Explore decaying exploration and its theoretical implications.
- Provide a proof or counterexample for convergence with sample backups; or propose a practically friendly unbiased backup estimator covered by the analysis.
- Add at least one larger, recognized benchmark to demonstrate practical utility.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and novel theoretical direction—convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games via Hannan-consistent selection with guaranteed exploration—and supports it with reasonable empirical evidence. Despite asymptotic focus and some looseness in constants, the conceptual contribution is strong and opens avenues for broader theoretical and practical advances.

Confidence
High.
