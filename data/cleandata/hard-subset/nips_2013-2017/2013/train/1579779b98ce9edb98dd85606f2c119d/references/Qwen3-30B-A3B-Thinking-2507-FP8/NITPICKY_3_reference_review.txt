

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, establishing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria when using -Hannan consistent selection functions. The authors provide a general template for SM-MCTS algorithms and prove convergence under the assumption of -Hannan consistency with guaranteed exploration. They empirically evaluate regret matching and Exp3 variants, showing comparable convergence rates to existing methods.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a significant contribution to the field. The formal analysis is thorough, with clear definitions and well-structured proofs. The empirical evaluation provides valuable insights into the practical implications of the theoretical results. The paper is well-written overall and makes a clear case for the novelty of their approach.

**Weaknesses:** Despite its strong theoretical contributions, the paper contains several minor but significant issues that undermine its technical precision and clarity:

1. **Notational inconsistencies:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. The key term "subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium" (in section 4.4) should be "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" (with epsilon, not a minus sign). The paper alternates between "NE" and "Nash equilibrium" without consistency. The game value is denoted as both "v" and "vh0" without clear justification, creating confusion.

2. **Formatting errors:** Algorithm 1 has inconsistent indentation (sometimes 2 spaces, sometimes 4 spaces), which is distracting and unprofessional. In the Exp3 section, the formula "wh,i = xh,i1" appears to be a typo (should likely be "wh,i = xh,i" or similar). The paper uses non-standard notation "〈0, 1〉" for the utility interval, which should be "[0, 1]".

3. **Technical ambiguities:** The paper claims to "disregard the incremental building of the tree" but fails to adequately justify why this simplification doesn't affect the convergence results. The statement "RetVal(u1, Xh, nh) = Xh/nh" is introduced as necessary for the formal analysis but isn't clearly explained as to why this is required. The paper states "RMM converges slightly slower in all cases" without providing statistical significance or error bars for these observations.

4. **Minor typographical errors:** The regret matching section contains "oth." (likely a typo for "otherwise"), and the definition of M(t) in Definition 4.8 uses inconsistent formatting for the payoff function "aij(t, (i1, ...it−1), (j1, ...jt−1))".

5. **Inconsistent terminology:** The paper uses "mean" and "average" interchangeably when referring to the return value, creating confusion about whether it's using the mean of all samples or the average strategy.

6. **Missing justification:** The paper claims that "most HC algorithms, namely RM and Exp3, guarantee exploration without any modification" but doesn't provide a brief explanation for why this is true, which would strengthen the argument.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the understanding of MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games, these minor but pervasive issues in notation, formatting, and technical precision significantly detract from the paper's quality. The paper's claim of being the "first formal analysis" is likely accurate, but the presentation of this result is undermined by these inconsistencies.

The paper would benefit from a careful proofreading to address these minor but important issues. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the technical execution is not as polished as would be expected for a NIPS submission. The minor flaws are not enough to invalidate the core contribution, but they collectively create a sense of carelessness that affects the paper's credibility.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the paper has significant theoretical merit, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and formatting issues, which are particularly egregious in a paper claiming to provide rigorous mathematical analysis, prevent it from meeting NIPS' high standards for technical precision and presentation. The paper would be significantly stronger with careful attention to these details.
