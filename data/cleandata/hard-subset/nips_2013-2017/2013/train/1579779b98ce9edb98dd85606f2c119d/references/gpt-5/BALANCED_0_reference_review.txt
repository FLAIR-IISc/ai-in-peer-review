Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that, when instantiated with any ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) selection rule that guarantees exploration and when using mean-value backups, converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε’-Nash equilibrium of the full game. The main theorem shows that the average strategies converge to a subgame-perfect (D^2 ε + δ)-NE, where D is the game tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The analysis proceeds by: (i) recalling HC learning in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending it to repeated games with bounded payoff errors, and (iii) inductively propagating bounded errors up the tree under mean backups. Empirically, the paper instantiates the template with Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, compares mean vs sample backups, and reports convergence on random games and on small adversarially selected cases.

This addresses a gap: while UCT enjoys strong results in sequential perfect-information games (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and CFR provides guarantees in imperfect-information EFGs, theory for simultaneous-move MCTS has been lacking; prior work even shows UCB-based selection can fail to reach NE in such settings (Shafiei et al.). The paper also positions against exact backward induction with LP/double-oracle pruning (Saffidine et al., Bosansky et al.), which can be costly and needs evaluation functions. The use of HC learning (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) as the selection engine is natural; the novelty lies in handling tree-structured composition with bounded-error analysis and establishing subgame-perfect convergence.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction from tree to repeated games with bounded errors and the induction over depth are sensible. The necessity of mean backups to control error accumulation is clearly motivated. The exploration wrapper A* and its (ε+γ)-HC guarantee are standard in bandit literature. The proof sketch is coherent; full proofs are claimed available in an appendix.
- Some technical clarity concerns remain around the RM instantiation: the update uses x(h,i’,j) derived from cumulative values Xhij; it would help to clarify whether this RM variant is HC under bandit feedback (classical RM needs full-information or unbiased estimators). This does not undermine the main theorem (which only assumes an ε-HC selector) but affects how strong the empirical instantiations are theoretically.
- No finite-time rates are provided, and the asymptotic approximation scales as D^2 ε (and empirically also with γ), which can be loose.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with clear notation, assumptions, and algorithmic template. The distinction between mean vs sample backups is explicit and tied to the analysis.
- Minor issues: Algorithm 1 could more explicitly separate node-level statistics (Xh, nh and per-joint-action Xhij) from selection-algorithm internal state; clarify whether exploration steps skip all updates or only those “belonging to A,” and whether node statistics are still updated during exploration. For RM, specify the feedback model and whether importance weighting or other unbiased estimators are used to preserve HC.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games to subgame-perfect approximate NE under general HC selectors. The repeated-game-with-error lens and the induced D-level induction are neat and broadly applicable. The need to back up means, while not standard in practice, is a crisp insight.

Significance
- The results justify and guide the use of MCTS in simultaneous-move domains and connect practice (RM/Exp3-based SM-MCTS) to NE computation. They may inform algorithm design in general game playing, pursuit-evasion, and card-game variants with simultaneous bids/actions.
- However, the asymptotic nature, lack of rates, and loose dependence on depth limit immediate practical impact. The experiments are informative but small-scale.

Pros
- First general convergence theorem for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad HC selection rules.
- Clean decomposition via repeated games with bounded error; generality beyond specific algorithms.
- Empirical evidence that mean vs sample backups have similar behavior; study of hard small games.
- Clear discussion of exploration and subgame perfection.

Cons
- Requires mean backups; standard sample backups lack a proof (though they appear to work empirically).
- No finite-sample convergence rates; the (D^2 ε) bound is likely loose.
- RM instantiation’s HC under bandit feedback is not justified; selection/update details could be tighter.
- Experiments are limited (small depths/branching), with no comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., CFR variants, alpha-beta with pruning on small games), and lack variance/error bars.
- Practical considerations (rollout policy dependence, memory growth for full expansion) are not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the RM update and its information model; either adopt a bandit-robust regret minimizer (e.g., Exp3-IX, RM with unbiased estimators) or state that RM is heuristic in experiments.
- Provide finite-time bounds by combining known regret rates for HC algorithms with your error-propagation analysis; even depth-dependent rates would be valuable.
- Analyze the gap between mean and sample backups: can concentration/martingale arguments recover convergence for sample backups, perhaps with averaging at selection time?
- Expand experiments: larger random games, sensitivity to γ and rollout policies, and comparisons to CFR/alpha-beta on small instances.
- Make explicit whether exploration updates node-level estimates; disentangle selection-state from value-estimation state in the pseudocode.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is novel and relevant for NeurIPS, grounding a popular heuristic in simultaneous-move games with a general convergence result. While the empirical section is modest and some implementation details need clarification, the main ideas are sound and likely to stimulate follow-up work on rates and broader EFG classes.

Confidence: medium.
