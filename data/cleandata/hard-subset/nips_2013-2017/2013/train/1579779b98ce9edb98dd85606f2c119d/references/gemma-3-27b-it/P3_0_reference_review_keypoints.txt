- Paper proves convergence guarantees for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, using a -Hannan consistent selection function to reach a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium.  
- Introduces a general SM-MCTS template applicable with any -Hannan consistent regret minimizer.  
- Instantiates template with Regret Matching and Exp3 and provides empirical validation on random and worst-case games.  
- Addresses an important open problem by providing a theoretical foundation for MCTS beyond sequential games.  
- Template’s generality makes the approach broadly applicable and extensible.  
- Proof is rigorous, leveraging Hannan consistency and decomposing the problem into repeated matrix games with bounded error.  
- Empirical results support theoretical claims and show competitive performance with existing algorithms.  
- Writing is clear, definitions precise, and the paper is well organized.  
- Related work coverage is thorough and demonstrates good literature awareness.  
- No theoretical bound on convergence rate is provided; experiments offer only limited insight into speed of convergence.  
- Convergence guarantee includes a D^2 constant factor, which may limit practical applicability.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to random and constructed worst-case games; lacks real-world game benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel).  
- Theoretical reliance on -Hannan consistency assumes attainment in practice, which can be challenging.  
- Repeated mention of UCB’s non-convergence feels repetitive and distracts from core contributions.  
- Proof sketch in Section 4.4 glosses over how errors propagate across subgames; more detail needed.  
- Algorithm 1’s “RetVal” function should be more explicitly defined and motivated.  
- Experiments should include error bars or confidence intervals to demonstrate statistical significance.  
- Authors conjecture existing SM-MCTS algorithms may converge; explicit analysis of specific algorithms (e.g., from [11,12]) would strengthen the claim.  
- For acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, general template, sound methodology, and empirical support.  
- Against acceptance: lack of convergence rate analysis and potentially impractical D^2 dependence.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with important theoretical advances; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to address convergence rate, practicality, and empirical breadth.  
- Reviewer recommendation: Accept (with minor revisions).
