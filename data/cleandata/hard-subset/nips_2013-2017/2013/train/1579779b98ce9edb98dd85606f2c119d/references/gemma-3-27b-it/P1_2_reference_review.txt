## NIPS Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of providing theoretical convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games. While MCTS has seen empirical success in these games, a formal analysis has been lacking. The authors present a general MCTS template utilizing regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection functions. They prove that using a Hannan-consistent selection function leads to convergence towards a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. They empirically validate their analysis using Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to existing MCTS algorithms.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the broader landscape of game theory and algorithmic game playing. It acknowledges the success of backward induction and its limitations regarding domain knowledge and computational expense. The work builds on the substantial body of MCTS research, particularly UCT, while explicitly addressing the theoretical gap for simultaneous-move games.  It appropriately cites relevant work on regret minimization (Hart & Mas-Colell, Auer et al., Leshno et al.) and previous applications of MCTS to games like Goofspiel and general game-playing. The authors acknowledge Shafiei et al.'s work showing UCB's lack of convergence in this setting, highlighting the novelty of their approach. 

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper’s main strength lies in providing the *first* formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. This is a significant step forward in understanding the theoretical underpinnings of this widely used algorithm.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general, allowing for instantiation with any -Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure. This flexibility is a key advantage.
* **Rigorous Proof:**  The proof, while detailed and largely relegated to the appendix, appears sound and leverages established results from online learning theory (Hannan consistency). The breakdown of the proof into repeated matrix games with bounded error is a clever approach.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments provide evidence supporting the theoretical results. The comparison with existing methods (though limited) and the analysis of worst-case scenarios are valuable.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is, for the most part, well-written and clearly organized. Definitions are provided, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical analysis provides convergence *to* an approximate Nash equilibrium but does not provide any bounds on the *rate* of convergence. This is a crucial limitation.  The empirical analysis offers some insight, but a more rigorous analysis of convergence speed would be highly desirable.
* **Tightness of Approximation:** The theorem guarantees convergence to a (D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the depth of the game tree.  This approximation factor (D^2) seems potentially large, particularly for deeper games.  Exploring methods to tighten this bound would be beneficial.
* **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the authors perform experiments on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios, a more comprehensive evaluation on a wider variety of game instances (e.g., more complex Goofspiel variants) would strengthen the findings.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The paper focuses on the theoretical aspects and doesn't delve into practical implementation details or optimizations.  Discussing issues like memory usage or computational complexity would increase the paper’s relevance to practitioners.
* **Overly Detailed Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 1 feels verbose.  The explanations within the algorithm itself are redundant with the preceding text and detract from readability.



**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Provides a fundamental theoretical result for a widely used algorithm in a challenging game setting.
* Offers a general and flexible MCTS template.
* Demonstrates strong theoretical grounding and rigorous proof.

**Con:**

* Lacks convergence rate analysis.
* The approximation factor in the convergence theorem is potentially large.
* Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of game playing and reinforcement learning. The proof of convergence is a valuable result, and the generality of the proposed template is commendable. While the lack of convergence rate analysis and the potentially loose approximation factor are weaknesses, they do not overshadow the paper’s overall strength. The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for publication at NIPS, contingent on the authors addressing the following minor revisions:

1.  **Discuss the limitations of the convergence rate.** Acknowledge the lack of explicit bounds and discuss potential avenues for future research in this area.
2.  **Consider a brief discussion of the tightness of the approximation factor (D^2).**  Is this bound known to be achievable? Can it be improved with different techniques?
3.  **Streamline Algorithm 1.** Remove redundant explanations within the algorithm itself.
