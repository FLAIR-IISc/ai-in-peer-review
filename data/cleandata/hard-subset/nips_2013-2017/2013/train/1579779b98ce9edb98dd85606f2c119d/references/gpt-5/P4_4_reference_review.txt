Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). While such games admit polynomial-time solutions via backward induction (and improvements like alpha–beta pruning or double oracle), practical depth-limited search often relies on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). Existing theoretical analyses of MCTS largely focus on sequential-move perfect-information games; for simultaneous-move games, UCB/UCT can fail to converge to Nash equilibrium (NE) (e.g., Shafiei et al., IJCAI GIGA workshop). This paper proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which, at each node, action selection is driven by any ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) online learning algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3 with exploration). The core theoretical contribution is a proof that, if selection is ε-HC and each action is attempted infinitely often, the algorithm converges almost surely to a subgame perfect approximate NE of the extensive-form game. The analysis proceeds by (i) establishing convergence of HC dynamics in repeated matrix games (with approximate variants), (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded errors, and (iii) inductively composing error bounds up the game tree. A key technical requirement for the proof is backing up mean returns rather than a single sample.

Empirically, the paper evaluates two HC procedures (Regret Matching and Exp3) on random and small worst-case games, comparing mean backup (RMM/Exp3M) against the usual single-sample backup (RM/Exp3). Both variants appear to converge; mean backup is slightly slower. The authors conjecture that widely used SM-MCTS variants also converge and that their analysis could be extended.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction from SM-EFGs to repeated matrix games with bounded error is standard in spirit and appears carefully executed. Using ε-HC guarantees and guaranteed exploration to ensure each joint action is sampled infinitely often is well-motivated; the modification A* that injects uniform exploration and its regret inflation by at most γ is plausible. The inductive argument over tree depth and the subgame-perfect guarantee via stronger bounds in subgames are reasonable. The requirement to back up mean returns aligns with the bounded-error composition.

  That said, major proofs are deferred to the appendix/arXiv. Some assumptions deserve clearer articulation: (i) the almost-sure eventual expansion of the full tree (given independent exploration at each node and finite depth this is likely, but a brief formal statement would help); (ii) precise dependence of the final ε on node-level ε-HC and exploration parameters; (iii) whether RM as instantiated truly guarantees exploration without additional perturbation (typically it requires explicit exploration). The final bound in Theorem 4.11 scales as O(D^2 ε) (plus δ); this is likely loose, and the constants (e.g., the 4(c+1) factor) arise from composing approximate best-response deviations and error terms.

- Experiments: The experiments provide qualitative support but are limited in scope: random games of modest size and exhaustive enumeration for depth-2, BF-2 with discrete utilities. No comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., CFR-style methods adapted to simultaneous moves, or alpha–beta/double-oracle variants), and no results on larger benchmark domains (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel variants) where SM-MCTS is used. The observed asymptotic convergences require up to 10^6 iterations in “worst-case” small games; finite-time convergence rates are not provided theoretically or empirically.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally readable, with clear definitions of the game model and algorithm template. The distinction between empirical frequencies and average strategies and their eventual equivalence is helpful.

- Some notational reuse and minor presentation issues could be improved: γ denotes both exploration rate and parameters inside Exp3; the role of c (error) versus ε (HC) could be emphasized earlier. The precise statement of Theorem 4.11 would benefit from reiterating what ε refers to and how δ is chosen.

- The submission includes acknowledgments and an arXiv link identifying the appendix, which violates double-blind reviewing policy. References are incomplete (many lack years/venues), which hinders situating the work.

Originality:
- To the best of my knowledge, this is the first formal proof that an MCTS-style algorithm for simultaneous-move perfect-information games converges to approximate NE under HC selection policies with guaranteed exploration. While the ingredients (HC in repeated games, error-bounded composition) are known, their combination for SM-MCTS and the subgame-perfect guarantee is novel. The requirement of mean backup is also a noteworthy algorithmic stipulation.

Significance:
- The theoretical contribution addresses a gap: practitioners have used SM-MCTS without guarantees, and UCT is known to fail in this setting. Establishing convergence under broad HC selection policies is valuable and likely to influence algorithm design. However, the practical impact is tempered by: (i) asymptotic-only guarantees with potentially loose D^2 dependence; (ii) the mean-backup requirement, which is atypical in practice; and (iii) limited empirical validation on larger or canonical domains and no finite-time rates. Still, within the niche of simultaneous-move perfect-information games, the result is meaningful.

Pros:
- Clear, general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS with ε-HC selection and exploration.
- Subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium guarantee via inductive analysis.
- Unifies RM and Exp3 instantiations; shows both behave similarly empirically.
- Addresses a known gap where UCT fails.

Cons:
- Asymptotic results only; no finite-sample bounds and loose O(D^2) dependence.
- Requires mean return backups; no proof for the more common sample backup variant.
- Experimental evaluation is limited and lacks comparisons to strong baselines and larger domains.
- Violates double-blind policy (acknowledgments, arXiv link), and references are incomplete.
- Some assumptions (guaranteed exploration, full-tree expansion) could be stated and justified more crisply in the main text.

Suggestions:
- Remove acknowledgments and any identifying links; complete and standardize references.
- Clarify assumptions and constants in the main theorems; explicitly state the dependence on exploration γ and HC ε.
- Provide finite-time convergence rates (even crude ones), or at least empirical convergence curves with confidence intervals across a wider range of depths/branching factors.
- Evaluate on established benchmarks for simultaneous-move games and compare against alternatives (e.g., serialized alpha–beta, double-oracle, CFR variants).
- Investigate whether the analysis can cover sample backups, perhaps with additional averaging or variance control.
- Discuss variance and stability implications of Exp3’s importance-weighted updates in deep trees.

Overall, the paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games, with room to strengthen empirical validation and presentation.
