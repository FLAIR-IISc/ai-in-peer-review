Summary
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where, at each node, the selection policy is any ε–Hannan consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3). The key theoretical result shows that if mean returns are backed up (as opposed to the latest sample), then SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the game-tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The analysis proceeds by: (i) establishing properties of HC algorithms in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending them to repeated games with bounded errors, and (iii) inductively composing error bounds along the tree depth. Empirically, the paper compares variants with mean vs. sample backups using regret matching and Exp3 on random and small worst-case games, observing convergence in all cases, with mean-backup variants converging slightly slower.

Relation to prior work: The paper is motivated by the success of MCTS/UCT in perfect-information sequential games (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and the absence of convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move settings, where UCB can fail (Shafiei et al.). It contrasts with exact backward induction using LP/double-oracle (Saffidine et al.; Bošanský et al.), which can be expensive. It leverages classic online-learning results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.; Blum & Mansour) on Hannan consistency. It also connects to prior SM-MCTS applications in Tron and GGP. Related literature on regret minimization in EFGs (e.g., CFR) is touched via a workshop citation but could be discussed more fully.

Qualitative evaluation

Strengths
- Quality: The main theorem is technically sound at a high level: reducing SM-MCTS convergence to HC guarantees in repeated games with bounded additive errors, and propagating these through the tree by induction. The paper is careful to formalize exploration requirements and the need for mean backups to obtain unbiased/controlled errors.
- Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first convergence guarantee for MCTS-style search in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, and it applies to a broad class of selection algorithms (any ε–HC with exploration).
- Significance: Establishes a theoretical foundation for a widely used practical approach in a game class where guarantees were missing, and provides subgame-perfect convergence. The template invites adoption of other HC algorithms.
- Clarity: The high-level narrative and structure of the analysis are understandable. The assumptions (HC, guaranteed exploration, mean backup) are explicit. Empirical sections align with the theory (mean vs sample propagation).

Weaknesses
- Practical gap: The convergence proof hinges on backing up mean returns. This is atypical in MCTS practice, and the experiments show it can slow convergence. The paper conjectures that sample backups also converge, but offers no proof or counterexample-specific conditions.
- Bounds: The D^2 dependence and the 4(c+1) factors in the intermediate results are likely loose. No finite-time convergence rates are provided, limiting practical guidance.
- Tree growth: The proof assumes the tree is eventually fully expanded and argues that early statistics do not harm eventual convergence, but this is not quantified. Practical MCTS uses progressive widening/limited expansion.
- Experiments: Conducted on small/random/worst-case synthetic games, with limited scale and comparisons. No head-to-head with strong alternatives (e.g., CFR in simultaneous-move perfect-info variants, alpha–beta with simultaneous pruning, or SM-specific MCTS heuristics beyond RM/Exp3). Confidence intervals and broader metrics (e.g., both players’ exploitability; subgame properties) are missing.
- Reference quality: Several citations are incomplete (years/pages). Foundational CFR work (Zinkevich et al. 2007 and successors) is not properly cited/discussed. The claim that RM and Exp3 “guarantee exploration” is only true with explicit exploration; the paper mixes two exploration schemes (on-policy γ in RM vs. A* that skips updates on random exploration), and this should be reconciled more clearly.
- Clarity/presentation: Pseudocode formatting is hard to parse; the RetVal choice should be emphasized earlier and the SM-MCTS vs. SM-MCTS-M nomenclature made consistent. Some notation is heavy and could be streamlined.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and general convergence result for SM-MCTS with subgame-perfect guarantees.
- Simple, modular proof technique likely extensible to other HC selection rules.
- Bridges a known theoretical gap highlighted by prior negative results for UCB in simultaneous moves.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical guarantees hinge on a nonstandard mean-backup variant and lack finite-sample bounds; practical impact is uncertain.
- Experimental validation is limited in scope and scale, with minimal comparative analysis.
- Loose bounds (D^2) and incomplete references/presentation.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theory:
  - Tighten depth dependence if possible (e.g., O(D ε) rather than O(D^2 ε)), or prove lower bounds for error accumulation.
  - Provide finite-time rates under standard HC algorithms with explicit exploration; quantify the time to approximate equilibrium as a function of D, branching factor, γ, and ε.
  - Analyze sample-backup SM-MCTS (the practical default): can unbiased or variance-reduced estimators or importance-weighted backups restore the proof? Formalize conditions under which sample backups converge.
  - Clarify and unify the exploration model. If using A* (random exploration without updates), explain why HC degrades only by γ and align this with the implemented RM “on-policy γ” scheme (which does update).
  - Make the “tree eventually complete” argument precise (e.g., expected time to full expansion under exploration) or relax it (progressive widening).
- Experiments:
  - Include larger and more structured domains (e.g., Goofspiel variants, pursuit–evasion benchmarks, GGP simultaneous-move games) and compare against CFR-style baselines and alpha–beta with simultaneous pruning.
  - Report both players’ exploitabilities and subgame exploitabilities to support subgame-perfect claims empirically; include confidence intervals over runs.
  - Investigate the effect of γ and of mean vs sample backups on convergence speed and asymptotic exploitability systematically.
- Presentation:
  - Clean up pseudocode and clearly label SM-MCTS vs SM-MCTS-M. State RetVal choice and its implications prominently.
  - Complete and correct references; add classic CFR (Zinkevich et al., 2007; Tammelin CFR+), online learning texts (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi).
  - Minor notation/typo fixes and reordering for readability.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper fills an identifiable theoretical gap with a general, plausible analysis and provides initial empirical support. While the bounds and practical assumptions could be stronger, the contribution is likely to stimulate follow-up work on theory for MCTS in simultaneous-move and broader EFGs. Strengthening references and clarifying exploration/backups would further improve the paper.
