Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a template (“SM-MCTS”) that uses any ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) selection rule (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3) at each simultaneous-move node. The central claim is that, provided exploration guarantees and propagating mean returns, the algorithm converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis proceeds by: (i) repeated matrix games with HC selection (standard), (ii) repeated games with bounded error, (iii) induction over the depth of the stacked matrix game. Experiments compare propagating current sample vs mean for RM and Exp3 on random and small “worst-case” games.

This is related to UCT [Kocsis & Szepesvári], CFR-style regret minimization in EFGs (Hart & Mas-Colell; CFR/CFR+ literature), MCTS in simultaneous move settings (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel), and adversarial bandits (Exp3). Previous works showed UCB selection may not converge to NE in simultaneous-move games; this paper proposes HC-based selection with mean propagation and proves asymptotic convergence.

Strengths
- Addresses an under-theorized regime: MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information games.
- Uses well-established HC machinery to derive asymptotic convergence; the induction argument is conceptually sound and aligns with stacked matrix-game intuition.
- Identifies a practical detail (propagating mean vs sample) that matters for theoretical guarantees; provides some empirical exploration.
- Experiments include both RM and Exp3 and consider “difficult” small games.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness/clarity:
1) Inconsistency in utility treatment for player 2. The model assumes u2(z)=1−u1(z) with payoffs in [0,1]. Algorithm 1 states “Player 2 does the same except using negated utilities.” Negation is inconsistent with u2=1−u1 unless payoffs are centered (e.g., zero-sum in [−1,1]). This is not a cosmetic issue: it affects regret updates and bandit estimators.

2) Pseudocode/statistics mismatch. Nodes are said to maintain only Xh (cumulative reward) and nh. Yet RM updates rely on Xhij (per joint-action statistics), via x(h,i,j)=Xhij if (i,j)≠(i1,j2). Such per-(i,j) statistics are never defined/maintained in Algorithm 1. This is a substantive gap: RM as described requires either counterfactual values or per-action estimates; the manuscript does not define how Xhij are computed/stored.

3) RM update notation is confusing/possibly incorrect. The update uses “j” without being clearly specified; the sampled joint action is (i1,j2) but the regret update is ∀i′, rh[i′]←rh[i′]+(x(h,i′,j)−u1). Which j? If it is the opponent’s sampled action j2, say so. Otherwise, clarify if this is an expectation over j with importance weighting. As written, it looks like “swap-in” of a child’s running estimate in place of true counterfactual payoff without addressing bias.

4) Equivalence of empirical frequencies and average strategies (Lemma 4.5). The paper uses on-policy sampling with exploration: P(a)=γ/|A|+(1−γ)σs(a). The definition of σ̄ appears to average σs, which are the “pre-exploration” distributions from regret tables. Then empirical frequencies will, in general, converge to the average of the mixed sampling distributions, not the averages of σs, unless σs is defined to already include the exploration mixture. The statement and proof sketch need precise definitions.

5) Repeated games with error (Def. 4.8) and induction. The key assumption is that observed payoffs aij(t) converge uniformly to a fixed matrix M (eventually within η). In SM-MCTS, those observed payoffs are themselves induced by deeper subgames whose estimates and action distributions evolve over time. The paper asserts this stabilizes by induction but provides only a brief informal justification. Given that this is the linchpin of Theorem 4.11, a precise argument (rates of stabilization, uniformity across (i,j), dependence on exploration) is necessary in the main text; deferring entirely to an appendix weakens confidence.

6) Constants and depth dependence. Theorem 4.11 claims subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-NE. The text says “errors of returned values will be in the order of d and players can gain 2d by deviating,” then “summing ... leads to the bound.” This is too hand-wavy: please provide the exact recurrence and constants. Also reconcile Cor. 4.7’s 4ε factor and Theorem 4.10’s 4(c+1) with the final D^2 dependence. Are these bounds tight? Why D^2 rather than O(D)?

7) Exploration and “guaranteed exploration.” The modified A* samples uniformly with probability γ “without updating any of the variables belonging to A.” Then Lemma 4.5 and regret guarantees (which depend on the actual sampling distribution) need to reflect that σs and updates do not incorporate exploration-induced actions. This risks persistent bias in estimates and regret definitions.

8) Exp3 estimator: xh,i ← xh,i + u1/σs(h,i). For adversarial bandits this is the usual importance-weighted estimator for rewards of selected arms. However, since payoffs depend on the opponent’s action (joint action), the variance can be large; the paper should at least acknowledge this and whether it affects the error constants c.

9) Tree expansion “will eventually happen with probability 1.” This is asserted but not proved in the main text. Given that complete-tree assumption underpins the analysis, at least provide a lemma (with conditions) in the main body.

Empirical evaluation:
- Very small domains. Random games and exhaustive BF=2, depth=2, utilities in {0,0.5,1} are tiny. No larger simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel variants) or comparisons to baselines (CFR, Double Oracle + AB, prior SM-MCTS variants) with exploitability curves.
- No error bars/confidence intervals; figures are referenced but not shown here; claims like “exactly matches the minimal achievable” need a derivation or citation.
- Only asymptotic behavior (up to 1e6 iterations) is reported; no finite-time rates, and mean-propagation is observed to be slower, which undermines practical appeal.

Presentation and formatting:
- Numerous typographical/notation issues (missing ε symbols, inconsistent A/A1/A(h), mh/nh unused).
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues), and citing a specific arXiv ID is not compatible with double-blind review.
- Algorithm formatting is hard to follow; line numbers/indentation inconsistent.

Pros
- Addresses an important open gap: theoretical convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
- Conceptually clean reduction via HC and depth-wise induction.
- Identifies a concrete algorithmic detail (mean propagation) required for proofs.

Cons
- Several technical inconsistencies (utilities, statistics needed for RM, σ definitions).
- Key convergence assumptions and constants insufficiently justified in the main text.
- Empirical evaluation is limited, lacks baselines, and suggests the provably-correct variant is slower.
- Poor clarity and numerous formatting/anonymity issues.

Questions for authors
- Clarify utility conventions and fix the player-2 negation issue.
- Specify exactly what statistics are maintained per node: do you keep per-(i,j) estimates Xhij? If so, define how they’re updated.
- Define σs used in averages: does it include exploration mixing? If not, how does Lemma 4.5 hold?
- Provide a formal sketch in the main text for Theorem 4.11’s D^2 bound with explicit constants.
- Prove the “complete tree built with prob. 1” claim in the main body.
- Improve experiments: add larger benchmarks, baselines, error bars.

Recommendation
Score: 5 (borderline). My leaning is reject in current form due to multiple technical and presentation issues that impede verification of the main claims. The contribution is potentially significant; with corrected algorithms/notation, a rigorous main-text proof sketch, stronger experiments, and proper anonymization/formatting, this could become a solid paper.
