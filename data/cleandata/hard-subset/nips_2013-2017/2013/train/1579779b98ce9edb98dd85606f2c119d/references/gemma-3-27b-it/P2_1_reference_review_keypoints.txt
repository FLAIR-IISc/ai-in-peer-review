- Paper fills a theoretical gap by proving MCTS converges in simultaneous-move games under ε-Hannan consistent selection, yielding subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibria.  
- Instantiates the template with Regret Matching and Exp3 and shows empirical convergence comparable to existing MCTS.  
- Strength: Addresses a fundamental open problem in MCTS theory for simultaneous-move games.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical contribution using decomposition into repeated matrix games with bounded error.  
- Strength: General MCTS template that can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures.  
- Strength: Empirical validation aligns with theoretical results and shows competitive convergence speed.  
- Strength: Clear writing, organization, and definitions.  
- Weakness: Convergence rate is not explicitly characterized and theoretical bounds may hide large constants.  
- Weakness: Experimental scope is limited (mostly random games and a limited worst-case); needs broader benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel).  
- Weakness: Relies on ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration—assumptions whose practical implications should be discussed.  
- Weakness: Proof simplification that disregards incremental tree building feels hand-wavy and its practical impact is unclear.  
- Quality: Technically sound, rigorous proof, and well-supported claims.  
- Clarity: Generally well-written but some mathematical notation needs more explanation for non-experts.  
- Originality: Highly original—first theoretical convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games; novel use of ε-Hannan consistency.  
- Significance: Potentially highly significant for both theoretical and practical developments in game-playing AI.  
- Pro arguments: Fills critical gap, strong guarantee, clear presentation, and empirical support.  
- Con arguments: Uncharacterized convergence rate, limited experiments, and simplification regarding tree building.  
- Overall: Strong paper recommended for accept with minor revisions.  
- Recommended revisions: discuss convergence-rate limitations, broaden experimental benchmarks, and comment on the impact of ignoring incremental tree building in the proof.
