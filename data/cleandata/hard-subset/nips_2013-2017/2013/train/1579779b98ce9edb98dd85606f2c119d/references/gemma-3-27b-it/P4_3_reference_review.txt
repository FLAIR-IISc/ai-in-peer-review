This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The core idea is to frame SM-MCTS as an iterative process of solving matrix games at each node of the tree, using regret-minimizing procedures for action selection. The authors prove that with an -Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They validate this with experiments using regret matching and Exp3, showing empirical convergence rates comparable to existing algorithms.

The paper builds upon existing MCTS literature (e.g., [14]) and extends it to a more general setting – simultaneous move games where theoretical guarantees are lacking. It relates to work on solving games with backward induction [7, 4, 8, 9] and contrasts MCTS with these methods, emphasizing the need for MCTS when domain knowledge is limited. The connection to Hannan consistency and online learning algorithms [15, 16] is clearly established. The use of regret matching and Exp3 as instantiations is well-motivated given their established theoretical properties. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The main contribution is a formal convergence proof for SM-MCTS, which is a significant advancement in the field. The proof appears to be sound, building on established concepts from online learning and game theory.
*   **Generality:** The framework is general, allowing for instantiation with any -Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions and background material are clearly presented.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments provide supporting evidence for the theoretical results and compare different selection algorithms.
*   **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature on game solving and MCTS.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Implications of Convergence Rate:** While the theoretical analysis guarantees convergence, the rate of convergence (tied to the parameter ) is not explicitly addressed. The experiments, while showing convergence, do not provide precise rate estimates.  The practical relevance of the convergence guarantee heavily relies on the value of  achieved in practice.
*   **Complexity of Proof:** The proof, while seemingly correct, is dense and requires significant background knowledge to fully grasp.  The summary in the paper is helpful, but a more intuitive explanation of the core ideas would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments primarily focus on randomly generated games and a worst-case scenario.  Evaluation on more diverse and challenging game instances (e.g., Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion games mentioned in the introduction) would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Discussion of UCB:** The paper briefly mentions UCB [17] and dismisses it due to lack of convergence guarantees. A more nuanced discussion of why UCB fails to converge, or potential modifications to address this issue, could be valuable.
* **Numerical Stability:** The paper acknowledges a practical issue with the Exp3 implementation, using a reformulation to address numerical overflow. While addressed, it highlights a potential limitation and could benefit from a discussion of its impact.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games.
*   The framework is general and provides a foundation for future research.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the convergence guarantee are unclear due to the lack of explicit rate analysis.
*   The proof is complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.
*   The experimental evaluation is limited in scope.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant, and the paper is well-written and presented. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work.  I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors consider adding a discussion of practical convergence rates and expanding the experimental evaluation in a future version.
