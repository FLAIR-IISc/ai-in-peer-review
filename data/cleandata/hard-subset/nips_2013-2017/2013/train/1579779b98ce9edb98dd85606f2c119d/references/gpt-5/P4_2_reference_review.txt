Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). While such games can be solved exactly by backward induction with LP-based cutoffs or double oracle pruning [8, 9], those approaches can be costly and require evaluation functions. In practice, Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is widely used [10–13], yet its convergence properties in simultaneous-move settings are not established; in fact, UCT can fail to converge in these games [17].

The paper proposes a general SM-MCTS template where the node-level selection policy is any ε-Hannan consistent (HC) procedure with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching [15] or Exp3 [16]). The central theoretical contribution shows that if each node uses such a selection rule and the algorithm backpropagates mean returns (rather than single-sample returns), then the empirical/average strategies induced by the search converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε’-Nash equilibrium, with ε’ scaling with the game depth D (stated as roughly ε D^2 up to arbitrarily small δ). The proof proceeds by: (i) recalling convergence of ε-HC learners in repeated matrix games to approximate minimax/NE; (ii) generalizing to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors and preserving approximate convergence; and (iii) inductively composing these error bounds across tree levels to obtain subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium guarantees.

Empirically, the paper compares backpropagating means versus single samples for RM and Exp3 on random and enumerated small games. Both variants converge empirically; mean backpropagation converges slightly slower. Worst-case small instances are used to gauge convergence rates; all tested HC variants eventually approach exploitability consistent with exploration-induced mixing.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated matrix games with bounded error is standard in spirit and appears carefully executed. Definitions of ε-HC, guaranteed exploration, and the A* exploration wrapper are appropriate; Lemma 4.4 (HC preserved with small additive penalty) and the convergence lemmas for empirical frequencies are consistent with known no-regret results [18, 19]. The extension to bounded-error repeated games (Lemma 4.9) and the inductive composition to extensive-form trees are plausible. The subgame-perfect property follows from the level-wise induction. The requirement to backpropagate mean returns is clearly identified as necessary for the proof.
- Completeness: Full proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is acceptable, though several key constants and the exact dependence on D could be clarified in the main text. The “eventual full-tree expansion with probability 1” is asserted; a brief sketch would strengthen completeness.
- Empirical support: Experiments are modest and primarily illustrate that practical variants (including single-sample backprop) behave similarly and converge in small/worst-case instances. No finite-time rates are derived; empirical curves partially fill this gap but on limited domains.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized and readable. The SM-MCTS pseudocode is helpful, and the two selection variants are described concretely.
- Some notation could be clearer: the bound in Theorem 4.11 is written as “(ε D2 + δ)”; it would help to explicitly state whether this is ε·D^2 or 2D·ε, and how constants from Lemma 4.9 compose across depth. Similarly, the role of RetVal and which variant is required for the theorem should be summarized more prominently when introducing Algorithm 1.
- Minor editorial issues exist (e.g., some reference entries incomplete), but they do not impede understanding.

Originality:
- To the best of available knowledge, this is the first formal convergence analysis of MCTS-style algorithms in perfect-information, simultaneous-move games. The idea that node-level ε-HC selection with guaranteed exploration implies convergence, via bounded-error reductions and induction on depth, is a novel and valuable connection. Prior work established non-convergence of UCT in this setting [17] and convergence of no-regret in single-stage games [15, 16, 18, 19]; this work bridges to the tree setting.
- The empirical component is not novel per se, but supports the theoretical claims and clarifies the mean vs. sample backprop distinction.

Significance:
- The theoretical result provides a principled foundation for SM-MCTS in a class of games used in practice (e.g., Goofspiel, pursuit–evasion, GGP). It offers a general recipe: plug in any ε-HC selector with exploration, and obtain asymptotic convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE.
- Practical impact is tempered by the asymptotic nature, looseness of bounds (depth-dependent constant potentially large), and the mean-backpropagation requirement. Finite-time rates are not provided, and experiments are limited to small synthetic instances. Nevertheless, establishing convergence is a meaningful step for this area.

Pros:
- Clear, general theoretical framework linking HC selection to SM-MCTS convergence.
- Subgame-perfect ε-NE guarantee in a setting where UCT fails and no prior MCTS guarantees existed.
- Applicability to common HC algorithms (regret matching, Exp3).
- Empirical observations consistent with theory and clarifying design choices.

Cons:
- Asymptotic result with potentially loose (depth-squared) error accumulation; no finite-time bounds.
- Requires backpropagating means for the proof; the standard single-sample variant remains unproven.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and lacks comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., CFR-based methods, alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning).
- Some technical details and constants in the main text could be more explicit.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify the exact dependence on D and ε in Theorem 4.11 and outline the error accumulation per level.
- Provide a brief proof sketch (or reference) for eventual full-tree expansion under exploration.
- Discuss whether the mean-backprop requirement can be relaxed, or outline conditions under which single-sample backprop also converges.
- Add finite-time analyses (even loose ones) or empirical scaling on larger, structured benchmarks; compare to CFR-based solvers and to [8, 9, 20].
- Improve reference formatting and consistency.

Recommendation rationale:
The paper offers a novel and technically sound asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS with HC selection, addressing a gap in theory for simultaneous-move games. While the bounds are asymptotic and experiments are limited, the contribution is clear and relevant to NeurIPS topics in online learning and game search. The work would benefit from clarified constants and stronger empirical comparisons, but the core theoretical advance is meaningful.
