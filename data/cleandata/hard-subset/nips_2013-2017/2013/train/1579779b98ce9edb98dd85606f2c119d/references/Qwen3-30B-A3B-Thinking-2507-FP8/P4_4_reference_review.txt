

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this class, prove convergence guarantees for algorithms using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions, and provide empirical validation. This work extends previous theoretical analyses of MCTS, which have largely focused on sequential games without simultaneous moves, such as the well-known UCT algorithm for sequential games [14].

The paper begins by establishing the problem context, noting that while exact solution methods exist for these games (e.g., backward induction with alpha-beta pruning [8, 9]), practical applications often rely on MCTS variants without convergence guarantees. The authors then formalize the game structure as "stacked matrix games," where each node represents a matrix game with successors that are themselves stacked matrix games. Their main contribution is a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games, along with a formal proof that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.

The technical core of the paper consists of four key sections: (1) analysis of repeated matrix games, (2) repeated matrix games with bounded error, (3) composition of these results for multi-stage games, and (4) the main convergence theorem for extensive-form games. The authors carefully define necessary concepts (ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) and provide rigorous proofs for their claims. The empirical evaluation compares mean propagation versus current sample value propagation, and assesses convergence rates on worst-case games, supporting the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a comprehensive formal proof of convergence that builds incrementally from simpler cases (matrix games) to the full game tree. The analysis of repeated games with bounded error (Lemma 4.9) is particularly well-executed and addresses a critical practical concern about error propagation in MCTS.
2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. Definitions, theorems, and proofs are presented in a logical sequence that makes the complex mathematical arguments accessible. The empirical evaluation is well-designed and clearly presented with appropriate figures.
3. **Originality:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature—no prior convergence guarantees exist for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The authors' approach of using regret minimization to establish convergence is novel for this game class, though they appropriately relate their work to existing regret-minimization literature [15, 16].
4. **Significance:** The theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous move games has practical importance, as these games are common in applications like card games (Goofspiel) and general game-playing competitions [3, 6]. The paper provides a foundation for understanding why MCTS works in these domains.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Scope Limitation:** The analysis is restricted to two-player zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, which is a specific subclass of extensive-form games. While this is a meaningful contribution, it does not address more complex game classes (e.g., games with imperfect information or more than two players) where MCTS is also widely used.
2. **Convergence Rate:** The paper establishes eventual convergence but does not provide finite-time bounds, which would be valuable for practitioners. The empirical analysis shows similar convergence rates for their mean-propagation variant versus standard MCTS, but without rate guarantees.
3. **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is limited to randomly generated games with small branching factors and depths. While this supports the theoretical claims, broader testing on more complex or real-world games would strengthen the practical relevance.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- Fills a significant theoretical gap in understanding MCTS for simultaneous move games.
- Provides a rigorous convergence proof that extends the theoretical foundation of MCTS beyond sequential games.
- Well-written with clear organization and comprehensive mathematical treatment.
- Empirical results support the theoretical claims.

*Con:*
- Analysis is limited to a specific game class (two-player zero-sum perfect information), which may reduce broader applicability.
- Does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, which are important for practical implementation.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to relatively small game instances.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in a specific but important class of games. The formal analysis is sound, well-structured, and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The authors provide a general framework for MCTS in simultaneous move games that can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures, and they prove convergence guarantees under reasonable assumptions. While the scope is limited to a specific game class and the paper does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, these limitations are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the significance of the theoretical contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful advancement in the theoretical understanding of MCTS for a class of games where it is widely used in practice.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is technically sound with strong theoretical guarantees (Quality), clearly written with a logical structure (Clarity), addresses an important gap in the literature (Originality), and provides meaningful theoretical justification for a widely used algorithm (Significance). While not addressing the most general case of extensive-form games, the contribution is substantial for the specific class of games considered.
