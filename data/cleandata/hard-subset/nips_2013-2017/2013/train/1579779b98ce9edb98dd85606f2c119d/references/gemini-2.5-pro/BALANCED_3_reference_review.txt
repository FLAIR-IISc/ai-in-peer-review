**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are popular and effective in practice for such games, a formal guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been a notable open problem. The authors bridge this gap by proposing a general MCTS template where the in-tree action selection policy is handled by a regret-minimizing procedure.

The main contribution is a formal proof that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the resulting MCTS algorithm's average strategy profile converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree. It first establishes convergence for single-stage matrix games using known results from online learning, then extends this to matrix games where payoffs are noisy estimates with bounded error. This extension is key, as it models the situation at non-leaf nodes of the MCTS tree, where backed-up values are estimates from subgame simulations. The paper instantiates this framework with two well-known regret minimizers, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, and provides an empirical evaluation on random and worst-case games, comparing the proven variant with a more standard MCTS implementation.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** This work provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This is a significant theoretical contribution that solidifies the foundation for using MCTS in this game class, addressing a gap highlighted by prior work showing that standard MCTS selection methods like UCB can fail to converge. The connection it establishes between ε-Hannan consistency from online learning and convergence in MCTS for games is novel and insightful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-constructed. The proof strategy, which models the problem inductively and treats subgame value estimates as payoffs with bounded error, is elegant and effective. The paper builds its argument clearly, starting from foundational lemmas about repeated matrix games (Lemmas 4.6, 4.9) and culminating in the main theorem for extensive-form games (Theorem 4.11).

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly motivated, the necessary background on game theory and MCTS is concisely provided, and the structure logically progresses from the proposed algorithm to its theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The separation of the analysis into distinct, well-explained steps (repeated games, games with error, full EFGs) greatly aids comprehension.

4.  **Constructive Empirical Analysis:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed. They not only validate the convergence claim but also honestly investigate the practical implications of the assumptions made in the proof. Specifically, the comparison between propagating the mean value (required by the proof) and propagating the current sample value (common in practice) is insightful, showing that the latter, while not covered by the theory, performs slightly better empirically. This provides a clear picture of both the theoretical contribution and its relation to practical implementations.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The primary theoretical result relies on a variant of MCTS that propagates the mean of collected rewards (`Xh/nh`) up the tree. However, the experiments show that the more standard approach of propagating the single reward from the latest simulation works equally well, if not slightly faster. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on *why* the proof requires the mean value and what the technical challenges are in extending the analysis to cover the standard single-sample propagation. Is the variance of single samples the key obstacle? A conjecture on this point would be valuable for future work.

2.  **Lack of Convergence Rate:** The analysis provides an asymptotic convergence guarantee but does not establish a finite-time convergence rate (i.e., how exploitability decreases as a function of the number of simulations). The underlying no-regret algorithms (RM, Exp3) have well-known regret bounds (e.g., `O(sqrt(T))`). It would be beneficial to discuss whether these bounds could be leveraged to derive an explicit rate for the overall MCTS algorithm, even if it is a loose one. This is mentioned as future work, but even a preliminary discussion of the challenges would add depth.

3.  **Clarity on the "M" Variant:** The distinction between the standard algorithm (e.g., RM) and the variant required for the proof (RMM, the "mean" variant) is introduced somewhat subtly in the description of Algorithm 1. This crucial distinction could be highlighted more explicitly earlier in Section 3 to ensure the reader understands which version of the algorithm the theoretical analysis applies to.

4.  **Dependence on Depth:** The final approximation bound in Theorem 4.11 has a dependency on `D^2` (where `D` is the game depth). While some dependency on depth is expected as errors accumulate, it would be interesting to know if the authors believe this quadratic dependency is tight or an artifact of the proof technique (e.g., summing deviation bounds at each level). A short comment on this could provide additional context.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first proof of convergence for MCTS in the important class of simultaneous move games.
*   Introduces a novel and general framework connecting no-regret online learning with MCTS.
*   The technical contribution is significant, sound, and clearly presented.
*   The work bridges a critical gap between the theory and widespread practice of MCTS.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The proof is for a non-standard variant of MCTS, though experiments show the standard one also works.
*   The analysis is asymptotic and does not provide a convergence rate.

**Decision**

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It tackles an important, long-standing open problem with a rigorous and elegant theoretical result. The work is a significant contribution to both the MCTS and algorithmic game theory literature. The identified weaknesses are primarily avenues for future research rather than fundamental flaws in the current work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
