

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of two-player zero-sum games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for SM-MCTS (Simultaneous Move MCTS) that uses any -Hannan consistent selection function for action sampling. They prove that this approach converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The paper builds on established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency) and game theory to provide the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a notable extension beyond the well-studied sequential move games.

The paper makes three key contributions: (1) a formal framework for SM-MCTS, (2) a proof of convergence to approximate Nash equilibrium using -Hannan consistent algorithms, and (3) empirical validation showing that the mean propagation variant converges effectively, though slightly slower than current sample propagation. The authors compare their approach to related work in game theory (e.g., [15, 16]) and MCTS applications (e.g., [10, 11, 12, 14]), positioning their work as the first theoretical foundation for MCTS in this specific game class.

## Strengths

### Quality
The theoretical contribution is substantial and well-executed. The paper provides a rigorous mathematical proof of convergence, building through careful definitions and lemmas (4.4-4.10) that establish the necessary conditions. The proof structure is logical, using induction through the game tree to establish convergence from leaf nodes up to the root. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (e.g., no finite-time bounds) and clearly distinguish between what they prove and what they conjecture. The empirical evaluation complements the theory well, showing that the mean propagation variant (required for the theoretical analysis) converges to approximate NE, though slightly slower than current sample propagation, which is consistent with the theoretical expectations.

### Originality
This paper fills a clear gap in the literature. While MCTS has been successfully applied to simultaneous move games in practice [10, 11, 12, 14], no convergence guarantees existed for these settings. The authors' approach of leveraging -Hannan consistency from online learning theory is novel in this context. They correctly identify that the key to convergence lies in the selection function's ability to explore all actions infinitely often (guaranteed exploration), and they connect this to established concepts in game theory. The paper distinguishes itself clearly from prior work by focusing on simultaneous move games rather than the more commonly studied sequential move games.

### Significance
The work has significant theoretical importance for bridging the gap between practical algorithms and theoretical understanding in game theory. It provides a formal justification for the empirical success of MCTS in simultaneous move games, which are important in applications like security domains [1, 2] and general game playing [6]. The authors' conjecture that many existing MCTS variants for these games also converge to -Nash equilibrium opens the door for future theoretical work. The paper advances the state of the art by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in this specific game class.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The formal definitions (Section 2) are precise and essential for understanding the problem. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is clear and well-annotated. The logical flow from matrix games to repeated games with error to extensive-form games is coherent. The paper makes good use of standard mathematical notation familiar to the game theory and machine learning communities. The empirical evaluation is well-designed and effectively supports the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Empirical Scope
While the empirical analysis is relevant, it is limited to a specific range of games (randomly generated with uniform utilities) and does not explore the full spectrum of simultaneous move games. The "worst-case" games identified in Section 5.2 are not guaranteed to be true worst cases due to the limited number of algorithm runs (100 per game). A more thorough exploration of different game structures would strengthen the empirical claims.

### No Finite-Time Bounds
The paper establishes convergence to -Nash equilibrium but does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, which is a limitation for practical applications. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but this gap makes it difficult to quantify the practical implications of their theoretical results.

### Limited Discussion of Practical Implications
While the paper provides a theoretical foundation, it doesn't sufficiently discuss the practical implications of the mean propagation versus current sample propagation. The empirical results show mean propagation converges slightly slower, but the paper doesn't fully explain why this is the case or how it might impact real-world applications where computational resources are limited.

### Conjecture About Existing Algorithms
The authors conjecture that many existing MCTS variants for simultaneous move games also converge to -Nash equilibrium, but they don't provide any evidence or partial results to support this claim. While the conjecture is reasonable, it would strengthen the paper to include at least a sketch of why these existing algorithms might satisfy the conditions for convergence.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal convergence proof is rigorous, well-structured, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The empirical evaluation provides good support for the theoretical claims, though it could be expanded to cover a broader range of game types.

The paper meets all the key criteria for a NeurIPS publication:
- **Quality**: High - The theoretical contribution is sound and well-supported.
- **Clarity**: High - The paper is well-organized and clearly written.
- **Originality**: High - The work is novel and addresses an important gap.
- **Significance**: High - The results advance the field and have practical implications.

The main limitations (no finite-time bounds, limited empirical scope) are acknowledged by the authors and are reasonable for a first theoretical analysis. The paper is clearly positioned within the existing literature and makes a distinct contribution to the field.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. It makes a significant theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature, with a rigorous proof and supporting empirical evidence. The work is well-executed, clearly written, and represents a meaningful advance in our understanding of MCTS convergence properties in a specific but important class of games.

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings, as it provides theoretical foundations for a widely used algorithm in game theory and AI applications. It also opens the door for future work on finite-time bounds and extensions to more complex game structures.

## Final Thoughts

The paper successfully bridges the gap between practical algorithms and theoretical understanding for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the convergence rate analysis is limited, the theoretical contribution is substantial and well-justified. The authors have made a clear and significant contribution to the field that will likely guide future research on MCTS and game-theoretic algorithms.
