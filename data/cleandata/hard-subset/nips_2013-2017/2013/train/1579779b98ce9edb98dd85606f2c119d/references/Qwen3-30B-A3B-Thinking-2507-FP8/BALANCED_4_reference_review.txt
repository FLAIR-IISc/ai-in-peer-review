

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template where any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure can be used for action selection, and prove that such algorithms converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This work extends prior research on regret minimization (regret matching, Exp3) and MCTS (which has previously been analyzed primarily for sequential games) to a class of games that includes many practical applications like security domains, card games (Goofspiel), and general game-playing competitions.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper fills a notable gap in the literature. While MCTS has been widely used in simultaneous move games in practice, no convergence guarantees existed prior to this work. The proof that any ε-Hannan consistent selection function leads to convergence to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium is technically sound and well-structured.

2. **Clear Formal Analysis**: The paper provides a rigorous inductive proof building from simple matrix games to the full extensive-form game. The lemmas and definitions are well-organized, making the complex proof accessible. The distinction between matrix games (leaf nodes) and the full game tree is particularly well-handled.

3. **Appropriate Empirical Evaluation**: The authors provide empirical validation of their theoretical claims, showing that both regret matching and Exp3 variants converge to approximate Nash equilibria. The comparison between mean propagation (required for formal analysis) and current sample propagation is insightful.

4. **Relevant to NIPS Community**: The work bridges algorithmic game theory and reinforcement learning, directly addressing a core topic at NIPS. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and clearly contributes to the field.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Finite-Time Convergence Bounds**: While the paper proves eventual convergence, it does not provide any finite-time convergence rates. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, as practitioners need to know how quickly convergence occurs. The empirical evaluation shows convergence rates but doesn't provide theoretical bounds.

2. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The experiments are conducted on randomly generated games and worst-case games, but the paper lacks evaluation on real-world applications where MCTS is commonly used (e.g., security games, Goofspiel). This weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

3. **Unexplained Performance Difference**: The paper notes that the mean propagation variant (required for the formal analysis) converges slightly slower than the current sample propagation variant, but doesn't adequately explain this difference. This is important for practitioners who might need to choose between these variants.

4. **Superficial Practical Implications**: While the paper mentions applications like security games, it doesn't clearly articulate how the theoretical results would change algorithm design or implementation in practice. The connection between the theory and real-world applications could be strengthened.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The theoretical contribution is significant, well-executed, and addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory and reinforcement learning.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Provide Finite-Time Bounds**: The authors should attempt to derive finite-time convergence bounds or at least discuss how such bounds might be estimated in practice. This would significantly increase the paper's practical utility.

2. **Expand Empirical Evaluation**: Include experiments on real-world games (e.g., Goofspiel) to better demonstrate the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

3. **Explain Mean Propagation Performance Difference**: Add a discussion explaining why the mean propagation variant converges slightly slower than the current sample variant, which would help practitioners understand the trade-offs.

4. **Strengthen Practical Implications**: Provide concrete examples of how the theoretical results would influence algorithm design in specific application domains (e.g., security games, card games).

## Overall Assessment
This is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a significant step toward understanding MCTS behavior in simultaneous move games. The paper is well-structured, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded to include real-world applications, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The authors' claim that their analysis could be extended to other MCTS variants is promising, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.
