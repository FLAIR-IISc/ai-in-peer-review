

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous move games, claiming to provide the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a template for SM-MCTS that uses any ε-Hannan consistent selection function (such as regret matching or Exp3) and prove that this leads to convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper includes theoretical analysis and limited empirical validation.

The main strength of the paper is its theoretical contribution. The authors establish a solid mathematical foundation for the convergence of MCTS in a specific game class where such guarantees were previously lacking. The formal analysis is rigorous, with careful definitions and proofs that build logically from matrix games to multi-stage extensive-form games. The connection between Hannan consistency and Nash equilibrium convergence is well-established in the literature, and the paper correctly identifies this as the key theoretical insight.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its contribution. First, the theoretical guarantee requires propagating mean values rather than current sample values, which the authors acknowledge is not standard practice in MCTS (see Section 5.1). Their empirical results show that this mean propagation variant actually converges slightly slower than standard MCTS (RM) in all tested cases, contradicting the practical value of their theoretical contribution. This is a critical disconnect between the theoretical claim and practical implementation.

Second, the empirical evaluation is severely limited. The authors claim to "empirically evaluate this claim" but only compare two variants of regret matching (with and without mean propagation) on small, randomly generated games. They fail to compare against established baselines like Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR), which is the standard approach for solving extensive-form games and has known convergence guarantees. The paper cites [17] showing that UCB doesn't converge to Nash equilibrium, but fails to demonstrate that their approach outperforms or even matches the performance of CFR or other well-established methods.

Third, the paper lacks a clear demonstration of why this theoretical contribution is significant for practical applications. The authors state that "the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on our template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms," but provide no comparative data with state-of-the-art methods. Without evidence that their approach outperforms existing methods in terms of convergence speed or solution quality, the practical relevance of the theoretical contribution is questionable.

The paper also makes an unsupported claim that "many of these algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium" and that their formal analysis "could be extended to include them." This is speculative without additional theoretical or empirical evidence. The authors do not provide any evidence that UCB or other existing MCTS variants actually converge to Nash equilibrium, nor do they attempt to prove this for any other specific algorithm.

While the paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games, the contribution is limited by its narrow scope and lack of practical validation. The authors present a theoretically interesting result but fail to demonstrate how it improves upon existing methods or provides practical advantages. The paper reads more like a theoretical exercise than a contribution that advances the state of the art in game-theoretic algorithms.

The paper's significance is further weakened by its limited empirical evaluation. The authors only test on small games with limited branching factors (up to depth 2, branching factor 2), which are far from the complex, large-scale games where MCTS is typically applied. The worst-case analysis in Section 5.2 is also limited to a small subset of games (only 3x3 utilities), which does not provide a comprehensive understanding of the algorithm's behavior.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a theoretically sound contribution to the understanding of MCTS convergence, it falls short on practical validation and comparative analysis against established baselines. The disconnect between the theoretical guarantee and the empirical performance of the required implementation (mean propagation) significantly undermines the paper's contribution. The reviewers would have been more persuaded by a paper that demonstrated both the theoretical guarantee and a practical advantage over existing methods, rather than a theoretical result that requires a modification that appears to hurt performance.

**Strengths:**
- Rigorous theoretical analysis
- Correctly identifies a gap in the literature
- Well-structured and clearly written

**Weaknesses:**
- The required implementation (mean propagation) converges slower than standard MCTS
- No comparison with established baselines like CFR
- Limited empirical evaluation on small, simple games
- Speculative claims about extending analysis to other algorithms
- Failure to demonstrate practical advantages of the theoretical result

Given the skepticism towards new approaches without thorough empirical validation, and the lack of evidence that the proposed method outperforms established techniques, I recommend rejection. The paper makes a theoretical contribution but fails to demonstrate its practical relevance or superiority over existing methods.
