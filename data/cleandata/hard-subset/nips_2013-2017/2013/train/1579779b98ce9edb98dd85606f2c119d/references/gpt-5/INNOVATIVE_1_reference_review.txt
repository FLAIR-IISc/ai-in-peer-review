Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The main contribution is a general SM-MCTS template that, when instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent (HC) selection rule that guarantees exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3), provably converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis proceeds by (i) establishing convergence of HC algorithms in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded observation error, and (iii) inductively propagating error bounds up the game tree to show eventual convergence to an O(D^2)·ε-NE, where D is tree depth (the theorem statement as written omits ε; see comment below). A key technical detail is that convergence requires backing up mean returns (SM-MCTS-M) rather than single-sample returns. Empirically, the authors compare RM and Exp3 with/without mean backups on random and small worst-case games, finding convergence in practice, with mean backups slightly slower.

Relation to prior work: While MCTS/UCT has had great empirical success in sequential perfect-information games, known counterexamples show UCT can fail in simultaneous-move settings. Alpha-beta with LP/double oracle can solve these games via backward induction but requires heavy domain knowledge or expensive cutoffs. Counterfactual regret minimization (CFR) applies broadly to imperfect-information EFGs but is not an anytime search procedure. This paper fills a theoretical gap by giving convergence guarantees for MCTS variants in simultaneous-move perfect-information games using online learning (HC) primitives.

Strengths
- Novel and bold: A first convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games using a very general condition (ε-Hannan consistency with exploration). This is a compelling conceptual bridge between online learning in matrix games and tree search.
- Generality/unification: The template captures multiple selection rules (RM, Exp3) and yields subgame-perfect approximate equilibria, not just equilibrium at the root.
- Sound high-level proof strategy: Reduction to repeated matrix games with bounded error and an induction over depth is clear and reusable.
- Practical insight: Identifies the mean-backup variant as theoretically required, and experiments suggest the empirical penalty is modest.
- Clarity of assumptions: The guaranteed exploration requirement is explicit (and practically achieved via standard exploration or mixing).

Weaknesses
- Tightness of bounds and notation: Theorem 4.11 states a (D^2 + δ)-NE; it appears the approximation should scale with ε (e.g., O(D^2 ε)), consistent with earlier lemmas. Please clarify the exact dependency on ε and constants (e.g., the “4(c+1)” factor).
- Mean backup requirement: Needing RetVal = Xh/nh is a nonstandard MCTS change. Is there a path to convergence with single-sample backups under additional conditions (e.g., importance weighting or explicit averaging at selection time)? This limits direct reuse of existing SM-MCTS code.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Random games and exhaustive tiny worst-case instances are useful sanity checks, but there are no large or recognized benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel variants, Tron domains). No finite-time performance comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., simultaneous alpha-beta + double oracle) or to CFR-style approaches adapted to this setting.
- Asymptotic-only theory: No finite-time rates are provided; the induction yields loose O(D^2) dependence and constants (e.g., 4(c+1) terms). Even a high-level dependence on branching factor/γ would be informative.
- Tree-completion assumption: The formal analysis assumes eventual complete expansion. While justified by guaranteed exploration “in the limit,” the practical implications for large trees (memory/time) are not discussed.

Quality
- The theorems and lemmas are plausible, with correct high-level dependencies and appropriate use of HC regret bounds, SLLN/martingale arguments, and induction over tree depth. Some proofs are deferred to the appendix. The empirical section is careful but small-scale.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized with a clean algorithm template. Some notation is dense; consider streamlining definitions of empirical vs. average strategies and consistently annotating where ε factors enter bounds. Algorithm 1 could benefit from clearer pseudocode for RetVal choices and explicit statement that both players run the same algorithm with sign-flipped utilities. Minor typos and reference formatting inconsistencies.

Originality
- High. To my knowledge, this is the first convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games under broad online-learning-based selection rules.

Significance
- Conceptually significant: it legitimizes a widely used heuristic (SM-MCTS) by tying it to HC guarantees and subgame-perfect equilibrium. It should influence future algorithm design and analysis in simultaneous-move domains and stimulate work toward finite-time bounds and broader EFG classes.

Questions/requests for authors
- Please clarify Theorem 4.11’s approximation factor: does it scale as O(D^2 ε) (and with the “4” from earlier lemmas), or is ε absorbed elsewhere?
- Can the mean-backup requirement be relaxed by using importance-weighted sample returns or by averaging at selection time? Any negative results for single-sample propagation?
- How sensitive is convergence to γ? Empirically, do larger trees show an exploration–bias trade-off consistent with the theory?
- Could you include at least one larger benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel) and a comparison to a practical alpha-beta + pruning baseline?

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, novel theoretical contribution establishing convergence of SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE under broad conditions.
- General template connecting MCTS and HC learning; likely to influence subsequent research.
- Empirical evidence supports practicality of the mean-backup variant.

Cons for acceptance
- Asymptotic-only guarantees with loose constants; limited empirical scope and no strong baselines.
- The requirement to back up means reduces plug-and-play appeal.
- Some notation/bound clarity issues.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces an important and novel theoretical foundation for SM-MCTS with a unifying perspective that outweighs the limitations in finite-time analysis and empirical breadth.
