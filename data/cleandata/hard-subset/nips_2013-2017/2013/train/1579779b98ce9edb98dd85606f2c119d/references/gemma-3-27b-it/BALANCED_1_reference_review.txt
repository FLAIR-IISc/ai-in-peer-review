This paper investigates the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a challenging area where theoretical guarantees are lacking. The authors provide a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting, proving convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under the assumption of using a -Hannan consistent selection function. The work builds upon the existing literature on MCTS in sequential games and extends it to a more complex game structure. The empirical evaluation demonstrates convergence speeds comparable to existing algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength lies in providing a theoretical convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. This is a significant advance, as previous work lacked such assurances. The use of -Hannan consistency as a key requirement is well-justified and connects the analysis to existing online learning theory.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general and can be instantiated with different regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games, offering flexibility in implementation.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the definitions, background, algorithm, and proof sketch. The notation is consistent, and the logical flow is easy to follow.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while not exhaustive, provide support for the theoretical claims and demonstrate that the proposed algorithms perform competitively with existing approaches. The analysis of worst-case games is a good addition.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, referencing relevant work in game theory, MCTS, and online learning.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Proof Sketch:** The paper provides a “proof sketch” and relegates the full proof to an appendix/arXiv link. While common, it makes a thorough assessment of the theoretical contribution challenging for reviewers. The level of detail in the sketch is insufficient to fully appreciate the technical subtleties. Critical steps are simply stated without sufficient justification, hindering a complete evaluation of the rigor.
* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves convergence, it doesn't provide concrete bounds on the convergence rate.  The theoretical result guarantees an approximate Nash equilibrium, but doesn't specify how quickly this is achieved, or how the approximation error scales with the number of iterations. This limits the practical implications of the result.
* **Exploration Parameter:** The role of the exploration parameter (γ) is not thoroughly investigated. The experiments test a few values, but a more systematic analysis of its impact on convergence speed and the quality of the equilibrium would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses on randomly generated games and a few worst-case scenarios. Expanding the empirical analysis to more complex and realistic game domains (e.g., Goofspiel or other relevant applications mentioned in the introduction) would strengthen the validation.
* **Incremental Tree Building:** The authors acknowledge disregarding the incremental tree building in their analysis, claiming it doesn't prevent eventual convergence. This simplification requires stronger justification. The impact of the tree-building process on the convergence rate could be significant.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in the field of game-theoretic algorithms: proving the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
* The theoretical results are novel and contribute to a deeper understanding of the properties of MCTS.
* The empirical results, while limited, provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
* The generality of the proposed MCTS template makes it a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The proof sketch is insufficient for a thorough assessment of the theoretical contribution.  A more detailed presentation of the key proof steps is needed.
* The lack of concrete convergence rate bounds limits the practical impact of the results.
* The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and doesn’t adequately demonstrate the algorithm’s performance in realistic game domains.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a valuable contribution to the field of game-theoretic algorithms. The theoretical result is significant, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. However, the lack of a complete proof and the limited empirical scope raise concerns.

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with a strong encouragement to resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Provide a more detailed and complete proof** in the main paper or supplement. Address the concerns regarding the sketch's lack of rigor.
2. **Investigate the convergence rate** and attempt to derive bounds on the approximation error.
3. **Conduct a more extensive empirical evaluation** including more complex game domains.
4. **Analyze the impact of the exploration parameter** (γ) on convergence speed and equilibrium quality.
5. **Justify the simplification of disregarding incremental tree building** more thoroughly, or relax this assumption in the analysis.



Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a compelling contribution to NIPS.
