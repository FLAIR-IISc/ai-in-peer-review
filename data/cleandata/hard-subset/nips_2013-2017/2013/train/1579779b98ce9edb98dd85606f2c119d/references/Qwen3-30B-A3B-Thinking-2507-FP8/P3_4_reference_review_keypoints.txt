- Significant theoretical contribution: proves convergence of MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games to subgame-perfect ε-Nash using ε-Hannan consistent selection (e.g., regret matching, Exp3).  
- Sound proof structure: builds from matrix games to extensive-form games via induction with logically structured arguments.  
- Formalizes integration of regret minimization procedures into MCTS, giving a principled action-selection template.  
- Empirical results on random games support theoretical claims and highlight effects of propagating mean values vs. current samples.  
- Major practical limitation: analysis assumes the full game tree is prebuilt, whereas MCTS is normally incremental—this may invalidate practical applicability.  
- No finite-time convergence bounds are provided, leaving practitioners without guidance on iterations required to reach a desired ε.  
- Incomplete comparison to recent MCTS variants for simultaneous move games (e.g., CFR-based methods), weakening the paper's positioning relative to state-of-the-art.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic and small worst-case games; lacks experiments on non-trivial, real-world simultaneous-move games.  
- Critical concern: the assumption of a fully built tree contradicts MCTS’s main advantage and undermines practical relevance of convergence guarantees.  
- Recommendation: acceptance with minor revisions contingent on addressing practical gaps.  
- Suggested revisions: provide finite-time bounds, analyze incremental tree construction effects, compare with recent simultaneous-move MCTS variants, and evaluate on meaningful real-world games.
