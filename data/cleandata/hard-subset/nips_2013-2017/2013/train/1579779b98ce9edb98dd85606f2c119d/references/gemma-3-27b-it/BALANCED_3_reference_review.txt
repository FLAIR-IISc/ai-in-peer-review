This paper investigates the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous move games. The authors provide a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting, proving that convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium is guaranteed when employing an -Hannan consistent selection function. The analysis is complemented by empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3, demonstrating comparable convergence speeds to existing algorithms.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, extending its established success in sequential games to the more complex domain of simultaneous moves. The problem is well-motivated, highlighting the limitations of backward induction and the practical need for simulation-based approaches like MCTS. The connection to regret minimization techniques from matrix game theory provides a solid foundation for the analysis. The formal proof of convergence is a substantial contribution, offering valuable insights into the behavior of MCTS in this setting. The empirical results, while not groundbreaking, support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed framework. The authors also acknowledge the limitations and potential for extending the analysis to other MCTS variants, fostering further research.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The formal proof of convergence is the strongest aspect of this paper. It provides a rigorous foundation for understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games, which was previously lacking.
* **Novelty:** The application of -Hannan consistency to analyze MCTS in this domain is a novel and insightful approach.  Linking MCTS to established results in regret minimization is clever.
* **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The definitions and background are clearly explained, making the core ideas accessible.  Algorithm 1 provides a clear overview of the SM-MCTS framework.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature on MCTS, game theory, and simultaneous move games, citing relevant works.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed algorithms perform comparably to existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Tightness of Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves convergence, the rate of convergence is not explicitly addressed, and the theoretical bounds are likely loose. The convergence guarantees relate to the approximation parameter epsilon, but don't provide concrete insights into how many iterations are needed to achieve a specific level of accuracy.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on randomly generated games and a limited set of worst-case examples. Exploring a wider range of game domains, particularly those with real-world applications (e.g., Goofspiel, security games), would strengthen the practical relevance of the findings.
* **Dependence on -Hannan Consistency:** The convergence guarantee relies heavily on the assumption of -Hannan consistency. Discussing the practical implications of this assumption and the sensitivity of the algorithm to violations of this condition would be valuable.  Are there scenarios where this assumption might not hold in practice?
* **Algorithmic Differences within MCTS:** The paper mainly focuses on proving convergence for a general MCTS template. A more detailed comparison of the performance and characteristics of different MCTS variants (e.g., UCT, Exp3-MCTS, Regret Matching MCTS) would be beneficial.  
* **Numerical Stability:** The mention of needing to modify Exp3 for numerical stability is a bit glossed over. A more detailed discussion of potential numerical issues and their mitigation strategies would be useful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games.
* The convergence guarantee is a valuable contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and appropriately contextualized within the existing literature.
* The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practical feasibility of the approach.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The convergence rate is not explicitly addressed, limiting the practical usefulness of the theoretical results.
* The empirical evaluation is relatively limited in scope.
* The strong reliance on -Hannan consistency could be a potential limitation in real-world applications.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the convergence rate analysis more detailed, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses.  

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** 

I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Discuss Convergence Rate:** Add a section discussing the limitations of the current analysis regarding convergence rate and potential avenues for future research in this direction.
2.  **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Include results on more diverse game domains, such as Goofspiel or variations of security games.
3.  **Address -Hannan Consistency:** Elaborate on the practical implications of the -Hannan consistency assumption and potential strategies for mitigating its impact in scenarios where it may not hold perfectly.
4.  **Clarify Numerical Stability:** Provide a more detailed explanation of the numerical stability issues with Exp3 and the rationale behind the modification used.
