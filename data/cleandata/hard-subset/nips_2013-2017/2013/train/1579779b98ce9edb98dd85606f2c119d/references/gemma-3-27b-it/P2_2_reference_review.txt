## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games. It establishes a general template for SM-MCTS algorithms, showing that using any -Hannan consistent regret minimizing procedure for matrix games as a selection function guarantees convergence to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium.  The authors support their theoretical findings with empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to existing algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, particularly its application beyond sequential games. While MCTS has been successfully applied to simultaneous-move games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), prior work lacked convergence guarantees. The paper builds on existing regret minimization techniques from the bandit literature (Regret Matching, Exp3) and adapts them to the SM-MCTS framework. It correctly positions itself in relation to previous attempts at solving simultaneous-move games using backward induction and alpha-beta pruning, highlighting the limitations of those approaches in scenarios with limited domain knowledge. The connection to Hannan consistency is a key contribution, linking established online learning theory to MCTS convergence.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of MCTS convergence, a crucial contribution to the field. The proof structure, building from matrix games to multi-stage games via induction, is well-defined.
* **Generality:**  The proposed template is general, allowing for the integration of various regret minimizing procedures. This offers flexibility and potential for future improvements by utilizing more advanced regret minimization algorithms.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are precise, and the algorithm descriptions are clear. The notation is consistent and aids in understanding the theoretical arguments.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while not exhaustive, demonstrate that the theoretical guarantees translate to practical convergence in randomly generated and worst-case games. The comparison to existing MCTS approaches is helpful.
* **Addresses a Significant Gap:** The work directly tackles a fundamental question about the theoretical properties of MCTS in a class of games where its practical success is well-documented but theoretical understanding is limited.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical analysis establishes convergence to an *approximate* Nash equilibrium, but provides limited information about the *rate* of convergence. While the empirical results offer some insight, a more detailed analysis of the convergence speed would be valuable.  The bound achieved (dependent on game depth) might be loose and could discourage practical use in very deep games.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the worst-case game analysis is a good start, expanding the set of evaluated games (including larger, more complex scenarios) would strengthen the findings.  The comparison to existing MCTS algorithms could be more detailed and comprehensive.
* **Practical Considerations:**  The paper acknowledges the potential for numerical instability with Exp3 (and addresses it). However, there is little discussion of other practical challenges that might arise when implementing these algorithms, such as memory usage or computational cost.
* **Assumptions:** The requirement of -Hannan consistency, while theoretically sound, might be restrictive. Investigating the robustness of the results to algorithms that are *approximately* -Hannan consistent could broaden the applicability of the framework.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in a challenging class of games.
* The results are generally well-supported by empirical evidence.
* The work is clearly written and well-organized.
* The problem addressed is important and relevant to the NIPS community.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The convergence rate analysis is limited.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The practical considerations are not fully explored.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical foundations of MCTS. The theoretical results are rigorous and the empirical evaluation supports the claims. While the convergence rate analysis and empirical scope could be improved, these are not fatal flaws.  The paper meets the NIPS criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance.  I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance.
