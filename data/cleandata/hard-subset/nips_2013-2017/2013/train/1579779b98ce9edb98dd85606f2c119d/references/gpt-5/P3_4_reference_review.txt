Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that, at each node, uses any ε–Hannan consistent (HC) selection algorithm with guaranteed exploration to choose joint actions, and proves almost-sure convergence of the empirical/average strategies to a subgame-perfect (ε·D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium (NE), where D is the game-tree depth. The analysis proceeds by: (i) revisiting convergence of HC learners in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error; and (iii) inductively composing the error across depths when backpropagating mean returns (SM-MCTS-M). Empirically, with regret matching (RM) and Exp3 at nodes, they observe convergence on random and “worst-case” small games; using mean backprop slows convergence slightly but still converges.

Relation to prior work: UCT lacks NE convergence in simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al.), while backward induction with LP/double-oracle is exact but costly. CFR guarantees convergence in imperfect-information EFGs, but typical SM-MCTS lacks theory. This paper provides the first asymptotic convergence guarantees for MCTS in this simultaneous-move setting under mild local assumptions. The work connects no-regret learning in matrix games to tree search.

Quality
Strengths
- The main theorem is technically sound at a high level: assuming node-wise ε–HC learners with guaranteed exploration and mean backprop, the induction from leaves to root using a bounded-error repeated-game analysis is a reasonable and well-structured proof strategy. The results on repeated games with bounded error are useful and clearly formulated.
- The abstraction that separates “selection algorithm is ε–HC” from the SM-MCTS mechanism is clean and modular.
- The empirical section, though limited, is consistent with the theory and clarifies the practical effect of mean backprop vs. sample backprop.

Weaknesses and gaps
- Conditional result: The core convergence theorem depends on using an ε–HC selection rule with guaranteed exploration at each node. While Exp3 with importance weighting fits standard HC assumptions under bandit feedback, the regret-matching instantiation described does not use unbiased importance-weighted full-information estimates and is not known to be HC under bandit feedback. The paper should either (a) prove that the specific RM update used here is ε–HC under the observed feedback, or (b) restrict formal claims to algorithms like Exp3 (or bandit RM variants) for which HC is established, and present RM results as empirical only.
- Mean backprop requirement: The proof assumes backpropagation of node-level sample means. This is nonstandard in MCTS, and the paper only offers empirical evidence that sample backprop also converges. A theoretical treatment (e.g., martingale arguments showing sample-return backprop still yields bounded errors at inner nodes) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time rates: The paper proves “eventual” convergence without rates. Given that RM and Exp3 have known regret rates, one could attempt to derive finite-sample exploitability bounds that propagate through depth (albeit pessimistically). Even an outline or discussion of how rates deteriorate with depth would help assess practicality.
- Loose constants and depth dependence: The (ε·D^2) factor and intermediate constants (e.g., 4(c+1)) appear pessimistic. Is D^2 necessary, or can it be improved to O(D) or even depth-independent under stronger assumptions (e.g., unbiased value estimates and concentration)? Clarifying tightness or providing examples approaching the bound would be valuable.
- Guaranteed exploration assumption: Requiring every joint action to be sampled infinitely often at every node is unrealistic in large games and leads to non-vanishing exploitability due to forced exploration. The experiments indeed saturate at the exploration floor. A discussion of decaying exploration or confidence-triggered exploration (and how this affects the HC and the proof) would improve practical relevance.
- Scope/terminology: Calling the class “perfect information with simultaneous moves” is potentially confusing because simultaneous moves create information sets of size >1 at a node. Please clarify the information structure precisely (perfect state observability but simultaneous action choice) and relate to standard EFG terminology.
- Empirical evaluation is limited: Only small random/worst-case synthetic games are used. No comparison to established baselines such as CFR or backward-induction with pruning/LP on medium instances (e.g., Goofspiel variants). No ablations on exploration schedules or branching/depth scaling beyond very small sizes.

Clarity
- Overall well-written and organized, with helpful definitions and lemmas. A few notational choices could be cleaner: (i) distinguish clearly between empirical frequencies and average strategies and stick to one for the main theorem; (ii) state explicitly whether mean vs. sample backprop is used in each experimental plot; (iii) define “value returned” to parents precisely when using mean backprop and how that interacts with HC updates.
- Consider moving the “complete tree eventually” lemma from the appendix into the main text with a brief proof sketch, since it is central to the asymptotic argument.
- Cite and discuss CFR and bandit variants of regret matching, which are highly relevant to the “local solver” design in EFGs.

Originality
- The core theoretical contribution—proving asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS with local ε–HC selection to subgame-perfect approximate NE—is, to my knowledge, new and fills a clear gap. The repeated-game-with-bounded-error lemma is a neat technical device.
- The algorithmic template is a natural combination of known techniques (MCTS + no-regret learners), but the tree-wise convergence analysis for simultaneous-move games appears novel.

Significance
- The result provides the first theoretical justification for a widely used heuristic (SM-MCTS) in this class of games and connects online learning guarantees at nodes to global equilibrium convergence. This is conceptually important and likely to influence further work (e.g., finite-time rates, general-sum, multi-player).
- Practical impact is tempered by the reliance on mean backprop, persistent exploration, and asymptotics. Strengthening the empirical evaluation on benchmark games and comparing against CFR would help demonstrate practical significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and relevant theoretical advance for a problem of current interest.
- Clean modular framework; broadly applicable to multiple node-level algorithms.
- Sound analysis with clear intuition and supporting experiments.

Cons for acceptance
- Conditional nature of the main theorem; mismatch between RM instantiation and HC assumptions.
- Requires mean backprop; no theory for standard sample backprop.
- No finite-time bounds; limited empirical scope and baselines.
- Loose constants and strong exploration assumptions reduce practical guidance.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a proof (or cite one) that the RM variant used is ε–HC under the paper’s feedback, or constrain formal claims to Exp3/bandit-RM and present RM as empirical.
- Attempt a finite-sample analysis by composing regret rates across depths; even pessimistic bounds would be informative.
- Investigate decaying exploration and its effect on convergence and exploitability; discuss practical exploration-exploitation schedules.
- Clarify terminology around information structure; add references to CFR and bandit regret matching.
- Strengthen experiments: include CFR as a baseline, evaluate on standard simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel), and report scaling with depth/branching.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept if the HC/RM mismatch is addressed (or claims adjusted) and clarity improved; otherwise borderline, given the theoretical novelty but practical limitations.
