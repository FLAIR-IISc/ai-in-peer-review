Summary: Proposes an MCTS template for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games and proves convergence to subgame-perfect ε‑Nash equilibrium using ε‑Hannan consistent selection.

Key theoretical contribution: First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, extending prior sequential-game results.

Methodology: Uses induction and Hannan consistency (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) to link online learning to approximate Nash equilibrium.

Empirical evaluation: Experiments on random and worst-case games show reasonable convergence rates, often matching or exceeding other MCTS variants.

Clarity: Paper is well-written, organized, and supported by clear definitions, algorithm descriptions, figures, and full proofs in the appendix.

Originality: Novel template for SM‑MCTS that can be instantiated with regret minimizers; addresses unique challenges of joint action selection.

Significance: Fills a theoretical gap with implications for theory and practice (security games, card games, general game-playing).

Strength — Rigour: Theoretical analysis is rigorous and the proofs are sound and well-motivated.

Weakness — Limited empirical comparisons: Lacks direct comparisons to specialized simultaneous-move algorithms (e.g., double-oracle, CFR variants).

Weakness — Computational complexity: Does not analyze computational feasibility or complexity relative to other approaches.

Weakness — Scope limitation: Focuses on perfect-information games and gives only brief discussion on extension to imperfect-information settings.

Recommendation (for): Strong theoretical contribution, technically sound, empirically supported — merits acceptance.

Recommendation (against): Missing comparisons to existing simultaneous-move methods and absence of complexity analysis are notable drawbacks.

Final assessment: Substantial, well-executed contribution that advances understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games and should be accepted.
