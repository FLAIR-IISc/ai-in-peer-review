## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a setting where theoretical guarantees are currently lacking despite practical success. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, when using an -Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They validate this with experiments using Regret Matching and Exp3 as the underlying regret minimization procedures, showing comparable convergence speeds to existing algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established success of MCTS in sequential games (e.g., Go) and acknowledges the existing algorithmic advancements in solving simultaneous-move games like Goofspiel using backward induction and alpha-beta pruning. However, it addresses a critical gap – the lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in these settings.  The work correctly positions itself against existing MCTS approaches for simultaneous games (e.g., [10, 11, 12]), noting that they lack formal convergence proofs. It also leverages the well-studied concepts of -Hannan consistency from online learning and bandit algorithms [15, 16]. The authors also cite relevant work demonstrating that naive UCB approaches *do not* converge in this setting [17], highlighting the novelty of their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength lies in providing the *first* convergence guarantee for a class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games. This is a significant theoretical advance for the field.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general and allows for instantiation with any -Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure, offering flexibility.
* **Soundness of Proof:** The proof appears rigorous and well-structured, building logically from repeated matrix games with bounded error to the full extensive-form game.  The decomposition and inductive reasoning are convincing.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, while not demonstrating superior performance to existing heuristics, confirm that the proposed algorithm converges in practice and at comparable rates. This provides confidence in the theoretical findings.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are precise and the notation is clear (though dense in sections 2 and 4).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical result guarantees convergence to an *approximate* Nash equilibrium, but does not provide concrete bounds on the convergence *rate*.  The -Nash equilibrium guarantee, while valuable, isn’t very tight. The experimental section addresses this somewhat, but a tighter theoretical bound would be highly desirable.
* **Practical Relevance of -Hannan Consistency:** While theoretically elegant, the requirement of -Hannan consistency might be restrictive. The paper acknowledges this and notes that modifications can be made to algorithms that don't natively possess this property, but it doesn't fully discuss the implications for commonly used MCTS heuristics.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental section primarily focuses on randomly generated games and doesn't include evaluation on real-world problems.  Demonstrating convergence on relevant, complex game instances would further strengthen the paper. The “worst case” experiments are useful, but lack context as to how those games relate to practical scenarios.
* **Dense Formal Analysis:**  Sections 2 and 4 are highly technical and require a strong background in game theory and online learning. While thorough, the density might hinder accessibility for a broader NIPS audience.  More intuitive explanations alongside the formalisms could improve comprehension.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result addressing a long-standing open problem in the field of game playing and reinforcement learning.
* The proofs appear sound and are well-motivated.
* The experimental results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
* The generality of the proposed template is a strength.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of concrete convergence rate bounds weakens the practical impact of the theoretical result.
* The reliance on -Hannan consistency could limit the applicability of the approach.
* The experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, including real-world game instances.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the NIPS community.  The theoretical guarantee of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a significant advancement. While the limitations regarding convergence rates and practical applicability exist, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses.  The work is technically sound, clearly presented (despite density in some sections), original, and potentially impactful.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest:

*  Discussing the implications of the -Hannan consistency requirement in more detail, potentially exploring ways to relax this constraint or identify commonly used heuristics that implicitly satisfy it.
*  Adding a discussion of potential future work focusing on tighter convergence bounds.
*  Expanding the experimental section with evaluation on a more diverse set of games, including real-world instances if possible.
*  Consider adding a less formal, intuitive explanation of the key concepts in Sections 2 and 4 to improve accessibility for a broader audience.
