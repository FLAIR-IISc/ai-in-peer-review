- Paper addresses MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move games and proves that using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function yields convergence to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium, supported by experiments with Regret Matching and Exp3.  
- Correctly situates itself relative to prior MCTS and simultaneous-move game work, noting prior methods lacked formal convergence proofs and that naive UCB can fail.  
- Primary strength: first theoretical convergence guarantee for a class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games.  
- Strength: general MCTS template allowing any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimizer to be instantiated.  
- Strength: proofs appear rigorous, using decomposition and inductive reasoning from repeated matrix games to extensive-form games.  
- Strength: empirical results confirm practical convergence at rates comparable to existing algorithms.  
- Strength: paper is generally well-written with precise definitions and clear (though dense) notation.  
- Weakness: no concrete bounds on convergence rates; ε-Nash guarantee is not tight.  
- Weakness: reliance on ε-Hannan consistency may be restrictive and implications for common MCTS heuristics are not fully discussed.  
- Weakness: experiments are limited (mostly random games) and lack evaluation on real-world or complex game instances.  
- Weakness: Sections 2 and 4 are highly technical and may be inaccessible to a broader audience without more intuition.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, significant theoretical advance with sound proofs and supporting experiments.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited practical impact due to missing rate bounds, reliance on ε-Hannan consistency, and limited experiments.  
- Overall assessment: strong contribution; strengths outweigh weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: discuss implications/relaxations of ε-Hannan consistency and identify heuristics that satisfy it.  
- Requested revisions: include discussion/future work on tighter convergence bounds.  
- Requested revisions: expand experimental evaluation to more diverse and real-world games.  
- Requested revisions: add informal, intuitive explanations for dense technical sections to improve accessibility.
