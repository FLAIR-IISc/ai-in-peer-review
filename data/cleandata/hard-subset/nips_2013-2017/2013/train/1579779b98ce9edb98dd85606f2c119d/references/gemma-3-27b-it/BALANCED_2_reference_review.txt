This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The core contribution is proving that MCTS, when instantiated with a -Hannan consistent regret minimizing procedure, converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The authors support this with empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to existing algorithms.

**Summary & Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper tackles a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS. While MCTS has proven incredibly successful in various game domains, particularly sequential games like Go, formal convergence guarantees remain elusive for simultaneous move games. Existing work, such as [10, 11, 12, 13], primarily focuses on practical application and empirical performance.  The authors correctly point out that simple adaptations of UCT ([14]) don't converge ([17]).  They build on the foundation of Hannan consistency ([15, 16]) and extend it to the context of MCTS in these games.  The connection to backward induction [7, 4, 8, 9] is appropriately framed as a comparison - these methods are often impractical due to domain knowledge requirements and computational expense. This work provides a theoretical underpinning for the empirical success of MCTS in this game class.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:**  Providing a convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games is a significant theoretical advance. This addresses a long-standing open question and offers a strong justification for the continued use of MCTS in these applications.
* **Soundness of Analysis:** The proof, leveraging Hannan consistency and an inductive argument, appears rigorous and well-structured. The careful consideration of error propagation in repeated matrix games with errors is a crucial component.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written, with clear definitions and a logical flow. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is concise and easy to understand.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The empirical results, while not exhaustive, provide valuable support for the theoretical findings. The comparison of propagation methods (mean vs. current sample) and the analysis of worst-case games are insightful.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors provide a comprehensive overview of relevant literature, including both theoretical and practical work in game theory and MCTS.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves *convergence*, it doesn't provide explicit bounds on the *rate* of convergence. The experiments provide some insight, but a more detailed analysis of the convergence speed (e.g., as a function of game parameters like depth and branching factor) would be valuable.
* **Practical Implications of -Hannan Consistency:** The paper relies on the assumption of -Hannan consistency. While this is a well-studied property, it's not always easy to verify or guarantee in practice. A discussion of the practical challenges of ensuring -Hannan consistency and its impact on the performance of the algorithm would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:**  The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.  The use of randomly generated and worst-case games is good, but evaluating the algorithms on more realistic game instances (e.g., variations of Goofspiel [3, 4], or games from general game-playing competitions [6]) would strengthen the findings.
* **Numerical Stability:** The mention of a modification to Exp3 to avoid numerical overflows is a good point, but it raises questions about the sensitivity of the algorithm to numerical precision and the potential for instability in more complex games.
* **Potential for Simplification:** The notation is dense and, in places, somewhat cumbersome.  Simplifying some of the definitions and equations could improve readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in the theoretical understanding of MCTS.
* The theoretical results are rigorous and well-supported.
* The empirical evaluation provides evidence of convergence and is consistent with the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
* The work has the potential to significantly impact the field of game theory and AI.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The lack of explicit convergence rate bounds limits the practical utility of the results.
* The empirical evaluation is relatively limited in scope.
* The reliance on -Hannan consistency may pose challenges for practical implementation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the lack of explicit convergence rate bounds and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation are weaknesses, the novelty and significance of the theoretical results outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept.** However, I strongly encourage the authors to consider addressing the weaknesses identified above in future work, particularly by investigating convergence rate bounds and expanding the empirical evaluation to include more realistic game instances.  Adding a discussion of the practical challenges of ensuring Hannan consistency would also be valuable.
