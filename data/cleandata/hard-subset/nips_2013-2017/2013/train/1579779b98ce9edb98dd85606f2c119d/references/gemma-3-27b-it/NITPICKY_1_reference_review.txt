This paper presents a convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The core idea is to frame SM-MCTS as an algorithm utilizing regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection functions, and proving convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under certain consistency conditions. The work builds upon existing theory for single-agent regret minimization and extends it to the multi-agent, simultaneous-move setting. While the theoretical result is valuable, and the empirical evaluation provides some supporting evidence, the paper suffers from numerous minor flaws in presentation, detail, and rigor, ultimately hindering its overall impact.

**Summary of Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a genuinely novel contribution, addressing a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of this widely-used algorithm.
*   **Rigorous Framework:** The paper attempts a formal proof, grounding the analysis in Hannan consistency and regret minimization, which adds credibility to the claims.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately connect their work to related areas like bandit algorithms, game theory, and MCTS, acknowledging the foundations on which they build.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, even if relatively limited, demonstrates an attempt to validate the theoretical findings.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is burdened with numerous minor writing issues. Sentences are often convoluted, and the notation, while standard in game theory, is not always clearly explained within the context of the MCTS algorithm. Repeated use of "namely" is distracting.
*   **Technical Detail & Rigor:** Despite the claim of a formal proof, several technical aspects feel underdeveloped. The transition between the repeated matrix game analysis and the multi-stage extensive form game is glossed over. The handling of the exploration parameter γ feels ad-hoc, and its impact on convergence is not fully explored. The statement "The constant 4 is caused by going from a pair of strategies with best responses within 2 of the game value..." is particularly unconvincing and lacks sufficient justification.
*   **Incomplete Proof:** While the paper references an appendix and arXiv link, the completeness of the proofs is crucial for NIPS. The reliance on omitted proofs substantially diminishes the impact of the paper. The "proof sketch" provided within the paper lacks sufficient detail to assess its validity.
*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section is weak. The choice of games is not well-motivated, and the results are not particularly compelling. The comparison between RMM and RM is marginal, and the conclusion that the presented findings generalize to other MCTS variants is unsupported. The selection of worst-case games appears somewhat arbitrary.
*   **Formatting Inconsistencies:** Minor formatting issues throughout the document detract from readability, including inconsistent spacing and punctuation.
*   **Redundancy:** Certain sections, such as the definition of matrix games, feel overly verbose and redundant, especially given the intended audience.

**Specific Comments & Concerns:**

*   **Definition 2:** The notation 'vmin' and 'vmax' are defined, yet not subsequently used within the definition.
*   **Section 3.1 & 3.2:** The descriptions of regret matching and Exp3 lack crucial details regarding implementation (e.g., how ties are broken, how exploration is handled when regret values are small).
*   **Algorithm 1:** The line numbers do not align with the text description of the algorithm, creating confusion.
*   **Section 4.1:** The definition of 'gmax(t)' is missing a subscript or explanation of what 't' refers to.
*   **Lemma 4.4:** The statement regarding guaranteed exploration needs further justification; simply stating that most HC algorithms have it is insufficient.
*   **Section 5.1:** The claim that RMM converges "slightly slower" requires statistical justification. A simple statement without supporting data is insufficient.
*   **Figure 3:** The y-axis labels are illegible and lack units.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical result represents a significant step towards understanding the convergence properties of MCTS in a challenging game setting.
*   The paper clearly articulates the connection between regret minimization and MCTS convergence.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The numerous technical and presentation flaws undermine the credibility of the claimed results.
*   The incomplete proof and weak empirical evaluation raise serious concerns about the robustness of the findings.
*   The paper fails to meet the NIPS standards for clarity, rigor, and completeness.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and potential significance of the results, the numerous shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and empirical validation lead me to recommend **rejection**. The paper requires substantial revisions to address the identified weaknesses before it can be considered for publication in a venue like NIPS. The authors need to provide a complete and rigorously justified proof, improve the clarity of the presentation, and strengthen the empirical evaluation with more compelling results and a better-motivated experimental setup. The level of polish is simply not at the standard expected for NIPS.
