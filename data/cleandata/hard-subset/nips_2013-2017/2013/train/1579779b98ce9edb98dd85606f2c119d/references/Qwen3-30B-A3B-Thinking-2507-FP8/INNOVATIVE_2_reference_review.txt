

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. Prior to this work, MCTS had been successfully applied in practice to such games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), but no theoretical convergence analysis existed for this class of games. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any -Hannan consistent selection procedure for matrix games, and prove that such algorithms converge to subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium.

The key novelty lies in bridging the gap between the practical success of MCTS in simultaneous move games and the lack of theoretical guarantees. The paper makes the bold contribution of establishing a formal connection between regret minimization in matrix games and convergence of MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. This is particularly significant because prior theoretical work on MCTS (e.g., for sequential games) has not addressed simultaneous move settings, which are fundamental to many real-world applications.

The authors' approach is elegant and general: they show that any -Hannan consistent algorithm (like regret matching or Exp3) can be used as the selection function within their MCTS template to guarantee convergence. The proof technique is clever, using induction over the game tree depth while carefully bounding errors introduced at each level. The formal analysis is rigorous and addresses a long-standing theoretical gap in the field.

The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical contribution well. The authors compare their approach with existing MCTS variants using regret matching and Exp3, showing that the empirical convergence rates are comparable to state-of-the-art methods. The careful analysis of the difference between propagating mean values versus current samples (Figure 2) provides useful insights for practitioners. The worst-case analysis (Figure 3) further strengthens their claims by demonstrating convergence even in challenging game structures.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with precise definitions and a logical flow from basic concepts to the main theorem. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature, acknowledging the lack of prior convergence guarantees for MCTS in this domain and distinguishing their approach from existing methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, addressing a significant theoretical gap.
2. The general template approach is elegant and broadly applicable to any -Hannan consistent procedure.
3. The theoretical contribution is substantial and justifies the practical use of MCTS in this domain.
4. The empirical validation supports the theoretical claims and provides useful insights for implementation.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper focuses on zero-sum perfect-information games, which is a specific class; extension to more general games would be valuable but is not the main contribution.
2. The convergence rate is not provided in the formal analysis, only empirically evaluated.
3. The paper doesn't directly compare against the most recent MCTS variants for simultaneous move games in terms of convergence speed.

Despite these minor weaknesses, the paper's contribution is highly significant. The authors have successfully bridged a critical gap between theory and practice for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This work provides a theoretical foundation that has been missing for practitioners using MCTS in this domain, and it opens up new avenues for research in algorithmic game theory.

The paper is clearly original and represents a substantial advance over previous work. While the specific algorithms (regret matching, Exp3) are not new, the application of these to MCTS in simultaneous move games with formal convergence guarantees is novel. The connection between regret minimization and MCTS convergence is particularly insightful.

In terms of significance, this work has the potential to influence both theoretical research (in algorithmic game theory) and practical applications (in security domains, general game-playing, etc.). The paper's contribution is not incremental but rather establishes a new theoretical direction for MCTS in a previously unanalyzed domain.

Given my criteria of prioritizing novelty and bold theoretical contributions over minor methodological imperfections, I believe this paper makes a strong case for acceptance. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the approach is novel, and the work addresses a clear gap in the literature that has been recognized by practitioners but not resolved by theorists.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- General template that can be instantiated with various -Hannan consistent procedures
- Rigorous theoretical proof with careful error bounding
- Empirical validation supporting theoretical claims
- Significant impact on both theory and practice of game-theoretic algorithms

Arguments against acceptance:
- Focus on a specific class of games (zero-sum perfect information)
- Convergence rate analysis is empirical rather than theoretical
- Some minor implementation details (e.g., propagation of mean values) could be further explored

Overall, this paper represents a significant theoretical advance that justifies the practical use of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The novelty of the approach, combined with the rigor of the theoretical contribution, makes it a strong candidate for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
