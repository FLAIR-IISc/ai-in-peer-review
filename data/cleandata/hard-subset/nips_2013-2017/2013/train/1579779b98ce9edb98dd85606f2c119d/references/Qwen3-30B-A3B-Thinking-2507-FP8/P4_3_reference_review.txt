

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms for this class, where any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games can be used as the action selection function. They formally prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures each action is attempted infinitely often), the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The authors empirically validate their claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection procedures on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work on game theory and MCTS. It builds upon foundational work in extensive-form games (e.g., [7, 8, 9]), regret minimization (e.g., [15, 16]), and MCTS for sequential games (e.g., [14]). The authors clearly identify the gap in the literature: while MCTS has been successfully applied to simultaneous move games in practice (e.g., [10, 11, 12, 13]), no convergence guarantees existed for these games, unlike for sequential games. The paper also properly references prior work on MCTS convergence for sequential games and acknowledges the limitations of UCT in simultaneous move settings [17].

**Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong and well-executed. The proof of convergence is thorough, progressing from simple matrix games to multi-stage extensive-form games through a series of well-constructed lemmas. The authors carefully distinguish between the empirical frequencies and average strategies, which is crucial for the convergence analysis. The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims by showing that the proposed algorithms converge to approximate Nash equilibria in practice. The paper acknowledges limitations (e.g., no finite-time convergence bounds) and suggests concrete future work. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Key concepts (e.g., Hannan consistency, subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium) are clearly defined with appropriate mathematical notation. The structure flows logically from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical evaluation. The formal proofs are presented in a clear, step-by-step manner, with the appendix providing additional details for the interested reader. The empirical results are presented with appropriate figures that effectively communicate the convergence behavior.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant original contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The authors' approach of using Hannan consistency as a convergence condition is novel for this context, and the general template they propose (which can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures) is a substantial extension beyond previous work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous NIPS publications on MCTS (e.g., [14]) and game-theoretic algorithms (e.g., [8, 9]) by addressing a fundamentally different class of games.

**Significance:** The results have important practical implications for the design and application of MCTS algorithms in domains with simultaneous move games, including security applications, card games, and general game-playing. The theoretical foundation provided here justifies the widespread practical use of MCTS in these contexts. The work opens new research directions, particularly regarding finite-time convergence bounds and generalization to imperfect information games. The empirical results demonstrate that the algorithms based on the proposed template converge at rates comparable to existing MCTS variants, suggesting practical utility.

**Strengths:**
- First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Well-constructed theoretical framework using Hannan consistency
- Thorough empirical validation with appropriate experimental design
- Clear and well-organized presentation of complex material
- Identifies a significant gap in the literature and provides a complete solution

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution
- The empirical analysis focuses on worst-case games but doesn't fully explore the relationship between game structure and convergence rates
- The connection to practical applications could be further developed (though the paper does mention security and game-playing applications)

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the MCTS literature for a well-defined class of games.
2. The theoretical contribution is rigorous, novel, and well-supported by empirical evidence.
3. The paper provides a general framework that can be applied to various regret-minimizing procedures.
4. The work has clear practical implications for game-playing algorithms in real-world applications.
5. The presentation is exceptionally clear and well-structured.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1. The lack of finite-time convergence bounds is a notable limitation of the theoretical contribution.
2. The empirical analysis, while appropriate, doesn't fully explore the relationship between game structure and convergence rates.

Overall, this paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory with clear practical implications. The work is of high quality, well-written, and addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the lack of finite-time bounds is a minor limitation, the theoretical guarantees provided are valuable and advance the state of the art in understanding MCTS for simultaneous move games. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
