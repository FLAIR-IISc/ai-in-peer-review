**Summary**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the literature on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): its convergence properties in games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The core idea is to use a regret-minimizing algorithm from online learning, such as Regret Matching (RM) or Exp3, as the in-tree selection policy. The main contribution is a formal proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the resulting MCTS procedure converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively, starting with convergence in single matrix games, extending it to games with bounded payoff errors, and then applying this to the tree structure where value estimates from subgames act as noisy payoffs for parent nodes. The theoretical results are supported by experiments on random and worst-case games, which validate the convergence and provide practical insights, such as comparing the propagation of mean values (required by the proof) versus current sample values (common in practice).

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this important class of games. MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for simultaneous move games, but this usage has largely been based on empirical success and intuition rather than theoretical guarantees. This paper provides a solid theoretical foundation, which is a major contribution to both the MCTS and algorithmic game theory communities. It elegantly bridges the gap between online learning theory (regret minimization) and heuristic search.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis appears sound and is well-structured. The proof strategy of building from simple repeated matrix games to repeated games with bounded error, and then applying this inductively up the game tree, is clear and logical. The leveraging of Hannan consistency is a powerful and appropriate tool for this analysis. The paper is a complete piece of work, containing a novel theoretical result, a clear algorithmic template, and an empirical evaluation that corroborates the theory.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It begins with clear definitions and background, presents the algorithm template and its instantiations, and then methodically builds the theoretical argument from the ground up. The separation of the analysis into lemmas for matrix games, games with error, and the final theorem for extensive-form games makes the complex proof accessible. The experimental section is also clear in its goals and presentation of results.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** A key finding in the empirical section is that propagating the current sample's value (as in standard RM) converges slightly faster than propagating the mean value (RMM), which is the variant required for the formal analysis. While the authors acknowledge this and conjecture that their proof could be extended, it does create a slight disconnect. The paper would be strengthened if it could offer more intuition or a brief theoretical argument as to why the standard variant might also converge, or why the proof technique specifically requires the mean.

2.  **Convergence Bound Dependency:** The final convergence bound in Theorem 4.11 depends on the square of the game's depth (D^2). This seems potentially loose and could be prohibitively large for deep games. Is this polynomial dependency on depth an artifact of the inductive proof technique (where errors accumulate at each level), or is it a fundamental property of the problem? A short discussion on the tightness of this bound would be valuable.

3.  **Analysis Simplification:** The formal analysis makes the simplifying assumption that the algorithm operates on a complete, pre-built game tree. The authors justify this by noting that guaranteed exploration ensures all nodes are eventually visited infinitely often. While this is a common and often necessary simplification in MCTS analysis, it sidesteps the transient dynamics of the initial tree-building phase. The collected statistics during this phase could, in principle, bias the search for a long time. The paper would be more complete if it briefly addressed why this initial phase does not prevent eventual convergence.

4.  **Clarity of Theorem 4.11:** The notation in Theorem 4.11, "(ε_D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium," is slightly opaque without digging into the appendix. It would be clearer if the theorem explicitly stated how ε relates to the base regret bound of the selection algorithm and how the D^2 term arises. For instance, stating the bound in a form like f(ε, D) where f is made more explicit would improve readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Pro:** Provides the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, filling a crucial and long-standing theoretical gap.
*   **Pro:** The proposed theoretical framework is elegant, sound, and connects MCTS to the well-established field of online regret minimization.
*   **Pro:** The paper is well-written, well-organized, and presents a complete contribution with both theory and experiments.
*   **Pro:** The results are significant and likely to be built upon by researchers working on MCTS, general game playing, and algorithmic game theory.

**Arguments against Acceptance**
*   **Con:** The proof only applies to a variant of the algorithm (mean-propagating) that is shown to be empirically slightly inferior to the more standard sample-propagating variant.
*   **Con:** The derived theoretical convergence bound has a potentially loose dependency on game depth (D^2).

**Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to the theory of Monte Carlo Tree Search. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the main theoretical result, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-executed and clearly presented. I strongly recommend acceptance. The suggested improvements are intended to further polish an already high-quality submission.
