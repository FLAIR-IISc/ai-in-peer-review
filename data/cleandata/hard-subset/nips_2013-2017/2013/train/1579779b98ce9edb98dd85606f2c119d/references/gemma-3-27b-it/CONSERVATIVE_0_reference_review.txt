This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a setting where theoretical guarantees are largely absent. The authors demonstrate that an MCTS algorithm utilizing a -Hannan consistent selection function converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. Empirical evaluation with regret matching and Exp3 provides support for the theoretical findings.

The core strength of this work lies in addressing a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS. Extending MCTS analysis beyond sequential games is a valuable contribution. The use of -Hannan consistency as a central requirement for convergence is a clear and well-defined condition. The authors correctly identify and build upon existing work in online learning (regret matching, Exp3) to establish their result. The inclusion of empirical results, while not exhaustive, is a positive step toward validating the theoretical claims.

However, I have several significant concerns. First, while the theoretical result is appreciated, the convergence rate implied by the -Hannan consistency requirement is often slow in practice. The paper acknowledges this implicitly, yet doesn’t deeply explore the practical implications for real-world games. A slow convergence rate renders the theoretical guarantee less useful. Second, the empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. The comparison to existing MCTS algorithms for these games is merely stated as "comparable" without detailed quantitative results or statistical significance testing. The chosen games – randomly generated and “worst case” – lack the complexity of naturally occurring games. The reliance on only two regret minimizing procedures feels restrictive; more diverse selection methods should be investigated. Third, the proof’s reliance on building the complete game tree before convergence can occur seems unrealistic for large games. The discussion around this simplification is insufficient to convince me it doesn’t fundamentally alter the results. A deeper analysis of the impact of incomplete tree construction on convergence would be needed. Fourth, the paper frequently mentions the potential for extending the analysis to other MCTS variants (e.g., UCB) but does not offer any concrete steps or insights into how this could be achieved. This weakens the argument for broader applicability. Finally, while the citations are generally appropriate, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in robust game theory and approximate Nash equilibrium computation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Fills a theoretical gap regarding MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Provides a clear and well-defined convergence criterion based on -Hannan consistency.
*   Empirical results, though limited, support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Slow practical convergence rate implied by the theory.
*   Limited empirical evaluation with simplified game settings.
*   Simplifying assumption of complete tree construction raises concerns about real-world applicability.
*   Lack of concrete guidance for extending the analysis to other MCTS variants.
*   Insufficient discussion of relevant related work.



Overall, this paper presents a theoretically interesting result but falls short of demonstrating significant practical impact. The convergence guarantee is valuable, but the limitations in convergence speed and empirical validation raise concerns about its real-world utility. I lean towards **reject** at this time, but would be open to reconsidering with a significantly revised version that addresses the concerns raised above, particularly regarding empirical validation and practical convergence rate analysis. The authors should either provide stronger empirical evidence of practical utility or acknowledge the limitations more explicitly and focus the paper on the theoretical contributions alone.
