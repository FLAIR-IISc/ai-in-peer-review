

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary
This paper addresses a fundamental theoretical gap in the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games and prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures each action is tried infinitely often), the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. They instantiate this template with regret matching and Exp3, and provide empirical evidence supporting their theoretical claims. This work connects the online learning literature (Hannan consistency) to MCTS in a novel way, addressing a significant gap in the field that has been known for years.

The paper relates well to prior work in several areas: the seminal UCT algorithm (Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006) for sequential games, Shafiei et al.'s (2011) work showing UCB doesn't converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous games, and the connection between regret minimization and Nash equilibrium (Hart and Mas-Colell, 2000). It also builds on recent advances in solving simultaneous move games using double-oracle methods (Bosansky et al., 2013).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical contribution**: This is the first paper to provide convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The theoretical framework is sound, well-constructed, and addresses a long-standing gap in the literature. The inductive proof strategy is clear and logically structured.

2. **Clear connection to online learning**: The paper effectively bridges the gap between online learning (Hannan consistency) and MCTS, demonstrating how concepts from regret minimization can be applied to game tree search.

3. **Empirical validation**: The authors provide reasonable empirical evidence supporting their theoretical claims, showing that their approach converges to approximate Nash equilibria in practice.

4. **Practical relevance**: The work addresses a critical gap that has been known in the field for years, making it highly relevant for practitioners using MCTS in games with simultaneous moves.

5. **Well-organized structure**: The paper progresses logically from definitions to the main theorem, with clear lemmas and supporting results. The notation is consistent throughout.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of convergence rate analysis**: While the paper proves convergence, it doesn't provide any bounds on the convergence rate. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, as practitioners need to know how many iterations are required to reach a desired accuracy. The empirical analysis in Section 5.2 is limited to depth-2 games, which doesn't capture the full complexity of larger games.

2. **Limited comparison with existing methods**: The paper claims that "many of these algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium" but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games. The empirical results only compare variants of their own algorithm (RMM vs. RM), rather than with established methods.

3. **Unexplained empirical observation**: The paper notes that propagating the mean values (RMM) converges slightly slower than propagating the current sample value (RM), but doesn't provide a thorough explanation for this difference. This is an important practical detail that deserves more discussion.

4. **Assumption about tree building**: While the paper states that the tree will eventually be built with probability 1, this is referenced to the appendix rather than being rigorously proven in the main text. This is a crucial assumption for the convergence proof.

5. **Limited scope**: The paper is restricted to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. While the authors mention future work to generalize to imperfect information games, the current work doesn't address these more complex settings.

6. **Numerical stability discussion**: The paper mentions a numerical stability issue with Exp3 but doesn't discuss how this might affect convergence in practice, which is important for practical implementation.

## Quality, Clarity, Originality, and Significance Assessment

**Quality**: The theoretical core of the paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof. The experimental design is reasonable but limited in scope. The authors are careful about the limitations of their approach. However, the lack of convergence rate analysis is a significant shortcoming for a paper claiming to provide convergence guarantees.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized. Definitions and notation are clearly presented. The main theorem and its proof are presented logically. The connection between online learning and MCTS is explained clearly. The only minor clarity issue is the numerical stability discussion in Section 4.1, which could be expanded.

**Originality**: This is a highly original contribution. While there's extensive work on MCTS in sequential games, this is the first to provide convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS is novel, and the paper clearly differentiates itself from previous contributions.

**Significance**: The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of a widely used algorithm. The results have direct implications for practitioners using MCTS in games with simultaneous moves (such as Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion games, and general game-playing competitions). The theoretical framework could potentially be extended to other algorithms and game classes, making this a foundational contribution to the field.

## Specific Comments

1. **Theoretical contribution**: The main theorem (Theorem 4.11) is a significant contribution to the field. The proof strategy of using induction on the depth of the game tree is elegant and appropriate for this problem.

2. **Empirical analysis**: The empirical results in Section 5.1 are helpful but could be strengthened with a more detailed explanation of why RMM converges slightly slower than RM. The authors should consider providing a small theoretical explanation for this empirical observation.

3. **Worst-case analysis**: The worst-case analysis in Section 5.2 is limited to depth-2 games with branching factor 2. For a more comprehensive analysis, the authors could consider including some examples of deeper games that are challenging for MCTS.

4. **Numerical stability**: The note about numerical stability in Section 4.1 (reformulating wh,i = xh,i - max_i' xh,i') is important but too brief. The authors should explain why this is necessary and how it might affect the convergence properties.

5. **Comparison with existing work**: The paper could be strengthened by including a more detailed comparison with existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games, such as those used in the general game-playing competitions (e.g., the work by Finnsson and Björnsson, 2008).

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that has been missing from the literature for years. The theory is sound, the empirical results support the theoretical claims, and the work has clear practical relevance.

The main areas for revision are:
1. Expand the discussion of the convergence rate and include a more detailed empirical analysis of convergence speed.
2. Provide a clearer explanation for the empirical observation that RMM converges slightly slower than RM.
3. Include a more comprehensive comparison with existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games.
4. Strengthen the discussion of the tree-building assumption and its relationship to the convergence proof.

The paper would benefit from these additions to strengthen the practical relevance of the theoretical results and provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's behavior.

## Final Thoughts

This paper fills a critical theoretical gap in the application of MCTS to simultaneous move games. While the work has some limitations in scope and practical convergence analysis, the core theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. The paper represents an important step toward understanding the theoretical properties of MCTS in a broader class of games, and I believe it will be of interest to both the machine learning and game theory communities. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the field that should be recognized at NIPS.
