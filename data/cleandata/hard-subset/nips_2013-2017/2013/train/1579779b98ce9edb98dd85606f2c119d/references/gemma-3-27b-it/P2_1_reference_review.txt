## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical gap in theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games. While MCTS is widely used in practice for these games (e.g., Goofspiel, general game playing), convergence guarantees were previously unknown. The authors provide a general template for MCTS in this setting, proving that under the use of a -Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They instantiate this template with Regret Matching and Exp3, and demonstrate empirically that the convergence speed is comparable to existing MCTS algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a fundamental open problem:** The lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a significant issue, and this paper directly tackles it.
* **Strong theoretical contribution:** The proof of convergence under -Hannan consistency is a substantial theoretical result.  The careful decomposition of the problem into repeated matrix games with bounded error is clever and well-executed.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general, allowing for instantiation with various regret-minimizing procedures.
* **Empirical validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the theoretical results align with practical performance, showing comparable convergence rates to existing algorithms.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, with clear definitions and a logical flow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Convergence Rate:** While convergence is proven, the rate of convergence is not explicitly characterized.  The theoretical bounds likely hide constants that could make the convergence slow in practice. The empirical results show convergence, but don’t provide a detailed analysis of the convergence *rate* as a function of game parameters.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on randomly generated games and a limited worst-case scenario. While a good starting point, more extensive evaluation on a diverse set of benchmark games (e.g., Goofspiel instances, more complex general game-playing games) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Assumptions:** The proof relies on the assumption of -Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration.  While these are standard assumptions for online learning, they are important to acknowledge and understand the implications if they are not fully met.
* **Incremental Tree Building Disregarded:** The simplification of disregarding the incremental tree building process in the proof, while justifiable for analytical convenience, feels slightly hand-wavy. It's not clear how significant this simplification is in practice.



**Addressing Review Criteria:**

* **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a rigorous proof and well-supported claims. The analysis is thorough, and the authors acknowledge limitations. It is a complete piece of work that provides a valuable theoretical contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The definitions are clear, and the logical flow is good. However, the mathematical notation could benefit from more explanation at certain points for readers less familiar with online learning theory.
* **Originality:** The work is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first theoretical analysis guaranteeing convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.  The approach of leveraging -Hannan consistency is novel and insightful. The related work section adequately references existing literature.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to be highly significant. It provides a theoretical foundation for the widespread use of MCTS in this class of games, opening doors for more principled algorithm design and analysis.  The results are likely to be valuable to both researchers in game theory and AI, and practitioners developing game-playing agents.




**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*  Fills a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS.
*  Provides a strong theoretical guarantee of convergence.
*  Is well-written and clearly presented.
*  Empirical results support the theoretical findings.

**Con:**

*  Convergence rate is not explicitly characterized.
*  Experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*  Simplification of disregarding incremental tree building.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. While the convergence rate remains an open question, the theoretical guarantees provided are valuable, and the empirical validation is encouraging.  The strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

Specifically, I recommend the authors:

*  Discuss the limitations of the convergence rate and potential factors influencing it.
*  Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of benchmark games.
* Briefly comment in the paper regarding the impact of disregarding the incremental tree building in the proof.
