This paper presents a convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a setting where theoretical guarantees are lacking despite practical success. The authors establish convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under the assumption of using an -Hannan consistent action selection procedure within the MCTS framework. They support this with empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3.

The primary strength of this work lies in its attempt to bridge a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS. The extension of MCTS analysis beyond sequential games is a valuable contribution. The formal proof, building from Hannan consistency and progressing through repeated matrix games with bounded error, is a substantial undertaking. The explicit connection to existing regret minimization algorithms like Regret Matching and Exp3 is also a positive aspect.

However, despite the commendable effort, I have several concerns. The most significant is the chasm between the theoretical contribution and its practical implications. The convergence guarantees, while mathematically sound, appear highly conservative. The convergence rate is dictated by the depth of the game tree (D), leading to a (D^2 + δ) approximation, which renders the result less compelling for deep or complex games. While the authors acknowledge this, it significantly diminishes the practical relevance of the theorem. 

Furthermore, the analysis relies on several simplifying assumptions. The conversion to repeated matrix games with bounded error, while a standard technique, introduces an error term (η) that directly impacts the quality of the approximation. The paper doesn't fully address how this error scales with the complexity of the game and the number of iterations. 

The empirical section, while helpful, feels somewhat limited. The comparison between propagating the mean vs. the current sample appears nuanced but not definitively resolved. More critically, the experiments are conducted on relatively small, randomly generated games, and a manually constructed "worst case" scenario. Demonstrating convergence on more realistic, larger-scale problems would substantially bolster the claims. The observation that RM and Exp3 perform similarly in these experiments isn't surprising given the limited scale. 

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of computational cost. While MCTS is known for its efficiency in many domains, the need for regret tracking and the potential for slow convergence due to the D^2 dependence raise concerns about scalability. A discussion of the computational overhead associated with -Hannan consistency would be valuable.

Finally, the citation of Shafiei et al. [17] showing UCB does not converge is good, but the paper doesn't thoroughly explain *why* the chosen regret-minimizing procedures do converge while UCB doesn’t. A more detailed comparison of the properties of different selection policies within the MCTS framework would strengthen the analysis.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Presents a rigorous, mathematically sound proof of convergence.
*   Connects the theoretical framework to well-established regret minimization algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The convergence rate (D^2 + δ) is likely too slow for practical applications in many games.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and scale.
*   The computational cost and scalability implications are not adequately addressed.
*   The practical relevance of the approximation guarantees is questionable.




**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **reject** with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. The theoretical contribution is valuable, but the current formulation and empirical validation are insufficient to justify acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to either significantly improve the convergence rate, demonstrate practical applicability on more challenging problems, or provide a more nuanced analysis of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and computational cost. The paper’s current state feels more like a first step towards a comprehensive theory rather than a definitive result.
