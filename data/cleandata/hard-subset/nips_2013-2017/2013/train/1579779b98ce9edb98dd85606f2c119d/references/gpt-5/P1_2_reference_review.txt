Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which the selection policy at each node can be any ε-Hannan consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3). The key technical result is an almost-sure convergence guarantee: when SM-MCTS uses mean-value backpropagation, it converges to a subgame-perfect ε·D^2 + δ Nash equilibrium (NE) in games of depth D, for any δ > 0, once the full tree has been explored. The proof builds up from (i) repeated matrix games: HC implies convergence of empirical strategies to an approximate NE; (ii) repeated matrix games with bounded observation error: HC still ensures convergence with a larger error term; and (iii) an induction over game depth, showing error accumulation across levels. Empirically, the paper compares two HC selectors (RM and Exp3), with and without mean backpropagation, on random games and small worst-case instances; all variants converge, with mean backpropagation slightly slower.

This contributes theoretical understanding for SM-MCTS, where prior practice often used UCT-like selection without convergence guarantees, and where UCT is known not to converge to NE in simultaneous-move settings (e.g., Shafiei et al.). The paper connects online learning (HC algorithms) and MCTS, in spirit similar to the well-known link between regret minimization and NE in normal-form/EFGs (e.g., Hart & Mas-Colell; CFR-style methods).

Quality:
- Theory: The structure of the argument is sound and well-motivated. The lemmas on HC in matrix games and their extension to bounded-error observations are standard in flavor and plausible; stacking them for depth-D games yields the main theorem. The requirement to propagate mean returns is clearly identified as necessary for the analysis. The assumption that the tree is eventually fully expanded under guaranteed exploration is reasonable, and the authors state a formal argument is in the appendix.
- Empirics: Experiments illustrate convergence trends, compare mean vs sample backprop, and include a small worst-case search for depth-2 games. They are consistent with the theory but limited in scale and scope; no finite-time rates are measured or compared.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The abstract algorithm and selection/update instantiations for RM and Exp3 are described concretely. A few suggestions:
- Be explicit about the “guaranteed exploration” variant used in Section 3 versus the A* exploration wrapper in Section 4 (on-policy mixing with updates vs. occasional uniform plays without updating). The two are not identical; clarify which is assumed in each result.
- The role of mean backpropagation: state early (e.g., in Section 3) that the convergence theorem requires RetVal = Xh/nh, and summarize why the sample return leads to unbounded bias accumulation.
- Tighten notation around σ̂ (empirical frequencies) vs σ̄ (average strategies) and emphasize which is used for evaluation in the experiments.

Originality:
To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE under mild conditions on the selector (ε-HC with exploration) and with a clear algorithmic template. Prior SM-MCTS work demonstrated strong empirical performance but lacked theory; UCT is known to fail to converge in simultaneous-move games. The extension to bounded-error repeated games and the depth-induction are novel in this context. Using RM/Exp3 as selectors inside MCTS is known, but the asymptotic NE convergence argument appears new.

Significance:
The result provides a principled foundation for SM-MCTS in a class of important games (Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion variants, GGP simultaneous-move games). The subgame-perfect NE guarantee is valuable theoretically. Practically, the reliance on mean backprop and the loose ε·D^2 bound limit immediate performance guarantees; no finite-time analysis is provided. Still, the work is likely to influence algorithm design and analysis in multi-agent search and multi-agent RL.

Strengths:
- Clear, general convergence theorem under broadly applicable assumptions.
- Unifying template tying online learning (HC) and MCTS in simultaneous-move settings.
- Careful decomposition: matrix games → bounded-error repeated games → depth induction.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory; comparison across selectors and backup choices.
- Subgame-perfect notion addressed, not only root value.

Weaknesses:
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time convergence rates or sample complexity bounds.
- The ε·D^2 scaling seems loose; discussion on tightness or possibilities for improving constants is limited.
- Mean backprop requirement differs from common MCTS practice; practical implications (variance, bias, efficiency) deserve deeper empirical study on larger benchmarks.
- Experiments are small-scale; no comparisons to strong baselines tailored to simultaneous moves (e.g., CFR/CFR+ adaptations, alpha-beta with pruning in simultaneous move games).
- Some implementation details of guaranteed exploration and expansion are deferred to the appendix; small inconsistencies in exploration treatment (A* vs on-policy mixing).

Questions for authors:
- Can you bound the rate at which node means converge sufficiently to control error accumulation, yielding an explicit finite-time bound (even if very loose)?
- Is the D^2 dependence tight? Would variance-reduced estimates, bootstrapping, or depth-dependent exploration improve it to O(D)?
- In practice, would backpropagating a suitable low-variance estimator (e.g., importance-weighted average with control variates) preserve the proof while mitigating the empirical slowdown?
- How sensitive are results to the exploration schedule γ (e.g., annealing γ to zero)?

Minor comments and suggestions:
- Clarify whether RM uses an explicit exploration mix (as in 3.1) or relies on A* in 4.2; align theory and experiments.
- Provide a brief proof sketch (main ideas) for the “eventual full expansion” claim in the main text.
- Report wall-clock comparisons for mean vs sample backprop to contextualize the convergence slowdown.
- Add a brief discussion comparing to CFR-type methods in simultaneous-move perfect-information games.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and general convergence guarantee for an important class of games.
- Solid theoretical contributions with clear assumptions and implications.
- Bridges two active areas (online learning and MCTS) in a principled way.
- Empirical results support the theory.

Cons for acceptance:
- No finite-time guarantees; asymptotic result with loose depth dependence.
- Practical impact tempered by requirement of mean backprop and small-scale experiments.
- Limited experimental comparison to alternative game-solving methods.

Recommendation: Weak accept. This paper advances theoretical understanding of SM-MCTS with a clean, broadly applicable result and reasonable empirical support. Strengthening the discussion of constants/finite-time behavior and clarifying exploration/backprop details would further improve the work.
