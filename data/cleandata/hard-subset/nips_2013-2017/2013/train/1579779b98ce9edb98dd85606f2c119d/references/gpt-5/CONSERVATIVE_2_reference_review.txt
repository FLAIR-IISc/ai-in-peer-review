Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses any -Hannan-consistent selection procedure (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) with guaranteed exploration. They prove almost sure asymptotic convergence of the induced average strategies to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with an error bound that scales with the game depth D (Theorem 4.11). The analysis proceeds by: (i) establishing that -Hannan consistency in repeated matrix games yields limiting play within 2 of value (and 4-NE for the empirical/average strategies), (ii) extending this to repeated games with bounded payoff errors, and (iii) inductively composing bounded-error subgames up the tree when mean returns are backed up. Empirically, they compare backing up means versus current samples for regret matching and Exp3 on random games and an exhaustive search over small worst cases, measuring exploitability; they observe convergence in both settings, with mean backups slightly slower.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear gap: theoretical convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games, where UCT is known not to converge.
- General template: the result covers any Hannan-consistent selection rule with guaranteed exploration, including regret matching and Exp3.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee, not merely root convergence.
- The proof structure (repeated games with bounded error and induction on depth) is conceptually clean.
- Empirical measurements report exploitability and show residual error consistent with exploration mixing.

Weaknesses
- Asymptotic only, with weak and depth-inflated constants. The bound in Theorem 4.11 scales as O(D^2) times the selection/regret and exploration terms; no finite-time/sample complexity guarantees are provided. This substantially limits practical significance for large trees.
- Critical proof assumptions are at odds with standard SM-MCTS practice. The convergence proof requires:
  - backing up mean returns rather than sampled returns (the latter being standard in MCTS), and
  - a modified exploration scheme A* in which forced-exploration rounds do not update the learner’s internal state.
  These modifications are plausible but nonstandard; the paper provides only empirical observations (not proofs) that standard backups still converge.
- The “repeated game with bounded error” assumption is nontrivial in the adaptive tree setting. Payoffs at an inner node depend on children whose policies and estimates evolve with time. The paper asserts eventual bounded errors via induction when backing up means, but the main text omits the delicate argument that the nonstationarity disappears almost surely and that each node’s effective matrix converges fast enough to make the regret guarantees applicable; all details are deferred to the appendix.
- Empirical evaluation is limited. Experiments are on small random games and handpicked worst cases, with up to 10^6 iterations, log–log plots, and no confidence intervals. There are no comparisons to established baselines for this class (e.g., backward induction with α–β pruning + LP/double oracle), nor to regret-minimization baselines such as CFR/CFR+ adapted to simultaneous moves. No wall-clock/time-normalized results, no study of sensitivity to γ or learning-rate choices, and no evaluations on standard benchmarks (Tron, Goofspiel, Oshi-Zumo variants) with known values.
- Some algorithmic details are unclear. Algorithm 1 maintains Xh and nh at nodes, but regret updates require per-joint-action statistics Xhij; this should be explicitly specified. The guarantee of “tij → ∞ almost surely for all joint actions” relies on per-player γ-exploration; please provide a formal argument that this is sufficient in the joint-action setting as used (and clarify whether exploration steps do or do not update node statistics Xh, nh).
- The role of persistent exploration and its impact on the limit is under-specified. In practice, the empirical residual exploitability equals the exploration floor, but the theorems subsume that into ε and c; an explicit connection of γ to the final bound would improve clarity.

Quality
Technically competent overall, but the main theoretical contribution relies on assumptions that deviate from standard SM-MCTS and lacks finite-time rates. Important proof details are deferred; the core bounded-error induction in an adaptive tree deserves more space in the main text. Empirical validation is too limited for NeurIPS standards and does not convincingly demonstrate practical benefits over strong baselines.

Clarity
Generally readable and organized, but several notational/algorithmic points need tightening:
- Precisely define what statistics are stored per node/joint action.
- Clearly specify when updates occur under exploration and how this interacts with Algorithm 1’s RetVal and Update steps.
- Make the constants in Theorem 4.11 explicit in terms of ε, γ, payoff range, and D, and reconcile with the observed residual exploitability due to γ.
There are incomplete references (missing years/pages), and some formatting issues (e.g., footnotes within math).

Originality
Moderate. The idea of using Hannan-consistent dynamics to approach NE in repeated play is classical; the contribution is adapting this to an MCTS template for simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs and proving subgame-perfect asymptotic convergence with mean backups. The claim of being the first formal analysis for this setting appears plausible, but please better contextualize relative to CFR in EFGs and prior analyses of MCTS convergence in turn-based games.

Significance
Theoretically interesting, but limited practical impact without finite-time/sample bounds, tighter constants, and stronger empirical validation versus established solvers. Given that exact backward induction is polynomial for this class, the main value would be for very large trees where exact methods are impractical; the present results do not yet provide actionable guidance there.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide finite-time bounds (even very loose), or at least a dependency on mixing γ and depth D that matches observed rates?
- Does your analysis extend to sample-backup SM-MCTS, or can you exhibit a counterexample?
- Precisely define and analyze how γ appears in c and the final error bound in Theorem 4.11.
- Clarify the data structures: are Xhij maintained, and do exploration steps update X/nh or not?
- Please compare against strong baselines (α–β with LP/double-oracle cutoffs, and CFR variants) on standard benchmarks with wall-clock normalization.

Pros
- Addresses an open theoretical question for SM-MCTS.
- General template covering RM and Exp3.
- Subgame-perfect asymptotic guarantee.

Cons
- Only asymptotic, depth-inflated bounds; no finite-time rates.
- Requires nonstandard mean backups and modified exploration.
- Key proof details deferred; bounded-error induction not convincing in main text.
- Weak empirical validation; no comparisons to strong baselines.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical idea is interesting, but the reliance on nonstandard assumptions, lack of finite-time analysis, and limited experiments leave the contribution below the bar for NeurIPS. Strengthening the proofs in the main text, adding finite-time bounds, and substantially improving empirical validation against established baselines would change this assessment.
