This paper addresses an important theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first convergence proof for its application in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree selection policy is instantiated by a regret-minimizing procedure. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the resulting MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash Equilibrium. The paper connects concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency, regret matching, Exp3) to the MCTS framework. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on random and worst-case games, evaluating instantiations of the framework with Regret Matching and Exp3.

This work builds upon a line of research that has successfully applied MCTS to simultaneous move games empirically (e.g., Finnsson & Björnsson, 2008; Lanctot et al., 2012) but without formal guarantees. It is particularly relevant given prior work showing that naive extensions of standard MCTS selection policies like UCB fail to converge to a Nash Equilibrium even in simple one-stage games (Shafiei et al., 2012). This paper provides the missing theoretical foundation, justifying the use of regret-based selection policies.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. To my knowledge, this is the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this class of games. This is a significant contribution that bridges the gap between the practical success of MCTS variants and the theoretical guarantees desired by the algorithmic game theory community. It establishes a solid theoretical underpinning for a class of algorithms that were previously only justified by empirical performance.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and the proof structure is elegant. The argument proceeds inductively from simple repeated matrix games (Section 4.2), to games with bounded error in payoffs (Section 4.3), and finally to the full multi-stage extensive-form game (Section 4.4). This approach of treating the value estimates from child nodes as noisy payoffs for the parent node's matrix game is insightful and powerful.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and well-structured. It clearly defines the problem setting, presents the general SM-MCTS algorithm, and then systematically builds the theoretical argument. The separation of the analysis into distinct lemmas for matrix games, games with error, and the final inductive step makes the complex proof comprehensible.

4.  **Completeness:** The work is a complete research contribution, combining a novel theoretical result with a targeted empirical analysis that validates and explores the implications of the theory. The experiments directly address a key assumption of the proof (propagating mean values) and investigate the practical convergence rate on difficult, "worst-case" instances.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Convergence Rate and Practical Implications:** The main limitation of the theoretical result is the lack of a finite-time convergence rate. The proof guarantees *eventual* convergence, but the experiments suggest this can be very slow (e.g., a million iterations on small games). The ε-bound in the main theorem (Theorem 4.11) depends on D^2, which might be quite loose and suggests poor scaling with game depth. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit discussion of this limitation. While a full rate analysis is likely beyond the scope of a single paper, acknowledging the slow convergence and the looseness of the bound is important for managing expectations about the algorithm's practical applicability.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** A key empirical finding is that propagating the single sample's return (RM) converges slightly faster than propagating the mean of returns (RMM), which is the variant required by the proof. The authors conjecture that the analysis could be extended to the single-sample case but provide no intuition as to why this might be true or what the technical challenges would be. This is a crucial point. The proof for RMM relies on the law of large numbers to ensure the estimated payoffs converge to their true expected values. This argument does not hold for the single-sample case, where the returns are high-variance, non-stationary estimates. A deeper discussion of this disconnect would significantly improve the paper, perhaps by outlining the challenges in analyzing the single-sample variant (e.g., dealing with correlated, biased estimates).

3.  **Clarity of the Proof's Intuition:** While the proof structure is sound, the intuition behind the D^2 error accumulation in Theorem 4.11 could be made more explicit in the main text. A sentence explaining that the error from one level of the tree (bounded by Lemma 4.9) becomes the input error `c` for the level above, and that these errors accumulate up the `D` levels of the tree, would help the reader grasp the origin of the final bound without needing to delve into the appendix.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:**
    *   The "M" suffix for the mean-propagating variants (RMM, Exp3M) is introduced somewhat late, in the experimental section. It would be helpful to define this variant more formally when Algorithm 1 is presented, perhaps by explicitly defining the two versions of the `RetVal` function.
    *   In the RM update rule (Section 3.1), the definition of `x(h, i, j)` is slightly confusing. It seems to imply re-using old estimates `Xhij` for all actions except the one sampled. A more standard approach in regret matching would be to construct an estimate of the full utility vector for player 1 (e.g., `u_vector[i'] = X_{hi'j}/n_{hi'j}`) and compute regret against that. Clarifying this update would be beneficial.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a strong and important paper that makes a foundational theoretical contribution to the MCTS literature. The originality and significance of providing the first convergence proof for SM-MCTS outweigh the identified weaknesses. The weaknesses primarily relate to the practical implications (slow convergence) and a disconnect between the proven algorithm and its slightly superior practical variant, which are excellent avenues for future work. The paper is well-argued, technically sound, and a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a significant theoretical milestone.
*   Elegant and sound proof technique connecting online learning theory with MCTS.
*   Solid experimental validation that explores the theory's assumptions and limitations.
*   Clearly written and well-structured.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis lacks convergence rates, and empirical convergence is shown to be slow.
*   The proven algorithm (using mean propagation) is empirically slightly inferior to the standard variant (using single-sample propagation), which remains unproven.

I recommend **acceptance**. The contribution is significant enough that it will likely become a cornerstone for future theoretical analysis of MCTS in more complex game settings. The weaknesses, while notable, do not undermine the core contribution and can be addressed in the camera-ready version and future research.
