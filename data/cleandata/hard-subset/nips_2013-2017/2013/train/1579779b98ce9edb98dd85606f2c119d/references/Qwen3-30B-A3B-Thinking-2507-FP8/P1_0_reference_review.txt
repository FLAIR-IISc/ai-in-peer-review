

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function, it will converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves, which is a notable advancement over the current state of the art (where most convergence results apply only to sequential games).

The paper builds upon prior work in game theory, particularly Hannan consistency and regret minimization algorithms (like regret matching and Exp3), and connects these concepts to MCTS. The authors provide a rigorous formal analysis with several key lemmas and theorems, supported by empirical evaluations on randomly generated games. The work is well-positioned in the context of recent NeurIPS papers on game theory and MCTS, particularly those addressing general game-playing and reinforcement learning for games.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the literature. While MCTS has been widely used in simultaneous move games (e.g., in Goofspiel, Tron), no formal convergence guarantees existed for these settings. This is a substantial contribution to the theoretical foundations of reinforcement learning and game theory.

2. **Rigorous Formal Analysis**: The paper provides a well-structured and rigorous proof of convergence. The authors carefully define the necessary concepts (ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration, etc.), build up the proof inductively, and provide a clear connection between regret minimization and Nash equilibrium convergence. The proofs in the appendix are thorough and well-motivated.

3. **Clear Connection to Prior Work**: The authors properly contextualize their work against existing literature, particularly noting the lack of convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games compared to sequential games. They correctly cite relevant prior work on regret matching, Exp3, and MCTS.

4. **Appropriate Empirical Validation**: The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims by comparing the convergence behavior of the proposed algorithms (with mean propagation) against variants without mean propagation. The experiments on worst-case games provide useful insights into the practical performance of the algorithms.

5. **Well-Structured and Clear Writing**: The paper is logically organized, with clear definitions, theorems, and proofs. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with game theory and online learning.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Finite-Time Convergence Bounds**: The paper provides asymptotic convergence guarantees but does not offer finite-time convergence rates or bounds. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, as users of MCTS need to know how many iterations are required to reach a desired level of accuracy. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion but do not address it in the main paper.

2. **Limited Empirical Scope**: The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on small games (depth 2 with branching factor 2). While this is sufficient for the theoretical claims, it doesn't demonstrate how the algorithm performs on larger, more complex games where MCTS is typically applied. The authors should provide more evidence of scalability.

3. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications**: The paper doesn't discuss how the theoretical results translate to practical implementation challenges. For example, the paper doesn't address how the choice of exploration parameter γ affects performance, or how the algorithm might be adapted for real-world applications.

4. **No Extension to Non-Zero-Sum Games**: The paper is limited to zero-sum games, which is a significant restriction since many practical applications involve non-zero-sum games. The authors should discuss the challenges of extending their results to non-zero-sum settings.

5. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Algorithms**: While the paper mentions that their convergence rate is comparable to recent MCTS algorithms, it doesn't provide a direct comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods for simultaneous move games. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the empirical evaluation.

## Specific Comments

1. **Theorem 4.11**: The proof of this main theorem is elegant and well-structured, but the bound of D² + δ is quite loose (where D is the maximum depth of the game). The authors should discuss whether this bound is tight or if it can be improved.

2. **Section 5.1**: The claim that "RMM converges slightly slower in all cases" is interesting, but the paper doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for this phenomenon. It would be valuable to discuss why the mean propagation variant converges more slowly in practice despite the theoretical guarantees.

3. **Figure 3**: The empirical worst-case games shown in Figure 3 are interesting, but the paper doesn't provide enough detail about why these particular games are difficult. A more thorough analysis of the game structure that leads to slow convergence would be helpful.

4. **Section 6**: The conclusion mentions future work on finite-time convergence bounds, but the paper itself should at least acknowledge the importance of this direction more prominently in the main text.

5. **Related Work**: The paper could better distinguish between their work and recent advances in MCTS for simultaneous move games. For example, the paper by Shafiei et al. (cited as [17]) shows that UCT does not converge to Nash equilibrium, but it's not clear how the authors' work relates to other MCTS variants that have been proposed for simultaneous move games.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear advancement in the understanding of MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened with more comprehensive experiments on larger games, the theoretical results are significant and well-supported.

The paper is clearly within the scope of NeurIPS, as it makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical foundations of reinforcement learning and game theory, which are core topics for the conference.

## Recommendation

**Accept** (Strong Accept)

This paper makes a clear and important theoretical contribution to the field of game theory and reinforcement learning. The formal analysis is rigorous, the empirical results support the theoretical claims, and the work addresses a significant gap in the literature. While there are some limitations in the empirical evaluation and the lack of finite-time bounds, these do not detract from the substantial theoretical contribution of the paper.

The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. Given the widespread use of MCTS in practical applications, this theoretical foundation is highly valuable and will likely influence future research in the area.

The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS, as it makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of algorithms that are widely used in practice. The work is original, significant, and well-executed.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-structured proofs
- Clear connection to prior work in regret minimization and game theory
- Empirical results supporting theoretical claims
- Well-written and well-organized paper

**Arguments for Rejection (Minor):**
- Lack of finite-time convergence bounds
- Limited empirical evaluation on larger games
- No extension to non-zero-sum games
- Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a valuable contribution to the field.
