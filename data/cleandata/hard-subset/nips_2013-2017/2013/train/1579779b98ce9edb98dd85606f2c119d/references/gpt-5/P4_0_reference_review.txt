Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games (EFGs) with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which, at each node, action selection uses any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) online learning algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching (RM), Exp3). The main theoretical result shows almost-sure eventual convergence of SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE): when selection is ε–HC and the backup propagates the mean return, the average strategies form an (ε·D^2 + δ)-NE for any δ > 0, where D is the tree depth. The proof builds on (i) convergence of HC procedures in repeated matrix games, (ii) an extension to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors, and (iii) an induction over tree depth to control error accumulation.

Empirically, the paper compares RM and Exp3 with and without mean-value backup on random games and on brute-force-searched “difficult” depth-2, BF=2 instances. All variants appear to converge; propagating means slows convergence slightly; worst-case convergence appears similar across RM/Exp3.

The work is positioned against: exact backward induction for simultaneous moves (with LP/DO-based pruning), practical SM-MCTS variants applied in Tron/Urban Rivals/general game-playing, and known non-convergence of UCB/UCT in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). It leverages standard HC algorithms (RM, Exp3) and relates to prior observations that HC dynamics approach equilibria in matrix games.

Quality
- Correctness and assumptions: The reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated matrix games with bounded errors is standard and the induction over depth is plausible. The conditions (ε–HC selection, guaranteed exploration, and mean backup) are clearly stated, and the asymptotic “almost sure” guarantees are appropriate for bandit-style learning dynamics. The extension to games with bounded payoff errors and the resulting factor increases (from ε to 4(c+1)ε in matrix games and then to ε·D^2 in trees) are logically consistent, albeit loose.
- Gaps/clarifications needed:
  - Algorithmic specification: Algorithm 1 stores per-node statistics (Xh, nh), but the regret-matching update later refers to Xhij (per joint action). This mismatch obscures how regrets are computed (instantaneous vs averaged values) and how estimates for counterfactual joint actions are obtained. Clarifying what statistics are maintained per edge/joint action, and whether x(h,i′,j) denotes a mean or cumulative value, would improve reproducibility.
  - Exploration: The text states RM and Exp3 “guarantee exploration,” but standard RM without explicit perturbation may fail to visit all actions infinitely often. The A* modification (with γ-uniform exploration) is introduced; it would help to state precisely which selection rules satisfy Definition 4.2 without modification and otherwise to consistently assume A* throughout.
  - Mean vs sample backup: The proof requires mean backup, whereas prior SM-MCTS often backs up sample returns. The experiments suggest both variants converge, but only the mean-backup variant is analyzed. A brief discussion of why sample backup might also work (e.g., as an unbiased martingale difference) or what breaks in the proof would be useful.
  - Finite-time behavior: No finite-sample convergence rates are provided. While asymptotic results are valuable, even rough dependencies on D, branching factor, γ, and ε for sample complexity would significantly strengthen the contribution.
- Empirics: The experiments are clean and directly target the theoretical claims (effect of mean backup; worst-case small games). However, they are limited in scope (small depths/branching factors, discretized payoffs) and do not compare against strong baselines (e.g., alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning, double-oracle, or domain-specific SM-MCTS variants) on standard benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel instances, Tron).

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized and readable, with a clear statement of the model, algorithm template, and proof roadmap. The notational distinction between empirical frequencies and time-averaged mixed strategies is helpful; Lemma 4.5 bridges them.
- Minor issues:
  - Notational inconsistencies: σ̂ vs σ̄ usage and in Theorem 4.11 “average strategies (σ̂1, σ̂2)” likely a typo—please standardize.
  - Algorithm details: clarify data structures (per-node vs per-edge statistics) and the exact form of x(h,i′,j) in regret updates.
  - Some references in the bibliography lack complete metadata (years/pages).
  - Provide more explicit pseudo-code for the selection/update steps for RM/Exp3 variants used in trees, including the γ-exploration mechanism.

Originality
- To the best of the provided context, this appears to be the first formal convergence analysis of MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The idea of plugging ε–HC algorithms at nodes and proving subgame-perfect convergence via bounded-error repeated games and induction is novel for this setting.
- The required mean backup and exploration modification are simple but important design choices that distinguish the provably convergent template from common practice.

Significance
- The result gives theoretical grounding for a widely used class of algorithms (SM-MCTS) in an important subclass of EFGs. This can inform algorithm design (e.g., adding explicit exploration, preferring mean backups when convergence is desired).
- Limitations reduce immediate practical impact: asymptotic-only guarantees, loose D^2 dependence, and deviation from common backup practice. Nonetheless, the framework is a solid step that can potentially be extended to tighter bounds, alternative backups, and broader game classes.

Pros
- Clear, general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under ε–HC selection with exploration.
- Subgame-perfect convergence; inductive proof over depth is conceptually clean.
- Empirical support demonstrating convergence behavior and the effect of mean backup.
- Bridges online learning (HC algorithms) with game-tree search in simultaneous-move settings.

Cons
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time rates. The D^2 factor and constants (e.g., 4(c+1)) are likely loose.
- Proof requires mean backup; common sample-backup variant is unproven.
- Algorithmic details (statistics maintained, regret updates) need clarification for reproducibility.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and baselines; missing standard benchmarks.
- Exploration guarantees for RM are not fully justified unless A* is consistently applied.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the maintained statistics (per-node vs per-joint-action), the exact regret update formulas, and resolve notation inconsistencies.
- State explicitly whether the selection rule includes enforced exploration (A*) in all theoretical results and experiments.
- Add a discussion (or preliminary lemma) on whether sample backups can be analyzed, possibly via concentration/martingale tools, or provide a counterexample if not.
- Provide finite-time convergence bounds (even if conservative) as a function of D, branching factor, ε, and γ.
- Expand experiments to include: (i) larger depths/branching factors; (ii) standard benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron); (iii) comparisons to alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning and double-oracle; (iv) sensitivity to γ; (v) exploitability with respect to both players.
- Complete and standardize references.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games, with clear potential to influence subsequent theoretical and empirical work. The main opportunities are tightening the theory, aligning it more closely with practical variants, and strengthening the experimental validation.
