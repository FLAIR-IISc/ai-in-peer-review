Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games (“stacked matrix games”). The main contribution is a proof template showing that if the node-level action selection algorithm is ε–Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3 with mixing), and if returns are backed up as running means, then SM-MCTS almost surely converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis proceeds by (i) recalling properties of Hannan-consistent learners in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) inducting over game-tree depth to bound deviation incentives at each level, yielding a bound of the form O(D·ε) (notation in Theorem 4.11 is a bit unclear; see comment). Experiments compare regret-matching and Exp3 variants, with and without mean backups, on random and small worst-case games, showing convergence, with mean backups converging slightly slower.

Relation to prior work: Earlier empirical SM-MCTS work used UCB-style selection but lacked convergence guarantees and can fail to reach NE (e.g., Shafiei et al.). The present paper is positioned as the first convergence result for SM-MCTS in this setting, leveraging standard no-regret algorithms (Hart & Mas-Colell; Exp3). Exact backward induction for simultaneous moves is known but requires LP/double-oracle at cutoffs; the value here is an asymptotic guarantee for simulation-based search without domain evaluators.

Strengths
- Clear, modular theoretical argument building on well-known no-regret results, extended to handle bounded payoff errors and composed over tree depth.
- Identification of a precise condition (mean backup and guaranteed exploration) under which SM-MCTS converges to an approximate, subgame-perfect NE.
- Empirical results are consistent with the theory and include both random games and a (limited) worst-case search; comparison of regret matching and Exp3 is useful.
- The work clarifies why commonly used last-sample backups complicate analysis, motivating the mean-backup requirement.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Practical relevance: The key convergence result relies on returning mean estimates rather than the latest sampled return. This deviates from standard MCTS practice for efficiency and often for performance. Experiments show mean backups converge slower. The theory does not cover the commonly used variant, and the conjecture that it also converges remains unproven here.
- Asymptotic only: No finite-time convergence rates or sample-complexity bounds are provided. The guarantees hinge on visiting every joint action infinitely often and building the complete tree—conditions that are unrealistic in large games and do not guide practical parameter choices (γ, rollout length, etc.).
- Bound clarity and constants: The final approximation bound (Theorem 4.11) is not presented with explicit constants or dependence on ε, branching factors, and exploration bias. The notation “(D2 + δ)–NE” appears to be missing ε (or a scaling factor), and earlier results contain terms like 4(c+1) without binding c to algorithmic parameters (e.g., γ). The lack of explicit constant propagation diminishes interpretability.
- Assumptions: The “repeated game with error η” assumes almost-sure uniform closeness of observed payoffs to a fixed matrix after some t0. In the SM-MCTS context, this relies on mean backups and stationary rollouts, but the paper does not make explicit the conditions under which the induced subgame payoffs are stationary when higher-level policies evolve. A rigorous argument (e.g., a uniform almost-sure convergence over all child joint actions via SLLN and guaranteed exploration) is only sketched.
- Experimental scope: The empirical analysis is limited to very small games (depth ≤ 2 for worst-case search) and random utilities in [0,1]. There is no comparison to exact backward induction with pruning or to other SM-MCTS variants reported in practice (beyond RM/Exp3). No scaling results, no sensitivity to branching factor beyond small settings, and no evaluation on established simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Tron) within the new framework.
- Missing practical features: Common MCTS techniques like progressive widening, non-uniform rollouts, or function approximation are not addressed; thus the gap between theory and practice remains large.
- Presentation issues: Some notation is imprecise or ambiguous (e.g., Theorem 4.11 constant; formatting of Algorithm 1; typos). Key proofs are deferred to an appendix on arXiv; the main text could do more to state precise assumptions and constants.

Quality
Technically, the high-level argument is sound and leverages established no-regret machinery, but several steps are only outlined. The central novelty is the composition over depth and the handling of payoff errors via mean backups; however, the lack of explicit constants and finite-time bounds limits the scientific utility. The experiments provide a sanity check rather than a rigorous validation.

Clarity
The paper is generally readable, but important details are deferred. The role of mean backups vs. last-sample backups should be highlighted earlier, and Theorem 4.11’s statement clarified with explicit dependence on ε and c(γ). Some typographical issues and algorithmic descriptions (e.g., updates using Xhij) could be tightened.

Originality
Moderate. Using Hannan-consistent selection at nodes is standard; showing asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS under mean backups and guaranteed exploration appears to be new in this precise form for simultaneous-move perfect-information games.

Significance
Limited to moderate. Theoretical reassurance for a narrow class of games and a particular MCTS variant is valuable, but the absence of rates, the reliance on mean backups, and the asymptotic nature restrict practical impact. Given that exact backward induction is feasible (albeit costly) in this class, the incremental value is mostly conceptual.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify Theorem 4.11: what is the exact approximation constant as a function of depth D, ε, exploration γ, and payoff range? Please correct the notation and provide an explicit bound.
- Can you bound c in Lemma 4.9 in terms of γ and depth? This would make the result more interpretable.
- Is there any partial progress toward finite-time bounds (e.g., O(1/√T) rates inherited from RM/Exp3 with additional depth factors)?
- Can you formalize the stationarity assumptions required for “repeated game with error” in the tree context, given evolving policies?
- Provide experiments on larger, structured games and compare to exact backward induction (with alpha-beta pruning) and other practical SM-MCTS variants. Include wall-clock time and sample complexity.
- Discuss whether progressive widening or decaying γ could be incorporated without breaking the proof, and what would be required to extend the analysis to last-sample backups.

Pros
- First convergence analysis for SM-MCTS under clear no-regret conditions.
- Clean conceptual bridge between online learning and MCTS in simultaneous-move settings.
- Empirical trends align with theory.

Cons
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time or scalable guarantees.
- Requires mean backups and full-tree exploration; limited practical relevance.
- Ambiguous constants and missing derivations for key error terms.
- Limited and small-scale experiments; no strong baselines.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper makes a conceptually reasonable but asymptotic contribution with limited practical relevance and insufficient empirical validation for NeurIPS standards. Strengthening the theory with explicit bounds/rates and broadening/grounding the experiments would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Reviewer confidence: High.
