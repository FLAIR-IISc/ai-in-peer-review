This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The authors establish a general template for SM-MCTS, leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection mechanisms. Their key theoretical result demonstrates that, under the assumption of -Hannan consistency, the proposed MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The analysis is complemented by empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3, showing convergence speeds comparable to existing approaches.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, which, despite its practical success, lacks rigorous convergence guarantees beyond two-player perfect-information sequential games. The extension to simultaneous-move games is a natural and important step, particularly given the relevance of these games in domains like security and general game-playing. The framework of using regret-minimizing procedures is a clever and principled approach, grounding the analysis in well-established online learning theory. The empirical validation, although not extensive, provides some confidence in the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper’s primary strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The convergence proof is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a formal guarantee for a class of games where such guarantees were previously lacking.
*   **General Framework:** The proposed MCTS template is generic, allowing for the integration of various regret-minimizing procedures. This flexibility increases the applicability of the results.
*   **Connection to Online Learning:** Grounding the analysis in -Hannan consistency leverages established theory from online learning, providing a solid foundation for the convergence proof.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The definitions and notations are well-defined, facilitating understanding.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of empirical results, even if limited, strengthens the paper by demonstrating the practical viability of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The reliance on -Hannan consistency is a strong assumption. While justified theoretically, it might limit the practical applicability of the algorithm, as achieving this consistency in complex games can be challenging. The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion on the practical implications would be beneficial.
*   **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical results only establish convergence *to* an -Nash equilibrium, without providing any bounds on the *rate* of convergence.  This is a common limitation in theoretical analyses of MCTS, but it’s a significant point, especially for practical applications. The empirical results offer some insights, but a more thorough investigation of the convergence rate would be valuable.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is relatively limited in scope. The experiments are primarily conducted on randomly generated games and a limited set of worst-case scenarios.  Testing on more diverse and realistic game instances would strengthen the empirical validation.
*   **Discussion of Existing Work:** The paper mentions UCB and notes its lack of convergence guarantees. However, a more detailed discussion of related work in SM-MCTS, beyond simply citing existing algorithms, would be helpful.  For instance, discussing the differences in the approach compared to other algorithms like those in [11, 12, 20] would add value.
*   **Numerical Stability:** The paper acknowledges the potential for numerical overflows when using Exp3 and suggests a workaround. This indicates a potential practical issue that could affect the implementation and performance of the algorithm. A more in-depth discussion of this issue and other potential implementation challenges would be useful.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   The results are mathematically sound and well-supported by the analysis.
*   The framework is general and adaptable to different regret-minimizing procedures.
*   The paper addresses an important gap in the existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The strong assumption of -Hannan consistency might limit the practical applicability of the algorithm.
*   The lack of convergence rate bounds is a significant limitation.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and could benefit from more extensive testing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of game-playing and reinforcement learning. While the assumptions and limitations discussed above prevent a highly enthusiastic recommendation, the paper's rigor, novelty, and clarity justify acceptance. 

**Recommendations for Improvement:**

*   Provide a more in-depth discussion of the practical challenges of achieving -Hannan consistency in complex games.
*   Investigate potential techniques for deriving convergence rate bounds, even if approximate.
*   Expand the empirical evaluation to include a more diverse set of game instances.
*   Provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed approach with existing SM-MCTS algorithms.
*   Further elaborate on potential implementation challenges, such as numerical stability.



**Rating:** Accept with minor revisions.
