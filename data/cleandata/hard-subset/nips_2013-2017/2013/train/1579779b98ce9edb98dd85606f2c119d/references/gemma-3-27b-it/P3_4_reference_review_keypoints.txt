- Main contribution: Proves convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function to reach a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.  
- Method: Presents a general MCTS template that uses regret-minimizing procedures (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3) for action selection.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments show Regret Matching and Exp3 converge in practice with speeds comparable to existing algorithms.  
- Positioning: Correctly situates work relative to MCTS, regret minimization literature, and prior limits of UCB in simultaneous-move settings.  
- Strength — theory: Strong theoretical advance providing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.  
- Strength — framework: General template allows instantiation with different regret-minimizing algorithms, broadening applicability.  
- Strength — clarity: Well-written, rigorous definitions and notation, with a clear algorithmic description.  
- Strength — experiments: Empirical results, though limited, support the theoretical claims and comparisons to recent MCTS variants.  
- Strength — transparency: Authors openly acknowledge limitations and conjectures about extending analysis.  
- Weakness — rate: Paper proves convergence but gives little useful insight or tight bounds on convergence rates (bound weak, proportional to tree depth).  
- Weakness — practical implications: Reliance on ε-Hannan consistency may harm practical performance; trade-offs between theory and exploration not fully addressed.  
- Weakness — empirical scope: Experiments limited (random games, only 100 runs for worst-case), lacking stronger baselines and broader game suites.  
- Weakness — exploration analysis: Insufficient investigation of the exploration parameter γ and its interaction with Hannan consistency and convergence speed.  
- Weakness — notation: Dense notation in places makes some proof details hard to follow.  
- For acceptance: Addresses a significant open problem with a general, rigorous framework and supporting experiments.  
- Against acceptance: Missing convergence-rate analysis, limited empirical rigor, and unclear practical relevance of strict Hannan consistency.  
- Overall: Strong theoretical contribution that outweighs empirical and rate-analysis weaknesses; recommended for acceptance.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Reviewer confidence: 8/10.
