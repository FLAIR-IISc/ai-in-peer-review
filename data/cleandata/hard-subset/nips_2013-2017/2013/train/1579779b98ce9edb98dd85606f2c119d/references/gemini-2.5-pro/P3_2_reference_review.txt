**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first convergence proof for its application in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection policy is based on a regret-minimizing procedure. The core theoretical contribution is proving that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (like Regret Matching or Exp3 with an exploration component), the algorithm's average strategies converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash Equilibrium. The proof proceeds inductively from the leaves of the game tree upwards. At the leaves (single matrix games), ε-Hannan consistency is known to lead to approximate Nash equilibria. For nodes higher up the tree, the values from child nodes are treated as noisy payoffs. The authors show that ε-Hannan consistency is robust to this bounded error, and the error in the resulting equilibrium accumulates as one moves up the tree. The theoretical analysis requires a variant of MCTS that backpropagates the running mean of rewards rather than the single sample's outcome. The paper complements this theory with experiments comparing this "mean-propagating" variant with the standard "sample-propagating" one on random and worst-case games, showing that both converge empirically, though the standard variant appears slightly faster.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The paper provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this important class of games. While MCTS has been used heuristically in such settings (e.g., general game playing), and regret minimization is the cornerstone of algorithms like CFR for imperfect information games, this work formally bridges the gap for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This is a foundational result that legitimizes the use of MCTS as a principled approximation algorithm in this domain.

2.  **Generality of the Framework:** The theoretical result is not tied to a specific algorithm like Regret Matching. Instead, it relies on the abstract property of ε-Hannan consistency. This provides a clear and powerful recipe for designing provably convergent MCTS algorithms for this game class: simply plug in any online learning algorithm with this property as the node selection policy.

3.  **Sound and Elegant Proof Structure:** The theoretical analysis is well-constructed. The idea of modeling the problem inductively, where parent nodes play a matrix game with noisy payoffs (the estimated values of the child nodes), is insightful. The formalization of this via "repeated matrix games with bounded error" (Section 4.3) is the key technical step that makes the induction work and is a valuable contribution in its own right.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Assumption of a Complete Tree:** The formal analysis makes a crucial simplifying assumption: "we disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree." While the paper argues this eventually happens with probability 1, this sidesteps the challenging non-stationarity of the learning problem during the tree-building phase. In the early stages of MCTS, value estimates are based on very few samples and can be highly biased. The analysis does not account for how these initial, potentially poor estimates might affect the long-term convergence trajectory. This is a significant gap between the analyzed algorithm and MCTS as it is actually implemented.

2.  **Clarity on Key Details:**
    *   The description of the Regret Matching update in Section 3.1 is terse. The definition of `x(h, i, j)` and its use in the update (`rh[i'] ← rh[i'] + (x(h, i', j) - u1)`) feels like a form of counterfactual regret, but this is not made explicit. Clarifying which player's action `j` is used and why this specific form of update is chosen would improve reproducibility and understanding.
    *   The derivation of the final ε-NE bound in Theorem 4.11, which appears to have a quadratic dependency on the depth D (`εD^2`), is relegated to the appendix. Providing more intuition in the main text for why the error accumulates quadratically (if that is indeed the case) rather than linearly would strengthen the paper.

3.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The experiments reveal that the theoretically justified variant (RMM, propagating the mean) converges slightly slower than the standard variant (RM, propagating the current sample). The paper conjectures that the standard variant also converges but provides no proof. This creates a disconnect where the proven algorithm is not the one practitioners would likely choose. While proving convergence for the standard variant is likely much harder due to the higher variance in the backpropagated values, this limitation should be discussed more prominently.

4.  **Lack of Convergence Rate Analysis:** The analysis is asymptotic, proving that the algorithm *eventually* converges. While this is a crucial first step, it provides no finite-time bounds on the convergence rate. For practical applications, understanding how the approximation error (exploitability) decreases with the number of simulations is paramount. The empirical analysis hints at the rate, but a theoretical treatment is missing.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first, much-needed convergence proof for MCTS in a wide and important class of games. This is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The general framework based on ε-Hannan consistency is elegant and offers a principled guide for future algorithm design.
*   The paper is well-structured and the core ideas are communicated effectively, despite some dense technical sections.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The assumption of a fully expanded tree for the analysis is a major simplification that ignores the exploration/exploitation dynamics during tree growth.
*   The disconnect between the proven algorithm and the better-performing practical variant weakens the immediate practical impact of the theory.

**Recommendation:**

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and theoretical significance, strongly outweigh its weaknesses. The contribution of providing the first convergence guarantee is substantial enough to merit publication at NIPS. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations that point towards important avenues for future work, rather than fatal flaws in the current contribution. I recommend **acceptance**. The authors should be encouraged to address the clarity issues in the final version and to be more explicit about the limitations of their analysis, especially regarding the full-tree assumption.
