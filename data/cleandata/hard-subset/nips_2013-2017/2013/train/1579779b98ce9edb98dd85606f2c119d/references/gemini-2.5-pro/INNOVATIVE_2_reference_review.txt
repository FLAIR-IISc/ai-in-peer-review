### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents the first formal proof of convergence for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the class of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. The central theoretical contribution is proving that if the action selection function is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (like Regret Matching or Exp3), the resulting MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree, starting with convergence in single matrix games and extending the result to games where payoffs are noisy estimates from subgames. The paper complements this theoretical analysis with an empirical evaluation on random and worst-case games, comparing the theoretically-grounded variant of their algorithm with more standard implementations and analyzing their convergence rates.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and the importance of its contribution. While MCTS and its variants are widely applied to games with simultaneous moves, their theoretical properties have remained elusive. This work provides the first formal convergence guarantee, filling a significant gap in the literature. It elegantly bridges the fields of online learning (through the concept of Hannan consistency) and heuristic search in games. This is not an incremental improvement but a foundational result that provides a solid theoretical underpinning for using MCTS in this game class.

2.  **Bold and General Approach:** The authors do not just prove convergence for a single, specific algorithm. Instead, they provide a general template and prove that *any* algorithm satisfying the property of $\epsilon$-Hannan consistency will lead to a convergent MCTS procedure. This is a powerful generalization that opens the door for future work to design and analyze new SM-MCTS variants based on the rich literature of regret minimization.

3.  **Sound Theoretical Framework:** The proof structure is logical and well-conceived. The idea of modeling the values from deeper subgames as a repeated matrix game with bounded error is clever and allows the authors to leverage existing results from online learning. The inductive argument to extend this up the game tree is a natural and effective way to tackle the problem.

4.  **Honest Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed to support the theoretical claims and explore their practical implications. Crucially, the authors directly test the performance of the variant required for their proof (propagating mean values) against the more common heuristic (propagating the current sample). They are transparent that the theoretically sound version converges slightly slower, which is an important and honest finding for practitioners.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplifications in the Analysis:** The formal analysis makes a key simplification by assuming a complete game tree and focusing on the long-run behavior of value estimates. While the authors argue this is justified because exploration is guaranteed, it sidesteps the complexities of the initial tree-growing phase where exploration and exploitation dynamics are intertwined. This is a common and often necessary simplification for such proofs, but it does mean the analysis doesn't fully capture the behavior of the algorithm from the very first iteration.

2.  **Loose Convergence Bound:** The final convergence bound on the approximation quality of the Nash Equilibrium, which appears to have a dependency on the square of the game's depth ($D^2$), may be quite loose for deep games. While establishing *any* bound is a major achievement, the practical implications of this specific bound are not deeply discussed. This is a minor point, as the primary contribution is the existence of a proof, not the tightness of the resulting bound.

3.  **Practicality of the "Proven" Variant:** The empirical results show that the version of MCTS required for the proof (using mean-value propagation) is slightly outperformed by the standard heuristic. This creates a small disconnect between theory and best practice. However, the performance difference is not dramatic, and the existence of a provably convergent variant is valuable in itself, even if a slightly different heuristic is used in practice.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Novelty:** This is a pioneering work that provides the first convergence proof for MCTS in an important class of games where theory has lagged far behind practice.
*   **Significance:** The result is foundational. It provides a theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous-move games and establishes a strong connection to the mature field of online regret minimization, which is likely to inspire follow-up research.
*   **Boldness:** The paper tackles a difficult, open theoretical problem and provides a general and elegant solution. This is precisely the type of ambitious work that advances the field, rather than just refining existing methods.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:

*   The analysis relies on a standard but significant simplification (assuming a complete tree for the convergence proof), which might not capture all dynamics of the algorithm.
*   The theoretically sound variant of the algorithm is shown to be slightly slower empirically than a more common heuristic not covered by the proof.

### Recommendation: Accept

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. Its primary contribution—the first convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games—is a significant and bold step forward. The paper tackles a long-standing open problem with a novel approach that connects two important subfields of machine learning and AI. While there are minor limitations in the analysis, such as the simplification of the tree-growing phase and the looseness of the bounds, these are entirely acceptable for a foundational paper of this nature. The value of establishing this theoretical beachhead far outweighs these minor issues. This work provides a new lens through which to understand and develop MCTS algorithms, and it is highly likely to be influential and built upon by future research.
