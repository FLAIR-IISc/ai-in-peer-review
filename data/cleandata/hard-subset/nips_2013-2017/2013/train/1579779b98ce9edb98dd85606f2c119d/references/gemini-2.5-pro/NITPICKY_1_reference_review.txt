### Summary

This paper investigates the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The central contribution is a proof that a generalized MCTS algorithm, which uses an ε-Hannan consistent online learning procedure (such as Regret Matching or Exp3) for in-tree action selection, converges to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is structured inductively, treating nodes in the game tree as repeated matrix games where payoffs from child nodes are noisy estimates. The authors support their theoretical claims with an empirical study on randomly generated and worst-case games, comparing the convergence of a variant required by their proof (propagating mean values) against a more standard implementation (propagating the last sample value).

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**

The paper tackles an important theoretical gap in the MCTS literature. The core idea of leveraging the properties of ε-Hannan consistent algorithms from online learning to establish convergence for MCTS in this game class is novel and insightful. The proof sketch, proceeding by induction from the leaves of the game tree upwards, is a logical approach.

However, the technical quality of the paper is severely undermined by a lack of precision and numerous minor flaws.

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions:** The formal analysis makes a crucial simplification by assuming the entire game tree is built and fixed, effectively analyzing a recursive learning algorithm on a static tree rather than the MCTS algorithm itself, which dynamically grows the tree. While the authors argue the tree will be fully explored with probability 1, this sidesteps the analysis of the exploration/exploitation dynamics during the tree-building phase, which is central to MCTS. This limitation is not sufficiently discussed and weakens the claim of analyzing MCTS convergence.
2.  **Lack of Precision:** Several key definitions and statements are imprecise.
    *   The paper claims to address "zero-sum" games but defines utilities as `u2(z) = 1− u1(z)`, which describes a constant-sum game. Consequently, the advice that Player 2 should use "negated utilities" is incorrect; Player 2 should optimize for `1 - u1`.
    *   The main result, Theorem 4.11, states convergence to a `(D^2 + δ)`-NE. The ε from the underlying ε-Hannan consistent algorithm is conspicuously missing from this bound, which seems incorrect. The dependency on the learning algorithm's quality should be explicit.
    *   The regret update rule for Regret Matching, involving the term `x(h, i, j)`, is opaquely defined and difficult to follow. This is a critical algorithmic detail that must be unambiguous.

**Clarity:**

The paper's clarity is a major weakness.

1.  **Pseudocode:** Algorithm 1, which describes the core SM-MCTS procedure, is highly ambiguous.
    *   The variable `T` is used to represent both the MCTS tree and the game's transition function.
    *   It is not specified how the set of "previously unselected" actions (line 2) is maintained or how an action is chosen from this set (line 3).
    *   The `Update` function is called twice (lines 7 and 14) during a single path back-propagation in a way that is confusing and potentially erroneous. The update at line 7 on the parent node `h` using the raw rollout value `u1` seems out of place.
2.  **Notational Inconsistencies:** The notation `7→` is used for the mapping arrow `→`. The use of `〈0, 1〉m` for empirical frequencies is non-standard. Such inconsistencies make the paper harder to read.
3.  **Typos and Formatting:** The manuscript is replete with typos and formatting errors. The term "ε-Hannan" is frequently written as "-Hannan". Equation (3) uses "oth." instead of "otherwise". Most strikingly, numerous citations in the bibliography are incomplete, missing essential information like year and page numbers (e.g., "[1] ... (AAMAS, , , )"). This suggests a lack of careful proofreading and detracts from the paper's professionalism.

**Originality:**

Despite the flaws in execution, the core contribution is original. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for an MCTS-style algorithm in simultaneous move games. It establishes a valuable connection between the fields of online learning and Monte Carlo tree search.

**Significance:**

The work is potentially significant. A rigorous theoretical foundation for MCTS in this game class would be a valuable contribution, justifying its use from a game-theoretic perspective. However, the significance of the current result is tempered by the strong simplifying assumption of a pre-existing full tree and the overall lack of clarity and precision in the presentation. The empirical section is also of limited significance, as it does not include experiments on well-known benchmarks mentioned in the introduction (e.g., Goofspiel), which would have provided a stronger connection to existing literature.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses an important and open theoretical question regarding the convergence of MCTS.
*   The primary theoretical result, proving convergence to an approximate subgame perfect NE, is a novel and non-trivial contribution.
*   The general framework connecting ε-Hannan consistency to MCTS selection policies is elegant and could inspire future work.

**Arguments against rejection:**
*   The paper suffers from a substantial number of clarity issues, including ambiguous pseudocode and imprecise definitions, which make the work difficult to verify and build upon.
*   The reliance on the simplifying assumption of a static, fully-expanded tree significantly limits the applicability of the theoretical result to the practical MCTS algorithm.
*   The manuscript is marred by numerous typos, formatting errors, and incomplete citations, indicating a lack of care in its preparation.
*   The empirical evaluation is preliminary and lacks comparison on standard benchmarks.

### Decision

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper is based on a strong, original idea with potential for high impact, the execution is not up to the standards of NIPS. The lack of clarity in the core algorithm, the imprecision in key theoretical statements, and the sheer number of formatting errors and typos collectively obscure the contribution and undermine my confidence in the work's technical soundness. The paper would require a major revision to address these fundamental issues before it could be considered for publication.
