

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous move games, which are an important class of zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms using regret-minimizing procedures for action selection, and prove that with ε-Hannan consistent selection functions, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in this class of games, addressing a known limitation in the literature where practical implementations (like UCT) lack theoretical guarantees.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a rigorous theoretical foundation. The authors establish a clear connection between Hannan consistency in repeated matrix games and convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in the full game tree. Their main theorem (Theorem 4.11) is well-motivated and proven through a careful inductive argument that builds from leaf nodes up to the root. The proof strategy is sound, with appropriate lemmas (4.4-4.10) that address key components like the impact of exploration and bounded error in repeated matrix games.

The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical results effectively. The authors compare their mean-propagation variants (RMM and Exp3M) with standard MCTS approaches (RM and Exp3) on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios. The results confirm that their theoretical approach converges to approximate Nash equilibrium, with only minor differences in convergence speed (Figure 2) and comparable performance in worst-case games (Figure 3). The authors are appropriately cautious in their claims, acknowledging that their analysis doesn't cover all practical MCTS variants but suggests they could be generalized.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The definitions and notation are introduced carefully and consistently throughout. The flow from simple matrix games to the full extensive-form game is logical, with each section building on the previous one. The key concepts (ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration, and the relationship to Nash equilibrium) are clearly explained. The appendix containing omitted proofs is a valuable addition that enhances reproducibility. The figures (though not visible in the text) are described as helpful for understanding the empirical results.

## Originality

This work makes a clear and significant original contribution. While MCTS has been widely used in simultaneous move games, no convergence guarantees existed prior to this paper. The authors' approach of connecting MCTS to regret minimization in repeated matrix games is novel, as is their formal proof that ε-Hannan consistency suffices for convergence. The paper distinguishes itself from prior work on backward induction (which requires domain knowledge) and sequential MCTS (which lacks guarantees for simultaneous moves). The connection to Hannan consistency, a concept from online learning, bridges two fields in a productive way.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous move games, which has been a common practice in applications like security games and general game-playing competitions. The paper addresses a practical gap where algorithms have been used successfully without theoretical grounding. The authors' claim that their analysis could extend to other MCTS variants (like UCT, which they acknowledge doesn't converge in some cases) has important implications for future algorithm design.

The work also opens several promising research directions, including finite-time convergence bounds and extensions to games with imperfect information. The empirical results showing comparable convergence speed to existing algorithms (Figure 3) suggest that the theoretical approach is practically viable, which enhances the significance of the contribution.

## Weaknesses

While the paper is strong overall, there are a few limitations. The formal analysis doesn't provide finite-time convergence bounds, which would be valuable for practitioners. The empirical evaluation is limited to small games (depth-2 with branching factor 2 for worst-case analysis), which may not represent the complexity of real-world applications. The paper could have included a more detailed comparison with UCT (which they cite as not converging) to better position their approach.

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, filling an important gap in the literature. The theoretical results are well-supported by empirical evidence, and the paper is clearly written with appropriate technical depth. The work has clear significance for both theoretical and applied research in game theory and reinforcement learning.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it's technically sound (Quality), well-structured (Clarity), offers a novel theoretical approach (Originality), and provides important theoretical grounding for a widely used algorithm (Significance). While the finite-time bounds would be a valuable addition, the current work represents a substantial advancement in the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**
