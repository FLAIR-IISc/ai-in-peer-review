### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the literature on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS is widely used in practice for such games, its theoretical properties have remained unproven, and popular variants like UCT-based MCTS are known not to converge to a Nash Equilibrium (NE).

The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the action selection mechanism within each game node is based on a regret-minimizing procedure. The core theoretical contribution is a proof that if this selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (i.e., every action is tried infinitely often), the resulting MCTS algorithm's average strategies converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is inductive, starting with the convergence of regret minimizers in single matrix games (leaf nodes) and showing that the values propagated upwards can be treated as payoffs with bounded error for the parent nodes. The analysis then demonstrates that ε-Hannan consistent algorithms also converge in these games with bounded error, allowing the argument to proceed up the game tree.

The authors instantiate their framework with two well-known regret minimizers: Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. They also conduct an empirical analysis to validate their claims, comparing the theoretically-required variant that propagates mean rewards with the more common variant that propagates the single sample reward. Their experiments on random and worst-case games show that both variants converge empirically, though the theoretically-justified version converges slightly slower.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper's technical quality is high. The theoretical analysis is the main contribution and appears to be sound. The proof strategy, which cleverly connects the MCTS framework to the established theory of online learning in repeated games, is elegant and well-executed. The key step of analyzing repeated games with bounded error (Lemma 4.9) is crucial and enables the inductive argument over the game tree's depth. The authors are careful and honest in their evaluation, for instance by pointing out that the variant required for their proof (propagating mean values, RMM) is empirically slightly slower than the standard approach (propagating sample values, RM). This transparency strengthens the paper. The empirical section, while not exhaustive, is well-designed to support the theoretical claims and investigate practical implications.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by highlighting the gap in existing literature. The definitions and background section is concise and precise. The core algorithm is presented in a general template before being instantiated with specific methods, which aids understanding. The high-level sketch of the proof in Section 4 is particularly helpful for grasping the main ideas before diving into the lemmas. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly with informative plots. A minor point for improvement would be a slightly more detailed discussion of the D² dependence in the final bound of Theorem 4.11. Is this bound tight, or an artifact of the proof technique? A sentence or two on this would be welcome.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS to a Nash Equilibrium in simultaneous move games. While the components used (MCTS, Regret Matching, Exp3, Hannan consistency) are known, their synthesis to solve this long-standing open problem is novel and insightful. It bridges the gap between the practical success of MCTS in this domain and the lack of theoretical justification, which is a significant contribution. The work correctly positions itself with respect to prior work, noting the practical applications of SM-MCTS and the known failure of UCB to converge.

**Significance:**
The results presented are very significant for both the game theory and MCTS research communities. For algorithmic game theory, it formally establishes MCTS as a valid and provable approximation algorithm for a non-trivial class of extensive-form games. For MCTS practitioners and researchers, it provides a theoretical foundation for designing and analyzing algorithms in domains beyond traditional sequential, perfect-information games like Go. The insight that the core of the problem lies in the per-node action selection strategy, which can be solved with regret minimizers, is an important and generalizable concept. The work is likely to be built upon, for example by researchers seeking to establish finite-time bounds or extend the analysis to more complex game classes (e.g., with imperfect information), as the authors suggest.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Result:** The paper provides the first-ever convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, solving a significant open problem.
2.  **Sound and Elegant Proof:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the inductive proof strategy of treating subgame values as payoffs with bounded error is a key technical insight.
3.  **High Significance:** The work provides a firm theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms and connects the fields of MCTS and online regret minimization in a novel way. It will likely inspire follow-up research.
4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is well-written, and the authors are transparent about the limitations and practical implications of their analysis (e.g., the performance of the mean-propagating variant).

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Loose Bound?:** The final convergence bound's dependence on the square of the game depth (D²) might be loose, and the paper does not discuss this. While providing *any* bound is a major achievement, this could be seen as a minor weakness.
2.  **Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to two-player, zero-sum games with perfect information. While this is a crucial first step, the practical applicability is narrower than for MCTS in general. (This is a very minor point, as foundational work must start somewhere).

### Conclusion

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to the theory of Monte Carlo Tree Search. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the theoretical results, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-executed, clearly written, and addresses a long-standing open question in the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
