Summary
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”) and analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in this setting. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where action selection at each node is driven by any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) no-regret procedure with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3), and where backpropagation can use either the current sample or the mean of past samples. The main theoretical contribution shows that, if one uses an ε–HC selection rule with guaranteed exploration and backs up mean values, the average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium (NE), where D is the tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The proof builds from: (i) convergence of no-regret dynamics to NE in matrix games; (ii) a robustness result for repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error; and (iii) an induction over the game tree that controls error propagation. Empirically, the paper compares mean vs sample backups and RM vs Exp3 on small random and worst-case depth-2 games, finding convergence in all cases, with mean backup slightly slower.

Relation to prior work
UCT is known to perform well in sequential perfect-information games but lacks guarantees in simultaneous-move games; prior work shows UCB can fail to reach NE in even one-shot simultaneous games. CFR provides convergence guarantees in imperfect-information EFGs but is not an MCTS method and is typically used differently. For simultaneous moves with perfect information, exact polynomial-time backward induction exists (and can be sped up with alpha-beta/double oracle), but is impractical for deep trees without heuristics. This paper appears to be the first to give general convergence guarantees for an MCTS-style algorithm to approximate NE in this class by leveraging HC selection.

Quality
- Theory: The high-level argument is sound and well-motivated. The reduction to repeated matrix games with bounded error, and the induction to control error accumulation up the tree, are appropriate. The ε–HC convergence to approximate NE in matrix games is known; the paper extends this to the bounded-error setting and composes the bounds. The D^2 dependence is plausible albeit likely loose. The need for guaranteed exploration and for mean backups is explicitly identified and justified. Some key proofs are deferred to the appendix; the paper asserts eventual full-tree expansion with probability 1 and that early-phase statistics do not harm asymptotic convergence—this is believable given explicit exploration, but it would be important that these details are correct in the supplementary.
- Experiments: Correctly scoped as sanity checks. They isolate the effect of mean vs sample backprop and compare RM vs Exp3 on small synthetic games, including worst-case instances for RM. Results are consistent with the theory; however, empirical scope is limited (tiny games, no larger benchmarks like Goofspiel/Tron, no comparison to search baselines). No finite-time rates are established.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well-organized: the game model is defined, the algorithm is specified, and the proof roadmap is easy to follow. The distinction between empirical frequencies and average strategies is explained, as is the role of exploration. Some notation is dense and a few formatting/typo issues occur in the provided text, but overall the exposition is understandable. It would help to more explicitly state all assumptions at the start of Theorem 4.11 (e.g., mean backup, exploration scheme), and to summarize constants in the final bound.

Originality
The combination of HC selection within SM-MCTS and the proof of convergence to subgame-perfect ε–NE for simultaneous-move, perfect-information zero-sum EFGs appears novel. The repeated-game-with-bounded-error lemma and its use to control MCTS backup noise are also useful. While no-regret to NE in matrix games is classical, the extension and composition within MCTS for this game class are original contributions.

Significance
The contribution is theoretically meaningful for a niche but relevant class of games where simultaneous moves break standard UCT guarantees. It provides principled guidance to use regret-based selection with explicit exploration and (ideally) mean backups. Practical impact is limited by: (i) asymptotic nature of the result with loose D^2 dependence and no sample-complexity bounds; (ii) the fact that the games considered are solvable exactly by backward induction (though the authors correctly argue depth-limited MCTS is often used in practice when evaluation functions are available); and (iii) small-scale experiments. Nonetheless, this is a useful foundational step that may inform extensions to broader EFG classes.

Pros
- First convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information zero-sum games.
- General template covering multiple HC selection rules (RM, Exp3).
- Clear identification of required ingredients: guaranteed exploration and mean backups.
- Clean proof structure via bounded-error repeated games and induction.
- Empirical results consistent with theory.

Cons
- Asymptotic only; no finite-sample rates or explicit dependence on branching factors.
- Error bound scales as ε D^2 and constants (e.g., 4(c+1)) seem pessimistic.
- Requires mean-value backprop, which slows convergence and is atypical in some MCTS implementations; no proof for the standard sample-backup variant.
- Proof relies on full-tree expansion; practical MCTS uses incremental trees and rollouts—more discussion of partial trees/heuristic evaluations would be helpful.
- Experiments are limited to very small games; no comparison to exact backward induction with pruning or to CFR-like baselines.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can you provide a finite-time convergence rate in terms of the regret rates of the selection algorithms, depth D, and branching factors?
- Is the mean-backup requirement essential, or can a suitably smoothed/sample-averaging backup suffice? Any counterexample for pure sample backups?
- Please make explicit and prove (in the paper or supplement) the statement that the incremental tree-building phase almost surely yields a complete tree under your exploration scheme, and that transients do not affect the limit.
- Consider updating internal variables during exploration rather than ignoring exploratory samples; how does this affect Lemma 4.4 and practice?
- Add experiments on larger benchmarks and compare to alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning and to CFR variants where applicable.

Recommendation: Weak accept
A solid and novel theoretical contribution with clear assumptions and a plausible proof strategy, albeit with limited empirical scope and asymptotic guarantees. I believe it will be of interest to the NeurIPS community working on game-solving and MCTS.

Confidence: High on overall assessment; medium on detailed proof constants (some proofs are in the appendix).
