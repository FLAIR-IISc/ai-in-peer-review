This paper investigates the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms, proving that convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium is guaranteed when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function. They empirically evaluate their framework with Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating comparable convergence speeds to existing MCTS algorithms for these games.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, which, despite its practical success, lacked formal convergence guarantees outside of two-player sequential games. The proposed template and convergence proof provide valuable insight into the behavior of MCTS in a more complex game setting. Relating simultaneous-move games to stacked matrix games is a clever and effective approach, enabling the application of regret-minimizing procedures designed for matrix games. The empirical evaluation adds credibility to the theoretical results, although the comparison to existing algorithms is somewhat limited.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a novel contribution, filling a significant gap in the literature.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The proof of convergence, based on ε-Hannan consistency, appears rigorous and well-motivated. The reduction to repeated matrix games and the inductive argument are logical and clearly presented.
*   **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general, allowing for instantiation with various regret-minimizing procedures.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Definitions are provided for key concepts, and the algorithm is described clearly.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper builds upon existing literature in both MCTS and algorithmic game theory, appropriately referencing related work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation focuses on relatively small games. While the results are encouraging, it's unclear how well the algorithms scale to larger, more complex scenarios. The comparison to existing algorithms (specifically those mentioned in the introduction [10, 11, 12, 13]) is limited to stating comparable convergence speeds without detailed quantitative comparison.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The theoretical analysis relies on the assumption of complete tree building, which may not be practical for large games. The impact of stopping the search prematurely on the convergence guarantees is not discussed.
*   **Numerical Stability:**  The note about potential numerical overflows in Exp3 highlights a practical concern. While addressed, this suggests potential issues when implementing the algorithm.
*   **Complexity of Proof:** The proofs are somewhat dense and may be challenging for readers unfamiliar with the technical details of online learning and game theory. While the main ideas are presented clearly, a deeper understanding requires careful study of the appendix.
*   **Exploration Parameter:** The optimal choice of the exploration parameter (γ) isn't extensively discussed. How sensitive are the results to the choice of this parameter?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in our understanding of MCTS.
*   The convergence proof is rigorous and provides a strong foundation for future research.
*   The general MCTS template offers a flexible framework for designing and analyzing algorithms for simultaneous-move games.
*   The empirical results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical viability of the approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and does not fully demonstrate the scalability of the algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis relies on simplifying assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.
*   The complexity of the proofs may make the paper inaccessible to a wider audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong contribution to the field of game-playing algorithms. The theoretical results are significant and the empirical evaluation provides supporting evidence. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest expanding the empirical evaluation to include larger and more complex games and providing a more detailed comparison to existing algorithms. Additionally, a brief discussion of the practical implications of the simplifying assumptions made in the theoretical analysis would be beneficial.
