This paper tackles the challenging problem of analyzing Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a setting where theoretical guarantees are largely absent. The authors provide a general template for MCTS algorithms and, crucially, prove convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under fairly general conditions – specifically, using an -Hannan consistent selection function. This is a significant contribution, as it provides a theoretical foundation for a widely used, yet poorly understood, class of algorithms. The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates comparable performance to existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move games is genuinely novel. Previous work has largely focused on empirical performance, lacking formal convergence guarantees. The connection to -Hannan consistency is clever and allows for a general framework.
*   **Significance:** Establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS is important. This work provides a solid theoretical foundation for algorithms used in domains like Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion games, and general game playing. The framework’s generality is also valuable, potentially encompassing existing algorithms.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the theoretical framework. The definitions and notation are standard, and the proofs appear sound (though a full verification is beyond the scope of this review). The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is easy to follow.
*   **Soundness:** The formal analysis appears rigorous. The use of induction and leveraging existing results on Hannan consistency provides a convincing argument for convergence.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical section feels somewhat limited. While it demonstrates comparable performance to existing methods, it doesn't convincingly showcase a substantial advantage or explore the implications of the theoretical results more deeply.  The choice of games (randomly generated and worst-case) is standard, but further exploration of specific game types could be beneficial. The comparison against recent methods is brief.
*   **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical results guarantee convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, but they provide little insight into *how quickly* this convergence occurs. The practical implications of the convergence bound (D<sup>2</sup>) are not fully discussed. 
*   **Practicality of -Hannan Consistency:**  While theoretically appealing, ensuring strict -Hannan consistency in practice might be difficult or computationally expensive.  A discussion of the practical trade-offs involved in implementing these selection functions would be helpful.
*   **Scope of Generalization:** The authors conjecture that their analysis could be extended to other MCTS algorithms. While plausible, this remains an open question. Providing even a preliminary sketch of how this might be achieved would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in game theory and AI.
*   The theoretical result is a significant advance, providing a much-needed foundation for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   The clarity of the presentation and the soundness of the analysis are commendable.
*   The empirical results, while not extensive, are consistent with the theoretical findings.
*   The work's potential impact on applications using MCTS in these types of games is substantial.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical validation is relatively limited and doesn't fully explore the practical implications of the theory.
*   The convergence rate is not explicitly addressed, making it difficult to assess the algorithm's efficiency.
*   The practical challenges of implementing -Hannan consistent selection functions are not fully discussed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution.  While the empirical validation could be more comprehensive and the convergence rate analysis more detailed, the novelty and importance of the theoretical result outweigh these weaknesses. I believe this paper represents a valuable addition to the NIPS literature and recommend **acceptance**. The paper prioritizes a novel and bold theoretical approach, which I value highly. The minor empirical shortcomings can be addressed in future work. I would encourage the authors to expand the empirical section and investigate the practical considerations of implementing -Hannan consistent selection functions in a follow-up study.
