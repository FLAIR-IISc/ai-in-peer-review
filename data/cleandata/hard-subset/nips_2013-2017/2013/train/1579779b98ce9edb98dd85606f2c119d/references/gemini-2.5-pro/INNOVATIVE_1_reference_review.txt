This paper addresses a significant and long-standing theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS and its variants have been successfully applied to two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, there has been a notable lack of formal guarantees that these methods converge to a Nash equilibrium. In fact, prior work has shown that popular selection strategies like UCB can fail to converge even in simple cases.

The main contribution of this paper is the first formal proof of convergence for a general class of MCTS algorithms in this setting. The authors introduce a template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the action selection mechanism within each node can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. The core theoretical result demonstrates that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the resulting MCTS procedure is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The proof is built elegantly via induction on the depth of the game tree. It begins by leveraging known convergence results for ε-Hannan consistent algorithms in single matrix games (the leaves of the game tree). It then cleverly extends this to "repeated matrix games with bounded error" to model the situation in parent nodes, where the payoffs from child nodes are stochastic estimates. This inductive step is repeated up to the root, yielding the main convergence theorem. The authors empirically validate their claims using two instantiations of their framework, Regret Matching and Exp3, on a set of random and worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** This work provides a foundational theoretical result for a widely used class of algorithms in a domain where such theory was previously absent. Establishing the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games is a major conceptual advance. It moves the use of these algorithms from a purely heuristic basis to one with a solid game-theoretic footing. This is precisely the type of bold, foundational work that is of high value.

2.  **Elegant Theoretical Framework:** The connection drawn between the ε-Hannan consistency property of an online learning algorithm used for node selection and the convergence of the global MCTS search to a subgame perfect equilibrium is the paper's key insight. This framework is not just a one-off proof; it provides a clear and general recipe for designing new, provably-convergent MCTS variants for this class of games. This has the potential to guide future research in a principled way.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The theoretical argument is built in clear, digestible steps, starting from the simple case of a repeated matrix game and progressively adding the complexities of stochastic payoffs (bounded error) and the recursive structure of the extensive-form game. This makes a potentially dense proof quite accessible to the reader.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplifying Assumption in Analysis:** The formal analysis is conducted under the assumption of a fully expanded game tree. The authors argue that since exploration is guaranteed, every node will be visited infinitely often, and thus the tree will eventually be built, with the statistics from the building phase not preventing convergence. While this argument is plausible, it sidesteps a rigorous analysis of the interplay between the non-stationary value estimates during the tree expansion phase and the convergence of the selection policies. This is a minor weakness in a first-of-its-kind proof but leaves room for future tightening.

2.  **Potential Looseness of the Convergence Bound:** The derived bound on the quality of the approximate equilibrium (ε') has a dependency on the depth of the game (D^2). This bound may be loose, and it is not clear if it is an artifact of the proof technique or an inherent property of the problem. While establishing *any* convergence bound is the primary achievement, the practical implications of this dependency are not deeply explored.

3.  **Disconnect between Theory and Empirical Performance:** The experiments honestly report that the variant required for the proof (propagating the mean value of rewards, "RMM") converges slightly slower than the more common heuristic of propagating the current sample's reward ("RM"). This creates a slight tension between the theoretically-justified algorithm and the empirically superior one. While this does not invalidate the theory, it suggests that the current analysis may not fully capture all the dynamics that lead to good practical performance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides the first-ever proof of convergence for MCTS in the important domain of simultaneous move games, settling an open question and providing a solid theoretical foundation for future work.
*   The novel framework connecting Hannan consistency with MCTS convergence is a significant conceptual contribution that is likely to be influential.
*   The work is bold and addresses a fundamental problem, prioritizing a significant new direction over a small, incremental improvement on existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical analysis relies on a simplifying assumption about the tree structure.
*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited and shows that the theoretically-grounded variant of the algorithm is slightly outperformed by a more standard heuristic.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The weaknesses identified are minor compared to the strength and significance of the main contribution. This is not an incremental paper that offers a slight improvement on an existing technique; it is a foundational piece of theoretical work that provides the "why" for a class of algorithms that, until now, have only been justified by empirical success. The novel connection between online learning theory and MCTS is a beautiful and important insight. This paper significantly advances our understanding of search algorithms in game theory and is an excellent fit for NIPS.
