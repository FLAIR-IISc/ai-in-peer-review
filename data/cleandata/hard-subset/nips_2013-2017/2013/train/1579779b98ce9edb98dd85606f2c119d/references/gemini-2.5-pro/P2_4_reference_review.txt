**Summary**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS is a popular and empirically successful algorithm for game-playing, its convergence properties are not well understood outside of two-player, perfect-information sequential games. The authors focus on the class of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The main contribution is a formal proof that a general template of MCTS for this game class, which uses an ε-Hannan consistent action selection procedure, converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built by induction on the game tree's structure, starting from the analysis of repeated matrix games (leaf nodes), extending it to games with bounded error (nodes whose children are estimations), and finally composing these results for the entire extensive-form game. The authors instantiate their framework with two well-known regret-minimizing algorithms, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, and provide an empirical analysis that supports their claims and compares the proven variant with a more common implementation heuristic.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Evaluation Criteria)**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality.
*   **Strengths:** The theoretical analysis is the paper's core strength. The proof strategy, which connects the concept of Hannan consistency from online learning to the convergence of a search algorithm in extensive-form games, is both sound and elegant. The inductive argument, building from simple matrix games to repeated games with bounded error, and then to the full game tree, is well-constructed and easy to follow. The claims are well-supported by the provided lemmas and theorems. The authors are also commendably honest about a key requirement of their proof—propagating the mean of rewards rather than the current sample's reward—and they explicitly test the performance implication of this requirement in their experiments. This transparency strengthens the credibility of the work.
*   **Weaknesses:** The final convergence bound has a dependency on `D^2`, where D is the depth of the game. This seems potentially loose, likely arising from the summation of errors at each level of the induction. A discussion on the tightness of this bound, or whether it's an artifact of the proof technique, would have been valuable. The analysis also relies on the assumption that the full game tree is eventually built, which is reasonable for a convergence proof (as it will happen with probability 1), but it sidesteps the complexities of the exploration/exploitation trade-off during the tree expansion phase.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.
*   **Strengths:** The authors do an excellent job of defining the necessary background concepts from game theory (Section 2) before introducing their algorithm and analysis. The structure of the formal analysis (Section 4) is logical, guiding the reader from the simplest case to the main theorem. The notation is consistent and the purpose of each lemma is well-explained. Algorithm 1 provides a clear template for the proposed SM-MCTS. The distinction between propagating the mean vs. the current sample is explicitly made, which is crucial for understanding both the theory and the experiments.
*   **Weaknesses:** No major weaknesses in clarity. A minor suggestion would be to explicitly state in the main text of Section 4.4 how the "bounded error" `c` from Lemma 4.9 is derived from the convergence guarantees of the subgames in the inductive step. While this is alluded to and detailed in the appendix, a sentence or two in the main text would improve the flow of the argument.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original in its primary contribution.
*   **Strengths:** To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the components used (MCTS, Regret Matching, Exp3) are well-established, their synthesis into a provably convergent algorithm for this game class is a novel and important contribution. It bridges the gap between the heuristic use of MCTS in these games and the rigorous analysis common in algorithmic game theory and online learning. It correctly places this work in context, citing prior practical applications of MCTS in such games [10, 11, 12] and distinguishing it from work on sequential games.
*   **Weaknesses:** The novelty lies in the theoretical analysis, not in the proposal of a new, state-of-the-art practical algorithm. The empirical results show that the proposed methods are comparable to, but not superior than, existing heuristics. This is perfectly acceptable for a theoretical paper but should be noted.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for both the game theory and machine learning communities.
*   **Strengths:** This work provides a firm theoretical justification for using MCTS-like algorithms in a broad and important class of games. Researchers and practitioners can now apply regret-minimization techniques within MCTS with the confidence that their strategies will converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium. This paper opens the door for further theoretical work, such as deriving finite-time performance bounds (as the authors note in their conclusion) or extending the analysis to more complex game classes (e.g., imperfect information). This is a foundational result that others are likely to build upon.
*   **Weaknesses:** The practical impact is slightly dampened by the finding that the theoretically-justified version of the algorithm (propagating the mean) is empirically slightly slower than the standard heuristic (propagating the current sample). While the authors conjecture that the standard heuristic also converges, this remains an open question. The experiments are conducted on random and small worst-case games; validation on a well-known benchmark like Goofspiel, which is mentioned in the introduction, would have strengthened the empirical claims and better demonstrated the practical significance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** It provides the first-ever convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a long-standing open problem.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, well-structured, and convincing.
3.  **High Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy for an informed reader to follow.
4.  **Connects Fields:** It elegantly connects concepts from online learning (regret minimization) with tree search algorithms for game playing.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
1.  **Limited Practical Novelty:** The proposed algorithms do not outperform existing heuristics empirically. The main contribution is purely theoretical.
2.  **Potentially Loose Bounds:** The theoretical bound (`D^2`) may not be tight, and this is not discussed.
3.  **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The proof requires a modification (propagating the mean) that slightly hurts empirical performance compared to the standard heuristic, whose convergence remains unproven.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant and novel theoretical result. It solves an important open problem by providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in the challenging domain of simultaneous move games. The technical quality is high, the writing is clear, and the contribution is foundational. While the practical implications are not groundbreaking in terms of performance, the theoretical justification it provides for a whole class of algorithms is of great value to the NIPS community. The weaknesses are minor in comparison to the strength of the main contribution. I recommend acceptance.
