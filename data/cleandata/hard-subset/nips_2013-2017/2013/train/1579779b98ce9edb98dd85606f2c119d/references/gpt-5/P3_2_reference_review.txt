Summary and contributions:
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which the selection policy at each node is any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3 with explicit exploration). The key theoretical result shows almost-sure convergence of SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium when node backups return mean returns (not the most recent sample). The analysis proceeds in three steps: (i) establish convergence properties for repeated matrix games when both players are ε–HC; (ii) extend to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error; (iii) inductively propagate these bounds up the game tree to obtain a subgame-perfect approximation guarantee that scales with depth D (stated as O(D^2) in the text). Empirically, the paper tests RM and Exp3 on random and small worst-case constructions and observes convergence; mean back-ups converge slightly slower than last-sample back-ups.

Relation to prior work:
- Distinguishes from alpha-beta-style exact/backward induction approaches with LP or double-oracle pruning for simultaneous-move games (Saffidine et al.; Bošanský et al.), which require evaluators/cutoffs and can be expensive.
- Contrasts with UCT-based SM-MCTS empirical work (e.g., Tron, GGP) and the negative result that UCB does not converge in a simultaneous move one-shot game (Shafiei et al.).
- Uses online learning notions (Hannan consistency; RM; Exp3) akin to classic results that no-regret dynamics approach equilibria in repeated games; extends to the stacked-tree setting and proves subgame-perfectness.
- Related but distinct from CFR-style guarantees in imperfect-information games; here the game class is perfect information with simultaneous moves and the proof technique is different.

Strengths:
- Novel and clean theoretical contribution: a general, algorithm-agnostic convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under broad conditions (ε–HC with exploration), including subgame-perfectness.
- The layered reduction (repeated matrix games → bounded-error repeated games → tree induction) is intuitive and leverages standard tools (martingale SLLN, regret bounds).
- The requirement for “guaranteed exploration” is realistic and satisfied by standard HC algorithms via mixing.
- The paper carefully distinguishes empirical vs average strategies and shows they coincide asymptotically under the sampling scheme.
- Empirical section, while limited, corroborates the qualitative claims and examines worst-case small games.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Bounds and constants are opaque in the main theorem. The statement of Theorem 4.11 uses “-Hannan consistent” and “subgame perfect ( D2 + δ)-NE,” but ε (and dependence on exploration γ) is missing from the displayed bound. Earlier lemmas suggest 4(c+1) factors; the final dependence appears to be O(D^2(ε+γ)), but this must be stated precisely. As written, the theorem is underspecified.
- The D^2 dependence is likely not tight. Given error at depth d is O(d), summing yields O(D^2); however, with more careful accounting (e.g., dynamic programming with weighted errors, or using variance-aware concentration with mean backups), an O(D) dependence may be achievable. At minimum, discuss tightness or provide a lower-bound example.
- The mean-backup requirement is crucial for the proof but diverges from common SM-MCTS practice. Empirically, last-sample backups converge too, but without guarantees. It would strengthen the paper to (i) formalize why last-sample backup fails in the current proof, (ii) identify sufficient conditions under which it would also work, or (iii) provide a counterexample.
- Assumptions around “eventually bounded error” in repeated games M(t) are strong and hinge on lower-level convergence happening “early enough.” The induction sketches this but leaves quantitative rates unstated; there are no finite-sample/high-probability bounds, which limits practical guidance.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: tiny depths/branching, synthetic payoffs, no comparison to strong baselines (e.g., serialized alpha-beta with LP/DO, or recent SM solvers), and no real domains (Goofspiel, Tron). No confidence bands or stopping criteria are reported. Claims of “comparable speed” are weakly supported.
- Clarity issues: notation around ε is inconsistent (rendered as a hyphen “-” in multiple places, including theorem statements). Algorithm 1 mixes joint action selection with per-player action selection (Exp3 section) and relies on data structures (Xhij) not introduced earlier. The Update definitions could be made more explicit.

Quality:
- The high-level proof strategy is sound and builds on established no-regret theory, but key constants, depth dependence, and the necessity of mean backups need tightening and clearer exposition. No fatal technical flaw is apparent; however, the final convergence guarantee should be stated with explicit dependence on ε and γ.

Clarity:
- Generally readable, but suffers from notation/rendering issues (ε), missing definitions (e.g., per-joint-action statistics), and occasional ambiguity in the algorithmic description. The paper would benefit from a consolidated theorem stating the final bound with all parameters, and a figure clarifying data kept at each node.

Originality:
- To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence result for MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information zero-sum games leading to subgame-perfect approximate NE using generic HC selection rules. This is a meaningful theoretical advance.

Significance:
- Simultaneous-move perfect-information games arise in several domains. Providing a general convergence foundation for SM-MCTS is valuable. However, practical impact would be greater with finite-time bounds and broader empirical validation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- State the main theorem with explicit constants: dependence on ε (HC error), γ (exploration), depth D, and payoff range. Clarify whether the bound is O(D^2(ε+γ)) and whether constants like 4 appear.
- Discuss the tightness of D^2 and whether O(D) is possible; provide either a lower bound example or a discussion of obstacles.
- Clarify Algorithm 1: define all maintained statistics (per-node vs per-joint-action), spell out Update for both RM and Exp3, and reconcile joint vs independent action selection.
- Elaborate on why mean backups are required; if possible, extend the proof to last-sample backups or give a counterexample.
- Expand experiments: include at least one real game (e.g., Goofspiel), compare to alpha-beta + LP/DO baselines, vary γ, and report confidence intervals.
- Minor: fix ε rendering; proof sketches that rely on SLLN/martingale arguments should cite precise theorems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under widely used HC selection rules.
- Clean conceptual contribution that is likely to be built upon.
- Subgame-perfectness is a strong property.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Main theorem’s bound is underspecified and likely suboptimal; clarity and constants need work.
- Empirical evaluation is limited and does not substantiate “comparable speed” claims.
- Reliance on mean backups reduces practical relevance without further justification or extension.

Recommendation: Weak accept if the authors clarify the main theorem (explicit ε, γ, D dependence), fix notation, and strengthen algorithmic clarity. Otherwise, borderline.
