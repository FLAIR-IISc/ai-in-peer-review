

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games, and prove that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This is particularly noteworthy because, as the authors correctly state, "to the best of our knowledge, no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general EFGs."

The paper's main strength lies in its novel theoretical contribution. It identifies a critical gap in the literature and provides a rigorous analysis that fills this gap. The insight that ε-Hannan consistent selection functions ensure convergence to approximate Nash equilibrium is both bold and well-supported by the formal proofs. The paper also wisely acknowledges limitations, such as the lack of convergence rate bounds and the focus on a specific game class.

The theoretical analysis is sound and well-structured. The authors build their proof through a careful inductive approach, starting with matrix games and extending to the full game tree. The proofs of Lemmas 4.4-4.7 and Theorem 4.11 are logically rigorous and address a problem that has been practically relevant but theoretically unexplored in MCTS literature. The empirical evaluation, while modest (testing on 100 random games with 100 runs each), appropriately supports the theoretical claims by showing that the mean propagation variants converge to approximate Nash equilibrium, albeit slightly slower than the current sample value variants.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and properly situates itself within the existing literature. It correctly distinguishes its contribution from prior work, particularly noting that UCT does not converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games (as shown by Shafiei et al.). The authors also thoughtfully address the relationship between empirical frequencies and average strategies, which is a subtle but important point in online learning.

The primary weakness is the limited scope of the empirical evaluation. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, a more comprehensive empirical analysis (e.g., testing on larger game trees or more diverse game structures) would have strengthened the practical relevance of the theoretical claims. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological completeness, I believe this limitation is outweighed by the paper's core theoretical contribution.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides the first theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous move games, which have been widely studied in practice but lacked theoretical grounding. This work opens the door for future theoretical analysis of MCTS in other game settings and provides a foundation for practitioners to confidently use MCTS in this class of games. The paper's focus on a specific but important game class is justified by the lack of prior theoretical work in this area.

In terms of originality, this paper is clearly novel. The authors thoroughly survey the related work (including prior MCTS applications to games like Goofspiel and Tron) and correctly identify the lack of convergence guarantees as a gap in the literature. The paper is significantly different from previous NIPS papers on MCTS (e.g., the UCT paper by Kocsis and Szepesvári) and makes a distinct theoretical contribution.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a bold, novel theoretical contribution that fills a significant gap in the literature. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive, the theoretical contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper's focus on a specific but important game class is justified by the lack of prior theoretical work in this area, and the authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their approach.

This paper is exactly the kind of work that NIPS should be publishing - it's theoretically sound, addresses a significant gap in the literature, and provides a foundation for future work. The reviewers who prioritize novelty and bold theoretical contributions will find this paper highly valuable. It represents a genuine advance in understanding MCTS convergence properties and will likely influence future research on algorithmic game theory and Monte Carlo methods.
