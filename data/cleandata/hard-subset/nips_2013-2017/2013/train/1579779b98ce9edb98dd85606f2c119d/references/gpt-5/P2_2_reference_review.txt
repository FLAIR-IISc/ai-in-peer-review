Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”) and analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) variants for them. The core contribution is a general SM-MCTS template that uses any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) selection algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching, Exp3). The authors prove almost sure convergence of the average strategies to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE), with an error that scales with the game depth D. The proof proceeds by: (i) analyzing repeated matrix games with HC learners, (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) inducting up the tree to handle error propagation across depths. A key technical choice is to back up mean returns (“SM-MCTS-M”) rather than the most recent sample, which makes the recursion amenable to bounded-error analysis. Empirically, the paper compares regret matching and Exp3 variants with/without mean backups on random and adversarially found depth-2 games, showing convergence and characterizing practical convergence speeds.

This sits within a line of work showing UCT’s limitations in simultaneous-move games (e.g., Shafiei et al.) and complements exact game-solving (backward induction with alpha-beta/double-oracle) and regret-minimization methods (RM, Exp3). To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move settings.

Quality
- Theory: The technical claims are plausible and appropriately scoped. The reduction to repeated matrix games with bounded error is standard and carefully adapted. Lemma 4.6 (two ε-HC players imply empirical strategies within v ± 2ε) and its bounded-error extension (Lemma 4.9; constants linear in the error) are sensible and correctly lead to an eventual (4(c+1)+δ)-NE in the repeated game. The main theorem (subgame-perfect (ε·D^2+δ)-NE under guaranteed exploration and mean backup) follows by induction, with error accumulation per level and deviation gains bounded per level.
- Assumptions: The results require two-player zero-sum, perfect information, finite tree, guaranteed exploration (or forced exploration with rate γ, which degrades ε by +γ), and, importantly, mean backups. The paper clearly states these and discusses why mean backups are needed for the analysis.
- Experiments: The experiments are modest but on point: they test the effect of mean backups and explore worst-case small games. They corroborate convergence and highlight that mean backups are slightly slower but still converge; the observed exploitability floor aligns with the exploration rate.
- Caveats: No finite-time/sample complexity rates; the asymptotic bound scales as O(D^2) and is likely loose. The practical impact of requiring full-tree eventual expansion is acknowledged but largely brushed aside theoretically.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-structured: definitions, algorithm template, and selection/update rules (RM, Exp3) are described. The proof skeleton is understandable, with full proofs relegated to an appendix.
- Minor issues: Some notation is dense and a few editorial rough edges appear (e.g., occasional missing spaces or typos). The dependence of the final error bound on ε and γ could be summarized more explicitly where Theorem 4.11 is stated (since exploration affects ε via ε+γ). It would also help to explicitly state in Theorem 4.11 that the mean-backup variant is assumed.

Originality
- The combination of MCTS with HC selection and a convergence analysis for simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs is, to my knowledge, new. Prior MCTS work in this domain lacked guarantees; CFR-style guarantees exist in imperfect-information formulations but are not MCTS. The framework of bounded-error repeated games to control propagation up the tree is a sensible, novel application in this context.

Significance
- The result fills a theoretical gap for a practically popular algorithmic family (MCTS) in a specific yet relevant class of games (simultaneous moves). It justifies using SM-MCTS with HC selection when one desires asymptotic approximate equilibria rather than only empirical performance. This is likely to be of interest to researchers in game search and learning in games.
- Limitations temper impact: the asymptotics, O(D^2) error scaling, reliance on mean backups (less common in practice), and lack of finite-time rates restrict immediate practical uptake. Still, it is a meaningful theoretical step that others can build on (e.g., tightening constants, deriving rates, relaxing assumptions).

Strengths
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under clear assumptions.
- General template covering multiple HC algorithms (RM, Exp3).
- Clean, modular proof structure; bounded-error framework is reusable.
- Empirical validation aligns with theory and probes worst-case behavior.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Asymptotic-only guarantees with loose depth dependence; no finite-time bounds. Suggest adding even crude rate bounds (in terms of visits per node) or discussing obstacles to deriving them.
- The necessity of mean backups is a practical deviation from common MCTS implementations. Suggest clarifying whether variants of discounted/averaged backups or robustified bootstraps could also be analyzed, and outlining what breaks without mean backups.
- The final bound’s dependence on γ (forced exploration) should be made explicit in the main theorem or a corollary, since experiments show it dictates an exploitability floor.
- Empirical section is relatively small-scale and synthetic; one or two domain benchmarks (e.g., a simultaneous-move game from GGP) would strengthen the case.

Questions for authors
- Can the D^2 factor be improved to O(D), given the error propagation argument mentions linear error and linear deviation gains per level?
- Does your analysis extend to using running-average action values without storing full joint-action matrices at nodes?
- Could you formalize convergence for the non-mean backup variant under additional assumptions (e.g., variance control), since it performs similarly in practice?

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and nontrivial theoretical contribution addressing a recognized gap.
- Soundness and clarity of the main arguments.
- Generality across HC selection rules and subgame-perfect convergence.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of finite-time bounds and likely loose asymptotic constants.
- Practical gap due to mean backups and exploration-induced exploitability.
- Limited empirical scope.

Overall
A solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. Despite limitations, it meets NeurIPS criteria for originality and quality, with reasonable clarity and meaningful significance for a subcommunity. I lean toward acceptance, especially if the authors clarify the mean-backup assumption in the main theorem and discuss γ’s impact in the final bound.
