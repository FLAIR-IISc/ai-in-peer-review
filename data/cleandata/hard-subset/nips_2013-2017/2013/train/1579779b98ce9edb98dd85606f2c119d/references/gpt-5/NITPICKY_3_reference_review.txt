Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a generic SM-MCTS template in which, at each node, players select actions using any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) bandit-style procedure with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching, Exp3). The analysis ignores incremental tree growth (arguing the full tree is eventually expanded with probability 1) and shows by induction on depth that if each local learner is ε–HC, then the induced SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The technical core develops convergence of ε–HC learners in repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors and lifts this through the tree, yielding a bound stated as O(D^2). Empirically, the paper compares mean-backup vs. sample-backup variants and RM vs. Exp3 on small random/worst-case games, finding both converge, with mean-backups slightly slower.

Strengths
- Addresses a real theoretical gap: convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games (where UCT can fail).
- Clean and modular view: node-local ε–HC learners with guaranteed exploration composed via an error-propagation argument.
- Subgame-perfect convergence is a nice property (not just root value convergence).
- The repeated games with bounded error formulation and the bridging lemmas are useful and could be of independent interest.
- Simple, reproducible algorithms (RM, Exp3) and sanity-check experiments that qualitatively match the theory’s asymptotics.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical correctness and consistency
- Theorem 4.11 statement appears dimensionally inconsistent. It states that the average strategies form a subgame-perfect (D^2 + δ)-NE. The dependence on the local regret ε (and the exploration γ that inflates it) is missing. Earlier results (Lemma 4.9, Theorem 4.10) yield approximations of the form 4(c+1)+δ; the induction should produce a bound like O(D·ε) or O(D^2·ε), not an absolute D^2. As written, the bound can be worse than 1 (the payoff range) for modest D and says nothing about ε. This needs correction and a precise constant trail (including the factors 2/4 introduced in Lemma 4.6/4.9 and the exploration penalty from Lemma 4.4).
- RM update appears to mix scales. The regret update uses x(h,i′,j) = Xhij (cumulative sum) for unplayed (i′,j) and u1 (single rollout) for the played (i,j), and then sets rh[i′] ← rh[i′] + (x(h,i′,j) − u1). Subtracting a single sample from a cumulative sum is inconsistent; presumably all terms should be means or single samples to keep regret consistent across actions. This is a serious bug unless clarified.
- Mean-backup variant: returning Xh/nh up the tree and then adding that mean again into the parent’s cumulative sum (Xh ← Xh + u1; nh ← nh + 1 where u1 is already a mean) yields a “mean of means” rather than an average of terminal payoffs, potentially biasing estimates. The proofs rely on bounded error, but it is not shown that this backup scheme maintains the required error properties or unbiasedness; the argument should explicitly bound the “mean-of-means” distortion.
- Guaranteed exploration of joint actions: Definition 4.2 requires tij→∞ for all pairs. With independent γ-mixing at each player this is true (each pair has probability ≥ γ^2/(|A1||A2|) per visit), but the paper should explicitly state the independence assumption and give the short proof (or reference).
- Lemma 4.5 (empirical frequencies vs. average strategies) is plausible via martingale SLLN, but the conditions (bounded increments, conditional independence of action draws given the mixed strategies) should be clearly stated. As written, it’s asserted without detail.
- The claim “we show [full-tree expansion happens almost surely] and that the statistics collected during the tree building phase cannot prevent the eventual convergence” is not substantiated in the main text; the appendix is referenced. Given how central this step is to replace the real algorithm by the “complete-tree” analysis, a succinct argument should be in the paper.

Clarity and presentation
- Numerous typos and notation inconsistencies: frequent use of “-Hannan consistent” and “-Nash equilibrium” with the ε missing/implicit; “wh,i = xh,i1.”; inconsistent indexing (i1, j2 vs i, j), “oth.”; dangling equation references. These hinder readability and raise doubts about the precise algorithm definitions.
- Algorithm 1 line references do not always match the described updates. The Update signature and RetVal behavior should be spelled out precisely for both backup variants and for each learner.
- References are incomplete or inconsistent (missing years/pages; journal/proceeding details often absent). For example, Hart & Mas-Colell’s title is incomplete, and several entries list only venue names.

Experimental evaluation
- Scope is limited: small synthetic games (depth ≤ 2) with uniform rollouts; no real domains (e.g., Tron), no stress-tests at larger depth/branching, and no comparison to other SM-MCTS variants beyond RM/Exp3. No finite-time curves beyond log-log plots; no error bars or statistical tests.
- Hyperparameters (e.g., η for Exp3 set to γ/|A|) are arbitrary without justification; sensitivity analyses are absent.
- The “worst-case” search is restricted to tiny grids and only identifies worst cases for RM. It is informative but not compelling.

Originality and significance
- The high-level result (asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS with HC selection) is a useful formalization, but conceptually it is a fairly direct lifting of no-regret dynamics in repeated games to trees via error propagation and exploration. Given that exact backward induction is polynomial for this class, the practical impact rests on large-scale instances; the current experiments fall short of demonstrating that impact.
- Related work discussion could be strengthened (e.g., clearer contrast to CFR-style guarantees in extensive-form games and to prior SM-MCTS analyses).

Questions for authors
- Please fix the constants in Theorem 4.11 and provide a clean bound with explicit dependence on ε, γ, D, and branching factors if any.
- Clarify the RM/Exp3 Update details to avoid scale mismatches; is x(h,i′,j) intended to be the latest sample, an unbiased estimator, or a running mean?
- Provide a brief, self-contained argument for eventual full tree expansion and for joint-action exploration.
- Can you avoid mean-backups by instead feeding running means to the local learners while backing up samples, and would the proofs still go through?

Pros
- Addresses a genuine theoretical gap; subgame-perfect convergence.
- General and modular framework applicable with multiple HC learners.
- The “repeated games with bounded error” lens is elegant.

Cons
- Technical inconsistencies (notably Theorem 4.11 constants; RM update scales).
- Heavy reliance on nonstandard mean-backups without a clean justification.
- Weak empirical section and incomplete references/notation.

Recommendation: Reject
This is a promising line of work, but the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation issues to meet NeurIPS standards. I encourage the authors to fix the algorithmic definitions and constants, include concise core proofs for key steps, improve clarity/notation/references, and strengthen the empirical evaluation. Confidence: High.
