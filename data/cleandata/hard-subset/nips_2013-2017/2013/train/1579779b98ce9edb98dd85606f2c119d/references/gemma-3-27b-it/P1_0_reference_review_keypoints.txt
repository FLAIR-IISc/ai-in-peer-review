- Proves convergence of a general MCTS template to an ε‑Nash equilibrium in zero‑sum, perfect‑information simultaneous move games.  
- Uses regret‑minimizing procedures (ε‑Hannan consistent selection functions) as action selection mechanisms in the MCTS template.  
- Empirically validates theory using Regret Matching and Exp3 on random and worst‑case games, showing comparable convergence speeds to existing algorithms.  
- Fills a notable gap in MCTS theory by addressing simultaneous move games and situating itself against prior work on backward induction and domain‑specific methods.  
- Citations are relevant and comprehensive, covering MCTS, game theory, and online learning literature.  
- Main theoretical strength: novel formal convergence proof for simultaneous move MCTS.  
- Template generality: allows any ε‑Hannan consistent selector, enabling use of diverse regret‑minimization advances.  
- Presentation is clear, well‑structured, and provides a helpful proof sketch with detailed supplementary material.  
- Empirical strength: evaluates multiple selection rules and includes worst‑case game analysis to demonstrate versatility.  
- Key weakness: no concrete bounds on the rate of convergence are provided.  
- Assumption concern: reliance on ε‑Hannan consistency may be strong and its practical sensitivity is unexplored.  
- Practical tuning issue: exploration parameter (γ) effects and guidance for choosing it are not thoroughly addressed.  
- Empirical limitations: experiments are limited to random and worst‑case games; lacks complex real‑world evaluations.  
- Numerical concern: potential overflow issues with Exp3 noted, with only a brief workaround discussed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses open problem, novel and sound theory, supportive empirical evidence, and broad template applicability.  
- Arguments against acceptance: absence of convergence rate bounds and potentially restrictive ε‑Hannan consistency assumption.  
- Overall verdict: strong contribution that merits acceptance despite shortcomings; recommended for NIPS.  
- Recommendation: Accept. Confidence: 8/10.  
- Suggested improvements: derive convergence rate bounds, test robustness to assumption violations, give guidance on γ selection, evaluate on real‑world games, and expand on Exp3 numerical stability mitigations.
