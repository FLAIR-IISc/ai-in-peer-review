## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental open problem in game theory and AI: proving convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games.  The authors present a general template for SM-MCTS, leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection mechanisms. They formally demonstrate that, using an -Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium.  The theoretical results are complemented by empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3, showing convergence speeds comparable to existing SM-MCTS algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established use of MCTS in sequential games (e.g., Go) and recognizes the lack of theoretical guarantees when applied to simultaneous-move settings. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing approaches like backward induction with cutoff conditions (requiring domain knowledge and computationally expensive methods like linear programming) and UCB-based MCTS, which has been shown *not* to converge to Nash equilibrium in even simple simultaneous games (Shafiei et al., 2017, cited). The work appropriately situates itself within the broader literature of regret minimization and online learning, drawing on concepts like Hannan consistency and relating the analysis to previous work in those areas (Hart & Mas-Colell, 1996; Auer et al., 2002).  The connection to stacked matrix games provides a useful framing.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:**  The core strength of this paper is the formal proof of convergence.  Providing theoretical guarantees for MCTS in this challenging setting is a significant contribution. The breakdown of the proof, starting with simple matrix games and inductively extending to more complex extensive-form games, is well-structured.  The use of -Hannan consistency is a clever approach.
* **Generality:**  The proposed template is general, allowing for instantiation with different regret-minimizing procedures. This flexibility is a major advantage.
* **Empirical Validation:** While not the primary focus, the empirical results offer some confidence in the practical applicability of the theoretical findings. The comparison with existing algorithms and the analysis of worst-case scenarios are helpful.
* **Clear Presentation (Generally):** The paper is well-organized and the definitions are clearly presented. The algorithm description (Algorithm 1) is easy to follow.
* **Strong Connection to Relevant Literature:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of relevant work in game theory, online learning, and MCTS.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical analysis establishes *convergence* to an -Nash equilibrium, but provides no guarantees regarding the *rate* of convergence.  The empirical results provide some insights, but a more rigorous analysis of the convergence speed would be valuable.  The constant factors in the convergence guarantees (particularly the D^2 term) are likely quite large, raising concerns about practical performance.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of perfect information is strong. While justified as a starting point, extending the results to imperfect information games would be a significant advancement.
* **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. While the worst-case game analysis is useful, expanding the experiments to more diverse and realistic game domains would strengthen the claims. The comparison to existing algorithms is primarily focused on exploitability, and other metrics (e.g., computation time, memory usage) would be beneficial.
* **Numerical Stability:** The paper briefly mentions the numerical stability issue with the Exp3 implementation and the workaround used. A more detailed discussion of potential numerical challenges and mitigation strategies would be helpful.
* **Clarity in Specific Sections:** Section 4, while rigorous, can be difficult to follow for readers not deeply versed in online learning theory. More intuitive explanations of some of the lemmas and theorems would improve accessibility.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant theoretical contribution:** The formal proof of convergence is a major step forward in understanding the behavior of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
* **Generality of the approach:**  The template allows for flexibility in choosing the regret-minimizing procedure.
* **Potential impact:** The results could inspire new algorithm design and analysis in areas such as security games and multi-agent systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Lack of convergence rate analysis:**  The absence of concrete bounds on the convergence speed limits the practical implications of the results.
* **Strong assumptions:** The assumption of perfect information restricts the applicability of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field. The formal proof of convergence is a significant achievement, and the generality of the proposed template is a major advantage. While the lack of convergence rate analysis and the strong assumptions are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance, *conditional on minor revisions*. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept with minor revisions.**

**Specific Suggestions for Revision:**

* **Address the convergence rate issue:** At least discuss the challenges of obtaining tighter bounds and potential avenues for future research.
* **Expand the empirical evaluation:** Include experiments with more diverse game domains and additional performance metrics.
* **Improve clarity in Section 4:**  Add more intuitive explanations of the key lemmas and theorems.
* **Elaborate on numerical stability:** Provide a more detailed discussion of potential numerical challenges and mitigation strategies.
* **Consider adding a discussion section:** Briefly outline potential future directions, such as extending the results to imperfect information games.
