### Summary
This paper presents the first formal proof of convergence for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the domain of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for MCTS algorithms where the in-tree selection policy is based on a regret-minimizing procedure from online learning. The central theoretical contribution is proving that if this selection function is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (like Regret Matching or Exp3), the resulting MCTS algorithm's average strategy profile converges almost surely to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction on the game tree's depth, showing that convergence in the subgames (leaves) provides bounded-error value estimates for the parent nodes, which is sufficient for convergence to propagate up to the root. The paper supports its theoretical claims with an empirical analysis on random and worst-case games, comparing variants based on Regret Matching and Exp3.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper's main contribution is a theoretical one, and the analysis appears sound. The proof strategy is elegant, cleverly reducing the problem in the extensive-form game to a recursive application of convergence results in repeated matrix games with bounded error. The authors are transparent about a key simplifying assumption for their analysis—namely, that the tree is complete—and provide a reasonable justification for why this is acceptable for an asymptotic convergence proof. The experimental section, while not exhaustive, provides a valuable sanity check for the theory and offers an interesting insight: the variant of MCTS required for the proof (propagating mean rewards) converges slightly slower than the more common heuristic variant (propagating the last sample's reward).

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It provides the necessary background on simultaneous-move games and MCTS before methodically building up the theoretical argument. The decomposition of the proof is logical, starting from simple repeated matrix games, extending to games with erroneous payoffs, and finally applying these lemmas inductively to the full extensive-form game. The notation is dense but consistent, and the algorithms are clearly presented. This clarity is crucial for a paper with a significant theoretical component.

**Originality:** The originality of this work is exceptionally high. While MCTS has been applied to simultaneous-move games for years, it has been treated as a heuristic without any convergence guarantees. Previous work has even shown that standard MCTS variants like UCT *fail* to converge. This paper breaks new ground by providing the first-ever convergence proof, fundamentally changing the status of MCTS in this domain from a practical heuristic to a provably sound approximation algorithm. The core idea of leveraging the theory of Hannan consistency and regret minimization from the online learning literature as the engine for an MCTS selection policy is a powerful and novel conceptual synthesis. It opens a new and principled direction for designing MCTS algorithms in complex game settings.

**Significance:** The significance of this contribution is substantial. It addresses a major open question at the intersection of game theory, search, and online learning. By establishing a formal link between regret minimization and MCTS convergence, the paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms. Researchers and practitioners can now use MCTS in simultaneous-move games with confidence that it is converging towards a solution concept of interest. Furthermore, the general framework allows for plugging in other regret minimizers, suggesting a rich vein for future research in developing more efficient and better-performing MCTS variants with theoretical guarantees. This work is likely to be highly influential and to inspire follow-up research on tightening convergence bounds, extending the analysis to imperfect information games, and designing new algorithms based on this framework.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Major Theoretical Breakthrough:** The paper provides the first proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, solving a significant and long-standing open problem. This is a foundational result.
*   **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The connection drawn between Hannan-consistent online learning algorithms and the MCTS selection policy is a novel, powerful, and generalizable idea.
*   **High Significance and Impact:** The result provides a theoretical justification for using MCTS in a broad class of games, paving the way for more principled algorithm design and analysis. It bridges a critical gap between theory and practice.
*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The complex theoretical argument is presented with remarkable clarity, making a difficult topic accessible.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   **Simplifying Assumption in Proof:** The analysis sidesteps the dynamics of incremental tree growth by assuming a complete game tree. While acceptable for an asymptotic proof, it is a departure from how MCTS operates in practice.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are illustrative rather than comprehensive. They do not compare against other state-of-the-art solvers or use standard benchmark domains, focusing instead on demonstrating the convergence property.

### Recommendation
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The arguments against are minor technical and empirical limitations that pale in comparison to the novelty and significance of the core theoretical contribution. This paper tackles a difficult and important problem with a bold and elegant new idea, delivering a foundational result that fundamentally advances our understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search. It is exactly the type of high-impact, conceptually-driven research that NIPS should showcase. The weaknesses identified are natural avenues for future work, which is a hallmark of an important paper.
