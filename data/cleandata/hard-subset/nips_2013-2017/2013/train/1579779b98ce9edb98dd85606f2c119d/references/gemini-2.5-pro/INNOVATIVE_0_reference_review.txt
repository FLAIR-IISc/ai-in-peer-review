### Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary**

This paper addresses a significant and long-standing theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS and its variants are widely and successfully used in practice for simultaneous move games, there has been a notable lack of formal guarantees that these algorithms converge to a Nash Equilibrium (NE), a stark contrast to the well-understood theory in sequential games. The authors tackle this problem head-on by presenting a general template for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous move games. The core idea is to instantiate the action selection mechanism within each node of the search tree with a regret-minimizing procedure from the online learning literature.

The main contribution is a formal proof that any MCTS algorithm following this template, using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function that ensures sufficient exploration, is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is elegantly constructed via induction on the game tree's depth. It first establishes convergence for single matrix games (the leaves of the tree) and then shows that the value estimates propagated up the tree can be treated as payoffs from a matrix game with bounded error, allowing the convergence property to be extended level by level to the root. The paper empirically evaluates instances of this framework using Regret Matching and Exp3, confirming their convergence and comparing the theoretically required variant (propagating mean rewards) with the more common heuristic (propagating the last sampled reward).

**Strengths**

1.  **High Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and the importance of the problem it solves. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in the context of simultaneous move games. This is not an incremental improvement but a foundational result that bridges the gap between the widespread practical application of MCTS in this domain and its theoretical understanding. It places a popular heuristic on solid theoretical ground and opens the door for more rigorous analysis of simulation-based search in complex games.

2.  **Bold and Elegant Theoretical Approach:** The theoretical framework is impressive. The connection drawn between MCTS and online learning through the lens of Hannan consistency is both insightful and powerful. The key step of modeling the subproblems at inner nodes as "repeated matrix games with bounded error" is a clever abstraction that allows the authors to manage the stochasticity inherent in the MCTS process and apply results from regret minimization theory. This provides a clear and principled pathway for the inductive proof.

3.  **Generalizability of the Framework:** The paper does not just prove convergence for one specific algorithm but for a whole class of algorithms based on ε-Hannan consistency. This makes the result more impactful, as it provides a template for designing provably convergent MCTS variants and potentially for analyzing existing ones. The conjecture that many existing practical algorithms also converge and could be analyzed with this framework seems plausible and points to fertile ground for future work.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper's contribution is significant, there are some aspects that could be improved, though they do not detract from the core result.

1.  **Potentially Loose Theoretical Bounds:** The final convergence guarantee provides an approximation error ε' that grows with the depth of the game (proportional to D^2). While establishing *any* bound is a major achievement, this bound may be quite loose in practice, potentially limiting its direct applicability for predicting performance. This is a common feature of initial theoretical results in this area, but it is worth noting.

2.  **Analysis of the "Mean Propagation" Variant:** The proof requires propagating the mean of collected rewards (RMM) rather than the reward from the current simulation (RM), which is more common. The experiments honestly show that RMM converges slightly slower than RM. This is a fascinating result that hints at a subtle disconnect between the requirements of the current proof technique and the best-performing heuristics. The paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of why this might be the case and what it implies for future theoretical analyses.

3.  **Simplifying Assumption in Proof:** The formal analysis assumes the game tree is completely built before analyzing the convergence of the collected statistics. While the authors correctly argue that guaranteed exploration ensures this will happen eventually, the analysis sidesteps the dynamics of the initial tree-growing phase. This is a reasonable simplification for a first result, but a more complex analysis incorporating the tree expansion could provide a more complete picture.

**Arguments for Acceptance**
*   **Pro:** The paper presents a highly novel and significant theoretical result, providing the first-ever convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
*   **Pro:** The theoretical framework is elegant, general, and connects two important fields (tree search and online learning) in a new way.
*   **Pro:** The work is foundational and is very likely to inspire follow-up research on tightening bounds, extending the analysis to other game classes, and designing new algorithms.
*   **Con:** The derived theoretical bounds on the approximation quality are likely loose.
*   **Con:** The empirical evaluation, while sufficient to support the claims, is not exhaustive.

**Recommendation**

This paper is a standout submission. It is a bold work that tackles a difficult and important open problem with a novel and elegant approach. The contribution is foundational, providing the first formal justification for using a large class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games. While the theoretical bounds could be tightened and the experiments expanded, these are minor issues in the face of the core achievement. This work prioritizes a significant new concept over methodological perfection, exactly the kind of research that pushes the field forward. I strongly recommend acceptance.
