- Proves that MCTS with an ε-Hannan consistent selection function converges to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games.

- Addresses a longstanding open problem by providing the first rigorous convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.

- Proposes a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimizer.

- Theoretical proofs are well-structured and appear mathematically sound, with clear lemmas and theorems.

- Empirically validates results using Regret Matching and Exp3, showing convergence speeds comparable to existing approaches; includes useful worst-case game analysis.

- Paper is clearly written, well-organized, and definitions/background are accessible.

- Does not provide explicit theoretical convergence rate bounds; only empirical insights on speed.

- Relies on the strong assumption of ε-Hannan consistency, whose practical applicability across algorithms is not fully explored.

- Experimental scope is limited: few games tested and lacks comparisons to more sophisticated simultaneous-move MCTS algorithms.

- The mean vs. current sample value experiments feel tangential and their theoretical implications are underdeveloped.

- Mentions possible extensions to other algorithms but lacks concrete discussion of how to do so.

- Quality: technically sound; score 8/10.

- Clarity: well-written and organized; score 9/10.

- Originality: first rigorous convergence proof in this setting; score 9/10.

- Significance: provides important theoretical foundation for MCTS in these games; score 8/10.

- Arguments for acceptance: solves a long-standing problem, provides rigorous and general theory, empirical validation, and clear presentation.

- Arguments against acceptance: missing convergence rate bounds, strong assumption (ε-Hannan consistency), and limited empirical evaluation.

- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions.

- Requested revisions: discuss potential for deriving/approximating convergence rate bounds; expand empirical comparisons with state-of-the-art simultaneous-move MCTS; elaborate on extending the analysis to other MCTS variants.
