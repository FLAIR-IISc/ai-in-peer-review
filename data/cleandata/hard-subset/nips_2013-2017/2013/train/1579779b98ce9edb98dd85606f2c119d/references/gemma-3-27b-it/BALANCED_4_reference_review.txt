This paper presents a convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The authors demonstrate that, by employing a -Hannan consistent selection function within the MCTS framework, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The analysis is complemented by empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3 as instantiation of the -Hannan consistent selection functions.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, extending its established convergence guarantees—previously limited to two-player perfect-information sequential games—to the more complex setting of simultaneous moves. The connection to Hannan consistency is a clever and well-motivated choice, providing a robust theoretical foundation. The empirical results, while not groundbreaking, support the theoretical claims and demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing algorithms. The explicit acknowledgment of the limitations and conjecture about extending the analysis to other algorithms is also commendable. The presentation is generally clear, although dense at times, particularly in the formal analysis section.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength lies in the formal convergence proof. This is a significant contribution to the theoretical foundation of MCTS, broadening its applicability.
*   **Clear Connection to Existing Theory:** The use of -Hannan consistency provides a link to well-established concepts in online learning and game theory, grounding the analysis in existing theoretical frameworks.
*   **General Template:** The proposed MCTS template is general and can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures, offering flexibility.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not exhaustive, provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrating comparable performance.
*   **Thoroughness:** The paper provides a detailed analysis with clear definitions and notations, attempting to cover all necessary aspects of the proof.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Complexity of Formal Analysis:** The formal analysis (Section 4) is quite dense and challenging to follow for readers not intimately familiar with the relevant theoretical background. More intuitive explanations or intermediate steps would be helpful.
*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on randomly generated games and a specific worst-case scenario. More diverse game types and comparisons with state-of-the-art algorithms would strengthen the empirical validation. The experiments do not provide a clear advantage over existing MCTS algorithms.
*   **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical analysis guarantees convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, but it does not provide any information about the *rate* of convergence. The empirical results offer some insights, but a more detailed analysis of the convergence speed would be valuable.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The paper does not fully address practical considerations such as computational cost and memory usage, which could be significant in large-scale games.
*   **Redundancy/Verbosity:** Certain sections (e.g., definitions and background) contain information that might be considered standard knowledge for the NIPS audience, making the paper somewhat verbose.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result that advances our understanding of MCTS.
*   The connection to Hannan consistency is a clever and well-motivated choice.
*   The empirical results, while limited, provide support for the theoretical claims.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented, despite the complexity of the material.
*   Addresses an open problem in the MCTS literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The formal analysis is complex and may be inaccessible to some readers.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope.
*   The paper does not provide a clear advantage over existing MCTS algorithms in practice.
*   The lack of convergence rate analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The theoretical result is significant, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. However, the complexity of the formal analysis and the limited scope of the empirical evaluation are weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Simplify the Formal Analysis:** Provide more intuitive explanations and intermediate steps to make the analysis more accessible. Consider adding illustrative examples.
2.  **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Include a broader range of game types and comparisons with state-of-the-art algorithms. Investigate the convergence rate empirically.
3.  **Address Practical Considerations:** Discuss the computational cost and memory usage of the proposed approach.
4.  **Streamline the Presentation:** Remove redundant information and focus on the key contributions of the paper.
5.  Consider exploring the conjecture mentioned in the conclusion about extending the analysis to other algorithms used in practice (like UCB) and possibly including preliminary results.
