

**Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for simultaneous move games. While MCTS has been widely used in practice for such games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), no convergence guarantees were previously known. The authors present a general template for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games and prove that using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (like regret matching or Exp3) ensures convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. They complement this with empirical validation showing comparable convergence rates to existing methods.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, filling a critical gap in the literature. The framework is general and flexible, allowing instantiation with various regret minimization procedures. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with careful definitions and logical progression from matrix games to extensive-form games. The empirical validation supports the theoretical claims, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in practice.

**Weaknesses:**
The scope is limited to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, which restricts broader applicability. The empirical evaluation is confined to small games (depth 2, branching factor 2), which may not adequately demonstrate practical utility. The paper lacks a clear comparison with existing state-of-the-art algorithms for simultaneous move games, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of the theoretical results. The connection to prior work could be strengthened by more explicitly distinguishing the authors' contribution from existing MCTS variants.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound with rigorous proofs and well-supported claims. The empirical evaluation complements the theory but would be strengthened with more comprehensive experiments. The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. Mathematical notation is consistent, and the logical progression from definitions to main theorem is easy to follow. The empirical results are presented effectively with appropriate visualizations.

**Originality:** The paper makes a novel contribution by providing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, where none existed previously. While the use of Hannan consistency in MCTS is not entirely new, its application to simultaneous move games represents a significant advancement.

**Significance:** The theoretical results provide a strong foundation for using MCTS as an approximation algorithm for simultaneous move games, which is significant for both theoretical game theory and practical applications. However, the limited scope and lack of comparison with existing methods reduce the perceived significance.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The main weaknesses are the limited scope and narrow empirical evaluation, which could be addressed with additional experiments and discussion.

**Suggestions for improvement:**
1. Expand the empirical evaluation to include larger game instances (higher depth, larger branching factors) to better demonstrate practical utility.

2. Provide a more detailed comparison with existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games, particularly those mentioned in Shafiei et al. [17], to establish the practical significance of the theoretical results.

3. Discuss potential extensions to more general game classes (imperfect information, non-zero-sum) to better position the work's broader impact.

4. Clarify the relationship between the theoretical ε and the practical convergence rates observed in experiments.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of finite-time convergence bounds, as suggested in the conclusion, to strengthen the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important theoretical contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions addressing scope and empirical evaluation limitations, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. The work provides a solid foundation for understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games and opens promising avenues for future research.
