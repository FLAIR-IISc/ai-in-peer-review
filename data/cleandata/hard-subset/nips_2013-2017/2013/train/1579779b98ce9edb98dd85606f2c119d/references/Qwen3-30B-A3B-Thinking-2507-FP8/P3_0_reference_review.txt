

# NIPS Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in simultaneous move games, a class of games that includes many practical applications such as Goofspiel, Tron, and general game-playing competitions. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, proving that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures attempting each action infinitely many times), the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.

The paper builds upon the established connection between regret minimization and Nash equilibrium in matrix games (via Hannan consistency), and extends it to the more complex setting of extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. This is a notable contribution because, as the authors correctly point out, "no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general EFGs," despite the widespread practical use of MCTS variants in these domains. The work relates to but extends beyond previous MCTS convergence results, which have primarily focused on sequential games (like Go) where UCT provides convergence guarantees, and specifically addresses the gap left by Shafiei et al.'s observation that UCT fails to converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: This is the first paper to provide a formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The theoretical contribution is substantial and fills a critical gap in the literature. The authors build a well-structured proof that proceeds by induction over the depth of the game tree, demonstrating convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

2. **Clear Theoretical Framework**: The paper establishes a solid theoretical foundation by carefully defining the required properties of the selection algorithms (Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration) and demonstrating how these properties lead to convergence. The connection to online learning theory (Hannan consistency) is well-motivated and appropriately applied.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The work provides theoretical justification for the widespread practical use of MCTS in games with simultaneous moves, which has been largely empirical without theoretical grounding. This bridges an important gap between theory and practice.

4. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The empirical results complement the theoretical analysis well, demonstrating that the proposed algorithms (RMM and Exp3M) converge to approximate Nash equilibria with convergence rates comparable to existing methods. The experiments on worst-case games are particularly valuable for understanding the algorithm's behavior in challenging scenarios.

5. **Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is clearly organized, with careful definitions, logical flow of arguments, and appropriate use of notation. The connection between the theoretical analysis and empirical results is well-maintained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Results**: The paper only considers zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. While this is a significant contribution, the scope is limited compared to the broader field of extensive-form games. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion, noting future work on imperfect information games, but this limitation should be more explicitly discussed in the main text.

2. **Lack of Finite-Time Convergence Bounds**: While the paper proves asymptotic convergence, it doesn't provide finite-time convergence bounds, which are typically expected in theoretical papers on learning algorithms. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion as part of future work, but it's a notable limitation for a NIPS paper.

3. **Insufficient Empirical Comparison**: The empirical evaluation shows that the proposed algorithms converge to approximate equilibria, but doesn't provide quantitative comparisons with state-of-the-art methods for simultaneous move games. The statement that convergence rates are "comparable" to existing methods is vague without specific metrics.

4. **Ambiguity in Subgame Perfect Definition**: The paper uses the term "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" without clearly defining what this means for approximate equilibria. For exact equilibria, subgame perfection means that the strategy profile is a Nash equilibrium in every subgame. For approximate equilibria, this definition needs to be clarified (e.g., whether the approximation bound should hold in every subgame).

5. **Connection to UCT**: While the paper mentions UCT's failure to converge (citing Shafiei et al. [17]), it doesn't explicitly explain why UCT fails to satisfy the required properties (Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration), which would strengthen the theoretical argument.

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

### Arguments For Acceptance
- First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, filling a significant gap in the literature.
- Rigorous theoretical analysis that builds on established concepts (Hannan consistency) and extends them appropriately.
- Clear presentation that makes the theoretical contributions accessible to the NIPS community.
- Empirical results that validate the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical relevance.
- Addresses a problem of significant practical importance (simultaneous move games are common in many applications).

### Arguments Against Acceptance
- Limited scope to zero-sum perfect-information games, which excludes a large class of important game-theoretic problems.
- No finite-time convergence bounds, which is a standard expectation for theoretical papers on learning algorithms.
- Empirical evaluation lacks direct quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art methods for simultaneous move games.
- The paper doesn't fully address the practical implications of the theoretical results (e.g., how the choice of exploration parameter γ affects convergence).

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Clarify the Subgame Perfect Definition**: Explicitly define what "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" means in the context of approximate equilibria. For example, state that for every subgame rooted at h, the strategy profile must be an ε-Nash equilibrium of that subgame.

2. **Discuss the Relationship to UCT More Explicitly**: In Section 3.1, explain why UCT fails to satisfy the Hannan consistency property. This would strengthen the argument for why the proposed algorithms are theoretically justified.

3. **Provide More Detail on the Error Bound**: In Theorem 4.11, the error bound is D² + δ. Clarify how this bound is derived from the inductive step (e.g., how the error accumulates across levels of the game tree).

4. **Expand Empirical Comparison**: Include a table comparing convergence rates (e.g., number of iterations to reach exploitability < 0.01) between the proposed algorithms (RMM, Exp3M) and state-of-the-art methods for simultaneous move games.

5. **Discuss Practical Implications**: Add a paragraph discussing how the theoretical results might influence the practical choice of MCTS variants in applications with simultaneous move games. For example, explain when one might choose regret matching over Exp3 based on the theoretical guarantees.

6. **Address the "Mean Propagation" Paradox**: The paper shows that propagating the mean (RMM) converges slightly slower than propagating the current sample (RM), despite the theoretical analysis requiring mean propagation. Explain this apparent contradiction.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, well-structured, and well-motivated. While the scope is limited to zero-sum perfect-information games, this represents a substantial step forward in understanding the theoretical foundations of MCTS in a class of games where practical applications are widespread.

The main weaknesses are the lack of finite-time convergence bounds and limited empirical comparison with state-of-the-art methods. However, these are common limitations in theoretical papers, and the authors acknowledge them as future work.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it bridges theoretical game theory and practical algorithms, addresses a significant gap in the literature, and makes a clear, well-supported theoretical contribution. The work has the potential to influence both the theoretical understanding of MCTS and its practical application in simultaneous move games.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that addresses a significant gap in the literature. While there are some limitations in scope and empirical evaluation, the theoretical novelty and rigor are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings and would likely stimulate further theoretical work in the area.
