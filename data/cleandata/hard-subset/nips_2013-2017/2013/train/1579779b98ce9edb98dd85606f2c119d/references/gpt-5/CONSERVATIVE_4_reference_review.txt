Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where, at each state, action selection is driven by any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) learner (e.g., regret matching or Exp3) with guaranteed exploration. The core theoretical claim is that if returns backed up are means (rather than the last sample), then the average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε′–Nash equilibrium, with ε′ scaling as O(D^2 ε) where D is the game-tree depth. The proof proceeds by (i) recalling HC properties in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) inductively composing these bounds up the tree. Experiments compare two instantiations (RM and Exp3) and two backup schemes (sample vs mean) on small random and enumerated worst-case games. Mean backup converges slightly slower; all variants appear to converge empirically.

This connects MCTS to online learning results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Exp3), relates to negative results for UCB in simultaneous move games (Shafiei et al.), and sits alongside exact backward induction methods (with LP/do) and regret-minimization approaches such as CFR/MCCFR (for imperfect information EFGs).

Strengths
- Clear, modular theoretical framework: matrix-game convergence with HC learners, extension to bounded-error payoffs, and an induction argument over the tree.
- Subgame-perfect convergence (not just at the root) is a strong property for this class.
- The “template” approach is broad: many HC learners can be plugged in.
- The paper explains why naive UCB can fail and justifies the HC-based design.
- Empirical section, while small, at least checks the role of mean vs sample backups and reports convergence in worst-case small instances.

Weaknesses and concerns about methodological soundness
- Strong algorithmic departures from standard MCTS: the theory requires returning the mean value (not last simulation return) and guaranteed per-node exploration that forces every joint action to be visited infinitely often. Both are atypical in practice. The claim that “statistics collected during tree building cannot prevent eventual convergence” is central yet deferred to the appendix; in the main paper the argument is underdeveloped. It remains unclear exactly under what conditions the full tree is built a.s. and how node visitation frequencies interact with independent per-player exploration to ensure tij → ∞ at every node.
- Asymptotic-only guarantees with loose constants: the bound degrades quadratically with depth (D^2) and includes constants like 4(c+1). No finite-time bounds are provided, and the empirical section does not quantify sample complexity. As a result, practical relevance is limited.
- Empirical evaluation is weak: only very small toy games, no comparison to strong established baselines such as CFR/MCCFR (which has provable convergence in extensive-form settings and practical success), double-oracle/alpha-beta variants, or domain results (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) where SM-MCTS has been used. The experiments do not test deeper trees, larger branching factors, or sensitivity to exploration γ. Figures suggest millions of iterations even for depth-2 tiny games.
- Relationship to CFR/MCCFR is not carefully positioned. Since the proposed method uses per-node regret minimization with sampling, the connection and differences to MCCFR-style sampling (which has convergence proofs) should be discussed. As stated, the “first formal analysis” claim for simultaneous-move MCTS may be accurate in this exact setting, but the overlap with regret-minimization in EFGs warrants a deeper comparison.
- Some technical details are underspecified in the main text: the exact RetVal definition at each level (child vs node), the handling of exploration in updates (A* modification), and the proof that early-phase rollouts do not bias asymptotic convergence.
- Clarity/notation issues: a few formatting glitches (e.g., σs(h,a) definition “oth.”), minor typos, and reliance on appendix for crucial arguments.

Quality
The core theoretical direction is sensible and mostly sound as far as presented, but key steps (tree growth, guaranteed visits, mean-backup necessity) are only asserted or relegated to the appendix. The lack of finite-time analysis and the weak experimental support against established baselines limit confidence in the practical impact.

Clarity
Generally readable and organized, but important assumptions and their necessity (mean backup, exploration) should be stated more prominently and contrasted with standard MCTS practice. A crisper statement of the final ε as a function of D, γ, and the HC parameter would help. Explicate how exploitability is computed in experiments.

Originality
Moderately novel within the narrow setting of perfect-information simultaneous-move games for MCTS. The reliance on HC selection and induction over depth is a natural extension of known results; the value is in assembling these pieces for SM-MCTS. However, the overlap with CFR/MCCFR suggests the conceptual novelty is incremental.

Significance
Potentially useful for theory-minded work on simultaneous-move games, but practical significance is limited by asymptotic nature, unfavorable depth dependence, and nonstandard algorithmic requirements. The experimental section does not demonstrate superiority or even parity to established methods on meaningful benchmarks.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide a precise theorem (in the main paper) showing that with your exploration mechanism the full tree is expanded and every node/joint action is visited infinitely often a.s. Clarify assumptions about rollout policies.
- Can you prove convergence with standard sample backups? If not, explain where the proof breaks and whether variance-reduction or averaging windows would suffice.
- Give finite-time convergence bounds leveraging known rates for RM/Exp3, even if loose.
- Compare against MCCFR and/or CFR on small simultaneous-move benchmarks; discuss similarities/differences formally.
- Quantify the dependence of the final ε on D, branching factor, and γ; is D^2 tight?
- Include larger/deeper games and sensitivity analyses for γ.
- Improve clarity of Algorithm 1 and the RetVal variants; fix notation/typos.

Pros
- Theoretical framework connecting HC learners to SM-MCTS convergence.
- Subgame-perfect asymptotic guarantee.
- Simple, general template.

Cons
- Requires nonstandard mean backup and strong exploration assumptions.
- Only asymptotic, depth-quadratic bounds; no finite-time rates.
- Minimal experiments, no strong baselines, small toy games.
- Key proofs/assumptions deferred; relation to CFR/MCCFR underexplored.

Overall score: 5 (weak reject)
