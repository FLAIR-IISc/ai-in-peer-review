Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). While exact methods (backward induction with alpha–beta pruning, double oracle) exist, they require evaluation functions or linear programs and are often impractical. In practice, SM-MCTS variants are used, but there are no convergence guarantees to Nash equilibrium (NE) in this setting; UCT can fail to converge even in one-shot simultaneous-move games.

The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template where the selection policy at each node is any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3 with exploration). They analyze the algorithm under a “mean backup” variant (propagating node means rather than the latest sample) and prove that, with probability 1, the empirical/average strategies converge to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The analysis proceeds by (i) proving asymptotic equilibrium properties in repeated matrix games when both players are ε-HC, (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) inductively composing errors across tree levels. Empirically, they compare regret matching and Exp3 with and without mean backups on random and small worst-case games, finding both variants converge, with mean backups slightly slower.

This connects to: (i) online learning/HC and convergence to (approximate) equilibrium in repeated games (Hart and Mas-Colell; Auer et al.), (ii) MCTS theory in perfect-information sequential games (Kocsis & Szepesvári), (iii) negative results for UCT in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.), and (iv) exact search/pruning methods for simultaneous moves (Saffidine et al.; Bošanský et al.). To my knowledge, a proof that SM-MCTS converges to (approximate) NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs under HC selection is novel.

Strengths:
- Clear theoretical contribution: the first asymptotic convergence guarantee for a broad class of SM-MCTS algorithms to subgame-perfect approximate NE in this game class.
- Generality: the template accommodates any ε-HC selection algorithm with guaranteed exploration; proofs are modular via repeated games with bounded errors and induction over depth.
- Correct emphasis on exploration and its effect on asymptotics; the paper formalizes the role of mean backups in the analysis.
- Empirical support: tests with regret matching and Exp3 on random and hardest small games show convergence trends consistent with the theory.
- Useful to practitioners: justifies the common use of SM-MCTS in domains like Goofspiel and pursuit-evasion, and suggests which selection policies are safe.

Weaknesses:
Quality
- The main theorem requires mean backups (return Xh/nh), while the more common sample backup has no proof. Experiments suggest sample backups also converge, but a formal bridge is missing.
- The asymptotic bound scales as ε D^2 (and with additional constant factors like 4(c+1) from intermediate lemmas). This seems loose; the argument that deviations accumulate as 2d per level and sum to D^2 may be conservative. Tightening to O(D) or better would materially strengthen the result.
- No finite-time rates. Since HC algorithms admit regret bounds, mapping them to convergence rates (with explicit dependence on depth, branching factor, and exploration γ) would improve significance.
- The role of fixed exploration γ is two-edged: it ensures guaranteed exploration but induces an asymptotic exploitability floor. The theory does not cover decaying exploration; practical algorithms often anneal γ to zero to remove bias.
- The algorithmic description is sometimes ambiguous. For RM, Select appears to choose joint actions, whereas Exp3 samples independently per player; Algorithm 1 has Select returning a joint action. The regret update uses x(h,i,j)=Xhij for unplayed pairs, which is unconventional and deserves justification. Clarifying independence vs. correlation and what state is maintained per joint/action would improve reproducibility.

Clarity
- The paper is generally readable, but notation is heavy and some statements (e.g., Lemma 4.5) rely on nontrivial martingale arguments that are deferred to an appendix. Some pseudo-code and update rules could be specified more precisely for both players.
- Several references are incomplete or inconsistently formatted.

Originality
- Novel convergence analysis for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move EFGs under HC selection. The approach of reducing to repeated games with bounded errors and lifting via induction is elegant and, to my knowledge, new in this context.

Significance
- The result fills a notable theoretical gap and can guide algorithm selection/design in simultaneous-move domains. However, the lack of rates and the need for mean backups limit immediate practical impact. Experiments are on small/random games; evaluation on standard benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel with standard scoring, Tron variants) and comparisons to other strong baselines (e.g., CFR-based methods, double-oracle search, serialized alpha–beta) would strengthen the empirical case.

Constructive suggestions:
- Extend the analysis to the sample-backup variant or identify conditions under which sample backups inherit the convergence guarantee. Alternatively, propose a hybrid backup with provable properties.
- Investigate decaying exploration schedules and prove convergence while mitigating asymptotic bias; analyze how guaranteed exploration can be maintained (e.g., via diminishing but summable exploration).
- Tighten the D^2 dependence; provide a careful error-propagation argument that may yield O(D) or logarithmic-in-depth constants.
- Provide finite-time bounds by combining regret rates (e.g., O(1/√t)) with error accumulation across levels; even pessimistic rates would be valuable.
- Clarify algorithmic details: whether Select samples independently per player, what information is updated on unplayed pairs, and how the two-player perspectives synchronize updates. A precise description of data structures per node (action and joint-action statistics) would help.
- Expand experiments: include real simultaneous-move benchmarks and compare against recent SM-MCTS heuristics and exact search with pruning; report wall-clock/sample complexity; ablate γ and depth/branching systematically.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First convergence guarantee for a widely used algorithmic template in simultaneous-move EFGs.
- General, modular theory likely to influence future work and unify practice.
- Subgame-perfect equilibrium notion is strong and appropriate.
- Empirical results are consistent with theory.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Asymptotic only; no rates; loose depth dependence.
- Requires mean backups; theory does not match common practice fully.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and baselines.
- Some clarity/rigor gaps in algorithm description and proofs deferred to appendix.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is clear and novel for NeurIPS, despite limitations. Strengthening clarity and empirical evaluation in the camera-ready would further improve the paper.
