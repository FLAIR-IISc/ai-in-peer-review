

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors present a general MCTS template that can use any ε-Hannan consistent selection procedure, proving that such algorithms converge to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibria. The work builds on established connections between online learning algorithms (like regret matching and Exp3) and Nash equilibrium computation in matrix games, extending these to the more complex setting of simultaneous move extensive-form games.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a substantial contribution by providing the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The proof structure is sound, with careful inductive reasoning across game tree levels, and the connection to Hannan consistency is well-motivated.
2. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Definitions, notation, and proofs are presented clearly, with good motivation for each step. The distinction between matrix games and extensive-form games is particularly well-explained.
3. **Empirical Support:** The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical results well, showing that the proposed approach converges to approximate equilibria in practice. The analysis of mean propagation versus current sample values is particularly insightful.
4. **Proper Contextualization:** The paper effectively situates its contribution within the existing literature on game theory and MCTS, correctly identifying the gap in prior work regarding convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Lack of Convergence Rate Bounds:** While the paper proves eventual convergence, it does not provide any concrete bounds on the convergence rate. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, as practitioners need to know how many iterations are required to reach a desired level of accuracy.
2. **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses on relatively small games (depth 2, branching factor 2), which may not reflect the behavior of the algorithms in larger, more complex games where MCTS is typically applied. The paper claims convergence rates are "comparable" to other algorithms but doesn't provide detailed comparisons.
3. **Underdeveloped Motivation for Previous Failures:** The paper mentions that UCT does not converge to Nash equilibrium (citing Shafiei et al.), but doesn't explain why this is the case or how the authors' approach overcomes these limitations in a way that previous work didn't.
4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the theoretical contribution is strong, the paper doesn't adequately discuss how these results might guide practitioners in selecting appropriate algorithms or parameters for real-world applications.

**Quality:** The theoretical contribution is strong, but the lack of convergence rate bounds limits the practical utility. The empirical evaluation is appropriate for the theoretical claims but too limited in scope to fully validate the practical relevance.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The mathematical presentation is precise and accessible to readers familiar with game theory and online learning.

**Originality:** This is a novel contribution to the field. While the connection between Hannan consistency and game-theoretic equilibria is known, the application to MCTS in simultaneous move games represents a significant new direction.

**Significance:** The theoretical contribution is significant for algorithmic game theory, but the practical impact is limited by the lack of convergence rate bounds and the small-scale empirical evaluation. The paper would be substantially more impactful if it could provide more concrete guidance for practitioners.

**Recommendation and Suggestions for Improvement:**

This is a strong theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. While the paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is sound, it would benefit from the following improvements:

1. **Add Convergence Rate Bounds:** Provide theoretical bounds on the number of iterations required to reach a desired level of accuracy. This would significantly strengthen the practical relevance of the work.

2. **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Include experiments on larger games that better reflect practical applications of MCTS. Comparing the proposed approach to specific prior algorithms (beyond just "recently proposed MCTS algorithms") would be valuable.

3. **Clarify Practical Motivation:** More thoroughly explain why previous MCTS approaches fail to converge to Nash equilibrium and how the proposed approach overcomes these limitations.

4. **Discuss Parameter Sensitivity:** Analyze how the convergence rate is affected by parameters like the exploration rate γ, and provide practical guidance on parameter selection.

5. **Include Comparison to Other Approaches:** Provide a more detailed comparison with existing algorithms that claim to handle simultaneous move games, rather than just stating that convergence rates are "comparable."

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution that should be of interest to the NeurIPS community, particularly those working at the intersection of game theory and reinforcement learning. With the suggested improvements, particularly regarding convergence rate analysis and empirical validation on more complex games, this work would be even stronger. As it stands, it represents a significant step forward in understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games, though the practical utility would be enhanced with the additional analysis mentioned above.
