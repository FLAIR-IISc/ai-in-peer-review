Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). While UCT has strong empirical performance in sequential perfect-information games, convergence guarantees in simultaneous-move settings are lacking and UCB-based selection can fail to converge to Nash even in one-shot games. The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template where, at each node, action selection for each player is governed by any ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) no-regret procedure with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3). They prove that if backups use mean returns (rather than single-sample returns), then the induced play converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect (D^2 ε + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the game-tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The proof proceeds by: (i) recalling convergence of ε-HC to (approximate) minimax in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending this to repeated matrix games with bounded observation error; and (iii) inductively composing these bounds up the tree to obtain the D^2 factor. Empirically, they compare mean-backup versus sample-backup variants with RM and Exp3 on random small games and an exhaustive set of tiny worst-case games, observing convergence in practice in both cases and slightly slower convergence with mean backups.

Quality
- Theory: The main convergence result is sound under the stated assumptions. The reduction to repeated games with bounded error is a natural and useful step, and the requirement of exploration is standard. The subgame-perfect conclusion is a nice strengthening. However:
  - Constants/notation: Several lemmas (e.g., 4.6 and 4.9) appear to omit factors of ε in the bounds (e.g., “v + 2” rather than “v + 2ε”), while Theorem 4.11 includes ε. Please clarify these constants consistently.
  - Backup rule: The proof critically relies on backing up mean returns (SM-MCTS-M). This diverges from common MCTS practice (single-sample backup), and while experiments suggest similar behavior empirically, the current theory does not cover the standard variant. It would be valuable to either extend the proof to sample backups or spell out precisely where the proof breaks and what additional assumptions would suffice.
  - Depth dependence: The D^2 ε bound is quite loose. The intuition given (“players can gain 2d by deviating” and summing over levels) suggests a cumulative error that might be tightened with a sharper induction or value-propagation analysis. It would strengthen the contribution to discuss whether an O(D ε) bound is possible or to provide a lower bound indicating D^2 is unavoidable under your assumptions.
  - Tree completion: The argument that the tree is eventually fully expanded with probability 1 under guaranteed exploration is plausible but not formalized in the main text. Given the dependence on visiting every joint action infinitely often at each node, a short, explicit argument (or pointer to a lemma in the appendix) would help.

- Experiments: The experiments are limited but consistent with the theory. They demonstrate:
  - Mean backups slightly slow convergence relative to sample backups.
  - Convergence on random small games and tiny worst-case constructions.
  However, they do not probe scalability, compare against strong baselines (e.g., alpha-beta with LP/DO pruning, or CFR-style methods adapted to this setting), or evaluate on standard simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) beyond prior citations. Measuring exploitability only for player 1 and fixing γ leads to an exploitability floor; it would be better to also report the symmetric measure and show how decreasing exploration affects convergence.

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable. The problem setting and algorithm template are clearly introduced. The analysis is outlined in the main text with proofs deferred. That said:
- Be consistent with ε throughout lemmas and theorems.
- Pseudocode could be clearer about backup choice (mean vs sample) and the role of independent selection per player in SM-MCTS.
- Define precisely the “guaranteed exploration” mixing and how it interacts with regret updates (you define A*; good) and clarify whether exploration actions update the regret learner or are off-policy samples (line with “without updating” is important; reinforce in experiments).

Originality
The paper provides, to my knowledge, the first formal asymptotic convergence guarantees for MCTS-style algorithms in perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, under broad classes of no-regret selection rules. The decomposition via repeated games with bounded error is a neat angle. While the use of Hannan-consistent learners to obtain minimax behavior in repeated games is classical, its integration into an MCTS template with a subgame-perfect convergence claim is novel.

Significance
Establishing convergence of a widely used algorithmic template in an important class of games addresses a real gap between practice and theory. This can influence future algorithm design for simultaneous-move planning and general game playing. The current bounds are asymptotic and somewhat weak, and the reliance on mean backups limits immediate practical impact; nonetheless, the result is a meaningful theoretical step that others can build on (e.g., tightening bounds, extending to sample backups, finite-time rates, and imperfect information).

Pros
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad no-regret selectors.
- Clear, modular analysis via repeated games with bounded error.
- Covers commonly used learners (RM, Exp3) with guaranteed exploration.
- Empirical results consistent with theory and provide useful insights about backup choices.

Cons
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time rates or practical guidance on iteration complexity.
- Requires mean backups; theory does not cover the standard sample-backup variant.
- Depth dependence D^2 ε likely loose; no discussion of tightness.
- Experiments are small-scale and lack comparisons to strong baselines on standard benchmarks.
- Some notational inconsistencies and missing constants in statements.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify constants involving ε in Lemmas 4.6 and 4.9 and ensure consistency across results.
- Either extend the analysis to sample backups or explicitly characterize additional conditions under which sample backups would inherit convergence.
- Investigate whether the D^2 factor can be improved, or provide a lower bound argument if not.
- Strengthen experiments: include larger instances, standard benchmarks, and comparisons to alpha-beta with pruning methods and CFR-like baselines; report both players’ exploitabilities and explore annealed exploration schedules.
- Provide a concise formal argument (or pointer) that guaranteed exploration implies all joint actions at each node are visited infinitely often and the full tree is eventually built.

Overall evaluation
A solid theoretical contribution addressing an important gap, with clear potential for follow-up work. Despite limitations in empirical scope and some technical caveats (mean backups, loose bounds), I lean toward acceptance, provided the presentation issues are addressed and the limitations are clearly acknowledged.
