This paper presents the first formal proof of convergence for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection policy is based on a regret-minimizing algorithm. The central theoretical result is that if the selection algorithm is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge to a subgame perfect $\epsilon'$-Nash equilibrium. This work builds upon the extensive literature on MCTS (e.g., UCT by Kocsis & Szepesvári) and regret minimization in online learning (e.g., Regret Matching by Hart & Mas-Colell; Exp3 by Auer et al.). It provides a crucial theoretical justification for applying simulation-based methods to this game class, a setting where standard MCTS variants like UCT are known to fail to converge.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The primary contribution of this paper is highly significant. While MCTS and its variants have been successfully applied empirically to simultaneous move games, a formal guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been a major open question. This paper provides the first such proof, bridging the gap between the practical success of MCTS and the theoretical foundations of algorithmic game theory. This result is a non-trivial extension of MCTS theory beyond the standard sequential setting.

2.  **Sound Technical Approach:** The proof strategy is elegant and well-structured. By leveraging the property of $\epsilon$-Hannan consistency from online learning, the authors establish a powerful connection. The inductive proof is methodologically sound: it begins by analyzing repeated single-stage matrix games (Lemma 4.6), extends this to games with bounded payoff errors (Lemma 4.9), and then uses this result to inductively climb the game tree from the leaves to the root, proving convergence to a subgame perfect equilibrium (Theorem 4.11). This decomposition of a complex problem into manageable parts is a core strength of the analysis.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It clearly introduces the problem domain, defines the necessary concepts from game theory, presents the SM-MCTS algorithm template, and outlines the main steps of the formal analysis. The distinction between propagating the current sample's value versus the mean value is clearly articulated and is central to both the theory and experiments.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Finite-Time Bounds:** The main convergence result is asymptotic. While proving eventual convergence is a crucial first step, the analysis provides no information on the *rate* of convergence. The final bound on the approximation quality ($\epsilon' = O(\epsilon D^2)$) depends on the game's depth $D$ but not on the number of iterations. A finite-time analysis, providing an error bound as a function of the computational budget (number of simulations), would be a substantially stronger and more practical result. This would allow for a principled comparison between different regret minimizers within the MCTS framework.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The formal analysis requires propagating the *mean* of all previous samples from a child node (the RMM/Exp3M variants). However, the empirical results show that the more standard approach of propagating the *current* sample's value (RM/Exp3) converges faster in all tested cases. This creates a disconnect: the algorithm proven to work is empirically inferior to a variant that lacks a proof. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper discussion of this phenomenon. Why is the current-sample variant harder to analyze? Is it due to the non-stationarity of the rewards observed by the parent node? The conjecture that the proofs can be extended is interesting but requires more justification to be convincing.

3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on randomly generated games and a small set of "worst-case" games found through a limited brute-force search. While useful for validating the theory, the evaluation would be more impactful if performed on well-known benchmark problems from the literature, such as Goofspiel, which the authors themselves mention in the introduction. This would allow for a direct comparison with prior empirical work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2012) and provide a better sense of the algorithm's practical performance.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues:**
    *   The regret update rule for Regret Matching is slightly ambiguous. The definition "x(h, i, j) = Xhij if (i, j) != (i1, j2), or u1 otherwise" uses the term `Xhij` which is not explicitly defined. I assume it refers to the estimated value of the child node `h_ij`, likely $X_{h_{ij}}/n_{h_{ij}}$, but this should be clarified for reproducibility.
    *   The proof sketch relies on the assumption of a pre-built complete tree, arguing that the statistics gathered during tree expansion do not prevent convergence. While this is intuitive, a more formal argument, even a brief one, would solidify the proof's foundation.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*   **Pro:** First formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a significant and novel theoretical contribution.
*   **Pro:** Elegant and sound proof technique connecting MCTS to Hannan consistency and online learning.
*   **Pro:** The paper is clearly written and presents a generalizable algorithmic template.
*   **Con:** The theoretical results are asymptotic, lacking finite-time convergence rates, which limits their practical applicability.
*   **Con:** A notable gap exists between the theoretically analyzed algorithm (mean-propagation) and the empirically superior one (sample-propagation), which is not fully explained.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation could be strengthened by using standard benchmark games.

**Recommendation:**

Despite the weaknesses, which primarily point toward avenues for important future work, the core contribution of this paper is strong, novel, and significant. Providing the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in this important class of games is a substantial achievement that will be of great interest to both the game theory and machine learning communities. Therefore, I recommend acceptance. The authors are encouraged to address the minor clarity issues and consider expanding the discussion on the theory-practice gap in the final version.
