

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper proposes a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that uses any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as the selection function. They prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function, the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The authors empirically evaluate their approach using regret matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence to approximate Nash equilibria.

## Strengths
- The paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, which has been primarily studied through empirical means.
- The formal analysis is generally sound, with clear definitions and logical progression from matrix games to multi-stage games.
- The experimental evaluation provides useful empirical evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

## Weaknesses
### Technical and Methodological Issues
1. **Inconsistent mathematical notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, such as "h0" versus "h_0" and "vmin" versus "v_{min}". In Section 4.3, Definition 4.8 contains a grammatical error: "almost surely exists t0 ∈ N" should be "there almost surely exists t0 ∈ N".

2. **Formal analysis flaws**: The paper claims to prove convergence to "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" (Theorem 4.11), but the proof only establishes convergence at the root node, not subgame perfection. The phrase "subgame perfect" appears to be misapplied throughout the paper.

3. **Inconsistent algorithm description**: The paper states "the standard way of defining the value to send back is RetVal(u1, Xh, nh) = u1" but later uses RetVal(u1, Xh, nh) = Xh/nh in the formal analysis. Algorithm 1 does not reflect this distinction, creating confusion.

4. **Incomplete proof of Lemma 4.5**: The claim that "empirical frequencies and average strategies will almost surely be equal" is incorrect. The martingale Strong Law of Large Numbers does not apply to adaptive strategies, and the authors provide no proof of this claim.

5. **Theoretical bound error**: The bound in Theorem 4.11 is stated as "D^2 + δ" but the paper uses "D" for depth (not "d" as mentioned in the proof sketch), creating inconsistency. The proof sketch mentions "d" as the level from the leaf nodes, but the bound is expressed in terms of "D" (maximal depth).

### Experimental and Presentation Issues
1. **Limited experimental scope**: The empirical analysis is restricted to depth-2 games with branching factor 2, which is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions about the algorithm's performance on larger games.

2. **Missing comparison**: The paper claims "the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on our template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms" but provides no actual comparison to other MCTS methods in the experiments.

3. **Incomplete references**: The reference list contains numerous entries with missing information (e.g., " , , , "), which suggests poor preparation.

4. **Ambiguous terminology**: The paper uses "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" without clearly defining how it differs from a regular ε-Nash equilibrium. The formal analysis does not establish subgame perfection, which is a stronger condition.

5. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper states "we conjecture that many of these algorithms also converge to -Nash equilibrium" without providing any evidence or supporting reasoning.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses an important theoretical gap in MCTS for simultaneous move games, the numerous technical inconsistencies and methodological flaws significantly undermine the contribution. The formal analysis contains critical errors in both the statements of results and the proofs, particularly regarding the subgame perfection claim. The experimental evaluation is too limited to support the broader claims made in the paper.

The paper's main contribution (a formal analysis of MCTS in simultaneous move games) is valuable, but the execution is flawed. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address the mathematical inconsistencies, clarify the theoretical claims, and expand the empirical evaluation to cover a more representative set of games.

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper contains too many technical errors and inconsistencies to be considered for publication at NIPS. While the general idea is promising, the execution lacks the precision and rigor expected in a NIPS paper. The errors in the formal analysis (particularly regarding the subgame perfection claim) are serious enough to invalidate the main contribution. The limited experimental evaluation further weakens the paper's claims. A revised version addressing these issues would be appropriate for resubmission, but as it stands, the paper does not meet NIPS standards for technical rigor.
