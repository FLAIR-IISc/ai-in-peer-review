Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that, at each node, uses any -Hannan-consistent selection rule with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) and, crucially for the theory, backpropagates mean returns rather than the most recent sample. The main theoretical result shows almost-sure convergence of the induced play to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with an approximation that scales as O(D^2) in the game depth D (Theorem 4.11). The analysis proceeds by (i) standard no-regret convergence to NE in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded, vanishing bias; and (iii) inductively propagating bounded errors up the tree. Empirically, the paper compares “mean-return” backpropagation vs. the standard “sample-return” variant with regret matching and Exp3 on random and small worst-case games, observing convergence in both, with mean-return slightly slower.

This work is positioned against traditional backward induction (with LP or double-oracle pruning) for simultaneous-move games and against UCT-style MCTS, for which negative convergence examples exist in simultaneous-move settings. It connects to online learning (Hannan consistency, Exp3, regret matching) and simulation-based search. It is related to regret-minimization in EFGs (e.g., CFR/MCCFR) but targets a different class (perfect information with simultaneous moves) and a different algorithmic template (tree search rather than traversals with counterfactual regrets).

Strengths
- Novel theory: To my knowledge, this is the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move EFGs, yielding subgame-perfect approximate NE under broadly applicable assumptions (no-regret selection + exploration).
- Generality: The template accommodates any -Hannan-consistent bandit/online learner (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), making the result reusable and extensible.
- Clean proof structure: The decomposition via repeated matrix games with bounded error is clear, and the inductive reasoning to the root is sound at a high level.
- Empirical sanity checks: Experiments confirm convergence in practice, including for the more common sample-return variant (albeit without a proof).

Weaknesses and detailed assessment
Quality
- The approximation bound is loose. The final error scales as O(D^2)ε (Theorem 4.11) and includes multiplicative constants (e.g., 4(c+1)) from intermediate lemmas. This may be an artifact of worst-case compounding, but it makes the guarantee conservative and of unclear practical bite.
- The mean-return backprop assumption is nonstandard in MCTS. Most implementations update node means but pass sample returns up the path. The proof requires using mean returns as RetVal, and while experiments suggest the standard variant also converges, no analysis is provided for it.
- The “guaranteed exploration” requirement (every joint action visited infinitely often) is very strong and can be debilitating in large branching factors; it also drives slow convergence and may not reflect practical exploration schedules.
- No finite-time or high-probability rates are provided. The asymptotic almost-sure result is valuable but of limited guidance for practitioners.

Clarity
- Overall clear, but some notation is inconsistent: σ̂ is used for empirical frequencies and σ̄ for averages; Theorem 4.11 states “average strategies (σ̂1(t), σ̂2(t)),” which seems to conflate hats and bars. Please clarify precisely which strategies converge and unify notation.
- The role of Definition 4.3 (off-policy uniform exploration without updating) vs. the intrinsic exploration of RM/Exp3 is a bit confusing. In some places the algorithms already guarantee exploration; in others A* is introduced. Tighten the exposition and justify not-updating during exploration.
- Several technical steps are deferred to the appendix; some intuition for the D^2 factor and the constants would help in the main text.

Originality
- Strong on the specific target: convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games appears new.
- That said, the connection to regret-minimization in EFGs (e.g., CFR, outcome sampling MCCFR) is under-cited and under-discussed. While those methods address imperfect-information games and use different sampling/traversal, they are relevant baselines and conceptual relatives.

Significance
- The result advances the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a nontrivial class of games and justifies practices that use regret-based selection in simultaneous-move domains.
- Practical impact is tempered by the loose error bounds, strong exploration assumption, and the need for mean-return backprop to use the theory as-is.
- Experimental evaluation is limited to small random/worst-case games; no tests on established simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) with exploitability computed by exact solution when feasible.

Questions for the authors
- Can the D^2 dependence be improved to O(D) with a sharper induction, or by using concentration (to keep error accumulation sublinear)?
- Is there a path to analyze the standard sample-return backprop variant? Even a partial result would improve practical relevance.
- How sensitive are the results to exploration schedules (e.g., decaying γ)? Can guaranteed exploration be weakened to ensure only action-level infinite visitation rather than every joint action?
- Why ignore updates during uniform exploration in A*? For RM, exploration is typically “on-policy”; for Exp3, exploration is integrated into the distribution.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add discussion and citations connecting to CFR/MCCFR and recent regret-minimization in EFGs; contrast assumptions and guarantees.
- Provide finite-time bounds (even loose) or high-probability statements for the repeated matrix game with bounded error, to give practitioners guidance on sample complexity.
- Unify notation (empirical vs. average strategies) and explicitly state which strategy profile converges in each theorem.
- Expand experiments: include at least one real simultaneous-move domain (e.g., small Goofspiel, Tron variants) and report exploitability against exact or high-quality solvers; include sensitivity to γ and selection algorithm.
- Explore weaker exploration assumptions (e.g., independent exploration per player) and analyze whether joint-action infinite visitation is necessary.

Pros
- First convergence proof for SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE.
- General template with multiple Hannan-consistent selectors.
- Clear theoretical framing via repeated games with bounded error.

Cons
- Loose asymptotic bounds with D^2 scaling; no finite-time rates.
- Requires mean-return backprop; standard practice unproven.
- Strong exploration assumptions; limited and small-scale experiments; missing CFR-related discussion.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The theoretical contribution is interesting and relevant, but the current version has limited experimental validation, strong assumptions that reduce practical impact, and could better situate itself among regret-minimization methods in EFGs. I encourage the authors to tighten bounds, clarify the algorithmic variants, relate to CFR/MCCFR, and expand experiments; with these, this could become a strong paper. Confidence: high.
