### NIPS Review

**Paper Title:** Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

**Summary:**
This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS is widely used in practice for various games, formal convergence guarantees have been largely confined to two-player, zero-sum games with sequential moves. This work focuses on the class of perfect-information, zero-sum games with simultaneous moves (also called "stacked matrix games"). The authors present a general template for a Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) algorithm where the in-tree action selection policy is based on any regret-minimizing procedure that is "ε-Hannan consistent." The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this class of algorithms converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively on the depth of the game tree, showing that regret-minimizing agents playing a matrix game with noisy payoffs (coming from the subgames below) will still converge to an approximate equilibrium. The authors empirically evaluate their proposed framework using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, demonstrating convergence and comparing a theoretically-required "mean-propagation" update rule with the more common "single-sample" update rule.

**Detailed Comments:**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The central theoretical claim—that SM-MCTS using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium—is well-supported by the provided formal analysis. The proof structure is sound and elegant, proceeding by induction over the depth of the game tree. It starts from the known convergence properties of regret minimizers in single matrix games (Lemma 4.6), extends this to games with bounded error in payoffs (Lemma 4.9), and then applies this result inductively to the game tree, where the values from child nodes are treated as noisy payoffs. This is a complete and rigorous piece of work.

However, there are a few points that temper the quality of the theoretical result:
1.  **Asymptotic Convergence:** The proof guarantees eventual convergence, but provides no finite-time bounds on the convergence rate. This is a common feature of such proofs but limits the practical utility of the guarantee. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a key limitation.
2.  **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The formal analysis requires that the value propagated up the tree is the *mean* of all rewards from a child node (the "M" variants like RMM). However, the empirical results show that propagating the value of the *current sample* (the standard RM variant) converges faster. The authors conjecture that their proof could be extended to the standard case but do not provide a sketch or intuition for how this would be done. This creates a disconnect: the theory justifies a slightly suboptimal variant of the algorithm. A stronger paper would bridge this gap more effectively, perhaps by arguing why the variance introduced by single-sample updates does not break the bounded-error assumption in the long run.
3.  **Dependence of ε on Depth:** The final bound on the equilibrium approximation is O(D²ε), where D is the game depth. While this is an important result, the quadratic dependence on depth suggests that for deep games, the guaranteed approximation could be quite loose.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem statement is clear, and the background definitions are precise. The authors do an excellent job of building their argument step-by-step in Section 4, which makes the complex proof accessible. The distinction between different algorithm variants (RM vs. RMM, Exp3 vs. Exp3M) is also clearly explained. Algorithm 1 provides a good template for the general SM-MCTS procedure.

A minor suggestion for improvement would be to make the relationship between the exploration parameter γ and the final ε in Theorem 4.11 more explicit. Lemma 4.4 states that an ε-Hannan consistent algorithm becomes (ε + γ)-Hannan consistent with exploration, so it seems this γ term should propagate into the final bound. Clarifying this would make the main theorem more precise.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first formal proof of convergence for any MCTS-style algorithm in the general class of perfect-information, zero-sum games with simultaneous moves. Previous work had either used MCTS variants heuristically in this setting or, as the authors cite, shown that the popular UCB selection strategy fails to converge. The key conceptual contribution is linking the convergence of MCTS to the property of Hannan consistency from online learning. This provides a new and powerful lens through which to analyze and design tree search algorithms for games, moving beyond the standard multi-armed bandit framework that underpins UCT.

**Significance:**
The results are highly significant for the fields of algorithmic game theory and AI for games.
1.  **Theoretical Foundation:** It places the use of MCTS in simultaneous move games on solid theoretical ground, justifying it as a sound approximation algorithm. This is a foundational result that researchers can build upon.
2.  **Algorithmic Design:** It provides a clear recipe for designing provably convergent MCTS algorithms for this game class: use a regret minimizer as the selection function. This is a practical and impactful insight.
3.  **Future Research:** The work opens up several avenues for future research, including deriving finite-time bounds, extending the analysis to imperfect-information games (a much harder problem), and analyzing the convergence of other heuristic MCTS variants.

The main limitation on its significance is the restriction to perfect-information games. Many of the most challenging simultaneous-move games (e.g., poker variants) involve imperfect information. Nonetheless, the class of games studied is important and this work is a crucial first step towards broader guarantees.

**Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**

**Pros:**
*   **Novelty:** Presents the first convergence proof for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, a long-standing open problem.
*   **Significance:** Provides a strong theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms and offers a principled approach for designing new ones.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proof is rigorous, well-structured, and based on a clever connection to Hannan consistency in online learning.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow.

**Cons:**
*   **Asymptotic Result:** The convergence proof lacks finite-time bounds, limiting its direct practical implications for determining required computation.
*   **Theory-Practice Gap:** The proof applies to a "mean-propagation" variant that is empirically shown to be slightly slower than the standard "single-sample" method, which is left unanalyzed.
*   **Limited Scope:** The result is for perfect-information games, while many interesting applications lie in imperfect-information settings.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a fundamental theoretical contribution. The originality and significance of providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in this game class are substantial. While the lack of finite-time bounds and the gap between the proven theory and the best practical variant are limitations, they are acceptable for a foundational paper of this nature and are clearly signposted as avenues for future work. The paper is well-executed, clearly written, and will be of high interest to researchers in game theory, reinforcement learning, and AI. I strongly recommend acceptance.
