

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a class of two-player zero-sum games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for SM-MCTS that can be instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure, and prove that such algorithms converge to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibria. This is a notable contribution as the paper identifies that "no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general EFGs," addressing an important theoretical gap.

The paper makes a strong connection between online learning theory (Hannan consistency) and game-theoretic algorithms. The authors build upon established concepts like regret matching and Exp3, and demonstrate how these can be integrated into MCTS for simultaneous move games. The formal analysis is well-structured, progressing from matrix games to extensive-form games through induction, and the key theorem (Theorem 4.11) provides a clear convergence guarantee. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical results, showing that the proposed variants (RMM and Exp3M) converge to approximate Nash equilibria at rates comparable to existing methods, with the mean propagation variant (RMM) showing slightly slower but consistent convergence.

**Strengths:**
1. **Novel theoretical contribution:** This is the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, filling a critical gap in the literature.
2. **Rigorous theoretical development:** The paper provides a clear and logically structured proof that builds from simple matrix games to extensive-form games with a solid inductive argument.
3. **Appropriate empirical validation:** The experiments on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios support the theoretical claims, and the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations in their empirical analysis.
4. **Clear connections to related work:** The paper properly situates its contribution within the broader literature on game theory, MCTS, and regret minimization, citing relevant NIPS and other conference papers.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited convergence rate analysis:** While the paper proves eventual convergence, it provides no finite-time convergence bounds, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The empirical analysis of convergence rates is limited to a specific set of games and doesn't provide a comprehensive rate comparison.
2. **Incomplete empirical worst-case analysis:** The "worst-case" games identified in Section 5.2 are based on a limited search space (depth-2, branching factor 2, utilities {0, 0.5, 1}), which may not represent the most challenging cases for the algorithms.
3. **Supplementary material dependency:** Some key proofs are relegated to the supplementary material (e.g., the proof of Theorem 4.11), which makes it harder to evaluate the completeness of the theoretical contribution without consulting additional material.
4. **Limited discussion of practical implications:** The paper could better articulate how these theoretical results improve upon existing methods (like alpha-beta pruning or double-oracle methods) in practical applications.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal analysis is sound and represents a significant advance in the theoretical foundations of MCTS for a class of games where such guarantees were previously lacking. While the empirical analysis supports the theoretical claims, the paper would be strengthened by providing finite-time convergence bounds and a more comprehensive analysis of convergence rates. The limited worst-case analysis could also be expanded.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical advances in machine learning and game theory. The work is of sufficient quality to be accepted at NIPS, though addressing the weaknesses mentioned above would further strengthen the paper.

**Pro/Con Summary:**
- **Pro:** First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games; solid theoretical foundation; well-structured proof; good empirical validation.
- **Con:** Limited finite-time convergence analysis; incomplete worst-case empirical analysis; some key proofs in supplementary material.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that should be accepted, with the understanding that the authors could further strengthen it by addressing the convergence rate limitations in future work.
