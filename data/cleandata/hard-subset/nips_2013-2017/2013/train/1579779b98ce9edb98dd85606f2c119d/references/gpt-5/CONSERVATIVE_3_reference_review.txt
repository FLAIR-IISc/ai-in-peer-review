Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. It proposes a template for SM-MCTS where the per-node selection procedure is any ε-Hannan consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3). The main theoretical result shows almost-sure convergence of the average strategies to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the game-tree depth; crucially, the analysis requires backing up mean returns (SM-MCTS-M) rather than single-sample returns. The proof proceeds by (i) standard results for HC in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) composing error up the tree by induction. Empirically, the paper compares RM vs. Exp3 with both sample and mean backups on random games and small “worst-case” constructed games, reporting convergence of exploitability with mean backup slightly slower.

Strengths
- Addresses a gap: there is little theory for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games; UCT can fail to converge to NE. The result provides a sufficient condition (HC + exploration + mean backups) for convergence to subgame-perfect approximate equilibria.
- The analysis is modular and leverages well-understood HC properties, providing a clear inductive path from leaves to root via bounded-error repeated games.
- The “template” formulation clarifies how to instantiate SM-MCTS with RM or Exp3 and the role of exploration in ensuring joint-action coverage.
- Empirical section, while limited, does attempt to examine convergence behavior and the impact of mean vs. sample backups.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practical relevance: The convergence proof requires backing up the running mean (not the current sample). This deviates from standard MCTS practice and appears to slow convergence empirically. The paper acknowledges this but does not provide theory for the more common sample-backup variant, which is what practitioners typically use.
- Assumptions and gap to practice: The analysis assumes the complete tree is eventually built and that statistics accrued during the build phase “cannot prevent eventual convergence,” but this is not rigorously quantified. There are no finite-time sample complexity bounds, and the dependence on the exploration parameter γ is not reflected in the final bound.
- Bound quality: The final (ε D^2 + δ) guarantee is quite loose, scales poorly with depth, and compounds with exploration-induced bias. It is unclear whether this dependence is inherent or an artifact of the proof technique. Tightness or lower bounds are not discussed.
- Selection/update details: The regret-matching updates seem to rely on using stored cumulative rewards Xhij for unplayed joint actions at a visit, which is a form of imputation that mixes bandit and full-information updates. The bias introduced by this approximation is hand-waved into the “bounded error” model, but more careful justification is needed that this indeed yields vanishing error at each node (especially off-policy estimates of unplayed pairs).
- Empirical validation: The experiments are limited to small random/worst-case synthetic games with uniform terminal payoffs. There is no comparison to established exact solvers (backward induction with LP or double-oracle pruning) or to state-of-the-art search in benchmark simultaneous-move domains. The million-iteration runs suggest slow practical convergence, and Exp3 lags substantially. No sensitivity analysis for γ or η, and no wall-clock/runtime reporting.
- Clarity and presentation: Several definitions and algorithmic steps are ambiguous or deferred to the appendix (e.g., precise “Update” semantics, treatment of joint vs. independent sampling in Select, how “previously unselected” is enforced when players act independently). There are minor typos and notational inconsistencies (e.g., “oth.” in Eq. (3), “Eolutionary,” inconsistent use of hats/bars).

Quality
- The high-level proof strategy is sound and builds on established HC results; however, key technical steps are deferred, the D^2 scaling is weak, and the reliance on mean backups limits practical import. Empirical evaluation does not thoroughly validate competitiveness against established baselines or in realistic domains.

Clarity
- Generally readable, but the algorithm description needs to be more precise and self-contained. Important assumptions (mean backups, guaranteed exploration, full-tree eventuality) should be highlighted up front as requirements, not after-the-fact justifications. Improve notation and fix typos.

Originality
- The result is incremental: it assembles known HC convergence in repeated games and propagates bounded error through a tree. Showing subgame-perfect convergence under these assumptions in SM perfect-information games is a useful specialization, but not conceptually surprising.

Significance
- Theoretical reassurance is valuable, but the strong assumptions, weak dependence on depth, and limited experiments reduce practical impact. Without finite-time bounds or results for standard sample-backup MCTS, it is hard to see near-term adoption.

Pros
- Provides a convergence guarantee for a class of SM-MCTS algorithms under clear (if strong) assumptions.
- Bridges online learning (HC) and MCTS in simultaneous-move settings.
- Empirically explores mean vs. sample backups.

Cons
- Requires mean backups and guaranteed exploration; no theory for standard sample-backup MCTS.
- Weak D^2 dependence; no finite-time/sample complexity bounds.
- Limited, synthetic experiments; no comparisons to established solvers or real domains.
- Some algorithmic details unclear; proofs largely deferred.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The theoretical result is technically correct at a high level and fills a niche, but the assumptions diverge from standard MCTS practice, the guarantees are weak, and the empirical validation is insufficient. Strengthening the paper would require (i) a precise, self-contained algorithm description with rigorous handling of feedback/imputation; (ii) finite-time bounds and a tighter depth dependence; (iii) analysis or counterexamples for the common sample-backup variant; and (iv) more compelling experiments against strong baselines on benchmark simultaneous-move games.
