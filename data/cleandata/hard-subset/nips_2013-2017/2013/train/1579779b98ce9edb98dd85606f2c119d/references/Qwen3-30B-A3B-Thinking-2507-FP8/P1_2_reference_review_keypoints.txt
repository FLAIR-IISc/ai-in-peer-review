- Paper proves that MCTS with ε-Hannan consistent selection (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) converges to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in two-player zero-sum simultaneous-move games.  
- Main contributions: (1) formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, (2) empirical validation showing mean-propagation variant (RMM) converges to approximate Nash.  
- Theoretical analysis builds from matrix games to extensive-form simultaneous-move games using induction on tree depth and careful error bounds.  
- Connects online learning (Hannan consistency) to game-theoretic equilibria within the MCTS framework.  
- Empirical evaluation tests RM and RMM on random and worst-case games (100 games × 100 runs), confirming theoretical claims.  
- Mean propagation vs. current-sample propagation distinction is important: mean propagation is required for formal guarantees, while sample propagation still empirically reaches approximate equilibrium.  
- Paper is technically sound, well-argued, and the proof technique is elegant.  
- Writing is clear, notation consistent, and figures (e.g., stacked matrix game) effectively illustrate concepts.  
- Originality: first formal convergence analysis of MCTS for simultaneous-move games and novel linking of Hannan consistency to MCTS convergence.  
- Significance: provides first theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous-move settings and guides future algorithm design via required properties (Hannan consistency + exploration).  
- Strengths: first formal result, rigorous proofs, comprehensive empirical support, clear exposition, and good literature context.  
- Weaknesses: no theoretical convergence-rate characterization; empirical tests limited to small-depth/small-branching games.  
- Additional weaknesses: slight ambiguity in the definition of “subgame perfect” for simultaneous moves; no treatment of non-zero-sum or imperfect-information extensions.  
- Recommendation: accept—strong theoretical contribution with appropriate empirical validation.  
- Suggested improvements: analyze or empirically characterize convergence rates, justify scalability or discuss challenges for larger games, clarify “subgame perfect” in simultaneous settings, and discuss practical/implementation implications of mean vs. sample propagation.
