**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a known theoretical gap in the literature: the lack of convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general MCTS template where the in-tree selection policy is handled by a regret-minimizing procedure from the online learning literature. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the resulting MCTS procedure is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium of the game. The proof proceeds by induction from the leaves of the game tree, modeling the values returned from child nodes as payoffs in a repeated matrix game with bounded error. The authors empirically evaluate their framework using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as selection policies, comparing the convergence of a variant required for their proof (propagating mean values) against the more standard approach of propagating the current sample's value.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper's primary strength is its theoretical rigor. The connection between ε-Hannan consistency, a well-established concept in online learning, and the convergence of MCTS in this game class is novel and provides a solid theoretical foundation for a family of algorithms. The inductive proof structure, which handles the propagation of noisy value estimates from subgames by analyzing repeated matrix games with bounded error, is technically sound.

However, there are significant weaknesses regarding the completeness of the work and the connection between theory and practice.
1.  A crucial assumption for the formal analysis is that the MCTS algorithm propagates the mean of all rewards observed through a node, rather than the reward from the most recent simulation. The paper's own experiments (Figure 2) show that this "RMM" variant converges more slowly than the standard "RM" variant. This creates a problematic disconnect: the theory provides guarantees for a demonstrably less effective version of the algorithm, leaving the performance of the practically superior version unexplained.
2.  The convergence guarantee is asymptotic. The paper lacks any finite-time analysis or convergence rate bounds, which are critical for understanding the practical performance of an approximation algorithm. The empirical results in Figure 3 suggest that convergence can be extremely slow, requiring millions of iterations to approach the equilibrium in even simple "worst-case" games. An asymptotic guarantee without a reasonable rate is of limited practical value.
3.  The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous. The experiments are limited to small, randomly generated games and a narrow set of "worst-case" games. Critically, the paper fails to compare its MCTS approach against established, state-of-the-art exact solvers for this game class, such as the backward induction with alpha-beta pruning methods cited in the introduction [8, 9]. Without this comparison, it is impossible to gauge the practical trade-offs (solution quality vs. computation time) of this MCTS approach.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, and the theoretical development is presented in a logical sequence, starting from simple matrix games and building up to the full extensive-form setting. The algorithms and definitions are stated clearly. The inclusion of figures illustrating the convergence behavior is helpful.

**Originality:**
The main contribution—the convergence proof—is original and addresses a long-standing open question. It is the first work to provide such a guarantee for MCTS in this game class. The algorithmic approach itself, however, is a combination of well-known techniques: MCTS, Regret Matching, and Exp3. The novelty lies not in a new algorithm, but in the analysis that connects these existing components. The authors even conjecture that their analysis could be extended to other existing algorithms, which suggests the contribution is primarily theoretical rather than algorithmic innovation.

**Significance:**
The paper has theoretical significance by formally justifying the use of a class of MCTS algorithms for approximating equilibria in simultaneous move games. This is an important step in bridging the gap between the heuristic success of MCTS and game-theoretic soundness.

However, the practical significance is questionable due to the issues mentioned under Quality. An algorithm that is proven to converge, but does so slowly and in a less effective configuration than standard heuristics, is unlikely to be adopted by practitioners. The lack of comparison to strong, non-MCTS baselines further obscures the practical relevance. For this work to have a greater impact, it would need to either provide analysis for the faster, standard MCTS variants or demonstrate a clear practical advantage over other existing solvers.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS to a Nash Equilibrium in perfect-information simultaneous move games.
*   The connection established between ε-Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is an elegant and important theoretical contribution.
*   The paper is methodologically sound and clearly presented.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The theoretical result applies to a variant of MCTS that is empirically shown to be slower than the standard heuristic, limiting its practical relevance.
*   The analysis is purely asymptotic, lacking finite-time bounds on convergence rate, which appears to be very slow in practice.
*   The experimental validation is weak, lacking comparisons to state-of-the-art non-MCTS baselines and evaluation on established benchmark games.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a sound and original theoretical result, its practical implications are underdeveloped and questionable. The work establishes a beachhead in the theory of MCTS for this game class, but the gap between the proven guarantees and a demonstrably useful, state-of-the-art algorithm remains substantial.
