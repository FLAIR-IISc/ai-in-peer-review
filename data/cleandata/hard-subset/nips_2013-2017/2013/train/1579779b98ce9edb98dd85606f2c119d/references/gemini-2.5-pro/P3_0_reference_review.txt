This paper addresses a significant and long-standing open question in the literature: the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for such games, theoretical guarantees have been elusive. The authors provide the first formal proof that a general class of MCTS algorithms, instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent action selection procedure, converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves.

The core idea is to frame the action selection problem within each node as a repeated matrix game played against an opponent. The paper's main theoretical contribution is a proof by induction over the depth of the game tree. The authors first establish (relying on known results from online learning) that two ε-Hannan consistent players converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium in a simple repeated matrix game. They then cleverly extend this to repeated matrix games where the payoffs are noisy but have a bounded error. This extension forms the crucial step of the induction: for a node at depth `d` from the leaves, the payoffs for its actions are the estimated values of its children subgames. By the inductive hypothesis, these child subgames have already converged to an approximate equilibrium, and the average values they propagate upwards are within a bounded error of the true subgame values. This allows the parent node to be treated as a game with bounded error, which in turn is shown to converge. Summing the potential gains from deviation at each level of the tree yields the final bound on the subgame perfect equilibrium. The paper also provides empirical results on random and worst-case games that support the theoretical claims and compare different algorithm variants.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** This work provides the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in this important game class. This is a foundational result that fills a major theoretical gap and provides a solid justification for using MCTS-like algorithms in simultaneous-move games from a game-theoretic perspective.
2.  **Generality of the Framework:** The proof is not tied to a single algorithm like Regret Matching, but applies to a broad and well-understood class of online learning algorithms: those that are ε-Hannan consistent. This makes the result powerful and widely applicable.
3.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The proof strategy, based on induction and the concept of repeated games with bounded error, is elegant and appears technically sound. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, building the argument step-by-step from simple matrix games to the full extensive-form case. The formal definitions and lemmas are precise.
4.  **Useful Practical Insights:** The empirical evaluation, while not the paper's main focus, provides valuable insights. The comparison between propagating the mean value (as required by the proof) and propagating the current sample value (common in practice) is particularly interesting. The finding that mean propagation is empirically slightly slower highlights a subtle but important gap between theoretical convenience and practical performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Proof:** The analysis is entirely asymptotic, proving that the algorithm *eventually* converges. It does not provide any finite-time bounds on the convergence rate or the number of simulations required to reach a certain quality of approximation (ε). While the final `D^2` term hints at how the error accumulates with depth, a more formal rate of convergence would significantly increase the paper's impact.
2.  **Simplifying Assumption in Analysis:** The proof assumes the MCTS tree is fully expanded. While the authors correctly argue that guaranteed exploration ensures this will happen eventually, this sidesteps the dynamics of tree growth, which is a fundamental aspect of MCTS. The analysis therefore applies to a "full-tree" regret-minimization algorithm rather than MCTS as it is typically implemented, where the tree grows selectively. While this simplification is likely necessary for tractability and acceptable for an initial asymptotic proof, it is a limitation that should be acknowledged more explicitly.
3.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on randomly generated games and small, exhaustively searched worst-case games. While sufficient to validate the core claims, the paper would be strengthened by evaluation on a well-known benchmark for this game class, such as Goofspiel, which is mentioned in the introduction. This would better situate the performance of the proposed methods relative to existing specialized algorithms.
4.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** A minor weakness is the finding that the theoretically justified variant (RMM) is empirically outperformed by the more standard variant (RM). While the authors report this honestly, it leaves a slightly unresolved tension. A deeper discussion of why this might be the case (e.g., higher variance of single samples introducing useful noise, or the slower reaction time of the mean to changing strategies) would be beneficial.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a very strong paper that makes a fundamental theoretical contribution to the study of MCTS. The problem is important, the approach is novel and elegant, and the results provide a much-needed theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations in scope (asymptotic analysis, limited experiments) that do not undermine the core contribution and represent clear avenues for future work. The paper is well-written, technically solid, and of high significance to the NIPS community, particularly those interested in reinforcement learning, planning, and algorithmic game theory. It clearly meets the bar for acceptance.
