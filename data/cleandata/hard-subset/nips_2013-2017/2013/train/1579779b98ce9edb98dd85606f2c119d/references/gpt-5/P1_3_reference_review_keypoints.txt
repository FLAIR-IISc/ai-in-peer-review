- Study of SM-MCTS for two-player zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, proposing a general SM-MCTS template using ε-Hannan-consistent selection with exploration.  
- Main theoretical claim: SM-MCTS with ε-HC selection and infinite sampling of every joint action converges a.s. to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium using mean-value backups.  
- Proof strategy: analyze repeated matrix games, extend to bounded payoff errors, and inductively compose errors over tree depth.  
- Builds on UCT/MCTS, simultaneous-move search literature, and online-learning HC methods; fills a gap since no prior convergence proof existed for this setting.  
- Theory is sensible and technically careful, leveraging HC properties and induction over depth.  
- Key theoretical issues: final bound in Theorem 4.11 is unclear/inconsistent—ε missing and dependence on depth (O(Dε) vs O(D^2ε)) and constants need clarification.  
- Notation bug: average strategies labeled inconsistently (σ̂ vs σ̄) and must be corrected.  
- Need an explicit lemma that guaranteed exploration implies every node and joint action is visited infinitely often a.s.  
- Practical requirement: proof relies on mean-backups (Xh/nh) rather than sample-backups, creating a theoretical/practical gap.  
- Experiments compare RM and Exp3 with current-sample vs mean-value backpropagation and show empirical convergence, with mean-backups slightly slower.  
- Experimental limitations: small depths/branching, random utilities, and no larger benchmarks (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel) or strong SM-MCTS baselines.  
- Clarity issues: fix notation, explicitly include ε in main theorem, clarify joint-action vs independent exploration, and motivate necessity of mean-backups.  
- Originality: novel and nontrivial convergence result adapting HC selection to SM-MCTS with error-propagation analysis.  
- Significance: provides theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous-move games and links online learning with MCTS, though lacks finite-time rates and broad empirical validation.  
- Pros: first convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS, modular analysis, applicable to multiple HC rules, and consistent empirical results.  
- Cons: unclear final bound and loose constants, reliance on mean-backups, limited experiments, and some notation/typo/reference issues.  
- Suggested improvements: state final approximation bound precisely as function of D, ε, (γ); provide finite-time bounds if possible; explore sample-backup variants with variance reduction; clarify exploration assumptions and update behavior.  
- Overall assessment: solid theoretical contribution warranting acceptance if bounds/notation are clarified and empirical/clarity issues are modestly addressed.
