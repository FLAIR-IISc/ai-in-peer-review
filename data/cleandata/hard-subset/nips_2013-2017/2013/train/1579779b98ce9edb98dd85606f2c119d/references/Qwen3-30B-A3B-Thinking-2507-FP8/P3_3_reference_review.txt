

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors present a general MCTS template that uses regret-minimizing procedures for action selection, and prove that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by connecting online learning theory (Hannan consistency) with MCTS convergence guarantees, a connection previously unexplored for simultaneous move games. The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims, showing that variants using regret matching (with mean propagation) converge to approximate Nash equilibria.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Theoretical Contribution**: The paper fills a critical gap in the literature. While MCTS is widely used in simultaneous move games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), no convergence guarantees existed for these settings. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is novel and well-motivated.

2. **Rigorous Formal Analysis**: The paper's theoretical framework is well-structured, with clear definitions and a logical progression from matrix games to multi-stage games. The main theorem (Theorem 4.11) is a substantial contribution that establishes convergence guarantees for the first time in this domain.

3. **Practical Relevance**: The distinction between propagating mean values versus current samples (Section 5.1) is a valuable practical insight that addresses a common implementation choice in MCTS. The empirical results clearly demonstrate that mean propagation (required for the theoretical analysis) has only a marginal impact on convergence speed.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with precise definitions and consistent notation. The theoretical results build logically on one another, and the empirical evaluation is appropriately designed to test the core claims.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Tree Construction Assumption**: The formal analysis assumes a complete tree is built before convergence analysis (Section 4), which is at odds with how MCTS is actually implemented. While the authors briefly acknowledge this, the assumption weakens the practical relevance of the convergence guarantee. The paper should explicitly address how the incremental tree construction affects the convergence rate.

2. **Lack of Convergence Rate Bounds**: The paper proves eventual convergence but provides no finite-time bounds on how quickly the algorithm converges to an ε-Nash equilibrium. For practical applications, convergence rates are crucial. The authors mention this as future work but it's a significant omission for a paper claiming to provide convergence guarantees.

3. **Limited Empirical Comparison**: The empirical evaluation focuses on comparing mean propagation versus current sample propagation and worst-case games, but doesn't compare with other algorithms that have known convergence properties in these games (e.g., Counterfactual Regret Minimization variants). A comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. **Worst-Case Analysis Scope**: The worst-case analysis (Section 5.2) is limited to very small games (depth 2, branching factor 2), which may not be representative of more complex games where MCTS is typically applied. The authors should discuss how their results might scale to larger games.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is elegant and well-executed. The paper's main weakness is the lack of practical convergence rate bounds, which limits the immediate applicability of the theoretical results. However, the theoretical foundation is strong and provides a solid basis for future work.

The paper is clearly written, addresses an important gap in the literature, and makes a substantial contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS. While the convergence rate is not addressed, the paper's theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant publication at NeurIPS.

## Recommendation

**Accept** - The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that fills a critical gap in the literature. The main weakness (lack of convergence rate bounds) is acknowledged by the authors as future work and doesn't undermine the strength of the theoretical results. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and provides a solid foundation for future research on MCTS in simultaneous move games.

## Specific Recommendations

1. Address the incremental tree construction assumption more thoroughly in the formal analysis, perhaps by showing that the tree building phase does not affect the eventual convergence.

2. Explicitly state the limitations of the worst-case analysis and discuss how the results might scale to larger games.

3. Consider adding a brief comparison with CFR-based algorithms (which have known convergence properties in similar settings) to strengthen the empirical evaluation.

4. In the conclusion, more clearly articulate how the theoretical results inform the design of practical MCTS implementations.

This paper provides a crucial theoretical foundation for understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games and should be of interest to the NeurIPS community working at the intersection of game theory and reinforcement learning.
