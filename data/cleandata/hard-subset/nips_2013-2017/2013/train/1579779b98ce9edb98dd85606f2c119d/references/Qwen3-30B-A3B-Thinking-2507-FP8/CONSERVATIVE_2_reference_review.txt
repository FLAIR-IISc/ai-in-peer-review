

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper claims to provide the first formal convergence proof for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general MCTS template using any -Hannan consistent selection function, prove convergence to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium, and evaluate two implementations (regret matching and Exp3).

The paper's theoretical contribution is modest. The main result (Theorem 4.11) is essentially a straightforward induction from established results on Hannan consistency in repeated matrix games to extensive-form games. The authors cite prior work on Hannan consistency (RM and Exp3), but fail to properly contextualize their contribution within the broader literature on game-theoretic algorithms. Crucially, they don't engage with the Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) framework, which is the standard approach for finding Nash equilibria in extensive-form games, including those with simultaneous moves. CFR already has strong theoretical guarantees and has been successfully applied to similar problems (e.g., in poker). The paper's claim of being the "first formal analysis" is questionable given the extensive prior work in this space.

The empirical validation is notably weak. The authors test only on small games (depth 2, branching factor 2) with randomly generated utilities, which is insufficient for meaningful conclusions about practical performance. More critically, they don't compare their approach against the standard CFR baseline, which is the natural point of comparison for Nash equilibrium finding in these games. The "worst-case" games they identify (Figure 3) are not truly worst-case but merely represent games where their specific implementations happened to perform poorly in limited trials. The paper shows that their method converges, but fails to demonstrate any advantage over existing methods.

The theoretical bound (D² + δ) appears loose. For games of moderate depth (D=10), this implies convergence to a 100-approximate equilibrium, which is not practically useful. The paper doesn't address the critical question of convergence rates - how many iterations are needed to reach a meaningful approximation? Without this, the theoretical result remains largely abstract.

The paper also makes unsubstantiated claims about the practical significance of their work. They state that "this justifies the usage of MCTS as an approximation algorithm for this class of games," but provide no evidence that their approach is superior to existing methods. In fact, CFR has been shown to converge to exact Nash equilibria in many games with similar or better performance than MCTS variants.

The paper's presentation is technically sound but lacks novelty. The main result follows standard induction patterns from online learning theory applied to game trees. The authors fail to clearly articulate how their work advances beyond what's already known, particularly given the existence of CFR, which has been extensively validated in practice for these exact problem classes.

The most significant weakness is the lack of meaningful comparison with established baselines. Without showing that their approach outperforms CFR or other standard game-theoretic algorithms, the paper's contribution is difficult to justify. The empirical section is limited to small-scale experiments that don't address the practical concerns of convergence rates and solution quality.

I acknowledge the paper's technical correctness and the clear presentation of the theoretical framework. The authors have properly defined the problem and provided a formal proof. However, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and practical significance to warrant publication at NIPS. The field of algorithmic game theory has already developed robust methods for solving these problems, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that MCTS with Hannan consistency provides any meaningful advantage.

In summary, while the paper makes a technically correct but incremental contribution, it fails to demonstrate significant novelty or practical value compared to existing approaches. The theoretical result is a straightforward extension of known concepts, and the empirical validation is inadequate. Without showing clear advantages over the established CFR framework or other state-of-the-art methods, the paper does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

**Recommendation: Reject**

**Strengths:**
- Correct technical proof of convergence
- Clear presentation of the theoretical framework
- Properly defined problem and mathematical notation

**Weaknesses:**
- Overstated novelty claim ("first formal analysis")
- No comparison against standard baselines (CFR)
- Insufficient empirical validation (only small games)
- Theoretical bound (D² + δ) is too loose for practical use
- Fails to demonstrate any practical advantage over existing methods
- Inadequate engagement with prior work on game-theoretic algorithms
