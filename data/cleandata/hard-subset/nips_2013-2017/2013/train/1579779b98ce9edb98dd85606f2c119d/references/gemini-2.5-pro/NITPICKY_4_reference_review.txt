This paper addresses an important theoretical gap in the literature: the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for such games, a formal guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been lacking. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where action selection at each node is handled by a regret-minimizing procedure. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the resulting average strategies converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. This result connects the field of online learning with game tree search and provides a theoretical foundation for using algorithms like Regret Matching and Exp3 within an MCTS framework for this game class. The authors supplement the theory with an empirical evaluation of their proposed algorithms.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides the first formal proof of convergence for an MCTS-style algorithm in simultaneous move games. This is a significant and novel contribution, addressing a question of both theoretical and practical importance. The framework of using ε-Hannan consistent procedures is elegant and general.
*   **Theoretical Approach:** The proof structure, which proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree and models the estimates from child nodes as a repeated game with bounded error, is a clever way to handle the complexities of the stochastic, nested optimization problem.
*   **Clarity of Goal:** The paper is well-motivated and clearly states the problem it aims to solve and its primary contributions.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the significance of the core idea, the manuscript suffers from a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, inconsistencies, and presentational flaws that detract from its overall quality.

*   **Technical Soundness and Clarity:**
    1.  **Zero-Sum vs. Constant-Sum:** The paper consistently refers to "zero-sum" games, but the utility function is defined as `u2(z) = 1 - u1(z)`, which describes a constant-sum game. While any constant-sum game can be linearly transformed into a zero-sum one, this lack of precision is confusing and technically incorrect as stated.
    2.  **Probability of Terminal State:** In Section 2, the probability of reaching a terminal state `z` is given as `πσ(z) = π1(z)π2(z)`. This formulation is incorrect. The probability of a path is the product of the probabilities of the *joint actions* taken at each decision node along that path, i.e., `∏ σ1(h, a1)σ2(h, a2)`. The authors' formulation seems to imply independence between player choices in a way that is not standard for computing path probabilities in EFGs.
    3.  **Analysis vs. Algorithm:** The formal analysis in Section 4 makes a critical simplifying assumption: "We disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree." This decouples the analysis from the MCTS algorithm itself, which is fundamentally defined by its selective tree expansion. The analysis applies more to a full-tree, iterative value backup algorithm rather than MCTS as it is known and practiced. The claim that statistics from the expansion phase "cannot prevent the eventual convergence" is not sufficiently justified.
    4.  **Inconsistent Algorithm Description:**
        *   The main pseudocode (Algorithm 1) uses a generic `RetVal` function. The text explains that returning the current sample (`u1`) is standard, but returning the mean (`Xh/nh`) is "required for the formal analysis." This is a critical discrepancy, especially since the empirical results show the analyzed version performs worse. The algorithm presented is not the one analyzed.
        *   In Section 3.1 (Regret Matching), the regret update rule is `rh[i′]← rh[i′] + (x(h, i′, j)− u1)`. The variable `j` is not defined in this context; presumably, it should be the action `j2` sampled for player 2. The notation for `x(h, i, j)` is also unnecessarily complex.
        *   In Section 3.2 (Exp3), the weight is defined as `wh,i = xh,i1`, which appears to be a typo. Footnote 1 provides a different, numerically stable formulation (`wh,i = xh,i−max...`), but the main text defines it as `wh,i = xh,i`. This is a direct contradiction.
    5.  **Exploration Definition:** Definition 4.3 presents a modified exploration scheme `A*` where, on exploration steps, actions are chosen uniformly "without updating any of the variables belonging to A." This is a non-standard form of exploration that discards information, and it is not clear why this specific, potentially inefficient, model was chosen for the analysis.

*   **Presentation and Formatting:**
    1.  **Anonymity Violation:** The paper includes a non-anonymized link to an arXiv preprint in Section 4. This is a serious violation of the double-blind reviewing policy.
    2.  **References:** The bibliography contains severe and numerous formatting errors (e.g., `(AAMAS, , , )`, `(AAAI-, , , )`). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference and suggests a lack of careful preparation.
    3.  **Minor Issues:** There are several minor typos ("oth." instead of "otherwise") and uses of non-standard notation (`7→` instead of `→`).

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

**Pro:**
*   First convergence proof for MCTS in this game class, a significant theoretical result.
*   Novel and elegant connection between online learning (Hannan consistency) and MCTS.
*   The paper is well-motivated and addresses a clear gap in the literature.

**Con:**
*   Numerous technical inaccuracies and inconsistencies in definitions and algorithm descriptions.
*   The formal analysis is based on a major simplifying assumption (a full, pre-existing tree) that distances it from the practical MCTS algorithm.
*   The specific algorithm variant required for the proof is shown to be empirically inferior to standard practice.
*   Serious presentation flaws, including a violation of double-blind review and a poorly formatted bibliography.

**Decision:**
While the central idea of the paper is novel and significant, the execution is flawed. The sheer number of minor and major technical inconsistencies, combined with poor presentation and a critical violation of review policy, makes it difficult to recommend acceptance in its current form. The gap between the practical algorithm and the simplified theoretical model that is analyzed is also a major concern. I believe the paper contains the seeds of an excellent contribution, but it requires a thorough revision to correct the errors, clarify the exposition, and more carefully bridge the gap between the theory and the practical MCTS algorithm. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**.
