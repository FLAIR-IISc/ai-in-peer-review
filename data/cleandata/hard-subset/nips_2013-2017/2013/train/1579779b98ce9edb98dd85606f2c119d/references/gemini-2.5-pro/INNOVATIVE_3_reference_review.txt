### Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**
This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first convergence guarantees for this class of algorithms in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are highly successful in practice, their theoretical underpinnings outside of purely sequential games are lacking. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection policy is governed by a regret-minimizing procedure from online learning. Their main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection algorithm is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration (visiting each action pair infinitely often), the resulting strategies converge to a subgame perfect $\epsilon'$-Nash equilibrium. The paper instantiates this framework with two well-known regret minimizers, Regret Matching and Exp3, and provides an empirical evaluation on synthetic games that supports the theoretical claims.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Originality and Significance (Major Strengths):**
The primary strength of this paper lies in its novelty and significance. To my knowledge, this is the first work to formally prove the convergence of MCTS to a Nash Equilibrium in this game class. This is a bold and important step. MCTS has been applied to simultaneous move games before, but typically with heuristics and without theoretical justification. For example, it is known that the popular UCT selection policy fails to converge even in simple matrix games. This paper provides a foundational bridge between the fields of online learning (specifically, regret minimization) and heuristic tree search, showing how to construct a provably sound algorithm.

This contribution is highly significant. It moves MCTS from a purely heuristic tool to a principled approximation algorithm for this domain, which has wide-ranging applications in security, economics, and general game playing. By establishing this theoretical bedrock, the paper opens up a new and promising direction for designing and analyzing MCTS in even more general game settings (e.g., with imperfect information). The core idea of using regret-minimizers as the selection "engine" inside MCTS is powerful and likely to inspire future work. This is precisely the kind of foundational, direction-setting research that is valuable to the community.

**Quality (Strong, with minor caveats):**
The theoretical analysis appears sound and is the centerpiece of the paper. The inductive proof structure—showing convergence in leaf nodes (matrix games), then extending this to games with bounded error (nodes one level up), and proceeding up to the root—is elegant and well-executed. The formalisms of $\epsilon$-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration are appropriately leveraged to build the argument.

However, there are two points where the analysis shows some limitations, which is acceptable for a first-of-its-kind result but worth noting.
1.  **Theory-Practice Gap:** The proof requires propagating the *mean* of sampled rewards up the tree. However, the authors' own experiments show that the more standard approach of propagating the *current* sample's reward converges faster. The authors are commendably transparent about this, but it creates a disconnect between the algorithm that is proven correct and the one that works better in practice. This isn't a fatal flaw, but rather an intriguing puzzle for future work: can the proof be extended to the empirically superior variant?
2.  **Simplifying Assumption:** The formal analysis disregards the incremental tree-building phase of MCTS and assumes a complete game tree for the asymptotic argument. The authors justify this by stating that guaranteed exploration ensures all nodes are eventually created and visited infinitely often. While this is a reasonable simplification for an initial proof, a more complete analysis would have to contend with the statistics collected during the early, highly biased, tree-building phase.

Despite these points, the theoretical contribution is substantial. These are not so much errors as they are frontiers for the next phase of this research program.

**Clarity (Excellent):**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building their argument from the ground up, starting with clear definitions, analyzing simple repeated matrix games, introducing the concept of games with bounded error, and finally combining these pieces to prove the main theorem for extensive-form games. Algorithm 1 is clear, and the descriptions of the Regret Matching and Exp3 instantiations are easy to follow. This clarity makes a dense theoretical contribution accessible.

**Empirical Analysis:**
The experiments successfully validate the paper's theoretical claims. They demonstrate that the proposed algorithms do, in fact, converge, and they provide insight into the performance difference between propagating the mean versus the current sample. The investigation into "worst-case" games is also interesting. The empirical section is not intended to show a state-of-the-art performance breakthrough on a large benchmark but to serve as a sanity check and illustration of the theory, a role it fulfills well.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty:** Presents the first formal proof of MCTS convergence to a Nash Equilibrium in simultaneous move games, filling a major theoretical void.
*   **Significant Impact:** Provides a principled foundation for applying MCTS in a broader class of games and establishes a strong link between online learning and heuristic search that will likely be influential.
*   **Bold Direction:** Tackles a fundamental problem with a clean and elegant theoretical approach, prioritizing a new concept over incremental improvements on existing methods.
*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The proof structure is sound and insightful, even with some simplifying assumptions appropriate for a foundational paper.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The variant of the algorithm required for the proof is shown to be empirically slower than the standard, unproven variant.
*   The analysis simplifies the MCTS algorithm by assuming a pre-existing tree structure for the asymptotic argument.
*   The experimental validation is on synthetic games and does not demonstrate a practical performance breakthrough on a complex, real-world problem.

**Recommendation:**
I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper makes a novel, significant, and much-needed theoretical contribution. It introduces a new direction for research in a popular and practical family of algorithms. While the methodology is not perfect—there is a gap between the proven theory and best practice, and some simplifying assumptions are made—these are acceptable trade-offs for a paper that is the first to solve a long-standing open problem. The boldness and importance of the core idea far outweigh these minor limitations. This work is likely to be highly cited and to spur further research into theoretically-grounded MCTS algorithms.
