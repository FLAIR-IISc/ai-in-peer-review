

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in understanding Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for simultaneous move games, which are important in many applications including security games and general game-playing. The authors present a general MCTS template for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, proving that using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions (like regret matching and Exp3) ensures convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This is a valuable contribution to the theoretical foundations of MCTS, as prior work has lacked formal convergence guarantees for this class of games.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which has been a notable gap in the literature. The proof structure, building from matrix games to extensive-form games through induction, is logically sound and well-structured.

2. **Formalization of Practical Algorithms:** The authors successfully formalize how regret minimization procedures (regret matching and Exp3) can be integrated into MCTS, providing a principled approach to action selection that ensures convergence.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation on randomly generated games provides useful insights into the practical performance of the proposed algorithms, particularly regarding the impact of propagating mean values versus current samples. The results support the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Relevance of Tree Construction Assumption:** The formal analysis makes a critical simplification by assuming the complete game tree is built before convergence analysis. In practice, MCTS incrementally builds the tree, and the paper doesn't adequately address how this incremental construction affects the convergence rate or whether the theoretical guarantees hold during the tree-building phase. This is a significant practical limitation since the main advantage of MCTS is its ability to handle large state spaces without building the entire tree.

2. **Limited Scope of Convergence Analysis:** While the paper proves convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium, it doesn't provide any finite-time convergence bounds. For practical applications, knowing how many iterations are needed to reach a desired ε is crucial, and this is absent from the analysis.

3. **Comparison to Recent MCTS Advances:** The paper mentions UCT doesn't converge to Nash equilibrium (citing Shafiei et al.), but fails to discuss how their results relate to more recent MCTS variants designed specifically for simultaneous move games (e.g., those using counterfactual regret minimization). This weakens the paper's contribution by not clearly establishing how it differs from and improves upon existing approaches.

4. **Limited Empirical Evaluation on Real-World Games:** The empirical evaluation focuses on randomly generated games and small worst-case scenarios. It would be more convincing to demonstrate convergence on non-trivial, real-world simultaneous move games (e.g., Goofspiel variants) to show practical relevance beyond synthetic examples.

**Critical Assessment:**

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. However, the practical relevance of the convergence guarantees is somewhat limited by the assumptions made in the formal analysis. The claim that "the algorithm eventually converges" is technically correct but doesn't address the key practical question: "How long will it take to converge in realistic scenarios?"

The most significant limitation is the assumption of a fully built tree, which contradicts the fundamental purpose of MCTS in large games. The authors acknowledge this simplification but don't adequately address how it affects the practical applicability of their results. For a paper submitted to NeurIPS, which values both theoretical rigor and practical relevance, this gap is substantial.

The empirical evaluation provides some useful insights but lacks depth in demonstrating the algorithm's performance on meaningful game instances. The paper would benefit from including comparison with state-of-the-art MCTS variants designed for simultaneous move games (e.g., those using counterfactual regret minimization) to show relative performance.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games, but its practical impact is limited by the assumptions made in the convergence analysis. The theoretical results are sound, but the paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Providing finite-time convergence bounds
2. Addressing the incremental tree construction process
3. Comparing with recent MCTS variants designed for simultaneous move games
4. Demonstrating performance on non-trivial game instances

Given the importance of the problem and the soundness of the theoretical results, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these limitations. The paper has the potential to become a foundational reference for MCTS in simultaneous move games, but needs to better bridge the gap between theory and practice.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

*Pros:*
- Fills a significant theoretical gap in MCTS convergence analysis
- Well-structured formal proof for simultaneous move games
- Provides a general template for MCTS algorithms using regret minimization
- Empirical validation of theoretical claims

*Cons:*
- Makes strong assumption of fully built tree (contradicting MCTS purpose)
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds
- Limited comparison with recent MCTS variants for simultaneous move games
- Empirical evaluation on synthetic games only

The paper's theoretical contribution is strong, but to maximize its impact at NeurIPS, the authors should address the practical limitations in their convergence analysis and provide more comprehensive empirical validation.
