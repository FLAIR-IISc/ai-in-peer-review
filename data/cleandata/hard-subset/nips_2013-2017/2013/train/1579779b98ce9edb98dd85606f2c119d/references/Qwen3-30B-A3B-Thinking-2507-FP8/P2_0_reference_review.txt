

# Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures each action is attempted infinitely often), the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper builds on existing work in online learning (Hannan consistency) and regret minimization (regret matching, Exp3), applying these concepts to MCTS in a novel context. The authors empirically validate their claims using two different ε-Hannan consistent procedures on randomly generated and worst-case games.

This work differs from previous MCTS research (e.g., UCT for sequential games) by providing convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games, where such guarantees were previously unknown. It extends the theoretical foundations of MCTS beyond the sequential game setting that has dominated the literature.

## Quality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides a technically sound theoretical framework with well-structured proofs. The authors build a compelling argument through a series of lemmas (4.4-4.10) that progressively establish the convergence guarantee.
- The theoretical analysis is complete in its scope, addressing the core problem of convergence in simultaneous move games, which has been a known gap in the literature.
- The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical analysis well, showing that the mean propagation variant (RMM) converges to approximate Nash equilibrium, with results consistent with the theoretical claims.
- The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that their analysis focuses on eventual convergence rather than finite-time bounds, and that their framework doesn't cover all practical MCTS variants.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper assumes the complete tree will eventually be built, which is unrealistic in practice with limited computational resources. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't provide analysis of how this assumption affects practical performance.
- The empirical validation is limited to small games (depth 2 with branching factor 2), which may not reflect the behavior in larger, more complex games where MCTS is typically applied.
- The paper lacks finite-time convergence bounds, which would be valuable for practical applications. The authors acknowledge this in the conclusion but don't provide a path forward.
- The theoretical analysis focuses on zero-sum perfect-information games, limiting its applicability to a broader class of games where MCTS is used.

## Clarity Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized, with clear progression from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical evaluation.
- Key concepts (ε-Hannan consistency, matrix games, subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium) are well-defined and explained with appropriate mathematical notation.
- The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and figures (Figures 2-3) effectively illustrate the core ideas and empirical results.
- The connection between the theoretical framework and practical implementation details is clear.

**Weaknesses:**
- The transition from matrix games to extensive-form games could be more thoroughly explained, particularly how the recursive structure of the game tree interacts with the regret minimization process.
- Some technical details in the proofs (particularly in the appendix) could be made more accessible to readers less familiar with game theory.
- The paper could better explain why mean propagation (RMM) is necessary for the theoretical guarantees, rather than just stating it as a requirement.

## Originality Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper identifies and addresses a clear gap in the literature: no convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
- The application of Hannan consistency to MCTS for simultaneous move games is a novel combination of techniques from online learning and game theory.
- The paper distinguishes itself from previous MCTS work (e.g., UCT for sequential games) by providing theoretical guarantees for a different class of games.
- The authors adequately reference related work, including previous MCTS approaches (e.g., UCT), regret minimization algorithms (regret matching, Exp3), and simultaneous move game solvers.

**Weaknesses:**
- While the specific application to simultaneous move games is novel, the core technique (applying Hannan consistency to MCTS) builds directly on existing work in online learning and regret minimization, so the novelty is somewhat incremental.
- The paper doesn't sufficiently explore how their framework might be extended to cover more practical MCTS variants (e.g., UCT) that aren't covered by their proof.

## Significance Assessment

**Strengths:**
- The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.
- The results have practical implications for applications like security domains and card games, where MCTS is widely used but lacks theoretical foundations.
- The paper bridges the gap between theoretical game theory and practical MCTS algorithms, providing a foundation for future work.
- The empirical results suggest that the mean propagation variant (RMM) converges to approximate Nash equilibrium, which aligns with the theoretical claims and provides practical guidance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The focus on zero-sum perfect-information games limits the applicability to more general game classes where MCTS is used (e.g., imperfect information games).
- The lack of finite-time convergence bounds reduces the practical utility of the theoretical results.
- The empirical validation is limited to small games, which may not reflect the behavior in larger, more complex games where MCTS is typically applied.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The theoretical framework is sound, the proofs are well-structured, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature and provides a foundation for future work in this area.

However, the paper has some limitations in scope and practical applicability. The focus on eventual convergence rather than finite-time bounds, the limited empirical validation to small games, and the restriction to zero-sum perfect-information games reduce the immediate practical impact of the results.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- First formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games (a significant gap in the literature)
- Sound theoretical framework with well-structured proofs
- Good empirical validation that supports theoretical claims
- Clear distinction from previous work on MCTS for sequential games
- Well-written and well-organized presentation
- Identifies a meaningful problem with clear practical relevance

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited to zero-sum perfect-information games (not general enough for broader impact)
- No finite-time convergence bounds (reduces practical utility)
- Empirical validation limited to small games (depth 2, branching factor 2)
- Theoretical analysis assumes complete tree construction (unrealistic in practice)

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field, addressing a clear gap in the literature with sound analysis. While it has limitations in scope and practical applicability, these are acknowledged by the authors, and the work provides a solid foundation for future research. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games.

The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a clear contribution that should be of interest to the NeurIPS community, particularly those working on reinforcement learning, game theory, and decision making under uncertainty.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the empirical section, consider expanding the evaluation to include larger games (e.g., depth 3-4) to better reflect practical applications.
2. Include a discussion of how the theoretical results might be extended to cover more practical MCTS variants (e.g., UCT).
3. Add a brief discussion of the computational cost of the mean propagation variant compared to the standard sample value propagation.
4. In the introduction, more clearly state the limitations of previous MCTS work in simultaneous move games to better highlight the novelty of this contribution.

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of game-theoretic algorithms. I believe it would be a good fit for NeurIPS.
