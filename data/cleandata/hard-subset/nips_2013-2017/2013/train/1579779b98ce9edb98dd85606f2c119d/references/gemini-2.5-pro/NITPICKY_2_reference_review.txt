**Summary:**
This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this game class, where the action selection mechanism can be instantiated by any regret-minimizing procedure. The main contribution is a formal proof that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the resulting MCTS algorithm's average strategy profile converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction on the game tree's depth, showing that convergence in the leaf subgames (matrix games) implies convergence in parent nodes, which can be modeled as matrix games with bounded error. The authors empirically evaluate their claims using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as selection functions, comparing a variant that propagates the mean value (as required by their proof) with the more standard approach of propagating the current sample's value.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles an important and unaddressed theoretical question. While MCTS variants have been successfully applied to simultaneous move games, formal convergence guarantees to a Nash Equilibrium have been lacking. Providing the first such proof is a significant contribution to the intersection of game theory and search algorithms.
2.  **General Framework:** The approach of defining a general template based on ε-Hannan consistency is elegant. It connects the MCTS literature with the extensive literature on online learning and regret minimization, potentially opening up new avenues for designing MCTS selection policies.
3.  **Sound Theoretical Approach:** The proof strategy, while sketched briefly, appears methodologically sound. The idea of modeling parent nodes as games with bounded error stemming from the stochasticity of the subgames is a clever way to structure the inductive proof.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of technical inaccuracies, unclear descriptions, and formatting issues that, in aggregate, undermine the work's quality and call its conclusions into question.

1.  **Technical Inaccuracies in Definitions:**
    *   In Section 2, the game is defined as zero-sum, but the utility for player 2 is given as `u2(z) = 1 - u1(z)`. This defines a constant-sum game (with sum 1), not a general zero-sum game where `u2(z) = -u1(z)`. This is a fundamental error in the problem formulation.
    *   The probability of reaching a terminal state `z` is defined as `πσ(z) = π1(z)π2(z)`. This notation is highly ambiguous for an extensive-form game. The probability of a history is the product of the policy probabilities for *all actions along the path*, not a product of two player-specific quantities. This requires a more formal and correct definition.

2.  **Flawed and Ambiguous Algorithm Description (Algorithm 1):**
    *   The pseudocode for SM-MCTS is confusing. It features two `Update` calls (lines 7 and 14). The first one, after expansion and rollout, is not explained and seems incorrect, as the value `u1` has not yet been propagated back to the parent `h`. The standard MCTS backpropagation happens only after the recursive call returns.
    *   The expansion condition on line 2, `∃(i, j) ... not previously selected`, is vague. It is not specified how this state is maintained or what its scope is (i.e., per-node visit or globally).
    *   The description of the Regret Matching update rule in Section 3.1 is non-standard and appears incorrect. The definition of `x(h, i, j)` and its use in the update `rh[i′]← rh[i′] + (x(h, i′, j)− u1)` is convoluted. Specifically, the update for the played action `i1` seems to be zero, which is not how regret matching works. The standard update compares the utility of an action against the utility of the action that was actually played, which is not what is described here.

3.  **Issues with the Formal Analysis:**
    *   A critical simplifying assumption stated in Section 4 is to "disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree." This is a major leap that sidesteps the core challenge of MCTS analysis. The statistics gathered during the initial, incomplete tree-building phase directly influence which parts of the tree are explored. Ignoring this phase severely limits the practical relevance of the proof to the actual MCTS algorithm.
    *   The paper refers to an appendix with proofs available on arXiv. This is a clear and serious violation of the double-blind review policy. The paper must be self-contained, and external links that de-anonymize the authors are unacceptable.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The paper repeatedly refers to figures (Figure 1, Figure 3) that are not included in the provided text, making it impossible to evaluate the corresponding claims and experimental results fully.
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted with numerous inconsistencies and incomplete entries (e.g., `(AAMAS, , , )`). This demonstrates a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
    *   The notation is sometimes non-standard, such as using `〈0, 1〉` in Section 4.1.
    *   The experimental results in Section 5.1 show that the variant required by the proof (RMM) converges *slower* than the standard variant (RM). While an interesting finding, it raises questions about the practical utility of the analyzed algorithm, a point the authors do not discuss in depth.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper aims to solve an important theoretical problem and presents a potentially valuable high-level idea, the execution is critically flawed. The combination of fundamental inaccuracies in the problem definition, a confusing and likely incorrect algorithm description, a proof that relies on an over-simplifying assumption, and a flagrant violation of the double-blind review policy makes the paper unacceptable for publication at NIPS in its current form. The sheer volume of minor and major issues suggests the work is premature and has not been subjected to sufficient internal review.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
