Summary
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses any selection rule that is ε-Hannan consistent (HC) with guaranteed exploration. They prove that, if node backups propagate mean returns (rather than the last sample), the average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The proof proceeds by: (i) recalling convergence of HC algorithms in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors; and (iii) inductively composing these error bounds up the game tree. Empirically, they instantiate the template with Regret Matching and Exp3, show convergence on random instances, and examine small worst-case instances, observing that mean-backup variants converge slightly slower than single-sample backups but to similar exploitability limits under persistent exploration.

Relation to prior work
- MCTS/UCT has strong guarantees in sequential perfect-information games, but UCT can fail in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). This paper fills a theoretical gap for SM-MCTS.
- It leverages classic online-learning facts (HC → convergence to equilibrium in repeated games) and applies them to stacked matrix games with bounded error propagation.
- Alternative exact solvers (backward induction with alpha-beta pruning, double oracle) exist but can be costly or require evaluation functions; this work justifies simulation-based methods theoretically for this class.
- The connection to CFR-style methods is mentioned tangentially; CFR is not the main focus.

Quality
Strengths:
- The main asymptotic convergence theorem (to subgame-perfect approximate NE) is technically sound under the stated assumptions. The reduction to repeated games with bounded errors and the induction over depth are natural and convincing.
- Clear identification of when the proof requires mean backups, and a careful treatment of exploration and HC assumptions.
- Empirical results are consistent with the theory and include both random and (empirically) hard small cases; they report exploitability curves and analyze effects of mean backup.

Weaknesses/limitations:
- Assumptions are somewhat restrictive: two-player, zero-sum, perfect information; persistent exploration; and mean-value backups. The latter two can be at odds with common practical choices (e.g., decaying exploration, last-sample backups).
- The approximation bound scales as O(D^2 ε), which is likely pessimistic. No finite-time rates are provided; experiments don’t quantify dependence on D or branching factor beyond small sizes.
- The experimental evaluation is modest: small depths/branching factors, limited baselines (only RM and Exp3 variants), and no comparisons to strong alternative solvers (e.g., simultaneous alpha-beta, double oracle) or to CFR variants.
- Some algorithmic details are easy to misread: Algorithm 1 returns a joint action from Select, yet the text says each player acts independently; clarify implementation symmetry. The role of RetVal and the precise conditions under which mean backups are required should be emphasized in the main text (not just for proofs).

Clarity
- Generally well written, with formal definitions and a logical proof narrative. The appendix (arXiv) is essential for completeness; adding more proof sketches in the main text would help.
- Minor notational issues (e.g., Select returning joint vs independent actions; consistent use of average vs empirical strategies) could be clarified. Explicitly state the exploration model for both RM and Exp3 and its impact on the asymptotic policies.
- The empirical section would benefit from confidence intervals and clearer discussion of the asymptotic exploitability floor imposed by exploration.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first general asymptotic convergence guarantee for MCTS-like algorithms in simultaneous-move perfect-information games, tying HC selection to subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium. The repeated-game-with-error lemma and the inductive composition are a neat, if natural, contribution. Overall originality is moderate-to-high.

Significance
- The result provides long-missing theoretical support for SM-MCTS and will interest researchers in game-playing, online learning in games, and search. However, the practical impact is tempered by the restrictive assumptions, lack of finite-time rates, and need for mean backups and persistent exploration. Still, it is a valuable step that others can build upon (e.g., sharpening bounds, relaxing assumptions).

Pros
- Clear theoretical contribution: convergence of SM-MCTS with HC selection to subgame-perfect approximate NE.
- Simple, general template applicable to RM, Exp3, and other HC learners.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory and explores worst-case small instances.
- Bridges MCTS and online learning frameworks in stacked matrix games.

Cons
- Asymptotic, potentially loose bounds (O(D^2 ε)), no finite-time guarantees.
- Requires mean backups and persistent exploration; limited discussion of decaying exploration or standard single-sample backups beyond empirical note.
- Experiments are small-scale and lack comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., simultaneous alpha-beta, double oracle, CFR variants).
- Some clarity issues in the algorithmic description and evaluation methodology.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Can the dependence on depth be reduced from D^2 to O(D), or is D^2 tight? Any lower bounds?
- Is mean backup strictly necessary? Can you provide a counterexample for last-sample backups, or characterize when last-sample backups still work?
- Can you analyze decaying exploration schedules that vanish asymptotically?
- Provide finite-time convergence rates (even if pessimistic) based on known regret bounds for RM/Exp3 and your error-propagation analysis.
- Expand empirical evaluation: larger depths/branching factors, comparisons to simultaneous alpha-beta, double oracle, and CFR-style methods; include confidence bands.

Recommendation
Weak accept. This paper addresses an acknowledged theoretical gap with a clean, general result and reasonable empirical support. Despite limitations (asymptotic/loose bounds and constrained setting), the contribution is of interest to NeurIPS and likely to spur follow-up work on tighter analyses and broader classes. My confidence is medium-high.
