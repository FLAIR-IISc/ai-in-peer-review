**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the theoretical guarantees of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for an MCTS algorithm (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree selection policy is instantiated by a regret-minimizing procedure from online learning. The main contribution is a formal proof that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the average strategies produced by the algorithm converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The authors provide empirical results using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection functions, comparing the convergence speed on random and empirically-derived worst-case games.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant open problem. While MCTS is widely used in practice for various game types, theoretical convergence guarantees have largely been confined to sequential turn-taking games. Providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in this class of simultaneous-move games is a novel and important contribution to both the game theory and heuristic search communities.

2.  **Theoretical Soundness (in principle):** The overall proof strategy is elegant. It methodically builds the argument from the well-understood case of repeated matrix games, extends it to games with bounded payoff errors, and then uses induction to generalize the result to the multi-stage (deep) game tree setting. This approach provides a clear and principled framework for analyzing the convergence of learning-based algorithms in extensive-form games.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the significance of the core idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws in its technical exposition, clarity, and presentation. As a reviewer, I find these issues too numerous and significant to overlook.

1.  **Clarity and Technical Precision:**
    *   **Algorithm Description:** The description of the Regret Matching variant in Section 3.1 is critically unclear. The update rule `rh[i′]← rh[i′] + (x(h, i′, j)− u1)` is ambiguous. First, the term `x(h, i, j)` is defined using `Xhij`, a variable that is never itself defined. One might infer it relates to the mean value `Xhij/nhij`, but this should be stated explicitly. Second, the opponent's action `j` in this formula is not defined; presumably, it should be the action `j2` that was actually sampled. The entire update mechanism is non-standard for regret matching and requires a much clearer derivation and justification.
    *   **Pseudocode Errors:** The text describing Algorithm 1 contains incorrect line number references. It states that updates are on "lines 8 and 14" and selection is on "line 10". A direct inspection of the pseudocode shows the updates are on lines 7 and 13, and selection is on line 9. This is a careless error that impedes understanding.
    *   **Formalism:** The formalism in Section 2 has minor but notable inaccuracies. The probability of reaching a terminal state `z` is given as `πσ(z) = π1(z)π2(z)`. For a behavioral strategy profile in a game tree, the reach probability is the product of the probabilities of all actions (by both players) along the single path to `z`, not a product of two separate player-specific path probabilities. While the intent may be clear, this lack of precision is concerning in a theoretical paper.

2.  **Gaps in Theoretical Analysis:**
    *   The entire formal analysis in Section 4 is predicated on a critical simplifying assumption: "we disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree." The authors claim that guaranteed exploration ensures this happens with probability 1 and that "statistics collected during the tree building phase cannot prevent the eventual convergence." This is a very strong claim that sweeps a major technical challenge under the rug. The non-stationary nature of the value estimates *while the tree is being built* is a fundamental aspect of MCTS. To ignore it without a formal argument is a significant gap in the proof. The analysis effectively studies an algorithm operating on a fixed, known tree structure, which is not MCTS.
    *   The sketch of the main theorem's proof (4.11) is overly brief. The claim that summing deviation gains on each level leads to the `D^2` bound is hand-wavy. A more detailed argument showing how the error propagates up the tree is necessary for the paper to be self-contained and convincing.

3.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Experiments:**
    *   The formal analysis requires propagating the mean value of returns (`RetVal(u1, Xh, nh) = Xh/nh`), which the authors call the "M" variant (e.g., RMM). However, the experiments in Section 5.1 consistently show that this theoretically-sound variant converges more slowly than the standard MCTS approach of propagating the single sample's return (RM). This creates an uncomfortable disconnect: the algorithm that is proven to work is empirically inferior to a similar variant that lacks a proof. The paper fails to discuss the implications of this gap, which significantly weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical contribution.

4.  **Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The list of references is extremely sloppy, with multiple entries containing placeholder text like `(AAMAS, , , )` and `(AAAI-, , , )`. This suggests a rushed preparation and a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   The paper refers to Figures 1 and 3, which are essential for understanding the game structure and experimental results, but these figures were not included in the manuscript provided for review. This constitutes a critical omission.
    *   The use of an arXiv link for the appendix is poor practice for a conference submission, which should be evaluated based on the submitted materials.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and important theoretical result, being the first to prove MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move games.
*   The connection established between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence provides a valuable framework for future research.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The description of a core algorithm (Regret Matching variant) is ambiguous and difficult to reproduce.
*   The main theoretical analysis relies on an overly strong simplifying assumption (a fixed, complete tree) that is not formally justified, sidestepping a key challenge of MCTS.
*   Experiments show that the algorithm variant required for the proof is empirically worse than a standard variant that is not covered by the proof, creating a theory-practice gap that is left unexplored.
*   The paper is rife with minor errors, including incorrect line references, notational inaccuracies, and severely malformed citations, indicating a lack of care.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the central idea of this paper is strong and addresses a significant problem, the execution is deeply flawed. The combination of a critically unclear algorithm description, a major unjustified assumption in the theoretical analysis, a troubling disconnect between the proven algorithm and empirical performance, and a general lack of polish and attention to detail leads me to recommend rejection. A thorough revision that clarifies the algorithms, properly addresses the tree-building phase in the analysis, discusses the theory-practice gap, and corrects the numerous presentation errors would be required to make this paper acceptable.
