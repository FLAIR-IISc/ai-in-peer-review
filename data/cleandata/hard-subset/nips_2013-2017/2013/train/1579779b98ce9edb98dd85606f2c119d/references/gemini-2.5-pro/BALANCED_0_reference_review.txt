This paper addresses the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors correctly note that while MCTS variants are often applied to this class of games, their theoretical guarantees are not well understood. In fact, prior work has shown that the popular UCT selection strategy fails to converge to a Nash equilibrium even in simple one-stage simultaneous move games.

The main contribution of this work is to provide the first formal proof of convergence for an MCTS-based algorithm in this game class. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that uses a regret-minimizing procedure as its in-tree selection policy. Their central theorem proves that if the selection policy is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the resulting strategies converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is built elegantly by first analyzing the behavior of regret minimizers in repeated matrix games, then extending this to games where payoffs have bounded error (to model the noisy value estimates from subgame rollouts), and finally applying an inductive argument over the depth of the game tree. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as instantiations of their framework, testing them on randomly generated and worst-case games.

### Strengths

1.  **Fundamental Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a significant and novel theoretical result, filling a long-standing gap in the literature. It places the use of MCTS in this important game class on solid theoretical ground, moving beyond empirical success to provide a formal justification for why certain variants should work.

2.  **Generality and Elegance of the Framework:** The proof is not tied to a single algorithm but applies to a broad class of MCTS variants based on any $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent selection rule. The proof technique itself, which models the noisy estimates from child nodes as a repeated game with bounded error, is insightful and could potentially be adapted to analyze other recursive, sampling-based algorithms.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The theoretical analysis appears to be sound. The step-by-step construction of the argument from simple matrix games to full extensive-form games is logical and easy to follow. The definitions of $\epsilon$-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration are appropriate for the problem setting.

4.  **Thorough and Honest Experimental Analysis:** The empirical section is well-executed. The authors not only demonstrate that their proposed algorithms converge but also carefully analyze the practical trade-offs. The comparison between propagating the mean value (required for the proof) and the current sample value (common in practice) is insightful. Furthermore, the search for and analysis of "worst-case" games demonstrates a commitment to rigorously understanding the algorithm's performance limitations.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas where it could be improved or where future work could be directed.

1.  **Asymptotic Convergence and Lack of Rate:** The main theorem is an asymptotic result, proving that the algorithm *eventually* converges. However, it provides no theoretical bounds on the rate of convergence. The empirical results suggest that convergence can be quite slow, requiring up to a million iterations to approach the equilibrium in small games (depth 2, branching 2). While deriving a finite-time bound is likely very challenging, acknowledging this limitation more explicitly and discussing the practical implications would be beneficial. A discussion of the factors that might influence the convergence rate (e.g., the specific game structure, the value of $\gamma$) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice in Update Rule:** The proof formally requires propagating the *mean* of rewards from a node (`RetVal = Xh/nh`). However, the experiments consistently show that this "M" variant is slightly slower than the standard MCTS approach of propagating the *current sample* (`RetVal = u1`). This creates a slight disconnect, where the provably correct algorithm is not the empirically superior one. The authors do a good job of showing this, but could go further by conjecturing why the sample-based variant also appears to converge and discussing the challenges in extending the proof to cover that case.

3.  **Potential Looseness of the Error Bound:** The final bound on the equilibrium approximation quality scales with $\mathcal{O}(D^2)$, where D is the maximum depth of the game. The inductive argument suggests error is accumulated at each level of the tree. It would be helpful if the authors could briefly comment on whether they believe this quadratic dependence on depth is fundamental to the problem or an artifact of the proof technique. Is it possible to achieve a tighter bound, perhaps linear in D, with a different analysis? This is a key parameter for the applicability of the algorithm to deeper games.

4.  **Contextualization with CFR:** The approach of using regret minimization within a game tree search is conceptually related to the Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) family of algorithms, which are the state of the art for imperfect information games. A short paragraph discussing the similarities and differences (e.g., MCTS is an on-policy sampling method, while vanilla CFR traverses the whole tree) would help readers situate this work within the broader context of regret-minimization-based game solvers.

### Summary

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a foundational contribution to the theory of game tree search. The strengths—a novel, general, and significant theoretical result backed by solid experiments—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future research. The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and addresses a well-motivated problem. It advances our understanding of MCTS and provides a template for designing provably convergent algorithms for an important class of games.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   First formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
*   The theoretical framework is general and based on the well-understood property of Hannan consistency.
*   The proof structure is elegant and insightful.
*   The paper is complemented by a thorough and honest empirical study.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The convergence result is asymptotic and lacks a rate, with empirical results suggesting slow convergence.
*   The theory requires a specific update rule that is shown to be empirically slightly slower than the standard one.

I recommend acceptance. The contributions are significant, and the paper meets the high standards of NeurIPS for quality, clarity, originality, and significance.
