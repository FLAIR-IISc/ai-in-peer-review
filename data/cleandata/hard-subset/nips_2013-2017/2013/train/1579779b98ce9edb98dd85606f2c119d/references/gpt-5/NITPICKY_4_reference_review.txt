Summary
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which each node’s simultaneous action selection is driven by a regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games (e.g., Regret Matching or Exp3), possibly with added exploration. The key theoretical contribution is an eventual-convergence result: if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent (HC) and guarantees that every joint action is sampled infinitely often, then SM-MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds bottom-up: first establish convergence of HC procedures in repeated matrix games; then extend to repeated matrix games with bounded errors; finally compose error bounds inductively along the tree to obtain the overall approximation bound (stated as O(D^2) in the depth D). Empirically, the paper compares “sample” vs “mean” backup variants and observes both converge in random and worst-case depth-2 games, with the mean-backup variant slightly slower.

Relation to prior work: The work builds on bandit/online learning regret results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Exp3), and the practical success of MCTS/UCT (Kocsis & Szepesvári), while noting that UCB can fail in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). It complements exact backward induction/pruning for simultaneous moves (Saffidine et al.; Bošanský et al.) and argues for convergence guarantees for simulation-based methods in that class.

Strengths
- Conceptually clear template: SM-MCTS with any ε-HC selection rule and explicit exploration.
- First rigorous convergence-to-equilibrium result for SM-MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games (to my knowledge).
- Bottom-up proof plan (matrix games → matrix games with error → stacked games) is natural and reusable.
- Practical relevance: shows that widely used regret-based selection (RM/Exp3) is justified, at least asymptotically; experiments broadly consistent with theory.
- Subgame-perfect equilibrium claim is stronger than root-only convergence.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and completeness
- Constants and missing ε factors: Several statements appear to drop explicit dependence on ε. E.g., Corollary 4.7 says strategies “form (4 + δ)-equilibrium,” seemingly missing a multiplicative ε. Theorem 4.10 has (4(c+1)+δ)-equilibrium with no ε. Theorem 4.11 states (ε D^2 + δ)-NE but earlier sections suggest factors 2 and 4 accumulating; it is unclear how the constants propagate and why the +1 term appears in c+1. These constants need to be carefully stated, justified, and made consistent throughout.
- “Repeated games with error” (Def. 4.8) assumes that for some t0 all observed payoffs remain within η of a fixed matrix M. In the MCTS setting, children’s policies evolve, so the induced payoffs are nonstationary; the assumption that they eventually stay within a uniform η (independent of t and action pair) should be argued more carefully. The inductive argument sketch mentions errors “in the order of d” but does not quantify how mean backups and exploration ensure the eventual boundedness required by Def. 4.8.
- “Mean” vs “sample” backups: The theory requires returning Xh/nh; experiments show “sample” backups also work empirically, but there is no theoretical bridge. The paper should either prove convergence under sample backups (even with worse constants) or clearly delineate when the sample variant can fail.
- Exploration modification (Def. 4.3): The exploration step “without updating any variables belonging to A” is nonstandard and may degrade practical regret. The paper proves (ε+γ)-HC for this variant, but does not discuss whether standard on-policy mixing (as in Exp3) would suffice for guaranteed exploration in the joint-action sense (Definition 4.2) and preserve better constants.
- Tree construction assumption: The proof “disregards the incremental building of the tree” and assumes the full tree is built eventually. This is plausible, but a precise argument is needed: the root is visited infinitely often; with guaranteed exploration, each joint action at a visited node is chosen infinitely often; hence every child is expanded; an induction shows all nodes are expanded infinitely often. This should be formalized to ensure no subtlety with expansion thresholds.
- Empirical evaluation methodology: For random games, it is unclear how best responses and the true value vh0 are computed (exactly via backward induction/LP? on which sizes?). For the “worst-case” search, only depth-2, branching factor 2 with payoffs in {0, 0.5, 1} is explored. The experimental scope is modest and does not assess robustness to larger branching factors/depths beyond small random instances. No variance/confidence intervals are reported. The claim that the plateau equals the exploration floor (e.g., 0.05) is asserted but not derived or tested across γ.
- Scope of class: Goofspiel is cited as an instance of the considered class, but the standard game involves private cards (imperfect information). This should be clarified (e.g., open-hand variant) to avoid confusion.

Clarity and presentation
- Notation inconsistencies and typos: 
  - T is used for both the tree and the transition function; this is confusing. Use different symbols.
  - In Alg. 1, references to Update and RetVal are unclear (e.g., line 8 vs line 14), and the return of RetVal(u1, Xh′, nh′) after expansion is odd; tighten pseudocode and define Update precisely.
  - Eq. (3): “oth.” likely means “otherwise”; please write it out. γ-on-policy sampling phrasing is unclear.
  - Eq. (4): “wh,i = xh,i1.” appears to be a typo; likely wh,i = xh,i, and η = γ/|A1(h)|.
  - Theorem 4.11 refers to “average strategies (σ̂1, σ̂2)” but σ̂ is defined as empirical frequencies; average strategies were denoted with bars. Use consistent hat/bar notation.
  - Many spelling errors: “arbitrarly,” “Eolutionary,” etc.
  - Interval notation uses angle brackets 〈0, 1〉 in some places and [0,1] in others.
- References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted (missing years, venues, pages; inconsistent punctuation/capitalization). This needs a thorough pass.

Originality and significance
- The setting and result (asymptotic convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE using HC bandits) are, to my knowledge, novel and relevant. However, the asymptotic nature, loose constants (O(D^2) dependence and factors 2/4), and requirement of mean backups limit immediate practical impact. No finite-time rates are provided.
- The empirical results, while suggestive, are limited in scope and do not compare against strong baselines like CFR/CFR+ or exact backward induction with pruning (beyond qualitative discussion).

Pros for acceptance
- First rigorous convergence analysis for SM-MCTS in this class; conceptually important.
- General template easily reuses standard HC algorithms.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee (not only at root).
- Empirical evidence aligns with the theory’s qualitative predictions.

Cons for acceptance
- Several technical statements are imprecise or inconsistent (missing ε factors, constants).
- Key proof steps are deferred to an appendix and only sketched here; the paper as written is hard to verify.
- Algorithmic description has ambiguities/typos and a symbol collision (T).
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale, with limited diagnostics and incomplete methodological details.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues and reference problems.

Questions for the authors
1. Please clarify and correct all constants in Lem. 4.6, Cor. 4.7, Thm. 4.10, and Thm. 4.11, explicitly tracking ε and c.
2. Can you formalize the argument ensuring full-tree expansion under the stated exploration condition?
3. Is there any theoretical handle on convergence with sample backups?
4. How are best responses and game values computed in experiments for larger random games?
5. Does the class include only perfect-information games? If so, please remove or qualify Goofspiel unless using an open-hand variant.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The core idea is interesting and potentially impactful, but the current manuscript has too many technical and presentational issues (constants, notation, pseudocode, references) and limited empirical validation. A careful revision correcting the theory, tightening the algorithmic description, and expanding/clarifying experiments would substantially strengthen the submission.
