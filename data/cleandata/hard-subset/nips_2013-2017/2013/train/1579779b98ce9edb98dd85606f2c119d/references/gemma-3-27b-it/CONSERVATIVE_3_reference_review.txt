This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The core contribution is demonstrating that an MCTS algorithm utilizing a -Hannan consistent action selection procedure will converge to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. While the problem setting is relevant, and the convergence result potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a lack of novelty in its theoretical contributions and a somewhat underwhelming empirical evaluation.

The primary strength lies in formalizing a convergence guarantee for a class of MCTS algorithms that have been applied empirically without such backing. The connection to -Hannan consistency provides a clear theoretical foundation. However, the result itself feels incremental. The core idea of using regret-minimizing procedures within MCTS isn't new (and is referenced), and the extension to simultaneous-move games, while non-trivial, doesn’t represent a fundamental breakthrough. The proof relies heavily on existing results regarding Hannan consistency and extends them to the multi-stage game setting, but the leap feels more like an application of established theory than a novel theoretical advancement. A significant portion of the analysis feels like a reiteration of known results in the context of this specific game class.

The paper’s clarity is reasonable, though the extensive use of notation, particularly in the formal analysis section, makes it difficult to follow without careful attention. The organization is logical, progressing from definitions and background to the algorithm description, theoretical analysis, and empirical results. However, the transition between the theoretical and empirical sections is somewhat abrupt.

Regarding originality, the paper's main weakness is that it doesn’t offer a significantly novel theoretical framework. It leverages existing concepts and adapts them to a specific game class. The empirical evaluation doesn’t strongly showcase a significant advantage of the proposed framework over existing approaches, further diminishing the sense of novelty. The authors acknowledge this somewhat by conjecturing that many existing algorithms also converge under similar conditions.

The significance of this work is moderate. While providing a theoretical justification for commonly used MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games is valuable, the convergence guarantee is approximate and relies on the relatively strong assumption of -Hannan consistency. The empirical results don’t demonstrate a substantial performance improvement over existing methods. The practical impact, therefore, is likely to be limited. The authors rightly point towards future work in generalizing the results to imperfect-information games, which would greatly increase the significance of the work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which is currently lacking.
*   Connects the convergence to well-established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency).
*   The empirical evaluation, while not groundbreaking, provides some validation of the theoretical results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution feels incremental and primarily involves applying existing theory to a new setting.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn’t demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   The convergence guarantee is approximate and relies on relatively strong assumptions.
*   The paper lacks a strong discussion of the limitations of the convergence rate.

**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper is technically sound and addresses a relevant problem, it lacks sufficient novelty and significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The theoretical contribution, while correct, is incremental, and the empirical results are not compelling. I encourage the authors to focus on either strengthening the theoretical results (e.g., providing tighter convergence bounds or extending the analysis to more general game classes) or conducting a more comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating a clear advantage over existing approaches. The current work feels more suitable for a more specialized conference or journal.
