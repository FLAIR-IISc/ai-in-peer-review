This paper addresses the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a challenging area with limited theoretical understanding despite the practical success of MCTS variants. The authors establish a general template for MCTS algorithms leveraging regret minimizing procedures for matrix games and prove convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under certain consistency conditions. The empirical evaluation demonstrates convergence speeds comparable to existing algorithms.

The core contribution – a convergence proof for a class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games – is potentially valuable. The connection to Hannan consistency and regret minimizing procedures provides a solid theoretical foundation. However, my assessment is largely skeptical due to the substantial gap between the theoretical result and practical applicability, combined with a lack of compelling novelty beyond the theoretical connection. 

The paper's strengths lie in its mathematical rigor. The proof, though summarized, appears sound, building upon established results in online learning and game theory. The authors clearly articulate the assumptions necessary for convergence (Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) and relate these to existing algorithms like Regret Matching and Exp3. The empirical evaluation, while not exhaustive, provides a preliminary validation of the theoretical findings.

However, the weaknesses are significant. First, the requirement of "-Hannan consistency" is quite strong. While algorithms like Regret Matching *are* Hannan consistent, this property doesn’t automatically translate to efficient or practical performance, particularly in complex games.  The constant factor in the approximation guarantee (D^2) – stemming from the depth of the game tree – is likely prohibitive for all but the shallowest games.  The practical impact of this theoretical result feels limited. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by comparing to existing algorithms without demonstrating a clear advantage.

Second, the paper lacks a strong motivation for the specific MCTS template chosen. The authors present it as “general”, but don’t convincingly demonstrate why this particular formulation is superior to other existing MCTS approaches for simultaneous-move games (e.g., those using UCB, despite its known convergence issues). The claim that "many" existing algorithms might also converge under their analysis is speculative and lacks justification. 

Third, the empirical evaluation is insufficient.  The randomly generated games and “worst case” games tested are not clearly defined, and the results presented are largely descriptive.  A more rigorous comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms (with statistical significance testing) is needed to demonstrate the practical relevance of the proposed approach. The observation that RMM converges slightly slower than RM is concerning and deserves further investigation. The results primarily showcase *that* convergence happens, but offer little insight into *how fast* or under what conditions. The choice of γ=0.05 is somewhat arbitrary and could significantly impact performance.

Finally, the paper is dense and suffers from a lack of intuitive explanation. The connection between the theoretical results and practical implementation could be made more accessible. The formal analysis section is heavily laden with notation, making it difficult to follow the key arguments.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical justification for MCTS in a relatively unexplored domain (simultaneous-move games).
*   Connects MCTS convergence to established concepts in online learning (Hannan consistency).
*   The mathematical analysis appears rigorous.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical result has limited practical impact due to the strong assumptions and large approximation factor.
*   Lacks a compelling demonstration of superiority over existing algorithms.
*   Insufficient empirical evaluation.
*   Dense and difficult to follow.
*   The "generality" of the template is not convincingly demonstrated.

**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the theoretical contribution is non-trivial, the practical limitations and lack of compelling empirical evidence outweigh the benefits. The paper feels like a mathematically interesting result in search of a compelling application.  The authors need to significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation, demonstrate a clear advantage over existing approaches, and provide a more accessible presentation of their work before it would be suitable for NIPS. I strongly suggest the authors consider focusing on specific game classes where the theoretical guarantees are more meaningful and demonstrate substantial practical improvements.
