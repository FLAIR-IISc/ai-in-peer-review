

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures all actions are tried infinitely often), the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The authors empirically validate their theoretical claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection procedures, comparing the convergence behavior of propagating mean values versus current sample values.

The paper builds on previous NIPS work in MCTS (e.g., Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006 on UCT) but addresses a critical gap: while MCTS has been widely applied to sequential games, no convergence guarantees existed for simultaneous move games. The authors properly contextualize their work against related NIPS papers on MCTS in sequential games and against game-theoretic work on Hannan consistency (e.g., Hart and Mas-Colell, 2000).

**Strengths:**
1. **Technical Soundness:** The formal proof is well-structured and addresses a meaningful theoretical gap. The authors establish a rigorous connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence through careful analysis of matrix games with bounded error, then extend this to the multi-stage extensive-form setting via induction. The proof techniques are sound, and the connection to established game theory concepts is well-motivated.

2. **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized with clear definitions, consistent notation, and logical flow from simple matrix games to complex extensive-form games. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1, 3.1, 3.2) are precise and well-explained. The supplementary material contains omitted proofs, which is helpful for verification.

3. **Novelty:** This is the first work to provide convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While previous NIPS papers (e.g., Shafiei et al., 2012) noted that UCT doesn't converge in these games, the authors provide a positive theoretical result for a broader class of MCTS algorithms. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS is novel and well-justified.

4. **Significance:** The results provide theoretical justification for using MCTS in applications involving simultaneous move games (e.g., security domains, game-playing competitions). The authors demonstrate that many existing MCTS algorithms for these games likely converge to ε-Nash equilibrium, which is a meaningful contribution to algorithmic game theory.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation focuses primarily on depth-2 games with small branching factors. While the authors perform a brute-force search for worst-case games, the results don't generalize to deeper or more complex games. The paper doesn't compare against other state-of-the-art algorithms for simultaneous move games, making it difficult to assess practical significance.

2. **Convergence Rate:** The paper proves eventual convergence but doesn't provide finite-time bounds or analyze the rate of convergence. This is a significant limitation for practical applications where convergence speed matters.

3. **Practical Implementation Details:** The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed method compares to existing implementations in terms of computational overhead or memory requirements. The γ parameter for exploration is used without thorough analysis of its impact on performance.

**Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Addresses a significant theoretical gap in MCTS for simultaneous move games (previously unaddressed in NIPS literature).
- Provides a general framework that unifies multiple MCTS variants under a common theoretical foundation.
- The formal analysis is technically sound and well-executed, with clear connections to established game theory concepts.
- Empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate practical relevance.

*Con:*
- Limited empirical evaluation on game structures beyond small depth-2 cases.
- No analysis of convergence rates or practical computational considerations.
- Doesn't compare against existing state-of-the-art algorithms for simultaneous move games.

The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory and MCTS. It successfully bridges the gap between online learning (Hannan consistency) and game tree search in a specific but important class of games. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is substantial and well-justified. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a foundation for future work on convergence rates and generalization to imperfect information games.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (quality), well-written and organized (clarity), provides a novel theoretical result for a specific class of games (originality), and addresses an important theoretical gap with practical implications (significance). The authors carefully acknowledge limitations and properly contextualize their work against prior NIPS publications.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field that should be of interest to the NIPS community. The results provide a solid foundation for future work on MCTS in game theory applications.
