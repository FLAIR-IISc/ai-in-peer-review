This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors establish a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting, showing that convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium is guaranteed when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function. The analysis is complemented by empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3, demonstrating comparable convergence speeds to existing MCTS algorithms.

The paper is well-structured and clearly presents the problem, related work, and the proposed analysis. The introduction effectively motivates the need for theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, where existing approaches often rely on domain knowledge or computationally expensive methods. The definitions and background section is thorough and provides the necessary mathematical foundation. The presentation of the SM-MCTS algorithm is clear, and the variants using regret matching and Exp3 are well explained. The formal analysis section is the core contribution and appears rigorous, building from repeated matrix games with bounded error to the general case of extensive-form games. The empirical evaluation provides support for the theoretical results.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a significant theoretical result by establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. This addresses a key open question in the field.
*   **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general and can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures, offering flexibility.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The formal analysis is well-structured and appears mathematically sound, with clear definitions and lemmas leading to the main theorem.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it relatively easy to follow the complex mathematical arguments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Significance of ε:** While the theoretical convergence guarantee is valuable, the paper doesn’t deeply explore the practical implications of the ε value and the factors influencing its magnitude. The constant factors involved in the convergence bound (e.g., D^2) might be large enough to render the theoretical guarantees less meaningful in practice for complex games.
*   **Limited Comparison:** The empirical comparison focuses on comparing the proposed algorithms with recent MCTS algorithms. A more extensive comparison with other game-solving techniques like backward induction (even with limitations) could provide further insights.
*   **Numerical Stability:** The mention of modifying the Exp3 formulation for numerical stability is a good point, but a discussion of potential issues arising from this modification would be beneficial.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of perfect information is strong. Discussing the potential challenges and directions for extending the analysis to imperfect information games could strengthen the work.
*    **Detailed Worst-Case Game Analysis:** While the authors analyze worst-case games, the analysis lacks a deeper understanding of *why* these are the worst-case games. Are there structural properties of these games that make them particularly challenging for MCTS?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of game theory and MCTS.
*   The theoretical contribution is substantial and provides a solid foundation for future research.
*   The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates the practical relevance of the proposed approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The practical implications of the convergence guarantees are not fully explored, particularly concerning the magnitude of ε.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, including comparisons with alternative game-solving techniques.
*   The analysis relies on strong assumptions (perfect information) that limit its applicability to real-world scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The convergence guarantees are a significant result, and the paper is well-written and presented. While there are some limitations regarding the practical implications and scope of the analysis, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above. Specifically, the authors should discuss the practical significance of the ε value, provide a more comprehensive empirical comparison, and acknowledge the limitations of the perfect information assumption.
