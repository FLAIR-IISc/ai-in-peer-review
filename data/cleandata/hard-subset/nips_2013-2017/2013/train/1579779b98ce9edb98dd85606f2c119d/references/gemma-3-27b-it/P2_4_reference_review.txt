## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) – its convergence properties in simultaneous-move games. The authors present a general MCTS template for zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games and prove that, when instantiated with an -Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium.  They empirically validate this result using Regret Matching and Exp3 as the regret minimizing procedures, demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to existing approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Fundamental Open Problem:** The lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a long-standing issue. This work provides the first rigorous convergence analysis, filling a crucial gap in the literature.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general, allowing for instantiation with *any* -Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure. This broad applicability enhances the practical relevance of the findings.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The proof of convergence, building on Hannan consistency, is well-structured and appears mathematically sound (though a detailed verification would require more time). The lemmas and theorems are clearly stated and logically connected.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results, while not exhaustive, provide evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The comparison against existing approaches in the field (even if only implicitly) is valuable.  The analysis of worst-case games is a particularly strong point.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions and background are clearly presented, making the core ideas accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper *proves* convergence, it doesn't provide explicit bounds on the convergence rate. This is a significant limitation. The empirical results offer some insight, but a theoretical rate would be highly desirable.
* **Assumptions:** The reliance on -Hannan consistency is a strong assumption. While reasonable, it's not universally satisfied by all regret minimization algorithms.  The impact of this assumption on the practical performance of different MCTS instantiations isn't fully explored.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experimental section, while helpful, is somewhat limited. The number of games tested (particularly in the worst-case analysis) could be larger, and the comparison to more sophisticated MCTS algorithms tailored for simultaneous-move games would be beneficial.
* **Practical Implications of Mean vs. Current Sample:** The experimentation around propagating the mean vs. the current sample value, while insightful, feels a little tangential to the main theoretical contribution. The difference in convergence speed seems minor, and the theoretical implications aren’t deeply explored.
* **Potential for Extension:** While the authors mention that their analysis *might* extend to other algorithms, the paper doesn’t detail how this could be done.  A more concrete discussion of the challenges and opportunities for extending the analysis would be valuable.



**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous and the experimental results are well-presented. However, the lack of explicit convergence rate bounds weakens the overall impact. **Score: 8/10**

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The notation is standard and the definitions are precise.  The logical flow is easy to follow. **Score: 9/10**

**Originality:**  The theoretical contribution – proving the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games – is highly original. While MCTS has been applied to these games before, this is the first rigorous analysis of its convergence properties. **Score: 9/10**

**Significance:** The results are significant.  They provide a theoretical foundation for the use of MCTS in a class of games where it is often the algorithm of choice, but previously lacked theoretical justification. This will likely encourage further research in this area. **Score: 8/10**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Solves a long-standing open problem in the field.
* Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for a widely-used algorithm.
* General framework applicable to different regret minimization procedures.
* Empirically validated results.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of explicit convergence rate bounds.
* Reliance on a strong assumption (-Hannan consistency).
* Limited empirical scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS. While the lack of explicit convergence rate bounds is a weakness, the originality and significance of the results outweigh this concern.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss the potential for deriving or approximating convergence rate bounds, even if it's just outlining the challenges involved.
2.  Expand the empirical evaluation to include comparisons with more state-of-the-art MCTS algorithms specifically designed for simultaneous-move games.
3.  Elaborate on the potential for extending the analysis to other MCTS variants.
