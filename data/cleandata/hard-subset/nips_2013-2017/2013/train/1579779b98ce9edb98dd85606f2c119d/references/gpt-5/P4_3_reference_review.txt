Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that, at each node, uses any ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) no-regret selection procedure (e.g., Regret Matching (RM) [15] or Exp3 [16]) with guaranteed exploration. The main theoretical result shows that when SM-MCTS backs up mean returns and each node’s selection algorithm is ε-HC with guaranteed exploration, the empirical/average strategies produced by self-play converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the game-tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary.

The proof reduces the multi-stage setting to repeated matrix games. It first shows that adding uniform exploration preserves approximate HC (ε → ε+γ), then establishes that self-play between ε-HC learners in (i) exact repeated matrix games and (ii) repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors converges to an approximate NE with explicit constants. By induction on depth, the paper bounds error propagation up the tree, yielding the D-dependent guarantee for the EFG. Empirically, the paper compares RM/Exp3 with either sample or mean backups on random games and on searched “hard” depth-2, branching-factor-2 games. Both variants converge; mean backups converge slightly slower.

The work relates to and contrasts with: UCT [14], which is known to fail to converge to NE in simultaneous-move games [17]; exact backward-induction-based methods with LP or double-oracle selection [8, 9]; and no-regret learning (RM [15], Exp3 [16]) in matrix games. To the authors’ knowledge, this is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games.

Strengths
- Clear and general template: any ε-HC selection method with guaranteed exploration can be plugged in.
- Formal convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE, addressing a known gap where UCT fails.
- Clean proof strategy via repeated games with bounded error and an induction over depth.
- Empirical results support claims and examine both RM and Exp3, and both sample vs mean backups.
- Discussion of practical variants (exploration mixing, average vs empirical strategies), with relevant lemmas (e.g., empirical vs average strategies).

Weaknesses
- Asymptotic guarantees only; no finite-time sample-complexity or rates.
- Theoretical constants appear loose: the (ε D^2) dependence and factors like 4(c+1). It is unclear whether O(D) is achievable.
- The mean-backup requirement is nonstandard relative to typical SM-MCTS practice; formal guarantees do not include the widely used sample-backup variant, although experiments suggest it also converges.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small synthetic games; lacks comparisons against strong baselines in established benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) and lacks direct head-to-head against recent SM-MCTS variants beyond RM/Exp3.
- Proof details are largely deferred to the appendix; some definitions/notation could be tightened in the main paper.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The reduction from EFG to repeated matrix games with bounded error and the use of HC properties are standard and correctly deployed. The exploration-modified algorithm A* and the preservation of HC (ε+γ) are plausible, and the bounded-error analysis is consistent with online learning literature. The induction argument for error propagation is reasonable, though the D^2 factor deserves clarification/justification and potentially tighter analysis.
- Completeness: The main claims are supported by lemmas and a theorem, with proofs deferred. The empirical section supports qualitative convergence but does not address finite-time behavior rigorously.
- Evaluation of weaknesses: The paper itself acknowledges the lack of finite-time bounds and the conservativeness of mean backups.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable, with a clear algorithmic template. However:
  - Specify early and consistently the two backup options (current sample vs mean) and which one is required for each theorem; explicitly name the enforced variant (e.g., SM-MCTS-M) in Algorithm 1 or provide a separate algorithm box.
  - Unify notation for empirical vs average strategies and avoid switching between σ̂ and σ̄ without reminders; Lemma 4.5 is helpful but could be stated earlier.
  - Clarify the “guaranteed exploration” property and provide a brief argument that the tree becomes complete almost surely.
  - Explain the source of the D^2 factor more explicitly in the main text.
  - Minor editorial issues: a few typos/formatting glitches (e.g., “oth.” in Eq. (3); line numbers and returns in Algorithm 1; reference formatting).

Originality
- The convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move games appears novel. Prior work showed UCT’s failure [17], and exact approaches relied on backward induction with LP/double oracle [8, 9]. The generality of allowing any ε-HC selection procedure is also a plus.

Significance
- The theoretical guarantee fills an acknowledged gap and can influence algorithm design in simultaneous-move settings, offering a principled alternative to UCT-like selection. However, practical impact is limited by asymptotic nature, lack of finite-time rates, and the mean-backup requirement. Extensions to imperfect information or multi-player settings, or provision of rates, would increase impact.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can the depth dependence be tightened from O(D^2) to O(D)? If not, provide an example showing D^2 is necessary, or discuss obstacles.
- Is mean backup strictly necessary for the theory, or can similar guarantees be shown for sample backups under additional conditions (e.g., variance control or mini-batching)?
- Provide finite-time high-probability bounds for convergence in repeated games with bounded error, and then translate them to the tree via the same induction.
- Include experiments on standard benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel [3,4], Tron [12]) and compare against other SM-MCTS variants (e.g., decoupled sampling), backward induction with pruning [8, 9], and CFR-style baselines where applicable.
- Report sensitivity to exploration parameter γ and selection hyperparameters; discuss trade-offs between exploration and convergence speed.
- Clarify reward normalization for Exp3 (assumes [0,1]) and whether rescaling is needed in deeper trees.

Pros for acceptance
- First formal convergence result for SM-MCTS in this setting.
- General, modular template leveraging established ε-HC algorithms.
- Subgame-perfect equilibrium guarantee, not just root value convergence.
- Clear connection to online learning theory; proofs plausible.

Cons for acceptance
- Only asymptotic guarantees with loose constants; no finite-time rates.
- Mean-backup requirement reduces practical alignment with common MCTS implementations.
- Limited empirical evaluation and missing baseline comparisons on established domains.
- Proof details mostly in appendix; some clarity/notation issues.

Minor comments
- Define RMM/Exp3M acronyms when first used.
- Fix small typos and unify reference formatting.
- Explicitly state that utilities are scaled to [0,1] and discuss implications for general ranges.
