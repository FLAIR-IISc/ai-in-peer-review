Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”) and analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in this setting. The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template where, at each node, each player selects actions via any Hannan-consistent (HC) selection algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching (RM) or Exp3). A key technical design is to back up mean returns rather than single-sample returns. The main theoretical result shows that, under these conditions, the empirical/average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε-NE, with an approximation bound that scales with depth D as O(D^2 ε) (Theorem 4.11). The analysis proceeds by: (i) reviewing convergence of HC algorithms in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded error; and (iii) inducting up the game tree, showing that each level’s bounded error yields approximate equilibria at higher levels. Empirically, the paper compares mean-backup vs sample-backup variants using RM and Exp3 on random and small worst-case games, showing both converge, with mean-backup slightly slower.

This work connects to: UCT and MCTS theory in sequential perfect-information games (Kocsis & Szepesvári), which does not cover simultaneous moves; prior observations that UCB can fail in simultaneous-move matrix games (Shafiei et al.); alpha-beta pruning/double-oracle approaches for simultaneous-move games (Saffidine et al., Bosansky et al.) that require evaluation functions/LPs; and HC selection dynamics leading to (approximate) equilibria in single-shot matrix games (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.). The paper appears to be the first to provide convergence guarantees for MCTS to approximate NE in this class of games. It would help to also cite regret-minimization in EFGs (e.g., CFR), though that targets imperfect-information settings.

Quality
- Theory: The statements are plausible and technically sound, with clear decomposition: HC convergence in matrix games; bounded-error extension; induction across depth. The requirement to propagate mean returns is essential to control error accumulation; the argument that the tree becomes fully expanded almost surely under guaranteed exploration is reasonable (details deferred to the appendix). The final bound is conservative (O(D^2 ε) plus small δ), and the dependence on exploration is discussed empirically (exploitability floor).
- Experiments: The empirical section is limited but consistent with the theory: mean-backup converges slightly slower; worst-case small games show convergence to the floor imposed by uniform exploration. However, experiments are restricted to synthetic small games; no larger or real benchmarks (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel) are included.
- Rigor vs practice: The theoretical guarantee requires mean backups; most practical SM-MCTS implementations use sample backups. The authors provide empirical evidence that sample-backup variants also converge, but a proof is not given.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The SM-MCTS pseudocode and node statistics are explained, as are the RM and Exp3 instantiations. Some notation and typos could be cleaned up (e.g., “oth.”, consistent naming of “SM-MCTS-M”, clarifying RetVal choices). It would be useful to more explicitly state the assumption and consequence that the full tree is eventually expanded under guaranteed exploration, and to clearly define whether empirical frequencies or average strategies are used when reporting exploitability (Sec. 5 mentions both in different places).

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first convergence result for MCTS to approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The reduction to repeated games with bounded error and the inductive argument across tree depth is a novel and neat way to connect online learning guarantees to MCTS in this setting. The empirical section is not novel methodologically but supports the theoretical claims.

Significance
The result provides theoretical justification for SM-MCTS with HC selection in a class of games where MCTS is widely used and prior theory is lacking. The asymptotic nature and loose bounds limit immediate practical impact, but the work should be of interest to researchers in game-theoretic planning and MCTS, and it may stimulate better-designed selection/back-up rules and further theoretical work (e.g., finite-time rates).

Strengths
- First convergence guarantee for MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information games.
- General template applicable to any HC selection algorithm with exploration; instantiated with RM and Exp3.
- Clear proof structure linking HC dynamics, bounded-error games, and induction across depth.
- Sensible empirical checks, including worst-case small games and discussion of exploration-induced exploitability floors.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time rates. Suggest adding at least heuristic rate discussion or a pointer to bounds for HC algorithms that could translate to depth-dependent rates.
- The D^2 ε bound is likely loose. Clarify whether tighter O(D ε) or node-dependent bounds are possible with refined analysis and when.
- The mean-backup requirement is a significant deviation from common MCTS practice. Either provide a proof (or conditions) for sample-backup variants or more systematic empirical evidence across diverse games.
- Experimental scope is limited. Consider adding results on established benchmarks (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel) and comparisons with alpha-beta/double-oracle baselines where feasible.
- Clarify the precise dependence on exploration parameter γ in the theoretical approximation (only asymptotic equilibrium vs exploration-mixed policies); and state explicitly that the limiting exploitability cannot drop below a γ-dependent floor.
- Related work: add citations and discussion of CFR/regret-minimization in EFGs to better position the contribution.

Minor comments
- Ensure consistent definitions of empirical vs average strategies in text and figures (Lemma 4.5 helps).
- Clean up pseudocode formatting; define RetVal choices upfront and use consistent naming (SM-MCTS vs SM-MCTS-M).
- A few typos and notation glitches (e.g., indices, “oth.”) should be corrected.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and relevant theoretical contribution filling a gap in MCTS theory.
- Technically sound with a clear, modular analysis.
- Practical guidance: use HC selection with exploration; mean backups stabilize convergence.

Cons for acceptance
- No finite-time guarantees; loose depth dependence.
- Mean-backup requirement not aligned with standard MCTS practice; no proof for sample-backup.
- Limited experimental validation on small synthetic games.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper advances our theoretical understanding of SM-MCTS in a meaningful way, despite the asymptotic and somewhat conservative nature of the guarantees and modest empirical scope.
