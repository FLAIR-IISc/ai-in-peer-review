## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of theoretically analyzing the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games – a crucial area where practical success lacks formal guarantees. The authors present a general MCTS template applicable to zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games, leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games to guide action selection. Their main theoretical contribution is proving that, with an -Hannan consistent selection function guaranteeing sufficient exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They validate this claim empirically using Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to recent MCTS algorithms in this domain.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly identifies a gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond sequential games. It builds on the existing body of work on MCTS for general game playing and simultaneous-move games (cited works [10-13]), noting their practical success but lack of convergence proofs. The connection to Hannan consistency ([15, 16]) and its application to online learning in games is well-established, and the authors effectively integrate this framework into the MCTS context. They acknowledge the limitations of UCB ([14]) as a convergence strategy and position their work as offering a theoretical foundation for MCTS variants.  The reference to double-oracle methods ([8,9]) for backward induction is relevant, highlighting the practical challenges that motivate the use of simulation-based approaches.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The most significant strength is the formal convergence guarantee. Providing a theoretical basis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a substantial advance, addressing a long-standing open question.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is general and can accommodate various regret-minimizing procedures.  This flexibility is a significant advantage.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of -Hannan consistency as a core requirement is a sensible approach, grounding the analysis in well-established online learning theory.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not exhaustive, provide evidence supporting the theoretical findings and demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing algorithms.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are clear, and the algorithm is presented in a straightforward manner.
* **Thorough Literature Review:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper establishes convergence *to* a Nash equilibrium, it does not provide concrete bounds on the *rate* of convergence.  This is a significant limitation, as practical applicability depends on how quickly the algorithm reaches a useful approximation. The experiments offer some insight, but a more rigorous analysis of convergence speed would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The requirement of -Hannan consistency, while theoretically sound, is a strong assumption.  In practice, achieving true Hannan consistency can be challenging.  A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to deviations from perfect Hannan consistency would be valuable.
* **Error Bound Scaling:** The derived error bound scales with the depth of the game tree (D^2). This could be prohibitive for deep games. While the analysis focuses on showing convergence *eventually*, a tighter bound or discussion of how this scaling affects practical performance would be helpful.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:**  The experiments are primarily focused on randomly generated games and a worst-case scenario. Expanding the evaluation to more complex and realistic game domains (e.g., Goofspiel variants) would strengthen the empirical validation.  The comparison to existing algorithms is somewhat limited.
* **Numerical Stability:** While acknowledging and addressing the potential for numerical overflow in Exp3, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of potential numerical issues and their mitigation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of MCTS.
* The convergence guarantee provides a strong foundation for the use of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
* The generality of the proposed template makes it a valuable contribution to the field.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of convergence rate bounds limits the practical implications of the theoretical results.
* The strong assumption of -Hannan consistency may be difficult to satisfy in practice.
* The limited experimental scope restricts the generalizability of the findings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution.  The convergence guarantee is a valuable result that advances our understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While the lack of convergence rate bounds and the strong assumptions are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work and the potential impact on the field outweigh these concerns.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should consider addressing the following points:

*   Provide a discussion of the sensitivity of the results to deviations from perfect Hannan consistency.
*   Expand the experimental evaluation to include more complex game domains.
*   Consider providing some preliminary analysis of the convergence rate, even if a tight bound is not achievable.
*   Elaborate on potential numerical issues and mitigation strategies.
