This paper tackles a challenging and important problem: analyzing the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a setting where theoretical guarantees are largely absent. The authors demonstrate a convergence result under fairly general conditions, specifically leveraging -Hannan consistent action selection procedures. The connection to regret minimization is well-motivated and the formal analysis appears rigorous, building upon existing results in online learning. This is a genuinely *novel* contribution; providing theoretical backing for a widely-used, yet poorly understood, algorithm in a non-trivial game setting is a significant step forward.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The primary strength of this work is its theoretical contribution. Establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a novel and valuable achievement.
*   **Generality:** The framework is general, allowing for instantiation with various regret-minimizing procedures.
*   **Rigorous Analysis:** The formal proof, while detailed and potentially challenging to fully verify without significant effort, appears sound based on the presented lemmas and theorem. The connection to -Hannan consistency provides a strong foundation.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results, though not extensive, offer some support for the theory, demonstrating convergence behavior comparable to existing algorithms. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their empirical evaluation.
*   **Clear Writing (mostly):** The paper is generally well-written, particularly the sections outlining the algorithm and formal analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. While the authors compare to existing algorithms, the game instances are relatively simple (randomly generated and worst-case matrices). Exploring more complex, realistic game scenarios would strengthen the validation.
*   **Practical Implications of Convergence Rate:** The theoretical convergence guarantees do not provide explicit bounds on the convergence *rate*. While the empirical results suggest reasonable convergence speed, a more detailed analysis of the rate (even empirically) would be beneficial.
*   **Discussion of UCB:** The dismissal of UCB as not converging to NE feels somewhat abrupt. A brief discussion of *why* it fails to satisfy the necessary conditions for the presented theorem (or other known limitations) would be helpful.
*   **Minor Clarifications:** A few definitions could benefit from slightly more elaboration (e.g., the meaning of “guaranteed exploration”).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in game theory and AI.
*   The theoretical contribution is substantial and the analysis appears correct.
*   The generality of the framework is a key strength.
*   The empirical validation, while limited, is supportive of the theory.
*   The novelty of the approach outweighs the minor weaknesses.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical evaluation lacks the breadth to definitively demonstrate the practical utility of the theoretical results.
*   The lack of explicit convergence rate bounds limits the practical impact of the theoretical findings.
*   The dismissive treatment of UCB could be perceived as a slight oversight.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and the convergence rate remains uncharacterized, the novelty and rigor of the analysis are compelling. I believe the strengths of this paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses. I am inclined to **accept** this paper, with the caveat that the authors consider expanding the empirical evaluation and potentially providing some discussion of the practical implications of the convergence rate in a future version. The contribution is bold and presents a new direction for analyzing MCTS in complex game settings. I would welcome seeing this work presented at NIPS.
