### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a notable gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS and its variants are widely used for sequential two-player games, their convergence properties in games with simultaneous moves have not been formally established. The authors focus on the class of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. They introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection policy at each node is treated as a repeated matrix game, solved using a regret-minimizing procedure.

The central contribution is a formal proof that this SM-MCTS framework converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The core condition for this convergence is that the action selection algorithm used within each node must be "ε-Hannan consistent" and must guarantee exploration (i.e., every joint action is eventually sampled infinitely often). The proof is constructed inductively over the depth of the game tree. It begins by leveraging known results that Hannan-consistent algorithms converge to a Nash Equilibrium in repeated matrix games. This is extended to show convergence to an approximate NE in repeated matrix games where payoffs are subject to a bounded error. In the context of the extensive-form game, the value estimates of child nodes serve as these noisy payoffs for the parent node's matrix game. By showing the error is bounded at each level, the authors prove by induction that the average strategies converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium for the entire game, where ε' depends on the depth of the game and the base ε of the selection algorithm.

The paper demonstrates this general template using two specific ε-Hannan consistent algorithms: Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. It also provides an empirical analysis comparing the convergence speed of different MCTS variants on random and worst-case games. Notably, the experiments compare propagating the mean of collected rewards (as required by the formal proof) versus propagating the most recent sample's reward (a common heuristic), finding the latter to be slightly faster in practice.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically strong. The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous, step-by-step proof structure that is outlined clearly in the main text and detailed in the appendix. The approach of reducing the problem to the convergence of regret-minimization algorithms in repeated games with bounded error is sound and effective. The authors are careful in their definitions and lemmas, building a coherent argument for their main theorem.

The experimental evaluation complements the theory well. It honestly reports that the variant required for the proof (propagating the mean reward) is empirically slightly slower than a common heuristic (propagating the current sample), highlighting a gap between current theory and practice. The inclusion of worst-case games provides valuable insight into the practical limitations and convergence rates of the proposed methods.

A potential weakness in the technical argument is the simplification made for the formal analysis: the proof disregards the incremental tree-building phase of MCTS and assumes a complete game tree is available from the start. The authors justify this by stating that exploration guarantees ensure all nodes will eventually be added and visited infinitely often. While this is reasonable for an asymptotic analysis, it sidesteps the non-trivial effects of early-iteration bias, where estimates are based on very few samples during the tree expansion phase. This simplification is a key difference between the analyzed algorithm and MCTS as it is typically implemented.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the background concepts are defined precisely. The structure of the formal analysis, moving from simple matrix games to extensive-form games with error propagation, is logical and easy to follow. The main algorithm is presented clearly, and the specific instantiations (RM and Exp3) are explained sufficiently. Figures and experimental descriptions are clear and effectively support the paper's claims. The authors have done an excellent job of making a complex theoretical argument accessible.

**Originality:**
The paper's primary contribution is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for an MCTS-style algorithm in simultaneous move games. It solves a well-known open problem and provides a firm theoretical foundation for using simulation-based search in this game class. The connection drawn between MCTS and the theory of online learning (specifically, Hannan consistency and regret minimization) is a novel and powerful conceptual bridge. While RM and Exp3 are existing algorithms, their integration into an MCTS framework to guarantee convergence in this setting is a new and significant contribution.

**Significance:**
The results presented are highly significant for both the game theory and AI/ML communities.
1.  It provides theoretical justification for using MCTS, a popular and practical algorithm, in a broader class of games than was previously understood.
2.  The general framework allows researchers to plug in any ε-Hannan consistent algorithm, opening the door for future work on developing better selection strategies with stronger theoretical guarantees.
3.  The work could inspire further theoretical analysis, such as deriving finite-time performance bounds (which the authors correctly identify as an area for future work) or extending the results to more complex game settings like those with imperfect information.
4.  For practitioners, it gives confidence that MCTS variants based on regret minimization are not merely heuristics but are grounded in sound theory, converging to a desirable solution concept.

The main limitation on the immediate significance is the lack of a convergence rate analysis. The asymptotic guarantee is crucial, but the empirical results suggest convergence can be slow. The final equilibrium approximation quality depends on `D^2`, which could be a loose bound for very deep games.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   **Novelty:** The paper presents a novel and first-of-its-kind convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, solving a significant open problem.
*   **Significance:** The result provides a strong theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms and connects the fields of heuristic search and online learning in a meaningful way.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, sound, and well-executed.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand, making the contribution accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   **Proof Simplification:** The analysis relies on the assumption of a pre-existing complete game tree, which abstracts away from the incremental expansion process that is central to MCTS. This simplification makes the analyzed algorithm differ from a standard MCTS implementation.
*   **Asymptotic Nature:** The analysis is purely asymptotic and provides no finite-time bounds or analysis of the convergence rate, which can be empirically slow. This limits the practical implications of the theoretical guarantee.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its novelty and significance in providing the first convergence proof for this problem, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The work represents a major theoretical advancement in the field.
