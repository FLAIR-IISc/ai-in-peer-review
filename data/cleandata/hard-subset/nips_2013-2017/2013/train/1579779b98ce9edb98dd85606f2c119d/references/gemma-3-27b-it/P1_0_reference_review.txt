## Peer Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. This is a significant gap in the MCTS literature, as most theoretical guarantees focus on sequential games. The authors propose a general MCTS template leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection mechanisms. They prove that, under the assumption of a -Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium.  Empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 validates the theoretical findings on randomly generated and worst-case games, demonstrating comparable convergence speeds to existing algorithms.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the well-established foundation of MCTS, particularly UCT, which has shown empirical success in various game domains. However, it directly addresses the lack of theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, highlighted as a key open problem. It correctly positions itself with respect to existing work on solving simultaneous move games via backward induction and alpha-beta pruning, noting their reliance on domain knowledge or computationally expensive methods. The work acknowledges existing MCTS applications in this game class (e.g., Tron, Urban Rivals) but distinguishes itself by providing formal convergence guarantees.  The citations are relevant and comprehensive, covering key areas of game theory, MCTS, and online learning.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength is the formal proof of convergence for a general MCTS template in simultaneous move games. This is a novel and valuable contribution, filling a crucial gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS.
* **Generality:**  The proposed template’s reliance on any -Hannan consistent selection function provides flexibility and potentially allows for leveraging advancements in regret minimization techniques.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written. Definitions are precise, and the core concepts are explained in a logical manner.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The empirical evaluation provides supporting evidence for the theoretical results and demonstrates the practicality of the approach.  Comparing performance to existing algorithms strengthens the findings.
* **Thoroughness:** The paper considers both Regret Matching and Exp3, showcasing the versatility of their framework. The inclusion of worst-case game analysis is commendable.
* **Detailed Proof Sketch:** While the full proof is in the supplementary material, the paper provides a well-structured overview of the key steps and lemmas, enhancing understandability.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper *proves* convergence to an -Nash equilibrium, it doesn't provide concrete bounds on the *rate* of convergence. The empirical results suggest comparable speed to other algorithms, but a more rigorous analysis of the convergence rate would be highly valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The assumption of -Hannan consistency, while standard in online learning, might be strong in practice. Exploring the sensitivity of the convergence guarantee to deviations from this assumption would be beneficial.
* **Practical Implications:** The exploration parameter (γ) impacts performance, and the optimal selection of this parameter isn’t thoroughly addressed.  Guidance on choosing appropriate values for different game scenarios would be helpful.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:**  The experiments focus on randomly generated and worst-case games. Evaluating the algorithm on more complex, real-world game instances could provide further insights into its performance.
* **Numerical Stability:** The paper acknowledges a potential issue with numerical overflow in Exp3 and suggests a workaround. A more detailed discussion of this issue and its impact on larger-scale problems would be appreciated.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*  The paper addresses a significant open problem in the MCTS literature.
*  The theoretical result is novel, mathematically sound, and well-motivated.
*  The empirical evaluation provides compelling evidence supporting the theoretical findings.
*  The generality of the proposed template opens avenues for future research.
*  The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of convergence rate bounds limits the practical impact of the theoretical result.
*  The strong assumption of -Hannan consistency might restrict the applicability of the algorithm.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of MCTS. The formal proof of convergence in simultaneous move games is a valuable result, and the empirical evaluation provides convincing evidence of its validity. While the lack of explicit convergence rate bounds and the reliance on strong assumptions are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the overall quality and novelty of the work. I believe this paper is a worthy addition to NIPS and recommend acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10 (High Confidence - this work is solid and fills a critical gap, though a convergence rate analysis would significantly strengthen it.)

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Investigate methods for bounding the convergence rate of the algorithm.
* Explore the robustness of the convergence guarantee to deviations from the -Hannan consistency assumption.
* Provide more guidance on selecting appropriate exploration parameters (γ).
* Consider evaluating the algorithm on more complex, real-world game instances.
* Further elaborate on the numerical stability issue in Exp3 and its potential mitigation strategies.
