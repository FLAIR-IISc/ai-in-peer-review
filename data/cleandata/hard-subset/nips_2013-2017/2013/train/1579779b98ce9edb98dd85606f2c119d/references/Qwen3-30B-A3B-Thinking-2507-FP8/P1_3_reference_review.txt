

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS has been widely used in practice for games like Go, Chess, and security applications, its convergence properties in simultaneous move games have remained unproven. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms applicable to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games (which they term "stacked matrix games"). They prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (like regret matching or Exp3), the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper also includes empirical evaluations comparing different variants of their algorithm on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios.

This work builds on previous theoretical results on MCTS in sequential games (e.g., Kocsis and Szepesvári) but extends them to the more challenging simultaneous move setting where standard UCT algorithms have been shown to fail to converge to Nash equilibrium (as noted by Shafiei et al.). The paper connects to the broader literature on Hannan consistency in repeated games (Hart and Mas-Colell), providing a theoretical bridge between online learning and game-theoretic solution concepts.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The authors provide a clear proof of convergence by induction, building on established concepts like Hannan consistency. The connection between Hannan consistency and convergence to approximate Nash equilibrium is well-motivated and the formal proof is sound. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical results by showing that the proposed algorithms converge at reasonable rates, matching or exceeding other state-of-the-art MCTS variants for these games. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations and provides complete proofs in the appendix.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. Key concepts are introduced with precise definitions, and the algorithm descriptions are clear and well-illustrated. The figures (particularly Figure 1 showing the stacked matrix game structure) effectively convey important concepts. The connection between the theoretical analysis and empirical results is well-explained, making the paper accessible to both theoretical and applied researchers.

**Originality:** This is the first paper to provide formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While there has been work on MCTS for sequential games, the extension to simultaneous move games presents unique challenges (e.g., joint action selection, failure of UCT), and this paper successfully addresses them. The general template for SM-MCTS that can be instantiated with any regret minimization procedure is a novel contribution that could be applied to many existing MCTS variants.

**Significance:** The results have substantial implications for both theory and practice. Theoretically, they fill an important gap in the understanding of MCTS algorithms. Practically, they provide a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in a wide range of games with simultaneous moves (security games, card games, general game-playing competitions), which have previously relied on heuristic approaches without convergence guarantees. This work could influence both the design of new algorithms and the theoretical understanding of existing ones.

## Weaknesses

**Limited empirical comparison:** While the paper compares their methods with other MCTS variants, it would have been stronger to include more direct comparisons with state-of-the-art algorithms specifically designed for simultaneous move games (e.g., double-oracle methods, CFR variants). The paper mentions that they "conjecture that many of these algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium" but doesn't provide evidence for this.

**Computational complexity:** The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of their algorithms compared to other approaches. While convergence guarantees are important, it's crucial to understand whether the algorithm is computationally feasible for large-scale applications, especially since the paper mentions applications in security domains.

**Scope limitation:** The paper focuses exclusively on perfect-information games, while many practical applications involve imperfect information (e.g., poker). While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, a more thorough discussion of the challenges in extending their results to imperfect information games would strengthen the paper.

## Recommendation

**For acceptance:** This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS. The empirical evidence supports the theoretical claims, and the work has clear implications for both theory and practice.

**Against acceptance:** The limited comparison with existing algorithms specifically designed for simultaneous move games and the lack of computational complexity analysis are notable weaknesses.

## Final Assessment

This paper presents a significant theoretical advance that fills a critical gap in the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal analysis is rigorous, the empirical evaluation is appropriate, and the work has clear implications for both theoretical game theory and practical applications. While the paper could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to existing algorithms and a discussion of computational complexity, the core contribution is substantial and well-executed.

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field, advancing our understanding of MCTS algorithms in a previously unexplored domain. It meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and should be accepted.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**
