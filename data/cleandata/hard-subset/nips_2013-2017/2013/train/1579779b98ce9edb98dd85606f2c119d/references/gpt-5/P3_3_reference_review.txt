Summary
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). The authors give a general template of SM-MCTS whose selection rule at each node is any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) no-regret algorithm with guaranteed exploration. They prove almost-sure asymptotic convergence of the empirical/average strategies to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium: at depth D, the profile is an (ε·D^2 + δ)-NE for arbitrary δ>0. The proof proceeds by (i) analyzing repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded error, and (iii) inductively composing errors up the tree when backing up mean returns. They discuss two instantiations (regret matching and Exp3), and empirically study convergence on random and small worst-case games. They find both mean-backup and “sample-backup” variants empirically converge, with mean-backup slightly slower.

Relation to prior work
There is substantial work on MCTS/UCT in sequential perfect-information games; for simultaneous moves, prior empirical work exists (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel), and Shafiei et al. showed UCT need not converge to NE in a one-shot simultaneous game. Alpha-beta style pruning for simultaneous moves and double-oracle/backward induction methods offer exact solutions but require domain evaluation/cutoff. The core theoretical ingredients—HC (Hart & Mas-Colell; Exp3; regret matching) implying approach to equilibria in matrix games—are standard. The originality here is lifting HC-based selection to MCTS in stacked matrix games with a convergence guarantee and making subgame-perfectness explicit. This complements CFR-style guarantees (typically for imperfect-information EFGs), and fills a gap for perfect-information simultaneous-move search.

Quality
- Theory: The high-level argument is sound and well-aligned with standard online learning results. The repeated-game-with-bounded-error abstraction is natural for internal-node value noise, and the inductive composition yields a D-dependent bound. However, constants and assumptions could be tightened/clarified:
  - The mismatch between factors in Lemma 4.6/4.10 (4(c+1)+δ) and the main theorem’s ε·D^2+δ bound is not fully reconciled in the main text; a clean end-to-end bound that explicitly tracks ε, γ, branching, and depth would help.
  - The requirement to back up mean values (rather than the standard immediate sample) is critical for the proof; the paper shows empirically both variants converge, but theory covers only the mean-backup. It would be valuable to state explicitly which results fail without mean backup.
  - The assumption “guaranteed exploration” is crucial; the construction via γ-mixing is standard, but the exact dependency of equilibrium error on γ (and its interaction with ε) is left implicit in the main theorem.
  - For the RM instantiation, the Updates use stored estimates Xh,ij for counterfactual actions. This is not standard full-information feedback and raises whether the concrete RM variant used is in fact HC under the induced feedback; the theory treats the selection rule abstractly as HC, but the paper should clarify that the concrete RM variant is used only empirically, not as a proved HC instance under MCTS feedback. In contrast, the Exp3 variant with importance weighting is HC under bandit feedback.
- Experiments: Methodology is careful for small synthetic games (random trees, exhaustive depth-2 search for hard instances). However, the empirical section is limited:
  - No evaluation on standard simultaneous-move domains (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) or comparison to established baselines (e.g., UCT variants known to be strong empirically, alpha-beta variants, double oracle).
  - No finite-time convergence characterization (e.g., dependence on depth/branching/γ), even empirically. The observation that mean-backup is slower is interesting but not deeply analyzed.

Clarity
The presentation is generally clear and self-contained, with useful definitions. Some notational and editorial issues could be improved:
- T is used both for the MCTS tree and the transition function; consider renaming one.
- Algorithm 1 pseudocode has minor inconsistencies (e.g., “oth.” for “otherwise”, “if h∈T” where T is also the tree). Clarify when nodes are expanded and how “previously unselected (i,j)” is tracked.
- The RM update should clearly distinguish whether full-information payoffs are observed or estimated, and what happens if Xh,ij is uninitialized.
- Several references are incomplete; please fix formatting and ensure all related work is properly cited.

Originality
The core contribution—provable asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS with HC selection to subgame-perfect approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move games—is, to my knowledge, novel and timely. The reduction to repeated games with bounded errors and the D-level induction are conceptually clean. Please clarify overlap/differences with prior workshop work (e.g., Lanctot et al., “MCTS in simultaneous move games”), and whether any prior proofs existed for special cases.

Significance
The result offers a theoretical foundation for using MCTS as an approximation method in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, a setting common in GGP and pursuit-evasion. While asymptotic and with loose constants, the guarantee is meaningful and could inform algorithm design. The practical impact would be stronger with finite-time rates and broader empirical validation.

Pros
- Clear, principled theoretical framework connecting HC online learning and SM-MCTS.
- Asymptotic convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE with explicit depth dependence.
- Simple, broadly applicable template; instantiations with Exp3/RM are natural.
- Empirical evidence (albeit limited) supports the claims and provides insight into backup choices.

Cons
- Theory requires mean-backup; practical MCTS typically uses sample backups. No theory for that case.
- No finite-time bounds; constants appear loose (D^2 scaling, factors from Lemma 4.10).
- Empirics on small synthetic games only; no comparison to strong baselines on standard domains.
- Clarity issues around the RM update and guaranteed HC under MCTS feedback.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Can you quantify the error propagation more tightly to reduce the D^2 factor? Is D or O(D) achievable?
- For the RM instantiation, is there a proof that your update scheme is HC under the induced partial/full information at a node, or should RM be considered purely empirical here?
- Do you see a path to extending the analysis to sample-backup (without mean), perhaps via martingale arguments?
- Please report results on at least one standard simultaneous-move benchmark and include baselines.
- Discuss the dependence of convergence on γ and branching factor empirically.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is novel and relevant, and the paper is largely sound. Strengthen clarity and empirical evaluation and clarify the scope of the HC assumption for specific instantiations.
