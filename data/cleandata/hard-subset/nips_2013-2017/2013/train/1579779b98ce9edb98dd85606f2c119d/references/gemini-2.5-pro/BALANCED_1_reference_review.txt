This paper addresses an important theoretical gap in the literature on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. This class of games, also known as stacked matrix games, is less studied than purely sequential games but captures important strategic scenarios. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection policy at each node is handled by a regret-minimizing algorithm suitable for matrix games.

The core theoretical contribution is proving that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (properties held by common algorithms like Regret Matching and Exp3), then the average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built elegantly by induction on the depth of the game tree. It first establishes convergence to an approximate Nash Equilibrium for repeated matrix games where players receive payoffs with bounded error. This result is then applied inductively, treating the MCTS value estimates from child nodes as the erroneous payoffs for the parent node's matrix game. The paper supports its theoretical claims with an empirical evaluation on random and worst-case games, comparing the theoretically analyzed variant with a more standard implementation and analyzing their convergence rates.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a fundamental and previously open theoretical question. While MCTS and its variants are immensely popular and successful in practice, their theoretical guarantees are largely confined to sequential games (e.g., convergence of UCT to minimax). Providing the first convergence proof for the simultaneous-move setting is a significant contribution that strengthens the theoretical foundations of MCTS and justifies its application to a broader class of games.

2.  **Sound and Elegant Theoretical Framework:** The proof strategy is novel and well-executed. The key insight of framing the problem at each node as a repeated matrix game with bounded error, where the error stems from the stochastic estimates of the subgames, is powerful. This allows the authors to leverage well-understood results from the online learning literature on Hannan consistency and regret minimization and extend them to a recursive, multi-stage setting. The step-by-step construction of the proof, from basic matrix games to the full extensive-form setting, makes the argument clear and convincing.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The problem is clearly motivated, necessary background and definitions are provided, and the theoretical analysis is built from the ground up. The inclusion of an empirical section that connects the theory to practice is a valuable addition.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Simplifying Assumption in the Analysis:** The formal analysis in Section 4 begins with the assumption that the game tree has been completely built, and the analysis focuses on the learning process thereafter. While the authors argue this is a valid simplification because each node-action pair will be visited infinitely often, this is a strong assumption that glosses over the dynamics of the tree-building phase. During the initial stages, value estimates are highly biased and based on very few samples. It is not immediately obvious that this initial, non-stationary phase does not affect the long-term convergence guarantees. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more formal justification of this simplification, perhaps by showing that the influence of the initial biased statistics vanishes over time.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Empirical Performance:** The analysis requires that the value propagated up the tree is the *mean* of all rewards from a child node (`Xh/nh`), which the authors denote as the 'M' variants (e.g., RMM). However, the experiments show that the standard MCTS approach of propagating the *most recent sample's* reward (e.g., RM) converges slightly faster in all tested cases. This creates a gap between the theoretically justified algorithm and the empirically superior one. While the authors conjecture that their analysis could be extended to the standard variants, the paper would be more impactful if it could provide some intuition or a sketch of an argument for why this might be true. Does the higher variance of single-sample updates aid exploration in a way that speeds up practical convergence, even if it complicates the proof? Addressing this could provide valuable insights for practitioners.

3.  **Potential Looseness of the Convergence Bound:** Theorem 4.11 provides a convergence bound for the ε-Nash equilibrium that appears to have a quadratic dependency on the game depth D (O(D²ε)). This dependency arises from summing the accumulated errors up the tree. While the logic is explained, it would be helpful for the authors to comment on whether they believe this bound is tight or if it is an artifact of the proof technique. A tighter analysis, if possible, would enhance the theoretical result.

4.  **Minor Clarity Issues in Algorithm Description:** The presentation of Algorithm 1 could be slightly clearer. The `RetVal` function, which is critical for distinguishing the theoretically analyzed version from the standard one, is introduced in the text but not as a parameter to the algorithm itself. It is also slightly confusing that line 13 calls `Update` and then line 14's `RetVal` seems to use the result of that update (`Xh`, `nh`), while the recursive call on line 11 returns a single utility `u1`. Clarifying the flow of values and which return value is used where would improve readability. For example, is the `u1` on line 11 the single sample from the rollout/terminal node, or is it already an averaged value from a deeper node `h'`? Based on the text, it seems to be the latter for the 'M' variants, which is an important detail.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a significant and novel theoretical result. The strengths in originality and significance far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future strengthening of the work. The paper provides a solid foundation for the analysis of MCTS in a broader and more complex game-theoretic setting. I strongly recommend acceptance.
