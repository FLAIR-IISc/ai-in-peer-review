

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that uses any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure for action selection, and prove that this approach converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This is particularly valuable as previous convergence guarantees for MCTS applied only to sequential games (e.g., the classic UCT algorithm), with no formal results for simultaneous move games.

The paper makes several important contributions. First, it establishes a clear theoretical connection between Hannan consistency in online learning and Nash equilibrium in game theory for this class of games. Second, it provides the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which have practical importance in security domains and general game-playing. Third, it offers a general algorithmic template that can incorporate various regret minimization procedures, with empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The inductive proof structure (starting from matrix games and extending to multi-stage games) is well-constructed and addresses an important gap in the literature.

2. **Clear Formalization:** The paper provides precise definitions and notation for the simultaneous move game setting, and carefully distinguishes between matrix games and extensive-form games. The connection between Hannan consistency and convergence to Nash equilibrium is well-explained.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental section provides appropriate empirical validation of the theoretical claims. The comparison between mean propagation (required for the formal analysis) versus current sample value propagation is particularly insightful and addresses a practical implementation question.

4. **Appropriate Contextualization:** The paper correctly situates its work within the broader literature on MCTS, simultaneous move games, and regret minimization, citing relevant prior work including the seminal papers on UCT and regret matching.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Convergence Rate Analysis:** The paper proves eventual convergence but does not provide any finite-time convergence bounds. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, as practitioners are often more interested in how quickly the algorithm converges rather than just whether it converges. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their conclusion, but it weakens the practical impact of their theoretical results.

2. **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is restricted to small games (depth 2, branching factor 2) and randomly generated utilities. While the worst-case analysis on small games is useful, the paper would benefit from evaluating the approach on larger, more complex games that better reflect practical applications. The comparison to other MCTS variants (beyond those covered by the theoretical proof) is limited.

3. **Practical Implications Not Fully Explored:** While the paper establishes theoretical convergence, it doesn't sufficiently discuss how these results can guide practical algorithm design. For example, the paper shows that mean propagation (required for the theoretical proof) converges slightly slower than current sample propagation. However, it doesn't provide guidance on when the theoretical guarantees might be worth the slight performance penalty in practice.

4. **Comparison to Alternative Approaches:** The paper mentions that their analysis could be extended to other algorithms like CFR (Counterfactual Regret Minimization), but doesn't compare their approach to these established methods that also converge to Nash equilibrium in similar settings. A brief comparison would strengthen the paper's significance.

**Recommendation:**

This paper makes an important theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, addressing a significant gap in the literature. While the theoretical results are sound and well-proven, the lack of convergence rate analysis and limited empirical scope somewhat diminishes the paper's impact. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:

1. Providing finite-time convergence bounds (even if asymptotic)
2. Expanding the empirical evaluation to larger games and more diverse game structures
3. Including a more detailed discussion of practical implications and when the theoretical guarantees might be worth the implementation trade-offs
4. Adding a brief comparison to alternative algorithms like CFR that also converge to Nash equilibrium in similar settings

Despite these limitations, the paper's theoretical contribution is substantial and addresses a clear gap in the literature. The results provide a solid foundation for future work in this area, and the general algorithmic template is valuable for practitioners. The paper meets the standards of quality and significance expected at NIPS, though the lack of convergence rate analysis is a notable limitation.

I recommend **acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the limitations mentioned in their revision. The paper's theoretical contribution is significant enough to warrant publication, and the weaknesses identified are primarily in the empirical and practical implications rather than the core theoretical results.
