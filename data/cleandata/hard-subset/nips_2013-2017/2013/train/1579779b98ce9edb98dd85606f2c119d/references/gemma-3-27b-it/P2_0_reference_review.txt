## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the challenging setting of zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games.  While MCTS is widely used in practice for these games (e.g., Goofspiel, general game-playing), a formal convergence analysis has been missing. The authors provide a general template for MCTS utilizing regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection mechanisms. They prove that, under the condition of using a -Hannan consistent selection function guaranteeing exploration of all actions, the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The theory is supported by empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3, demonstrating comparable convergence speeds to recent algorithms in randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant gap:** The lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a substantial issue. This work directly addresses this, providing a crucial foundation for understanding the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is quite general, allowing for the integration of various regret-minimizing procedures.  This flexibility is a strong point.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:**  The proof of convergence, leveraging -Hannan consistency, is mathematically rigorous and well-structured.  The inductive argument for extending the convergence from leaf nodes to the entire game tree is clear.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not extensive, support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical viability of the approach.  Comparing performance against existing algorithms is valuable.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and proofs. The definitions are precise, and the algorithm descriptions are understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical result guarantees convergence to an -Nash equilibrium, but provides no information regarding the *rate* of convergence.  The experiments offer some insights, but a more detailed analysis of convergence speed (e.g., with respect to the number of iterations or simulations) would be beneficial.
* **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments primarily focus on randomly generated games and a worst-case scenario constructed by the authors.  Evaluating on more diverse and established game instances would strengthen the empirical support.  The exploration parameter γ is fixed, and a sensitivity analysis to its value is missing.
* **Practical Relevance of ε:** The convergence guarantee is to an *ε*-Nash equilibrium. While mathematically sound, the practical impact of a non-zero ε needs to be discussed further.  How does the value of ε relate to meaningful gameplay or decision-making?
* **Assumptions & Applicability:** The assumption of perfect information is a strong one. Discussing the potential challenges and modifications needed to extend the analysis to imperfect-information games would be valuable.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper references relevant work, a more thorough discussion of how this work *builds* upon existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous-move games (like those in [11, 12, 20]) would be helpful. The authors state they conjecture many algorithms also converge, but don't detail how their analysis could be extended to them.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS. The convergence proof is a significant result, addressing a long-standing open question. While the experiments could be more extensive and the convergence rate analysis is missing, the paper provides a solid foundation for future research.  The clarity of presentation and the generality of the approach further enhance its value.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant theoretical gap in the field.
* Provides a rigorous and well-structured convergence proof.
* Offers a general MCTS template with practical relevance.
* Empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* Clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lack of analysis of convergence rate.
* Limited scope of experimental evaluation.
* Theoretical convergence to an ε-Nash equilibrium with little discussion of practical implications.
* Could benefit from a more detailed connection to existing algorithms in the field.

**Rating:**  **Accept (with minor revisions)**

**Recommendation:** I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  The authors should be encouraged to:

* Discuss the practical implications of the ε parameter and provide some guidance on choosing appropriate values.
* Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse game instances.
* Add a section discussing potential future work on extending the analysis to imperfect-information games and existing algorithms.
* Include a sensitivity analysis of the exploration parameter γ.
