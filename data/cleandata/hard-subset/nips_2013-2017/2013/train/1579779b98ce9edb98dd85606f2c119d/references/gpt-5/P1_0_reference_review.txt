Summary
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template where, at each node, the joint action is chosen by independent selection algorithms for each player. If the selection algorithm is ε–Hannan consistent (HC) and guarantees exploration (each joint action is attempted infinitely often), and if node backups return the mean of observed rewards (SM-MCTS-M), they prove almost-sure convergence of empirical frequencies to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis proceeds by: (i) reviewing HC in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors; and (iii) inductively composing these to bound deviation incentives up the tree, yielding a subgame-perfect (O(D^2) + δ)-NE for depth D. Empirically, they instantiate the template with regret matching and Exp3, and show convergence on random games and small worst-case searches; they also examine the impact of mean vs sample backups.

Relation to prior work: The paper connects online learning (Hannan consistency; Hart & Mas-Colell, Exp3) with MCTS, extending beyond classic UCT (known to fail in simultaneous-move settings) to provide the first formal convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move games. It complements alpha–beta based exact/backward-induction approaches with pruning (Saffidine et al.; Bosansky et al.) that require evaluation functions/LP cutoffs, and relates to CFR-style regret minimization used in imperfect-information games and prior comparisons of UCT vs CFR in simultaneous games (Shafiei et al.).

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: A general and simple recipe—“plug any ε-HC selection routine with guaranteed exploration into MCTS and back up means”—with a clean proof of convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE.
- Technical development: Clear reduction from repeated matrix games to repeated games with bounded error to extensive-form trees via induction; the bounded-error lemmas are likely of independent interest.
- Generality: Applies to any ε-HC selector (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), not tied to a bespoke algorithm.
- Subgame perfection: The limit strategies are shown to be subgame perfect, a stronger property than root optimality.
- Empirical sanity checks: Experiments with RM/Exp3 show convergence and explore the effect of mean vs sample backups.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Statement and tightness of the main bound: Theorem 4.11 states a (D^2 + δ)-NE without explicit dependence on ε (and constants from Lemma 4.9). Earlier text suggests dependence on ε and on per-level error, and Section 4.2/4.3 yields factors like 4(c+1). As written, D^2 is dimensionless and can exceed the [0,1] payoff range, making the bound vacuous and seemingly missing ε. Please restate the theorem with precise constants and explicit ε dependence; clarify whether the dependence is O(ε D^2) or O((ε+γ) D^2), and whether the quadratic depth dependence is intrinsic or an artifact of the proof technique.
- Backup requirement: The proofs require backing up the mean value (SM-MCTS-M). This is a nontrivial departure from most practical SM-MCTS that use the latest sample (or biased estimators). Although experiments show both variants converge empirically, the theory currently covers only the mean-backup variant. Can the analysis be extended to sample backups (e.g., via martingale concentration and bias control), or at least discuss conditions under which they would also converge?
- Exploration and joint action coverage: The “guaranteed exploration” assumption requires each joint action to be tried infinitely often. The A* modification proposes per-player uniform exploration without updating. Please clarify why this ensures tij → ∞ almost surely for every (i,j) under independent per-player exploration (i.e., lower-bounded product probabilities), and whether standard RM/Exp3 with γ-mixing suffice. Also discuss efficiency trade-offs versus structured exploration that targets under-sampled joint actions.
- Experimental scope: Empirical evaluation is limited to small random games and exhaustive depth-2, BF-2 searches. Including more realistic simultaneous-move domains (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel variants) and comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., CFR/MCCFR variants adapted to simultaneous moves, double-oracle/serialized alpha–beta) would strengthen the practical message. Reporting both players’ exploitability and convergence curves across depths/branching factors with confidence intervals would improve robustness.
- Finite-sample rates: The paper offers asymptotic guarantees only. Even heuristic rates or empirical scaling with D, branching factor, and γ would be valuable; the absence is noted as future work.
- Presentation and references: There are several typos and notational glitches (e.g., “oth.” in Eq. 3; “once” vs “ones” in Exp3; some missing hats/bars consistency). Many citations are incomplete (years/pages missing) and should be fixed. Algorithm 1 could be clarified (explicitly identify the mean-backup variant and how Select composes joint actions).

Quality
- The theoretical core is sound and well-motivated, contingent on clarifying the main theorem’s constants/ε dependence. The assumptions (ε-HC, exploration, mean backups) are stated and justified. Empirical results support the convergence claim but are not extensive.

Clarity
- Generally readable, with helpful structure and definitions. However, the main theorem statement and constants need correction; algorithmic details (backup choice, exploration mechanism) should be made explicit. Please polish typos and complete references.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad HC selection. The bounded-error repeated-game lemmas and their composition are a useful contribution.

Significance
- Provides a theoretical foundation for a widely used class of methods in simultaneous-move games. While the asymptotic bound may be loose, the result is likely to influence future algorithm design and analysis, and the template is broadly applicable.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel and general theoretical guarantee for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
- Clean connection to HC online learning and subgame perfection.
- Practical template applicable with standard selectors (RM, Exp3).
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Main theorem’s bound appears misstated/loose; missing explicit ε dependence.
- Theory requires mean backups and strong exploration; practical implications under sample backups are unproven.
- Limited experimental scope and baselines.
- Presentation issues and incomplete references.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying/correcting Theorem 4.11’s statement (constants and ε), explicitly delineating the backed-up estimator required for the guarantee, and improving the presentation. Expanding experiments and discussing finite-time behavior would further strengthen the paper.
