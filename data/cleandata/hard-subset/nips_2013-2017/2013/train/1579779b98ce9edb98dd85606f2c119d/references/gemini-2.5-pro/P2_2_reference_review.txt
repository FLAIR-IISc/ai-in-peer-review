### **Summary**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS is widely used in practice for various games, including those with simultaneous moves, formal guarantees of its convergence to a Nash Equilibrium (NE) have been elusive for this class of games. The authors propose a general template for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The core idea is to use an ε-Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure (such as Regret Matching or Exp3) for action selection within each game state (node). The main contribution is a formal proof that this MCTS template converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium, where ε' depends on the game's depth and the ε of the selection algorithm. This is, to my knowledge, the first such convergence guarantee for MCTS in this game class. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on random and worst-case games, demonstrating that the provably convergent algorithms are empirically competitive with existing heuristics.

### **Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known and important open problem: the lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. Providing the first proof of convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium is a major contribution. It bridges the gap between the widespread practical use of MCTS in these settings and the formal understanding of its behavior, lending crucial theoretical justification to its application.

2.  **Technical Soundness and Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The proof strategy, which proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree, is elegant. It starts by analyzing repeated matrix games with perfect observations (leaf nodes), extends this to games with bounded payoff errors (interior nodes receiving values from their children), and then composes these results up the tree. The use of ε-Hannan consistency as the core property for the selection mechanism is a clever way to leverage existing results from online learning theory.

3.  **Constructive Framework:** The paper does not just analyze one specific algorithm but provides a general template. By showing that *any* ε-Hannan consistent procedure can be plugged in, the authors provide a modular and extensible framework. This makes the result more powerful and applicable to future developments in regret-minimization algorithms.

4.  **Well-Designed Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are not just a simple validation but are designed to answer specific questions raised by the theory. The comparison between propagating the mean value (as required by the proof) and propagating the single sample value (as is common in practice) is particularly insightful. The search for worst-case game instances also provides a more robust evaluation than relying solely on average performance over random games.

### **Weaknesses (Areas for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity of the Proof Sketch:** While the overall proof idea is sound, the sketch provided in the main paper (Section 4.4) is quite brief and could be made more intuitive. Specifically, the leap to a subgame perfect **(εD^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium** in Theorem 4.11 is not clearly justified by the preceding text. The description suggests that errors of order *d* accumulate at level *d*, and then states that "Summing the possible gain of deviations on each level leads to the bound in the theorem." This sounds more like a linear or cumulative bound (e.g., proportional to D) rather than a quadratic one (D^2). A clearer explanation in the main paper of how the error propagates quadratically with depth would significantly improve the paper's readability and impact.

2.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The analysis requires propagating the *mean* of rewards (the "M" variants), whereas the experiments show that propagating the *current sample* (the standard variants) performs slightly better in practice. The paper notes this but doesn't delve into the theoretical implications. It would strengthen the paper to discuss *why* the proof requires the mean and what theoretical obstacles prevent the analysis from applying to the more common single-sample propagation. Is it a limitation of the proof technique, or is there a fundamental reason why the single-sample variant might not converge in all cases (even if it does empirically)?

3.  **Convergence Rate:** The paper proves eventual convergence but provides no analysis of the convergence rate (i.e., how many iterations are needed to achieve a given ε'). While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the lack of any finite-time bounds is a notable limitation, especially for a NIPS audience where such bounds are common. The empirical results suggest a polynomial rate, but a theoretical discussion, even if it only establishes a loose bound, would be a valuable addition.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   **Regarding Theorem 4.11:** Could you please elaborate on the origin of the D^2 term in the bound? My reading of the inductive step suggests that at a node at depth *d* from the leaves, the error *c* from its children is O(d-1), leading to an exploitability of O(d) at that node. Summing this over D levels seems to lead to a bound of O(D^2). Is this the correct intuition? A sentence or two clarifying this in Section 4.4 would be very helpful.
*   **Definitions:** The distinction between "empirical frequencies" (σ̂) and "average strategies" (σ̄) is crucial. While Lemma 4.5 shows they converge to each other, it might be beneficial to define both clearly in Section 4.1, as the terms are used throughout the analysis and experiments.
*   **Exploration (Definition 4.3):** The construction of A* involves choosing a random action "without updating any of the variables belonging to A." Is this standard? Often, exploration steps are also used for learning. Could you comment on whether the proof would still hold if the updates were performed during these γ-exploration steps?
*   **Scope:** The work is confined to the perfect information setting. While this is a standard and necessary first step, a brief discussion in the conclusion on the key challenges to extending this analysis to imperfect information games (e.g., where regret minimization is applied over information sets) would be welcome.

### **Final Assessment**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of game-playing algorithms. It provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a result of high importance to both the game theory and reinforcement learning communities. The technical quality is high, and the analysis is supported by relevant experiments. While there are areas where the clarity could be improved, particularly regarding the details of the error propagation in the proof, these are addressable weaknesses. The strengths, most notably the significance of the main theoretical result, far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper is a clear advancement of the state of the art and would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
