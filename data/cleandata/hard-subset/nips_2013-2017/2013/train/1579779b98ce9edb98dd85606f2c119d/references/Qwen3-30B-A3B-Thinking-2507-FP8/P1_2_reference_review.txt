

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a formal analysis showing that MCTS algorithms using ε-Hannan consistent selection procedures (such as regret matching or Exp3) converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This work builds upon the established connection between online learning algorithms and game-theoretic equilibria, extending it to the MCTS framework for simultaneous move games. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this game class, and (2) empirical validation showing that the mean propagation variant (RMM) converges to approximate Nash equilibrium, with the theoretical analysis explaining the observed behavior.

The paper is well-positioned within the broader context of game-theoretic algorithms for AI. It correctly acknowledges prior work on MCTS in sequential games (e.g., UCT [14]) and the limitations of existing algorithms for simultaneous move games [17], while highlighting that no convergence guarantees existed for MCTS in this setting. The authors also properly contextualize their work within the literature on Hannan consistency [15, 16] and its relationship to Nash equilibrium.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by theoretical analysis. The authors provide a comprehensive proof of convergence, building from basic matrix games up to extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The proof technique is elegant, using induction on the depth of the game tree and carefully bounding the error propagation at each level. The formal analysis addresses a critical gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that no convergence guarantees were known for MCTS in simultaneous move games prior to this work.

The empirical evaluation is appropriate and well-designed. The authors test two variants (RMM and RM) on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios, with appropriate statistical analysis (100 games, 100 runs each). The results confirm the theoretical claims, showing that both algorithms converge to approximate Nash equilibrium, with the mean propagation variant (RMM) converging slightly slower but still effectively. The paper also properly addresses the practical concern of propagating mean values versus current samples, which is a common implementation detail in MCTS.

A minor weakness is that the paper does not provide a detailed analysis of the convergence rate, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. The theoretical analysis provides guarantees about eventual convergence but not about how quickly the convergence occurs. The empirical results show convergence rates, but a more rigorous theoretical characterization of the rate would strengthen the paper.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors carefully define all necessary concepts (from game theory to MCTS) before building their theoretical framework. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the figures (particularly Figure 1, which illustrates the stacked matrix game structure) effectively communicate key concepts.

The writing is accessible to researchers in both game theory and machine learning. The authors do an excellent job of connecting concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency) to game theory (Nash equilibrium) and to MCTS. The formal analysis is presented in a logical sequence, with each lemma building on the previous one. The empirical section clearly explains the experimental setup and results.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS readership, as it bridges theoretical machine learning (online learning algorithms) with practical applications (game-playing AI).

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution to the field. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first formal analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games. While MCTS has been widely applied to such games in practice (e.g., [10, 11, 12, 13]), the theoretical guarantees were previously unknown. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is novel, and the authors correctly position this work as filling a critical gap in the literature.

The paper also makes an important theoretical distinction between the "mean propagation" variant (required for the formal analysis) and the "current sample value" propagation used in previous MCTS implementations. This distinction is both theoretically and practically significant, as the authors demonstrate that the mean propagation variant is necessary for the convergence guarantees, while the current sample variant still converges to approximate equilibrium.

The paper's approach is not merely a novel combination of existing techniques, but a fundamental extension of theoretical understanding to a previously unanalyzed class of games.

## Significance

This work has significant implications for both theory and practice. Theoretically, it provides the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which will inform future research on algorithmic game theory. Practically, it justifies the widespread use of MCTS in applications like security games [1, 2] and general game-playing [6, 10, 11, 12] from a theoretical perspective.

The results are likely to influence future work on MCTS variants, as the authors correctly suggest that many existing algorithms for simultaneous move games (which they conjecture also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium) may be covered by their analysis. This could lead to a more unified theoretical understanding of MCTS algorithms in game-playing contexts.

The paper's significance is further enhanced by its potential to guide future algorithm design. By identifying the properties (Hannan consistency with guaranteed exploration) that ensure convergence, the paper provides a clear framework for developing new MCTS variants.

## Strengths

- First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Elegant theoretical proof using induction and careful error bounding
- Comprehensive empirical validation confirming theoretical claims
- Clear explanation of the distinction between mean propagation and current sample propagation
- Proper contextualization within the broader literature on MCTS and game theory
- Well-written, well-organized, and accessible to a broad audience

## Weaknesses

- The paper does not provide a theoretical characterization of the convergence rate, though this is acknowledged as a limitation
- The empirical evaluation is limited to relatively small games (depth ≤ 2, branching factor ≤ 2 in worst-case analysis), which may not reflect the scale at which MCTS is typically applied
- The definition of "subgame perfect" in simultaneous move games is slightly ambiguous compared to sequential games (though the paper does clarify this in context)
- The paper does not explore extensions to non-zero-sum or imperfect information games, though the authors acknowledge this as future work

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that fills a significant gap in the literature on MCTS algorithms. The formal analysis is sound, the empirical validation is appropriate, and the results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of MCTS in game-playing AI.

The paper meets all the quality criteria for NIPS, with strong theoretical grounding and clear empirical validation. It is original, addressing a previously unanalyzed class of games, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The significance of the work is substantial, as it provides the first theoretical justification for the widespread use of MCTS in simultaneous move games.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the convergence rate, either theoretically (if possible) or by providing more extensive empirical analysis of how the rate depends on game parameters.

2. While the paper acknowledges that the empirical evaluation is limited to small games, it would be helpful to provide some discussion about why these games are representative of the broader class of simultaneous move games, or to include a brief discussion of potential challenges in scaling to larger games.

3. The definition of "subgame perfect" in simultaneous move games could be slightly clarified, as this term has a specific meaning in sequential games that doesn't directly translate to simultaneous move settings.

4. The paper could mention some practical implications of the mean propagation vs. current sample propagation distinction, such as how it affects implementation and computational efficiency in real-world applications.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. The theoretical results are important and the empirical validation is appropriate. The paper is well-written and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
