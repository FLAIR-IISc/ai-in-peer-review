## NIPS Paper Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental open problem in reinforcement learning and game theory: proving convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games. The authors establish a general template for SM-MCTS, leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection mechanisms. They prove that, under the condition of using an -Hannan consistent selection function guaranteeing exploration of all actions, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The theoretical results are supported by empirical evaluations using Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating comparable performance to existing MCTS algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the success of MCTS in sequential games (e.g., Go) and acknowledges the lack of theoretical foundation for its application in simultaneous-move games. It contrasts with prior work that relies on domain knowledge (evaluation functions, cutoff conditions via LP or double oracle) for backward induction-based algorithms. The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of UCB-based MCTS in this context (Shafiei et al., 2017) and position their work as providing the first theoretical guarantees for convergence in this setting.  They connect the work to the broader field of online learning and Hannan consistency (Hart & Mas-Colell, 1996; Auer et al., 2002). The paper also correctly cites relevant work on applying MCTS to similar games like Goofspiel (Lanctot et al., 2015) and general game playing (Finnsson, 2007).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength is the rigorous proof of convergence for a general SM-MCTS template. This is a significant step forward in understanding the theoretical properties of MCTS beyond sequential games.
* **Generality:** The framework's ability to accommodate any -Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure is a major advantage, providing flexibility and potential for leveraging advancements in online learning algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments, though not exhaustive, provide initial evidence that the theoretical results translate to practical performance. The comparison to existing methods is valuable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are clearly presented, and the logical flow from background to theoretical analysis to empirical results is easy to follow.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant literature and appropriately position their contribution within the existing body of work.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:**  The theoretical analysis guarantees *convergence* but provides no insights into the *rate* of convergence. The experiments offer some hints, but a more detailed investigation of convergence speed would be beneficial.  The  O(D^2) term in the convergence bound (Theorem 4.11) suggests a potential scaling issue with game depth, which could be explored further.
* **Error Bound Complexity:** The convergence bound relies on an error term (η in Lemma 4.9) which, while theoretically sound, might be difficult to estimate in practice. The paper doesn’t discuss practical strategies for controlling this error.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are somewhat limited in scope.  While the authors test on randomly generated and "worst-case" games, exploring a wider range of game structures and branching factors would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Practical Implications of Hannan Consistency:** While the theoretical framework relies on -Hannan consistency, discussing the practical considerations of ensuring this property in real-world implementations would be valuable.
* **Assumptions:** The proof assumes a complete tree is built. While the authors acknowledge this simplification, a discussion of how this assumption impacts real-world implementations (where tree building is inherently limited) would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** This is the first theoretical analysis of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
* **Soundness:** The theoretical results are rigorously presented and appear logically sound.
* **Potential Impact:** The work has the potential to influence the design and analysis of MCTS algorithms for a wider range of game-theoretic applications, including security and multi-agent systems.
* **Significance:** Provides a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used algorithm class.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Rate of Convergence Analysis:** Lack of a concrete convergence rate analysis limits the practical utility of the theoretical result.
* **Simplified Experimental Setup:** The experiments, while valuable, could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a strong contribution to the field. The theoretical results are significant and address a long-standing open problem. While the lack of a concrete convergence rate analysis and the limited scope of the experiments are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. The paper is well-written, clearly presented, and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

**Recommendation:** **Accept with Minor Revisions**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. The authors should:

1.  Discuss the implications of the O(D^2) scaling factor in Theorem 4.11 and potential strategies for mitigating its impact.
2.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the practical considerations of ensuring -Hannan consistency in real-world implementations.
3.  Consider including some discussion of the impact of incomplete tree building on the convergence guarantees.
4.  Potentially expand the empirical evaluation to include a wider range of game structures and branching factors.
