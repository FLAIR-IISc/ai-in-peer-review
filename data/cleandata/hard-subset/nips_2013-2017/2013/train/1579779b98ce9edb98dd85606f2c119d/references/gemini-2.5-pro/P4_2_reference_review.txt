This paper addresses the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS variants have been used in practice for this game class (e.g., Finnsson & Björnsson, 2008; Lanctot et al., 2012), they have lacked formal convergence guarantees to a Nash equilibrium (NE). Indeed, prior work has shown that the popular UCT selection strategy does not converge to NE in this setting (Shafiei et al., 2009). The central contribution of this work is to provide the first formal proof of convergence for a class of MCTS algorithms in these games.

The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree selection policy at each node is treated as a repeated matrix game. The key insight is to instantiate this selection policy using any regret-minimizing procedure that is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent, a property from online learning ensuring that an agent's average regret is bounded. The paper proves that if the selection algorithm is $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (i.e., every joint action is tried infinitely often), the resulting MCTS algorithm's average strategies converge to a subgame perfect $\epsilon'$-Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree. It first establishes convergence for single matrix games using known results about Hannan consistency, then extends this to repeated matrix games with bounded error (to model the noisy value estimates from deeper subgames), and finally applies this result recursively up the game tree. The framework is instantiated and empirically evaluated using two well-known Hannan-consistent algorithms: Regret Matching and Exp3. The experiments confirm the convergence and compare the practical performance of different algorithm variants.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The main theoretical claims appear sound and are well-supported by a structured proof. The approach of connecting the MCTS selection problem to the theory of no-regret online learning is a strong foundation for the analysis. The work is a complete contribution, presenting a general theoretical framework, specific instantiations, and an empirical evaluation that validates the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to discuss the assumptions made for the analysis, such as the requirement to backpropagate the mean of rewards rather than the current sample's reward, which is a key difference from many practical MCTS implementations.

A point that could be strengthened is the simplification made at the start of Section 4, which disregards the incremental building of the tree and assumes a complete tree is already built. The paper argues this will happen with probability 1 and that statistics gathered during the tree-building phase do not prevent eventual convergence. While this is intuitive, a more formal argument addressing how the initial, biased estimates from a partially built tree affect the long-term regret bounds would make the proof more rigorous.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the literature. The theoretical development in Section 4 is presented in a logical, bottom-up fashion, starting with simple matrix games and building towards the full extensive-form game setting, which makes the complex proof strategy accessible to the reader. Algorithm 1 provides a good overview of the SM-MCTS template.

However, the presentation of the specific algorithms could be slightly improved for clarity. The `Update` and `RetVal` functions in Algorithm 1 are central to the different variants, but their exact definitions and interactions are spread across the text. For instance, it is stated that returning the mean `Xh/nh` is required for the formal analysis, but this is presented as a variant ("SM-MCTS-M"). A clearer structure might be to present the provably convergent algorithm first in its entirety, and then discuss practical modifications like returning the current sample value.

**Originality:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of search in adversarial domains. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for an MCTS-style algorithm in simultaneous-move games. The primary novelty lies in the synthesis of MCTS with concepts from online learning. While Regret Matching and Exp3 are established algorithms, their application and formal analysis as selection policies within an MCTS framework for this class of games is new and insightful. This work moves beyond heuristic approaches and provides a principled method for designing provably convergent search algorithms for these games.

**Significance:** The results are important as they provide a solid theoretical foundation for the use of MCTS in a well-defined and relevant class of games. This addresses a notable gap between the widespread practical application of MCTS and its theoretical understanding outside of purely sequential, perfect-information games. The general framework, based on the abstract property of $\epsilon$-Hannan consistency, is a powerful contribution, as it allows future no-regret algorithms to be readily incorporated into this provably convergent MCTS template.

The practical significance is tempered slightly by the empirical finding that the provably convergent variant (propagating the mean) converges somewhat slower than the more common heuristic (propagating the single-sample value). Furthermore, the derived bound on the approximation quality of the equilibrium, $O(\epsilon D^2)$, suggests the approximation error may grow quickly with game depth, though this may be an artifact of the proof technique. Nonetheless, the theoretical guarantee itself is a major step forward and is likely to inspire further research into developing faster, provably convergent algorithms and tightening these analytical bounds.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Pro:**
*   Presents the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games, solving an important open problem.
*   The connection established between MCTS selection policies and Hannan-consistent online learning algorithms is novel, elegant, and provides a principled design framework.
*   The paper is technically sound, well-structured, and clearly written.
*   The results provide a strong theoretical justification for using MCTS in this game class, bridging a gap between theory and practice.

**Con:**
*   The analysis relies on the simplifying assumption of an already-built tree, and the argument that this does not affect convergence could be more formal.
*   The theoretical bound on the approximation quality depends quadratically on game depth, which could be loose for deep games. A discussion on the tightness of this bound is absent.
*   The empirical evaluation shows that the provably correct algorithm variant converges slightly slower than a common heuristic, which may limit its immediate practical adoption.
