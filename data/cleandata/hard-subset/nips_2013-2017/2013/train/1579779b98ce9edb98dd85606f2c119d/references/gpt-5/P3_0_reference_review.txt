Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a template SM-MCTS that, at each node, selects joint actions via any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), and backs up either sample outcomes or node means. The main theoretical contribution shows that if the selection routines are ε–HC and each joint action is explored infinitely often, then the empirical/average strategies converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect (approximately) Nash equilibrium; specifically, to an (ε D^2 + δ)-NE, where D is the game tree depth and δ > 0 arbitrary (Theorem 4.11). The proof reduces convergence at internal nodes to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error (due to stochastic backup and exploration), establishes that HC is preserved under uniform exploration (Lemma 4.4), and that HC learning in bounded-error games yields approximate NE (Lemma 4.9). Empirically, the paper compares mean- vs. sample-backup variants using regret matching and Exp3 on random and small worst-case games, showing asymptotic convergence and a modest slowdown when using mean backup.

Relation to prior work
- UCT/UCB-based SM-MCTS has been used empirically, but may fail to converge in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). The paper avoids UCB, focusing on HC selection rules.
- CFR/MCCFR provide convergence guarantees to NE in general two-player zero-sum EFGs (including simultaneous moves), but they are not MCTS in the classic sense; they do regret minimization over information sets rather than per-node bandits. The paper positions itself as the first convergence result for MCTS-style algorithms in simultaneous-move games; a clearer discussion contrasting with CFR/MCCFR (sampling, backups, complexity, and assumptions) would strengthen the narrative.
- The pruning/backward induction works cited for simultaneous-move games (alpha-beta, double oracle) are exact/heuristic search; this paper targets sampling-based methods with minimal domain knowledge.

Strengths
- Clear, modular theoretical framing: reduce SM-MCTS to repeated matrix games with bounded observation error, then apply HC guarantees.
- Generality: covers any ε–HC selection (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), ensures subgame-perfect convergence, and highlights the need for guaranteed exploration.
- Addresses a real gap: convergence of MCTS-like methods in simultaneous-move trees.
- Empirical validation supports the claims qualitatively and examines worst-case microdomains; the observation that mean backups slightly slow convergence is useful.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Tightness of the bound: The (ε D^2 + δ) guarantee is likely loose. Errors accumulate linearly in depth while deviation losses are bounded per level; with a refined induction (e.g., summation of per-level approximation errors), an O(D ε) bound seems plausible. Providing a tighter bound, or discussing why D^2 is inherent, would improve the contribution.
- Mean backups: The convergence proof relies on returning node means rather than sample outcomes, which deviates from standard MCTS practice. While experiments suggest sample backups also converge, the lack of theory for the sample-backup variant is a limitation. Could importance weighting or variance control restore the proof under sample backups?
- Empirical scope: Experiments are small-scale (random utilities; brute force worst cases for D=2, BF=2). No benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) or wall-clock comparisons to established baselines (UCT variants, alpha-beta for simultaneous moves). The experimental section is insufficient to establish practical significance.
- Claims vs. CFR/MCCFR: The statement that “no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general EFGs” should be qualified: CFR/MCCFR are sampling-based regret-minimization methods with convergence guarantees in general EFGs, though not classic “MCTS.” A careful discussion is needed to avoid confusion and to delineate algorithmic and theoretical differences.
- Modeling limitations: No chance nodes, no >2 players, and perfect information only. The paper should discuss whether chance can be incorporated (it typically can in MCTS) and how the analysis would change.
- Clarity/notation: Some pseudocode is hard to parse (line numbers, RetVal/u1 consistency). Theorem 4.11 as written appears to omit ε in the bound (“(D^2 + δ)”)—likely a typo. The regret-matching update mixes x(h,i’,j) with Xhij/u1; this needs clearer definition (cumulative vs. instantaneous). Lemma 4.5 (average vs. empirical strategies) relies on martingale SLLN; a brief sketch would help.

Quality
- Theory is largely sound and builds on established HC/online learning results; the bounded-error repeated game lemma is the key technical step.
- Proof details are deferred to an appendix; main ideas are plausible, but tighter constants and coverage of sample backups would increase rigor.
- Experiments support asymptotic claims but are too limited to assess practical performance.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but algorithmic details and some notation could be clarified.
- Related work should be expanded to include MCCFR/CFR and discuss differences.

Originality
- Novel convergence guarantee for MCTS-style algorithms in simultaneous-move perfect-information games using local HC selection and mean backups.
- The reduction to bounded-error repeated games is sensible though conceptually close to standard regret-based analyses.

Significance
- Theoretical significance for a class of algorithms widely used in practice; may influence design of SM-MCTS with regret-based selection.
- Practical impact is tempered by mean-backup requirement, loose bounds, and limited experiments.

Suggestions and questions
- Can the bound be improved to O(D ε) with a sharper induction or error-propagation argument?
- Can you extend the analysis to sample backups using martingale concentration and almost-sure convergence?
- Discuss how to incorporate chance nodes and whether the results extend beyond two players.
- Add experiments on standard benchmarks and compare to UCT variants and simultaneous-move alpha-beta/double oracle.
- Clarify the relationship to CFR/MCCFR and cite accordingly (e.g., Zinkevich et al. 2007; Lanctot 2009).

Pros
- First asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS with HC selection.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee; simple, general template.
- Solid theoretical framing grounded in online learning.

Cons
- Loose D^2 bound; no finite-sample rates.
- Reliance on mean backups; no proof for standard sample backups.
- Limited empirical evaluation; insufficient comparison to strong baselines.
- Related work omissions/claims need tightening.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution fills a notable gap for simultaneous-move MCTS and is likely to be of interest to the NeurIPS community. Strengthening related work, clarifying algorithmic details, and expanding experiments would further improve the paper.
