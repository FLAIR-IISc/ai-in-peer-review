This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a relatively unexplored area compared to sequential games. The authors establish a theoretical framework showing that MCTS, when employing a -Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure, converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. This is a valuable contribution, as existing theoretical guarantees for MCTS are largely confined to perfect-information sequential games. The empirical evaluation provides some evidence supporting the theoretical findings.

However, despite the potential significance of the work, several shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to more substantial analytical concerns, necessitate a negative recommendation. My assessment leans heavily towards rejection due to the cumulative effect of these flaws, which suggest a lack of meticulousness that undermines confidence in the theoretical claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is genuinely novel. This is a clear gap in the literature that the paper attempts to address.
*   **Theoretical Framework:** The proposed framework linking -Hannan consistency to MCTS convergence is a logical and potentially powerful approach.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of experiments, even if limited, adds some credibility to the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Rigor:** Throughout the paper, the notation is inconsistent and occasionally imprecise. For example, the definition of `A = A1 × A2` is followed by `A1(h) = {1 . . . mh}` and `A2(h) = {1 . . . nh}`. This implies that A is a Cartesian product of sets of *cardinality* `mh` and `nh`, rather than sets themselves, leading to confusion. The variable `h` is used inconsistently to denote both a state *and* an inner state, potentially causing ambiguities.
*   **Assumptions & Simplifications:** The claim of disregarding the incremental tree building and assuming a complete tree is a significant simplification. While stated as a means to simplify the proof, the justification that this simplification doesn’t affect convergence isn't sufficiently robust. It is unclear under what conditions this simplification holds. The impact of this approximation on the convergence rate is also not addressed.
*   **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical guarantees provide convergence to an *approximate* Nash equilibrium, but the approximation factor (`D^2` in Theorem 4.11) is potentially very large, rendering the result practically irrelevant for games with even moderate depth. This issue isn’t adequately discussed. The paper doesn’t offer insights into how this rate might be improved.
*   **Regret Matching and Exploration:** The discussion of regret matching and exploration parameter `γ` feels superficial. The authors state that most HC algorithms guarantee exploration without modification, yet proceed to define a modified algorithm A*. This creates confusion.
*   **Empirical Evaluation - Limited Scope:** The empirical evaluation is limited in scope. The experiments use randomly generated games and only two algorithms (Regret Matching and Exp3). The choice of parameters (e.g., γ) isn’t adequately justified. The statement that the presented games are not "guaranteed" to be worst-case is concerning; a more systematic search for challenging scenarios should have been conducted. The comparison between RMM and RM, while insightful, doesn't provide a conclusive justification for the theoretical choice of using the mean.
*   **Proof Omissions & Accessibility:** The reliance on an appendix and an arXiv link for "omitted proofs" is problematic. A NIPS paper should ideally contain all essential proofs within the main text or a well-integrated supplementary material. This reliance makes it difficult for reviewers to fully assess the validity of the claims.
*   **Citation Issues**: Reference [17] is cited to show that UCB does not converge to a Nash equilibrium, yet the citation itself (Shafiei et al.) appears to be a workshop paper, which is a relatively weak form of publication for such a strong claim.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS.
*   Presents a novel framework for analyzing MCTS convergence.

**Con:**

*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and lack of mathematical rigor.
*   Simplifying assumptions with unclear justification.
*   Weak convergence rate with limited practical relevance.
*   Limited scope of empirical evaluation.
*   Inaccessible proofs.
*   Questionable citation quality.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Reject.** While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a novel theoretical framework, the numerous flaws in presentation, rigor, and scope of evaluation outweigh the contributions. The lack of attention to detail and the potential for significant practical limitations in the convergence rate raise serious concerns about the reliability and impact of this work. I recommend the authors address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting to a venue more suited to preliminary results. Specifically, the paper needs a complete overhaul to enhance mathematical clarity, justify simplifying assumptions, and provide a more compelling empirical validation.
