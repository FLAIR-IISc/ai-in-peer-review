This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The authors establish a general template for MCTS algorithms and prove convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium under the assumption of using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function. The work is accompanied by empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for MCTS in a game setting where such guarantees are largely absent. Addressing the lack of theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond two-player sequential games is a valuable contribution. The formulation using ε-Hannan consistency is appropriate, and the proof sketch, while requiring supplemental material for full details, appears logically sound. The empirical evaluation, although limited, provides some support for the theoretical findings.

However, the paper suffers from a multitude of weaknesses, ranging from minor technical inconsistencies to more significant concerns about the practicality and impact of the results. 

First, the notation is occasionally inconsistent. For instance, the definition of *R* in Section 4.1 uses both G(t) and g(t) – it’s unclear if these represent cumulative and average regret respectively, or if one is a typo. The use of ‘t’ as both a time step and a variable within equations is also confusing.

Second, the formal analysis relies heavily on asymptotic convergence. While theoretically interesting, the convergence rate is not explicitly characterized and the practical implications of the bounds (D^2 + δ) are unclear. A convergence rate of D^2, where D is the game depth, is likely slow and may render the algorithm impractical for larger games. The paper does acknowledge this implicitly by only evaluating on small games.

Third, the implementation details in the empirical section are vague. The “randomly generated games” lack specifics – how are the utilities distributed? What is the branching factor? Lack of these details makes it difficult to assess the reproducibility and generalizability of the results. The comparison to existing MCTS algorithms is brief and doesn't provide a comprehensive benchmark. Saying convergence speed is “comparable” is insufficient without quantified data.

Fourth, the claim that many existing algorithms likely also converge is speculative and unsubstantiated. The authors merely "conjecture" this, providing no concrete evidence. 

Fifth, the presentation of Algorithm 1 is awkward.  Lines 8 and 14, “Update(h, i, j, u1)”, are overly abstract. The function ‘Update’ isn’t defined anywhere within the main paper (presumably it’s related to regret update mechanisms, but this should be explicit). The notation `Xhij` used within the Update function lacks definition, and it is not evident from the main text how Regret Matching and Exp3 fit into this update process.

Sixth, the discussion of UCB being excluded because it doesn’t converge to a Nash equilibrium is a somewhat weak justification. Many practical algorithms don't have theoretical guarantees, but are nonetheless useful. The authors could have briefly discussed *why* UCB fails in this setting, providing more insight.

Finally, the acknowledgements section is overly verbose and includes irrelevant information about computing infrastructure.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Addresses a gap in the existing literature.
*   Empirical results, although limited, are consistent with the theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Weak convergence rate and limited practical implications.
*   Numerous minor technical flaws and inconsistencies.
*   Vague implementation details and lack of a comprehensive benchmark.
*   Speculative claims about the convergence of existing algorithms.
*   Overly detailed and irrelevant acknowledgements.
*   Poorly described algorithms.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty of the theoretical result, the numerous flaws – particularly the unclear presentation, impractical convergence rate, and lack of robust empirical validation – outweigh the contributions. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should significantly revise the paper, addressing the technical inconsistencies, providing a more thorough empirical evaluation, and clarifying the practical implications of their theoretical findings. A detailed discussion on the convergence rates and the effect of the game’s parameters would be crucial for improving the value of the work.
