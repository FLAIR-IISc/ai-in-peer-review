This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for the class of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The central contribution is a proof that MCTS, when using an ε-Hannan consistent algorithm for action selection (such as Regret Matching or Exp3) and a specific update rule, converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively, showing that if the values from child nodes are accurate within a certain bound, the selection policies will converge to an approximate Nash Equilibrium at the parent node, introducing a slightly larger error bound. The authors provide an empirical evaluation comparing their provably convergent variant, which propagates the mean of collected rewards, against the more standard approach of propagating the single outcome of the current simulation.

**Strengths:**

The primary strength of this work is that it addresses a significant and long-standing theoretical gap in the MCTS literature. While MCTS and its variants are widely applied to various game types, formal convergence guarantees outside of sequential, perfect-information games are scarce. Providing the first convergence proof for this class of simultaneous move games is a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of these popular algorithms.

The paper is well-written and the structure of the formal analysis is clear. The authors methodically build their argument from foundational results in repeated matrix games (Lemmas 4.4-4.7) to games with bounded errors (Lemmas 4.9-4.10), and finally to the full extensive-form game setting (Theorem 4.11). This approach makes the theoretical argument easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the theoretical novelty, the paper has several weaknesses that temper its overall significance and practical impact.

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The convergence proof relies on a critical modification to the standard MCTS framework: propagating the mean value of a node (`Xh/nh`) up the tree instead of the result of the current simulation (`u1`). The authors' own empirical results (Figures 2 and 3) demonstrate that this modification, while necessary for the proof, consistently leads to slower convergence than the standard method. This creates a problematic disconnect: the paper proves convergence for a version of the algorithm that appears to be empirically inferior. While theoretical soundness is important, its value diminishes if it only applies to a less effective variant of an algorithm. The work does not provide a compelling reason for a practitioner to adopt this modified, provably convergent, but slower algorithm over the established, unproven, but faster one.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation Against Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is narrow in scope and fails to benchmark against relevant established methods. The paper's introduction correctly identifies backward induction with alpha-beta pruning as an exact, polynomial-time solver for this game class. A rigorous evaluation would compare the MCTS approach against these established solvers to demonstrate where the trade-off between computation time and solution quality makes MCTS a preferable choice. The current experiments only compare two minor variants of MCTS against each other, which provides limited insight into the algorithm's performance in the broader context of game-solving techniques.

3.  **Limited "Worst-Case" Analysis:** The empirical analysis of convergence rate is based on a brute-force search for "worst-case" games within an extremely limited space (depth 2, branching factor 2, utilities from {0, 0.5, 1}). Generalizing from such a small, discrete set of toy problems is difficult. The resulting analysis does not provide strong evidence about the algorithm's performance characteristics on larger or more complex games that would be of practical interest.

4.  **Limited Scope of Theoretical Guarantees:** The presented framework relies on ε-Hannan consistency. While this covers important algorithms like Regret Matching and Exp3, it notably excludes UCB-based selection strategies, which are arguably the most popular and successful MCTS variants in practice. The authors acknowledge that UCB does not converge in this setting, but this highlights that the new theoretical understanding does not yet extend to many of the most widely used methods.

**Summary of Arguments for/against Acceptance:**

*   **For Acceptance:**
    *   Presents the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous move games, filling a notable theoretical gap.
    *   The theoretical framework is general, applying to a class of selection mechanisms (ε-Hannan consistent algorithms).
    *   The paper is well-written and the proof structure is logical and clear.

*   **Against Acceptance:**
    *   The theoretical result is only valid for a modified version of MCTS that is empirically shown to be slower than the standard variant.
    *   The empirical evaluation is weak, lacking comparisons to established non-MCTS baselines for this game class.
    *   The analysis of convergence rates is based on a search over a trivial set of games, limiting the generalizability of the findings.
    *   The proof does not cover many of the most popular and practical MCTS selection strategies, such as UCB.

In conclusion, while the paper makes a valid theoretical contribution, its practical significance is questionable. The core result proves the convergence of an algorithm that seems to perform worse than existing heuristics. The paper lacks the rigorous empirical validation against established baselines that would be necessary to demonstrate the utility of this approach. The work is a sound piece of theory, but it feels incomplete as a contribution to a top-tier conference like NIPS, where a stronger connection between theory and state-of-the-art practice is expected. Before this work can be endorsed, the authors should either extend their analysis to cover more practical MCTS variants or provide a much more compelling empirical case that their provable method is competitive with, or superior to, other established solvers under certain conditions.
