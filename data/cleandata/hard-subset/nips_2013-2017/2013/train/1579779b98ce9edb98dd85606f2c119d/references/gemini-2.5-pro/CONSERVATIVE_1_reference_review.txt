This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for the class of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The central contribution is a proof that MCTS, when instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure for action selection, converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The authors build their proof inductively, treating the values returned from subgames as noisy payoffs in a repeated matrix game with bounded error. They demonstrate this framework with two established regret minimizers, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, and provide an empirical analysis on randomly generated and small worst-case games to evaluate convergence behavior.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses a significant and acknowledged theoretical gap. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for various game types, formal convergence guarantees outside of standard sequential games are scarce. Providing the first such proof for this class of simultaneous-move games is a commendable theoretical goal.
*   The proof structure is general. By framing the convergence in terms of ε-Hannan consistency, the result is not tied to a single algorithm but provides a template for a family of convergent MCTS methods.
*   The theoretical approach of modeling the problem as a hierarchy of repeated matrix games with bounded error is a sound and logical way to structure the inductive proof.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's primary weaknesses lie in a notable disconnect between the theoretical results and their practical implications, as well as an insufficient empirical validation.

1.  **Theory-Practice Disconnect:** The formal analysis requires a modification to the standard MCTS value propagation mechanism. Specifically, the proof relies on parent nodes receiving the *mean* of all rewards from a child node (termed RMM/Exp3M) rather than the reward from the *current* simulation. However, the authors' own experiments in Section 5.1 (Figure 2) consistently show that this "mean" propagation method (RMM) converges more slowly than the standard method (RM). This is a critical issue: the paper provides a convergence guarantee for a variant of the algorithm that appears to be empirically inferior. While the authors conjecture that the analysis could be extended to the standard, better-performing variants, this is not demonstrated. As it stands, the paper proves the correctness of a seemingly suboptimal algorithm, which significantly lessens the practical impact of the theoretical contribution.

2.  **Insufficient Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is not rigorous enough to support the paper's claims or demonstrate its significance.
    *   **Lack of Baselines:** The experiments exclusively compare variants of the proposed MCTS template (RM vs. RMM, Exp3 vs. Exp3M). There is no comparison against other established methods for solving these games. The introduction mentions exact solvers based on backward induction and alpha-beta pruning [8, 9]. A comparison of solution quality versus computation time against these solvers, even on smaller games, is essential to contextualize the performance of an approximation algorithm like MCTS. Without such baselines, the claim that the algorithm's convergence speed is "comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms" is unsubstantiated.
    *   **Choice of Testbed:** The evaluation is conducted on randomly generated games and a small set of "worst-case" games discovered through brute-force search. This is a limited testbed. The paper would be far more compelling if it demonstrated performance on well-known benchmark games from this class, such as Goofspiel, which is mentioned in the introduction but absent from the experiments. Using established benchmarks is crucial for reproducibility and for comparing results to prior and future work.

3.  **Modest Algorithmic Originality:** The core algorithmic idea—using regret-matching techniques for action selection within MCTS for simultaneous move games—is not entirely new. For instance, Lanctot et al. (2012, cited as [20]) previously explored using Regret Matching in MCTS for Goofspiel. Therefore, the primary contribution of this paper is the theoretical proof, which, as discussed above, has significant limitations in its current form.

4.  **Proof Assumptions and Bounds:** The analysis simplifies the problem by assuming the game tree is completely built, arguing this will happen eventually. This sidesteps the initial, and most critical, phase of MCTS where the tree structure is discovered. While a common analytical tool, this simplification weakens the connection to the practical algorithm. Furthermore, the resulting approximation bound of O(εD^2) on exploitability has a quadratic dependence on the game depth D, which may be too loose to be meaningful for deeper games.

**Summary of Arguments:**

**Pro-Acceptance:**
*   It is the first work to provide a formal convergence proof for MCTS in this game class, addressing an open theoretical question.
*   The proof framework is general and based on the sound theoretical foundation of Hannan consistency.

**Con-Acceptance:**
*   The main theoretical result applies to a modified version of MCTS that is empirically demonstrated to be slower than the standard version, creating a significant gap between theory and practice.
*   The empirical evaluation is weak, lacking comparisons to established baseline algorithms and tests on standard benchmark problems. This makes it difficult to assess the practical value or performance of the method.
*   The algorithmic novelty is limited, with the main contribution being a proof of questionable practical relevance.

In conclusion, while the paper takes on an important theoretical challenge, its contributions are undermined by the gap between the proven theory and practical algorithm performance, and by a lack of rigorous empirical validation against established methods. The work is more of a preliminary theoretical exercise than a complete contribution. For the paper to be suitable for publication, the authors would need to either extend their proof to cover the standard, empirically superior MCTS variants or provide a much more thorough experimental section that includes standard benchmarks and comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines, thereby demonstrating the practical significance of their approach.
