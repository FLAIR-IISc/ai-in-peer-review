- First paper to give convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, proving convergence to subgame-perfect ε-Nash under ε-Hannan consistent selection.  
- Introduces a general MCTS template and proves convergence via induction on game-tree depth; instantiated with regret matching and Exp3.  
- Novel connection drawn between online learning (Hannan consistency/regret minimization) and MCTS.  
- Empirical results support theoretical claims, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in practice.  
- Paper is well-organized with clear definitions, consistent notation, and a logical proof structure.  
- Work is practically relevant for practitioners working with MCTS in simultaneous-move games.  
- Major weakness: no convergence-rate bounds are provided, limiting practical usefulness; experiments for rate are restricted to depth-2 games.  
- Limited empirical comparisons: experiments compare only variants of the authors’ methods (RMM vs RM), not established MCTS baselines.  
- Empirical observation that RMM (mean propagation) converges slightly slower than RM (sample propagation) is reported but not explained.  
- Critical tree-building assumption (tree eventually built with probability 1) is referenced to the appendix instead of being rigorously shown in the main text.  
- Scope is narrow: restricted to zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games and does not address imperfect-information or broader game classes.  
- Numerical stability issue with Exp3 is mentioned but not analyzed for its impact on convergence or implementation.  
- Quality: strong theoretical core and rigorous proof; experimental design is reasonable but limited; lack of rate analysis is a key shortcoming.  
- Clarity: generally clear and well-written; numerical stability discussion needs expansion.  
- Originality and significance: highly original, fills a long-standing theoretical gap, and has potential to be foundational for further extensions.  
- Suggested revisions: add convergence-rate discussion and deeper empirical analysis, explain RMM vs RM behavior, compare to existing MCTS algorithms, and strengthen tree-building assumption discussion.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Final assessment: important and well-executed theoretical contribution despite limited empirical scope and practical-rate analysis; of strong interest to ML and game-theory communities.
