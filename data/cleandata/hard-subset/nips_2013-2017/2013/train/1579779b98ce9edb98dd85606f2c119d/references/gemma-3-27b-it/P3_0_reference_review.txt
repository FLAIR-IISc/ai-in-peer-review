## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental open problem in the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): proving convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move games. The authors present a general MCTS template for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, demonstrating that using a -Hannan consistent selection function leads to convergence to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They instantiate this template with Regret Matching and Exp3, and empirically validate their theoretical results on randomly generated and worst-case games. The work is a significant step towards establishing a solid theoretical foundation for MCTS in a broader class of games beyond sequential ones.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Crucial Open Problem:** The lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a major limitation. This paper directly addresses this gap, providing a much-needed theoretical framework.
* **Generality of the Template:**  The proposed template is generic, allowing for the use of *any* -Hannan consistent regret minimizing procedure. This makes the work broadly applicable and allows for future extensions.
* **Rigorous Proof:** The proof of convergence, while relying on known results regarding Hannan consistency, demonstrates a clear understanding of how to apply these concepts to the specific challenges of simultaneous-move games.  The decomposition of the problem into repeated matrix games with bounded error is a clever and effective approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation provides valuable support for the theoretical findings, showing convergence in practice and demonstrating comparable performance to existing algorithms.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are precise and the core ideas are presented in a logical manner. 
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good awareness of the existing literature on MCTS, game theory, and related algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves convergence, it doesn't provide any bounds on the *rate* of convergence.  The experiments offer some insight, but a tighter theoretical bound would be highly valuable. The constant factor in the approximate Nash Equilibrium is quite large (D^2) potentially limiting practical applicability.
* **Practicality of the Constant:**  The dependence on D^2 in the convergence guarantee might be too strong for many practical applications.  While the authors acknowledge this implicitly with the empirical comparison, a discussion of techniques to mitigate this dependency would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments primarily use randomly generated games and a few constructed worst-case scenarios. Testing on more real-world game instances (e.g., Goofspiel, variations of pursuit-evasion) would strengthen the empirical evidence.
* **Assumptions on Hannan Consistency:** The theoretical analysis relies heavily on the assumption of -Hannan consistency. While commonly used, it's important to acknowledge that achieving this in practice can be challenging, and the convergence guarantee is conditional on this assumption being met.
* **Repetitive Discussion of UCB:**  The paper repeatedly mentions that it doesn't focus on UCB and why it doesn’t converge. While valid, this feels somewhat repetitive and distracts from the core contribution.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 4.4 (Proof Sketch):** The jump from convergence in subgames to subgame perfect equilibrium feels a bit hand-wavy.  More detail regarding how the errors propagate and combine across different subgames would be helpful.
* **Algorithm 1 (SM-MCTS):**  The algorithm description is clear, but the “RetVal” function could be more explicitly defined and motivated.
* **Experiment Design:** It would be useful to include error bars or confidence intervals in the experimental results to demonstrate the statistical significance of the findings.
* **Connection to Existing Algorithms:** The authors conjecture that many existing SM-MCTS algorithms also converge. It would be valuable to explicitly analyze specific algorithms (like those from [12, 11]) and show how they fit within the proposed template.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, addressing a long-standing open problem.
* **Generality & Flexibility:** The proposed template is broadly applicable and allows for the use of different regret minimizing procedures.
* **Sound Methodology:** The proof is rigorous and based on well-established concepts from game theory and online learning.
* **Empirical Support:** The experimental results provide strong support for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Convergence Rate Analysis:** The lack of bounds on the convergence rate weakens the practical impact of the results.
* **Potential Practical Limitations:** The D^2 dependence in the convergence guarantee may be too strong for some applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of game-playing AI. While the lack of a convergence rate analysis and potential practical limitations are weaknesses, the importance of the theoretical result and the soundness of the methodology outweigh these concerns. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NIPS, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the reviewer comments and potentially exploring the convergence rate in future work.

**Rating:**  **Accept (with minor revisions)**.  I recommend the authors address the comments regarding the convergence rate, practicality, and empirical evaluation to further strengthen the paper.
