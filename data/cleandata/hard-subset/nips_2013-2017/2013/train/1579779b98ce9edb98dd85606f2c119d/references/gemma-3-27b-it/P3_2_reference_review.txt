## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental open problem in game theory and AI: proving convergence guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games. The authors present a general template for SM-MCTS algorithms, leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as a selection mechanism.  They formally demonstrate that, under the condition of using a -Hannan consistent selection function, their MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. Empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3 demonstrates convergence rates comparable to existing SM-MCTS algorithms. The work builds upon the foundations of MCTS, regret minimization, and game theory to provide theoretical underpinnings for a widely used, yet previously poorly understood, algorithmic approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:**  This is the most significant strength of the paper.  Providing a convergence proof for SM-MCTS fills a critical gap in the literature.  The connection to -Hannan consistency provides a robust foundation and clarifies the necessary conditions for convergence. The proof strategy, building from matrix games to extensive-form games, is well-structured.
* **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is highly general, allowing for the integration of various regret-minimizing procedures. This flexibility enhances the applicability of the results.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental section, while not exhaustive, offers valuable insights. The comparison with existing algorithms demonstrates practical relevance and supports the theoretical findings. Showing results for both Regret Matching and Exp3 is a good choice.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions and background material are clearly presented, making the core concepts accessible.  The algorithmic description (Algorithm 1) is concise and understandable.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors thoughtfully place their work within the context of existing research on MCTS, regret minimization, and game theory, appropriately referencing relevant literature (e.g., [15, 16, 17, 20]).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical analysis guarantees *convergence* but provides little insight into the *rate* of convergence. This is a significant limitation, particularly for practical applications.  While the empirical evaluation hints at comparable rates to existing algorithms, a more detailed analysis of the convergence speed is needed.
* **Practical Considerations:** The analysis hinges on the assumption of a complete tree (stated after Lemma 4.4). While the authors acknowledge this simplification and argue for its eventual validity, it's unclear how quickly this full tree is built in practice, impacting the effectiveness of the convergence guarantee.  Real-world MCTS implementations typically operate with limited tree depth and computational resources.
* **Exploration Parameter (γ):** The impact of the exploration parameter γ on the convergence rate and equilibrium quality isn't thoroughly investigated.  Finding the optimal γ for different game structures is crucial for practical performance.
* **Worst-Case Games:** The empirical analysis uses randomly generated and worst-case games. The provided worst case results appear to be selected based on the algorithm's performance (the most difficult games for each method), and are not necessarily representative of general worst-case behavior.  A more rigorous worst-case analysis, potentially focusing on specific game structures known to be challenging for MCTS, would be beneficial.
* **Numerical Stability:** While the authors mention addressing numerical overflow issues in Exp3, the potential for numerical instability in the regret calculations, particularly in deep or complex games, could be further addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant theoretical challenge in the field of game-theoretic AI. The convergence proof is a substantial contribution and establishes a firm foundation for the use of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While the paper has some limitations regarding convergence rate analysis and practical considerations, these do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work.  

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a novel and significant theoretical result, filling a critical gap in the literature.
* Offers a general framework for SM-MCTS algorithms with provable convergence guarantees.
* Empirically validates the theoretical findings.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Lacks detailed analysis of the convergence rate.
* The theoretical model relies on a simplifying assumption (complete tree) that might not accurately reflect practical MCTS implementations.
*  Limited investigation of the exploration parameter and its impact on performance.




**Rating:**  **Strong Accept** (7/10). I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of publication at NIPS. I recommend the authors address the weaknesses regarding convergence rate analysis and practical considerations in future work.
