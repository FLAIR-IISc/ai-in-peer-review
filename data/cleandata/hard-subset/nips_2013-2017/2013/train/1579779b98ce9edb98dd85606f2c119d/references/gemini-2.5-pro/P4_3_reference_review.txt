**Summary of the Main Ideas**

This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors note that while MCTS variants are used in practice for this game class (e.g., in general game playing), a formal guarantee of their convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been an open problem. Previous work has even shown that standard MCTS selection strategies like UCB can fail to converge.

The main contribution of this paper is a formal proof that a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) converges to a subgame perfect epsilon-Nash equilibrium. The key condition for this convergence is that the action selection mechanism within each node of the game tree is an epsilon-Hannan consistent algorithm. The paper demonstrates this using two well-known regret-minimizing procedures: Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. The proof is structured inductively. It begins by leveraging the known result that two Hannan-consistent players converge to a Nash equilibrium in a repeated matrix game. It then extends this to the case of repeated matrix games with bounded error, which serves as a model for the noisy value estimates from child nodes in an MCTS tree. Finally, it applies this result inductively over the depth of the game tree to prove convergence for the entire extensive-form game. The paper also includes an empirical evaluation that compares the convergence speed of different variants, including the theoretically justified version (propagating mean values) and the more common practical version (propagating the current sample value).

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The central claim—that SM-MCTS using an epsilon-Hannan consistent selection rule converges to an epsilon-NE—is well-supported by a rigorous theoretical analysis. The proof structure is sound, logically building from foundational results in online learning to the more complex setting of extensive-form games. The introduction of "repeated matrix games with bounded error" is a key and effective step for modeling the propagation of value estimates up the game tree. The empirical section complements the theory by investigating convergence rates and comparing different algorithmic variants on both random and worst-case games.

A minor weakness is the simplifying assumption made at the start of Section 4, where the analysis disregards the incremental tree growth and assumes a complete game tree. While the authors argue this is acceptable for an asymptotic proof (as all nodes will be visited infinitely often), the dynamics of value estimation during the tree expansion phase are non-trivial. A more detailed argument on why the statistics collected during this early phase do not prevent eventual convergence would strengthen the analysis. Additionally, the theoretical analysis requires propagating the mean value of rollouts (`Xh/nh`), whereas the experiments show that propagating the current sample value (`u1`) leads to slightly faster convergence. This creates a small disconnect between the proven theory and the empirically better-performing variant.

**Clarity:**
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work and motivates the need for a convergence proof. The background section provides all necessary definitions. The core algorithm is presented as a general template (Algorithm 1), with specific instantiations for Regret Matching and Exp3 detailed subsequently, which aids reader comprehension. The structure of the formal analysis, moving from simple matrix games to the full extensive-form game, is logical and easy to follow.

One area for potential improvement is the intuition behind the final error bound. The main result (Theorem 4.11) states an epsilon-NE where epsilon depends on the square of the game depth D. The derivation is in the appendix, but a brief, intuitive explanation in the main text for this quadratic dependency would be beneficial for the reader.

**Originality:**
The primary contribution of this work is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first paper to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this class of simultaneous move games. While the constituent parts—MCTS and regret minimization algorithms like RM and Exp3—are well-established, their synthesis into a provably convergent framework for these games is a novel and important contribution. The theoretical approach of analyzing MCTS through the lens of Hannan consistency in repeated games with bounded errors is a creative way to bridge the gap between online learning theory and game tree search.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant. MCTS is one of the most successful and widely used algorithms for game playing, and this work fills a crucial theoretical gap regarding its application outside of purely sequential games. By providing a formal justification for using regret-minimization techniques within MCTS, the paper provides a solid foundation for future research and practice. The general nature of the proof, applicable to any epsilon-Hannan consistent algorithm, makes the result broadly useful. Researchers and practitioners working on game AI for simultaneous move games can now employ a family of algorithms with a guarantee of convergence, which is a substantial advancement over relying on purely empirical evidence. The work is likely to be built upon, for instance, by seeking finite-time convergence bounds or by extending the analysis to more complex game classes, such as those with imperfect information.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in perfect-information, simultaneous move games, a long-standing open problem.
2.  **Technical Soundness:** The proof is well-structured and builds upon established concepts from online learning and game theory in a rigorous manner.
3.  **High Significance:** The result provides theoretical justification for a widely used class of algorithms and opens avenues for further theoretical analysis of MCTS in more general game settings.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and accessible to readers with a background in game theory and machine learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Asymptotic Result:** The convergence guarantee is asymptotic, and the paper does not provide finite-time bounds, which can limit direct practical application.
2.  **Loose Bound:** The derived error bound for the epsilon-NE scales quadratically with the game depth (D^2), which may be too loose to be meaningful for very deep games.
3.  **Theory vs. Practice Discrepancy:** The theoretically analyzed algorithm variant is shown to be slightly empirically inferior to a similar variant that is not covered by the proof.
