Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The main contribution is a general SM-MCTS template that, when instantiated with a selection rule that is ε-Hannan consistent (HC) and guarantees exploration, is shown to converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis proceeds by: (i) reviewing repeated matrix games and HC; (ii) showing that empirical/average strategies of HC learners converge to approximate NE, including in the presence of bounded payoff errors; and (iii) composing these results inductively up the game tree, yielding an eventual convergence guarantee whose approximation error scales with depth. The analysis requires returning the mean of accumulated rewards rather than the most recent sample during backpropagation. Experiments with regret matching (RM) and Exp3 instantiate the template and empirically validate convergence on random and “worst-case” small games; using mean backpropagation slows convergence slightly.

This work sits between bandit/online learning (Hannan consistency; EXP3) and game-tree search. It addresses a gap: UCT has strong performance in sequential perfect-information games but is known not to converge in simultaneous-move settings; exact backward induction with LP or double oracle is expensive. Previous SM-MCTS variants existed without provable convergence. The paper provides the first asymptotic convergence analysis for SM-MCTS in this setting and connects it to standard HC algorithms.

Quality:
- Theory: The proof strategy is standard and sensible: reduce local selection to repeated matrix games, prove approximate equilibrium under HC, handle stochasticity and exploration-induced bias via “repeated game with bounded error,” and propagate error through depth. The “guaranteed exploration” and mean-backprop assumptions are clearly identified and used. The main theorem claims almost-sure convergence to a subgame-perfect (approximately) ε-Nash equilibrium with an error that grows with depth (stated as O(D^2), with constants from the lemmas like 4(c+1)). The argument that incremental tree growth does not prevent eventual convergence is plausible given guaranteed exploration. Some constants appear loose (e.g., the factor 4 in matrix-game equilibrium; quadratic blow-up with depth), and the statement of Theorem 4.11 seems to omit an explicit ε factor in the bound (it reads “(D^2 + δ)-NE” but earlier results suggest an ε-dependent bound). Please clarify the exact dependence on ε and constants.
- Algorithms: The RM and Exp3 instantiations match known HC algorithms. The requirement to return means rather than latest samples is nonstandard in MCTS but is justified by the analysis; experiments confirm only modest slowdown.
- Clarity of pseudocode/update rules: There are places that need tightening. In RM, x(h,i,j) = Xhij if (i,j) ≠ chosen else u1 mixes cumulative sums and single samples; presumably this is meant to use the most recent sample for the chosen joint action and some estimate for others—please clarify. In the template, Select is described as choosing a joint action; for Exp3, each player samples independently, which is fine but should be consistent in description. The “A*” exploration step that does not update learner state is important—please justify this choice more explicitly.
- Empirics: The experiments are correct for the paper’s claims (eventual convergence and the effect of mean-backprop) but limited. They are small-scale, emphasize asymptotics, and do not compare to strong baselines like CFR or search with alpha-beta pruning/double oracle in the same domains. No finite-time convergence rates are provided, and exploitability is reported primarily for small toy games.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized, with precise definitions and a clear proof roadmap. However, several notational and algorithmic details are confusing:
- Be consistent and explicit about empirical vs average strategies; Lemma 4.5 is subtle and deserves a short proof sketch or clearer statement of conditions.
- Clearly specify RetVal and when the mean must be used vs when sample values are acceptable; currently this is split between text and pseudocode.
- Fix minor typos and grammar (e.g., missing ε in the final theorem statement; occasional awkward phrasing).
- Provide more intuition for why errors accumulate to O(D^2), and whether this is tight.

Originality:
The contribution appears novel in this specific setting: a rigorous asymptotic convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under HC selection, with error propagation analysis. While the components (HC in repeated games, exploration guarantees, mean returns) are known, the combination and the inductive composition over a simultaneous-move tree are, to my knowledge, new. The empirical observation that mean backprop slows but preserves convergence is useful practitioner guidance.

Significance:
The result is theoretically meaningful for researchers using SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a class relevant in general game playing and certain pursuit-evasion/carding domains. Establishing that widely used HC selection rules yield (eventual) approximate NE bolsters confidence in these methods. That said, the impact is limited by:
- Asymptotic nature of the guarantees, loose constants, and quadratic depth dependence.
- The nonstandard mean-backprop requirement, which diverges from mainstream MCTS practice.
- Limited experimental validation and lack of finite-time bounds or comparisons to best-known alternatives.
Still, as a first convergence analysis, it provides a useful foundation.

Strengths:
- First rigorous convergence analysis of SM-MCTS to (subgame-perfect) approximate NE under broad HC selection.
- Clear reduction to repeated games with bounded error; principled inductive proof structure.
- Practical instantiations with RM/Exp3 and empirical validation.
- Identifies and isolates key conditions (guaranteed exploration, mean backprop).

Weaknesses:
- Asymptotic only; no finite-sample rates or depth-sensitive constants optimized.
- Error bound scales as O(D^2) and constants (e.g., factor 4) seem loose; missing ε in theorem statement.
- Algorithmic details (updates, selection, RetVal) need clearer, unambiguous specification.
- Empirical section is small-scale and does not compare to strong baselines (e.g., CFR) or report on larger benchmarks.
- The exploration scheme that does not update learner state is a particular choice; alternative schemes and their effect are not discussed.

Questions and suggestions:
- Clarify Theorem 4.11’s bound: exact dependence on ε, D, and exploration γ; is the D^2 factor tight?
- Can the analysis be extended to sample-backprop (the common MCTS setting), perhaps with additional mixing or averaging assumptions?
- Provide finite-time guarantees by combining known regret bounds for RM/Exp3 with your error propagation analysis.
- Compare empirically to CFR (or CFR-BR) and to alpha-beta/double-oracle search on the same simultaneous-move benchmarks.
- Improve pseudocode and RM update description; ensure consistency of joint vs independent selection.
- Discuss the practical choice of γ and its effect on exploitability (since uniform exploration induces residual exploitability).

Recommendation:
Weak accept. The paper makes a clear, novel theoretical contribution on an under-analyzed but relevant class of games. Tightening the statement/proofs, clarifying algorithms, and expanding empirical comparisons would strengthen it further.
