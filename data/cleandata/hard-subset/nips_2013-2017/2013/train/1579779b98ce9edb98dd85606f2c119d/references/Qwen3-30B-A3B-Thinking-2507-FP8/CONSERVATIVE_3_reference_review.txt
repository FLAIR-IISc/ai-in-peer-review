

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper proposes a formal convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template using regret-minimizing procedures (regret matching and Exp3) for action selection, and prove that with ε-Hannan consistent selection functions, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. They empirically validate their claims on randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper addresses a genuine gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games, where convergence guarantees have previously been lacking.

2. The formal analysis is technically sound and provides a valuable theoretical foundation for MCTS in this specific class of games. The proof is well-structured and addresses the key challenges of simultaneous moves.

3. The paper clearly distinguishes between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation, acknowledging that their formal analysis doesn't cover all existing MCTS variants (e.g., UCT-based approaches).

4. The empirical evaluation is thorough, with appropriate comparisons between mean propagation (RMM) and current sample propagation (RM) variants, and analysis of worst-case convergence behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Practical Significance:** The main contribution is theoretical, but the empirical results show that the theoretically justified RMM variant converges *slightly slower* than the current practice (RM). The difference is small in small games but becomes more apparent in larger games. This is counterintuitive to the paper's claim that their method "justifies the usage of MCTS as an approximation algorithm," since practitioners would prefer faster convergence.

2. **Overstated Claims:** The paper claims to be "the first formal analysis of convergence of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves." However, the references [10, 11, 12, 13] cited as prior work on SM-MCTS appear to be practical applications of MCTS in simultaneous move games, which suggests the theoretical gap they claim to fill may not be as significant as presented.

3. **Simplified Assumptions:** The authors acknowledge they "disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree." This is a significant simplification that may not reflect practical MCTS implementations, where the tree is built incrementally and the number of simulations is finite. The theoretical guarantee of convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium is for the limit as the number of iterations approaches infinity, but the paper doesn't provide finite-time convergence bounds, which are crucial for practical applications.

4. **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** While the paper compares RMM to RM (the current practice), it doesn't compare to other established methods for solving simultaneous move games, such as backward induction with alpha-beta pruning [8, 9] or double-oracle methods [9]. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether MCTS (with or without the proposed theoretical guarantees) offers any practical advantages over existing exact methods.

5. **Questionable Practical Value:** The paper acknowledges that "the depth-limited search algorithms based on backward induction require domain knowledge (an evaluation function)" and that "for practical applications and in situations with limited domain knowledge, variants of simulation-based algorithms such as MCTS are typically used." However, the empirical results show that the theoretically justified method (RMM) converges slower than the current practice (RM), which undermines the practical value of the theoretical contribution.

**Critical Assessment:**

As a skeptical reviewer, I find the theoretical contribution interesting but limited in its practical significance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a specific class of games, but the practical implications are questionable given that the theoretically justified variant converges slightly slower than current practice. The paper's claim that "this justifies the usage of MCTS as an approximation algorithm for this class of games" is undermined by the empirical results showing that the method they propose (RMM) is slower than the method currently used (RM).

The paper would have been stronger if it had provided:
1. Finite-time convergence bounds that would be useful for practitioners
2. A clear demonstration of when MCTS would be preferred over exact methods (like backward induction with alpha-beta pruning)
3. A comparison to established game-solving methods for simultaneous move games

**Recommendation:**

I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a technically sound theoretical contribution, the practical significance is limited, and the empirical results actually undermine the practical value of the theoretical framework they propose. The paper presents a new theoretical framework but doesn't demonstrate that it leads to better performance than existing methods. In NIPS, we prioritize work that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, and this paper falls short in that regard.

The authors would benefit from conducting more extensive empirical comparisons with established baselines (like backward induction with alpha-beta pruning and double-oracle methods) to demonstrate when their theoretical framework provides a practical advantage. Without such evidence, the contribution remains largely theoretical with limited practical implications, which is not sufficient for NIPS.
