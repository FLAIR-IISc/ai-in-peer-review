- Summary: Proves that an SM-MCTS template using ε-Hannan consistent selection converges to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium, with empirical validation using Regret Matching and Exp3.

- Context: Addresses the gap in theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games and situates the work among regret-minimization and MCTS literature.

- Relation to prior negative results: Correctly notes UCB-based MCTS can fail to converge in simultaneous games (Shafiei et al., 2017).

- Theoretical contribution: Formal, inductive proof of convergence from matrix games to general extensive-form simultaneous-move games.

- Key technical tool: Use of ε-Hannan consistency for action selection as the main mechanism ensuring convergence.

- Generality: Template allows instantiation with different regret-minimizing procedures, increasing applicability.

- Empirical validation: Experiments with Regret Matching and Exp3 show convergence speeds comparable to existing SM-MCTS methods.

- Presentation: Paper is generally well-organized with clear definitions and an easy-to-follow algorithm description.

- Literature grounding: Strong connection to relevant work in game theory, online learning, and MCTS.

- Convergence rate weakness: No theoretical bounds on convergence speed; constants (notably D^2) may be large and impractical.

- Strong assumption: Analysis assumes perfect information, limiting applicability to imperfect-information games.

- Empirical scope weakness: Experiments are limited in diversity and metrics (mainly exploitability); more domains and cost metrics needed.

- Numerical issues: Brief mention of numerical stability problems with Exp3 and a workaround; more detail needed on mitigation.

- Accessibility: Section 4 is rigorous but hard to follow for readers not expert in online learning; needs more intuition.

- Arguments for acceptance: Significant theoretical advance, general template, and potential impact on multi-agent and security domains.

- Arguments against acceptance: Lack of convergence-rate analysis and restrictive perfect-information assumption reduce practical impact.

- Overall recommendation: Strong paper; accept with minor revisions.

- Revision suggestions: Discuss convergence-rate challenges and future work, expand experiments and metrics, add intuitive explanations in Section 4, detail numerical-stability fixes, and include a discussion on extending to imperfect information.
