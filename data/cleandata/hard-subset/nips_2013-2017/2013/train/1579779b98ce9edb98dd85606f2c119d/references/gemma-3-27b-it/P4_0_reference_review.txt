This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, proving convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under certain conditions. The approach leverages -Hannan consistent selection functions within an MCTS template. Empirical validation is provided using regret matching and Exp3.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. It correctly identifies a gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS outside of sequential games. The connection to -Hannan consistency is a key contribution and provides a strong foundation for the convergence proof. The authors accurately position their work relative to previous research, citing relevant papers on MCTS, game theory, and related algorithms like UCT and double-oracle methods. The introduction effectively highlights the practical relevance of simultaneous-move games with examples like Goofspiel and general game-playing.  The extensive formal analysis and inclusion of the proof sketch (with a link to the full proof) are commendable. The empirical evaluation, while not exhaustive, provides a reasonable assessment of the proposed framework and comparison to existing methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Novelty:**  The convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a significant contribution.  Existing literature lacks such guarantees.
*   **Soundness:** The use of -Hannan consistency provides a strong theoretical basis for the proof. The inductive proof structure appears logically sound (based on the provided sketch).
*   **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are clear, and the algorithm descriptions are easy to follow.  The use of Algorithm 1 is helpful.
*   **Relevance:** Simultaneous-move games are an important class of games with practical applications, and this work advances the theoretical understanding of solving them with MCTS.
*   **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a thorough overview of relevant literature, including references to key papers in MCTS, game theory, and related areas.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Practical Significance of Approximation:** While the theoretical results are valuable, the convergence to a *subgame perfect approximate* Nash equilibrium (with an epsilon determined by the depth of the tree) may not be practically useful in games with very large depths. A discussion of the practical implications of this approximation bound would be beneficial.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Scope:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While it demonstrates convergence, it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms for specific games. The “worst-case” games tested appear to be randomly generated, and a comparison with more challenging game instances (e.g., those from game-playing competitions) would be valuable.
*   **Numerical Stability:** The note about rewriting the Exp3 update for numerical stability suggests a potential practical concern that could benefit from further discussion.
*    **Redundancy**: Some definitions and notations are repeated throughout the paper, making it a bit verbose. For example, the definition of a matrix game is reiterated several times.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound result addressing a significant gap in the literature on MCTS.
*   The convergence proof is a valuable contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The empirical evaluation provides reasonable support for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The practical implications of the approximation guarantee could be limited for games with large depths.
*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and doesn’t provide a comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art algorithms.
*    The paper's length and some redundancies could be reduced without sacrificing clarity.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened and the practical implications of the approximation bound deserve further discussion, the novelty and soundness of the work make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the practical relevance of the approximation bounds and a more comprehensive empirical evaluation would improve the paper significantly.
