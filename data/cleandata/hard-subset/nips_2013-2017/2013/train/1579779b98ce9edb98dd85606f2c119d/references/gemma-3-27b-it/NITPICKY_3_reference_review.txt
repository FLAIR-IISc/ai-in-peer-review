This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors demonstrate that an MCTS algorithm employing a -Hannan consistent action selection procedure converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The work is accompanied by empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3 as instantiations of the -Hannan consistent procedure. 

While the problem addressed – theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games – is undeniably important and largely unexplored, and the presented results are a valuable step towards filling this gap, I find several aspects of the paper concerning, ranging from minor technical details to broader issues regarding the clarity of the exposition and the presentation of empirical results. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis addressing the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous move games is a significant contribution. Prior work lacks such guarantees, making this a genuinely novel exploration.
*   **Formal Rigor:** The authors attempt a formal proof of convergence, which, while dense, is a commendable effort.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper correctly situates itself within the broader context of game theory, MCTS, and online learning, referencing relevant prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The notation throughout the paper is frequently inconsistent and often poorly defined. For example, the definition of `A = A1 × A2` is immediately followed by `A1(h) = {1 . . . mh}` and `A2(h) = {1 . . . nh}`, creating confusion regarding the relationship between `A` and `A1`, `A2`. Similarly, the frequent switching between using `u1` for player 1's utility and using `u1` as a variable representing a utility value within an equation is a recurring issue. This lack of clarity permeates several sections, hindering comprehension.
*   **Proof Omissions & Hand-Waving:** The authors state that proofs are included in the appendix and on a website, which is insufficient for a NIPS submission. Key steps within the main text are glossed over with references to these external resources. A NIPS paper should be self-contained as much as reasonably possible. Furthermore, the justification for certain simplifications, such as assuming a complete tree for the convergence analysis, feels incomplete. While the authors state this assumption is justified, the argument requires more nuance.
*   **Empirical Evaluation Shortcomings:** The empirical evaluation, while present, feels underdeveloped. The choice of “worst case” games is not clearly defined. The visualization of results (Figure 3) is not informative, lacking clear labels and scales. The statement that the experiments demonstrate a convergence rate "comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms" needs more precise quantification and comparison; a qualitative statement is insufficient. The explanation for why RMM converges slower than RM is not convincing.
*   **Minor Technical Errors:** The definition of -Nash equilibrium (Equation 1) is standard, but its immediate application in the context of subgame perfect equilibrium feels slightly disconnected. A clearer explanation of how the -bound propagates across subgames would be helpful.  The statement that most Hannan consistent algorithms guarantee exploration requires justification, as many don't naturally.
*   **Formatting Issues:** Numerous minor formatting issues are present, including inconsistent spacing, and the occasional use of LaTeX commands that don't render properly.

**Specific Concerns & Questions:**

*   In Section 4.2, the notation `ti` and `tj` are introduced without clear definition. Are these cumulative action counts?
*   The leap from Lemma 4.6 to Corollary 4.7 feels abrupt. The justification for multiplying the error bound by 4 lacks sufficient explanation.
*   In the conclusion, the authors state they "will focus our future research on providing finite time convergence bounds." This is a major limitation of the current work and should be more prominently discussed in the limitations section.
*   The claim that other algorithms converge to -Nash equilibrium "likely" is weak. The authors should either provide some evidence for this claim or refrain from making it.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a significant open problem in game theory and MCTS.
*   Presents a novel theoretical result.
*   Attempts a rigorous mathematical proof.

**Con:**

*   Numerous clarity and technical issues hinder comprehension.
*   Empirical evaluation is weak and lacks sufficient detail.
*   Proofs are relegated to external resources, making the paper less self-contained.
*   The overall presentation lacks polish and precision.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novelty and potential significance of the work, I lean towards **rejection**. The numerous issues regarding clarity, technical detail, and the presentation of empirical results significantly detract from the paper's overall quality. While the core idea is promising, the paper requires substantial revisions to meet the standards of NIPS. Addressing the notational inconsistencies, providing complete proofs within the main text or a well-integrated appendix, and significantly strengthening the empirical validation are essential for a successful resubmission.  The current version is too rough and imprecise to warrant acceptance.
