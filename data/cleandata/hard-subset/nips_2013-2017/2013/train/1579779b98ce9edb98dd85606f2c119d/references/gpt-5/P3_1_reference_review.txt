Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”) and analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in this setting. The authors present a general SM-MCTS template where, at each node (matrix game), the selection policy is any ε–Hannan consistent (HC) online algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3). The main theoretical claim is eventual convergence of SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE): if each node uses an ε-HC selector and mean-backups are used, then the average (or empirical) strategies converge almost surely to an O(approximation)-NE, with error growing with depth. The proof proceeds by: (i) convergence guarantees for ε-HC play in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded error; and (iii) an induction over tree depth to bound accumulated error, yielding subgame-perfectity. Empirically, the paper compares “sample-backup” vs “mean-backup” variants with Regret Matching and Exp3 on random and small worst-case games, showing convergence in both cases and slightly slower convergence with mean backups.

This fills a gap: while UCT is well understood in sequential perfect-information games, convergence theory for simultaneous-move EFGs is scarce, and prior empirical works (e.g., Tron/GGP) lacked guarantees; UCT can even fail to converge here (Shafiei et al.). The work connects MCTS to online learning (Hannan consistency) similarly to how CFR connects regret minimization to equilibrium computation in imperfect-information games.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: clean reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated (noisy) matrix games and an induction over depth. The “guaranteed exploration + ε-HC” template is natural and general.
- Novelty: to my knowledge, this is the first general convergence result for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games to subgame-perfect approximate NE.
- Subgame-perfectity: the induction ensures guarantees in every subgame, not just at the root.
- Empirical sanity checks: experiments corroborate convergence of the proposed template and quantify the effect of mean vs sample backups.
- Relates to well-established online learning theory (regret matching, Exp3) and to known pitfalls with UCB in simultaneous moves.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Constants and dependence on ε appear inconsistent. Lemma 4.6 correctly yields v ± 2ε bounds and Corollary 4.7 yields a (4ε + δ)-NE. However, Lemma 4.9 and Theorem 4.10 state bounds like v ± 2(c+1) and (4(c+1)+δ)-NE, seemingly independent of ε, which is dimensionally and conceptually odd: the approximation should degrade with ε (and with exploration γ via Lemma 4.4’s ε+γ). Likewise, Theorem 4.11 states a (D^2 + δ)-NE, with no ε (or γ) dependence; earlier discussion suggests errors accumulate with depth d and best-response gains add up, but independence from ε is implausible. I suspect missing ε factors (and possibly γ) in Lemma 4.9, Theorem 4.10 and Theorem 4.11. Please check and correct constants throughout; more principled big-O statements (e.g., O(D(ε+γ))) would help.
- Pessimistic error scaling: a D^2 dependence is very loose and may arise from compounding generous worst-case bounds. A refined analysis (e.g., controlling bias via mean backups and using concentration to bound propagated error) could plausibly yield O(D(ε+γ)) rather than O(D^2).
- Mean-backup requirement: the proof critically uses returning Xh/nh, which is nonstandard in MCTS practice and empirically slows convergence. It would be valuable to either prove convergence also for sample backups (e.g., via stochastic approximation arguments) or clearly delineate when the mean-backup is essential.
- Exploration handling: A* mixes in uniform exploration and does not update the base learner on exploration steps. While Lemma 4.4 argues ε→ε+γ and guarantees exploration, not updating can slow learning and may be unnecessary for HC in some algorithms; a discussion/justification would help.
- Experimental scope: experiments are on small random and tiny worst-case games with utilities in {0, 0.5, 1}. This is fine for a theory paper, but stronger evidence in larger, structured simultaneous-move domains (e.g., Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion variants) and comparisons to specialized alpha-beta pruning in simultaneous move games or to CFR variants would strengthen the paper.
- Clarity/typos: there are several notational slips (e.g., missing ε factors as above), and the statement of Algorithm 1 is terse. Some definitions (e.g., of RetVal variants) could be clearer. A small set of figures is referenced but not fully described here.

Quality
The high-level proof strategy is sound and aligns with established notions in online learning; the reduction to repeated games with bounded error is natural. However, the constants appear inconsistent/mistyped, and the D^2 bound is likely improvable. The empirical work is limited but consistent with claims.

Clarity
Generally well-written, with helpful motivation and background. Still, I recommend:
- Fix constants and explicitly track dependence on ε and γ throughout.
- Clarify the mean-backup requirement and its role in the induction.
- State precisely whether “empirical frequencies” vs “average strategies” are used in the final guarantee (you prove they coincide asymptotically, Lemma 4.5, but be explicit in the main theorem).
- Provide a short proof sketch in the main text for Theorem 4.11.

Originality
Genuinely new convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS with simultaneous moves, leveraging HC at nodes. Prior work showed failures of UCT and empirical successes, but no general convergence theory.

Significance
The result legitimizes a widely used class of methods and may influence practice in simultaneous-move domains. The current bounds are asymptotic and loose; still, this is a meaningful first step. Extensions to finite-sample rates and imperfect information would be impactful.

Questions and suggestions to authors
1) Please correct and unify the dependence on ε (and γ) in Lemma 4.9, Theorem 4.10, and Theorem 4.11. As written, these are likely typos.
2) Can you bound the propagated error more tightly to get O(D(ε+γ))? Where exactly does the D^2 arise?
3) Is mean-backup strictly necessary? Could you prove convergence with sample backups by appealing to the law of large numbers at each node and uniform exploration?
4) How sensitive are results to the rollout policy? You assume complete-tree expansion eventually; do particular rollout choices affect bounded-error constants c?
5) Could you compare against simultaneous alpha-beta and CFR variants on a modest benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel depth 3–4)?

Pros
- First rigorous convergence analysis for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games
- General template using any ε-HC selector with exploration
- Subgame-perfect convergence guarantee
- Clear connection to online learning; simple algorithms (RM/Exp3) instantiate the template

Cons
- Apparent mistakes/omissions in constants; very loose D^2 bound
- Requires mean backups; weaker empirical performance for that variant
- Limited experimental scope
- Asymptotic only; no finite-sample rates

Overall, I find the conceptual contribution solid and relevant. Fixing the constants, clarifying the role of mean backups, and expanding experiments would substantially strengthen the paper.
