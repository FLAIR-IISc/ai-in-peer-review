- Addresses convergence of MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games by presenting a general MCTS template using regret-minimizing selection.
- Proves that using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function yields convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.
- Builds on online learning and regret minimization (Hannan consistency, regret matching, Exp3) and adapts these to MCTS for simultaneous-move games.
- Empirically validates theoretical claims using two ε-Hannan consistent procedures on randomly generated and worst-case small games.
- Provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, extending theory beyond sequential-game UCT results.
- Theoretical framework is technically sound with well-structured proofs (lemmas 4.4–4.10) that progressively establish convergence.
- The analysis is complete for the stated scope and carefully acknowledges its limitations (eventual convergence focus, not finite-time).
- Empirical results show the mean propagation variant (RMM) converges to approximate Nash equilibria, consistent with theory.
- Assumes the complete game tree will eventually be built, which is unrealistic under typical computational constraints.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to very small games (depth 2, branching factor 2), limiting evidence of practical scalability.
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds, reducing direct practical utility for time-limited applications.
- Restricts theoretical results to zero-sum perfect-information games, limiting applicability to broader game classes (e.g., imperfect information).
- Paper is well-organized with clear definitions (ε-Hannan consistency, matrix games, ε-Nash) and useful pseudocode and figures.
- Transition from matrix games to extensive-form games and some appendix proofs could be explained more thoroughly for readers less familiar with game theory.
- Needs clearer justification for the necessity of mean propagation (RMM) for theoretical guarantees.
- Novelty is solid in applying Hannan consistency to simultaneous-move MCTS, though technique builds on existing online learning work (somewhat incremental).
- Does not sufficiently explore extensions to practical MCTS variants like UCT that are widely used but not covered by the proof.
- Significant theoretical contribution with practical implications for domains using MCTS (e.g., security, card games), offering a foundation for future work.
- Overall recommendation: accept — strong theoretical contribution with sound analysis and supporting experiments despite scope and practical limitations.
- Suggested improvements: evaluate larger/deeper games, discuss extension to practical MCTS variants (UCT), analyze computational cost of mean propagation, and better highlight previous limitations in simultaneous-move MCTS in the introduction.
