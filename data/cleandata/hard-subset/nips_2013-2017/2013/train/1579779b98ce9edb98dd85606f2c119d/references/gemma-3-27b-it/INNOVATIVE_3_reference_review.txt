This paper tackles a challenging problem: providing theoretical guarantees for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games. The authors establish a convergence result for a general template of MCTS algorithms, relying on -Hannan consistency of the action selection procedure. This is a significant contribution, as theoretical understanding of MCTS outside of perfect-information sequential games is lacking. The connection to regret minimization is clever and allows leveraging existing theory. The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a genuinely novel contribution. Existing work often relies on empirical performance or specific algorithms without convergence guarantees.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof of convergence, leveraging -Hannan consistency, is well-structured and provides a solid foundation for understanding the algorithm's behavior.
*   **Generality:** The proposed MCTS template is flexible and can accommodate different regret-minimizing procedures, offering a broad applicability.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The definitions and background material are presented clearly.
*   **Relevance:** Simultaneous-move games appear in diverse applications (Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion, GGP), making the theoretical results broadly relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical analysis establishes convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, but provides no guarantees on the *rate* of convergence. The dependence on *D* (depth) in the approximation bound is concerning and suggests potential scalability issues. While empirical results are presented, a more thorough analysis of convergence speed, perhaps scaling with game size, would be valuable.
*   **Practicality of -Hannan Consistency:** While theoretically sound, enforcing strict -Hannan consistency can be computationally expensive or require careful tuning of exploration parameters. The paper acknowledges this in the discussion of exploration and the modification to algorithms lacking guaranteed exploration.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation, while demonstrating reasonable performance, is relatively limited in scope. Exploring a wider range of game types and sizes, and comparing against more sophisticated baselines would strengthen the empirical validation. The chosen “worst case” games aren’t fully justified.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of perfect information, while stated, limits the immediate applicability of the results to more complex, real-world scenarios.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant theoretical result addressing a long-standing open problem in the field of game theory and MCTS.
*   The theoretical framework is well-defined and provides a valuable foundation for future research.
*   The empirical results, though limited, are encouraging and support the theoretical findings.
*   The generality of the proposed MCTS template allows for flexibility in implementation and adaptation to different games.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of convergence rate guarantees and the potential scalability issues associated with the approximation bound raise concerns about the practical applicability of the results.
*   The empirical evaluation is relatively limited and doesn’t fully demonstrate the algorithm’s performance in challenging scenarios.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While the convergence rate is a concern, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work are compelling. The authors have identified a fundamental gap in our understanding of MCTS and provided a valuable step towards closing it. The paper is perhaps not *perfect* – further work could improve the empirical evaluation and explore rate of convergence – but the core contribution is strong enough to warrant acceptance, particularly given NIPS’s preference for bold, novel ideas over incremental improvements. I would recommend acceptance, with encouragement to the authors to address the convergence rate issue in future work.



**Rating:** Accept (weakly, pending author response to potential rate of convergence concerns)
