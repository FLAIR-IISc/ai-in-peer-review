

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

## Summary
This paper addresses the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, a significant gap in the literature. The authors propose a general template for MCTS using -Hannan consistent regret minimization procedures, proving convergence to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The work builds on regret matching and Exp3 algorithms, extending them to the simultaneous move game setting. While the problem is well-motivated and the theoretical contribution is potentially valuable, the paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and unclear explanations that undermine the main claims.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a genuine gap in the literature regarding convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal analysis, while problematic, represents a meaningful step toward theoretical understanding of MCTS in this domain. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical claims, and the paper is generally well-structured with clear definitions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Notational Inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for key concepts. For example, it defines "v" as the value of the game in equation (2) but later uses "v" in regret analysis without clarification. The distinction between "empirical frequencies" and "average strategies" (Lemma 4.5) is muddled, with the paper claiming "the empirical frequencies and average strategies will almost surely be equal" when it should state that their difference converges to zero.

2. **Unjustified Simplifying Assumption**: In Section 4, the authors claim to "disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree" and assert that "this will eventually happen with probability 1." This is a critical assumption that lacks justification. The paper does not prove that the tree will be completed with probability 1, nor does it explain why the statistics collected during tree building cannot prevent eventual convergence.

3. **Inconsistent Treatment of Mean Propagation**: The paper states that the formal analysis "requires the algorithms to return the mean of all the previous samples instead of the value of the current sample" (Section 5.1), but this requirement is not clearly derived from the theoretical analysis. The empirical results in Section 5.1 show RMM (returning mean) converges slightly slower than RM (returning current sample), contradicting the paper's claim that the mean propagation is necessary for convergence.

4. **Reference to Non-Existent Supplementary Material**: The paper states "any proofs that are omitted in the paper are included in the appendix available in the supplementary material and on http://arxiv.org (arXiv:1310.8613)." However, arXiv:1310.8613 is a real paper about a different topic (not about MCTS in simultaneous move games), creating confusion and raising reproducibility concerns.

5. **Mathematical Errors in Theoretical Claims**: The constant "4" in Corollary 4.7 is claimed to result from "going from a pair of strategies with best responses within 2 of the game value... which multiplies the distance by two." This reasoning is mathematically questionable. The paper should provide a clear derivation of the constant.

6. **Ambiguous Definitions**: The paper claims "the empirical frequencies and average strategies form (4 + δ)-equilibrium" but does not define what "form" means in this context. The paper also fails to clarify the relationship between the matrix game value v and the game value vh0 in the extensive form game.

7. **Inadequate Experimental Justification**: The paper claims "the convergence rate of all the variants is similar" (Section 5.2) without providing statistical evidence or confidence intervals to support this claim. The empirical results lack sufficient detail to draw meaningful conclusions.

## Recommendation

The paper addresses an important problem with potential significance for the game theory and AI communities. However, the theoretical claims are undermined by multiple technical inconsistencies, unjustified assumptions, and mathematical errors that prevent the main convergence result from being rigorously established. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to address these fundamental issues.

While the paper's core idea is promising, the technical flaws are substantial enough to prevent acceptance at NIPS. The authors should either provide rigorous proofs for the omitted claims, correct the notational inconsistencies, or significantly revise the theoretical analysis to align with the empirical results.

The paper would be better suited for a venue with more tolerance for preliminary theoretical results, but it does not meet NIPS's standards for technical rigor and clarity. I recommend rejection due to these significant technical shortcomings.
