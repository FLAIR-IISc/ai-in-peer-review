

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for a specific class of games: two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function, it converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by connecting established results in online learning (Hannan consistency) with MCTS convergence guarantees in a game-theoretic setting. The authors empirically validate their claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection strategies, showing that their MCTS variant with mean propagation (RMM) converges to ε-Nash equilibrium.

The paper builds on foundational work in game theory (backward induction, Hannan consistency) and MCTS literature, but correctly identifies the lack of convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which has been a practical gap despite the algorithm's widespread use in applications like Goofspiel and general game-playing.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is novel and well-justified.

2. **Rigorous Proof**: The proof structure is elegant, proceeding by induction on game depth and leveraging established results from online learning. The formal analysis in Section 4 is thorough and addresses the key challenge of handling errors in the repeated matrix games.

3. **Clear Definitions**: The paper provides precise definitions of the game model, MCTS variants, and key concepts (Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration), making it accessible to readers familiar with game theory and online learning.

4. **Practical Insight**: The paper identifies a crucial implementation detail that has been overlooked in previous MCTS work: using the mean of previous samples (RMM) rather than the current sample value (RM) is necessary for the theoretical guarantees. This is an important practical insight.

5. **Appropriate Experimental Design**: The empirical evaluation tests the theoretical claim that RMM converges to ε-Nash equilibrium, and the experiments are well-designed to compare with the standard RM approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Application**: The paper focuses exclusively on zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, which is a relatively narrow class of games. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but it significantly reduces the broader applicability of their results. Most real-world applications of MCTS involve imperfect information (e.g., poker), which is not addressed.

2. **Lack of Finite-Time Bounds**: While the paper establishes asymptotic convergence, it does not provide any finite-time convergence bounds. This is a significant limitation for practical applications, as practitioners care about how quickly the algorithm converges to an ε-equilibrium.

3. **Narrow Empirical Evaluation**: The empirical analysis is limited to small games (depth 2, branching factor 2) and random utilities. The paper doesn't test on larger, more challenging games or compare with other state-of-the-art algorithms for simultaneous move games (e.g., double-oracle methods [9] or CFR variants).

4. **Missing Comparison with Related Algorithms**: The paper doesn't compare with other MCTS variants that have been proposed for simultaneous move games, such as those discussed in [17] (Shafiei et al. on UCT). A comparison would strengthen the claims about the relative performance of the proposed approach.

5. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: While the paper acknowledges the limited scope, it doesn't thoroughly discuss the practical limitations of the approach, such as the impact of the exploration parameter γ on convergence rate or how the approach scales to larger games.

## Critical Analysis

### Methodology
The theoretical framework is well-constructed and builds on established results in online learning and game theory. The key insight that Hannan consistency implies convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium is well-supported. However, the assumption that the complete tree is built before convergence (Section 4) is a simplification that might not hold in practice. In reality, the tree grows incrementally, and the analysis should address how the incremental growth affects the convergence guarantees.

The paper correctly identifies that using the mean of previous samples (RMM) rather than the current sample value (RM) is necessary for the theoretical guarantees. This is a valuable contribution that addresses a practical issue in MCTS implementations.

### Related Work
The related work section is adequate but could be strengthened. The paper cites the key papers on MCTS and game theory, but doesn't discuss how their results relate to recent advances in solving extensive-form games with imperfect information, which is a very active area. For example, the paper doesn't discuss how their results might connect to CFR (Counterfactual Regret Minimization) variants that have been successful in imperfect information games like poker.

### Empirical Analysis
The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and doesn't fully support the paper's claims about practical relevance. The experiments on worst-case games (Figure 3) are valuable, but the paper doesn't provide results on larger, more realistic games or compare with other state-of-the-art algorithms. The paper could have included a comparison with the double-oracle method [9] or other recent approaches to simultaneous move games.

### Significance
The significance of the paper is somewhat limited by its narrow scope. While the theoretical contribution is important for understanding MCTS in this specific class of games, it doesn't directly address the more challenging and practically relevant problem of imperfect information games. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it would be stronger if the authors could discuss potential extensions to imperfect information games.

## Specific Comments

1. **Section 4.4 (Main Theorem)**: The proof sketch is clear, but the authors should explicitly state the relationship between the exploration parameter γ and the convergence rate. How does γ affect the bound in Theorem 4.11? This would be valuable for practitioners.

2. **Section 5.2 (Empirical Convergence Rate)**: The paper states that "the games that are most difficult for one method are difficult also for the other," but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. A more detailed analysis of why certain games are harder would strengthen the empirical section.

3. **Section 6 (Conclusion)**: The paper mentions "future research on providing finite time convergence bounds," which would be a significant improvement. However, the authors should also discuss the potential extension to imperfect information games, even if it's speculative.

4. **Figures**: Figure 2 could be improved by including error bars to show the variance across the 100 random games. This would provide a clearer picture of the reliability of the results.

5. **Comparison with UCT**: The paper mentions that UCT does not converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games [17], but doesn't compare the convergence rate of the proposed approach with UCT on the same games. A brief comparison would help contextualize the contribution.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The paper is well-written, the proofs are rigorous, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. However, the scope is limited to a specific class of games (perfect information, simultaneous move, zero-sum), which reduces the broader significance of the work.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it included:
1. A comparison with other MCTS variants for simultaneous move games
2. Results on larger, more challenging games
3. Discussion of potential extensions to imperfect information games
4. Finite-time convergence bounds

## Recommendation

**Accept with Minor Revisions**

The paper is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. The main weaknesses (limited scope, lack of finite-time bounds, narrow empirical evaluation) can be addressed with minor revisions. The authors should expand their empirical evaluation to include larger games and comparisons with other algorithms, and provide more discussion about the limitations and potential extensions of their work.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear contribution to the field. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
