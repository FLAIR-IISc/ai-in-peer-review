This paper presents a convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a notable gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS. The authors establish convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under the assumption of using a -Hannan consistent action selection procedure. They instantiate this with regret matching and Exp3 and provide empirical validation demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to existing methods.

The primary strength of this work lies in its attempt to address a foundational theoretical question regarding MCTS in a non-trivial game setting. The extension of MCTS analysis beyond two-player perfect information sequential games is a valuable contribution. The proof framework, leveraging -Hannan consistency, is a reasonable approach and provides a solid theoretical underpinning. The empirical validation, though not extensive, supports the theoretical findings.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while correct, feels somewhat conservative. The authors acknowledge that existing algorithms likely also converge, but frame the work as providing a general template rather than directly analyzing those algorithms. This feels like a missed opportunity. The convergence guarantee itself is to an *approximate* Nash equilibrium, with the approximation factor tied to the depth of the game. While mathematically sound, this diminishes the practical impact of the result, particularly for deep game trees. 

The empirical evaluation is limited in scope. The comparison to existing MCTS algorithms is primarily based on convergence *speed*, rather than final equilibrium quality. The choice of randomly generated and worst-case games, while helpful, doesn't necessarily reflect real-world applications where domain-specific knowledge is available. The claim that the empirical speed of convergence is "comparable" requires more rigorous statistical support. Furthermore, the empirical exploration of different exploration parameters (γ) feels superficial. A more thorough exploration of the impact of γ on convergence rate and equilibrium quality would strengthen the findings. The section discussing the influence of propagating the mean versus the current sample value is particularly weak, showing only marginal differences.

The writing is generally clear, but at times overly dense and relies heavily on mathematical notation without sufficient explanation for a broader audience. While the definitions are comprehensive, they could be presented in a more accessible manner. The connection between the theoretical analysis and the practical implications is not always clear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant open problem in the MCTS literature.
*   Provides a rigorous theoretical analysis guaranteeing convergence to an approximate NE.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The framework is generalizable to various action selection procedures.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The convergence guarantee is to an approximate NE with a depth-dependent error bound.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited in scope and lacks rigorous statistical analysis.
*   The paper doesn't analyze or improve upon existing MCTS algorithms directly, instead presenting a more general framework.
*   The writing style is occasionally dense and difficult to follow.

**Overall Assessment:**

I view this paper as a solid, but not groundbreaking, contribution. While the theoretical results are valuable, the practical impact is limited by the approximate convergence guarantee and the lack of a more comprehensive empirical evaluation. The paper represents incremental progress, rather than a significant leap forward. I lean towards **reject** with encouragement to strengthen the empirical evaluation and explore the analysis of existing MCTS algorithms within their framework. A major revision addressing these concerns is needed to warrant acceptance. The paper feels somewhat overly cautious and doesn't fully capitalize on the potential of its theoretical results. The emphasis on theoretical soundness is admirable, but it comes at the expense of practical relevance and thorough empirical validation.
