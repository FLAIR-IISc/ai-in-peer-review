**Summary of the paper:**
This paper addresses a notable theoretical gap in the literature: the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous move games. The authors propose a general template for MCTS algorithms for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The core idea is to use a regret-minimizing procedure, such as Regret Matching or Exp3, as the in-tree selection strategy. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection strategy is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the strategies derived from the MCTS visit counts will converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree, modeling decision-making at each node as a repeated matrix game where the payoffs from child nodes are noisy estimates. The authors empirically evaluate their proposed algorithms (specifically, variants using Regret Matching and Exp3) on randomly generated and empirically-derived worst-case games, comparing the convergence speed when propagating the current sample value versus the running mean value from simulations.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and, to my knowledge, open theoretical question. While MCTS is widely used in practice, its theoretical guarantees are largely confined to sequential, perfect-information games. Providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in the context of simultaneous move games is a significant contribution that strengthens the theoretical foundations of the algorithm and justifies its use in this game class.
2.  **Novel Theoretical Framework:** The approach of framing the problem in terms of ε-Hannan consistency and analyzing the system as a hierarchy of repeated games with bounded error is elegant and insightful. This framework provides a clear pathway for the proof and is general enough to accommodate any selection algorithm that satisfies the ε-Hannan consistency property.
3.  **Clarity of High-Level Idea:** The main argument—that regret minimizers at the leaves will converge, providing approximately correct values to parent nodes, which in turn allows regret minimizers at those nodes to converge—is intuitive and well-communicated.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**
Despite the significance of the contribution, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor errors, inaccuracies, and ambiguities that collectively detract from its quality and trustworthiness. A manuscript for a top-tier venue like NIPS should exhibit a higher level of precision and polish.

1.  **Technical and Definitional Imprecision:**
    *   **Zero-Sum vs. Constant-Sum:** The game is defined with utilities `u2(z) = 1 - u1(z)`, which is a constant-sum game, not a zero-sum game (`u2(z) = -u1(z)`). While strategically equivalent, this is a fundamental definitional error. It leads to a direct contradiction in Section 3, where it is stated that "Player 2 does the same except using negated utilities." Does player 2 use `-(1-u1)` or `-u1`? The distinction matters for the learning dynamics.
    *   **Path Probability:** The definition of path probability `πσ(z) = π1(z)π2(z)` is highly ambiguous and likely incorrect for an extensive-form game. The probability of reaching a terminal node is the product of the strategy probabilities for *both* players for each action taken along the unique path to that node. The notation used is underspecified and non-standard.
    *   **Exploitability Metric:** The empirical evaluation uses `vh0 − u(σ̂1, br)` as the measure of exploitability. This is the exploitability of player 1's strategy only. The standard definition of exploitability for a strategy profile involves summing the incentives to deviate for *both* players. This should be clarified and justified, or the standard metric should be used.

2.  **Algorithmic Ambiguity:**
    *   **Regret Matching Update:** The regret update rule presented in Section 3.1 is non-standard and poorly explained. The definition of `x(h, i, j)` and its use in the update `rh[i′]← rh[i′] + (x(h, i′, j)− u1)` is confusing. Standard regret matching computes regret for an action `i'` relative to the action actually taken, `i1`, (i.e., `payoff(i') - payoff(i1)`). The formulation here seems to use the value from the subgame `u1` as a baseline for all actions, and uses historical cumulative rewards `Xhij` for unplayed actions. This is a critical detail that is either a novel and unjustified variant or an error.
    *   **Expansion Step:** In Algorithm 1, Line 3, the mechanism to "Choose one of the previously unselected (i, j)" is left undefined. Is it random, sequential, or based on some other heuristic? This detail can affect the search behavior.

3.  **Theoretical Analysis Concerns:**
    *   **Simplifying Assumption:** The analysis begins by assuming the game tree is fully built, arguing that this will happen eventually. This is a very strong simplification that sidesteps the entire exploration/exploitation dynamic during the tree-building phase. The statistics gathered during this transient phase could bias the search in ways that are not accounted for in the asymptotic analysis.
    *   **Clarity of Bounds:** The final convergence bound in Theorem 4.11 includes a `D^2` term. The brief justification for this quadratic dependency on depth D is plausible but could be explained more clearly. The jump from Lemma 4.6 (`v + 2ε`) to Corollary 4.7 (`4ε + δ`) is also explained in a hand-wavy manner.

4.  **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** The paper is replete with minor but distracting errors.
    *   **Notation:** The symbol `T` is overloaded to mean both the transition function (Sec 2) and the MCTS tree (Sec 3). Action sets are defined as `{1...mh}` instead of `{1...m_h}`. The arrow `7→` is used instead of `→`. The interval notation `〈0, 1〉` is non-standard. The hyphen in "ε-Nash equilibrium" is unconventional.
    *   **Typos:** There are numerous spelling errors, including "arbitrarly" (twice), `xh,i1` instead of `xh,i`, "oth." instead of "otherwise", "Eolutionary", and "Proceeding".
    *   **References:** The reference list is poorly and inconsistently formatted, with missing information (e.g., page numbers, venues) and inconsistent styles.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a significant theoretical result.
*   The high-level theoretical approach is sound, novel, and generalizable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The paper is undermined by a large number of minor errors, from typos to notational inconsistencies.
*   There are significant definitional inaccuracies (zero-sum vs. constant-sum) and ambiguities in core algorithmic details (Regret Matching update).
*   The theoretical analysis relies on a strong simplifying assumption (pre-built tree) that ignores the crucial transient phase of MCTS.
*   The overall lack of polish and precision is not up to the standard expected for NIPS.

**Recommendation:**
While the core idea is strong and the main result is significant, the execution is unfortunately sloppy. The density of minor errors, combined with more serious ambiguities in definitions and algorithms, makes the paper difficult to endorse in its current form. The work shows great promise, but it requires a thorough round of revision to correct the inaccuracies, clarify the ambiguities, and polish the presentation. Therefore, I recommend **rejection** for this submission, but I would strongly encourage the authors to revise and resubmit their work to a future conference.
