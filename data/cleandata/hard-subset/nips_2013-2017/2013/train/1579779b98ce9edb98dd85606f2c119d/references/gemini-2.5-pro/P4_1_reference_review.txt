This paper addresses the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants have been successfully applied to such games in practice, a formal guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been a notable open problem. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection policy is governed by a regret-minimizing procedure.

The main contribution is a formal proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures guaranteed exploration (i.e., every joint action is selected infinitely often), the average strategies produced by SM-MCTS converge almost surely to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is built via induction on the depth of the game tree. It first establishes convergence for single-stage matrix games using known results from online learning. It then extends this to matrix games where payoffs are noisy estimates with a bounded error. This extension is key, as it models the situation at an internal node of the game tree, where the payoffs for actions are the estimated values of the resulting subgames. By showing that the error in these value estimates remains bounded, the authors inductively prove convergence for the entire extensive-form game. The paper instantiates this framework with two well-known regret minimizers, Regret Matching and Exp3, and provides an empirical analysis comparing the convergence speed of different algorithm variants on random and worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This is a significant and novel contribution that fills a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS. It places the heuristic use of MCTS in this game class on solid theoretical footing, which is of high value to both the game theory and AI communities. The result is general, applying to any MCTS variant that employs an ε-Hannan consistent selection rule.
*   **Technical Quality:** The formal analysis presented in Section 4 is the core strength of the paper. The proof strategy, which models the problem as a hierarchy of repeated matrix games with propagating bounded errors, is both elegant and effective. The argument is built carefully, from foundational lemmas on ε-Hannan consistency in matrix games (Lemma 4.6) to the main inductive proof for deep extensive-form games (Theorem 4.11). The claims are well-supported by this theoretical analysis.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It begins with a clear set of definitions and background, presents the algorithmic framework logically, and then systematically develops the theoretical argument step-by-step. The distinction between propagating the current sample value versus the mean value is explained clearly, as is its relevance to the formal proof. The empirical section is also clear in its objectives and presentation of results.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experimental section provides valuable insights that complement the theory. The direct comparison between propagating the mean value (required for the proof) and the single sample value (common in practice) is particularly useful. The finding that the single-sample variant converges slightly faster, despite not being covered by the proof, is an interesting result that validates current practice and points to important directions for future theoretical work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Simplifying Assumption in Analysis:** The formal proof operates under the simplification that the game tree is fully constructed before the analysis of convergence begins. The authors argue this is acceptable for an "almost surely" result because guaranteed exploration ensures all nodes are eventually created and visited infinitely often. However, this idealization sidesteps the complex interplay between tree growth and value estimation that characterizes the early phases of MCTS. While this does not invalidate the result, a discussion of the potential impact of this assumption would strengthen the paper.
*   **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The proof necessitates propagating the mean of rewards from sub-simulations (the "M" variants). The experiments, however, show that propagating the single, most recent sample reward (the standard variants) performs slightly better empirically. This creates a small disconnect between the proven algorithm and the one that appears preferable in practice. While the authors correctly identify this and suggest their analysis could be extended, the theory for the better-performing variant remains an open question.
*   **Convergence Bound:** The final approximation quality of the equilibrium depends on D^2, where D is the maximum depth of the game. For games with significant depth, this bound could be quite loose. It is unclear from the text whether this quadratic dependency is an artifact of the proof technique (summing errors at each level) or a fundamental property of the problem. A brief discussion on the potential tightness of this bound would be a useful addition.

In summary, the paper presents a foundational theoretical result on the convergence of MCTS for an important class of games. The strengths—originality, technical soundness, and significance—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily limitations of the current analysis that open clear avenues for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Provides the first, much-needed convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
2.  The theoretical contribution is significant, novel, and technically sound.
3.  The proposed framework is general and can incorporate a wide class of regret-minimizing algorithms.
4.  The paper is clearly written and well-structured, with empirical results that provide valuable context for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The theoretical analysis relies on a simplifying assumption that the game tree is pre-existing, rather than growing dynamically.
2.  The algorithm variant covered by the proof is shown to be slightly slower empirically than a standard variant for which a proof is not provided.
