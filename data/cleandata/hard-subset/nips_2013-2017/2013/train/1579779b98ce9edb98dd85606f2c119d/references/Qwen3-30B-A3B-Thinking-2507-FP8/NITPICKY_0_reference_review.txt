

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper proposes a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template using regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games and prove convergence to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium when using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions. They empirically validate their approach using regret matching and Exp3.

## Quality

The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine the formal claims. In Definition 4.2, the authors state "limt→∞ tij =∞ holds almost surely ∀(i, j) ∈ A1 ×A2" without defining tij (it should be the count of joint action (i,j) selections). This omission creates ambiguity in the proof.

In Section 3.1, the regret matching strategy definition contains a notational inconsistency: "σs(h, a) = r+h[a]/R+sum if R+sum > 0 otherwise 1/|A1(h)|". The "otherwise" clause should specify "if R+sum = 0" rather than "otherwise" to avoid confusion with negative regret sums.

The paper claims that "the statistics collected during the tree building phase cannot prevent the eventual convergence" (Section 4), but provides no justification for this critical claim. This is a significant gap in the proof.

Theorem 4.11 states convergence to a "D² + δ"-Nash equilibrium, but fails to explain why the bound is quadratic in depth rather than linear. This appears to be a potential error in the analysis.

In Section 5.1, the paper defines exploitability as "vh0 − u(σ̂1, br)", but this is not standard. Exploitability should be defined as maxσ' u(σ', br(σ')) - u(σ), where br(σ) is the best response to σ. The paper's definition misses the maximization over σ'.

## Clarity

The paper suffers from inconsistent notation throughout. For example:
- σs(h, a) vs. σs(h, a) vs. σs(h, a)
- M for both the game matrix and the game with errors (M(t))
- "subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium" vs. "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium"

The paper refers to "the game tree" without providing a clear definition of what constitutes a "game tree" in this context, which is problematic for a paper about extensive-form games.

In Section 4.2, Lemma 4.5 states "lim sup t→∞maxi∈A1 |σ̂1(t, i)− σ̄1(t, i)| = 0" without defining σ̂1(t, i) or σ̄1(t, i). These terms should be defined earlier in the paper.

The reference to "arXiv:1310.8613" appears to be outdated (2013) for a paper presumably submitted in a recent NIPS conference, suggesting possible confusion in the supplementary material.

## Originality

The paper claims to be the first formal analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games, but this is questionable. The cited work by Lanctot et al. (2013) on "Monte Carlo tree search in simultaneous move games with applications to Goofspiel" appears to be directly related and is not sufficiently distinguished from the current work. The paper should provide a clearer comparison with this prior work.

The paper cites Shafiei et al. [17] who showed UCB doesn't converge to NE, but doesn't adequately address how their approach differs from other MCTS variants that have been used in practice for these games.

## Significance

The empirical evaluation is limited to randomly generated games and a few worst-case examples. The paper doesn't provide a comparison with other MCTS algorithms that have been used in practice for these games, making it difficult to assess the practical significance of their approach.

The paper claims "empirically evaluate this claim" but doesn't demonstrate that their algorithms converge faster than existing methods, only that they converge. The empirical results show that RMM converges slightly slower than RM variants (Section 5.1), which undermines the practical value of their theoretical contribution.

## Minor Issues

- In Section 3.2, "wh,i = xh,i1" appears to contain a typo (should be "wh,i = xh,i").
- The reference to [17] is incomplete (missing year, page numbers).
- The paper uses inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "u1(σ1, σ2) = σ1Mσ2" vs "u1(br, σ2)− u1(σ1, σ2) ≤ ").
- The algorithm is labeled as "SM-MCTS(node h)" but the description refers to "Algorithm 1: Simultaneous Move Monte Carlo Tree Search", creating confusion.

## Conclusion

While the paper addresses an interesting problem, the technical inconsistencies, notational ambiguities, and insufficient empirical evaluation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The formal claims are undermined by several critical gaps in the analysis, particularly the unexplained D² bound and the unproven claim about tree building phase statistics. The empirical results also fail to demonstrate a clear advantage of the proposed approach over existing methods.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues, particularly by clarifying the proof, providing proper definitions for all terms, and conducting a more comprehensive empirical comparison with existing MCTS variants for simultaneous move games. As it stands, the paper does not make a sufficiently strong contribution to the field.
