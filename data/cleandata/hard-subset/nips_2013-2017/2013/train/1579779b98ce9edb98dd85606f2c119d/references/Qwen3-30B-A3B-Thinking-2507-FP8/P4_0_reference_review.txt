

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous move games. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games, and provide formal convergence guarantees to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibria. Previous work on MCTS has primarily focused on sequential perfect-information games (e.g., [14]), while convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games were lacking. The authors build on the theory of Hannan consistency from online learning [15, 16] and extend it to multi-stage game settings.

The paper's main contribution is a formal proof that MCTS algorithms using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions converge to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibria in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The proof proceeds by first establishing convergence properties in repeated matrix games, then extending these results to multi-stage games through induction. The authors provide two specific instantiations of their template: one using regret matching [15] and another using Exp3 [16], both of which are known to be Hannan consistent.

**Strengths:**

1. **Technical soundness:** The formal analysis is rigorous, with well-structured proofs and careful handling of the challenges in extending Hannan consistency to multi-stage settings. The paper properly addresses the non-trivial aspects of simultaneous move games, where standard MCTS approaches fail to converge (as noted by Shafiei et al. [17]).

2. **Empirical validation:** The paper includes thorough empirical evaluation showing that the algorithms converge to approximate Nash equilibria at rates comparable to existing methods. The experiments on worst-case games provide convincing evidence of the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized with clear section headings, consistent notation, and sufficient detail for experts to follow the arguments. The figures effectively illustrate the empirical results, particularly in showing the convergence behavior of different algorithms.

4. **Novelty:** This is the first paper to provide formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a significant gap in the literature. The general template approach is elegant and could inspire future work on MCTS variants.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of finite-time convergence bounds:** The formal analysis focuses on asymptotic convergence (as iterations approach infinity), but does not provide finite-time convergence bounds. While the empirical evaluation addresses this partially, the paper could have discussed potential approaches for establishing such bounds.

2. **Mean propagation requirement:** The paper shows that propagating mean values (rather than current samples) is necessary for the formal analysis, which differs from some previous MCTS implementations. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that this approach converges slightly slower, but the paper does not provide sufficient theoretical explanation for this difference.

3. **Conjecture about other algorithms:** The authors conjecture that many existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games also converge to ε-Nash equilibria, but do not provide specific evidence or a framework for extending their proof to these cases.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Offers a general template that can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures
- Combines theoretical rigor with empirical validation
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature on MCTS applications

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution
- Does not fully address how to extend the theoretical results to more complex game settings (e.g., with imperfect information)

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal analysis is sound, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the paper clearly articulates the novelty of their approach. The work is clearly within NIPS scope, as it bridges algorithmic game theory and reinforcement learning. The paper meets all four criteria:

- **Quality:** The technical content is rigorous, with well-supported theoretical claims and empirical validation.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized, with clear notation and sufficient detail for experts.
- **Originality:** This is the first paper to establish convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
- **Significance:** The results provide a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in a class of games with practical applications in security domains and general game playing.

The paper's contribution is important because it provides a theoretical basis for using MCTS in simultaneous move games, which was previously only studied empirically. This has implications for practitioners who rely on MCTS for real-world applications. The work advances the state of the art in algorithmic game theory and MCTS, and the results are likely to be built upon by future research. I recommend acceptance to NIPS.
