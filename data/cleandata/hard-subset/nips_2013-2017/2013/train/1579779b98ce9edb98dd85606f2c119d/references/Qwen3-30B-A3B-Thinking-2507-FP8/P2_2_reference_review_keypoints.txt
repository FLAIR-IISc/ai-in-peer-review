- Paper proves convergence of MCTS in two-player zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions.  
- Introduces a general MCTS template instantiable with any regret-minimizing procedure and shows convergence to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibria.  
- Theoretical contribution: first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.  
- Rigorous formal analysis: builds proofs from matrix games to extensive-form games, handling stochastic selection errors and exploration bias.  
- Clear theoretical framework: defines ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration and proves their properties.  
- Empirical validation: experiments with regret matching and Exp3 show convergence rates comparable to existing MCTS variants.  
- Good literature contextualization: properly cites prior work on MCTS, regret minimization, and game theory.  
- Limited experimental comparison: lacks broad benchmarking against other practical MCTS algorithms for simultaneous-move games.  
- No finite-time convergence bounds: analysis shows eventual convergence but not how many iterations are needed for a given ε.  
- Narrow scope: restricted to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, excluding imperfect-information and more complex settings.  
- Minor notational ambiguity regarding the return value definition (RetVal) that could be clarified.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound, comprehensive, and well-structured theoretical analysis supported by experiments.  
- Clarity assessment: generally well-written and organized, though differences between RMM and RM could be explained more explicitly.  
- Originality: highly original—fills a notable gap by applying regret-minimizing procedures to MCTS in simultaneous-move games.  
- Significance: important theoretical advance with practical relevance for game AI and algorithmic game theory.  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills literature gap, provides a general ε-Hannan-consistent framework, rigorous analysis, supportive experiments, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks finite-time bounds, limited experimental breadth, and restricted game scope.  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept (Final Rating: 7) — significant and well-executed theoretical contribution.
