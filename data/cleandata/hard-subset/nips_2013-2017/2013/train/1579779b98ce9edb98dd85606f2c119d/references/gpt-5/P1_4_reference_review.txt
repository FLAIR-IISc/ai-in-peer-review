Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). The authors present a template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) whose selection at each node can be any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) bandit algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching or Exp3). A key design choice for the analysis is to back up mean returns (SM-MCTS-M) rather than the most recent sample. The theoretical contribution is an asymptotic convergence guarantee: under ε-HC selection and guaranteed exploration, the induced average strategies converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The bound scales with depth D; in the main theorem it is stated as O(ε D^2) (the statement appears to miss an ε in the text). The proof proceeds by (i) repeated matrix games, (ii) repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors, and (iii) induction over depth in the extensive-form tree. Empirically, the paper compares mean vs sample backups and RM vs Exp3 in random small games and enumerated worst cases, showing convergence in practice and that mean backups are slightly slower.

Relation to prior work
- MCTS/UCT: Prior work (Kocsis & Szepesvári 2006) analyzes UCT in single-agent settings; UCT lacks general convergence guarantees in simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al. 2009). This paper focuses on algorithms other than UCB/UCT to obtain guarantees.
- Exact search in simultaneous move games: Backward induction and alpha-beta pruning adapted to simultaneous-move games (Saffidine et al.; Bosanský et al.) can solve small games but need evaluation functions and LP/DO pruning.
- Regret minimization: HC algorithms (Hart & Mas-Colell; Exp3 of Auer et al.) are known to minimize regret in matrix games and to converge to Nash in self-play. The paper builds on these guarantees at each node and extends them to trees with simultaneous moves, a setting not previously analyzed for MCTS.
- CFR: Although CFR is for imperfect-information games, it is relevant as it applies regret locally at information sets. The paper’s per-node HC selection is an analogue in perfect-information simultaneous-move games.

Quality
- Theory: The reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated games with bounded error is sound in spirit. The technical steps (Lemma 4.6 and 4.9) are reasonable and leverage standard HC properties, extended to bounded payoff perturbations. The induction over depth is plausible; however, constants appear loose and the main theorem’s bound appears as (D^2+δ) without ε, which is likely a typographical omission. The assumption of “guaranteed exploration” and eventually complete tree expansion is stated; the proof details are deferred to the appendix/arXiv, which is acceptable but weakens the self-contained nature of the NeurIPS submission.
- Empirics: Experiments are limited to small random games and exhaustive depth-2, branching-2 worst cases with simple payoffs. They effectively illustrate the need for mean backups in the proof (and their slight slowdown), and compare RM vs Exp3, but do not stress scale, nor compare to strong baselines (e.g., SM-UCT variants, alpha-beta with pruning), nor report statistical significance or sensitivity beyond a few γ values.
- Completeness: No finite-time convergence rates are provided. Given HC rates (e.g., O(1/√T)), a corollary translating these into exploitability vs iterations would strengthen the contribution.

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized, with clear algorithmic template and pseudocode. The notation is heavy but standard for EFGs.
- Minor issues: Theorem 4.11 appears to miss ε in the bound; please clarify the exact dependence on ε, γ, and D. Some references are incomplete and formatting is inconsistent. The statement “we show that [complete tree] will eventually happen with probability 1” should be at least sketched (argument: fixed γ > 0, finite tree, Borel–Cantelli implies infinite visits and eventual expansion).
- Suggest explicitly stating whether the convergence guarantee applies to empirical frequencies, average strategies, or both (you have Lemma 4.5 equating them asymptotically).

Originality
- The specific result—first asymptotic convergence guarantees for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games using per-node HC selection and mean backups—appears novel. While the ingredients (HC in repeated games, induction over tree depth) are known, their combination in this setting and the subgame-perfect ε-NE guarantee are new.
- The idea of using mean backups to align with the analysis is a useful insight for theory.

Significance
- Theoretical: Provides a long-needed justification for SM-MCTS-style methods in simultaneous-move games, a class that appears in several applications.
- Practical: Asymptotic nature and loose D^2 constants limit immediate practical guidance. Nonetheless, the result may guide algorithm choices (use HC selection, consider mean backups) and future finite-time analyses.

Pros
- Clear theoretical framework linking HC bandits to SM-MCTS convergence.
- Subgame-perfect equilibrium guarantee, not just root value convergence.
- Covers multiple selection algorithms (RM, Exp3) via the HC property.
- Empirical sanity checks corroborate convergence and document the mean-backup trade-off.

Cons
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time bounds or sample complexity.
- Likely loose constants (4(c+1), D^2 accumulation); missing ε in Theorem 4.11.
- Experiments are small-scale and do not compare to strong baselines across standard benchmarks.
- Dependence on mean backups reduces practical performance slightly; discussion of when this is necessary in practice is limited.
- Some proofs deferred; references and notation could be tightened.

Questions and suggestions
- Please correct and precisely state Theorem 4.11, including explicit dependence on ε and γ. Can the D^2 factor be reduced to O(D) with a sharper induction?
- Provide a finite-time corollary: if regret after T steps is O(1/√T), what is the resulting exploitability rate at depth D?
- Clarify the role of empirical vs average strategies in practice; do both converge under your assumptions (with Lemma 4.5)?
- Expand experiments: include benchmarks like Goofspiel/Tron; compare to SM-UCT, alpha-beta variants; report sensitivity to γ and depth/branching; show exploitability over wall-clock time.
- Outline (even briefly) the argument that the tree becomes complete a.s. under guaranteed exploration.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper addresses a gap with a clear and correct asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games, using broadly applicable HC selection. While empirical evaluation and bounds could be stronger, the theoretical contribution is of interest to the NeurIPS community working on planning, game theory, and online learning. With minor revisions and clearer statements, it merits inclusion.
