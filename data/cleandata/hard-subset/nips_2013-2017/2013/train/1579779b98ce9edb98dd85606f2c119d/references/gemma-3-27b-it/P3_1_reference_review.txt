## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental open problem in the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) – proving convergence guarantees for games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general framework for SM-MCTS based on leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as action selection mechanisms. They formally prove that, using a -Hannan consistent selection function, their SM-MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. The theoretical results are supported by empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating competitive convergence speeds compared to recent SM-MCTS algorithms.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly identifies the gap in theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games.  While MCTS has seen practical success in this domain (cited works [10, 11, 12, 13]), formal convergence guarantees were lacking.  The connection to regret minimization (RM) and the use of Hannan consistency ([15, 16]) are well-established concepts from online learning, effectively bridging the gap between game theory and algorithmic approaches.  The authors acknowledge the non-convergence of UCB ([17]) and appropriately focus on algorithms with proven regret bounds. They also relate to recent works utilizing alpha-beta pruning [8,9] and double-oracle methods [9] for backward induction, highlighting the advantage of their simulation-based approach when domain knowledge is limited.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength of this work is the formal proof of convergence. This is a significant contribution to the field, providing a solid foundation for the theoretical analysis of SM-MCTS. The proof structure, building from matrix games to multi-stage games, is well-reasoned and presented clearly.
* **Generality:** The proposed framework is general, allowing for the instantiation with any -Hannan consistent regret-minimizing procedure. This offers flexibility and the potential for further improvements by incorporating more sophisticated regret minimization algorithms.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while not exhaustive, provide initial evidence supporting the theoretical findings. Demonstrating comparable convergence rates to existing algorithms is encouraging.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions and background material are thorough, making the core concepts accessible to a broad audience.
* **Addressing Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations, such as the conservative convergence rate implied by the theoretical analysis, and suggest potential future research directions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Practical Implications of Hannan Consistency:** While Hannan consistency is a strong theoretical guarantee, its practical implications for performance in complex games are not fully explored. The convergence rate bound stemming from the theorem might be loose, and the empirical evaluation doesn't sufficiently address this.  A more detailed analysis linking the -value in the convergence guarantee to real-world performance metrics would be valuable.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is relatively limited. The games used are either randomly generated or simplified worst-case scenarios.  Testing on more complex, real-world games (e.g., Goofspiel variations) would strengthen the claims. Furthermore, a more thorough comparison to other SM-MCTS variants (beyond just convergence speed) considering factors like computational cost and memory usage is missing.
* **Lack of Discussion on Exploration Parameter (γ):**  The role of the exploration parameter (γ) in the algorithms is briefly mentioned but not thoroughly analyzed. Its influence on convergence speed and the trade-off between exploration and exploitation deserve a more in-depth discussion.  How does the choice of γ affect the theoretical convergence guarantee?
* **Numerical Stability:** The mention of modifying Exp3 for numerical stability is good, but a brief discussion of potential numerical issues in the general framework would be helpful.
* **Theorem 4.11 - Tightness of Bound:** While the theorem is correct, it's unclear how tight the bound  (D^2 + δ) is.  Is this bound achievable in practice, or is it a worst-case scenario that rarely occurs?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of game theory and AI.
* The theoretical contribution is substantial and provides a valuable foundation for future research.
* The empirical results support the theoretical claims and demonstrate the practicality of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents the key concepts and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited and does not fully demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.
* The practical implications of the theoretical convergence guarantee are not thoroughly explored.
* The discussion of the exploration parameter and numerical stability could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The formal proof of convergence for SM-MCTS is a major achievement. While the empirical evaluation could be more extensive and the practical implications of the theoretical results could be explored further, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Expand the empirical evaluation:** Include tests on more complex games and compare to a wider range of SM-MCTS algorithms.
* **Analyze the impact of the exploration parameter (γ):** Conduct experiments to investigate the effect of γ on convergence speed and discuss its implications for the theoretical guarantee.
* **Provide a more detailed discussion of the tightness of the convergence bound in Theorem 4.11.**
* **Briefly address potential numerical issues and their mitigation strategies.**
* **Discuss the limitations of the Hannan consistency requirement in practical scenarios.**
