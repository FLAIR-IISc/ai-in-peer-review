

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player zero-sum games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures each action is attempted infinitely often), the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The authors empirically evaluate their approach using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection procedures, showing convergence rates comparable to existing MCTS algorithms for these games.

This work builds on several lines of previous research: the theoretical foundation of MCTS in sequential games (e.g., UCT), regret minimization algorithms (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), and game-theoretic concepts (e.g., Hannan consistency, Nash equilibria). The paper correctly identifies that while MCTS has been widely used in simultaneous move games (e.g., in Goofspiel, Tron, and general game-playing), no convergence guarantees existed for these settings, which is a notable gap compared to the well-established theory for sequential games.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This is a major gap in the literature that the authors successfully address.

2. **Rigorous Formal Analysis**: The proof is well-structured, proceeding from simple matrix games to extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors carefully handle the bounded error introduced by the stochastic nature of the selection functions and the exploration bias.

3. **Clear Theoretical Framework**: The paper establishes a solid theoretical framework by defining the necessary concepts (ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) and proving their properties in a systematic manner.

4. **Appropriate Experimental Validation**: The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical analysis well. The experiments on randomly generated and worst-case games provide reasonable evidence supporting the theoretical claims.

5. **Proper Contextualization**: The paper clearly situates itself in the literature, properly citing relevant work on MCTS, regret minimization, and game theory.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Comparison**: While the paper compares their mean propagation variant (RMM) with the current sample value variant (RM), it lacks a thorough comparison with other MCTS algorithms that have been used in practice for simultaneous move games. A more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. **No Finite-Time Convergence Bounds**: The theoretical analysis establishes eventual convergence but doesn't provide finite-time convergence bounds. For practical applications, knowing how quickly convergence occurs (i.e., how many iterations are needed to reach a given ε) would be highly valuable.

3. **Limited Scope of Games**: The paper only addresses zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's worth noting that many practical applications (e.g., card games with imperfect information) involve more complex game structures.

4. **Minor Notational Ambiguity**: The paper states "the standard way of defining the value to send back is RetVal(u1, Xh, nh) = u1" but then later explains that "RetVal(u1, Xh, nh) = Xh/nh is required for the formal analysis." This could be clarified more explicitly to avoid confusion.

## Quality Assessment

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The formal analysis is comprehensive and well-structured, building from simpler cases to the more complex extensive-form games. The authors are careful to address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The experimental results support the theoretical claims, though the experimental comparison with other MCTS algorithms could be more thorough.

## Clarity Assessment

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the logical flow from matrix games to extensive-form games is clear. The authors provide adequate background and definitions for the key concepts. The paper would benefit from slightly more explicit explanation of the difference between the mean propagation variant (RMM) and the current sample value variant (RM), but this is a minor issue.

## Originality Assessment

This work is highly original. As the authors correctly state, they are filling a significant gap in the literature, as no convergence guarantees were known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general extensive-form games. The approach of using regret-minimizing procedures as the selection function for MCTS in this context is novel. The paper properly differentiates itself from previous work on MCTS in sequential games (e.g., UCT) and on regret minimization algorithms.

## Significance Assessment

The results are significant for both theoretical and practical reasons. The convergence guarantees provide a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in simultaneous move games, which have been widely used in practice (e.g., security games, Goofspiel, general game-playing). This work will likely be of interest to researchers in algorithmic game theory, reinforcement learning, and game AI. The paper advances the state of the art by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games, which is a demonstrable advancement over previous work.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Fills a significant gap in the literature with the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Provides a theoretically sound framework that can be instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent procedure
- The formal analysis is rigorous and well-structured
- The empirical results support the theoretical claims
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds, which would be valuable for practical applications
- Limited experimental comparison with other MCTS algorithms used in practice for simultaneous move games
- Focuses only on zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, not more general game structures

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap in the literature. While the paper has some limitations (particularly regarding finite-time bounds and comprehensive experimental comparison), the theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. The paper makes a clear and valuable contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory and MCTS.

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It represents a solid theoretical advancement that is likely to be influential for future work in MCTS and game theory.
