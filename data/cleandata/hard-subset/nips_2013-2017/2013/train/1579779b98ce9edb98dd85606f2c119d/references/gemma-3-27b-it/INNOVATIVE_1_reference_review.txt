This paper presents a convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond the well-studied sequential setting. The core idea – framing SM-MCTS as a repeated matrix game and leveraging Hannan consistency – is genuinely novel and constitutes a strong contribution. The authors correctly identify the lack of convergence guarantees for MCTS in this domain and provide a solid theoretical foundation, demonstrating convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under fairly general conditions. The empirical validation, while not extensive, supports the theoretical claims and shows competitive performance.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move games is a significant advancement. This is a rarely explored area and the authors tackle a truly open problem.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof of convergence based on Hannan consistency is well-structured and appears sound. The authors carefully address the nuances of extending existing theory to this more complex game structure.
*   **Generality:** The template nature of the proposed MCTS algorithm, allowing instantiation with different regret-minimizing procedures, increases its applicability and impact.
*   **Clear Presentation:** Despite the theoretical depth, the paper is relatively well-written and organized, with clear definitions and explanations.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experiments, even with their limited scope, provide initial validation of the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are primarily conducted on randomly generated games and a limited set of worst-case scenarios. A more comprehensive evaluation on established simultaneous-move game benchmarks (e.g., variations of Goofspiel, more complex pursuit-evasion games) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Convergence Rate:** While convergence is *proven*, the rate of convergence isn’t characterized, either theoretically or empirically. Knowing how quickly the algorithm converges to the approximate Nash equilibrium is crucial for practical applications.
*   **Practical Implications of the -Hannan Consistency Requirement:** The paper emphasizes the need for -Hannan consistency. Discussing the practical implications of this requirement and potential challenges in finding efficient regret-minimizing procedures that satisfy it would be valuable.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of zero-sum, perfect information is strong. A discussion of how the results might be extended (or fail to extend) to imperfect information or non-zero-sum games would broaden the paper's impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in the theory of MCTS.
*   The theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and have the potential to significantly impact the design and analysis of MCTS algorithms for simultaneous-move games.
*   The generality of the proposed framework makes it widely applicable.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic.
*   Even with the limited empirical results, there is sufficient support for the theoretical claims.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and doesn't fully demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed framework.
*   The lack of a convergence rate analysis limits the paper's immediate practical impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper presents a genuinely novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of MCTS and game theory. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and the lack of a convergence rate analysis is a minor drawback, the theoretical novelty and generality of the results outweigh these concerns. This work opens up new avenues for research in the application of MCTS to a broader class of games and provides a valuable theoretical foundation for future advancements. I believe this paper has the potential to be highly influential in the field. The minor flaws do not detract from the considerable value of this work.
