This paper addresses the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS-style algorithms have been practically applied to this game class, a formal proof of their convergence to a Nash Equilibrium has remained an open problem. The central contribution of this work is to provide such a proof. The authors propose a general template for MCTS where the action selection mechanism within each game state (node) is handled by a regret-minimizing procedure. The main theoretical result shows that if this selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. This framework is instantiated with two well-known regret minimizers, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical analysis on randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its significant and novel theoretical contribution. It provides what appears to be the first formal proof of convergence for an MCTS-based algorithm in simultaneous move games. This result bridges a crucial gap between the widespread practical use of simulation-based methods in complex games and their theoretical understanding from an algorithmic game theory perspective. The connection drawn between MCTS and the online learning framework of ε-Hannan consistency is insightful and provides a powerful analytical tool.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The proof is built up logically, starting from the known properties of regret minimizers in simple repeated matrix games, extending the analysis to games with bounded payoff errors, and finally applying this result inductively to the entire extensive-form game tree. This step-by-step approach makes the argument clear and convincing.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established by highlighting the lack of theory for MCTS in this domain. Algorithm 1 presents a clean, abstract template for Simultaneous Move MCTS, which is then clearly instantiated with RM and Exp3. The definitions and theoretical lemmas are presented sequentially, leading effectively to the main theorem.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions**

1.  **Theoretical Bound:** The main result (Theorem 4.11) establishes convergence to a (εD^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium, where D is the depth of the game. The quadratic dependence on the game depth seems potentially loose and could lead to a very weak bound for deep games. The proof sketch suggests this arises from the summation of errors at each level of the induction. It would greatly improve the paper if the authors could provide more intuition for this D^2 term in the main text and discuss whether this is an artifact of the proof technique or a fundamental property. A brief discussion on potential avenues for tightening this bound would also be valuable.

2.  **Simplification of the MCTS Process:** The formal analysis in Section 4 makes a key simplifying assumption: it analyzes the convergence of the node policies on a complete, pre-existing game tree, assuming every node-action pair is tried infinitely often. While the "guaranteed exploration" property ensures this happens eventually, this framing sidesteps the analysis of the tree policy itself, i.e., how MCTS allocates its simulation budget across different subtrees. The proof is thus more about the convergence of nested regret minimizers with noisy value estimates than about the full dynamics of the MCTS search process. This is a reasonable simplification to make the proof tractable, but it should be stated more explicitly. The authors should clarify that their analysis focuses on the convergence of the strategies within the nodes, under the condition that the search visits them sufficiently, rather than analyzing the search-directing mechanism itself.

3.  **Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical analysis successfully validates the theoretical claim that the proposed algorithms converge. However, its scope is somewhat limited.
    *   The experiments show that propagating the last sampled value (as in standard RM) converges slightly faster than propagating the mean value (RMM), which is the variant required by the proof. This is an interesting result, but it leaves an open question about whether the proof can be extended to cover the empirically superior and more standard variant. The authors conjecture this is possible, but a brief discussion on the technical challenges involved would be welcome.
    *   The evaluation could be strengthened by including other baselines. For instance, empirically demonstrating the known non-convergence of a UCB1-based MCTS [17] on one of the worst-case games would provide a powerful contrast and further motivate the paper's approach.

4.  **Role of the Exploration Parameter (γ):** The experiments use a fixed exploration parameter γ, which correctly leads to convergence to an approximate equilibrium where the exploitability is bounded by a function of γ. The paper would benefit from a short discussion on the use of a decaying γ schedule. Theoretically, would the proof framework still hold? Empirically, would it allow for convergence to a 0-NE in the limit? Many regret-minimization bounds rely on decaying learning/exploration rates, and touching on this would connect the paper more closely to that literature.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro:** A highly significant and novel theoretical result, providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The paper is well-written, and the proof technique connecting MCTS to regret minimization is elegant.
*   **Con:** The theoretical bound on approximation quality appears loose. The analysis simplifies the MCTS search process by assuming a full tree. The empirical evaluation, while supportive, could be more comprehensive.

**Recommendation: Accept**

Despite the weaknesses, which are primarily opportunities for future work and refinement, the core theoretical contribution of this paper is strong, novel, and important. It provides a solid foundation for the analysis of simulation-based algorithms in a challenging class of games. The paper is well-executed and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
