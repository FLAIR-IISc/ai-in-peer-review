## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles a fundamental open problem in the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS): proving convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move games. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this setting, leveraging regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games as the core action selection mechanism. Crucially, they formally prove that using an -Hannan consistent selection function leads to convergence to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They empirically validate this with Regret Matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence speeds comparable to existing algorithms. 

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the growing body of work applying MCTS to game-playing, particularly noting the success of MCTS in sequential games (e.g., Go) but the lack of theoretical guarantees in simultaneous-move settings. The authors acknowledge previous work on alpha-beta pruning and double-oracle methods for backward induction, correctly identifying their computational expense. They also accurately represent the limitations of UCB in this context, citing recent work demonstrating its lack of convergence. The paper appropriately references relevant literature on regret minimization (Regret Matching, Exp3) and Hannan consistency, establishing a solid foundation for their theoretical analysis. 

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The proof of convergence is the major strength of this paper. Providing theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a significant advancement. The use of Hannan consistency as a key ingredient is insightful and provides a clear pathway for analyzing other MCTS variants.
* **General Framework:** The proposed MCTS template is general, allowing for instantiation with different regret-minimizing procedures. This broadens the potential applicability of their analysis.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The definitions and notation are presented rigorously, and the algorithmic description is easy to follow. 
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while not extensive, provide empirical support for the theoretical results, demonstrating that the proposed algorithms converge in practice. The comparison to recent MCTS algorithms is valuable.
* **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors are upfront about the limitations of their work, specifically the lack of finite-time convergence bounds and the conjecture regarding extending the analysis to other MCTS variants.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves *convergence*, it provides little insight into the *rate* of convergence. The empirical results offer some indication, but a more detailed theoretical analysis of the convergence speed (even asymptotic) would be highly valuable. The bound obtained is weak, proportional to the game tree depth.
* **Practical Relevance of -Hannan Consistency:**  The theoretical guarantee relies on -Hannan consistency.  While theoretically sound, the practical implications of enforcing this property (and potentially sacrificing exploration) in real-world game-playing scenarios isn't fully addressed. Is the improvement in theoretical guarantees worth the potential performance trade-off?
* **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. The experiments are primarily conducted on randomly generated games, and the analysis of the "worst case" games is not fully rigorous (e.g., only 100 runs). A more extensive empirical study, including comparison to stronger baselines and analysis on a wider range of game instances (e.g., Goofspiel), would strengthen the results.
* **Limited Discussion of Exploration vs. Exploitation:** The role of exploration (parameter γ) is mentioned but not thoroughly investigated. A more in-depth analysis of how the exploration parameter affects the convergence rate and the quality of the resulting equilibrium would be beneficial.  How does the choice of γ interact with the Hannan consistency requirement?
* **Notation Overload:** While generally clear, the paper sometimes suffers from a dense use of notation, which can make it challenging to follow the details of the proofs.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in MCTS research, providing the first theoretical convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move games.
* The generality of the proposed framework and the rigorous theoretical analysis are commendable.
* The empirical results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of analysis of the convergence rate is a significant limitation.
* The empirical evaluation could be more extensive and rigorous.
* The practical relevance of enforcing strict Hannan consistency needs further discussion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of MCTS. While the lack of convergence rate analysis and the limited empirical scope are weaknesses, the significance of the convergence proof outweighs these concerns. I believe this paper is a worthwhile addition to NIPS.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**

**Confidence:** 8/10 (High - the theoretical contribution is substantial, but improvements in the empirical analysis would strengthen the paper further.)
