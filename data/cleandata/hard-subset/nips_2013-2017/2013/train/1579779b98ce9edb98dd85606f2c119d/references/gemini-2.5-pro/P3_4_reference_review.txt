**Summary**

This paper addresses a significant open problem in the theory of game-playing algorithms: the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous move games. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for such games, their theoretical guarantees have been elusive. The authors provide the first formal proof that a general class of MCTS algorithms converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The core idea is to frame the action selection within each game node as a repeated matrix game. By requiring the selection strategy to be an ε-Hannan consistent regret minimizer (such as Regret Matching or Exp3), the authors prove convergence via an inductive argument over the depth of the game tree. The proof elegantly handles the propagation of errors from lower-level subgames, showing that convergence holds even when payoffs are noisy estimates from deeper simulations. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on random and empirically-derived worst-case games.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** This paper provides a landmark theoretical result. The convergence of MCTS in anything beyond standard sequential games has been a major open question. By connecting the MCTS selection step to the well-developed theory of online learning and regret minimization (specifically Hannan consistency), the authors establish a solid theoretical foundation for using MCTS in this important class of games. This work successfully bridges the gap between the practical success of simulation-based methods and the rigorous demands of algorithmic game theory.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The proof structure is sound and well-reasoned. The paper correctly identifies the key challenges: ensuring sufficient exploration and handling the noisy, biased value estimates that propagate up the tree. The inductive proof, which first establishes convergence for a single matrix game, then extends it to matrix games with bounded error, and finally applies this recursively up the game tree, is a powerful and appropriate technique for this problem. The definitions (ε-Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) are precise and form the basis for a solid argument.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. It begins with a clear motivation and background, defines the SM-MCTS template, and then builds the theoretical argument step-by-step from simple matrix games to the full extensive-form case. The proof sketch in Section 4 provides excellent intuition before the formal details. The experimental section is also clear in its goals and presentation of results.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** A primary weakness is the discrepancy between the algorithm variant required for the proof and the variant that performs better empirically. The formal analysis (specifically, the bounded error argument in Lemma 4.9) requires propagating the *mean* of all rewards from a child node (RMM/Exp3M). However, the experiments consistently show that the standard MCTS practice of propagating the value from the *current* sample (RM/Exp3) converges faster. While the authors acknowledge this and conjecture their proof could be extended, the paper as it stands provides a theoretical guarantee for a seemingly suboptimal algorithm. This gap reduces the practical implications of the formal proof.

2.  **Lack of Convergence Rate Analysis:** The paper proves asymptotic convergence (i.e., as t → ∞) but provides no finite-time bounds on the convergence rate. The final bound on the equilibrium approximation quality, O(D²ε), depends on the game depth D, but it's unclear how the number of iterations `t` affects ε. For practical applications, understanding the convergence *rate* is crucial. The empirical results on the "worst-case" games suggest that convergence can be extremely slow, requiring millions of iterations to reach a good approximation even in a simple game. Without theoretical rate bounds, it is difficult to assess the algorithm's practical feasibility for larger games.

3.  **Limited Scope of Empirical "Worst-Case" Analysis:** The search for worst-case games was limited to depth 2, branching factor 2, and a discrete set of utilities. While this is a reasonable empirical approach, it should be noted that these may not be the true worst-case instances. The slow convergence observed is concerning, and it is possible that more complex games exhibit even slower rates.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you elaborate on the technical barrier to extending the proof to the single-sample backpropagation case (the standard RM/Exp3)? My intuition is that the value estimates `X_h/n_h` are no longer unbiased estimators of the subgame value at any finite time, which would complicate the bounded error argument. Is this the core issue, or is there another subtlety?
*   The final approximation quality depends on D². This seems like a potentially loose bound that would make the approach impractical for deep games. Is this dependence an artifact of the proof technique (e.g., summing worst-case deviations at each level), or do you believe it is fundamental to the problem?
*   In Section 3.1, the regret update uses `x(h, i', j) = X_hij` for actions not taken. This seems to imply that the algorithm requires access to the running totals of all children to update the regret for one child. This is a non-standard regret update and seems computationally inefficient. Standard regret matching would typically update regret based on a vector of estimated rewards for each action `i'`, not just the single reward `u1`. Could you clarify this update rule and its relation to standard regret matching in repeated games?
*   The paper focuses on two-player zero-sum games. While this is an important first step, a brief discussion on the potential for extending these results to general-sum or n-player games would be valuable for situating the work in a broader context. The regret minimization framework is not limited to zero-sum settings, suggesting a possible path forward.

**Conclusion**

This is a very strong paper that makes a foundational contribution to the theory of MCTS. The originality and significance of providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games are undeniable. The technical approach is elegant and sound. The main weaknesses are the gap between the proven algorithm and the empirically superior one, and the lack of finite-time convergence bounds, which limits the immediate practical takeaway. Despite these limitations, the theoretical breakthrough is substantial enough to merit publication at a top-tier conference like NIPS. The work opens up many avenues for future research, including tightening the analysis, extending it to more practical algorithm variants, and generalizing it to broader game classes.
