### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first convergence guarantees for its use in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for such games, their theoretical properties have been poorly understood, with prior work even showing that popular methods like UCT fail to converge to a Nash Equilibrium (NE).

The authors propose a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting, where the action selection at each node is handled by a regret-minimizing procedure. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if this selection procedure is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (i.e., every action is selected infinitely often), the resulting MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is constructed inductively, starting with single-stage matrix games, extending the analysis to games with bounded payoff errors, and finally applying this framework to the entire game tree. The paper instantiates this template with two well-known regret minimizers, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, and provides an empirical evaluation on random and worst-case games. The experiments compare the provably convergent variant with a more standard MCTS implementation and analyze their empirical convergence rates.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel and important theoretical result. It provides the first formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash Equilibrium for a class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games. This is a foundational contribution that solidifies the theoretical underpinning for using MCTS in this game class, moving beyond purely empirical success.

2.  **Generality of the Framework:** The authors present their convergence proof not for a single algorithm, but for a general template of MCTS based on any ε-Hannan consistent action selection module. This makes the result powerful and broadly applicable, covering a family of algorithms including those based on Regret Matching and Exp3. It provides clear guidance for designing provably convergent MCTS algorithms for this domain.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The problem is clearly motivated, the necessary background and definitions are concisely introduced, and the theoretical analysis builds logically from simple matrix games to the full extensive-form setting. The proof sketch is intuitive and the overall argument is easy to follow for readers familiar with game theory and online learning.

4.  **Soundness of Analysis:** The proof technique, which relies on showing that nodes at a higher level of the game tree can be modeled as a matrix game with bounded errors coming from the subgames below, is elegant and sound. Leveraging the properties of Hannan consistency to bound regret and prove convergence to an approximate NE in this setting is a clever application of established online learning theory to the MCTS framework.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or strengthened.

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice (Mean vs. Sample Propagation):** The formal analysis requires that the MCTS algorithm backpropagates the *mean* reward of a child node (`Xh/nh`), whereas standard MCTS implementations (including the better-performing variants in the paper's experiments) backpropagate the single *sample* reward from the current rollout (`u1`). The authors are transparent about this discrepancy and their experiments show that the "mean propagation" variant converges slightly slower. While the authors conjecture their proof could be extended, the paper would be significantly strengthened by bridging this gap. Even a high-level discussion of the technical challenges involved in proving convergence for the sample-propagation case, or an intuitive argument for why it might hold, would be a valuable addition.

2.  **Asymptotic Convergence and Lack of Rate Analysis:** The main theorem guarantees eventual convergence to an ε'-NE but provides no information about the *rate* of convergence. For an anytime algorithm like MCTS, the convergence rate is a critical aspect of its performance. While deriving finite-time bounds is noted as future work, the current analysis is purely asymptotic. This limits the practical implications of the theoretical result, as it doesn't tell us how many simulations are needed to reach a certain quality of approximation.

3.  **Bound on Approximation Quality:** The final bound on the equilibrium approximation is given as O(ε * D^2), where D is the game depth. This quadratic dependence on depth seems potentially loose and may be an artifact of summing worst-case errors at each level of the induction. A brief discussion on whether this bound is believed to be tight, or if it could be improved (e.g., to O(ε * D)), would be helpful for understanding the scalability of the approach.

4.  **Experimental Scope:** The experiments effectively support the paper's claims but could be slightly expanded. The evaluation on "worst-case" games is insightful, but these games are found through a search on a very restricted set of small games. Including results on a well-known benchmark for this class, such as Goofspiel (which is mentioned in the introduction), would provide a more standardized point of comparison and strengthen the empirical section.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty & Significance:** First formal proof of MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games, addressing a long-standing open question.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound, rigorous, and well-presented.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Generality:** The results apply to a broad class of algorithms based on ε-Hannan consistency.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical result applies to a variant of MCTS (mean propagation) that is shown to be slightly inferior in practice to the standard version (sample propagation).
*   The analysis is purely asymptotic and lacks convergence rates, which are important for anytime algorithms.

### Decision

This paper presents a high-quality, technically sound, and significant contribution to the field. The establishment of the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games is an important step forward. The weaknesses identified, while valid, primarily relate to the limitations of the current analysis and represent exciting avenues for future work rather than critical flaws in the present contribution. The paper's strengths, particularly its novelty and theoretical rigor, far outweigh these limitations. I strongly recommend **acceptance**.
