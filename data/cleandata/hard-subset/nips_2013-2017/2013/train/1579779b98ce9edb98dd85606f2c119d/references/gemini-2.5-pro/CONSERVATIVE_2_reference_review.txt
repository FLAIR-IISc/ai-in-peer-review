**Summary:**
This paper addresses a theoretical gap in the literature concerning Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that utilizes regret-minimizing procedures for action selection within each node. The main contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the resulting MCTS algorithm's average strategy profile converges almost surely to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium of the game. The proof proceeds by induction on the depth of the game tree, modeling subgames as repeated matrix games with bounded error derived from the estimates of child nodes. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on random and empirically-derived worst-case games using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as instantiations of the framework.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Addresses a Notable Theoretical Gap:** The paper tackles an important and open question. While MCTS variants are used in practice for simultaneous move games, a formal guarantee of their convergence to Nash equilibria has been lacking. This work provides the first such proof, which is a significant theoretical contribution.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Framework:** The approach of leveraging ε-Hannan consistency from online learning literature as the core property for the selection function is elegant and well-motivated. The inductive proof structure, which models parent nodes as playing matrix games with bounded error from their children's value estimates, is a sound and logical way to extend the analysis from single matrix games to deep extensive-form games.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem, definitions, and the proposed algorithm template are presented clearly. The structure of the formal analysis is laid out logically, making the theoretical argument relatively easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation Against Baselines:** The primary weakness of this paper lies in its empirical evaluation. The experiments exclusively compare variants of the proposed framework (RM vs. RMM, Exp3 vs. Exp3M). While this sheds light on the internal properties of their method (e.g., the effect of propagating mean values), it fails to position the work within the broader context of existing SM-MCTS algorithms. The introduction cites several practical SM-MCTS algorithms [10, 11, 12, 20], yet none are included as baselines in the experiments. The claim that the "empirical speed of convergence... is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms" is an unsubstantiated assertion without direct, side-by-side comparisons on the same set of problems. A rigorous evaluation would require demonstrating performance against established, practical (even if unproven) methods.
2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practical Performance:** The formal analysis requires propagating the mean of previous samples (the 'M' variants, RMM and Exp3M) to bound the error. However, the empirical results in Figure 2 and 3 consistently show that this variant converges *slower* than the standard approach of propagating the current sample's value. This creates a problematic disconnect: the method that is formally proven is empirically inferior to a similar heuristic that lacks a proof. The paper does not adequately address the practical implications of this finding. If the price of a convergence guarantee is a slower convergence rate, the practical utility of the result is diminished, especially in a field where performance is paramount. The authors conjecture their analysis could be extended, but as it stands, the proven algorithm is not the one practitioners would likely choose based on these results.
3.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is asymptotic and relies on the assumption that the entire game tree is eventually built and every node-action pair is sampled infinitely often. While this is a standard assumption for this type of convergence proof, it sidesteps the core challenge of MCTS in practice, which is to find the best possible strategy within a finite computational budget where only a fraction of the tree can be explored. The analysis does not provide finite-time bounds on the convergence rate, which would be of much greater practical significance.
4.  **Modest Algorithmic Novelty:** The contribution is almost entirely theoretical. The proposed "template" is instantiated using well-established algorithms from the online learning literature (Regret Matching, Exp3). The novelty lies in placing these algorithms within an MCTS loop for this game class and proving convergence, not in the design of a new, high-performing algorithm. While valuable, this may be a better fit for a more theory-focused venue.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents a solid, novel theoretical result by being the first to prove the convergence of an MCTS-based algorithm to a Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games. This is a commendable achievement. However, the work is let down by an empirical evaluation that is not rigorous enough for a top-tier conference like NIPS. The lack of comparison to relevant and established baselines makes it impossible to assess the practical significance of the proposed methods. Furthermore, the fact that the provably convergent variant of the algorithm is empirically slower than its unproven counterpart raises serious questions about the impact of the theoretical contribution on practice.

While the theory is sound, the paper does not make a compelling case that its contributions advance the state of the art in a demonstrable, practical way. The claims of performance are not supported by the necessary evidence, and the work feels incomplete without a more thorough experimental section and a deeper discussion on bridging the gap between the theory and the empirically superior heuristics.
