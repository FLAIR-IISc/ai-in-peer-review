**Summary:**
This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS-based algorithms are used in practice for this game class, a formal proof of their convergence to a Nash Equilibrium has been an open question. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that utilizes a regret-minimizing procedure for action selection within each node. The main contribution is a formal proof showing that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent (e.g., based on Regret Matching or Exp3), the algorithm's average strategy converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical analysis on randomly generated and small worst-case games, evaluating the convergence speed of different instantiations.

**Strengths and Weaknesses:**

**Quality:**
The paper's main strength is its formal analysis. The theoretical result is novel and addresses a recognized gap in the literature. The approach of leveraging the properties of Hannan-consistent algorithms from the online learning literature and extending them via an inductive argument over the game tree is methodologically sound.

However, there are several weaknesses that temper the quality and impact of this contribution:
1.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The formal analysis requires a specific variant of the algorithm where the *mean* of all rewards from a child node is propagated up the tree (denoted RMM/Exp3M). However, the paper's own experiments (Figures 2 and 3) demonstrate that this variant converges more slowly than the standard practice of propagating the most recent sample's value. This creates a significant gap: the algorithm that is proven to converge is not the one practitioners would likely use, and the superior practical variant remains without a convergence guarantee. The authors' conjecture that the proof could be extended is not a substitute for a proof.
2.  **Simplifying Assumptions in Analysis:** The formal analysis (Section 4) makes the simplifying assumption that the complete game tree is built and available. This assumption is made to facilitate an asymptotic analysis but it fundamentally ignores the defining characteristic of MCTS, which is its selective and asymmetric tree expansion. Practical MCTS performance hinges on its ability to avoid exploring large parts of the game tree. An analysis that assumes the full tree is present may not accurately capture the dynamics of the algorithm in practice.
3.  **Loose Theoretical Bounds:** The final bound on the approximation quality in Theorem 4.11 scales with the square of the game depth (D^2). For games of any significant depth, this bound becomes extremely loose, which questions the practical tightness of the analysis. A more refined analysis yielding a tighter bound would be a much stronger result.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The background, algorithm, theoretical analysis, and experiments are presented in a logical sequence. The definitions are precise, and the structure of the proof is explained before delving into the lemmas. The use of Algorithm 1 as a general template is effective.

**Originality:**
The paper is original in being the first to provide a convergence proof for MCTS in this class of games. While the components used (MCTS, Regret Matching, Exp3) are all well-established, their combination within this theoretical framework to prove convergence is a novel contribution. The work clearly situates itself with respect to prior applications of MCTS in simultaneous move games, which have been largely empirical.

**Significance:**
The significance of the work lies in providing a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in simultaneous move games. This is an important step forward from a purely theoretical standpoint.

However, the practical significance is limited by the issues mentioned under Quality.
1.  **Lack of Empirical Validation Against Baselines:** The experimental evaluation is insufficient. It is performed on randomly generated games and small "worst-case" games. It critically lacks a comparison to existing, state-of-the-art MCTS methods designed for simultaneous move games, such as those cited by the authors [10, 11, 12]. The claim that the convergence speed is "comparable" is not substantiated with direct evidence. Without demonstrating that the proposed (and proven) methods are competitive with or superior to established, albeit unproven, heuristics on recognized benchmarks, the practical impact is unclear.
2.  **No New State-of-the-Art:** The paper provides a justification for existing ideas rather than advancing the state of the art in performance. The experiments do not show that instantiating MCTS with Regret Matching or Exp3 leads to a better game-playing agent than existing approaches. A contribution is more significant if it either enables new capabilities, significantly improves performance, or provides a much tighter understanding of an algorithm's behavior. This work provides a first-pass understanding, but the loose bounds and weak empirical section limit its impact.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   It presents the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous move games, addressing a known open problem.
*   The connection drawn between ε-Hannan consistency in online learning and convergence in MCTS is elegant and theoretically sound.
*   The paper is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The proof relies on an algorithm variant (propagating the mean) that is shown to be empirically inferior to standard practice, creating a significant theory-practice gap.
*   The analysis makes a strong simplifying assumption of a full game tree, which is contrary to the principle of selective search in MCTS.
*   The empirical evaluation is weak, lacking comparisons against established MCTS baselines on standard benchmark problems.
*   The theoretical convergence bounds are loose, potentially limiting their practical relevance for deep games.

**Conclusion:**
While the paper presents a novel and technically interesting theoretical result, its contribution is undermined by a disconnect between the analyzed algorithm and its more practical variant, as well as a lack of rigorous empirical validation against established methods. The simplifying assumptions made in the proof further distance the result from the realities of MCTS application. For a venue like NIPS, a stronger link to practice, either through a proof that covers empirically superior variants or through a comprehensive experimental section demonstrating competitive performance against state-of-the-art baselines, would be expected. As it stands, the paper is a valuable theoretical exercise but falls short of being a complete and impactful contribution to the field.
