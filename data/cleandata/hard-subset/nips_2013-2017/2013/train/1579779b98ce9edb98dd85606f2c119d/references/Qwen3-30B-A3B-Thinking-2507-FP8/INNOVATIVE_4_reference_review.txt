

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms, specifically for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. Prior to this work, no convergence guarantees were known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves, despite the algorithm's widespread practical use in such settings. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games, and they prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection function, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by extending MCTS convergence analysis from sequential to simultaneous move games, which is a major step forward in the algorithmic game theory literature. The authors develop a rigorous framework for analyzing MCTS in this setting, with a clear inductive proof structure that handles the recursive nature of extensive-form games. Their key insight is to connect MCTS with regret minimization algorithms, which are well-studied in online learning and game theory.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from background to formal analysis to empirical evaluation. The authors effectively relate their work to prior contributions in the field, particularly noting the limitations of existing methods like UCT in simultaneous move games. The empirical evaluation, while not exhaustive, provides reasonable support for the theoretical claims, showing that the proposed approach converges to approximate Nash equilibria with comparable speed to other MCTS variants.

**Strengths:**
- **Novelty:** This is the first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games, addressing a significant gap in the literature.
- **Theoretical contribution:** The paper provides rigorous proofs of convergence to ε-Nash equilibria, which is a substantial theoretical advance.
- **Connection to existing work:** The authors successfully bridge the gap between MCTS and regret minimization algorithms, which have been well-studied in online learning.
- **Practical relevance:** The proposed framework is immediately applicable to existing MCTS implementations, as demonstrated by the empirical evaluation with regret matching and Exp3.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper doesn't provide tight convergence rate bounds, which is a limitation of the theoretical analysis.
- The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited to specific game types and doesn't compare against all possible MCTS variants in the literature.
- The paper assumes complete knowledge of the game structure, which may limit applicability to some real-world scenarios.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original, addressing a fundamental theoretical question that has been overlooked in the MCTS literature. The connection between MCTS and Hannan consistency is novel and provides a new perspective on how to analyze these algorithms. While regret minimization has been used in game theory before, its application to MCTS in simultaneous move games is a fresh contribution that significantly advances the field.

**Significance:** The paper has high significance for both theoretical and applied researchers. Theoretical computer scientists will appreciate the rigorous convergence guarantees, while practitioners will find the framework immediately applicable to improve existing MCTS implementations in simultaneous move games. The results provide a theoretical foundation for the widespread use of MCTS in this domain, which previously lacked such justification.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, with careful proofs and clear exposition. The authors have made a genuine effort to be thorough in their analysis, and the paper is well-organized and easy to follow for readers with a background in game theory or machine learning.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory, addressing a fundamental gap in our understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded, the theoretical novelty and rigor of the paper far outweigh this limitation. The authors have provided a framework that will likely guide future research on MCTS in games, and their results justify the use of MCTS in this domain from a theoretical perspective.

The paper is a strong contribution that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. It is the first to establish convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a substantial theoretical leap. The work is well-executed, clearly presented, and addresses a problem that has been of practical interest for years without theoretical foundation. I believe this paper will be influential in the field, and I recommend it for acceptance at NIPS.
