### **Summary**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the literature on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. The core theoretical contribution is proving that if the action selection function is "ε-Hannan consistent" and guarantees exploration, the resulting MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium, where ε' depends on the game's depth. The proof is built by induction, cleverly framing the problem at each level of the game tree as a repeated matrix game where payoffs from subgames are observed with bounded error. The authors empirically validate their claims using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as instantiations, comparing the provably convergent version (which propagates mean rewards) against the more standard variant (which propagates the single sample reward).

### **Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a well-known and important open problem. While MCTS variants have been successfully applied to simultaneous move games in practice, they have lacked theoretical guarantees. Providing the first convergence proof is a substantial contribution that bridges the gap between theory and practice, formally justifying the use of these algorithms.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Proof Technique:** The connection drawn between ε-Hannan consistency from online learning and the convergence of MCTS in this game class is insightful. The inductive proof strategy, which models subgame value estimates as payoffs with bounded error (Lemma 4.9, Theorem 4.10), is a powerful and elegant way to handle the nested structure of extensive-form games. This technique could potentially be adapted to analyze algorithms in other classes of games.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and structured. The theoretical argument is developed in a clear, step-by-step manner, starting from simple repeated matrix games and progressively building up to the full extensive-form setting. The definitions are precise, and the notation is consistent, making the complex analysis relatively easy to follow.

4.  **Generality:** The framework is not tied to a single algorithm but provides a general template applicable to any ε-Hannan consistent procedure. Demonstrating this with two well-known algorithms, Regret Matching and Exp3, strengthens the paper's claims.

### **Weaknesses (Arguments for rejection/revision)**

1.  **Potential Technical Flaw in Algorithm Description:** The description of the Regret Matching update in Section 3.1 appears to be incorrect. The update is given as `rh[i'] <- rh[i'] + (x(h, i', j) - u1)`, where `x(h, i', j)` is defined as the *cumulative reward sum* `Xhij`. In regret matching, the update should be based on an estimate of the expected payoff, not the cumulative sum. The correct update should use the *average* reward, i.e., `Xhij / nhij`. Using the cumulative sum would cause the regrets to grow unboundedly, violating the assumptions of the algorithm. This is a critical detail that undermines the correctness of the described RM variant and the corresponding experiments.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** The formal proof relies on propagating the *mean* of all rewards (`RetVal = Xh/nh`), which the authors call the "M" variant (e.g., RMM). However, the experiments consistently show that this provably convergent variant performs slightly worse than the standard approach of propagating the current sample's reward (e.g., RM). The paper does not provide a clear technical explanation for *why* the proof requires mean propagation, nor does it explore the implications of this performance gap. While the authors conjecture their analysis could be extended, as it stands, the paper proves convergence for a version of the algorithm that is empirically inferior.

3.  **Loose Theoretical Bound:** The final convergence guarantee is for an `(D^2 * ε + δ)`-Nash equilibrium, where D is the game depth. The quadratic dependency on depth seems quite loose and may not be practical for deep games. While providing any bound is a contribution, a discussion of whether this `D^2` factor is an artifact of the proof technique or an inherent property of the problem would be valuable.

4.  **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments primarily serve to confirm the theoretical convergence and compare the "mean" vs. "sample" propagation methods. The evaluation would be stronger if it included comparisons against other state-of-the-art, non-provably-convergent SM-MCTS baselines (e.g., UCB-based methods mentioned in the introduction). This would help quantify the practical performance cost of ensuring convergence.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Clarify the Regret Matching Update:** This is the most critical point. Please verify the update rule in Section 3.1. If it is indeed a typo and the experiments used the average reward, the paper should be corrected. If the experiments used the cumulative sum as written, the results are questionable and need to be rerun with the correct implementation.

*   **Explain the Necessity of Mean Propagation:** The paper should explicitly state *why* the proof requires `RetVal = Xh/nh`. The likely reason is that the mean reward `Xh/nh` converges to the true subgame value `vh`, which is necessary to satisfy the "repeated game with bounded error `η`" condition (Definition 4.8) for the induction to work. A single sample `u1` does not have this convergence property. Making this connection explicit would significantly strengthen the link between the algorithm and the proof.

*   **Discuss the `D^2` Bound:** A short discussion on the tightness of the `O(D^2)` bound would be appreciated. Is there any intuition to suggest it could be improved to `O(D)`, or are there fundamental reasons for the quadratic growth?

*   **Expand Experiments:** To better contextualize the results, consider adding a comparison to a UCB-based SM-MCTS on one of the worst-case games. It would be interesting to see if the UCB variant fails to converge as theory predicts [17] and how its anytime performance compares to RM and Exp3.

*   **Minor Suggestion on Notation:** The notation `x(h, i, j)` in the RM section is slightly confusing. It could be defined more clearly as an estimate of the counterfactual payoff for player 1 taking action `i'` against player 2's sampled action `j`.

### **Overall Recommendation**

This paper makes a very strong and novel theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in a broad and important class of games. The proof technique is elegant and the paper is well-written. However, the potential technical error in the description of the Regret Matching algorithm is a serious concern that must be addressed. Assuming this is a correctable error, the paper's contributions are significant enough for acceptance at NIPS. The other weaknesses, such as the theory-practice gap and the limited experiments, point to areas for improvement but do not invalidate the core result. I lean towards acceptance, conditional on a thorough clarification of the RM update rule.
