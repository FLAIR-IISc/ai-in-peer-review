Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template whose selection rule can be any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) online learning algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3). The main theoretical contribution is an almost-sure convergence guarantee: when mean returns are backed up, the empirical/average strategies converge to a subgame-perfect (approximately) Nash equilibrium, with approximation scaling with game depth. The analysis proceeds in stages: (i) repeated matrix games with HC players converge to approximate NE; (ii) this stability carries over when payoffs are perturbed by bounded errors; (iii) an induction over tree depth yields convergence for stacked matrix games (simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs). Empirically, the paper compares RM vs Exp3, and mean vs sample backups, on random and small worst-case games, observing convergence in practice and a small slowdown when using mean backups.

This builds on and contrasts with the strong empirical success of UCT, the lack of theory for simultaneous-move games, and negative convergence results for UCB in this setting (Shafiei et al.). It complements regret-minimization approaches in EFGs (e.g., CFR) by focusing on perfect-information simultaneous-move trees and simulation-based planning.

Strengths
- Novel and timely theoretical guarantee: to my knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, under broad conditions on the selection rule (any ε–HC algorithm with exploration). That’s an appealing and general bridge between online learning and MCTS.
- Clean modular analysis: the decomposition into (approximate) HC behavior in repeated matrix games, robustness to bounded payoff errors, and induction across depth is conceptually elegant and reusable.
- Subgame-perfect convergence, not just root-value convergence, strengthens the result.
- Empirical section, while modest, supports the theoretical picture and clarifies the practical tradeoff of mean vs sample backups.
- The template potentially covers many variants beyond RM/Exp3 and suggests a principled path to design convergent SM-MCTS algorithms.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Asymptotic-only: no finite-sample rates or sample complexity bounds. The constants are loose: the matrix-game result yields a 4(c+1)+δ approximate NE, and the overall bound scales quadratically in depth D (ε·D^2 + δ). It is unclear whether the D^2 factor is an artifact of proof or intrinsic.
- Proof requires mean-backup (Xh/nh), whereas most MCTS implementations propagate the last sample; empirically the latter converges slightly faster, but the theory does not cover it.
- The “complete tree” simplification in the analysis is sensible asymptotically but sidesteps important questions about tree growth, selective expansion, and how rollout noise interacts with regret updates before full coverage.
- Experiments are limited in scale and scope (random/worst-case tiny games, shallow depths, limited runs), and comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., CFR variants tailored for simultaneous-move perfect-info, recent pruning/DO methods) are minimal.
- Guaranteed exploration is assumed; while standard for HC algorithms, it can materially degrade asymptotic exploitability and practical performance, and the interplay with backpropagated noise is not deeply examined.
- Some presentation/notation issues: Theorem 4.11’s statement appears to drop the ε factor in the bound (reads as D^2 rather than ε·D^2); a few typos and incomplete references (several bib entries lack venue/year).

Quality
The theoretical development is sound in spirit and leverages well-established HC properties. The extension to bounded-error repeated games and the inductive composition are sensible; however, several constants and the D^2 scaling could be tightened, and some technical steps are deferred to the appendix. The empirical section is honest about tradeoffs but too small to fully validate practical relevance.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The algorithmic template is spelled out, and assumptions are explicit. Please fix notational slips (ε in Theorem 4.11), improve the exposition around guaranteed exploration vs on-policy exploration, and ensure all references are complete.

Originality
High. The convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS with HC selection is, to my knowledge, new and meaningful. The analysis pathway (HC → bounded-error → inductive stacking) is an elegant synthesis rather than an incremental tweak.

Significance
Moderate to high. The result gives theoretical backing to a widely used class of methods in domains with simultaneous moves and suggests a general recipe to obtain convergent planners by plugging in HC bandit/online-learning algorithms. With tighter bounds and broader experiments, this could influence future SM-MCTS design and analysis.

Questions and suggestions
- Can the D^2 dependence be reduced to O(D) with a more careful error accounting (e.g., using potential arguments or bias-variance decomposition across levels)?
- Is there a path to cover sample backups theoretically (e.g., via concentration plus bias control), or to show equivalence under additional conditions?
- Provide a finite-time bound for a simplified setting (e.g., depth-2 games) to give practitioners guidance on sample complexity.
- Analyze sensitivity to the exploration rate γ and whether annealing γ→0 preserves convergence while improving asymptotic exploitability.
- Compare against a simultaneous-move adaptation of CFR (or CFR-BR) and the alpha-beta pruning + DO baselines on slightly larger synthetic games.
- Clarify whether the convergence claim applies to empirical frequencies, average strategies, or both; Lemma 4.5 suggests they coincide asymptotically, but the final theorem statement should be precise.

Pros
- First general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS with broad selection rules.
- Conceptually elegant proof structure; subgame-perfect convergence.
- Practical guidance: mean backups suffice; RM/Exp3 are viable choices.

Cons
- Asymptotic only; loose constants and D^2 scaling.
- Requires mean backups; limited empirical validation; incomplete comparisons.
- Some presentation/notation rough edges.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper introduces a significant new theoretical direction for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, with a broadly applicable template and clear connections to online learning. Despite asymptotic-only guarantees and modest experiments, the novelty and conceptual contribution justify acceptance, and the work is likely to inspire tighter analyses and broader applications.
