### Summary

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree action selection at each node is handled by a regret-minimizing algorithm for matrix games. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if this selection algorithm is "ε-Hannan consistent" and guarantees exploration (like Regret Matching or Exp3), the resulting MCTS procedure converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively on the depth of the game tree, cleverly modeling the problem at each node as a repeated matrix game where the payoffs from child nodes are estimates with a bounded error. The authors empirically validate their framework, comparing the theoretically required version of their algorithm (which propagates mean rewards) against the more common practical version (which propagates the last sampled reward), and find that both converge, with the common version being slightly faster.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles an important and open problem. While MCTS is widely used in practice for various game types, including those with simultaneous moves, its theoretical underpinnings outside of sequential, perfect-information games like Go are weak. This work provides the first formal proof of convergence to a Nash equilibrium for this substantial class of games, which is a foundational contribution. This bridges the gap between the practical success of MCTS and the theoretical guarantees desired by the algorithmic game theory community.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and elegant. The core idea of using ε-Hannan consistency as the key property for the selection function is insightful. The inductive proof structure, which frames the problem at each level of the tree as a repeated game with bounded error propagating from the levels below, is a powerful and well-executed technique. The formal definitions and lemmas are laid out logically, building from known results in matrix games to the novel setting of a multi-stage game tree search.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It starts with a clear motivation and background, defines the problem setting precisely, and then builds its theoretical argument step-by-step. The distinction between the abstract SM-MCTS template and its specific instantiations (Regret Matching, Exp3) is helpful. The formal analysis section, despite its technical depth, is easy to follow due to the progressive introduction of concepts (matrix games -> games with error -> full extensive-form games).

4.  **Honest and Insightful Experiments:** The empirical evaluation is not just a validation of the theory but provides interesting insights. The authors directly test a key requirement of their proof—propagating the mean of the rewards—and find that it converges slightly slower than the standard method of propagating the last sample. This transparency is commendable and highlights an interesting disconnect between what is currently provable and what might be optimal in practice. The analysis on "worst-case" games further strengthens the empirical claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The main weakness is the slight disconnect between the proven algorithm and the one that performs best in practice. The proof relies on propagating the mean rewards (RMM/Exp3M), but experiments show propagating the last sample (RM/Exp3) is slightly faster. The paper conjectures that the proof could be extended, but offers no intuition as to why this is difficult or how it might be achieved. The paper would be strengthened if the authors could briefly discuss the technical challenges in analyzing the last-sample propagation variant. Is the issue that the payoff estimates `a_ij(t)` no longer converge to a stable mean, thus violating the conditions of Lemma 4.9? A short discussion on this would add significant value.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Result:** The convergence proof is asymptotic and does not provide finite-time bounds on the rate of convergence. While proving asymptotic convergence is a major first step, the practical utility of MCTS often depends on its any-time performance. The dependence of the final approximation quality on the game depth D (ε_D^2) suggests that the quality of the equilibrium could degrade substantially in deeper games. While a full finite-time analysis is likely beyond the scope of this paper, acknowledging and briefly discussing the implications of the error's dependency on depth would be beneficial.

3.  **Simplifying Assumption of a Complete Tree:** The proof begins by assuming the full game tree is available, arguing that this will happen eventually with guaranteed exploration. While this is a standard and reasonable simplification for an asymptotic proof, the argument could be made more rigorous. A sentence or two explaining why the statistics collected during the initial tree expansion phase do not bias the long-term result (e.g., because any finite number of initial samples becomes negligible as the number of iterations goes to infinity) would add confidence.

4.  **Positioning of Prior Work:** The convergence of two Hannan-consistent players to a Nash Equilibrium in a matrix game (related to Lemma 4.6) is a classic result in the online learning and game theory literature. The paper cites relevant work but could be slightly clearer in the text that this specific lemma is an adaptation of known results to their framework, serving as a building block for their main, novel contribution in the MCTS setting.

### Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first-ever proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The proof technique is novel, elegant, and could potentially be adapted to other MCTS variants or game classes.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow.
*   The experimental results are insightful, honest, and directly engage with the assumptions of the theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The proven algorithm is shown to be slightly empirically inferior to a common heuristic variant that remains unproven.
*   The analysis is purely asymptotic, offering no finite-time bounds or convergence rates, which limits its practical implications.

**Recommendation:**
This is a strong paper that makes a clear and important theoretical contribution to the field. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and mostly point toward avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The paper solidly meets the criteria of quality, clarity, originality, and significance for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
