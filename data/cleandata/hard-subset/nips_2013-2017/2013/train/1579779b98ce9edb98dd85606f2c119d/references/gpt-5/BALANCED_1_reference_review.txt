Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies two-player zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). While exact backward induction with pruning exists for this class, practical search often uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). Prior work shows UCT can fail to converge to Nash equilibrium (NE) in simultaneous-move settings. This paper proposes a general SM-MCTS template where, at each node, the selection policy is any no-regret (ε–Hannan-consistent) bandit/online-learning algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., Regret Matching (RM), Exp3). The main theoretical result shows that, if utilities are backpropagated as means (not last samples), SM-MCTS with such selection converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate NE, with an approximation error that scales with the HC parameter and the game depth D (Theorem 4.11 yields an O(D^2 ε) bound). The proof proceeds by (i) analyzing repeated matrix games under no-regret, (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded error, and (iii) inductively composing errors across depths. Empirically, they compare propagating means (RMM/Exp3M) versus last-sample values (RM/Exp3) on random and worst-case small games, observing convergence in both, with mean-propagation slightly slower.

The work connects to: (i) no-regret dynamics converging to (approximate) equilibria in zero-sum matrix games; (ii) negative results for UCT in simultaneous moves (Shafiei et al. 2009); (iii) practical SM-MCTS variants in GGP/Tron; and (iv) exact algorithms via backward induction with pruning/double oracle. To my knowledge, formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move EFGs were lacking; this paper fills that gap under clear assumptions.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: formal convergence of SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE in a well-defined but practically relevant class (zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move).
- Generality: the selection policy can be any ε–Hannan-consistent algorithm with guaranteed exploration (RM, Exp3).
- Sound proof strategy: builds from established no-regret results in repeated matrix games, extends to bounded-error settings, and composes errors inductively across the tree.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee, not just root equilibrium, is a meaningful strengthening.
- Empirical section, though modest, probes behavior of mean vs sample backpropagation and worst-case small instances.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality/technical
- Dependence on mean backpropagation: The main convergence proof requires returning mean values (SM-MCTS-M). This deviates from standard MCTS practice (last-sample backups), and the paper only provides empirical hints that last-sample variants also converge. Suggest: either (a) extend the theory to last-sample (perhaps via martingale concentration and step-size control), or (b) analyze a broader class of backpropagation schemes (e.g., exponential moving average) and delineate conditions that ensure the “bounded error” property needed in Section 4.3.
- Constants and depth dependence: The final bound scales as O(D^2 ε) (Theorem 4.11) after already incurring a factor 4(c+1) at the matrix level. This is likely very loose and may not reflect practical performance. Suggest: tighten the induction by tracking per-level errors more carefully, or show an O(D ε) bound under slightly stronger assumptions (e.g., faster convergence at leaves).
- Guaranteed exploration modification A*: In Def. 4.3, exploration steps are not used to update the learner; Lemma 4.4 claims (ε+γ)–HC still holds. This is plausible but nonstandard; please provide a succinct proof or cite a result showing that “ε–HC + independent dithering without updates” yields (ε+γ)–HC.
- Repeated games with bounded error (Def. 4.8): The assumption that after t0 all per-entry payoff errors are bounded by c almost surely is critical. Given that deeper nodes continue adapting, please clarify why the induced payoff process seen at a node becomes a bounded perturbation of a fixed matrix (uniformly in time) and how t0 is established along the induction (i.e., show that leaf convergence implies bounded error above, uniformly after some time).
- Empirical methodology: Experiments are restricted to very small randomly generated/worst-case depth-2 games with uniform exploration γ. While acceptable for an initial study, stronger evidence could include: (i) deeper trees and asymmetric branching; (ii) known benchmark simultaneous-move games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron variants) with exploitability estimates; (iii) comparisons to other baselines (e.g., simultaneous alpha-beta, double oracle) and to UCT as a performance baseline even if not theoretically convergent.

Clarity
- Minor issues: Some notation is dense; in Sec. 3.1, the update rh[i′] += x(h,i′,j) − u1 leaves j implicit—clarify the dependence on the opponent’s sampled j. Clarify the definition and usage of Xhij (is it cumulative or mean?). Define RetVal and its role earlier and unify the naming of RM/RMM.
- Proof availability: Many proofs are deferred to appendix/arXiv. Given page limits, a more detailed sketch of the key inductive step and the origin of the D^2 factor would help readers assess the tightness and necessity of assumptions.

Originality
- The theoretical guarantee for SM-MCTS in this setting appears novel and meaningful. The general “plug-in any HC algorithm” perspective is appealing and may inform other EFG classes.

Significance
- Establishes a foundation for SM-MCTS that practitioners can trust under clear conditions. However, the practical impact is somewhat tempered by the mean-backup requirement and loose asymptotic bounds; finite-time bounds would substantially increase significance.

Questions for authors
1) Can you characterize when last-sample backups fail to satisfy the “bounded error” condition, or provide a counterexample? Any path to extending the analysis to last-sample with diminishing exploration?
2) Is the D^2 dependence intrinsic to your proof technique, or can it be improved (e.g., via variance reduction, deeper coupling of levels)?
3) How sensitive are results to the exploration schedule (constant vs decaying γ)? Could decaying γ lead to better asymptotic exploitability while preserving guaranteed exploration?
4) Can you report wall-clock performance and convergence on a larger simultaneous-move benchmark?

Pros
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information zero-sum games.
- General and modular reliance on no-regret selection.
- Subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium guarantee.
- Empirical evidence broadly consistent with theory.

Cons
- Requires mean backups; no theory for standard MCTS backups.
- Loose depth-dependent bounds; no finite-time rates.
- Limited experimental scope.
- Some technical assumptions and proof dependencies need clearer exposition.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contribution is clear and fills a gap; with improved exposition and discussion of mean vs last-sample backups and tighter analysis, this could be an influential reference. My confidence is moderate.
