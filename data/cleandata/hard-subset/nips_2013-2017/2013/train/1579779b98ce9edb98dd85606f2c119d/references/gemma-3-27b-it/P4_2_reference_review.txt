This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The core idea is to frame SM-MCTS as an algorithm utilizing regret-minimizing procedures for matrix games to select actions, and demonstrate convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under specific conditions (namely, -Hannan consistency). The work is supported by empirical evaluation on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper builds upon existing MCTS literature, particularly noting the lack of theoretical guarantees for simultaneous move games, contrasting it with the well-established convergence results for sequential games. It acknowledges prior work applying MCTS to games like Goofspiel and Urban Rivals (citations [3, 4, 11, 12]), but highlights the novelty of providing convergence guarantees. The paper also references relevant work on regret minimization algorithms like regret matching [15] and Exp3 [16]. The relation to backward induction [7, 8, 9] is presented as motivation for using simulation-based methods.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The formal proof of convergence for a class of SM-MCTS algorithms is a significant contribution. This addresses a long-standing open question in the field and provides a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of these algorithms.
*   **Generality:** The framework is general, allowing for the instantiation of the MCTS template with various regret-minimizing procedures.
*   **Clear Definitions:** The paper provides clear and precise definitions of the relevant concepts, including simultaneous-move games, matrix games, and Nash equilibria.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation, although limited, provides some support for the theoretical results and demonstrates the practical applicability of the approach.
*   **Well-Structured:** The paper is well-organized and logically flows from introduction and definitions to the formal analysis and empirical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope. The study primarily focuses on randomly generated games and a few worst-case scenarios. A more thorough evaluation on a broader range of benchmark games (e.g., specific instances of Goofspiel or other relevant games) would strengthen the claims.
*   **Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves convergence, it does not provide explicit bounds on the rate of convergence. This makes it difficult to assess the practical efficiency of the algorithm.
*   **Assumptions:** The convergence guarantee relies on the -Hannan consistency of the regret-minimizing procedure. While this is a standard assumption, it's worth discussing the practical implications of this assumption and how it might be violated in real-world scenarios.
*   **Numerical Stability:** The text mentions a reformulation of the Exp3 update rule to address numerical overflow. The implications of this modification and its potential impact on the convergence analysis should be discussed more thoroughly.
*   **Connection to Existing SM-MCTS:** The paper mentions algorithms like UCB [12] that are known to be effective but don’t converge. The conjecture that the current analysis could be extended to include them warrants more discussion. What are the key challenges in extending the analysis?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of game-theoretic algorithms.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides a valuable contribution to our understanding of MCTS.
*   The generality of the framework allows for the application of various regret-minimizing procedures.
*   The empirical results, while limited, provide some support for the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope and lacks a comprehensive comparison with existing SM-MCTS algorithms.
*   The paper does not provide explicit bounds on the rate of convergence.
*   The reliance on the -Hannan consistency assumption could be a limitation in certain applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field. The convergence analysis of SM-MCTS is a valuable result that addresses a long-standing open problem. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive and the lack of convergence rate bounds is a drawback, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. The potential for extending this analysis to other SM-MCTS variants is also a promising direction for future research.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
