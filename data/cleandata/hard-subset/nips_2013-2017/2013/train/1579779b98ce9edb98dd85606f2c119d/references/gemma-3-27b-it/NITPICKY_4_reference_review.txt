This paper presents a convergence analysis for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in simultaneous-move games, a setting where theoretical guarantees are largely absent. The authors establish convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium under the assumption of using a -Hannan consistent selection function within the MCTS framework. The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond sequential games. However, the level of scrutiny applied to details, and the insistence on perfection, leads to significant concerns despite the conceptual novelty.

**Summary:**

The core idea is to frame simultaneous-move games as a series of matrix games at each state and leverage the existing theory surrounding regret minimization in matrix games (specifically, -Hannan consistency) to prove convergence of MCTS. The authors instantiate this template with Regret Matching and Exp3 and provide empirical validation. The mathematical framework is relatively thorough, and the authors attempt to connect their results to existing work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move games is genuinely novel. The connection to -Hannan consistency provides a solid foundation for proving convergence.
* **Rigorous Approach:** The authors attempt a rigorous mathematical treatment, including detailed definitions and proofs (with a promise of supplemental material).
* **Empirical Evaluation:** The inclusion of experiments, even if limited, is a positive step towards validating the theoretical results.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly defines the problem and its relevance to game theory and AI.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Overly Conservative Proof Approach & Unnecessary Abstraction:** The choice to abstract away to a "complete tree" before applying the Hannan consistency argument feels artificial and obfuscates the actual algorithm’s behavior. The authors acknowledge this, stating it will “eventually” happen, but this reliance on limiting arguments weakens the overall claim. A more direct analysis of the algorithm as it *actually* runs, with the tree growing incrementally, would be more convincing.
* **Minor Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Precision:** Numerous minor inconsistencies detract from the paper’s overall clarity. For example, the definition of *R* in Section 4.1 is ill-defined without an index *t*. Throughout the paper, the notation is occasionally ambiguous (e.g., use of 'h' for both a state *and* a utility function). These issues suggest a lack of meticulous attention to detail.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail & Scope:** The empirical evaluation is limited. The random games are not well characterized. The "worst-case" games are selected based on 100 runs – a small number for claiming identification of genuinely challenging cases. Furthermore, the comparison between RMM and RM is minimal; a more comprehensive ablation study would be beneficial. The claim that “RMM converges slightly slower” is not sufficiently substantiated.
* **Redundancy & Verbosity:** The paper is overly verbose and repetitive, particularly in the formal analysis section. Many equations are stated without sufficient motivation or explanation.
* **Missing Citations:** The claim in the introduction that UCB "does not converge to Nash equilibrium" requires a stronger citation than merely [17]. There is a body of work demonstrating the limitations of UCB, and a more comprehensive review would be expected.
* **Questionable Practical Impact:** While theoretically sound, the constant factors in the convergence guarantees (particularly the D^2 term) suggest the algorithm may be impractical for games with even moderate depth. This limitation should be discussed more openly.
* **Triviality of Worst-Case Games:** The empirically identified worst-case games, while showing convergence, are likely to be trivial in the context of real-world game-playing scenarios. The authors should acknowledge this limitation.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Novel theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS.
* Rigorous (though imperfect) mathematical analysis.
* Addresses a significant gap in the literature.

**Con:**

* Excessive reliance on abstraction and limiting arguments in the proof.
* Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies in notation and presentation.
* Limited and insufficiently detailed empirical evaluation.
* Verbosity and redundancy.
* Questionable practical applicability due to convergence rate.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, but the numerous minor flaws and the somewhat artificial nature of the proof diminish its impact. The lack of thoroughness in presentation and experimentation raises concerns about the robustness of the results. While the idea is excellent, the execution falls short of NIPS standards.

**Recommendation:**

**Reject.** 

I recommend rejection, primarily due to the combination of technical imprecision, minor errors, and the overly cautious approach to the proof that weakens the theoretical contribution. While the authors have identified a significant problem and taken a commendable step towards a solution, the current manuscript requires substantial revision to meet the quality standards expected at NIPS. Specifically, a more direct analysis of the algorithm, a more thorough empirical evaluation, and meticulous attention to detail are required.
