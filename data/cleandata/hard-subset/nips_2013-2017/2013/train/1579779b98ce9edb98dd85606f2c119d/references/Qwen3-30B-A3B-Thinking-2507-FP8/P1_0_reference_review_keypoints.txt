- Summary: Provides the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in zero-sum simultaneous-move extensive-form games via a template using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions.

- Strength 1: Addresses a significant theoretical gap by proving convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.

- Strength 2: Presents a rigorous, well-structured formal analysis with clear definitions, lemmas, and proofs.

- Strength 3: Properly situates the work within prior literature on Hannan consistency, regret minimization, and MCTS.

- Strength 4: Empirical validation supports theoretical claims and compares mean propagation variants against others on worst-case games.

- Strength 5: Paper is well-written, logically organized, and accessible to readers familiar with game theory and online learning.

- Weakness 1: No finite-time convergence rates or bounds are provided, limiting practical guidance on iterations needed.

- Weakness 2: Empirical evaluation is limited to small games (depth 2, branching factor 2) and lacks scalability evidence.

- Weakness 3: Limited discussion of practical implementation issues, such as choice of exploration parameter γ and adaptations for real-world use.

- Weakness 4: Results are restricted to zero-sum games with no extension or discussion for non-zero-sum settings.

- Weakness 5: Lacks direct empirical comparison with recent state-of-the-art MCTS variants for simultaneous-move games.

- Specific Comment 1: The D² + δ bound in Theorem 4.11 is loose; authors should comment on tightness or potential improvement.

- Specific Comment 2: No theoretical explanation for why mean propagation variants (RMM) converge slightly slower in practice.

- Specific Comment 3: Figure 3’s worst-case games lack detailed analysis explaining why they cause slow convergence.

- Specific Comment 4: Future work on finite-time bounds is mentioned only in the conclusion and should be emphasized in the main text.

- Specific Comment 5: Related work section should better distinguish this work from other MCTS advances for simultaneous-move games (e.g., Shafiei et al.).

- Overall Assessment: Strong theoretical contribution with solid proofs and supportive experiments, despite empirical and practical limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept (Strong Accept).

- Pro-Accept Arguments: First formal convergence guarantees; rigorous analysis; clear ties to regret minimization; supportive empirical results; well-written and significant contribution.

- Con-Accept Arguments: Missing finite-time bounds; limited empirical scope on larger games; no non-zero-sum extension; insufficient practical implementation discussion.
