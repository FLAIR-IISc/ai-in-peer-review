Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template whose selection step at each node is any ε–Hannan-consistent procedure for the local matrix game (e.g., regret matching or Exp3). The main theoretical result shows almost-sure convergence to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium when (i) all joint actions are explored infinitely often and (ii) backpropagated values are empirical means rather than single-sample returns. The analysis proceeds by: (1) repeated matrix games with HC learners; (2) repeated matrix games with bounded approximation error; and (3) an induction over tree depth that accumulates error, yielding a bound scaling with depth (reported as O(D^2 ε)). Experiments on random small games and worst-case hand-picked shallow games compare RM/Exp3 with and without mean backpropagation, finding similar asymptotic behavior but somewhat slower convergence with mean returns.

Strengths
- Addresses an understudied setting: simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs. To my knowledge, this is among the first asymptotic convergence guarantees for MCTS-like methods in this class.
- Clean, modular proof strategy that builds on established online learning tools (Hannan consistency) and composes them over the tree via bounded-error lemmas.
- The requirement that selection be ε–HC aligns with well-studied algorithms (regret matching, Exp3), grounding the approach in established methods.
- The result is subgame-perfect, not just at the root, which strengthens the game-theoretic interpretation.
- The paper is generally clearly written; main assumptions and algorithmic template are stated; empirical section aligns qualitatively with the theory (mean-backprop slows convergence slightly).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Asymptotic only, with very loose depth dependence. The bound appears to scale quadratically with depth (D^2 ε) and includes additional constants (e.g., 4(c+1) in intermediate results). This is unlikely to be useful in practice and is not compared to alternative solvers (backward induction with LP/DO pruning) or to CFR variants that have known convergence in EFGs.
- Crucial dependence on mean backpropagation and uniform exploration. Standard MCTS (and most deployed SM-MCTS) propagates the most recent sample and often decays exploration. The theory discards these practical choices and instead assumes guaranteed, non-vanishing exploration and mean returns. This undermines the claim that the results justify practical MCTS use; at minimum, the algorithmic gap to common practice should be discussed more rigorously.
- The analysis assumes the full tree is eventually built and claims that early statistics “cannot prevent eventual convergence.” This is a strong asymptotic idealization. A formal statement and proof of how partial-tree growth and expansion policies interact with selection to preserve the assumptions should be included in the main text, not deferred.
- Experimental evaluation is too limited to support practical significance. Games are tiny (depth ≤ 2, branching ≤ 2–4, payoffs {0, 0.5, 1}) with at most 10^6 iterations; no results on benchmark simultaneous-move domains (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion), and no comparisons to established equilibrium solvers or to CFR(-B/+) baselines adapted to simultaneous moves. No finite-time behavior characterization.
- Some technical details are under-specified or unconventional. For regret matching, the update uses x(h,i′,j) formed from current estimates Xhij for unplayed joint actions; potential bias and variance implications are not discussed. For Exp3, actions are chosen independently per player; joint selection effects and the robustness of importance weighting under this coupling deserve more careful treatment.
- Clarity issues and constants: The main theorem’s dependence on ε and D is not stated cleanly in the body (notation “(D2 + δ)-NE” is ambiguous). The origins and tightness of constants (2(c+1), 4+δ) could be better motivated. Several results critical to the flow are deferred to the appendix.

Relation to prior work
The paper appropriately cites UCT’s failure in simultaneous games and prior SM-MCTS applications. However, comparison to CFR-style methods (which are established in EFGs and have convergence guarantees in zero-sum settings) is minimal; a stronger positioning relative to CFR/CFR+ (and recent variants) is needed. Likewise, discussion of alpha-beta with LP/DO pruning as a baseline is cursory; practical tradeoffs should be empirically examined.

Questions for the authors
- Can the mean-backprop requirement be relaxed? Is there a counterexample showing that single-sample backpropagation breaks the analysis?
- Can exploration be decayed without losing convergence (e.g., γt → 0)? If yes, what changes in the proof and constants?
- What is the precise dependence on ε and D in Theorem 4.11? Please state an explicit bound in the main text with all constants.
- How does this approach compare empirically to CFR variants on standard simultaneous-move benchmarks in terms of exploitability vs. iterations/wall-clock?
- For RM updates using x(h,i′,j) built from Xhij, can you bound the bias/variance and show it fits the bounded-error model with explicit c(d) as a function of depth?

Pros
- Conceptually clean use of HC learners to obtain asymptotic subgame-perfect convergence.
- Addresses a gap in theory for SM-MCTS.
- Results are general over the choice of ε–HC selection algorithms.

Cons
- Strong assumptions (mean returns, perpetual exploration, full tree) diverge from practice.
- Weak, asymptotic-only bounds with poor scaling; no finite-time rates.
- Experiments are too small and omit key baselines and benchmarks.
- Several technical details and constants lack clarity in the main text.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline)
The theoretical contribution is interesting, but the current form lacks practical relevance and rigorous empirical validation. Tightening the bounds, clarifying constants, relaxing assumptions toward standard SM-MCTS, and adding substantive experiments against established baselines on recognized domains would significantly strengthen the paper.

Confidence: High
