## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the theoretical convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move extensive-form games.  The authors establish a general template for SM-MCTS, demonstrating that using a -Hannan consistent regret minimizing procedure for action selection guarantees convergence to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. They instantiate this template with Regret Matching and Exp3, empirically validating their claims on randomly generated and worst-case games, finding performance comparable to existing SM-MCTS algorithms.  The core contribution lies in providing a theoretical foundation for SM-MCTS, a previously largely empirically-driven area, and identifying a crucial condition ( -Hannan consistency) for convergence.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the established success of MCTS in sequential games (e.g., Go) and the recent algorithmic advances in game theory, particularly concerning security applications and large games. It addresses a significant gap in the literature: the lack of convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. Existing approaches to simultaneous-move games often rely on backward induction with computationally expensive domain knowledge or linear programming, motivating the study of MCTS as a practical alternative. The authors correctly position their work against previous SM-MCTS implementations (e.g., [10, 11, 12]) which lacked formal convergence proofs, and contrast it with UCB-based methods proven to *not* converge [17].  The work also draws heavily from the online learning literature, specifically -Hannan consistency [15, 16] to provide the theoretical basis for their convergence argument.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of the paper is the rigorous proof of convergence for a general SM-MCTS template. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for a technique previously used primarily empirically.
* **Generality:**  The framework is general, allowing for instantiation with any -Hannan consistent regret minimizing procedure. This broadens the applicability of the results.
* **Clear Presentation of Theory:**  The theoretical section is well-structured and explains the necessary concepts ( -Hannan consistency, subgame perfect equilibrium) clearly.  The use of lemmas and a stepwise inductive proof enhances clarity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, while not groundbreaking, provide support for the theoretical claims and demonstrate competitive performance with existing methods.  The inclusion of worst-case game experiments is particularly valuable.
* **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their analysis (e.g., not addressing UCB-based methods) and suggest avenues for future work.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Convergence Rate:** The theoretical guarantees establish convergence to an -Nash equilibrium, but do not provide any bounds on the *rate* of convergence.  The empirical section offers some insight into this, but a more detailed analysis of convergence speed would be beneficial.
* **Practical Relevance of ε:** The -Nash equilibrium guarantees rely on the parameter ε, which represents the acceptable deviation from optimal play. While theoretically sound, the practical implications of choosing a specific ε value, and its impact on algorithm performance, are not discussed.
* **Complexity of the Proof:** While clear in its structure, the length and technical detail of the proof might make it challenging for readers outside of the theoretical game theory/online learning communities to fully grasp.
* **Limited Novelty in Experiments:** The experimental results demonstrate good performance, but do not showcase any significant advantages over existing SM-MCTS algorithms.  Exploring more complex game scenarios or comparing against stronger baselines would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
* **Minor Formatting Issues:**  The paper contains minor formatting inconsistencies, especially in equations and references. (e.g. reference [17] is not consistently formatted.)



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a crucial theoretical foundation for a widely used but previously unanalyzed algorithm (SM-MCTS).
* The generality of the framework and the rigorous proof of convergence are significant contributions.
* The empirical results support the theoretical claims.
* Addresses a key open question in the field of game playing.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The convergence rate is not analyzed, limiting the practical utility of the theoretical results.
* The empirical evaluation lacks novelty and doesn't demonstrate substantial improvements over existing methods.
* The complexity of the proof might hinder accessibility for some readers.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field.  The theoretical results are significant, and the paper provides a valuable foundation for future research on SM-MCTS.  While the lack of convergence rate analysis and limited novelty in the empirical section are weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  The strengths of the paper – particularly the rigorous proof of convergence – outweigh the weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend that the authors consider adding a discussion of the practical implications of the ε parameter and explore avenues for analyzing the convergence rate of the algorithm. Addressing the minor formatting issues would also be beneficial.
