

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for SM-MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. Their main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the algorithm uses an ε-Hannan consistent selection function (which ensures all actions are attempted infinitely often), the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This is a substantial theoretical advance, as prior to this work, no convergence guarantees existed for MCTS in simultaneous move games or general extensive-form games.

The paper is well-structured and builds a strong theoretical foundation. The authors carefully define the problem space, provide necessary background on matrix games and extensive-form games, and then develop their convergence proof through a series of well-motivated lemmas and theorems. The key insight is that by using Hannan-consistent algorithms (like regret matching or Exp3) for action selection, the MCTS algorithm will eventually converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds by induction over the game tree depth, showing that convergence in subgames implies convergence in higher-level games.

The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical contributions well. The authors compare two variants of their approach (propagating mean values vs. current sample values) and evaluate convergence on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios. The results show that the mean-propagation variant (which is required for the theoretical guarantees) converges slightly slower than the current-sample variant, but both reach similar equilibrium quality. The experiments also provide evidence that other MCTS variants (not covered by their proof) likely converge to approximate Nash equilibria, supporting the authors' conjecture.

The paper makes a strong case for its novelty and significance. The authors clearly articulate the gap in the literature: while MCTS variants like UCT have been successfully applied to simultaneous move games in practice, no convergence guarantees existed. This theoretical foundation is crucial for practitioners who rely on MCTS in applications like security games, general game-playing, and other domains where simultaneous move games are common. The paper directly addresses a fundamental question that has been overlooked in the literature, making it a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical novelty and boldness in providing convergence guarantees for a class of games where none existed before. While the empirical results are somewhat expected (they confirm the theoretical claims), they provide valuable validation and show that the theoretical approach aligns with practical performance. The paper is well-written, with clear definitions and a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses. The convergence rate analysis is limited to asymptotic guarantees without providing finite-time bounds, which is acknowledged by the authors. The empirical analysis focuses primarily on the regret matching variant, with less exploration of the Exp3 variant's performance characteristics. However, these are relatively minor concerns given the paper's primary contribution.

In terms of significance, this work has the potential to influence both the theoretical understanding and practical application of MCTS in a wide range of games. By providing a formal justification for using MCTS in simultaneous move games, the paper bridges a critical gap between theoretical game theory and practical algorithm design. This is particularly valuable for researchers and practitioners working on game-theoretic AI applications.

The paper is clearly original, as the authors demonstrate through a thorough literature review that no prior work has established convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games. The related work section appropriately cites key contributions to MCTS in sequential games, security games, and game-theoretic algorithms, making it clear how this work extends the state of the art.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The authors present a bold approach that addresses a fundamental gap in the literature, providing a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with various regret-minimizing procedures. While the empirical results are somewhat expected given the theoretical guarantees, they provide valuable validation. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold theoretical contributions, I believe this paper makes a substantial advance in understanding MCTS in a previously unanalyzed class of games. The theoretical insights are significant, and the paper provides a foundation for future work on finite-time convergence bounds and extensions to more complex game structures.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper for NIPS. The novelty of the theoretical contribution outweighs the minor limitations in empirical analysis, and the paper makes a clear, important advance in the field.
