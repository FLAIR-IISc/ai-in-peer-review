

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for simultaneous move games, a class of two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games, using any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure for matrix games as the action selection function. They prove that if the selection function satisfies ε-Hannan consistency (ensuring all actions are attempted infinitely often), the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper complements this with empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection procedures, demonstrating convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in both randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The authors provide a rigorous formal proof of convergence, establishing a clear connection between regret minimization in repeated matrix games and MCTS convergence in extensive-form games. The proof is structured inductively, starting from leaf nodes (simple matrix games) and moving up the game tree, with careful handling of error bounds in each level. The lemmas and theorems are well-motivated and logically connected. The empirical evaluation is appropriate, using exploitability as the measure of convergence to Nash equilibrium and comparing two variants of the algorithm (mean propagation vs. current sample value). The authors acknowledge limitations in the empirical scope (small game sizes) while providing meaningful results that support their theoretical claims. The paper is a complete contribution, not merely a position paper, and the authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The definitions, background, and formal analysis are presented with mathematical precision and clarity. The authors effectively use figures to illustrate key concepts (e.g., the structure of stacked matrix games) and present empirical results in a clear, visual manner. The notation is consistent throughout, and the connection between the formal analysis and the practical implementation is well-explained. The appendix provides additional proofs that support the main results, making the paper sufficiently detailed for an expert to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The paper makes a novel contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While MCTS has been successfully applied to these games in practice, the lack of theoretical guarantees has been a recognized gap. The authors' approach of using ε-Hannan consistent selection functions to ensure convergence is both novel and generalizable, as it can be instantiated with any such procedure. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work on MCTS in sequential games (e.g., UCT) and from regret minimization in repeated games. The authors demonstrate thorough knowledge of related work in game theory and MCTS, citing relevant papers from NIPS, AAAI, and other venues.

**Significance**: The results have important theoretical and practical significance. The paper establishes a formal foundation for using MCTS as a reliable approximation algorithm for simultaneous move games, which are prevalent in applications like security domains and card games. The empirical results showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in practice provide valuable validation for the theoretical claims. The work bridges a critical gap between theoretical guarantees and practical algorithms in algorithmic game theory. The authors also identify promising directions for future work, including finite-time convergence bounds and generalizations to imperfect information games.

**Strengths**: 
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Rigorous and well-structured theoretical analysis
- Clear connection between regret minimization and MCTS convergence
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation supporting theoretical claims
- Well-written with excellent organization and notation

**Weaknesses**: 
- Limited empirical evaluation on larger games (depth 2, branching factor 2)
- Does not provide a tight bound on convergence rate
- Does not explicitly compare with other state-of-the-art algorithms for simultaneous move games
- Focuses narrowly on zero-sum perfect information games without discussing potential extensions

**Arguments for acceptance**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a well-motivated problem in algorithmic game theory. The formal analysis is sound, the empirical results support the claims, and the work is clearly positioned within the relevant literature. The results have both theoretical importance and practical implications for applications using MCTS in simultaneous move games.

**Arguments against acceptance**: The empirical evaluation is limited to small games, which might not capture the full complexity of real-world applications. The paper does not provide a tight convergence rate bound, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal analysis is rigorous and well-executed, and the empirical results provide meaningful validation. While the empirical scope is limited, the theoretical contribution stands on its own merits. The paper addresses a well-defined gap in the literature and provides a valuable foundation for future work in this area. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical content accessible to the NIPS audience. The work advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and provides a solid theoretical basis for the practical use of MCTS in simultaneous move games.
