### Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. While MCTS and its variants are widely applied to various games, including those with simultaneous moves, formal guarantees of their convergence to a Nash Equilibrium (NE) have been lacking for this class of games. The authors present a general template for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (SM-MCTS). The core theoretical contribution is a proof that if the action selection mechanism within this template is an ε-Hannan consistent procedure with guaranteed exploration, then the overall MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash Equilibrium. The authors connect the problem to the online learning literature, leveraging concepts of regret minimization. They instantiate their framework with two well-known regret-minimizing algorithms, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical analysis on random and worst-case games, which demonstrates convergence and interestingly shows that the variant required for the proof (propagating mean values) converges slightly slower than the more standard approach of propagating the current sample's value.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and importance. It provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This is a foundational result that legitimizes the use of MCTS as a principled approximation algorithm in this domain, moving it beyond a purely heuristic approach.

2.  **Elegant and General Proof Structure:** The analytical approach is sound and well-constructed. The key insight is to model the decision-making process at each node as a repeated matrix game where the payoffs (from child subgames) are noisy estimates. By proving that ε-Hannan consistency is robust to this bounded error (Lemma 4.9), the authors build an elegant inductive argument over the depth of the game tree. The generality of the result, which applies to any ε-Hannan consistent selection function, is a major advantage over a proof tailored to a specific algorithm like Regret Matching.

3.  **Clear Connection to Established Fields:** The paper successfully bridges the gap between MCTS search algorithms and the theory of online learning and regret minimization. This connection is not only crucial for the proof but also provides a clear and powerful lens through which to understand and design future MCTS variants for this game class.

4.  **Honest and Insightful Empirical Analysis:** The experiments are well-designed to support the theoretical claims. Crucially, the authors are transparent about the performance of the variant required for their proof (using mean-value backpropagation, RMM) versus the standard approach (using the sample value, RM). The finding that RMM is slightly slower is an important practical detail and demonstrates a high degree of scientific integrity. The investigation into worst-case games further strengthens the empirical validation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Results:** The convergence guarantees are asymptotic (as the number of iterations approaches infinity). The paper does not provide finite-time bounds on the rate of convergence. While this is a common first step for such theoretical results, the lack of a rate makes it difficult to know how many iterations are needed to achieve a desired ε-NE for a given game. The authors acknowledge this as future work.

2.  **Dependence of the Equilibrium Bound on Game Depth:** The final approximation bound (ε') for the subgame perfect ε'-NE is shown to be a function of the game depth D (O(D²)). For games with substantial depth, this bound could become quite loose, potentially limiting the practical implications of the guarantee. A discussion on the tightness of this bound would be beneficial.

3.  **Simplification of Tree Expansion:** The proof simplifies the analysis by assuming a complete game tree and showing that this state is reached with probability 1. While this is a reasonable simplification for an asymptotic proof, it sidesteps the analysis of the bias introduced by the statistics collected during the initial, incomplete tree expansion phase. A more detailed argument for why this initial phase does not prevent eventual convergence would strengthen the claim.

**Detailed Evaluation against NIPS Criteria:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound, leveraging established concepts in a novel way. The claims are well-supported by both the formal proofs (outlined in the paper and detailed in the appendix) and the targeted experimental results. The work is complete and the authors are careful to evaluate the practical trade-offs of their theoretically-motivated algorithm variant (RMM).

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem is motivated well in the introduction, the necessary background and notation are defined concisely, and the structure of the formal analysis is explained before diving into the lemmas. The distinction between the general SM-MCTS template and its specific instantiations (RM, Exp3) is clear. The experimental section is also easy to follow.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence to a Nash equilibrium for MCTS in this class of games. The technique of using Hannan consistency and analyzing repeated games with bounded errors as an inductive step in the game tree is a novel and powerful contribution. The work is well-situated within the existing literature, citing both practical MCTS applications and prior theoretical work on exact solvers.

*   **Significance:** The results are highly significant for the NIPS community, particularly for researchers in reinforcement learning, planning, and algorithmic game theory. This paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms in a domain where such foundations were previously lacking. It is very likely that other researchers will build upon these ideas, for example, by seeking to establish finite-time bounds, extending the analysis to imperfect information games, or designing new selection mechanisms with better theoretical properties. It demonstrably advances the state of the art in understanding simulation-based game-playing algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first, much-needed proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
*   The result is general, applying to any selection function with ε-Hannan consistency.
*   The proof technique, bridging MCTS with online learning theory, is novel and elegant.
*   The paper is well-written, and its claims are supported by solid theory and honest empirical evaluation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The convergence analysis is purely asymptotic, lacking finite-time rates.
*   The resulting equilibrium approximation quality depends on the depth of the game, which may be loose for deep games.

**Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a foundational theoretical contribution to the understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are largely avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a significant open problem. I strongly recommend acceptance.
