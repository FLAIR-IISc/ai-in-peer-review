

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a critical theoretical gap in the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) to simultaneous move games, a class of games that includes many practical applications like card games and general game-playing. The authors present a formal convergence analysis for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, which is a significant theoretical contribution as prior work lacked convergence guarantees for MCTS in this setting.

The main contribution is a general template for MCTS algorithms that uses any ε-Hannan consistent regret minimization procedure for action selection. The authors prove that when using such a procedure, MCTS converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. They provide a rigorous proof by first establishing convergence results for matrix games with bounded error, then extending these results to the multi-stage extensive-form game setting through induction. The empirical evaluation demonstrates that their approach using regret matching and Exp3 converges to approximate Nash equilibria, with the mean-propagation variant (RMM) showing slightly slower convergence than the current-sample variant (RM), though both converge.

**Strengths:**
1. **High novelty**: The paper fills a critical gap in the literature. Prior work on MCTS has focused on sequential games (e.g., Go, Chess), but simultaneous move games are common in applications like Goofspiel, Urban Rivals, and general game-playing. The authors' connection between MCTS and Hannan consistency is a fresh perspective that opens new theoretical avenues.

2. **Theoretical contribution**: The formal convergence proof is technically sound and well-structured, building from matrix games to extensive-form games through a clear inductive argument. The proof techniques (Hannan consistency, regret minimization) are well-chosen and appropriate for the problem.

3. **Clear motivation**: The paper effectively establishes why simultaneous move games matter and why existing MCTS variants lack theoretical foundations.

4. **Complementary empirical analysis**: The experiments validate the theoretical claims, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria in practice, and provide useful insights into the propagation of mean values versus current samples.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Limited practical implications**: While the paper establishes asymptotic convergence, it doesn't provide finite-time convergence bounds or analyze how the convergence rate compares to state-of-the-art MCTS variants in practical settings. This limits the immediate utility for practitioners.

2. **Narrow empirical scope**: The empirical analysis focuses on small games (depth 2) and random game generation. It would be stronger to include larger, more complex games or real-world applications.

3. **Conjecture about other algorithms**: The authors conjecture that many existing MCTS algorithms for simultaneous move games also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium, but provide no evidence or analysis to support this beyond the empirical results for their specific variants.

**Pro and Con for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, addressing a significant theoretical gap.
- Novel connection between MCTS and Hannan consistency in matrix games.
- Well-structured theoretical analysis with rigorous proofs.
- Empirical results support the theoretical claims.
- Provides a solid foundation for future work on MCTS in simultaneous move games.

*Con:*
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds, which would strengthen the practical relevance.
- Limited empirical evaluation on complex or real-world games.
- Does not fully address how this approach compares to existing MCTS variants in terms of practical performance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a class of games where such guarantees were previously unknown. While the paper doesn't provide finite-time bounds or extensive empirical evaluation on large-scale games, the novelty of the theoretical contribution far outweighs these limitations in my view. The connection between MCTS and Hannan consistency is particularly insightful and opens new directions for theoretical analysis of MCTS in game-theoretic settings. 

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a strong case for its theoretical contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper's theoretical contribution to be significant and impactful for the field. It advances our understanding of MCTS in a practically important setting and provides a foundation for future work. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened, the theoretical contribution is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

The paper's focus on a well-motivated problem with a clear theoretical gap, combined with its novel approach and sound analysis, makes it a strong candidate for NeurIPS. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to the theoretical foundations of MCTS that will likely influence future research in game-theoretic AI.
