This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses, at each node, a selection rule that is ε-Hannan consistent in the corresponding stage matrix game and satisfies guaranteed exploration. They prove eventual convergence to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with an error that scales (roughly) as ε·D^2 + δ for game depth D (Theorem 4.11), via an induction over depth that leverages results on repeated matrix games with bounded observation error. Empirically, they instantiate the template with regret matching and Exp3, evaluate on random and worst-case small games, and study the effect of mean vs. sample backups. The work connects bandit/online learning guarantees (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) with SM-MCTS, complements negative results for UCB in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.), and relates to prior MCTS work (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and algorithmic game solving (e.g., double oracle; alpha–beta for simultaneous moves).

Quality: The theoretical development is sound and well-motivated: ε-HC implies low regret in leaves; bounded-error lemmas lift guarantees up the tree. Assumptions (guaranteed exploration, eventual full-tree expansion, mean backups) are clearly stated, and the effect of exploration on regret is analyzed. Experiments corroborate asymptotic convergence and compare selection rules, though they are limited in scale.

Clarity: Generally clear, with precise definitions and a readable proof sketch; however, the mean-backup requirement and its relationship to practical SM-MCTS variants could be emphasized earlier, and the dependence of the final error bound on ε and D should be stated unambiguously. Minor notation and algorithmic presentation could be streamlined.

Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first convergence analysis of MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, bridging Hannan consistency with tree search. This general template and the bounded-error composition argument are novel in this context.

Significance: Establishing convergence of widely used SM-MCTS variants is important for both theory and practice. That said, the result is asymptotic with loose D^2 dependence and no finite-time rates; experiments are small-scale and do not compare against CFR-style baselines in simultaneous-move settings.

Pros for acceptance:
- First general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under broad selection rules.
- Clean linkage between online learning and game-tree search.
- Subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium guarantee.
- Empirical confirmation across two canonical HC algorithms.

Cons against acceptance:
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time bounds and loose D^2 factor.
- Requires mean backups and explicit exploration; practical impact and trade-offs underexplored.
- Experimental evaluation is modest in scale and breadth.
