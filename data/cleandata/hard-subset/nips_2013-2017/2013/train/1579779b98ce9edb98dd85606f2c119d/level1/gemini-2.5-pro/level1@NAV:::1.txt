This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the literature on Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for selecting actions within a node. The central theoretical contribution is proving that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent (such as Regret Matching or Exp3) and guarantees exploration, the MCTS algorithm's average strategy converges to a subgame perfect approximate Nash Equilibrium. This work builds upon established results in online learning for matrix games and extends them to the more complex setting of extensive-form games, where node payoffs are stochastic estimates from subgame simulations. This provides a rigorous foundation for using MCTS in this game class, moving beyond the purely empirical justifications and known failures of UCT-based approaches seen in prior work.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality & Significance:** The primary strength is the novelty and importance of the main theoretical result. Proving convergence for MCTS in this setting is a long-standing open problem, and this paper provides a clear and valuable solution. It successfully bridges the fields of online learning and tree search, providing a principled framework for algorithm design. The findings are highly significant for researchers and practitioners in areas like general game playing and security games, who can now employ MCTS with formal performance guarantees.
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proof is built logically, starting from known results in repeated matrix games and extending them to games with noisy payoffs (representing subgames) and finally to the full extensive-form game via induction. The authors are careful to state their assumptions, such as the requirement to backpropagate mean rewards for the proof to hold. The experimental section is thorough, corroborating the theory and providing insightful comparisons between the provably convergent variants and their more heuristic counterparts.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity of Theoretical Bound:** The convergence guarantee in Theorem 4.11 depends on the game's depth (D^2). This bound might be quite loose for deep games, and the paper would benefit from a brief discussion on its potential tightness and practical implications.
*   **Practical vs. Theoretical Variants:** The experiments show that the variant required for the proof (propagating mean values) converges slightly slower than the more standard approach of propagating the last sample's value. While this is an honest and interesting result, it slightly tempers the immediate practical applicability of the provably correct version.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** Solves a major open theoretical problem. The contribution is novel, significant, and technically solid. The paper is well-written and supported by strong empirical evidence.
*   **Con:** The proven convergence bound may be loose, and the provably correct algorithm is slightly slower in practice.

In summary, this is an excellent paper presenting a foundational theoretical result. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. It represents a clear and important advance for the field, and I strongly recommend acceptance.
