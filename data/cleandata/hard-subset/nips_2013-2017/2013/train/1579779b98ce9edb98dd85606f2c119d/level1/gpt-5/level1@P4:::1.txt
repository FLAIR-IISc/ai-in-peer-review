Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where, at each state, the joint action is sampled using a selection procedure instantiated by a no-regret (ε–Hannan-consistent) algorithm (e.g., regret matching or Exp3). The main theoretical result shows that if the selection procedure is ε–Hannan-consistent and ensures exploration so that all joint actions are sampled infinitely often, then SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε·D^2–Nash equilibrium (up to an arbitrarily small δ), where D is the game-tree depth. The proof proceeds by: (i) analyzing repeated matrix games, (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) inducting over the tree depth, noting that errors accumulate across levels. A key technical requirement for the proof is to back up mean returns rather than single-sample returns.

Empirically, the authors evaluate the template instantiated with regret matching and Exp3 on random games and on empirically identified difficult (worst-case) depth-2 games. They compare using mean backups (required by the theory) vs single-sample backups (standard in practice), and observe that both variants appear to converge to approximate NE, with mean backups converging slightly more slowly.

This work connects established no-regret learning in repeated games (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) with MCTS (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and addresses a gap noted by prior work showing UCT may not converge in simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al.). It complements exact backward induction and pruning approaches for simultaneous-move games (Saffidine et al.; Bošanský et al.) by giving sampling-based convergence guarantees.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Theoretical results: The reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated matrix games with bounded error is clearly articulated. The use of ε–Hannan consistency to bound exploitability via best-response inequalities is standard. The extension to “games with error” is reasonable, and the induction argument over depth explains the D^2 dependence. The proof relies on guaranteed exploration to ensure every joint action is sampled infinitely often; the A* modification is appropriate and preserves approximate no-regret up to +γ. The lemma that empirical frequencies converge to average strategies with probability 1 is plausible given martingale SLLN/LLN arguments.
- Assumptions and limitations: The convergence proof requires mean backups, while standard MCTS uses single-sample backups; the experiments suggest the latter may also converge, but without guarantees. The constants are loose (D^2 factor and “4(c+1)” in the matrix-game stage), and no finite-sample rates are provided. The setting is restricted to two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves.
- Experiments: The experiments validate qualitative convergence and compare backup variants, including worst-case searches over small game spaces. They are limited in scale and do not provide statistical confidence intervals or comprehensive sensitivity analyses (e.g., exploration rate schedules, branching/depth scaling). Nonetheless, they support the main claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized: background and notation, algorithmic template, selection methods, theory (matrix games → errors → tree), and experiments. A few presentation issues could be improved:
- Algorithm 1 formatting is hard to follow (line breaks, indices, and return values). A cleaner pseudocode with explicit per-player updates and a precise definition of RetVal would help.
- Some typographical issues: e.g., “oth.” in Eq. (3); “wh,i = xh,i1” likely a typesetting artifact; clarify that wh,i is stabilized by subtracting max to avoid overflow.
- The “guaranteed exploration” definition and its satisfaction by independent ε-greedy (or γ) exploration could be more explicitly justified (e.g., via Borel–Cantelli).
- Clarify the role of A* (off-policy exploration without updating) vs the on-policy γ exploration used in experiments.

Originality
To the best of knowledge as of NIPS 2013, this is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games. The synthesis of no-regret learning with MCTS in this setting, and the analysis via repeated games with bounded error, appears novel. Prior MCTS work either targets sequential perfect-information games or lacks guarantees in simultaneous-move settings; the paper directly addresses this gap.

Significance
The result provides a theoretical foundation for using SM-MCTS in domains with simultaneous moves (e.g., Goofspiel variants, pursuit-evasion, GGP benchmarks). While the asymptotic nature, the mean-backup requirement, and the loose bounds limit immediate practical impact, establishing convergence is an important step. The approach is likely to influence algorithm design and analysis for broader EFG classes and motivate work on finite-time bounds and practical variants.

Strengths
- Clear and general convergence theorem for SM-MCTS with ε–Hannan-consistent selection.
- Sound proof structure using repeated games with bounded error and induction over depth.
- Connection to well-known no-regret algorithms (regret matching, Exp3).
- Empirical study confirms qualitative convergence and compares backup variants.
- Subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium guarantee.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical guarantees require mean backups; standard single-sample backups are not covered.
- Asymptotic, loose bounds (O(D^2)); no finite-time rates or dependence on branching factor.
- Experiments are limited to small/random games and shallow worst-case instances; no large or canonical benchmarks (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel) to demonstrate scalability.
- Some presentation/notation issues and minor typos; algorithm description could be clearer.
- Exploration treatment (on-policy vs A*) and its effect on bias and convergence constants could be elaborated.

Arguments for acceptance
- First rigorous convergence analysis for SM-MCTS; fills a recognized theoretical gap.
- General template applicable to multiple selection algorithms with no-regret guarantees.
- Subgame-perfect convergence adds strength to the result.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory and informs practical choices (mean vs sample backups).

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical variant commonly used (sample backups) lacks theoretical coverage.
- No finite-time analysis; bounds are too loose to guide practice.
- Experimental evaluation is modest in scope and omits larger, real-game benchmarks.
- Some clarity issues in exposition/pseudocode.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide finite-sample convergence rates (even conservative) in terms of ε, D, branching, and γ.
- Tighten constants in the depth-wise induction or explore variance-reduced backups to improve bounds.
- Extend theory to single-sample backups or characterize conditions under which they are safe.
- Evaluate on larger structured games (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel) and report scaling with depth/branching.
- Clarify exploration schemes, their induced bias, and recommended γ schedules (possibly decaying).
- Improve algorithm presentation and fix typographical issues.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and original asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS with no-regret selection, with reasonable empirical support, and addresses an important gap in the MCTS literature for simultaneous-move games.
