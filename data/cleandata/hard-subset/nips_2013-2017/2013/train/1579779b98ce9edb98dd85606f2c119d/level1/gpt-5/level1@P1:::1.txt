Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which each node applies a no-regret (ε–Hannan consistent) selection algorithm for the local matrix game, with explicit exploration. The main theoretical result shows that if the selection method is ε–Hannan consistent and guarantees visiting every action pair infinitely often (e.g., through uniform exploration), then SM-MCTS converges (almost surely, in the limit) to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The asymptotic error scales with game depth D as O(ε D^2) (Theorem 4.11), with constants derived via intermediate results on repeated matrix games with bounded error. The analysis requires returning mean values up the tree (as opposed to immediate sample values). Empirically, using regret matching and Exp3 as the node selection algorithms, the paper confirms convergence on random and empirically hard small games, and compares “mean propagation” vs “sample propagation,” noting a small slowdown with mean propagation.

This work fills a gap: while UCT is well-studied in sequential perfect-information games, it is known not to converge to NE in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al. 2011). Prior exact methods (backward induction with LP or double oracle/pruning) are expensive; sampling methods such as CFR/MCCFR provide convergence guarantees in general EFGs but are not the same as SM-MCTS used in practice in simultaneous-move settings. The contribution provides the first convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate NE in this class, leveraging no-regret learning (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.; Blum & Mansour).

Quality:
- Theory: The proof strategy is sound and standard in spirit: (i) show that ε–HC dynamics converge to approximate NE in matrix games; (ii) handle bounded observation errors; (iii) propagate error up the tree, using induction over depth and guaranteed exploration to ensure sufficient visitation; (iv) conclude subgame-perfect approximate NE. The lemmas are plausible and appropriately cited; missing details are deferred to the appendix. Two concerns: (1) the D^2 dependence and multiplicative constants (e.g., 4(c+1)+δ) appear loose; a clearer, tight accounting of how c(d) grows with depth would strengthen the result and might reduce D^2 to O(D). (2) The assumption that the complete tree is eventually built “with probability 1” under guaranteed exploration is stated but not proved in the main text; it would help to summarize the key argument (positive probability reachability over a finite tree implies almost-sure eventual expansion).
- Algorithms: The SM-MCTS template is clear; the regret-matching and Exp3 instantiations are described with exploration. The requirement to propagate means, while natural for the analysis, differs from typical MCTS practice; the empirical section usefully quantifies the (small) slowdown.
- Experiments: The evaluation is careful in what it claims (asymptotic convergence), but limited in scope: small random games and exhaustive search of tiny worst cases. There is no comparison to strong baselines such as (MC)CFR or simultaneous-move alpha-beta/double-oracle search in comparable settings, and no error bars. Still, the experiments corroborate the theory and document the behavior of RM vs Exp3 and mean vs sample propagation.

Clarity:
The paper is mostly well-written, with notation defined and the algorithm spelled out. A few suggestions:
- Improve Algorithm 1’s presentation: clearly distinguish the two return rules (“sample” vs “mean”) and name the two variants consistently (SM-MCTS vs SM-MCTS-M). As written, RetVal(...) appears twice; it would help to explicitly state which variant is used where in theory/experiments.
- Section 4: briefly sketch why “complete tree built eventually” holds under guaranteed exploration.
- Fix minor typos and consistency (e.g., “the once” → “the ones”, consistent use of bars/hats for average vs empirical strategies, unify γ’s roles in RM/Exp3).

Originality:
The convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games under ε–HC selection is, to my knowledge, new. The composition of no-regret dynamics over a game tree with bounded-error propagation and subgame-perfectness is novel in this context. The empirical part is incremental but appropriate to validate the theoretical predictions.

Significance:
Conceptually significant for the MCTS and game-theoretic planning communities: it justifies a widely used heuristic (SM-MCTS) with principled convergence guarantees in a nontrivial class of games. Practically, the guarantees are asymptotic and rely on uniform exploration and mean propagation, which can slow down learning; tighter bounds and finite-time rates would increase impact. The results should motivate further work on variance reduction, adaptive exploration, and rates (e.g., deriving O(D·ε + D·polylog/√T) bounds using known regret rates of RM/Exp3).

Pros:
- First convergence proof of SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE in simultaneous-move games.
- General template: any ε–HC selection with guaranteed exploration works.
- Clear connection to online learning/regret minimization; well-grounded lemmas for bounded-error repeated games.
- Empirical confirmation; practical note on mean vs sample propagation.

Cons:
- Asymptotic only; no finite-sample rates; constants and D^2 dependence seem loose.
- Experiments limited to small games, without comparison to (MC)CFR or simultaneous-move alpha-beta/double-oracle.
- Assumption of complete-tree eventual expansion is only informally justified in main text.
- Some presentation clarity issues around algorithm variants and notation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a finite-time convergence bound by combining known regret rates of RM/Exp3 with the error-propagation induction; even a rough O(D^2/√T) rate would be valuable.
- Tighten the depth dependence; analyze whether per-level error adds linearly (O(D)) rather than quadratically.
- Include a baseline comparison to (MC)CFR on the same games, and to pruning-based simultaneous-move solvers where feasible.
- Add confidence intervals and larger/deeper random games; report rollout policies and parameter sensitivity (γ, learning rates).
- Clarify the exploration/expansion argument and the exact conditions under which sample-propagation converges (the experiments suggest it may, but can you characterize when?).

Overall recommendation:
This is a solid theoretical contribution with appropriate empirical corroboration. Despite limitations in rates and experiments, the novelty and clarity warrant acceptance. I recommend accept (possibly weak accept) contingent on addressing clarity and adding discussion on rates and depth dependence.
