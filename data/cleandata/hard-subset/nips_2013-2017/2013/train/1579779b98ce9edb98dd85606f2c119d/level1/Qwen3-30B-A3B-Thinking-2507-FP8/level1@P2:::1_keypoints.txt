- Addresses convergence of MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, filling a theoretical gap.
- Presents a general MCTS template and proves convergence to approximate Nash equilibria using ε-Hannan consistent selection methods (e.g., regret matching, Exp3).
- Leverages and extends the connection between online learning (Hannan consistency) and game-theoretic equilibrium to the MCTS tree setting.
- Proof strategy is induction from leaves upward with careful error propagation, yielding a convincing theoretical argument.
- Shows that ε-Hannan consistent algorithms with guaranteed exploration suffice for convergence—an elegant and powerful insight.
- Paper is very well-written and organized, with precise definitions and a logical build-up from matrix games to extensive-form games.
- Empirical validation supports theory: experiments compare mean vs. sample propagation and analyze worst-case games, highlighting differences between RM and RMM.
- Results are significant for practical applications (e.g., security games, Goofspiel) and apply broadly to multiple MCTS variants via the general template.
- Limitation: scope restricted to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games; extensions to imperfect information are only briefly mentioned.
- Limitation: no finite-time (finite-sample) convergence bounds; empirical results show convergence but do not quantify rates.
- Limitation: limited empirical comparison to existing MCTS variants (e.g., UCT) on speed or accuracy across benchmarks.
- Arguments for acceptance: first formal convergence guarantee in this setting, rigorous proofs, strong empirical support, and a broadly applicable framework.
- Arguments against acceptance: restricted problem scope, lack of finite-time bounds, and limited empirical comparisons.
- Overall assessment: technically sound, clearly written, novel and significant contribution; recommendation for acceptance.
