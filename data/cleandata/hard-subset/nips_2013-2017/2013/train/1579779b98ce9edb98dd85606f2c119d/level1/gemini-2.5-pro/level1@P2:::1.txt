### Paper Summary

This paper investigates the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with different action selection mechanisms at each node. The main contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection mechanism is an $\epsilon$-Hannan consistent algorithm with guaranteed exploration (such as Regret Matching or Exp3), the overall MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash Equilibrium. This is a significant result, as prior to this work, MCTS was applied to such games largely based on empirical success without formal convergence guarantees. The paper connects the well-studied field of online learning in repeated matrix games to the MCTS framework for extensive-form games. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical evaluation on randomly generated games and carefully selected worst-case instances, confirming convergence and comparing different algorithmic variants.

### Review

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contribution is the centerpiece and appears sound. The proof strategy, sketched in the main body and detailed in the appendix, is logical and well-constructed. It cleverly bridges the gap between the known convergence properties of Hannan-consistent algorithms in single-stage (matrix) games and the multi-stage structure of extensive-form games. This is achieved by modeling the stochastic returns from deeper parts of the MCTS tree as a bounded error on the payoffs of the matrix game at the current node, and then proving convergence via induction on the depth of the game tree.

The experimental evaluation is also well-executed. The authors are careful and honest in their analysis. A key requirement for their proof is that nodes propagate the mean of all collected rewards, rather than the value of the most recent sample. Instead of just stating this, they implement and compare both variants (e.g., RM vs. RMM). The results honestly show that the variant required for the proof (RMM) converges slightly slower, which is an interesting and valuable finding for practitioners. The inclusion of empirically-derived "worst-case" games to test the algorithms is a strong point, as it provides more convincing evidence than tests on average-case random games alone.

#### Clarity

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem by highlighting the practical success of MCTS and the simultaneous lack of theoretical guarantees in this game class. The necessary background on game theory and MCTS is presented concisely in Section 2. The algorithm template (Algorithm 1) is easy to understand, and the specific instantiations with Regret Matching and Exp3 are explained well. The structure of the formal analysis section is particularly good, as it builds the argument step-by-step from simple repeated matrix games to games with bounded error, and finally to the full extensive-form setting. This makes the logic behind the main theorem accessible even without immediately diving into the full proof in the appendix. The figures are informative and support the claims made in the text.

#### Originality

The paper's originality lies in being the first to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While MCTS itself is not new, and neither are regret-minimizing algorithms like Regret Matching and Exp3, their synthesis to establish this theoretical guarantee is a novel and important contribution. The paper correctly positions itself against prior work, noting that previous applications of MCTS in this domain were empirical and that popular selection strategies like UCB have even been shown to fail to converge. By identifying Hannan consistency as the key property for the local selection strategy that enables a global convergence guarantee, the authors provide a significant new insight into why and how MCTS can work in this setting.

#### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant. MCTS is one of the most impactful algorithms in AI for game playing, but its theoretical underpinnings are still being developed, especially for games beyond the classic two-player, sequential, perfect-information setting. This work provides a crucial piece of that theoretical foundation, justifying the use of MCTS in a broader and important class of games from a principled, game-theoretic perspective. This is a demonstrable advance in the state of the art of our understanding of simulation-based search algorithms. Researchers in computational game theory and multi-agent systems are likely to build upon this work, for instance, by trying to prove similar results for other classes of games (e.g., with imperfect information) or by developing selection mechanisms with faster convergence rates that still satisfy the necessary theoretical properties. The general template provided also invites the integration of other regret-minimizing algorithms from the online learning literature.

---

#### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Presents a Novel, Foundational Result:** This is the first work to prove that MCTS converges to an approximate Nash Equilibrium in simultaneous move extensive-form games, filling a major gap in the literature.
*   **Technically Sound:** The proof structure is elegant and appears correct, building from established results in online learning to the more complex MCTS setting.
*   **Connects Disparate Fields:** The paper provides a strong, formal link between online learning theory (Hannan consistency) and Monte Carlo tree search, which is a valuable conceptual contribution.
*   **Good Experimental Design:** The empirical analysis is thoughtful, including a comparison of the theoretically-required variant with a more common practical one, and testing on worst-case game instances.
*   **High Clarity and Significance:** The paper is well-written, and the result is important for both the MCTS and computational game theory communities.

#### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   **No Finite-Time Bounds:** The analysis proves eventual convergence but does not provide rates of convergence or finite-time bounds on the approximation error. This is acknowledged as future work but is a limitation of the present result.
*   **Potentially Loose Error Bound:** The derived error bound in the main theorem depends on the depth of the game tree ($D^2$), which may be loose for very deep games.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While well-designed, the experiments are on relatively small, synthetic games. Evaluation on larger, established benchmark games would further strengthen the paper's practical claims.

#### Recommendation:

The arguments in favor of acceptance are significantly stronger. The paper provides a landmark theoretical result for a widely used algorithm in an important class of games. The limitations identified are natural avenues for future research and do not detract from the importance of the foundational contribution made here. This is a high-quality, original, and significant paper that would be an excellent addition to the NIPS program. I strongly recommend acceptance.
