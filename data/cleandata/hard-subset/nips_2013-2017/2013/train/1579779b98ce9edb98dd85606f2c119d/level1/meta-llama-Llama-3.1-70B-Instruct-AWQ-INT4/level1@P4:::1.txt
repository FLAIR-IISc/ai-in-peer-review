**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games" presents a formal analysis of the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated using various selection methods. They formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game. The authors also empirically evaluate this claim using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods on randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games.

**Relation to Previous Work**

The paper builds upon previous work on MCTS in sequential games and extends it to simultaneous move games. The authors mention that while MCTS has been successfully applied to various games, there is a lack of theoretical analysis of its convergence in games with simultaneous moves. They also reference previous work on Hannan consistency and its relation to Nash equilibrium convergence.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a rigorous formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games, filling a gap in the existing literature.
2. The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated using various selection methods, making the results applicable to a broad range of games.
3. The empirical evaluation of the algorithms on randomly generated games and worst-case games provides insight into the practical performance of the algorithms.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes that the game has perfect information and simultaneous moves, which may not be the case in all real-world games.
2. The analysis is limited to zero-sum games, and it is unclear whether the results can be extended to non-zero-sum games.
3. The empirical evaluation is limited to a specific set of games and selection methods, and it is unclear whether the results generalize to other games and methods.
