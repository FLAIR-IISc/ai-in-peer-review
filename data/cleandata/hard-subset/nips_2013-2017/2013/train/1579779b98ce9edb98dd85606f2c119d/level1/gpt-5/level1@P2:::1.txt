Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games (EFGs) with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which the per-node selection rule can be any ε–Hannan consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching (RM) or Exp3). The main theoretical result shows that, when payoffs are in [0,1] and mean returns are backed up, SM-MCTS with such selection converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε·D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium (NE), where D is tree depth and δ>0 arbitrary. The proof proceeds by: (i) convergence of ε–HC dynamics in repeated matrix games to approximate NE; (ii) extension to repeated games with bounded payoff errors; and (iii) induction up the game tree, bounding error accumulation per level. Empirically, the paper validates convergence on random games and “empirically worst” small games, instantiating RM and Exp3, and compares mean-backup vs sample-backup variants.

This fills a gap: prior MCTS theory largely focuses on sequential perfect-information games (UCT) and bandit settings [Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006], while UCT has known failures in simultaneous-move games [Shafiei et al., 2011]. The paper builds on classic HC/online learning results (Hart & Mas-Colell, Exp3; Blum & Mansour) and connects them to MCTS in stacked matrix games. It complements simultaneous-move pruning/BI work [Saffidine et al., Bošanský et al.], and the empirical SM-MCTS literature in games like Tron and general game playing.

Quality:
– Theory: The assumptions are clear (ε–HC selection, guaranteed exploration, mean value backup, bounded utilities, complete tree eventually explored). The reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated games with bounded errors and the depth-wise induction are well-motivated. The subgame-perfect ε–NE guarantee is stronger than mere root optimality. The bound’s scaling O(D^2 ε) is conservative but justified by summing per-level deviations. Reliance on mean backup is essential to control bias; the paper is explicit about this and empirically examines the effect.
– Empirics: Experiments are modest but consistent with the theory: both RM and Exp3 variants converge on random instances; convergence floors match the exploration rate; mean-backup converges slightly slower. The “empirical worst cases” for tiny games are informative but limited in scope. There is no comparison to non-HC MCTS baselines (e.g., UCT) beyond prior work, nor to exact BI/alpha-beta or CFR-type methods on benchmark games.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized: definitions, algorithm template, and lemmas are clear; assumptions are stated; algorithmic details for RM/Exp3 are explicit. Some proofs are deferred, which is acceptable, but a brief proof sketch for the main theorem would aid readability. There is a minor potential confusion about exploration: Definition 4.3 introduces off-policy exploration (A* that does not update learner state when exploring), while the RM/Exp3 instantiations use on-policy mixing; a short clarification that both variants preserve HC (with appropriate constants) would help. Figures are informative; typos are minor.

Originality:
The theoretical convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games under HC selection appears novel for NIPS. The template unifies RM and Exp3 within MCTS and yields subgame-perfect convergence. The repeated-game-with-error lens is a neat device. The empirical portion is less original but still useful for corroboration.

Significance:
The result provides the first principled theoretical foundation (to my knowledge) for using MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games. This is relevant to GGP, pursuit-evasion, and toy card games like Goofspiel. However, the practical impact is tempered by the asymptotic nature, lack of finite-sample rates, and potentially loose depth dependence (D^2). The requirement to propagate means (rather than most common sample backup) may limit immediate applicability, though the empirical gap is small.

Strengths:
- Clear, general theoretical framework tying HC selection to SM-MCTS convergence.
- Subgame-perfect approximate NE guarantee; handles stochasticity via bounded-error repeated games.
- Instantiation with well-known HC algorithms (RM, Exp3); guaranteed exploration formalized.
- Empirical confirmation; analysis of mean vs sample backup.

Weaknesses:
- Asymptotic result only; no finite-time/sample complexity bounds. Depth dependence (O(D^2 ε)) likely loose.
- Proof requires mean-value backup; most MCTS implementations use immediate sample backups. Extension to that case is conjectured but unproven here.
- Experiments are small-scale, synthetic; no evaluation on standard simultaneous-move benchmarks or comparison to strong alternatives (CFR variants, alpha-beta for simultaneous moves, or double-oracle).
- Exploration scheme and its interaction with HC are presented in two flavors (A* vs on-policy mixing) without a unified bound; needs a clearer statement of which is used in theory vs practice and the precise HC constants.
- Focus restricted to perfect information; no path yet to imperfect-information EFGs where MCTS is also used.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a brief proof sketch for Theorem 4.11 and quantify where D^2 arises; discuss whether tighter depth dependence is plausible.
- Unify the exploration treatment: state a lemma guaranteeing HC and convergence under the exact on-policy mixing used in Algorithms 3.1/3.2, with explicit constants.
- Add experiments on established simultaneous-move domains (e.g., Tron variants, Goofspiel) and compare to alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning and to CFR(-BR) where applicable.
- Report wall-clock or node-expansion normalized performance, and confidence intervals, not just mean exploitability vs iterations.
- Discuss prospects for finite-time bounds (e.g., via known regret rates for RM/Exp3 and mixing) and for extending the proof to sample-backup MCTS.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and clean theoretical contribution establishing convergence of SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE under broad conditions.
- Bridges online learning (HC) with MCTS in a setting where UCT fails; of interest to the NIPS community.
- Clear exposition; empirically corroborated on synthetic instances.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lacks finite-time convergence analysis and uses assumptions (mean backup, eventual full expansion) that reduce practical immediacy.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scope and does not benchmark against strong alternatives on recognized tasks.
- Depth dependence and constants are likely pessimistic; practical significance may be modest.

Overall, the paper meets the NIPS criteria well on quality, clarity, and originality, and moderately on significance. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening clarity around exploration/backup and expanding empirical comparisons.
