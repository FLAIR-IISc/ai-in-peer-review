## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves – a challenging and important area, as existing theoretical guarantees for MCTS largely focus on sequential games. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, under the condition of using a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, and builds upon prior work in algorithmic game theory and MCTS (e.g., [14, 18, 19]).

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The proof of convergence, leveraging -Hannan consistency, is a valuable contribution. The authors clearly define the necessary conditions and provide a detailed, albeit lengthy, proof sketch. The empirical evaluation, using Regret Matching and Exp3, provides supporting evidence for the theoretical results and demonstrates convergence on both randomly generated and “worst-case” games. The comparison to existing MCTS variants ([20, 11]) is also helpful.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The presentation is dense and can be difficult to follow, particularly the formal analysis section. While the authors acknowledge that the convergence rate isn’t explicitly determined, a discussion of factors influencing the speed of convergence would be beneficial. The empirical evaluation, while present, feels somewhat limited. A more comprehensive comparison with other MCTS algorithms, and a deeper analysis of the “worst-case” games, would strengthen the findings. Furthermore, the practical implications of the approximation error (dependent on the game depth) are not fully explored. The claim that many existing MCTS variants likely also converge based on this analysis feels somewhat speculative without further investigation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games.
*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   Well-connected to existing work in game theory and MCTS.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and challenging presentation.
*   Limited empirical evaluation.
*   Convergence rate not explicitly addressed.
*   Speculative claim regarding other MCTS variants.



**Overall:** I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the presentation could be improved and the empirical evaluation expanded, the theoretical results are significant and justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to improve clarity and address the points raised above.
