- Presents theoretical and empirical analysis of MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.  
- Core contribution: proves MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium under -Hannan consistency of the selection method and exploration requirements.  
- Experimental validation using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games.  
- Fills a gap: extends MCTS theory from sequential games to simultaneous-move games.  
- Leverages Hannan consistency in a clever, theoretically sound way.  
- Well-positioned within related work (cites UCT, double-oracle, etc.).  
- Combines rigorous theory with empirical validation, including intentionally crafted worst-case games.  
- Theoretical rigor: the convergence proof is a significant and valuable contribution.  
- Clear problem definition and explicit assumptions.  
- Strong empirical validation; insightful analysis of mean vs. current-sample propagation.  
- Well-written and organized despite technical complexity.  
- Explores MCTS variants beyond the core theoretical framework.  
- Weakness: the proof is long and complex, needing a more intuitive explanation for non-experts.  
- Weakness: lacks theoretical bounds on the convergence rate.  
- Weakness: experiments limited to random and a few worst-case games; more real-world game evaluations would help.  
- Weakness: sensitivity to the exploration parameter γ not explored in depth.  
- Weakness: had to modify Exp3 for numerical stability, indicating a practical implementation concern.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a significant open problem with an original theoretical result, supported by empirical evidence and clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: accessibility issues due to proof complexity, missing convergence-rate guarantees, and limited experimental breadth.  
- Overall: strong paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to add intuitive proof explanation, discuss convergence-rate limitations, and broaden experiments.
