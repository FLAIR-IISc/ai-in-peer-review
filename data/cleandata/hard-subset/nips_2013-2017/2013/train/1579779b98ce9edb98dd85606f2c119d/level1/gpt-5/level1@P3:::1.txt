Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where the selection policy at each node is any ε–Hannan-consistent (HC) no-regret algorithm with guaranteed exploration, and shows that, if mean returns are backpropagated, the average strategies converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect (ε·D^2+δ)-Nash equilibrium (NE), where D is the depth. The theoretical path is: (i) establish convergence properties for repeated matrix games played by ε-HC algorithms; (ii) extend to repeated games with bounded payoff error; (iii) compose errors inductively up the game tree to obtain subgame-perfect convergence. Instantiations with Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 are evaluated on random games and small adversarial cases; both converge empirically, with mean-return backpropagation slightly slower than sample-return.

This sits at the intersection of MCTS and online learning in games. It complements negative results for UCT in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.) and the literature on exact solvers via backward induction with LP/double-oracle. The analysis echoes classic results on no-regret dynamics in zero-sum games (e.g., Blum & Mansour) and is conceptually related to CFR-style local regret minimization in EFGs (Zinkevich et al., 2007), though here the setting is perfect information with simultaneous moves.

Quality:
- Theory: The proof strategy is sound and well-motivated: no-regret implies approach to minimax in matrix games; bounded payoff perturbations preserve approximate convergence; induction up the tree yields a subgame-perfect approximate NE. The introduction of “guaranteed exploration” and the A* modification is reasonable, and Lemmas 4.6–4.10 are consistent with standard no-regret facts, though constants (notably the factor 4 and the linear dependence on the error c) look conservative. The main theorem’s dependence on D^2 also seems loose given the informal “2d per level” argument; a tighter linear-in-D bound may be possible with a more careful induction and value aggregation.
- Technical concerns/clarifications:
  - Tree expansion: The analysis assumes a complete tree and argues eventual completeness with exploration. A formal lemma quantifying that every node and joint action is visited infinitely often almost surely under incremental expansion would strengthen the argument and connect the asymptotic proof to the actual SM-MCTS procedure (especially when expansion stops at first unexpanded child).
  - Guaranteed exploration ⇒ joint-action coverage: With per-player independent exploration of each action with probability ≥γ/|Aq(h)|, the product ensures each joint action has a fixed positive probability each visit. A brief justification (e.g., Borel–Cantelli) that tij→∞ almost surely would help.
  - Lemma 4.5 (empirical frequencies vs average strategies): The claim is plausible, but the argument via the martingale SLLN should be sketched more explicitly because σs are adapted, not i.i.d. Clarify assumptions needed for the convergence of realized action frequencies to the averages of mixed strategies.
  - Mean vs sample returns: The proof requires mean-return backpropagation. This is atypical in MCTS and empirically slower. The paper shows both variants converge in small cases; a discussion on whether the proof can be extended to sample returns (e.g., via unbiasedness plus concentration) would be valuable.
  - Exploration schedule: Fixed γ imposes an irreducible exploitability floor (observed in experiments). A decaying γt (with ∑t γt=∞) would preserve infinite exploration and allow asymptotic removal of exploration bias; can the analysis accommodate this, and how would it affect constants?
- Experiments: The experiments confirm asymptotic convergence qualitatively and illustrate the mean-vs-sample trade-off and worst-case small games. However, they are limited in scale and scope. Additional measurements would improve credibility: exploitability versus iterations across varying D and branching factors; sensitivity to γ; comparisons against alternative baselines (e.g., simultaneous alpha–beta with pruning, CFR variants adapted to simultaneous moves); and wall-clock/sample complexity.

Clarity:
The paper is generally readable, with a clear algorithmic template and proofs sketched at the right level. Some notation is dense (σ̂ vs σ̄), and the update equations in RM/Exp3 could be clarified (e.g., defining x(h,i,j) and the role of RetVal). Explicitly distinguishing what is proven versus conjectured (e.g., extension to other MCTS variants) would avoid overreach. Minor typos exist.

Originality:
To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to (subgame-perfect) approximate NE under broad conditions on the selection policy. The connection between local HC dynamics and global equilibrium in simultaneous-move perfect-information trees is novel in the MCTS literature. The use of no-regret selection is conceptually close to CFR’s local regret minimization but in a different game class and algorithmic framework.

Significance:
The theoretical guarantee is a meaningful contribution: it justifies a popular heuristic class (SM-MCTS) and sets a foundation for further analysis. That said, the asymptotic nature, conservative constants (O(D^2ε)), and limited empirical validation temper immediate practical impact. Strengthening finite-time/sample complexity bounds by leveraging known regret rates for RM/Exp3 and tightening the depth dependence would raise the significance. Given ongoing interest in general game playing and simultaneous-move domains, the work is timely and relevant.

Pros:
- Clear, general template linking SM-MCTS to no-regret selection with a convergence guarantee.
- Subgame-perfect approximate NE guarantee, not just root optimality.
- Applies to multiple standard HC algorithms (RM, Exp3); empirically validated.
- Illuminates why UCT may fail and how to repair it via no-regret dynamics.

Cons:
- Asymptotic result with loose constants; O(D^2ε) may be pessimistic.
- Proof requires mean-return backprop, which is nonstandard and slower empirically; the commonly used variant lacks theory here.
- Exploration is fixed; asymptotic suboptimality due to γ; no analysis of decaying exploration.
- Experimental evaluation is small-scale; lacks stronger baselines and runtime/sample complexity analysis.
- Some technical steps (tree expansion, Lemma 4.5) are asserted with limited detail.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a formal lemma on eventual full-tree expansion and joint-action visitation.
- Analyze decaying exploration schedules and their impact on convergence and final exploitability.
- Derive finite-time bounds by composing per-node regret rates (e.g., RM’s O(1/√T)) through the depth, and aim to tighten the D dependence.
- Attempt to extend the proof to sample-return backpropagation (perhaps via averaging in the selection updates only).
- Expand experiments: larger depths/branching, sensitivity to γ, comparisons to simultaneous alpha–beta/double-oracle and CFR-style baselines, and report wall-clock/sample complexity.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical insight is novel and relevant to the NeurIPS community; while some constants and assumptions are conservative and the experiments modest, the contribution establishes a clear and useful foundation for SM-MCTS.
