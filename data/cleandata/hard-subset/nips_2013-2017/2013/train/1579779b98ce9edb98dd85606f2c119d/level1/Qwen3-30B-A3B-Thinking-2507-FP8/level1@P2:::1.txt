

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in a specific class of games: zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. Previous work on MCTS convergence has primarily focused on sequential games (e.g., the UCT algorithm for Go), with no guarantees for simultaneous move games. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games, prove that using -Hannan consistent selection methods (like regret matching or Exp3) ensures convergence to approximate Nash equilibrium, and provide empirical validation.

The paper builds upon the established connection between online learning algorithms and game-theoretic equilibrium concepts. The authors leverage Hannan consistency from online learning literature (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000; Auer et al., 2002) and extend it to the MCTS framework. This is a natural extension but requires careful adaptation to the game tree structure. The authors clearly relate their work to prior MCTS applications in simultaneous move games (e.g., Finnsson & Björnsson, 2008; Shafiei et al., 2012) while highlighting the lack of convergence guarantees in previous work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a substantial theoretical advance. The proof strategy—using induction from leaf nodes upward, with careful handling of error propagation—is well-structured and convincing. The key insight that -Hannan consistent algorithms with guaranteed exploration suffice for convergence is both elegant and powerful.

2. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The definitions (Section 2) are precise, the formal analysis (Section 4) builds logically from simple matrix games to extensive-form games, and the empirical evaluation (Section 5) directly tests the theoretical claims. The use of lemmas to build toward the main theorem is particularly effective.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The authors don't just prove theory—they empirically validate it. The experiments comparing mean propagation versus current sample propagation (Figure 2) and the worst-case game analysis (Figure 3) provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical claims. The careful distinction between RM (regret matching) and RMM (regret matching with mean propagation) is particularly insightful.

4. **Significance:** This work provides a crucial theoretical foundation for using MCTS in practical applications involving simultaneous move games (e.g., security games, Goofspiel). The general template approach means the results apply to multiple existing MCTS variants, not just the specific algorithms tested.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Results:** The paper focuses exclusively on zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. While this is a well-defined and important class, the authors briefly mention in the conclusion that future work could extend to imperfect information games. A brief discussion of why these extensions are challenging would strengthen the paper.

2. **Empirical Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves convergence, it doesn't provide finite-time bounds for the convergence rate. The empirical analysis (Section 5.2) shows convergence on worst-case games but doesn't quantify the rate. This is a natural next step that would make the results even more impactful for practitioners.

3. **Comparison to Other Approaches:** The paper mentions that previous MCTS variants (like UCT) don't have convergence guarantees in simultaneous move games (citing Shafiei et al., 2012), but it doesn't compare the empirical performance of their RMM and Exp3M variants to these other approaches in terms of speed or accuracy on standard benchmarks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games (a significant theoretical contribution)
- Well-structured, rigorous proof with clear theoretical insights
- Empirical results strongly support theoretical claims
- Provides a general framework applicable to multiple selection methods
- Addresses an important gap in the literature that has practical implications

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope to zero-sum perfect-information games
- No finite-time convergence bounds provided
- Limited comparison to existing MCTS variants in empirical evaluation

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field, addressing a significant gap in the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The proof is technically sound, the writing is clear, and the empirical validation supports the theoretical claims. The paper meets all four NIPS criteria:

- **Quality:** The paper is technically sound with complete theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for guaranteed exploration).
- **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and written. The mathematical notation is precise and well-explained.
- **Originality:** This is a novel contribution to the literature, building on previous work on Hannan consistency but applying it to a new context (MCTS in simultaneous move games) where convergence guarantees were previously unknown.
- **Significance:** The results have clear implications for practical applications of MCTS in games with simultaneous moves, providing theoretical justification for using MCTS in this setting.

The paper's main limitation is its restricted scope to zero-sum perfect-information games, but this is a reasonable starting point for a theoretically rigorous contribution. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest extensions to imperfect information games in the conclusion.

I recommend acceptance. This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCTS and provides valuable guarantees for practitioners working with these games. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory and reinforcement learning.
