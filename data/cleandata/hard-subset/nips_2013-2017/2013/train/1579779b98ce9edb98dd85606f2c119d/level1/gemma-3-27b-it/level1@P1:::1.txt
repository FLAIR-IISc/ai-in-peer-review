This paper presents a theoretical and empirical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The core contribution lies in proving that, under certain conditions (specifically, -Hannan consistency of the selection method and exploration requirements), the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The authors validate this claim with experiments using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper is well-structured and tackles a significant open problem in the field of game-playing AI. Prior work on MCTS primarily focuses on sequential games, and this paper directly addresses the lack of theoretical guarantees for simultaneous-move games. The connection to Hannan consistency is a clever and theoretically sound approach. The authors effectively relate their work to existing literature, citing relevant papers on game theory, MCTS, and related algorithms (e.g., UCT, double-oracle methods).  The inclusion of both theoretical analysis and empirical validation strengthens the overall contribution.  The thorough experimental setup, including the creation of "worst-case" games, is commendable.  The connection to previous work on regret matching and Exp3 is appropriately highlighted.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof of convergence is a significant contribution and provides a solid foundation for understanding the behavior of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
* **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the class of games being considered and the assumptions made.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical applicability of the approach.  The analysis comparing propagation of the mean vs. current sample is insightful.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature and effectively position their work within the field.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter.
* **Exploration of Variants:** Investigating the convergence of MCTS variants beyond the core theoretical framework demonstrates forward thinking.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of Proof:** While rigorous, the proof is lengthy and complex, potentially making it difficult for readers without a strong background in game theory to fully grasp.  A more intuitive explanation of the key ideas could be beneficial.
* **Convergence Rate:** The paper establishes convergence *to* an approximate NE, but doesn't provide strong guarantees on the *rate* of convergence. The empirical analysis provides some insights, but a theoretical bound on the convergence speed would be valuable.
* **Limited Scope of Games:** The experiments are primarily conducted on randomly generated games and a limited number of "worst-case" scenarios.  Evaluating the algorithm on a wider range of real-world games (e.g., variants of card games mentioned in the introduction) would further strengthen the findings.
* **γ parameter sensitivity:** Although examined, the impact of the exploration parameter γ isn't explored in great depth and its influence on the convergence rate could be more carefully studied.
* **Numerical Stability:** Mentioning the need to modify the Exp3 implementation for numerical stability highlights a potential practical issue that should perhaps be addressed more directly.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of game-playing AI.
* The theoretical contribution (convergence proof) is substantial and original.
* The empirical results strongly support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
* The work has the potential to advance the state of the art in MCTS algorithms for simultaneous-move games.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proof may limit its accessibility to a broader audience.
* The lack of strong guarantees on the convergence rate is a weakness.
* The experimental evaluation could be expanded to include a wider range of games.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The proof of convergence is a valuable result, and the empirical validation provides compelling evidence of the algorithm's effectiveness. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is well-suited for acceptance to NIPS.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting the authors to consider adding a more intuitive explanation of the core ideas in the proof and briefly discussing the limitations regarding the convergence rate.  Expanding the experimental evaluation with a more diverse set of games could also be a valuable addition.
