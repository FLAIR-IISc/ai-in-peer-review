This paper investigates the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, under certain conditions (specifically, using a -Hannan consistent selection method with adequate exploration), the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is a central contribution, filling a gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in this game class. The work is complemented by empirical evaluations using regret matching and Exp3, demonstrating convergence on randomly generated and “worst-case” games.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The formal proof appears rigorous, building upon established concepts in online learning (Hannan consistency) and game theory. The reduction to repeated matrix games and the inductive argument for convergence seem well-structured. The authors carefully state the assumptions required for the convergence guarantees. However, the complexity of the proof makes it difficult to fully assess its correctness without significant effort. The empirical evaluation is adequate, demonstrating convergence for the tested algorithms and game instances. The authors acknowledge the limitations of the empirical setup (searching only a limited game space for worst-case scenarios). The discussion of the propagation of the mean vs. current sample values adds nuance, although the performance difference appears small. 

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, though dense. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and situates it within the existing literature. The definitions are precise, and the notation is mostly consistent. Algorithm 1 provides a good high-level overview of the SM-MCTS process. However, the formal analysis section (Section 4) is challenging to follow. The proofs are relatively concise, and some steps could benefit from more detailed explanation. The frequent references to the appendix and arXiv preprint alleviate this somewhat. The empirical sections are clear, and the figures are well-labeled.

**Originality:** The primary novelty lies in the theoretical convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While MCTS has been successfully applied to these games previously, a formal analysis was lacking. The connection to -Hannan consistency provides a solid theoretical foundation. The empirical evaluation, while not groundbreaking in terms of results, helps validate the theoretical claims. The paper builds upon existing work in online learning and game theory but combines these concepts in a novel way to analyze MCTS.  The authors correctly cite relevant prior work (e.g., Shafiei et al. showing UCB’s non-convergence).

**Significance:** This work is significant because it provides a theoretical justification for the use of MCTS in a class of games where analytical solutions are computationally expensive. Establishing convergence guarantees increases confidence in the algorithm's performance and allows for a more principled understanding of its behavior.  The results could be valuable to researchers working on game-playing AI, security applications, and other areas where simultaneous-move games arise. The general MCTS template and the connection to -Hannan consistency could inspire further research on algorithm design and analysis.  The identified limitations and future research directions (finite-time bounds, imperfect information games) point to potential avenues for continued work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contribution: Provides the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Sound methodology: The proof is based on established concepts and appears rigorous.
*   Empirical validation: The experimental results support the theoretical claims.
*   Clear presentation: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written.
*   Significance: Addresses an important open problem and advances the understanding of MCTS.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Complexity of the proof: The formal analysis is challenging to follow and requires significant expertise.
*   Limited empirical scope: The experimental evaluation is limited to a specific set of game instances.
*   Minor performance difference: The difference between propagating the mean and current sample values seems small in the empirical results.
*   Dense writing style: The paper's density might hinder accessibility for some readers.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. The convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a valuable result. While the paper has some minor weaknesses in terms of clarity and empirical scope, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance**.
