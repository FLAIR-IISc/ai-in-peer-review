

# Peer Review: "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, and prove that when instantiated with an ε-Hannan consistent selection method (like regret matching or Exp3) with guaranteed exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. This is a substantial theoretical contribution, as prior to this work, no convergence guarantees existed for MCTS in such games, despite its widespread practical use in applications like security games and general game-playing.

The paper relates well to previous NIPS work: it extends the theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond sequential games (where UCT convergence was established) to simultaneous move games. It builds on foundational work in online learning (Hannan consistency, regret minimization) and game theory (Nash equilibrium concepts), while addressing a gap in the application of these techniques to practical game-solving algorithms. The authors correctly reference seminal works on MCTS (Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006) and regret minimization (Hart and Mas-Colell, 2000), though they could have more explicitly connected to recent NIPS work on CFR (Counterfactual Regret Minimization) in extensive-form games.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof of convergence. The formal analysis proceeds logically from matrix games to extensive-form games through a series of well-motivated lemmas. The authors carefully distinguish between empirical frequencies and average strategies (Lemma 4.5), which is crucial for the convergence argument. The experimental evaluation complements the theoretical results well, showing that the algorithms converge to approximate NE on both randomly generated and worst-case games. However, the paper could have included a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the theoretical approach (e.g., the dependence on the depth parameter D in the convergence bound, which may be impractical for deep games). The experimental section is well-designed, but the paper would benefit from more concrete examples of the "worst-case" games to illustrate the theoretical claims.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written with clear definitions and logical flow. The mathematical notation is consistently used, and the key concepts (Hannan consistency, subgame perfect NE) are well-explained. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 1 and variants) are precise and would enable reproduction. However, the paper would be more accessible with a single figure illustrating the game tree structure for simultaneous move games, rather than relying on the description in Figure 1. The authors' decision to use mean propagation (rather than single sample values) is well-justified theoretically but could have been more clearly motivated for practitioners.

**Originality:** This is a strong contribution with significant novelty. Prior to this work, no convergence guarantees existed for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The paper's main insight—connecting Hannan consistency in matrix games to convergence in extensive-form games—represents a novel theoretical framework. The paper distinguishes itself from related work (e.g., Shafiei et al. on UCT) by providing provable guarantees, rather than empirical observations. The authors correctly note that their template covers a broad class of algorithms, while previous work focused on specific variants. This work is substantially different from recent NIPS papers on MCTS (e.g., Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006; Browne et al., 2012) which focused on sequential games.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for both theory and practice. The paper provides theoretical justification for the widespread use of MCTS in simultaneous move games (e.g., Goofspiel, general game-playing competitions), which previously lacked formal guarantees. This bridges a critical gap between theoretical game theory and practical algorithm design. The results could influence the design of future MCTS algorithms, particularly for security applications where game-theoretic guarantees are important. The authors also identify promising future directions (finite-time bounds, extension to imperfect information games), which further enhances the significance of the work.

**Strengths:**
- First formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Rigorous connection between online learning (Hannan consistency) and game-theoretic convergence
- Well-designed empirical evaluation confirming theoretical claims
- Clear and general algorithmic template applicable to various selection methods
- Strong relation to established concepts in game theory and online learning

**Weaknesses:**
- The convergence bound depends on game depth (D²), which may be impractical for deep games
- No finite-time convergence analysis, only eventual convergence
- Limited discussion of how the results compare to other algorithms (e.g., CFR) in practice
- Experimental section could better illustrate the "worst-case" games
- The paper would benefit from more concrete examples of practical applications

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a practically important problem
- The proof is technically sound and well-structured
- The empirical results support the theoretical claims
- The work fills a clear gap in the literature
- The paper is well-written and clearly presented

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The convergence bound may be too loose for practical applications
- Limited discussion of how this work compares to existing practical algorithms
- The paper doesn't provide concrete guidance for practitioners on implementing the proposed approach

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that makes a clear advance in understanding MCTS algorithms. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important gap in the literature. While the convergence bound may be loose for very deep games, the theoretical framework is novel and significant. The empirical results provide good support for the theoretical claims. This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, particularly for researchers interested in the theoretical foundations of game-theoretic algorithms. I recommend acceptance.
