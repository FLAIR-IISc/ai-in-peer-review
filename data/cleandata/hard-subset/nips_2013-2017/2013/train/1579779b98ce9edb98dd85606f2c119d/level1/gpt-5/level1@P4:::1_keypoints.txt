- Proposes SM-MCTS template where joint actions are sampled by ε–Hannan-consistent selection (e.g., regret matching, Exp3).
- Main theorem: with ε–HC selection and guaranteed exploration, SM-MCTS converges a.s. to a subgame-perfect ε·D^2–Nash equilibrium (up to δ).
- Proof outline: analyze repeated matrix games → extend to games with bounded payoff error → induct over tree depth; errors accumulate across levels.
- Critical technical requirement: back up mean returns (not single-sample returns) for the convergence proof.
- Empirical evaluation: regret matching and Exp3 on random and worst-case depth-2 games; compare mean vs single-sample backups.
- Empirical finding: both backup variants appear to converge to approximate NE in practice; mean backups converge slightly more slowly.
- Connects no-regret learning (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) with MCTS (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and addresses convergence gaps for simultaneous-move games.
- The reduction from SM-MCTS to repeated matrix games with bounded error is clearly articulated and technically sound.
- Use of ε–Hannan consistency and best-response inequalities to bound exploitability is standard and appropriate.
- Extension to “games with error” and depth-wise induction explaining the D^2 dependence is reasonable.
- Proof depends on guaranteed exploration (A* modification) to ensure every joint action is sampled infinitely often.
- Convergence of empirical frequencies to average strategies is plausible via martingale SLLN/LLN arguments.
- Main limitation: theoretical guarantees require mean backups; standard single-sample MCTS lacks formal coverage.
- Bounds are loose (D^2 factor, “4(c+1)” term) and no finite-sample convergence rates are provided.
- Problem setting is restricted to two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves.
- Experiments validate qualitative convergence but are limited in scale, lack confidence intervals, and omit sensitivity analyses.
- Paper is generally clear and well organized but has presentation/pseudocode and typographical issues that need fixing.
- The treatment of “guaranteed exploration” and the distinction between A* and on-policy γ exploration should be more explicit.
- Original contribution: first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games (as of NIPS 2013).
- Significance: provides a theoretical foundation for SM-MCTS and is likely to influence further work on EFGs and MCTS variants.
- Strengths: clear general convergence theorem, sound proof structure, links to known no-regret algorithms, empirical support, subgame-perfect guarantee.
- Weaknesses: mean-backup requirement, asymptotic/loose bounds, limited experimental scope, exposition and exploration-setup ambiguities.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a recognized theoretical gap, general template, and supportive experiments aligning with theory.
- Arguments against acceptance: lacks theory for common sample-backup variants, no finite-time analysis, and modest empirical evaluation.
- Suggested improvements: provide finite-sample rates, tighten constants or use variance reduction, extend theory to single-sample backups, test larger canonical games, and clarify exploration schemes and pseudocode.
- Overall verdict: technically sound, original asymptotic convergence result with reasonable empirical support that addresses an important gap in MCTS for simultaneous-move games.
