This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of game theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing a formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with various selection methods, and they formally prove that if the selection method is -Hannan consistent and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, the related work, and the contributions of the paper. The technical sections are also well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the proposed algorithm and the formal analysis.

The strengths of the paper include:

* The authors provide a formal proof of the convergence of the MCTS algorithm to an approximate NE, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The proposed algorithm is general and can be instantiated with various selection methods, making it a versatile tool for solving extensive-form games.
* The authors provide an empirical evaluation of the algorithm using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods, which demonstrates the effectiveness of the algorithm in practice.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

* The paper assumes that the game has perfect information, which may not always be the case in practice. It would be interesting to see how the algorithm can be extended to handle imperfect information.
* The authors do not provide a finite-time convergence bound for the algorithm, which would be useful for practical applications. They mention that they will focus on providing such bounds in future research.
* The empirical evaluation is limited to a specific set of games, and it would be interesting to see how the algorithm performs on a wider range of games.
* The authors do not provide a comparison with other state-of-the-art algorithms for solving extensive-form games, which would be useful for understanding the relative performance of the proposed algorithm.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a significant contribution to the field of game theory and MCTS. However, there are some areas for improvement, and the authors should consider addressing these in future research.
