### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with various selection mechanisms. The central contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent (a concept from online learning signifying bounded average regret) and guarantees sufficient exploration, then the MCTS algorithm's average strategy profile converges to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The approximation error of the equilibrium is shown to depend on ε and the depth of the game tree. The paper specifically considers instantiations using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3, two well-known Hannan-consistent algorithms. This theoretical contribution is supported by an empirical analysis on randomly generated and empirically-derived worst-case games, which confirms the convergence and compares the practical performance of different algorithmic variants.

This work connects the fields of online learning and game-playing search. While no-regret algorithms like RM and Exp3 are known to converge to correlated or Nash equilibria in repeated matrix games, and MCTS is a popular practical algorithm for game tree search, a formal link proving MCTS convergence in this game class was previously missing. This paper fills that important gap, contrasting with prior work showing that popular MCTS selection methods like UCB do not converge to Nash equilibria in simultaneous-move settings.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel theoretical contribution. It provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. This is a significant result that places a widely used family of practical algorithms on a solid theoretical foundation from the perspective of algorithmic game theory. By framing the selection mechanism in terms of ε-Hannan consistency, the paper provides a general recipe for designing provably convergent MCTS algorithms, which is a valuable insight for future research.

2.  **Technical Soundness:** The formal analysis presented in Section 4 is well-structured and technically sound. The proof strategy, which proceeds by induction from the leaf nodes up the game tree, is elegant. The key idea of modeling the decision problem at each node as a repeated matrix game with bounded error (where the error comes from the stochastic estimates of the subgame values) is clear and effective. The step-by-step development, from standard matrix games (Lemma 4.6) to games with bounded error (Lemma 4.9) and finally to the full extensive-form game (Theorem 4.11), makes the argument easy to follow and convincing.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. It begins with a comprehensive background on the game-theoretic concepts and MCTS, making it accessible. The algorithmic template (Algorithm 1) and its specific instantiations (RM and Exp3) are described precisely. The formal analysis is laid out logically, with definitions and lemmas building towards the main theorem.

4.  **Empirical Support:** The experimental evaluation in Section 5 is well-conceived and provides valuable insights that complement the theory. The experiments directly address a key detail from the formal analysis—the requirement to propagate mean values versus the more standard practice of propagating the current sample's value. The results showing that both converge, with the theoretically-required variant being slightly slower, is an important practical finding. Furthermore, the analysis on empirically identified worst-case games demonstrates the robustness of the convergence.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of the Main Theorem's Bound:** The statement of the main result, Theorem 4.11, claims convergence to a subgame perfect `(ε_D^2 + δ)`-Nash equilibrium. However, the term `ε_D` is not explicitly defined in the paper, making the precise nature of the final error bound unclear. The textual description of the proof suggests an error accumulation that depends on the depth `D` and the base regret bound `ε`, but the final quadratic form is not immediately obvious from the preceding lemmas. A more precise definition and derivation of the final bound would strengthen the paper's central claim.

2.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Analysis:** The convergence proof is asymptotic, guaranteeing that the algorithm *eventually* converges. The analysis does not provide any finite-time bounds on the rate of convergence (i.e., how many iterations are required to achieve a given approximation quality `ε'`). While providing such bounds is acknowledged as future work and is a significantly harder problem, the lack of any rate information is a limitation for understanding the practical applicability of the theoretical result.

3.  **Simplifying Assumption in Proof:** The formal analysis makes the simplifying assumption that the entire game tree is built and then statistics are collected. The paper argues this is valid for an eventual convergence proof since exploration guarantees all nodes are eventually visited infinitely often. While this is a common and often necessary simplification for such proofs, it sidesteps the analysis of the initial tree-building phase where statistics are biased and exploration is more directed. A brief discussion justifying why this initial phase does not prevent eventual convergence would have been beneficial.

**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Provides the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in an important class of games (perfect-information, simultaneous-move).
    *   The theoretical framework is general, based on the well-understood property of Hannan consistency, making it applicable beyond the specific RM and Exp3 examples.
    *   The technical argument is sound and connects two important areas of research: online learning and game tree search.
    *   The paper is well-written and supported by relevant, well-designed experiments.

*   **Con:**
    *   The final error bound in the main theorem is not clearly defined or derived in the main text.
    *   The analysis is purely asymptotic and provides no information on the rate of convergence.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly the novelty and significance of its core theoretical result, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It addresses a long-standing open question and provides a crucial theoretical underpinning for MCTS in a non-trivial setting. The paper represents a solid and important contribution to the field.
