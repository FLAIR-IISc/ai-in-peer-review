- Proves convergence of a general MCTS template in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games to an approximate subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium when using ε-Hannan consistent selection with guaranteed exploration.  
- Fills a theoretical gap by extending MCTS convergence results from sequential to simultaneous-move games.  
- Builds on online learning (Hannan consistency, regret minimization) and game-theory foundations; references seminal MCTS and regret works.  
- Could more explicitly connect to recent CFR and related NIPS work.  
- Technically sound: rigorous proofs progressing from matrix games to extensive-form games and careful treatment of empirical vs. average strategies.  
- Experimental evaluation supports theoretical claims on random and worst-case games but could show more concrete worst-case examples.  
- Convergence bound depends on game depth (D²), which may be impractical for deep games.  
- Lacks finite-time convergence analysis (only eventual convergence shown).  
- Well-written and clear notation; algorithms are precise and reproducible.  
- Would benefit from a single illustrative figure of the simultaneous-move game tree and clearer practitioner motivation for mean propagation.  
- Original and novel: first provable MCTS convergence result for simultaneous-move games linking Hannan consistency to extensive-form convergence.  
- Significant for both theory and practice, with implications for game-playing and security applications and promising future directions (finite-time bounds, imperfect information).  
- Strengths: first formal proof, rigorous online-learning connection, general algorithmic template, and supporting experiments.  
- Weaknesses: loose depth-dependent bounds, limited practical comparisons (e.g., CFR), insufficient practical guidance, and need for clearer worst-case experimental examples.  
- Acceptance arguments: substantial theoretical contribution, sound proofs, empirical support, fills a literature gap, well-presented.  
- Rejection arguments: bounds may be too loose for practice and limited guidance/comparison to existing practical algorithms.  
- Overall recommendation: strong contribution; reviewer recommends acceptance.
