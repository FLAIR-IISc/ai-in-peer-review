This paper presents a formal convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The core contribution is proving that, under certain conditions (specifically, using a -Hannan consistent selection method with suitable exploration guarantees), MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The authors support their theoretical results with empirical validation on randomly generated and “worst-case” games, demonstrating convergence behavior comparable to existing algorithms.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS, particularly extending it beyond the well-studied setting of sequential games. The proof appears rigorous, building on established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency) and applying them to the simultaneous-move context. The careful consideration of exploration requirements alongside Hannan consistency is crucial and well-justified. The empirical evaluation provides reasonable support for the theoretical claims. However, the empirical section feels somewhat limited. While demonstrating convergence, a deeper investigation of the convergence *rate* (e.g., how the approximation error decreases with increasing iterations) would strengthen the findings. The choice of "worst case" games is not clearly defined and further justification of this selection would be helpful. It’s a complete piece of work within its defined scope, carefully evaluating both strengths and weaknesses (though the weaknesses section could be expanded - see below).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The definitions are clear, and the logical flow of the proof is reasonably easy to follow, although a strong mathematical background is required. Algorithm 1 is clearly presented. However, the notation can be dense, and at times, the connection between the formal analysis and the practical implications for MCTS implementation could be made more explicit. A more intuitive explanation of how the simultaneous-move aspect influences the convergence guarantees compared to sequential games would be beneficial.

**Originality:** The problem of formally analyzing MCTS in simultaneous-move games is genuinely novel. While MCTS has been applied to these games previously, there was a lack of theoretical guarantees. The connection to Hannan consistency and the proof of convergence represent a significant advance. The authors adequately reference related work, including papers on MCTS, game theory, and online learning. Checking proceedings of NIPS 2013-2017, this work appears to be a novel contribution.

**Significance:** This work is significant because it provides a theoretical foundation for understanding and improving MCTS in a class of games with practical applications (e.g., Goofspiel, security games). The convergence guarantee, even approximate, is valuable for practitioners and researchers. This opens avenues for further research on refining MCTS algorithms for simultaneous-move games and potentially extending the analysis to more general game settings. The potential to generalize the proofs to existing MCTS variants is also a valuable direction.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novel theoretical result:** The paper establishes convergence guarantees for MCTS in a previously unanalyzed setting.
*   **Sound methodology:** The proof is based on well-established concepts and appears rigorous.
*   **Empirical validation:** Experiments support the theoretical claims.
*   **Potential impact:** The work can influence the development of more robust and efficient MCTS algorithms for practical applications.
*   **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally clear and easy to follow (for a technically proficient reader).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited empirical analysis:** The experimental evaluation is somewhat superficial and lacks a detailed analysis of convergence rates. The selection of "worst case" games lacks clear justification.
*   **Dense notation:** The mathematical notation can be challenging for some readers.
*   **Relatively narrow scope:** The results are specific to zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While significant, the generalizability to other game types is not immediately clear.
*   **Weakness section could be expanded:** The authors acknowledge limitations but could further discuss potential challenges and avenues for future research.




**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical result is significant, and the empirical validation is reasonable. While the empirical analysis could be more thorough, and the notation somewhat simplified, these are minor shortcomings. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above (specifically, expanding the empirical section and providing more intuition for the theoretical concepts).
