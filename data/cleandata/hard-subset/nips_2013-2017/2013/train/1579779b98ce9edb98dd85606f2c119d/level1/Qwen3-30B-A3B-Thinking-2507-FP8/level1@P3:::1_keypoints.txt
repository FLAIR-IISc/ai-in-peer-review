- Addresses gap: provides theoretical convergence guarantees for MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.  
- Main contribution: presents a general MCTS template and proves any ε-Hannan consistent selection method with exploration converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium.  
- Theoretical approach: extends online learning (Hannan consistency) results from matrix games to full game trees via induction and error-propagation analysis.  
- Proof structure: progresses from matrix games (Lemma 4.6) to repeated games with bounded error (Lemma 4.9) to the main theorem (Theorem 4.11).  
- Exploration/tree assumption: assumes complete tree-building occurs with probability 1; justified but not exhaustively explored.  
- Empirical setup: evaluates regret matching and Exp3 with mean propagation (RMM, Exp3M) and current-sample propagation on random and worst-case games.  
- Empirical findings: both methods converge to approximate Nash equilibria; mean propagation converges slightly slower than current-sample propagation.  
- Worst-case analysis: experiments on worst-case games offer practical insights into algorithm performance under adverse conditions.  
- Strength: first formal convergence guarantee for simultaneous-move MCTS, filling a clear theoretical gap.  
- Strength: rigorous, well-structured proofs that handle exploration and error propagation.  
- Strength: empirical results support theoretical claims across tested games.  
- Strength: strong connection to established online learning theory, explaining empirical success of prior MCTS variants.  
- Weakness: proof ignores incremental tree construction central to practical MCTS; practical implications insufficiently explored.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation limited to small games (depth ≤2, branching ≤2), reducing practical generality.  
- Weakness: no finite-time convergence bounds provided, only asymptotic guarantees.  
- Weakness: mean propagation’s slower empirical performance is noted but not explained.  
- Comparison: extends prior NIPS MCTS work by providing formal convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move settings.  
- Significance: important theoretical and practical contribution for using MCTS in simultaneous-move applications (security games, general game-playing).  
- Recommendation: Accept — high-quality theoretical contribution with supportive experiments, despite empirical scope and some assumptions.
