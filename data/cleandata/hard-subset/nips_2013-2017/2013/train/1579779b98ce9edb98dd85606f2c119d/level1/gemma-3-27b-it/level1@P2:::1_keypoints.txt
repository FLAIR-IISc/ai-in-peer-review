- Core contribution: proves MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games under a β-Hannan consistent selection method with sufficient exploration.
- Theoretical technique: builds rigorous proofs using online learning (Hannan consistency) adapted to simultaneous-move contexts.
- Key condition: requires a Hannan-consistent selection policy combined with explicit exploration guarantees.
- Empirical support: experiments on random and "worst-case" games show convergence behavior comparable to existing algorithms.
- Empirical limitation: experiments are limited; the convergence rate (how error decreases with iterations) is not thoroughly analyzed.
- Game selection concern: the definition and justification of the chosen "worst-case" games is unclear and needs clarification.
- Technical quality: proofs are sound and rigorous, extending theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond sequential games.
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized, but notation is dense and requires a strong mathematical background.
- Practical clarity gap: connections between the formal analysis and practical MCTS implementation could be made more explicit.
- Intuition missing: needs a clearer intuitive explanation of how simultaneous moves affect convergence compared to sequential games.
- Originality: novel theoretical analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, filling a gap in prior work.
- Related work: adequately references MCTS, game theory, and online learning literature; appears novel versus NIPS 2013–2017 proceedings.
- Significance: provides a valuable theoretical foundation with practical implications for games like Goofspiel and security games.
- Future directions: opens avenues to refine MCTS variants and possibly generalize proofs to broader game settings.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theoretical result, sound methodology, supporting experiments, potential practical impact, and generally clear presentation.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical depth, dense notation, narrow problem scope (zero-sum, perfect information, simultaneous moves), and a weakly expanded weaknesses discussion.
- Recommendation: overall strong paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to expand empirical analysis and improve intuition/clarity.
