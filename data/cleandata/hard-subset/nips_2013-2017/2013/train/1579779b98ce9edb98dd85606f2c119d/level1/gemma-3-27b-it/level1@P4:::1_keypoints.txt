- Investigates convergence of MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games and presents a general MCTS template.  
- Proves that MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection method with sufficient exploration.  
- The convergence proof is the central contribution, filling a theoretical gap for MCTS in this game class.  
- Empirical evaluations with regret matching and Exp3 demonstrate convergence on random and “worst-case” games.  
- The paper is technically sound and builds on established online learning and game-theory concepts.  
- The reduction to repeated matrix games and the inductive convergence argument are well-structured but complex.  
- Proof complexity makes correctness hard to fully verify without substantial effort.  
- Empirical evaluation is adequate but limited in scope, searching only a restricted game space for worst-case scenarios.  
- Discussion of propagating mean vs. current sample values is nuanced, though empirical differences are small.  
- Generally well-written and organized, with precise definitions and consistent notation, but dense in places.  
- Section 4’s formal analysis is challenging to follow and could use more explanatory detail.  
- Novelty lies in the theoretical convergence guarantee and the use of ε-Hannan consistency for MCTS analysis.  
- Significance: provides theoretical justification for MCTS in simultaneous-move games and suggests directions for further work (finite-time bounds, imperfect information).  
- Acceptance arguments: strong theoretical contribution, sound methodology, supporting experiments, clear presentation, and practical significance.  
- Rejection concerns: proof complexity and accessibility, limited empirical scope, small empirical differences between variants, and dense writing.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with minor weaknesses; recommend acceptance.
