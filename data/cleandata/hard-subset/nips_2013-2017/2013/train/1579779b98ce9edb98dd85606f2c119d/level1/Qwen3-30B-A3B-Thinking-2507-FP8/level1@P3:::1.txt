

# Peer Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS has been successfully applied in games like Goofspiel and Tron, no convergence guarantees existed for these settings. The authors present a general MCTS template for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, proving that any ε-Hannan consistent selection method (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) with guaranteed exploration converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The paper builds upon seminal work in online learning (Hannan consistency, regret matching [15]) and game theory (Nash equilibrium [18]). It extends these concepts to the sequential setting of extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, which are often represented as "stacked matrix games" (Figure 1). The authors' key insight is that the convergence of online learning algorithms in matrix games can be extended to the full game tree through induction, with error bounds propagating through levels of the tree.

The theoretical contribution is strong and well-structured. The paper progresses logically from matrix games (Lemma 4.6) to repeated games with bounded error (Lemma 4.9), culminating in the main theorem (Theorem 4.11) for extensive-form games. The proof strategy is sound, and the authors appropriately handle the challenges of exploration and error propagation. The simplifying assumption about the complete tree (Section 4) is justified by noting it occurs with probability 1, though this could be more thoroughly addressed.

Empirically, the paper evaluates regret matching (RM) and Exp3 with both mean propagation (RMM, Exp3M) and current sample propagation. The results (Figures 2-3) confirm the theoretical claims: both methods converge to approximate Nash equilibrium, with mean propagation slightly slower but still effective. The empirical analysis on worst-case games (Section 5.2) provides valuable insights into the practical performance of these algorithms.

**Strengths:**
1. **Theoretical novelty:** First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games, addressing a clear gap in the literature.
2. **Rigorous proof structure:** Clear progression from simple matrix games to complex extensive-form games with proper error handling.
3. **Strong empirical validation:** Experiments support the theoretical claims on both randomly generated and worst-case games.
4. **Practical relevance:** Explains why many existing MCTS variants (like those in [11, 12, 13]) likely converge to Nash equilibrium, justifying their empirical success.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Simplified tree-building assumption:** The proof ignores the incremental tree construction process, which is central to MCTS. While the authors argue this happens with probability 1, the practical implications of this assumption aren't thoroughly explored.
2. **Limited empirical scope:** Experiments focus on small games (depth ≤ 2, branching factor ≤ 2), which may not reflect real-world applications. Larger games would better test the practical significance of the convergence guarantees.
3. **No finite-time bounds:** The paper proves eventual convergence but doesn't provide finite-time convergence rates, which would be valuable for practitioners.
4. **Mean propagation tradeoff:** The paper notes mean propagation converges slightly slower (Section 5.1) but doesn't explain this tradeoff between theoretical guarantees and empirical speed.

**Comparison to prior NIPS work:** This paper stands apart from previous NIPS MCTS work (e.g., [14, 17, 20]) by providing formal convergence guarantees rather than empirical results. While [14] introduced UCT for sequential games, this paper extends the theoretical foundation to simultaneous move games, a significant advance. The connection to Hannan consistency ([15, 16]) is particularly well-motivated and builds on established online learning theory.

**Significance:** This work has substantial significance for both theory and practice. Theoretically, it bridges online learning and game theory for a class of games previously lacking rigorous analysis. Practically, it provides a foundation for using MCTS in security games, general game-playing, and other applications where simultaneous moves are common. The authors' conjecture that many existing MCTS variants converge to Nash equilibrium is plausible and would be valuable to verify.

**Pro/con for acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Well-structured, rigorous theoretical contribution
- Strong empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Clear connection to established online learning theory
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature

*Con:*
- Limited empirical evaluation on small games
- Tree-building assumption not fully justified for practical implementation
- No finite-time convergence bounds
- Mean propagation slower than current practice without clear explanation

**Recommendation:** Accept. This is a high-quality theoretical contribution that addresses a significant gap in the literature. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded to larger games, the theoretical advances are substantial and well-supported. The paper makes a clear, important contribution to understanding MCTS in games with simultaneous moves, with implications for both theory and practice in algorithmic game theory. The connection to Hannan consistency is particularly elegant and well-executed, making this a valuable addition to the NIPS literature.
