This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are popular and empirically successful in this domain, a formal guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been a notable open problem. The authors tackle this by proposing a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree selection policy can be instantiated by any suitable algorithm for matrix games.

The main contribution is a formal proof showing that if this selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the overall SM-MCTS procedure is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively on the game tree's depth, leveraging foundational results from online learning. It begins by showing that ε-Hannan consistent players converge to an approximate equilibrium in a single matrix game (the leaves of the tree). It then demonstrates that this convergence holds even when payoffs have bounded error, which models the situation for nodes higher up in the tree whose children's values are estimated from the MCTS process. The paper empirically evaluates this claim using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as instantiations of ε-Hannan consistent selection policies, confirming the theoretical convergence and comparing different algorithmic variants.

This work connects well with previous research. It fills a crucial theoretical gap for a widely used class of algorithms, especially since prior work [17] has shown that the popular UCB1 selection policy fails to converge in simultaneous move games. It builds upon the rich literature of regret minimization in repeated games ([15], [16]) by innovatively using these algorithms as a component within a larger search framework for extensive-form games.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is the core strength, providing the first known convergence proof for MCTS in this game class. The proof strategy, based on induction and treating subgame value estimates as payoffs with bounded error, is sound and elegant. The claims are well-supported by the outlined proof structure. The authors are also careful in their evaluation, noting that their proof requires a specific variant of MCTS (propagating mean values, denoted RMM/Exp3M) and then empirically comparing it to the more standard practice of propagating the current sample's value (RM/Exp3). This demonstrates intellectual honesty and adds depth to the analysis.

One point of concern lies in the main result, Theorem 4.11. The theorem states a dependency on the game depth D as O(D^2) in the approximation error. However, the accompanying textual sketch of the proof suggests a linear accumulation of error ("On level d from the leaf nodes, the errors... will be in the order of d... Summing the possible gain... leads to the bound"). This suggests a linear dependency on D, not quadratic. This discrepancy could be a typo or a subtlety lost in the summary, but it warrants clarification as it affects the tightness of the bound. Furthermore, the analysis is asymptotic, providing no finite-time convergence rates, which the authors rightly identify as an area for future work.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It logically progresses from background and definitions to the algorithm, the formal analysis, and the empirical results. The motivation is laid out compellingly in the introduction, and the distinction between the general MCTS template and its specific instantiations is helpful. Algorithm 1 provides a clear and concise description of the core procedure. The empirical results are presented cleanly in graphs with logarithmic scales that effectively illustrate the convergence trends. The aforementioned point about the O(D^2) bound is the primary area where clarity could be improved.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. The primary contribution—a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games—is a novel and significant result. While MCTS has been applied to these games before, and algorithms like Regret Matching are well-established, the synthesis of these two areas through the lens of Hannan consistency is new. This work establishes a formal bridge between the theory of online learning for single-stage decisions and recursive, simulation-based search for multi-stage games, which is a valuable conceptual contribution.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. It places the use of MCTS in simultaneous-move games on solid theoretical ground, transforming it from a successful heuristic into a provably correct approximation algorithm. Researchers and practitioners in areas like general game playing and computational game theory are likely to use and build on these ideas. The theoretical framework can guide the design of new MCTS algorithms with guaranteed properties. The empirical finding that the standard sample-propagating MCTS also converges (and often faster) is an important practical result that also suggests that the theoretical analysis could likely be extended, opening up clear avenues for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in an important class of games where such guarantees were missing.
*   The connection between local ε-Hannan consistency and global convergence to a Nash equilibrium is a novel and powerful insight.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-supported by both theory and experiments.
*   The results are significant and will likely be influential in the fields of search, planning, and computational game theory.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is asymptotic and does not provide finite-time convergence bounds.
*   There is a lack of clarity regarding the dependency of the error bound on the game depth (D vs. D^2), which should be addressed.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that solves an important open problem with a rigorous and elegant theoretical analysis, complemented by insightful experiments. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which can be addressed with a small revision. I strongly recommend acceptance.
