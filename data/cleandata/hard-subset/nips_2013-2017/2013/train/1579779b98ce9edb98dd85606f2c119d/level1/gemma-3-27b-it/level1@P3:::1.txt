This paper presents a convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The core contribution is proving that, under certain conditions (specifically, the use of a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration), the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The authors support this theoretical result with empirical validation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods, applied to both randomly generated and worst-case game instances.

The paper is well-motivated. MCTS is a popular technique for these types of games, but a theoretical understanding of its convergence properties has been lacking. The problem of applying MCTS to simultaneous-move games is indeed more complex than the standard sequential case, and the authors correctly identify this gap in the literature. The connection to Hannan consistency is insightful, providing a robust framework for analyzing the algorithm's behavior. The inclusion of both theoretical and empirical evaluation strengthens the paper.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The formal proof of convergence is a significant achievement. The authors carefully define the necessary conditions and provide a detailed analysis.  The connection to Hannan consistency provides a well-established foundation.
* **Clear Presentation of the Algorithm:** Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the core MCTS loop for simultaneous move games, making it easy to understand the proposed template.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments confirm the theoretical results and demonstrate the practical viability of the approach. The consideration of worst-case games is a good test of robustness.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately position their work within the broader context of game theory and MCTS, citing relevant prior research (e.g., backward induction, UCT, regret matching, Exp3).
* **Well-defined problem:** The paper tackles a clearly defined problem with relevance to a growing area of research (game playing, security).

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Proof:** While rigorous, the proof is dense and lengthy, particularly the sections concerning repeated matrix games with bounded error.  While an appendix is mentioned, navigating such a complex proof requires significant effort from the reader.  Providing more intuition or high-level summaries within the main text would improve accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments primarily focus on games with relatively small depth (2) and branching factor (2).  While this allows for exhaustive testing, it is unclear how well the results generalize to more complex game instances.  Scaling up the experiments to larger, more realistic games would be valuable.
* **Exploration Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the exploration parameter γ in Exp3 and regret matching can significantly impact performance. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the convergence rate to different values of γ. A more systematic study of this parameter would be beneficial.
* **Comparison to Existing MCTS Variants:** The paper acknowledges that other MCTS variants exist for this class of games (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction) but doesn't provide a detailed comparative analysis beyond stating that they likely also converge. A direct comparison on the benchmark games would strengthen the paper. Specifically, contrasting the results with the algorithm from Lanctot et al. (2014) would be a valuable addition.
* **Practical implications:** The convergence bounds established are likely quite loose.  While theoretically important, it's not clear how useful these bounds are for practical applications, where the goal is often rapid convergence to a good (but not necessarily optimal) solution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of game-theoretic algorithms.
* The theoretical contribution (convergence proof) is substantial and novel.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented (despite the complexity of the proof).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The complexity of the proof might hinder reproducibility and full understanding for some readers.
* The limited empirical scope raises questions about the generalizability of the results.
* The lack of detailed comparison with existing MCTS variants leaves some questions unanswered.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The convergence proof is a major achievement, and the empirical validation provides confidence in the results. While the paper has some weaknesses, particularly regarding the complexity of the proof and the limited empirical scope, these are outweighed by its strengths.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors consider:

1.  Adding more intuitive explanations to the proof, perhaps with intermediate lemmas stated more clearly.
2.  Expanding the empirical evaluation to include larger game instances and a comparison with existing MCTS variants (Lanctot et al., 2014).
3.  Performing a sensitivity analysis of the exploration parameter γ.
4. Discussing the practical implications of the convergence bounds (e.g., how they relate to achievable performance in real-world applications).
