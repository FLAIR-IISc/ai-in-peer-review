This paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the study of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for its use in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) and prove that if the in-node action selection strategy is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the algorithm as a whole converges to a subgame perfect approximate Nash Equilibrium (NE). This result connects the well-understood field of online learning in repeated matrix games to the more complex setting of search in extensive-form games. The core of their proof relies on an elegant inductive argument over the depth of the game tree. They first establish that ε-Hannan consistent players converge to an approximate NE in repeated matrix games with bounded errors in observed payoffs. They then show that the MCTS framework, when propagating mean values up the tree, creates precisely this scenario: nodes at a given level play a matrix game where the payoffs are the noisy (but boundedly so) estimated values of the child subgames. The paper instantiates and empirically validates this framework using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as selection strategies, confirming convergence on random and empirically-derived worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The primary strength of this paper is its fundamental contribution. While MCTS variants have been applied to simultaneous move games in practice, their theoretical properties were unknown; indeed, popular methods like UCT were known to fail. This work provides a solid theoretical foundation, justifying the use of MCTS in this domain from a game-theoretic perspective. The result is both novel and important for the intersection of game theory, search, and online learning.

2.  **Generality of the Framework:** The authors wisely frame their proof around the abstract property of ε-Hannan consistency rather than tying it to a specific algorithm. This makes the result powerful and broadly applicable to any online learning algorithm satisfying this property, extending its relevance beyond just RM and Exp3.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and well-structured. The reduction of the extensive-form game problem to a series of repeated matrix games with bounded error (Section 4.3) is a key conceptual insight that makes the inductive proof possible. The lemmas are presented logically, building progressively towards the main theorem.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, the definitions are precise, and the structure of the formal analysis is explained before diving into the details. Algorithm 1 provides a clear template for the methods under consideration.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Error Accumulation:** The sketch of the main proof for Theorem 4.11 is quite brief. It states that the error accumulates, leading to a bound of (D^2 * ε + δ). The jump to a quadratic dependency on the depth D is not immediately obvious from the sketch, which seems to imply a linear summation of errors. A more detailed explanation in the main text of how errors from lower levels compound to affect the bounds at higher levels would greatly improve the paper's self-containment and clarity. Is this bound tight, or is it an artifact of the proof technique?

2.  **Assumption of a Static, Complete Tree:** The analysis makes the simplifying assumption that the game tree is completely built before the learning process begins at each node. The authors argue this is acceptable because guaranteed exploration ensures all nodes are eventually created and visited infinitely often. However, this sidesteps the dynamic interplay between tree expansion and value estimation, which is at the heart of MCTS. The "bounded error" `c` for a parent node depends on the quality of the value estimates of its children, which in turn depends on the number of simulations passed through them. In practice, `c` is not fixed but decreases as `t -> infinity`. A discussion of how this dynamic affects the analysis—even an informal one—would strengthen the connection between the theory and the practical algorithm.

3.  **Mean vs. Sample Value Propagation:** The theoretical proof requires propagating the mean value of a child node (`Xh/nh`), whereas standard MCTS practice and some prior work often propagate the result of the single current simulation. The authors' experiments interestingly show that propagating the single sample (RM) converges slightly faster than propagating the mean (RMM), which their theory requires. This raises an important question: does the proof fail for single-sample propagation, or could it be extended? A brief discussion on the theoretical challenges of analyzing the single-sample case would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Convergence Rate vs. Quality:** The paper proves asymptotic convergence to an equilibrium of a certain quality (the ε' in ε'-NE). However, it does not analyze the finite-time convergence *rate*. While this is a difficult problem and likely beyond the scope of a single paper, it is the more critical question for practical applications. The empirical results provide a glimpse, but the paper would benefit from being more explicit that the theoretical bounds are on the quality of the final strategy, not the speed of reaching it.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel theoretical contribution to the MCTS literature. It formally establishes the convergence of a general class of MCTS algorithms for an important class of games where such guarantees were previously lacking. The technical approach is insightful and the results are well-supported by experiments. Despite some minor weaknesses regarding the clarity of the proof sketch and simplifying assumptions, the core contribution is strong. This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   First formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Introduces a general and elegant framework based on ε-Hannan consistency.
*   Technically sound and well-presented.
*   Addresses a clear gap in the literature with significant implications for both researchers and practitioners.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on a simplified model (static tree) that abstracts away some of the dynamics of MCTS.
*   The proof for the main theorem is only sketched in the main body, with the crucial error accumulation step being somewhat opaque.
