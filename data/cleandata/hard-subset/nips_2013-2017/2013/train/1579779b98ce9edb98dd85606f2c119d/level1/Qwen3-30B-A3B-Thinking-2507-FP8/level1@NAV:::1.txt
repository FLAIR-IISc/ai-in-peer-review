

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) convergence in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as the selection method. Their key theoretical contribution is proving that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The authors empirically validate this claim using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated games and worst-case examples, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria.

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a critical gap in the literature—previous work on MCTS (e.g., Kocsis and Szepesvári, 2006; Shafiei et al., 2013) focused exclusively on sequential games without simultaneous moves, and no convergence guarantees existed for this important class of games. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with a well-structured induction proof that builds from matrix game convergence to extensive-form games. The empirical evaluation effectively supports the theoretical claims, demonstrating that both selection methods converge to approximate Nash equilibria, with the mean-propagation variant (RMM) showing slightly slower convergence than current-sample variants.

Strengths include strong theoretical grounding, clear connection to online learning literature (Hannan consistency), and practical relevance to applications like game-playing and security domains. The paper is well-written with precise definitions and sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.

Weaknesses include the assumption of complete tree construction (which may not hold in practice), lack of finite-time convergence bounds, and limited comparison with other MCTS variants beyond the theoretical scope. The empirical analysis of worst-case games is valuable but could include more game types.

The work makes a novel contribution to game theory and algorithmic design, advancing the state of the art in understanding MCTS behavior. It's significant for practitioners using MCTS in simultaneous-move games and for researchers seeking theoretical foundations for simulation-based algorithms. The paper meets all quality criteria and is well-positioned for NIPS.

Pro: Novel theoretical contribution addressing a significant gap, technically sound proof, strong empirical validation, clear connections to prior work.
Con: Limited practical considerations regarding tree construction, no finite-time bounds, some empirical comparisons could be expanded.

Accept.
