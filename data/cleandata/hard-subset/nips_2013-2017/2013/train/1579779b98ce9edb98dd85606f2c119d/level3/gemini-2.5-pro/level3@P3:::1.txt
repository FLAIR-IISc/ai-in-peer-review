This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. This is an important class of games for which MCTS is a popular practical algorithm, yet its convergence properties have been poorly understood. The authors present a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) and prove its convergence to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The core theoretical result is that convergence is guaranteed if the action selection mechanism within each node is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration. The paper validates this result empirically using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods, demonstrating convergence on both randomly generated and empirically identified worst-case games. This work provides a much-needed theoretical justification for using MCTS in this domain, connecting the algorithm's behavior to established concepts in online learning and regret minimization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of building their theoretical argument, starting from the simple case of repeated matrix games, extending it to games with bounded error, and finally using induction to prove the main theorem for multi-stage extensive-form games. This step-by-step approach makes the complex proof accessible and easy to follow.

2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The main contribution—providing a sufficient condition (ε-Hannan consistency) for the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous move games—is a significant and useful result. It provides a formal basis for the use of MCTS in this setting and offers clear guidance for designing new, provably convergent variants. This is a solid step forward from prior work, which has been largely empirical or focused on purely sequential games.

3.  **Empirical Support:** The experimental section is well-designed and provides convincing support for the paper's claims. The comparison between propagating the mean value (as required by the proof) and propagating the current sample value (common in practice) is insightful. The search for and analysis of "worst-case" games adds depth to the evaluation and strengthens the empirical conclusions.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Novelty of Proof Technique:** While the application of the theoretical tools is novel, the core proof technique itself relies heavily on established results from the online learning literature, namely that Hannan-consistent players converge to a Nash equilibrium in repeated games. The main technical steps involve showing that this property is robust to the noise introduced by sampling in subgames. While sound, this approach feels more like a careful extension of existing theory to a new domain rather than a fundamentally new proof technique, which may limit its perceived originality.

2.  **Omission of UCB/UCT Analysis:** The paper dismisses UCB/UCT—by far the most popular family of MCTS algorithms—in a single sentence, citing prior work showing its non-convergence. While correct, this feels like a missed opportunity. A more detailed discussion on *why* UCB fails where regret-minimizing approaches succeed would have provided valuable context and insight. For example, is the failure mode fixable? Could a UCB variant be designed to satisfy the ε-Hannan consistency property? A comparative analysis, even at a high level, would significantly strengthen the paper's impact and relevance to the broader MCTS community.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Experiments:** A key weakness is the disconnect between the formal proof and the experimental results. The theoretical guarantee of convergence is provided only for the variant where the *mean* of the subgame's rewards is propagated up the tree (SM-MCTS-M). However, the experiments show that the more standard approach of propagating the *current sample's* value also converges, and often does so faster. The paper notes this but offers no explanation for why this discrepancy exists or why the standard variant works in practice. This gap leaves an important question unanswered. The authors should at least provide a conjecture or discussion on how the proof might be extended to cover the standard case.

**Evaluation Summary:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, with a clear theorem and proof structure. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and a solid set of experiments. The work is complete and rigorously presented.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is very clearly written, well-organized, and the main ideas are communicated effectively. The notation is consistent and the inductive proof structure is easy to follow.
*   **Originality:** Moderate. The paper is the first to provide a formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in this class of games, which is a novel and important contribution. However, the theoretical machinery used in the proof is largely adapted from existing work on regret minimization and online learning, making the technical approach somewhat incremental.
*   **Significance:** Good. The results are important for researchers working on MCTS and algorithmic game theory, as they provide a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used algorithm. The sufficient condition for convergence is a practical and useful guideline. The significance could have been higher with a more thorough treatment of UCT and an explanation for the convergence of the standard MCTS variant.

Overall, this is a good paper that makes a valuable contribution to the MCTS literature. It provides the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a noteworthy achievement. The main weaknesses are a potential lack of technical novelty in the proof itself and a gap between what is proven theoretically and what is shown to work best empirically. Despite these points, the paper is a solid and welcome addition to the field.
