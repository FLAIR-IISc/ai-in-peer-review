Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that can use essentially any no-regret (ε-Hannan-consistent) selection algorithm at each node (e.g., regret matching, Exp3). The main theoretical result shows that, provided guaranteed exploration, SM-MCTS with mean-value backup converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in the limit. The proof proceeds by induction on depth: (i) no-regret learners in matrix games converge to approximate equilibria; (ii) this persists under bounded payoff errors; and (iii) composing these along the tree yields asymptotic convergence with an error that scales with depth. The analysis explicitly requires propagating mean returns rather than single-sample returns.

Empirically, the paper compares “mean-value propagation” against the more standard “current-sample propagation,” and evaluates regret matching and Exp3 on random games and small adversarially selected games. All variants appear to converge empirically; mean-value propagation converges slightly slower than sample propagation.

Relative to prior work, this fills a notable gap: UCT-style analyses do not cover simultaneous moves, and UCB selection can fail in such settings (Shafiei et al.). The approach is aligned with no-regret ideas common in normal-form learning and CFR-style reasoning but tailored to perfect-information simultaneous-move EFGs. To my knowledge at the time of writing, this is the first general asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The asymptotic convergence proof is careful and builds on standard no-regret tools, adapted to repeated games with bounded error and then lifted to trees via induction. The exploration wrapper and the distinction between empirical frequencies and average strategies are handled cleanly. I did not find logical gaps.
- Scope and limitations: The result is asymptotic; no explicit finite-sample convergence rates are provided. The need to assume complete tree expansion “eventually” is reasonable for asymptotics but sidesteps sample-complexity questions central for practice.
- Experiments: Well-aligned with the theory and designed to probe convergence behavior and the effect of mean vs sample backups. The games are small (by necessity for exhaustive worst-case search) and random ensembles are reasonable. The finding that mean-backup slightly underperforms is clearly documented.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The algorithmic template and selection methods are described at a level reproducible by an expert reader. The proof roadmap is easy to follow, and the appendices reportedly contain full details. Minor notation inconsistencies and a few typos are noted below.

Originality
- Conceptually: The general “plug any ε-Hannan-consistent selection rule into SM-MCTS” with an asymptotic convergence guarantee for simultaneous-move games is new and timely.
- Technically: The induction via repeated games with bounded errors is a neat and reusable proof pattern. While no-regret to equilibrium is classical in matrix games, its composition across a perfect-information simultaneous-move tree is a substantive extension.

Significance
- The result provides the first general asymptotic justification for SM-MCTS, which is practically important in domains with simultaneous actions.
- Impact is tempered by the lack of rates; many practitioners will want explicit iteration complexity as a function of depth, branching factor, exploration, and the regret rate of the base algorithm. Still, the result gives a unifying theoretical foundation and suggests a broad class of convergent variants.

Strengths
- General and modular: works with any ε-Hannan-consistent selection policy with guaranteed exploration.
- First asymptotic convergence result for SM-MCTS; extends known insights beyond sequential-move trees where UCT-style analyses exist.
- Clean, principled proof structure (matrix → matrix with error → tree).
- Empirical study is sensible and probes issues raised by the theory (mean vs sample backups).
- Well written and technically careful.

Weaknesses
- No finite-sample rates; the main theorem provides only asymptotic convergence with depth-dependent constants.
- The requirement to back up mean values seems to slow convergence empirically, and it is unclear whether the theory can be extended to sample-value backups (the variant used more often in practice).
- Experiments are on small games, which is understandable but limits generality of empirical takeaways.
- The “eventual full tree” assumption is not quantified; time-to-full-expansion may dominate in large games.

Questions for the authors
- Finite-sample rates: If the base selection algorithm has a per-node regret bound R(T) (e.g., O(√T) for Exp3/RM), can you derive an explicit bound on exploitability after T simulations as a function of depth D, branching factors, exploration γ, and R(T)? Even conservative rates (poly(D) times R(T) plus exploration bias) would be valuable.
- Bias/variance tradeoff in backups: Can the analysis be extended to allow a convex combination of mean and sample backup (e.g., a Polyak averaged target) that might reduce the slowdown while retaining the proof?
- Exploration: Your A* wrapper does not update statistics on exploratory moves. Does updating during exploration (common in practice) break any steps, or would Lemma 4.4 still go through with minor modifications?
- Best-response computation in experiments: How exactly is exploitability computed in simultaneous-move trees—full backward induction at evaluation checkpoints? Any approximation error?
- Tree growth: Can you provide concentration-style arguments bounding the time to full expansion under your guaranteed exploration?

Suggestions
- Consider reporting confidence bands around exploitability curves and sensitivity to γ and learning rates (for Exp3).
- Discuss whether the constants in Theorem 4.11 (scaling like D) are tight, and whether subgame-perfectness forces this scaling.

Minor typographical/notation issues
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Line 144: “negated utilities,” not “inverted.”
- Algorithm 1, line 10: (a1, a2) should be (i, j) or (σ1, σ2); ensure consistent action vs. strategy notation throughout.
- There are a few other (i, j) vs. a notation inconsistencies; a pass for consistency would help.

Pros for acceptance
- First general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS; broadens theoretical understanding.
- Clean, correct analysis with a modular template applicable to many selection rules.
- Empirical study supports the theory and provides insights on backup choices.

Cons for acceptance
- No rates; practical guidance on iteration complexity is missing.
- Empirical games are small; mean-backup underperforms the commonly used sample-backup.
- Incremental relative to well-known no-regret convergence in matrix games and sequential-move MCTS results.

Overall assessment
A well-written, technically solid paper that answers an important open question for SM-MCTS by leveraging no-regret learning. I have a mild reservation about impact due to the absence of finite-time rates and the incremental nature relative to known sequential-move analyses, but I still view this as a valuable contribution that will interest the NeurIPS community.
