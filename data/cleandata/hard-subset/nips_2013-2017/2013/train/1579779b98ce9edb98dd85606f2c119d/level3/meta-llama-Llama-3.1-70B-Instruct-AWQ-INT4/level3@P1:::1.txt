This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of game theory and Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. The authors provide a thorough analysis of MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, proving that the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium under certain conditions. The paper is well-written, and the authors do an excellent job of explaining the technical details of their work.

The main contribution of the paper is the proof that MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium in the considered class of games. This result is significant because it provides a theoretical foundation for the use of MCTS in these types of games. The authors also provide empirical evidence to support their theoretical result, demonstrating that the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium in practice.

One potential concern with the paper is that the proof technique may not be particularly novel. While the authors do provide a clear and well-written explanation of their proof, it is possible that the underlying ideas are not particularly new or groundbreaking. However, this does not detract from the significance of the result, which is still an important contribution to the field.

Another limitation of the paper is that it does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms. The authors should consider comparing their results to existing analyses of UCB variants, as this would provide a more complete understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of different MCTS algorithms.

The paper also raises an interesting question about the propagation of mean values versus current sample values in MCTS. The theoretical result only proves convergence for the propagation of the mean, yet the experiments show that current sample-value propagation also works. The authors should provide more explanation for this discrepancy, as it is not entirely clear why the two approaches should yield similar results.

Overall, the paper is technically interesting, but the contribution appears to be incremental rather than groundbreaking. The authors provide a useful sufficient condition for convergence, but the result is not particularly surprising or novel. However, the paper is still a valuable contribution to the field, and the authors should be commended for their thorough analysis and clear writing.
