## Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors establish a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting and prove that, under certain conditions—specifically, employing a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration—the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The theoretical results are empirically validated through experiments on randomly generated and worst-case games using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods.

The work addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, which, despite its practical success, lacked formal convergence guarantees outside of two-player perfect-information sequential games. The presentation is clear and well-structured, effectively outlining the problem, contributions, and experimental setup. The connection to related work, particularly in algorithmic game theory and MCTS variants, is also well-established. The proof technique, leveraging -Hannan consistency and inductive reasoning, is a logical approach to tackle the complexity of simultaneous-move games. 

However, the novelty of the proof technique itself is a concern. While sound, the application of -Hannan consistency to this context feels somewhat straightforward, and the review lacks discussion of related work employing similar techniques in other domains. A stronger justification of the technical contribution would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms. Given the existing analyses of UCB in other game-theoretic settings, comparing and contrasting with those findings would strengthen the work. 

A key point requiring clarification is the discrepancy between the theoretical result, which proves convergence for propagation of the *mean*, and the empirical observation that current sample-value propagation also works well. The authors acknowledge this but don’t provide a compelling explanation; further investigation and discussion of this point are necessary.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-presented paper with a useful sufficient condition for convergence. However, the contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Fills a gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Provides a clear and rigorous proof of convergence under reasonable conditions.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical claims.
*   Well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty in the proof technique.
*   Lack of analysis and comparison with UCB/UCT algorithms.
*   Unexplained discrepancy between theoretical guarantees and empirical findings regarding mean vs. sample propagation.
*   Incremental contribution.

**Recommendation:** Accept with minor revisions addressing the concerns outlined above, particularly regarding the novelty of the proof, the exclusion of UCB/UCT, and the explanation for the observed empirical success of sample-value propagation.
