This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, a class that includes games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors introduce a general Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing algorithm for the in-tree selection policy, focusing on regret-matching and Exp3. The central contribution is a proof that this approach converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for the use of MCTS in this game class, advancing beyond more preliminary analyses, such as that of Lanctot et al. (2013), which explored a similar setup but lacked a formal convergence proof.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, exceptionally well-written, and clearly organized. The theoretical analysis, which forms the core of the paper, is presented with admirable clarity, making a complex, multi-step proof accessible. The supporting empirical results, while straightforward, are well-chosen to validate the theoretical claims and explore practical variants (e.g., propagating mean values versus current sample values).
*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary contribution—the formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for MCTS in simultaneous-move games—is both novel and significant. While MCTS has been applied empirically to these games for some time, this work is the first to provide such a formal guarantee, closing an important theoretical gap. The inductive proof technique, which establishes convergence in leaf-node subgames and propagates this guarantee up the game tree, is elegant and effective. This result helps justify the use of MCTS as a principled approximation algorithm for this game class from a game-theoretic perspective.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Insight into Inner-Node Dynamics:** The main weakness of the analysis is that it provides limited insight into the dynamics at the inner nodes of the game tree before convergence. The proof structure relies on the eventual convergence at lower levels of the tree to prove convergence at higher levels. While this inductive step is valid for proving the final result, it does not deeply characterize how the strategy estimates at inner nodes evolve over time, especially during the early and intermediate stages of the search.
*   **Disconnect from Broader Game Theory Literature:** The paper claims convergence to a *subgame perfect* approximate Nash equilibrium, a key concept in extensive-form games. However, the analysis does not fully connect with or leverage the rich body of literature on subgame perfection. The result is proven from first principles within an online learning framework, but it misses an opportunity to situate the work more broadly within algorithmic game theory by relating it to other equilibrium concepts and established results for this class of games. This makes the contribution feel somewhat isolated within the MCTS community rather than serving as a strong bridge to the game theory community.

**Constructive Suggestions:**

To strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding and impact, I suggest the authors more explicitly connect their analysis to the existing literature on subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games. Citing foundational work on the existence of such equilibria (e.g., Harris, "On the Existence of Subgame-Perfect Equilibrium in Games of Perfect Information," Econometrica, 1990) would provide important context and strengthen the theoretical basis of the work.

Furthermore, the discussion could be enriched by briefly contrasting the properties of the MCTS-derived equilibrium with those found by classical backward induction algorithms, which often rely on computationally expensive solvers (e.g., linear programming) at each node. This would better highlight the trade-offs and positioning of the MCTS approach.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable and novel theoretical contribution to the MCTS literature. The weaknesses identified are primarily opportunities for improvement and do not detract from the core result.
