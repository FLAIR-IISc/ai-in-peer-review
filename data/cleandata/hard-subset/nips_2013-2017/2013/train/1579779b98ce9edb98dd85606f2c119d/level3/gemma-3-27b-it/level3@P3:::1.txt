This paper investigates the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors establish a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under the condition that the selection method is -Hannan consistent and satisfies exploration requirements. The results are empirically validated on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios.

The work builds upon a growing body of research applying MCTS to game-playing, notably extending beyond the well-studied sequential move games. Prior work, such as [13, 11, 10], demonstrates MCTS’s practical success in these games, but lacked formal convergence guarantees. This paper addresses that gap, providing a theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The connection to regret-minimizing procedures from algorithmic game theory, specifically -Hannan consistency [15, 16], is a clever and central contribution.  Related work on solving simultaneous-move games exactly, such as backward induction [7, 4] and double-oracle methods [9], is well-cited and contrasted with the practicality of MCTS when domain knowledge is limited.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Soundness:** The proof of convergence under the stated conditions is the paper’s core strength.  It provides a valuable guarantee for MCTS’s behavior in a previously unanalyzed setting.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The definitions are precise, and the notation is consistent. Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the general MCTS template.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments support the theoretical results, demonstrating convergence on both random and worst-case games. The comparison of propagating the mean versus sample values offers useful insights.
* **Useful Sufficient Condition:** Establishing -Hannan consistency as a sufficient condition for convergence is a valuable contribution, providing a concrete criterion for algorithm design.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Novelty of Proof Technique:** While the result itself is valuable, the proof technique, relying heavily on -Hannan consistency and repeated game analysis, appears to be a fairly standard approach in online learning and algorithmic game theory. The novelty seems to lie in *applying* these techniques to MCTS in simultaneous move games, rather than in developing fundamentally new proof methods.
* **UCT/UCB Omission:** The paper explicitly avoids discussing UCB/UCT algorithms [14], despite their widespread use in MCTS.  Given existing analyses of UCB in related settings (although not directly in simultaneous move games), a discussion of why these algorithms are not covered by the theoretical framework, or a comparison of their empirical performance, would strengthen the paper.  Shafiei et al. [17] is correctly cited as showing UCB's lack of convergence in these games, but further discussion of why the authors chose not to investigate it further would be beneficial.
* **Mean vs. Sample Propagation Discrepancy:** The theoretical result proves convergence for propagation of the *mean*, whereas the experiments show that propagating the current *sample* value also works well. The paper acknowledges this discrepancy but doesn’t provide a compelling explanation.  Is the convergence rate simply slower with sample propagation?  Are there conditions under which the sample value propagation is also guaranteed to converge? This requires further investigation.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While solid and correct, the contribution feels somewhat incremental. The theoretical result, while important, doesn't represent a major paradigm shift in MCTS or game-theoretic algorithms.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, providing convergence guarantees for a previously unanalyzed class of games.
* The results are clearly presented, well-supported by empirical evidence, and build upon a strong foundation of related work.
* The work is likely to be of interest to researchers in both game theory and artificial intelligence.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The proof technique lacks significant novelty.
* The omission of UCB/UCT is a notable weakness, given their prominence in the field.
* The discrepancy between the theoretical result (mean propagation) and the experimental findings (sample propagation) requires further explanation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good, technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games.  However, it is not a groundbreaking result. The lack of novelty in the proof technique and the omission of a discussion of UCB/UCT hold it back from being truly outstanding. I recommend **acceptance**, but with encouragement to address the weaknesses mentioned above in a revised version. Specifically, a more detailed explanation of the sample propagation discrepancy and a discussion of the relationship to UCB-based algorithms would significantly improve the paper.
