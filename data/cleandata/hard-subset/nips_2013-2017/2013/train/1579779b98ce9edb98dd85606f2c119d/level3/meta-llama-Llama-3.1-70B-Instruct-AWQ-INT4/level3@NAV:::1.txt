This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games and formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The main contribution of the paper is the formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a sufficient condition for convergence, which is a significant contribution to the field.

However, the proof technique may lack novelty, as it builds upon existing results in the field. Additionally, the paper does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms, which is a limitation of the study. The authors should consider comparing UCB variants with their proposed algorithm, as existing analyses have shown that UCB can be effective in certain situations.

Another concern is that the theoretical result only proves convergence for propagation of the mean, yet experiments show that current sample-value propagation also works. This discrepancy needs to be explained, as it may indicate a limitation of the theoretical analysis.

Overall, the paper is technically interesting, but the contribution appears incremental. The authors have built upon existing results and provided a formal analysis of MCTS algorithms in a specific class of games. While the paper is well-written and clearly presented, it may not be highly outstanding in terms of originality or significance.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a formal analysis of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.
* The authors present a sufficient condition for convergence, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proof technique may lack novelty, as it builds upon existing results in the field.
* The paper does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms, which is a limitation of the study.
* The theoretical result only proves convergence for propagation of the mean, yet experiments show that current sample-value propagation also works.
* The contribution appears incremental, building upon existing results in the field.

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the concerns mentioned above, particularly the lack of novelty in the proof technique and the limitation of not analyzing UCB/UCT algorithms. Additionally, the authors should provide a clear explanation for the discrepancy between the theoretical result and the experimental findings.
