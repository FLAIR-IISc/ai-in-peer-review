## Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms applied to zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The core contribution lies in proving that MCTS, when instantiated with any ε-Hannan consistent selection method and satisfying certain exploration requirements, converges to an ε-Nash equilibrium. This is a significant step forward as theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games have been previously lacking. The paper is well-written and technically sound, clearly presenting the theoretical framework and relating it to existing work in game theory and MCTS. 

The authors effectively position their work within the broader context of algorithmic game theory, referencing relevant literature on security games, general game-playing, and existing MCTS variants. The proof strategy, leveraging the properties of Hannan consistency, is clearly outlined, and the discussion of the limitations – specifically the asymptotic nature of the convergence result – is honest and appropriate. The empirical evaluation, comparing mean-value propagation to sample-value propagation, provides some practical insight, though the observed slight underperformance of the mean-value approach is not particularly striking. The attempt to assess empirical convergence rates through worst-case game analysis is valuable, even if it doesn’t yield explicit rates.

However, a key weakness is the absence of explicit convergence rates. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest it may be inherent given the reliance on ε-Hannan consistency, it significantly impacts the practical significance of the result. It would be beneficial to explore whether stronger regret-rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP3) could lead to more concrete convergence bounds. Furthermore, the contribution feels somewhat incremental given the existing theoretical understanding of MCTS in sequential-move games. While extending these results to simultaneous moves is valuable, the impact is lessened by the asymptotic nature of the convergence guarantee. 

Finally, there are a few minor issues: Figure 1 incorrectly labels “leafs” as “leaves,” line 144 uses “inverted” instead of “negated,” and Algorithm 1, line 10, uses (a1, a2) where (i, j) or (σ1, σ2) would be more consistent with the notation used elsewhere. These inconsistencies, while minor, detract slightly from the overall clarity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and correct.
*   Addresses an important open problem in MCTS theory.
*   Clear presentation and connection to related work.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of explicit convergence rates.
*   Incremental nature of the contribution.
*   Minor notational inconsistencies.



**Overall Assessment:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational issues. While the lack of convergence rates is a limitation, the theoretical contribution is valuable, and the paper is well-executed.
