This paper presents a general template of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games and proves that if the algorithm uses an -Hannan consistent selection function, which assures attempting each action infinitely many times, the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium of the extensive form game. The authors also empirically evaluate this claim using two different -Hannan consistent procedures: regret matching and Exp3.

The main contribution of this paper is the proof of asymptotic convergence to ε-Nash for simultaneous-move games. This result is significant, as it provides a theoretical guarantee for the convergence of MCTS algorithms in this class of games. The authors also provide empirical evidence to support their theoretical results, demonstrating the convergence of the algorithms in practice.

The paper is well-written and technically correct. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their results, and the proofs are thorough and well-organized. The empirical evaluation is also well-designed, providing insight into the performance of the algorithms in practice.

One limitation of the paper is the lack of convergence rates. The authors only provide an asymptotic convergence result, without providing any explicit convergence rates. This may be a limitation of the ε-Hannan consistency assumption, but it would be useful to explore whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP) yield explicit convergence rates.

The empirical evaluation also provides some interesting insights. The authors compare propagating mean reward values versus propagating current sample values, and find that mean-value propagation slightly underperforms sample-value propagation. This suggests that the choice of propagation method may have a significant impact on the performance of the algorithms.

The paper also attempts to provide insight into empirical convergence rates. The authors perform a brute force search through all games of depth 2 with branching factor 2 and utilities from the set {0, 0.5, 1}, and find that the convergence rate of the algorithms is similar, with the variants with propagating means always converging a little slower.

Overall, this paper provides a significant contribution to the field of game theory and MCTS algorithms. The theoretical results are well-established, and the empirical evaluation provides useful insights into the performance of the algorithms in practice. However, the lack of convergence rates is a limitation, and future work should aim to address this issue.
