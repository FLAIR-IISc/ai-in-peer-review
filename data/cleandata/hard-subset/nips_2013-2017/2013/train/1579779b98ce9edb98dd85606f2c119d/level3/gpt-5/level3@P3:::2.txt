Summary and relation to prior work
This paper analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron). The authors present a general SM-MCTS template that uses a no-external-regret selection rule at each node (e.g., regret matching or Exp3) and prove almost-sure convergence of the empirical strategies to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the full extensive-form game. The proof proceeds by (i) establishing convergence properties of Hannan-consistent learners in one-shot matrix games, (ii) extending these to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) composing these bounds inductively up the game tree to obtain a subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium. They complement the theory with experiments on random and adversarially chosen small games, showing convergence with regret matching and Exp3, and the practical impact of backing up mean returns versus single-sample returns.

The work builds on and significantly strengthens Lanctot et al. (2013), which introduced SM-MCTS variants for simultaneous-move games and provided preliminary analyses but no convergence guarantees to NE. It also situates itself with respect to empirical successes of UCT while noting that UCB-based selection can fail to converge in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al., 2013).

Strengths
- Clear, well-written paper with careful definitions and a clean algorithmic template.
- Main theoretical contribution is new: a formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS using Hannan-consistent selection (regret matching, Exp3), including a subgame-perfect notion of approximate equilibrium.
- Proof technique is principled and modular: from matrix games to repeated games with bounded error to induction over tree depth.
- Empirical results corroborate the theory and explore practical design choices (mean backups vs. sample backups), across random and challenging instances.
- Useful to practitioners: validates a widely used approach for simultaneous-move perfect-information games, with a general template that admits multiple selection rules.

Weaknesses
- Limited provable insight into the behavior at inner nodes: the analysis treats them via error propagation from leaves rather than structural properties (e.g., equilibrium selection, sensitivity to payoff perturbations).
- Theoretical bounds are qualitative/asymptotic and likely loose (D^2 dependence, 4(c+1) constants), with no finite-sample rates.
- The exposition of subgame perfection is somewhat insular to the constructed induction; it does not connect to classical game-theoretic results on subgame-perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games nor to refinement concepts.
- Experimental scope is modest (small depths/branching factors) and does not compare against strong baselines like CFR-type methods adapted to simultaneous moves beyond citing prior negative results for UCT.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The assumptions (finite game, perfect information, guaranteed exploration, Hannan consistency) are clearly stated. The reduction to repeated games with bounded error and the induction proof are well-motivated. The need to back up mean values for the proof is explicit, with empirical discussion of the trade-off.
- Completeness: The theoretical claims match the presented proofs; experiments support the claims but do not provide finite-time rates.
- Honesty: Limitations are acknowledged (e.g., no finite-time bounds).

Clarity
- Very well organized and readable. Suggestions:
  - Emphasize early that the main limit result is subgame-perfect ε-NE and restate precisely in the introduction.
  - Clarify earlier the role and necessity of mean backups versus sample backups and explicitly flag where the proof breaks without means.
  - Streamline notation around σ̂ (empirical frequencies) vs. σ̄ (average strategies), perhaps limiting to one when possible.

Originality
- Novel formal analysis for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games, extending preliminary work (Lanctot et al., 2013) and going beyond prior negative results for UCB-based selection. The template “MCTS + any Hannan-consistent selector” is conceptually simple yet original in this context.

Significance
- Advances theoretical understanding of a commonly used heuristic in an important game class (simultaneous-move). The result is likely to influence both algorithm design (selection rules) and evaluation in this domain. Stronger bounds or finite-time analyses would further increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Connect to subgame-perfect concepts and equilibrium refinements. While Theorem 4.11 yields subgame-perfect approximate equilibria via induction, placing this in the broader literature on subgame perfection in simultaneous-move games would strengthen the contribution. Consider citing work such as Harris (1990) on existence of subgame-perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move settings and discussing how your construction relates to backward induction, equilibrium selection, and refinements (e.g., trembling-hand perfection).
- Provide at least high-level finite-time guidance (even heuristics) on convergence rates in terms of depth D, branching factor, exploration γ, and regret rates of the selector.
- Expand empirical evaluation with larger depths/branching factors and include comparisons to other regret-minimization-based tree algorithms (e.g., CFR variants adapted to simultaneous moves), beyond UCT.
- Discuss the practical gap between mean versus sample backups: when can sample backups be expected to behave identically asymptotically, and what is lost in the analysis without means?

Pros for acceptance
- First rigorous convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to approximate NE (subgame-perfect) under widely used regret-minimizing selection rules.
- Clear and well-presented theory supported by experiments.
- Broad applicability and practical relevance.

Cons against acceptance
- Asymptotic only; constants and depth dependence are loose; no finite-time rates.
- Limited structural insight into inner-node behavior beyond error compounding.
- Missing connections to broader game-theoretic literature on subgame perfection and refinements.

Overall evaluation
A strong and timely theoretical contribution with clear exposition and supportive experiments. Despite asymptotic and scope limitations, the work substantially advances understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. Recommendation: accept (borderline-to-strong), with minor revisions to strengthen connections to subgame-perfect theory and to discuss finite-time behavior.
