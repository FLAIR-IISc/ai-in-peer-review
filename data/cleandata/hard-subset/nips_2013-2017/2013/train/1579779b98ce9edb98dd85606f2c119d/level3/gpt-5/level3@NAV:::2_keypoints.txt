- Analyzes MCTS in two-player zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron).  
- Proposes a general SM-MCTS template instantiated with regret-minimizing selection rules and guaranteed exploration.  
- Proves almost-sure convergence of SM-MCTS to an approximate Nash equilibrium under guaranteed exploration.  
- Core technical result: if selection is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), induced SM-MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect approximate NE.  
- Empirical results corroborate theory using regret matching and Exp3 and evaluate practical backup choices (means vs samples).  
- Advances prior work (Lanctot et al., 2013) with a substantially stronger, more general formal analysis.  
- Theoretical development is careful and technically sound; uses inductive proof propagating bounded errors from leaves to ancestors.  
- Experiments are appropriately scoped and consistent with theoretical claims.  
- Limitations: no finite-time/sample complexity bounds and a relatively loose depth (D^2) dependence in error accumulation.  
- Paper is clear, well-organized; algorithms and assumptions (Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) are crisply stated.  
- Originality: first general convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games using regret-minimizing selection.  
- Significance: provides theoretical grounding for SM-MCTS variants used in practice and should influence algorithm design and evaluation.  
- Weakness: inductive argument gives limited per-node insight—does not illuminate inner-node dynamics or equilibrium selection.  
- Suggestion: clarify per-depth constants and whether the D^2 dependence can be sharpened.  
- Suggestion: relate the inductive proof to backward induction/subgame-perfect equilibrium literature and cite Harris (1990); discuss equilibrium refinements (e.g., trembling-hand).  
- Suggestion: include (even coarse) finite-sample guarantees to improve practical impact.  
- Arguments for acceptance: first rigorous convergence analysis for SM-MCTS with regret-based selection, clear writing, reproducible methodology, and empirical validation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of finite-time bounds, loose depth dependence, and limited characterization of inner-node behavior.
