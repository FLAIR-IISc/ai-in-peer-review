- Paper provides a formal proof of MCTS convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games using Hannan-consistent selection methods.  
- Builds on Lanctot et al. (2013) and clarifies conditions under which MCTS performs well in these game types.  
- Exceptionally clear writing, precise definitions, consistent notation, and logical progression of analysis.  
- Algorithm presentation (Algorithm 1) and explanations of regret-matching and Exp3 variants are well-executed.  
- Empirical results, though limited, corroborate theoretical findings on random and “worst-case” games.  
- Good contextualization within existing game theory and MCTS literature and thorough theoretical framework.  
- Main theoretical strength: fills a gap by proving convergence for simultaneous-move MCTS beyond sequential-game results.  
- Key weakness: analysis assumes optimal strategies at leaf nodes, limiting insight into MCTS behavior at inner nodes.  
- Key weakness: lack of an explicit connection/proof tying convergence to subgame perfect Nash equilibria.  
- Key weakness: empirical evaluation is narrow, focusing on small games and limited scenarios.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel significant theoretical result, rigorous and clear presentation, and supportive empirical evidence.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited applicability due to inner-node and subgame-perfection gaps and insufficient empirical breadth.  
- Overall assessment: strong, valuable theoretical contribution with clarity that outweighs limitations; authors acknowledge weaknesses.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Requested revisions: expand discussion of the leaf-node inductive limitation and its effects on inner-node insights.  
- Requested revisions: address the subgame-perfect equilibria connection (cite Harris 1990) and clarify whether the proof implies subgame perfection.  
- Requested revisions: add a brief future-work discussion on finite-time convergence bounds and extension to imperfect-information games.
