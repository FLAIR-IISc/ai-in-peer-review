This paper studies the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with different action selection mechanisms. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent (a concept from online learning) and guarantees sufficient exploration, then the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. This general condition is instantiated with two known regret-minimizing procedures: Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. The paper complements this formal analysis with an empirical evaluation on randomly generated and hand-selected "worst-case" games, confirming the convergence and comparing variants of the proposed algorithms. This work addresses an important theoretical gap, as MCTS is widely used in practice for such games, but formal convergence guarantees have been lacking.

**Quality**
The paper is of good technical quality. The theoretical analysis is presented soundly, building its argument from the well-understood case of repeated matrix games, extending it to games with bounded payoff errors, and finally applying this framework inductively to the extensive-form game tree. The proofs appear to be correct. The experimental setup is reasonable, providing support for the theoretical claims and investigating important practical questions, such as the difference between propagating the mean value (as required by the proof) versus the current sample value (as is common in practice).

A weakness in the quality of the work is a disconnect between the theory and the experiments. The formal proof of convergence relies on propagating the mean of all rewards through a subgame (the "RMM" and "Exp3M" variants). However, the experiments show that the more standard MCTS approach of propagating the single, current sample's reward also converges, and often slightly faster. While the authors note this empirical result, they offer no theoretical justification or intuition for why this variant also converges. This leaves a gap in the completeness of the work, as the proven condition seems stricter than what is required in practice.

**Clarity**
The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined with appropriate background and notation. Algorithm 1 provides an excellent, high-level view of the Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) template. The structure of the formal analysis is logical and easy to follow, as it builds from simple to more complex settings. The empirical results are presented clearly with informative graphs. The writing is precise and the claims are stated without ambiguity.

**Originality**
The primary contribution of this paper—a formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games—is original and fills a known gap in the literature. Framing the convergence requirement in terms of the ε-Hannan consistency of the node selection policy is a novel and useful way to connect MCTS theory to the rich literature on online learning and regret minimization.

However, the novelty of the proof *technique* itself may be somewhat limited. The core theoretical machinery relies on established results showing that Hannan-consistent players converge to a Nash equilibrium in repeated games. The main technical step is to extend this to an extensive-form setting by treating the estimated values of subgames as noisy payoffs and showing via induction that the error accumulation is bounded. While this is a valid and valuable application, it could be viewed as an incremental, though non-trivial, extension of existing proof techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach.

**Significance**
The results are significant in that they provide the first formal justification for using a class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games. Practitioners have been using these methods for some time, and this paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for their use as approximation algorithms. The general condition for convergence is also a useful contribution that could guide the design of new MCTS variants for this game class.

The significance is somewhat lessened by two factors. First, the analysis does not cover the most widely used family of MCTS algorithms based on UCT/UCB. The authors correctly note that UCB has been shown not to converge in even simple simultaneous-move settings, but a more thorough discussion or empirical comparison against UCB variants would have helped contextualize the practical importance of the proposed algorithms. Second, the result is an asymptotic convergence guarantee, with no analysis of the convergence rate. The empirical results on "worst-case" games suggest that convergence can be quite slow, which may limit the practical impact. Overall, the paper is a solid, valuable contribution but perhaps not a groundbreaking one that will fundamentally change the field.

**Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in the important class of simultaneous-move extensive-form games.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-organized.
*   Introduces a useful sufficient condition (ε-Hannan consistency) for convergence, linking MCTS to online learning theory.
*   Empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims and investigates practical algorithm variants.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The novelty of the proof technique is moderate; it is largely an application of existing online learning theory to the MCTS setting.
*   The analysis excludes the most popular UCT/UCB variants of MCTS.
*   There is a theoretical gap, as the proof only covers propagating mean values, while experiments show propagating single sample values also works.
*   The contribution feels like a solid, incremental step rather than a major advance.

In conclusion, this is a good paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution by formally proving the convergence of a class of MCTS algorithms in a setting where such guarantees were previously absent. While the contribution could be described as incremental, it is a necessary and well-executed piece of work that strengthens the theoretical foundations of a widely used algorithm. I recommend acceptance.
