This paper presents a formal analysis of the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as a function for selecting the next actions to be sampled. They formally prove that if the algorithm uses an -Hannan consistent selection function, which assures attempting each action infinitely many times, the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium of the extensive form game.

The paper is well-written, clear, and well-organized. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, including the necessary background and definitions. The formal analysis is rigorous and well-presented, with the main contribution being the new formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games.

The authors also provide empirical evaluations of their claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods on randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games. The results show that the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on their template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms for these games.

The paper builds upon prior work by Lanctot et al. (2013), who treated the setup but provided more preliminary analysis. The current paper provides a more comprehensive and rigorous analysis, including a formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The proof technique used in the paper is inductive, assuming optimal strategies at leaf nodes. The authors show that the algorithm will eventually converge close to the optimal strategy in the leaf nodes and inductively prove that it will converge also in higher levels of the tree.

One major weakness of the paper is the limited provable insights about the inner nodes of the game tree. The analysis fails to connect to subgame perfect Nash equilibria and other game-theoretic tools. A possible suggestion for future work is to tie the analysis to subgame-perfect concepts and cite Harris (1990) on the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

The paper is also original, as it provides a novel combination of familiar techniques. The authors clearly explain how their work differs from previous contributions, and they adequately reference related work.

The significance of the paper is high, as it addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way, providing a unique theoretical and pragmatic approach to the problem.

In conclusion, this paper provides a significant contribution to the field of game theory and MCTS algorithms. The formal analysis is rigorous and well-presented, and the empirical evaluations support the theoretical claims. While there are some limitations to the analysis, the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a rigorous and well-presented formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games.
* The authors provide empirical evaluations of their claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods.
* The paper builds upon prior work and provides a more comprehensive and rigorous analysis.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and well-organized.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The analysis fails to connect to subgame perfect Nash equilibria and other game-theoretic tools.
* The paper provides limited provable insights about the inner nodes of the game tree.
* The authors could have provided more discussion on the implications of their results and the potential applications of their work.

Rating: 8/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations of their analysis and provide more discussion on the implications of their results.
