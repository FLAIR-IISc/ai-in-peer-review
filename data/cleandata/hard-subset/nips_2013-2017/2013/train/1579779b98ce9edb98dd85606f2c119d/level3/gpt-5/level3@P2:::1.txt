Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It gives a general SM-MCTS template that uses, at each internal node, a no-regret selection rule for the local matrix game. The main theoretical result shows that if the per-node selection is ε–Hannan consistent (HC) with guaranteed exploration, and mean returns are propagated (SM-MCTS-M), then the empirical (or average) mixture strategies converge almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the full game. The approximation error scales with the game-tree depth D (linearly, by induction on depth). The analysis proceeds via: (i) standard convergence of HC dynamics in repeated matrix games; (ii) extension to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error; and (iii) compositional induction over the game tree. Empirically, the paper evaluates regret matching (RM) and Exp3 as selection rules on random and searched “worst-case” small games, showing convergence to approximate NE. Interestingly, although the proof requires mean backups, the authors observe similar convergence when backing up the most recent sample.

This work connects to classic results on HC learning and convergence in repeated games (e.g., Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.; Blum & Mansour), and to MCTS variants used in simultaneous-move domains where prior theory is scarce. It complements negative observations about UCB/UCT in simultaneous moves (Shafiei et al.) by providing sufficient conditions for convergence under no-regret selection.

Strengths
- Clear, modular proof strategy that cleanly links no-regret learning at nodes to global approximate equilibrium play.
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS-like methods in simultaneous-move, perfect-information EFGs under broadly applicable assumptions (ε–HC + exploration).
- Subgame-perfect convergence, not just at the root.
- Empirical validation with two canonical no-regret algorithms (RM and Exp3), including random games and adversarially selected small worst cases.
- Useful practical takeaway: RM/Exp3-based SM-MCTS with exploration is principled for these games.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty of the proof technique is limited: the argument largely reuses known properties of HC dynamics and an induction over depth with bounded error accumulation. The main technical contribution is in cataloging conditions and carrying out the induction carefully, rather than introducing fundamentally new analysis.
- The theoretical guarantee depends on backing up means; yet experiments show that sample-value backups also converge and often faster. The paper should explain why the proof technique cannot handle sample backups, and whether the mean requirement is an artifact. Can bounded-variance martingale arguments extend to sample backups?
- UCB/UCT are not analyzed, though they are widely used; even if UCB can fail, a discussion of variants (e.g., adding explicit exploration or smoothing) and their relation to ε–HC would strengthen the practical relevance.
- The error bound in Theorem 4.11 is stated somewhat opaquely (“( D2 + δ )”). Please make constants explicit (e.g., O(D ε) or precise 2Dε or 4Dε) and clarify how the exploration parameter γ influences the asymptotic exploitability floor, as observed empirically.
- Empirical scope is limited: small games with uniform rollouts and fixed γ. Including a larger benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) or sensitivity to rollout policies and branching/depth would increase confidence in practical impact.
- The argument that the full tree is eventually built “with probability 1” is plausible via guaranteed exploration but could benefit from a brief lemma with rates.

Quality
Technically sound within its scope. The reduction to repeated games with bounded error and the induction over depth are correct and well motivated. Assumptions are explicit (ε–HC, guaranteed exploration, mean backups). Proof components rely on standard no-regret facts and concentration/martingale tools. Empirical evaluation is consistent with the theory but limited in scale; no finite-time bounds are provided.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Definitions are clear; the algorithmic template and RM/Exp3 instantiations are concrete. Minor issues:
- State the final approximation bound precisely (constants, dependence on D, ε, and γ).
- Unify terminology regarding “empirical frequencies” vs “average strategies” early, pointing to Lemma 4.5 to justify using either in plots.
- Briefly justify the “eventually full tree” claim.

Originality
Conceptually incremental: it assembles known HC convergence and applies it to MCTS in simultaneous-move trees via an induction. There appears to be no prior formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under these assumptions, which is a useful novelty for this niche. The empirical observation that sample backups also converge is interesting but unexplained theoretically.

Significance
Moderate. For researchers and practitioners working on simultaneous-move planning, the result provides a principled recipe and convergence guarantee. The sufficient condition (ε–HC + exploration) applies to standard algorithms (RM/Exp3). However, the empirical scope is modest, finite-time rates are absent, and many practitioners still use UCT variants, which are not analyzed here. Overall, a solid, useful step rather than a breakthrough.

Arguments for acceptance
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad, practical selection rules.
- Clean, general template with conditions satisfied by RM and Exp3.
- Clear exposition and reasonable experiments corroborate claims.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical novelty is limited; techniques are largely standard.
- Does not analyze or adapt UCT-style algorithms despite their prevalence.
- Mean-backup requirement in theory vs sample-backup success in practice is unresolved.
- Empirical evaluation is small-scale; no finite-sample guarantees.

Questions for the authors
- Can you make Theorem 4.11’s bound explicit, including dependence on ε and γ? How does the exploration floor observed empirically relate to the bound?
- What obstacles prevent extending the analysis to sample-value backups? Could a bias-variance or mixing argument bound the induced errors?
- Are there UCT variants that, with explicit exploration, satisfy ε–HC or your “guaranteed exploration” and might fit your framework?
- Could you include a medium-size benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel) to illustrate practical performance?

Overall assessment
A well-executed, clearly presented paper that provides the first formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under widely applicable conditions, with supportive experiments. While not highly novel analytically and somewhat limited empirically, it fills a real gap and should be of interest to the NIPS community working on game search and online learning. I lean toward weak accept, contingent on clarifying constants, discussing sample vs mean backups, and expanding the empirical section or discussion of UCT variants.
