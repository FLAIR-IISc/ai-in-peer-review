### Summary

This paper addresses the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. While MCTS variants have been successfully applied to this class of games, a formal analysis of their convergence to a Nash Equilibrium (NE) has been a significant open problem. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the action selection mechanism within each node is treated as a repeated matrix game. The core contribution is a proof that if this selection mechanism is an ε-Hannan consistent (no-regret) learning algorithm that guarantees sufficient exploration, the strategies derived from the MCTS visit counts will asymptotically converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium of the full extensive-form game. The proof works by induction over the depth of the game tree. The authors complement their theoretical analysis with experiments on randomly generated and worst-case games, using Regret Matching and Exp3 as instantiations of their framework. These experiments confirm the convergence empirically and also investigate the practical difference between propagating the mean reward (as required by the proof) versus the current sample's reward (a common heuristic).

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous proof structure that logically extends known results from the online learning literature (convergence of no-regret learners in matrix games) to the more complex setting of extensive-form games searched via MCTS. The analysis carefully accounts for the errors introduced in parent nodes due to the stochastic, sampled estimates from child subgames. The authors are also honest about the limitations of their work, primarily that the convergence result is asymptotic and does not provide finite-time convergence rates.

The experimental section is well-designed to support the theoretical claims and provide further insight. The comparison between propagating the mean reward versus the current sample reward is particularly valuable, as it connects the theoretical requirements to practical implementations. The search for "worst-case" game instances is a good-faith effort to understand the empirical convergence rates in challenging scenarios.

### Clarity

The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem is clearly motivated, and the necessary background on game theory and MCTS is concisely introduced. The main algorithm is presented in a general template, which effectively highlights the modularity of the selection mechanism. The theoretical analysis, while complex, is broken down logically from single matrix games to repeated games with errors, and finally to the full extensive-form game setting, making the argument easy to follow.

A few minor points could be addressed to improve clarity:
- In the caption for Figure 1, "leafs" should be corrected to "leaves".
- The text mentions that Player 2 uses "inverted" utilities; the more standard term is "negated".
- There is a minor notational inconsistency in Algorithm 1 and the surrounding text. For instance, `Select(h)` on line 10 returns `(i, j)`, but other parts of the paper refer to actions as `a`, `(a1, a2)`, etc. Standardizing this would be helpful.

### Originality

The paper's main contribution—the first proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games—is highly original and significant. This work closes a well-known theoretical gap. While MCTS convergence is understood for sequential games (e.g., UCT) and no-regret learning is a classic topic, the synthesis of these ideas to provide guarantees for the simultaneous-move case is novel and non-trivial. The generality of the proof, which applies to a whole class of ε-Hannan consistent algorithms, is a strong point and distinguishes it from a potential analysis of a single, specific algorithm. The work is well-situated within the literature and appropriately references prior work on MCTS applications and game-theoretic solution concepts.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are important for both researchers and practitioners. For researchers, it provides a solid theoretical foundation for using MCTS in this game class, justifying its use as a principled approximation algorithm from the perspective of algorithmic game theory. This could open the door for further theoretical analysis, such as deriving convergence rates or extending the results to more general game classes. For practitioners who already use MCTS in domains with simultaneous moves, this paper provides formal assurance that their methods are sound and will converge to an equilibrium solution in the limit.

The primary weakness that tempers the paper's significance is the lack of finite-time convergence rates. An asymptotic guarantee is an excellent first step, but without rates, it is difficult to quantify how many simulations are needed to reach a desired level of approximation (ε), or to theoretically compare the efficiency of different selection algorithms (e.g., Regret Matching vs. Exp3).

### Discussion and Suggestions

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution. The argument for acceptance is based on its originality in providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in this important game class. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and has clear significance.

The main reservation is the absence of convergence rates. This leads to a key question for the authors: Could the analysis be extended to provide explicit convergence rates if stronger assumptions are made about the selection algorithm? For instance, if one uses an algorithm like Exp3, which has a known regret bound of O(√T), can this be leveraged to derive a rate of convergence to an ε-Nash equilibrium for the overall SM-MCTS algorithm in terms of the number of simulations? A result of this kind would elevate the paper's impact significantly.

Furthermore, the empirical result that propagating the current sample value outperforms propagating the mean (which is required by the proof) is interesting. It would be beneficial if the authors could speculate on why this might be the case and discuss the challenges in extending their proof to cover this more practical and empirically superior variant.

Despite this limitation, the paper stands as a complete and important piece of work. It successfully resolves a long-standing open question and provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.
