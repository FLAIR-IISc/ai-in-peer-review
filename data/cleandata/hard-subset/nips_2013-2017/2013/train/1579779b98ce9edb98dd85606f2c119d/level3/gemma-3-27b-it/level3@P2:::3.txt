This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The core contribution is a proof demonstrating that MCTS algorithms utilizing any ε-Hannan consistent selection method will asymptotically converge to an ε-Nash equilibrium. This is a significant step towards establishing theoretical foundations for MCTS in this challenging game class, moving beyond purely empirical successes.

The work is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing approaches like backward induction in practical scenarios and the lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The authors correctly position their contribution relative to prior work on MCTS in sequential games and the broader field of algorithmic game theory. The formalization is rigorous, and the proofs appear sound, building upon established concepts like Hannan consistency and regret minimization. The experimental evaluation, while not providing concrete convergence rates, offers valuable insights into the practical behavior of the proposed framework, comparing different propagation strategies (mean vs. current sample).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The proof of convergence is a substantial achievement, filling a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
* **Generality:**  The framework is remarkably general, applicable to any selection method satisfying the ε-Hannan consistency criterion, providing flexibility and potential for integration with diverse learning algorithms.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible. The notation is generally clear, and the algorithm is presented in a concise and understandable manner.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experimental results, although not focused on precise rate analysis, provide empirical support for the theoretical findings and offer practical insights into algorithm behavior.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Convergence Rates:** The most significant limitation is the absence of explicit convergence rates. While the authors acknowledge that obtaining such rates may be difficult under the given assumptions, it would be valuable to investigate whether stronger regret-rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP3) can yield more concrete convergence guarantees.  This is a key area for future work.
* **Incremental Contribution:** While technically sound and valuable, the contribution feels somewhat incremental. MCTS convergence has been established for sequential games; extending this to simultaneous moves, although non-trivial, doesn’t represent a paradigm shift. The impact might be limited if tighter rates cannot be established.
* **Minor Technical Issues:** There are a few minor typos and notation inconsistencies. For instance, Figure 1 refers to "leafs" instead of "leaves". Line 144 uses “inverted” instead of “negated”. In Algorithm 1, line 10, the selection of actions (a1, a2) feels inconsistent with other uses of (i, j) and (σ1, σ2). Consistent notation would improve readability.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are relatively limited in scope, primarily focusing on randomly generated games and a "worst-case" set. Exploring a wider range of game instances and comparing performance against existing algorithms more comprehensively would strengthen the empirical evaluation.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in the field of MCTS and algorithmic game theory.
* The theoretical results are rigorously proven and contribute to a better understanding of MCTS’s behavior in complex game environments.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, making the complex concepts accessible to a broad audience.
* The empirical evaluation, while limited, provides supporting evidence for the theoretical findings.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of explicit convergence rates is a significant limitation, potentially hindering the practical applicability of the results.
* The contribution, while valuable, is incremental and may not represent a major breakthrough in the field.
* The minor technical issues (typos, notation inconsistencies) detract from the overall polish of the paper.


**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and technically correct paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While the lack of convergence rates and the incremental nature of the contribution are concerns, the rigor of the proof and the clarity of the presentation justify acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notation inconsistencies and typos. The authors should also consider discussing the potential for deriving convergence rates under stronger regret bounds in the conclusion or future work section.

**Rating:** Accept (with minor revisions)
