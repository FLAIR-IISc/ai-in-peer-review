

This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, establishing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria under specific conditions. The authors present a general MCTS framework where selection can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure, proving that -Hannan consistent selection methods with guaranteed exploration lead to convergence to subgame perfect -Nash equilibria. The empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games supports the theoretical claims.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a useful sufficient condition for convergence that advances understanding of MCTS in this domain. The theoretical contribution is technically sound, with proper formalization and empirical validation, though the proof technique builds upon established Hannan consistency results from online learning (e.g., [15, 16]) rather than introducing fundamentally novel mathematical approaches. This represents a significant weakness in originality.

The most concerning omission is the lack of analysis for UCB/UCT algorithms, which are widely used in MCTS applications. Given the extensive literature on UCT in sequential games (e.g., [14]), the authors should address how their framework relates to these popular methods and why their convergence guarantees don't apply to UCT (as noted by Shafiei et al. [17]). Additionally, the paper proves convergence only for mean propagation but empirically observes convergence with current sample-value propagation—this discrepancy requires explanation.

While the work is technically interesting and fills an important gap in MCTS theory for simultaneous-move games, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The significance is limited to a specific class of games, and the results don't substantially advance the broader state of the art in game theory or reinforcement learning.

Pro: Clear presentation, useful sufficient condition, solid empirical validation. Con: Lack of novelty in proof, omission of UCB analysis, unexplained empirical results. Overall, the paper makes a modest contribution to the literature but falls short of being a highly significant advancement. I recommend rejection as the contribution is incremental and lacks sufficient novelty to warrant publication at NIPS.
