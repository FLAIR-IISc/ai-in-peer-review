

This paper addresses the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a class of games including Goofspiel and Tron. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection method with guaranteed exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. This extends the work of Lanctot et al. (2013), who treated the setup but provided only preliminary analysis.

The paper's main strength lies in its clear theoretical contribution: a formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, which fills a significant gap in the literature. The writing is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and the experimental results complement the theoretical analysis effectively. The authors demonstrate convergence on both randomly generated and worst-case games using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods, providing empirical support for their theoretical claims.

The quality of the paper is strong, with sound theoretical analysis and well-executed experiments. The clarity is excellent, with proper definitions and logical flow from problem statement to analysis to experiments. The originality is notable, as this is the first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The significance is high, as it provides a theoretical foundation for the widespread practical use of MCTS in this game class.

However, the analysis has a notable weakness: it provides limited provable insights about inner nodes of the game tree, which are critical for understanding MCTS behavior beyond leaf nodes. More importantly, the paper fails to properly connect its analysis to subgame perfect Nash equilibria, despite claiming convergence to "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium." The proof technique is inductive, assuming optimal strategies at leaf nodes, but does not explicitly address how this relates to the broader concept of subgame perfection. The authors should cite Harris (1990) on existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games to strengthen this connection.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with a solid theoretical contribution that addresses an important gap. While the connection to subgame perfection needs strengthening, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
