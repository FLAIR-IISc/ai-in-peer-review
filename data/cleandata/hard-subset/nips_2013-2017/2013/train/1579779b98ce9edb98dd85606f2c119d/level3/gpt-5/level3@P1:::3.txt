This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that instantiates the node-level selection with any regret-minimizing (ε-Hannan-consistent) algorithm that guarantees exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3 with γ-exploration). The main theoretical result shows eventual convergence of the induced SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE): if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and explores all actions infinitely often, then the average/emprical strategy profile converges to an (O(ε)·poly(D))-NE, where D is the tree depth. The proof proceeds by: (i) establishing convergence properties of ε-Hannan-consistent play in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error; (iii) composing these bounds up the game tree, with error accumulation across depths; and (iv) requiring a mean-value backup variant (SM-MCTS-M) to control the bias. Empirically, the paper compares mean-value backups vs. sample-value backups and tests regret matching and Exp3 on random and “worst-case” small games. Both variants converge; mean backups are slightly slower.

Relation to prior work: While UCT has strong empirical performance in sequential perfect-information games, it lacks guarantees in simultaneous-move games and can fail even in one-stage games (Shafiei et al.). Exact backward induction with LP or double-oracle is costly in practice. This paper fills a theoretical gap by tying SM-MCTS convergence to no-regret online learning, building on classical results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.; Blum & Mansour) and tailoring them to the tree-structured, simultaneous-move setting.

Strengths
- First general asymptotic convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games, yielding subgame-perfect approximate NEs under broad selection rules (ε-Hannan consistency + exploration).
- Clean, modular proof structure (matrix games → bounded-error games → tree composition).
- Theoretical analysis reconciles empirical/average strategies (Lemma 4.5) and accounts for exploration mixing (Lemma 4.4).
- Empirical validation across two standard no-regret algorithms (regret matching, Exp3), with random and adversarially constructed small games; convergence trends align with theory.
- Writing is generally clear and careful about assumptions (bounded utilities, guaranteed exploration).

Weaknesses and questions
- Asymptotic only: no finite-sample rates for exploitability. This limits practical guidance and makes it hard to compare algorithms or choose exploration parameters.
- The approximation factor grows with depth (Theorem 4.11 suggests ≈ ε·D^2 + δ). The bound seems loose and could be pessimistic for deep trees. Can constants be improved, or the depth dependence reduced (e.g., via variance-reduced backups or non-uniform exploration)?
- Mean-value backups are required by the proof but empirically underperform sample-value backups. Can the analysis be extended to sample backups (e.g., via martingale concentration and bias control), or to other widely used bootstrapping schemes?
- Experiments are small-scale and synthetic (depth ≤ 2 for worst-case search), with limited coverage of benchmark simultaneous-move games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron). Stronger experimental evaluation would bolster significance.
- Exploration mixing introduces an irreducible exploitability floor ~O(γ); some results merely converge to this floor. Guidance on choosing γ and its interaction with depth and regret rates would be useful.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The decomposition from repeated games to bounded-error games to tree induction is well motivated; the lemmas align with standard no-regret arguments. Assumptions (bounded utilities, guaranteed exploration, eventual full-tree expansion) are stated, and the reduction to mean backups is explicit. I did not find logical gaps, though the constants/accumulation in the D^2 factor could be better explained and, if possible, tightened.
- Empirical methodology is reasonable for a theory paper but limited in scope; still, it supports the main claims.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable, with clear algorithms and definitions. Suggestions:
  - Be explicit where mean backups are required for the proof (and where sample backups fail).
  - Summarize the source of the D^2 depth dependence (per-level error, best-response slack, and summation).
  - Unify notation for actions (i, j) vs. a; consistently distinguish empirical frequencies vs. average strategies and state which is used in experiments.

Originality
- The main convergence theorem for SM-MCTS under no-regret selection appears new and fills a recognized gap between practice and theory.
- The approach leverages known no-regret theory but contributes a novel composition over simultaneous-move game trees.

Significance
- Scientifically meaningful: provides the first general convergence guarantee for a widely used class of algorithms in simultaneous-move games.
- Practical impact is tempered by the absence of finite-time rates and by the mismatch between the analyzed backup (mean) and commonly used backups (sample). Nonetheless, the result offers a principled foundation likely to inspire further theoretical and algorithmic developments.

Specific questions/suggestions
- Finite-time rates: If the selection algorithm has regret R(T) (e.g., O(√T) for Exp3 or RM), can you derive an explicit T-dependent exploitability bound at the root, tracking how error compounds with depth and with exploration γ?
- Can the bounded-error analysis be strengthened to handle sample backups via concentration bounds on the propagated returns (controlling variance/bias), perhaps yielding similar asymptotics and a rate with worse constants?
- Explore depth scaling empirically (larger D, varying branching factors), report exploitability vs. iterations, and sensitivity to γ. Include benchmarks like Goofspiel or Tron.
- Discuss alternatives to uniform exploration (e.g., decaying γ, exploration schedules tied to visit counts) that retain guarantees while reducing the limiting exploitability.

Pros for acceptance
- First rigorous convergence result for SM-MCTS with broad applicability.
- Solid, well-structured theory grounded in no-regret learning.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory and clarifies the mean vs. sample backup tradeoff.
- Clear relevance to the NIPS community (online learning, game theory, search).

Cons against acceptance
- No finite-time convergence rates; depth-dependent bound likely loose.
- Empirical evaluation is modest and omits larger benchmarks.
- Theoretical guarantees rely on mean backups, which underperform in practice.
