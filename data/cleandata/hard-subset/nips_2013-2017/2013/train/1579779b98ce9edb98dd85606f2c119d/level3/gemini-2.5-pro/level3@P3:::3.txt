This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any no-regret learning algorithm as the in-tree selection policy. The central contribution is a formal proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the MCTS procedure will asymptotically converge to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This work addresses a notable gap in the MCTS literature, as prior convergence guarantees (e.g., for UCT) have largely been confined to sequential-move games, and UCB-based approaches are known to fail in simultaneous-move settings. The theoretical claims are supplemented by an empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection policies, confirming convergence and comparing practical implementation choices.

**Quality**
The paper is of high technical quality. The main theoretical result—the proof of asymptotic convergence—is sound and well-structured. The inductive proof strategy, which starts from the leaf nodes (simple matrix games) and works up the tree while accounting for the bounded error introduced by the stochastic estimates from lower levels, is elegant and convincing. The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their work, explicitly noting that the convergence result is purely asymptotic and provides no convergence rates.

The experiments are well-designed to serve two purposes: first, to empirically validate the convergence claim, and second, to investigate a key discrepancy between the theoretical requirements and common practical implementations. The comparison between propagating the mean value of a child node (required for the proof) versus propagating the single sample value from the current rollout (common in practice) provides a valuable insight.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem is well-motivated, the necessary background on simultaneous-move games and no-regret learning is provided, and the general SM-MCTS algorithm is described before being instantiated with specific selection strategies. The flow from the analysis of simple repeated matrix games to games with bounded error and finally to the full extensive-form game is logical and easy to follow.

However, there are several minor issues that could be addressed to improve clarity:
- In Figure 1, the plural of "leaf" is "leaves", not "leafs".
- On Line 144, when discussing player 2's perspective, "negated utilities" would be more precise than "inverted utilities".
- There are notational inconsistencies in Algorithm 1 and the surrounding text. For instance, line 10 uses `(i, j)` for the selected joint action, which is inconsistent with the use of `a1, a2` or `σ1, σ2` in other parts of the paper. Standardizing the notation for actions and strategies throughout would improve readability.

**Originality**
The work is original in its specific focus and contribution. While the idea of using no-regret learners within game-tree search is related to established algorithms like Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR), this paper is the first to formally frame this approach within a general MCTS template for simultaneous-move games and provide a formal proof of convergence to an ε-Nash equilibrium. This fills an important theoretical gap, providing a firm justification for a class of algorithms that have been used more heuristically in the past.

The contribution could also be viewed as somewhat incremental. The convergence of two interacting no-regret players to a Nash equilibrium in a single matrix game is a classic result. The main novelty here is extending this reasoning to the multi-level, stochastic estimation setting of MCTS.

**Significance**
The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution by providing the first convergence guarantees for a general class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games. This formalizes and legitimizes the use of MCTS in this domain from an algorithmic game theory perspective.

The significance of the impact is tempered by two factors. First, the lack of convergence rates is a major limitation. An asymptotic guarantee confirms that the algorithm *will* eventually work, but it gives practitioners no guidance on how many simulations are required to achieve a desired level of accuracy (ε). This makes it difficult to compare the efficiency of this approach to others or to budget computation time. Second, the empirical results show that the variant required for the proof (mean-value propagation) is slightly outperformed by the more standard sample-value propagation. While both converge, this creates a slight disconnect between the theory and best practice.

A crucial question for future work, which would significantly increase the impact of this result, is whether explicit convergence rates can be derived under stronger assumptions. For example:
- If the selection strategy has a known regret bound, such as O(√T) for Exp3, can this be used to derive an explicit convergence rate for the overall MCTS algorithm in terms of the number of simulations? Answering this would bridge the gap between this asymptotic result and more quantitative guarantees.

In summary, this is a well-executed and valuable paper that provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for MCTS in an important class of games. Its primary weakness is the absence of convergence rates, which limits its immediate practical utility and impact. Nonetheless, it is a strong contribution that clarifies the theoretical underpinnings of these methods and opens up clear avenues for future research.
