This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of game theory by analyzing the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated using any no-regret selection method and prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem, and the technical sections are well-organized and easy to follow. The use of notation and definitions is consistent throughout the paper, and the authors provide a thorough explanation of the key concepts.

The main theoretical contribution of the paper is the proof of asymptotic convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium for simultaneous-move games. This result is significant because it provides a theoretical foundation for the use of MCTS algorithms in this class of games. The authors also provide empirical results that demonstrate the convergence of the algorithm in practice.

One limitation of the paper is that the convergence result is asymptotic only, and no convergence rates are provided. The authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that it may be inevitable under the assumption of ε-Hannan consistency. However, it would be interesting to explore whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP) yield explicit convergence rates.

The empirical results presented in the paper are also informative. The authors compare the performance of two variants of MCTS: one that propagates mean reward values and another that propagates current sample values. The results show that the mean-value propagation variant slightly underperforms the sample-value propagation variant. The authors also attempt to provide insight into empirical convergence rates by evaluating the algorithms on a set of randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games.

Overall, the paper is a significant contribution to the field of game theory, and the results have implications for the use of MCTS algorithms in practice. The paper is well-written, and the technical results are sound. However, the lack of convergence rates is a limitation, and further research is needed to address this issue.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of game theory.
* The results have implications for the use of MCTS algorithms in practice.
* The paper is well-written, and the technical results are sound.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The convergence result is asymptotic only, and no convergence rates are provided.
* The paper does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the empirical results.
