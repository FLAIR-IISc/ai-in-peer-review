This paper analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template instantiated with regret-minimizing selection rules and proves almost-sure convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) under guaranteed exploration. The core technical contribution shows that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), then the induced SM-MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect approximate NE; empirics corroborate with regret matching and Exp3 and also discuss the practical choice of backing up means versus samples. Relative to prior work (Lanctot et al., 2013), which treated this setup and offered preliminary insights, this paper delivers a substantially stronger, more general formal analysis.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and technically sound. The inductive proof—assuming optimal or near-optimal behavior at the leaves and propagating bounded errors to ancestors—supports the convergence claim; experiments are appropriately scoped and consistent with the theory. Limitations include the lack of finite-time rates and a relatively loose depth dependence in the error accumulation.

Clarity: The paper is clear, well-organized, and readable; algorithms and assumptions (Hannan consistency, guaranteed exploration) are stated crisply. The empirical section clearly explains the design choices (mean vs sample backups).

Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information extensive-form games using regret-minimizing selection, going beyond UCT-style analyses and prior preliminary results.

Significance: The result provides theoretical grounding for SM-MCTS variants used in practice in domains like Tron and Goofspiel, and should influence both algorithm design and evaluation in simultaneous-move settings.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The inductive argument yields limited per-node insight: it bounds cumulative error but does not illuminate dynamics or equilibrium selection at inner nodes. Clarifying per-depth constants and whether the D^2 dependence can be sharpened would strengthen the contribution.
- The analysis establishes convergence to (approximate) subgame-perfect equilibrium for the constructed process but does not connect to broader game-theoretic results on existence and refinements. I recommend relating the inductive proof explicitly to backward induction and subgame-perfect concepts and citing Harris (1990) on the existence of subgame-perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games. Discussion of equilibrium refinements (e.g., trembling-hand/perfection) would also contextualize the guarantees.
- No finite-sample guarantees; even coarse rates would improve practical impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- First rigorous convergence analysis for SM-MCTS with regret-based selection.
- Clear writing and reproducible methodology.
- Empirical validation across methods (RM/Exp3).

Arguments against acceptance:
- No finite-time bounds; loose depth dependence.
- Limited connection to established SPNE existence/refinement literature.
- Inner-node behavior not characterized beyond error accumulation.
