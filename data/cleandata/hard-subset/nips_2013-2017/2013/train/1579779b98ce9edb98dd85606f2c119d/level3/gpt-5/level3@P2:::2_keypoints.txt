- Studies MCTS for two-player zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games; introduces SM-MCTS template using Hannan-consistent (HC) selection with exploration and proves convergence to approximate NE using mean backups.  
- Proof approach: (i) analyze repeated matrix games under HC selection, (ii) extend to repeated matrix games with bounded error from sampling/exploration, (iii) inductively compose guarantees up the tree to obtain subgame-perfect approximate equilibria.  
- Instantiates template with regret matching and Exp3 and empirically compares mean-backup vs sample-backup on random and adversarial small games.  
- Builds on MCTS/UCT and regret-minimization literature; addresses a prior theoretical gap where practical SM-MCTS lacked convergence guarantees.  
- Novelty: first general convergence guarantee to approximate NE in simultaneous-move EFGs under broad HC selectors with explicit treatment of exploration and mean backups.  
- Strength: clear, useful template that decouples tree search from HC selection in matrix games.  
- Strength: solid, organized theory; the “repeated game with bounded error” abstraction is clean and reusable.  
- Strength: concrete algorithm instantiations and experiments that support theoretical claims.  
- Strength: addresses convergence of MCTS beyond purely sequential perfect-information games.  
- Weakness: limited insight into inner-node learning dynamics beyond asymptotic error propagation; no finite-time/sample-complexity bounds.  
- Weakness: discussion of subgame perfection is insufficiently connected to classical SPNE literature (e.g., Selten, Harris); need to clarify what is new algorithmically versus existence results.  
- Weakness: theory requires mean backups while many practical systems use sample backups—need (i) explanation of when sample backups break the proof, (ii) formal conditions for their convergence, or (iii) quantification of asymptotic bias.  
- Weakness: approximation bounds scale poorly with depth (appears quadratic in D); need discussion of tightness and potential improvements (avoid loose constants, variance reduction, bootstrapping).  
- Weakness: experiments are small-scale and synthetic; recommend benchmarks (Goofspiel/Tron), confidence intervals, sensitivity to γ/selection parameters, and exploitability metrics.  
- Quality: technically sound with clear assumptions; main limitations are asymptotic results and possibly loose depth dependence.  
- Clarity: well written and organized; suggest summarizing the final bound explicitly in main text and clarifying empirical frequencies vs average strategies earlier.  
- Originality: high — provides a valuable new convergence result and the bounded-error abstraction.  
- Significance: meaningful theoretical advance that justifies a family of SM-MCTS algorithms and suggests avenues for finite-time and imperfect-information extensions.  
- Pros for acceptance: first general convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad HC selectors; clean template usable with standard online learning algorithms; clear exposition and supporting experiments.  
- Cons for acceptance: asymptotic-only results, loose depth-dependent bounds, limited empirical evaluation, and weaker integration with subgame-perfect equilibrium literature.  
- Questions for authors: can you derive finite-time guarantees (function of t, D, γ)? Is the D^2 dependence tight or improvable? Under what conditions do sample backups converge? How sensitive are results to exploration rate γ and is annealed exploration compatible?  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept — fills a genuine theoretical gap and is well presented, but would benefit from stronger experiments and deeper discussion of subgame perfection and finite-time behavior.
