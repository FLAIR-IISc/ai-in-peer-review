## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) applied to zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors establish a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under the condition that the selection method is -Hannan consistent and satisfies certain exploration requirements. The work builds upon prior efforts (Lanctot et al., 2013) by providing a more rigorous theoretical foundation. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly structured. The definitions are precise, and the inductive proof technique, relying on optimal strategies at leaf nodes, is presented logically. The empirical evaluation, using regret matching and Exp3, supports the theoretical findings and demonstrates convergence on both randomly generated and specifically designed “worst-case” games. The inclusion of experiments comparing different propagation methods (mean vs. current sample) adds nuance to the practical understanding of the algorithm.

A key strength of this work lies in its originality. While MCTS is widely used in these games, formal convergence guarantees were previously lacking. This paper fills that gap, providing a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory. The significance of this result is considerable, as it justifies the use of MCTS as a reliable approximation algorithm and opens avenues for further research into refining convergence rates and extending the analysis to more complex game classes.

However, the analysis has a notable weakness: it provides limited insight into the inner nodes of the game tree. The convergence proof heavily relies on the behavior at leaf nodes and doesn’t fully leverage subgame perfect Nash equilibrium concepts. This limits the ability to draw strong conclusions about the algorithm’s behavior in deeper, more complex games. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more explicit connection to existing game-theoretic tools and a citation of Harris (1990) regarding the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games, as suggested.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contribution with a novel convergence proof.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Supportive empirical results.
*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited analysis of inner nodes of the game tree.
*   Lack of connection to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts.



**Overall:** Despite the identified weakness, the paper represents a significant advance in our understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The strengths of the work – its theoretical rigor, clarity, and empirical validation – outweigh the limitations. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the connection to subgame perfect equilibria and the cited work.
