

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors develop a general MCTS template for these games that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as the selection method. They prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and satisfies exploration requirements, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game. The authors empirically validate their theoretical claims using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated games and worst-case game instances.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in this class of games, which has been widely used in practice (e.g., for games like Goofspiel and Tron) but lacked theoretical guarantees. While Lanctot et al. (2013) previously addressed similar games, their work provided more preliminary analysis without rigorous convergence proofs. The authors' inductive proof technique, which assumes optimal strategies at leaf nodes and extends to higher levels of the game tree, represents a notable methodological advance.

The paper demonstrates strong quality through its rigorous mathematical analysis and careful empirical validation. The formal proof is technically sound, addressing convergence guarantees for the MCTS algorithm in this specific game class. The experimental results confirm the theoretical claims, showing that MCTS variants using regret matching and Exp3 converge to approximate NE on evaluated games. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

The originality of this work is substantial. While MCTS has been extensively studied in sequential games, this paper addresses a gap in the literature for simultaneous-move games. The connection between Hannan consistency in matrix games and convergence to NE in extensive-form games represents a novel theoretical insight. The authors clearly differentiate their work from prior contributions, including Lanctot et al. (2013), by providing rigorous convergence guarantees rather than empirical observations.

The significance of this work is high for both theoretical and practical communities. Theoretical advances in understanding MCTS algorithms for game-theoretic applications are valuable for the broader field of algorithmic game theory. Practically, the paper provides a theoretical justification for using MCTS as an approximation algorithm in simultaneous-move games, which have numerous applications in security domains and game-playing competitions. The authors' observation that many existing MCTS variants for these games likely converge to NE, even if not covered by their proof, suggests broader applicability.

The paper's main weakness lies in its limited connection to subgame perfect Nash equilibria, a critical concept for extensive-form games. The authors prove convergence to an approximate NE but fail to explicitly connect this to subgame perfection. This is a significant oversight since the paper's title and content focus on extensive-form games where subgame perfection is a standard solution concept. The analysis would be strengthened by explicitly showing how their convergence guarantees relate to subgame perfection, and by citing Harris (1990) on the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games.

Another minor weakness is that the paper does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, only asymptotic guarantees. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a discussion of how their theoretical results might inform practical implementation choices would have added value.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout, and the experimental setup is well-justified.

For acceptance, I note the following arguments:

PROS:
- First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games
- Rigorous mathematical proof technique with clear inductive structure
- Well-executed empirical validation confirming theoretical claims
- Clear and well-organized presentation of results
- Addresses a meaningful gap in the literature with practical implications

CONS:
- Fails to explicitly connect convergence to subgame perfect Nash equilibria
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds
- Does not fully leverage game-theoretic concepts for the extensive-form setting

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a class of games where it has been widely applied without theoretical justification. The formal analysis is sound and the empirical validation is convincing. While the failure to connect to subgame perfect equilibria is a notable weakness, the paper's core contribution remains significant and advances the field. I recommend acceptance at NIPS 2013.
