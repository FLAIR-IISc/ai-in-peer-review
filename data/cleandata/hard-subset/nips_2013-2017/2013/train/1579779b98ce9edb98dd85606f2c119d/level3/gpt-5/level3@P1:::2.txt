This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The authors present a general template for Simultaneous-Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated by any -Hannan-consistent selection rule with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching and Exp3). The central theoretical result shows almost-sure convergence of SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium: for game depth D, the average strategies converge to a subgame-perfect (D^2 + δ)-NE for any δ > 0, under the requirement that backups use means of returns (not single samples) and that each joint action is tried infinitely often. The proof proceeds by (i) convergence in repeated matrix games with -Hannan consistency; (ii) extension to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error; and (iii) an induction over tree depth showing bounded error propagation from leaves to root. Empirically, the authors validate convergence on random games and empirically hard instances, comparing regret matching and Exp3, and show that mean-backups slightly slow convergence but still achieve the expected exploitability floor imposed by forced exploration.

Relation to prior work: Earlier simultaneous-move MCTS work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013; Perick et al., 2012) studied selection strategies empirically and noted that UCB/UCT can fail to converge even in one-shot matrix games (Shafiei et al., 2011). This paper advances the theory by giving the first general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS with -Hannan-consistent selection and explicit exploration, complementing algorithmic advances in simultaneous-move alpha–beta search (Saffidine et al., 2012; Bosansky et al., 2013) and regret-minimization literature (Hart and Mas-Colell; Auer et al., Exp3). The paper appropriately distinguishes average versus empirical strategies and uses standard regret notions to tie online learning to equilibrium computation.

Strengths
- Clear and well-organized exposition with precise definitions and assumptions; the SM-MCTS template is easy to instantiate with common selection rules.
- Novel theoretical contribution: almost-sure convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information games under broad assumptions on selection (any -Hannan-consistent algorithm with guaranteed exploration).
- Insightful proof structure: reduction to repeated matrix games with bounded error and depth-induction; explicit constants (e.g., 4(c+1) in matrix games and D^2 dependence overall) make assumptions and limitations transparent.
- Empirical results are consistent with the theory, include random and adversarially chosen instances, and compare two widely used selection rules (regret matching and Exp3), including mean vs. sample backups.
- Practical takeaway: explicit exploration guarantees convergence; mean backups are sufficient (and only mildly slower empirically).

Weaknesses
- The main bounds appear loose: the D^2 dependence and the constant blow-ups (e.g., 4(c+1)) may be pessimistic; there are no finite-time rates, which limits practical guidance.
- The theoretical analysis hinges on mean backups and on having the full tree eventually; the implications for commonly used sample-backup variants and progressive widening/large action spaces are not formalized.
- The treatment of “inner nodes” is largely via error propagation; there is limited structural insight into how simultaneous-move interaction at interior nodes affects convergence speed or constants.
- While the main theorem already claims subgame-perfect approximate NE, the exposition could better contextualize this notion relative to the game-theoretic literature on subgame perfection in simultaneous-move perfect-information games and make the implications more explicit.

Quality: Technically sound. Assumptions are stated, key lemmas are standard or carefully adapted (Hannan consistency with exploration; convergence in repeated games with bounded error; martingale arguments for average vs empirical strategies). The induction over depth is sensible and well-motivated. The empirical methodology is appropriate for validating asymptotic behavior and comparing variants.

Clarity: Very clear and readable. Pseudocode and notation are consistent; assumptions and parameters (γ for exploration) are explicit. One suggestion: emphasize earlier that the theoretical guarantees require mean backups and guaranteed exploration, and separate clearly what is proven vs. conjectured for other variants.

Originality: High. To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information games under broad selection rules with regret guarantees, moving beyond prior empirical studies and negative results for UCT.

Significance: Strong. Simultaneous moves occur in many domains; providing convergence guarantees for widely used MCTS-style methods is valuable to both theory and practice. The result should influence how practitioners design SM-MCTS (e.g., enforcing exploration and mean backups) and motivate further work on finite-time rates and extensions to imperfect information.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss finite-time convergence rates or at least provide heuristic guidance on iteration complexity as a function of D, branching factor, and γ; report confidence intervals to convey variability in convergence speed.
- Tighten constants if possible, or explain whether D^2 is intrinsic or an artifact of the proof; explore whether bootstrapping variance reduction or better backup schemes can improve constants.
- Clarify the status of sample-backup variants: provide counterexamples or partial guarantees, or state precise conditions under which they still converge.
- Strengthen the game-theoretic context for subgame perfection: explicitly relate the result to standard existence and backward-induction arguments in simultaneous-move perfect-information trees, and add references to classic results on subgame-perfect equilibria in such settings.
- Comment on scalability: interaction with large action spaces, progressive widening, and function approximation for selection policies.

Arguments for acceptance
- First principled convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under broad and practically relevant selection rules.
- Clean theory with clear assumptions and a general template practitioners can adopt.
- Solid empirical support on random and adversarial instances.

Arguments against acceptance
- No finite-time rates; constants may be loose (D^2), limiting immediate practical impact.
- Reliance on mean backups and full-tree eventuality leaves a gap to many practical implementations.
- Limited structural insight into inner-node dynamics beyond error propagation.

Overall recommendation: I lean toward acceptance given the novelty and clarity of the theoretical contribution and its relevance to the NeurIPS community, with the above suggestions aimed at strengthening the final version.
