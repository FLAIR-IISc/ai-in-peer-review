- Paper studies SM-MCTS with per-node ε–Hannan-consistent selection and shows empirical average strategies converge a.s. to a subgame-perfect approximate NE when mean returns are backed up; approximation error scales linearly with tree depth D.  
- Proof strategy: use HC convergence in repeated matrix games, extend to bounded-payoff-error repeated games, then induct over game-tree depth.  
- Empirically evaluates RM and Exp3 on random and adversarial small games; observes convergence and that sample-value backups also converge (often faster) despite theory requiring mean backups.  
- Strength: clear, modular proof linking local no-regret learning to global approximate equilibrium play.  
- Strength: first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS-like methods in simultaneous-move, perfect-information EFGs under broadly applicable assumptions (ε–HC + exploration).  
- Strength: guarantees subgame-perfect convergence, not only root-level.  
- Strength: empirical validation with canonical no-regret algorithms and useful practical takeaway that RM/Exp3 with exploration is principled for these games.  
- Weakness: theoretical novelty limited—largely applies known HC dynamics and induction rather than introducing fundamentally new analysis.  
- Weakness: theory depends on mean backups while experiments show sample backups work; paper should explain why analysis cannot handle samples and whether martingale/variance arguments could.  
- Weakness: UCB/UCT variants are not analyzed; a discussion of variants with explicit exploration and relation to ε–HC would increase practical relevance.  
- Weakness: Theorem 4.11’s error bound is stated opaquely; constants and O(D ε) dependence and the role of exploration γ should be made explicit.  
- Weakness: empirical scope limited to small games with uniform rollouts and fixed γ; larger benchmarks and sensitivity studies would strengthen practical impact.  
- Weakness: claim that the full tree is eventually built w.p.1 via exploration is plausible but should be supported by a lemma with rates.  
- Quality: technically sound and well-motivated; assumptions are explicit, but no finite-time bounds are provided.  
- Clarity: generally well written with clear definitions; needs precise statement of final approximation bound and unified terminology for empirical frequencies vs average strategies.  
- Originality: conceptually incremental but fills a niche—no prior formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under these assumptions; unexplained empirical success of sample backups is interesting.  
- Significance: moderate—useful to researchers/practitioners in simultaneous-move planning but limited by small experiments, no finite-sample guarantees, and absence of UCT analysis.  
- Argument for acceptance: first convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under practical selection rules; clean template and corroborating experiments.  
- Argument against acceptance: limited analytic novelty, unresolved mean vs sample backup gap, lack of UCT analysis, and small-scale empirical evaluation.  
- Questions for authors: make Theorem 4.11 explicit including ε and γ dependence; explain obstacles to analyzing sample-value backups and whether bias-variance/mixing arguments apply; identify UCT variants that could satisfy ε–HC or guaranteed exploration; consider adding a medium-size benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel).  
- Overall assessment: well-executed, clearly presented work that fills a gap; reviewer leans weak accept subject to clarifying constants, addressing sample vs mean backups, and expanding empirical discussion or UCT-related analysis.
