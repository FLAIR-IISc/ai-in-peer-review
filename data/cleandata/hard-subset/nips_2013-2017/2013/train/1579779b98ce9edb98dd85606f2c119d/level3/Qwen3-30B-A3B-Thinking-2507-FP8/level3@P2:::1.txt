

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with various selection methods, and formally prove that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games and satisfies exploration requirements, then MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game. The paper complements its theoretical analysis with empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this specific class of games. Previous work on MCTS has primarily focused on empirical performance in sequential games (e.g., UCT for Go), with little theoretical foundation for simultaneous move games. The authors build on established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency) and game theory (Nash equilibrium), demonstrating how these concepts can be applied to MCTS in simultaneous move games. This connects well with NIPS 2013-2017 work on game-theoretic algorithms, particularly the growing interest in MCTS for game playing (e.g., Kocsis and Szepesvári's UCT algorithm) and the increasing focus on game theory in machine learning.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with a clear formal proof of convergence under specific conditions. The theoretical analysis is well-structured, moving from matrix games to multi-stage extensive-form games through induction. The empirical evaluation complements the theory by validating the main claim on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios. However, the paper would be stronger with more detailed discussion of the convergence rate (the theoretical bound is asymptotic without finite-time guarantees) and a more thorough comparison with existing MCTS variants. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their conclusion.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem definition, formalization, and proof structure are clear and accessible. The authors effectively use definitions, lemmas, and theorems to build their argument. The empirical section is well-organized with appropriate visualizations. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why mean propagation (as required by the theory) works slightly worse than current sample propagation in practice (as shown in Figure 2), which appears to contradict the theoretical requirement.

**Originality:** While the paper provides a valuable theoretical contribution, the proof technique itself is not particularly novel—it applies known results from online learning to a new context. The authors acknowledge this by stating their main contribution is "providing a sufficient condition for convergence." The paper does not analyze widely used UCT/UCB selection methods, which are prevalent in practice (e.g., Shafiei et al. [17] show UCB doesn't converge to NE in simple simultaneous move games). The authors should address this gap, as UCT is one of the most popular MCTS variants. The connection between Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence is the main novelty, but it's somewhat incremental given the established literature on regret minimization in games.

**Significance:** The paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS for a relevant class of games. The sufficient condition provided (using Hannan consistent selection methods) is useful for practitioners to understand when MCTS will converge to NE. However, the significance is somewhat limited because the result only applies to specific selection methods (not the most widely used ones), and the empirical evaluation shows the convergence rate is comparable to existing methods rather than superior. The paper contributes to the growing body of work on MCTS but doesn't represent a major leap forward in the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The paper fills an important theoretical gap, provides a formal convergence guarantee for a class of games previously unaddressed, and offers a useful sufficient condition for practitioners. The writing is clear, the theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical validation supports the main claim.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. The proof technique is a standard application of existing results rather than a novel theoretical approach. The paper fails to address widely used MCTS variants like UCT/UCB, which limits its practical relevance. The theoretical result only applies to mean propagation, while the experiments show current sample-value propagation also works, creating a disconnect between theory and practice that isn't adequately explained.

**Overall assessment:** This is a solid, well-written paper that makes a technically interesting contribution to the theory of MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the theoretical result is valuable, the paper's incremental nature and limited scope (not addressing widely used algorithms like UCT) prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS. The authors should strengthen the paper by addressing UCT variants and explaining the mean propagation vs. current sample-value discrepancy.

I recommend *weak acceptance* given the paper's technical soundness and clear presentation, but with the caveat that the contribution is somewhat incremental. The paper would benefit from more discussion of the connection to widely used algorithms and addressing the mean propagation issue in the experiments.
