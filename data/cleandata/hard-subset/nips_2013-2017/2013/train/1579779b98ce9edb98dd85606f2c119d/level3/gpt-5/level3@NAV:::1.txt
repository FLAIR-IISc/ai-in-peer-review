Summary and relation to prior work: The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. It provides a generic SM-MCTS template whose selection subroutine can be any ε–Hannan-consistent procedure with guaranteed exploration. The main theorem shows almost-sure convergence to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with an approximation scaling as O(D^2 ε) in game depth D, when mean returns are propagated. Empirically, the authors instantiate the template with regret matching (Hart & Mas-Colell) and Exp3 (Auer et al.), validating convergence on random and difficult hand-crafted games, and observe that several practical variants—including those that propagate the current sample—also appear to converge. The work connects standard online-learning regret guarantees with stacked matrix games and echoes earlier observations about UCT’s failure in simultaneous-move settings (e.g., Shafiei et al.). It complements prior convergence results for CFR-style methods in imperfect-information EFGs by addressing SM-MCTS in this specific class.

Strengths:
- Clear problem statement and a useful sufficient condition for convergence grounded in ε–Hannan consistency and exploration.
- Clean inductive proof structure via repeated matrix games with bounded error; careful handling of empirical vs average strategies.
- Solid empirical validation across random and adverse instances; results are reproducible in spirit.

Weaknesses:
- Novelty of the proof technique is limited; the reduction to bounded-error repeated games and induction over depth leverages well-known online-learning machinery.
- Theoretical result requires mean-propagation, yet experiments suggest sample-return propagation also works; the paper should explain this discrepancy (e.g., bias/variance trade-offs, martingale arguments, or conditions under which bias vanishes).
- UCB/UCT and its variants, ubiquitous in practice, are not analyzed or experimentally compared. Given existing negative results for vanilla UCB in SM games, a discussion and empirical comparison to UCT variants with exploration or regret-based tweaks would strengthen impact.
- The D^2 dependence and constants (e.g., 4(c+1)) seem loose; no finite-sample rates are provided.

Evaluation:
- Quality: Generally sound; assumptions (guaranteed exploration, complete-tree eventuality) are reasonable but should be explicitly quantified.
- Clarity: Well organized and readable; notation is heavy in places but acceptable.
- Originality: Incremental; the sufficient condition is useful but proof ideas are standard.
- Significance: Moderate; likely to inform practitioners selecting selection policies for SM-MCTS, but missing UCT analysis dampens practical reach.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Theorize why sample-return propagation appears to converge; delineate conditions or provide counterexamples.
- Include UCT variants in experiments; discuss why prior negative results persist or can be mitigated.
- Tighten bounds or provide finite-time rates; discuss dependence on exploration γ.

Arguments:
Pros: clear, correct, useful sufficient condition; good empirical validation; relevant to NIPS audience.
Cons: limited novelty; missing UCT analysis; theory–practice gap on return propagation; loose bounds.

Overall: technically interesting, well presented, but incremental.
