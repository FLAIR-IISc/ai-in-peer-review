## Review of “Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games”

This paper studies the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games and formally prove that, under the condition of using a -Hannan consistent selection method with specific exploration requirements, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE). The empirical evaluation, conducted on both randomly generated and specifically crafted "worst-case" games, supports the theoretical findings and suggests similar convergence behavior for other MCTS variants.

The work builds upon a significant body of research on game theory and MCTS. Recent advancements have brought game-theoretic models into practical applications, notably in security and large-scale game playing [1, 2]. The authors rightly point out the gap in theoretical understanding regarding MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a setting where traditional backward induction methods can be computationally expensive [7, 8] and require significant domain knowledge [8, 9]. Existing work has explored MCTS variants in these contexts [10, 11, 12, 13], but without formal convergence guarantees. This paper directly addresses that gap. The connection to the well-established field of regret minimization in matrix games, utilizing techniques like regret matching [15] and Exp3 [16], provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The formal proof of convergence is a significant contribution. Establishing a sufficient condition – the use of a -Hannan consistent selection method – provides a valuable theoretical underpinning for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. Definitions are precise, and the algorithm is described in a straightforward manner. The connection between the theoretical concepts and the algorithmic template is clearly articulated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation provides evidence supporting the theoretical claims. Testing on both randomly generated and "worst-case" games increases confidence in the robustness of the findings.
*   **General Template:** Presenting a general MCTS template adaptable to various selection methods is a practical contribution, allowing for future exploration of different algorithms within this framework.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Proof Technique:** While the application to simultaneous-move games is novel, the underlying proof technique itself (relying on -Hannan consistency) is well-established in the online learning literature.  The originality of the theoretical contribution feels somewhat incremental. A more in-depth discussion of how the proof differs or extends existing work in online learning would be beneficial.
*   **Exclusion of UCB/UCT:** The paper explicitly excludes analysis of the widely used Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) / Upper Confidence Bound 1 applied to Trees (UCT) algorithms, citing existing work demonstrating their lack of convergence to Nash equilibrium [17]. However, given the prevalence of UCB/UCT in MCTS applications, a more detailed consideration and comparison – perhaps outlining the specific reasons for their failure and potential modifications – would strengthen the paper.  Existing analyses of UCB variants in similar settings should be acknowledged.
*   **Discrepancy Between Theory and Experiment:** The theoretical results are proven specifically for propagating the *mean* reward value, yet the experimental results suggest that propagating the *current sample* value also leads to convergence. This discrepancy needs to be explained. Is the convergence rate simply slower? Are the experimental conditions sufficient to overcome a theoretical limitation?
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While a valuable addition to the field, the paper’s contribution doesn’t appear to be highly groundbreaking. The convergence result, while useful, feels like a natural extension of existing work on regret minimization and MCTS.  It might not significantly impact the day-to-day practice of MCTS researchers.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a clear gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   The formal proof provides a valuable sufficient condition for convergence.
*   The empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical novelty is limited.
*   The exclusion of UCB/UCT feels incomplete without a more thorough discussion.
*   The discrepancy between the theoretical result and experimental findings requires further clarification.
*   The overall contribution, while positive, is somewhat incremental.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field, demonstrating a rigorous analysis of MCTS in a relatively unexplored setting. However, the limitations outlined above prevent it from being a truly outstanding submission. I rate it as a **weak accept**. Addressing the discrepancy between theory and experiment, acknowledging and discussing related work on UCB/UCT, and providing a more detailed discussion of the novelty of the proof technique would significantly strengthen the paper.
