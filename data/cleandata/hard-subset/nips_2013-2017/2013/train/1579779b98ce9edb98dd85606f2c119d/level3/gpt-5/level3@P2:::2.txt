Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It introduces a general SM-MCTS template that can be instantiated by selection rules that are Hannan-consistent (HC) in matrix games and ensure sufficient exploration. The core theoretical result shows that if the selection function is ε-HC and guarantees exploration, then SM-MCTS—when using mean backups—eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The proof proceeds by (i) analyzing repeated matrix games under HC selection, (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded error (arising from sampling and exploration), and (iii) inductively composing these guarantees up the tree to obtain subgame-perfect approximate equilibria. The authors instantiate the template with regret matching and Exp3, and empirically validate convergence on randomly generated games and adversarially selected small worst cases, comparing mean-backup and sample-backup variants.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on the MCTS/UCT line (Kocsis & Szepesvári) and on regret-minimization (regret matching; Exp3). Prior simultaneous-move MCTS application papers (e.g., Tron, general game playing) provided practical methods but lacked convergence theory, and UCB is known not to converge to NE in simultaneous-move settings (Shafiei et al.). A closely related precursor (Lanctot et al., 2013 workshop) presented preliminary analysis for SM-MCTS. This paper is, to my knowledge, the first to give general convergence guarantees to approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move EFGs under HC selection, with an explicit treatment of exploration and mean backups.

Strengths
- Clear and useful general template: decouples tree search from the choice of HC selection algorithm in matrix games.
- Solid, organized theory: the “repeated game with bounded error” abstraction is clean and reusable; the inductive argument up the tree is natural and well explained.
- Concrete instantiations (regret matching, Exp3) and experiments demonstrate the claims and compare mean vs. sample backups.
- The result addresses a known theoretical gap: convergence of MCTS beyond purely sequential perfect-information games.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Limited insight at inner nodes beyond error propagation: while the bounded-error framework is elegant, it provides asymptotic guarantees only and does not illuminate how information sharing or value estimation within non-leaf nodes affects learning dynamics. Finite-time/sample complexity bounds would markedly strengthen the contribution.
- Subgame-perfect analysis and connections: Theorem 4.11 states subgame-perfect approximate NE, but the discussion does not sufficiently relate this to the classical SPNE literature. Please contextualize with standard existence results for perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (e.g., Selten’s subgame perfection) and later refinements (e.g., Harris 1990), clarifying what is new here (algorithmic convergence to SPNE-approx) versus what is known (existence).
- Practicality of mean backups: the theory requires mean backups (as also used in some SM-MCTS variants), but many practical systems propagate the latest sample. While your experiments suggest both converge, the gap grows with depth. It would help to (i) articulate when sample backups break the proof, (ii) formalize conditions under which sample backups still converge, or (iii) quantify the asymptotic bias introduced by exploration and bootstrapping without means.
- Bounds scale poorly with depth: the approximation grows with depth (appears quadratic in D in Theorem 4.11’s statement), which may be pessimistic. Discuss tightness, opportunities for sharpening the constants (e.g., avoiding the factor 4 from Lemma 4.6/Cor. 4.7), and whether variance reduction or bootstrapping from value estimates could improve depth dependence.
- Experiments are small scale and mostly synthetic: results on Goofspiel/Tron or other benchmark simultaneous-move domains would increase external validity. Adding confidence intervals, sensitivity to γ and selection parameters, and reporting exploitability for both players would improve the empirical section.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The reductions from HC in matrix games to bounded-error matrix games and the inductive composition are plausible and carefully argued, with omitted proofs available. Assumptions are clear: ε-HC selection with guaranteed exploration and mean backups in a fully built tree. The main limitations are asymptotic nature (no rates) and potentially loose depth dependence.

Clarity
Well written and organized. Definitions, assumptions, and algorithmic variants are presented clearly. The role of RetVal and the necessity of using means are explained. Suggestions:
- Summarize the final bound cleanly in the main text (state the dependence on ε, γ, and D explicitly).
- Clarify the distinction between empirical frequencies and average strategies early, then rely on Lemma 4.5 to justify using either downstream.
- Expand the discussion of subgame perfection and its relation to classical results.

Originality
High. Providing convergence guarantees for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move EFGs under broad HC selectors is new. The “repeated game with bounded error” abstraction and the convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE are valuable contributions beyond earlier workshop/preliminary treatments.

Significance
Meaningful theoretical advance: it justifies a family of SM-MCTS algorithms widely used in practice and gives a pathway to analyze other selection schemes. While asymptotic, it is likely to influence follow-up work on finite-time bounds and extensions to imperfect information.

Pros for acceptance
- First general convergence result for SM-MCTS to (subgame-perfect) approximate NE under broad conditions.
- Clean, general template usable with standard online learning algorithms (RM, Exp3).
- Clear exposition; experiments support the asymptotic claims and compare design choices.

Cons for acceptance
- Asymptotic only; no finite-time/sample complexity bounds.
- Depth-dependent approximation bound appears loose.
- Experimental evaluation is limited in scale and breadth.
- Discussion of subgame-perfect equilibrium could be better integrated with classical literature.

Questions for authors
1) Can your analysis yield any finite-time guarantees (perhaps assuming uniform exploration and bounded variance) for the gap to NE as a function of t, D, and γ?
2) Is the D^2 dependence tight, or can it be improved with alternative backups or selection schedules?
3) Can you formalize conditions under which sample backups (without means) still converge?
4) How sensitive are the results to the exploration rate γ? Is adaptive/annealed exploration compatible with your proof?

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper fills a genuine theoretical gap with a general, well-presented analysis and provides actionable insights for SM-MCTS design. Strengthening the discussion of subgame-perfect equilibria and adding broader experiments would further improve the paper. My confidence is high.
