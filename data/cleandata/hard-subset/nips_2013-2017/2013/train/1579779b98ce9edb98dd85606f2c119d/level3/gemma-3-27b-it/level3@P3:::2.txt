This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) applied to zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors establish convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under certain conditions, specifically leveraging -Hannan consistent selection methods within the MCTS framework. The work builds upon preliminary analysis by Lanctot et al. (2013) and offers a significant theoretical advance, clarifying the conditions under which MCTS can be expected to perform well in these game types.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The definitions are precise, the notation is consistent, and the progression of the analysis is logical and easy to follow. The presentation of the algorithm (Algorithm 1) and the explanations of the regret matching and Exp3 variants are particularly commendable. The empirical results, while not extensive, corroborate the theoretical findings, demonstrating convergence on both randomly generated and “worst-case” games. The connection to existing literature is appropriate, citing relevant work in game theory and MCTS.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength is the formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. This fills a notable gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS beyond sequential games.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a complex topic accessible. The definitions, notation, and algorithmic descriptions are clear and concise.
*   **Solid Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, although limited in scope, provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
*   **Well-Contextualized:** The paper effectively positions its contribution within the broader context of game theory, MCTS, and related work.
*   **Thoroughness:** The inclusion of related work and detailed explanation of the theoretical framework demonstrate a strong understanding of the relevant field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Insight into Inner Nodes:** The analysis hinges on assuming optimal strategies at the leaf nodes of the game tree. This limits the insights gained regarding the behavior of MCTS in the inner nodes, where the exploration and exploitation balance is more complex. While acknowledged by the authors, this is a significant limitation.
*   **Lack of Connection to Subgame Perfect Equilibria:** The analysis doesn't directly tie into the concept of subgame perfect Nash equilibria, a central notion in game theory. While the authors state that convergence is to a *subgame perfect* -Nash equilibrium, the proof does not explicitly demonstrate this property.
*   **Narrow Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The empirical analysis focuses on relatively small games and a limited set of scenarios. Expanding the experimental evaluation to larger and more diverse games would strengthen the conclusions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical result, addressing a long-standing open question in the field of MCTS.
*   The clarity and rigor of the presentation are exceptional.
*   The empirical results provide supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
*   The work is well-positioned within the existing literature and builds upon previous work in a meaningful way.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The limitations of the analysis regarding inner nodes and subgame perfection restrict the broader applicability and impact of the results.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The clarity of the presentation and the significance of the theoretical result outweigh the limitations of the analysis. The authors acknowledge the weaknesses of their work, which further demonstrates their intellectual honesty. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.

Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand the discussion on the limitations of the analysis:** Further elaborate on why the inductive proof relying on leaf node optimality restricts insights into inner nodes.
2.  **Address the connection to subgame perfect equilibria:** Cite Harris (1990) as suggested and explicitly discuss how the convergence guarantee relates to the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games. Briefly explain *why* the proof leads to subgame perfection or acknowledge if a stronger statement cannot be made.
3.  **Consider a brief discussion on future work:** Expand on the stated plans for future research, particularly regarding finite-time convergence bounds and generalization to imperfect information games.
