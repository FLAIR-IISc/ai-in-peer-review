## Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms applied to zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The core contribution lies in proving that, under the condition of ε-Hannan consistency of the selection method used within the MCTS framework, the algorithm converges to an ε-Nash equilibrium. This is a significant step towards theoretically grounding the practical success of MCTS in these games, where convergence guarantees were previously lacking. The authors effectively frame MCTS as a general template, instantiable with various selection methods, broadening the applicability of the result. 

The work is well-motivated, positioning itself within the growing field of algorithmic game theory and highlighting the limitations of existing approaches like backward induction which require domain knowledge or computationally expensive methods. The paper clearly defines the necessary concepts and establishes a solid theoretical foundation. The empirical evaluation, comparing mean-value propagation versus sample-value propagation, provides some practical insight, albeit showing a slight underperformance for the mean-value approach. Further experiments attempt to provide insights into convergence rates, however, this is a key limitation of the work.

A major weakness is the lack of explicit convergence rates. While the authors acknowledge this, stemming from the reliance on ε-Hannan consistency alone, it limits the practical utility of the theoretical result. The reviewer's query regarding whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP3) could yield explicit rates is highly relevant and a potential direction for future work. The empirical results, while demonstrating convergence, don't offer a clear characterization of the speed of that convergence. Additionally, the impact of the work feels somewhat incremental, given the existing theoretical understanding of MCTS in sequential-move games. 

Despite these limitations, the paper is technically sound and well-written. However, several minor issues detract from the polish of the manuscript. These include typos (e.g., “leafs” should be “leaves”, “inverted” should be “negated”), and notation inconsistencies (Algorithm 1 line 10 using (a1,a2) while the rest of the paper uses (i,j)). 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides the first theoretical convergence guarantee for a general class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games.
*   Well-written, technically correct, and clearly presented.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of convergence rates limits the practical significance of the result.
*   Incremental nature of the contribution given existing MCTS theory in sequential games.
*   Minor polish issues (typos and notation inconsistencies).



**Overall assessment:** Accept with minor revisions. The authors should address the typos and notation inconsistencies. While the lack of convergence rates is a significant limitation, the theoretical contribution is valuable and justifies acceptance, particularly if the authors acknowledge this limitation prominently in the final version.
