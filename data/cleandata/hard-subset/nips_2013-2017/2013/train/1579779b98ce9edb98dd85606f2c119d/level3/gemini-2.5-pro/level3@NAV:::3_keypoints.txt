- Scope: analyzes convergence of MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves.  
- Framework: proposes a general MCTS framework usable with any ε-Hannan consistent selection method (e.g., Regret Matching, Exp3).  
- Main theory: proves asymptotic convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.  
- Contribution: extends MCTS convergence theory from sequential-move to simultaneous-move games using no-regret learning.  
- Technical quality: technically sound with a rigorous inductive proof from matrix games to the full game tree.  
- Transparency: authors openly acknowledge limitations, particularly the asymptotic nature of results.  
- Empirics: empirical evaluation supports the theory and compares mean-reward propagation versus sample-value propagation.  
- Clarity: well-written and organized but contains minor notational inconsistencies and typos (e.g., "leafs"→"leaves", "inverted" vs "negated", inconsistent action notation).  
- Originality/significance: provides the first formal convergence guarantee for a broad class of MCTS in simultaneous-move games—novel and significant.  
- Key weakness 1: no explicit convergence rates provided, which limits practical applicability; recommend analyzing rates assuming specific regret bounds (e.g., O(√T)).  
- Key weakness 2: work may be seen as an incremental extension from sequential MCTS; empirical result that mean-value propagation is slightly outperformed by sample-value propagation creates a minor theory-practice disconnect.  
- Overall: solid and valuable theoretical result whose impact is tempered by the absence of convergence rates.
