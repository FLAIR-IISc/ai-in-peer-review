This paper studies the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum, perfect-information, extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this setting and provide the first formal proof that such an algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The key theoretical contribution is establishing a sufficient condition for this convergence: the node selection strategy must be ε-Hannan consistent and guarantee exploration. This connects the MCTS framework to well-understood algorithms from online learning like Regret Matching and Exp3. The paper's claims are supported by experiments on randomly generated and worst-case games. This work addresses an important theoretical gap, as MCTS has been used in this game class empirically, but without the formal guarantees provided here.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and presents a valuable theoretical result. The main strength is the formal link it establishes between ε-Hannan consistency and MCTS convergence, providing a solid foundation for designing provably correct algorithms for these games.

However, several concerns limit the paper's impact. The primary weakness is that the proof technique for the main theorem—an inductive argument from leaf nodes up the game tree—may lack significant novelty, appearing as a straightforward extension of known results from single-stage matrix games to a multi-stage setting. A more significant issue is the disconnect between the theory and practice presented. The proof requires propagating the mean of rewards up the tree, whereas the experiments demonstrate that the standard MCTS approach of propagating the current sample value also converges effectively. The paper needs to better explain this discrepancy, as it calls into question the necessity of the conditions required for the proof.

Furthermore, while the paper correctly notes that UCB/UCT methods do not converge in this setting, it dismisses them too quickly. Given the prevalence of UCT, a deeper discussion or experimental comparison would better contextualize the work and strengthen the motivation for seeking alternative, provably convergent methods.

In summary, this is a technically sound paper with a useful, clear result. However, its contribution feels incremental due to the potentially limited novelty of the proof technique and the unresolved gap between the theoretical analysis and practical algorithm variants. While the work is a good step forward, its overall significance would be higher if these issues were addressed.
