This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated by various selection methods. They formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem and the relevant background material. The formal analysis is rigorous and well-organized, making it easy to follow. The empirical evaluation is also well-designed, providing insights into the performance of the proposed algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is its contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. The authors provide a sufficient condition for convergence to an approximate NE, which is a significant result. The empirical evaluation also demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm in practice.

However, there are some concerns regarding the novelty of the proof technique. The authors rely on existing results on -Hannan consistency and regret minimization, which may not be new to the field. Additionally, the paper does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms, which may limit the impact of the results.

Another concern is that the theoretical result only proves convergence for propagation of the mean, yet experiments show current sample-value propagation also works. This discrepancy needs to be explained, as it may affect the validity of the results.

Overall, the paper is technically interesting but not highly outstanding. The contribution appears incremental, building on existing results in the field. While the paper is well-written and provides a significant result, it may not be a breakthrough in the field of game theory or MCTS.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a significant theoretical result on the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous move games.
* The empirical evaluation is well-designed and provides insights into the performance of the proposed algorithm.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The proof technique may not be novel, relying on existing results in the field.
* The paper does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms, which may limit the impact of the results.
* The discrepancy between the theoretical result and the experimental findings needs to be explained.

Rating: 7/10

Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

Minor revisions:

* Clarify the contribution of the paper in the context of existing results on -Hannan consistency and regret minimization.
* Provide a clear explanation for the discrepancy between the theoretical result and the experimental findings.
* Consider adding a discussion on the implications of the results for widely used UCB/UCT algorithms.
