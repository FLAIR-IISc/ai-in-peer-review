This paper studies the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with different action selection mechanisms at each node. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration (i.e., every joint action is tried infinitely often), then the MCTS algorithm's average strategy converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The paper demonstrates this with two known ε-Hannan consistent algorithms, Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical evaluation on randomly generated games and on games designed to be worst-case scenarios, comparing the proposed variants and confirming their convergence.

This work addresses an important theoretical gap in the MCTS literature. While MCTS and its variants have been successfully applied to simultaneous move games in practice, formal convergence guarantees have been elusive. This paper provides the first such guarantee, relating the convergence of the overall MCTS procedure to the well-understood properties of regret minimization algorithms from online learning.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of the paper is its formal proof of convergence for a class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games. Establishing a sufficient condition (ε-Hannan consistency) for convergence to an approximate Nash Equilibrium is a valuable contribution that places the use of these algorithms on a more solid theoretical footing. This moves MCTS from being a successful heuristic to a provably sound approximation method for this game class.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of building their argument, starting from the known properties of regret minimizers in single matrix games, extending this to matrix games with bounded error, and finally using an inductive argument to prove the result for the full extensive-form game tree. This logical progression makes the technical content accessible.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed to support the theoretical claims. The authors not only confirm that their proposed algorithms converge but also compare the theoretically-required "mean propagation" update rule with the more standard "current sample" update rule. The investigation into empirically-derived worst-case games is a nice addition that provides insight into the practical convergence rates and challenges for these algorithms.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against Acceptance / Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Originality of the Proof Technique:** While the result itself is new for MCTS, the core proof technique appears to be a relatively straightforward, inductive application of well-established results from online learning and game theory. The fact that two Hannan-consistent players converge to a Nash Equilibrium in a repeated matrix game is a known concept. The paper's novelty lies in showing how this property can be propagated up a game tree where the payoffs (subgame values) are noisy estimates. While a valid and useful extension, this might be viewed as an incremental rather than a groundbreaking theoretical contribution.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice:** A significant weakness is the gap between the algorithm variant that is formally analyzed and the variants that perform best in practice. The proof in Section 4 relies on propagating the *mean* of accumulated rewards from a child node back to the parent (`RetVal = Xh/nh`). However, the experiments in Section 5 show that the standard MCTS approach of propagating the *current sample's* value (`RetVal = u1`) converges as well, and often slightly faster. The paper does not provide a theoretical explanation for this, nor does it discuss why the proof technique fails for the standard (and better-performing) variant. Extending the analysis to cover this case or explaining the discrepancy would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.

3.  **Limited Scope Regarding UCB/UCT:** The paper dismisses the most widely used MCTS selection strategy, UCB (and the UCT algorithm), in a single sentence by citing prior work showing it does not converge to a Nash Equilibrium. While this is a valid reason not to focus on it, the paper would be more significant if it provided more context. A brief discussion of *why* UCB fails in this setting (e.g., its inability to handle the non-stationary opponent strategies inherent in regret-matching players) and how regret-based methods overcome this limitation would better connect the work to the broader MCTS literature and highlight the importance of the proposed approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a good, solid paper that makes a clear and useful contribution to the field. It provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in an important class of games, which is a valuable result. The paper is technically sound and clearly presented. The main limitations are the somewhat incremental nature of the proof technique and the disconnect between the proven method and the empirically superior standard method. Despite these weaknesses, the paper's positive contributions—providing a firm theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms—are significant enough to warrant acceptance. It successfully bridges concepts from online learning and tree search in a way that is likely to be useful for future research in this area.
