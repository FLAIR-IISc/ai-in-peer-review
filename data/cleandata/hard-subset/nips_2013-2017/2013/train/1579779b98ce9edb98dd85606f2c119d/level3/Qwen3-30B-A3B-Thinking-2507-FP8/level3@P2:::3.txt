

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper provides a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. Their main theoretical contribution is a proof that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and satisfies exploration requirements, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The paper complements this with empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a gap in the literature: while MCTS variants have been widely used in simultaneous-move games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), no convergence guarantees existed for these settings. Previous work on MCTS primarily focused on sequential games (e.g., UCT for Go), and this paper extends the theoretical foundation to simultaneous-move games. The approach of connecting regret minimization to MCTS is elegant and general, showing that the convergence properties of the underlying regret minimization algorithm transfer to the MCTS framework.

**Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and carefully developed. The authors prove the convergence result through a series of lemmas that build on Hannan consistency and extend to repeated matrix games with bounded error. The formal analysis is technically correct, and the proof strategies are well-motivated. The empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims, demonstrating that the proposed methods converge to approximate Nash equilibria. However, the convergence result is asymptotic only, without providing finite-time convergence rates. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it would have been valuable to explore whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., the √T regret bound for Exp3) could yield explicit convergence rates. The absence of such rates is a notable limitation for practical applications, though the authors reasonably argue this may be inevitable under the ε-Hannan consistency framework.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The definitions and notation are carefully introduced, and the main theorem is presented in a logical sequence. The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-organized, with clear figures illustrating the convergence behavior. Minor notational inconsistencies exist (e.g., Algorithm 1 line 10 uses (a1,a2) instead of (i,j), and "leafs" appears as "leaves" in Figure 1), but these are easily fixable and do not significantly impede understanding.

**Originality:** This work is original in the context of MCTS for simultaneous-move games. While MCTS has been applied to such games in practice, the authors provide the first formal convergence guarantees. The connection between regret minimization and MCTS in this setting is novel, and the paper effectively distinguishes itself from prior work on MCTS in sequential games (e.g., UCT for Go). The authors appropriately cite related work, including recent advances in MCTS for general game-playing (e.g., [10, 11, 12, 13]) and prior work on backward induction for simultaneous-move games (e.g., [7, 8, 9]).

**Significance:** The results are significant for the field as they provide a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which are common in security domains, card games, and general game-playing competitions. The paper justifies the practical success of MCTS in these domains from a theoretical perspective. However, the incremental nature of the contribution (extending MCTS theory from sequential to simultaneous-move games) somewhat limits its impact compared to more transformative advances. The absence of explicit convergence rates also limits the practical applicability, though the authors rightly note that the empirical results show comparable convergence to other MCTS variants.

**Pro Acceptance Arguments:**
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games
- Elegant general template connecting regret minimization to MCTS
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with careful proofs
- Empirical results support theoretical claims
- Well-written and clear presentation

**Con Acceptance Arguments:**
- Convergence is only asymptotic with no explicit rates provided
- Incremental contribution given existing MCTS theory for sequential games
- Minor notational inconsistencies that could confuse readers
- Limited discussion of how the results compare to other theoretical approaches (e.g., counterfactual regret minimization)

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-executed paper that fills an important theoretical gap. While the absence of convergence rates and the incremental nature of the contribution slightly limit its impact, the paper provides valuable theoretical grounding for a widely used algorithm. The authors have made a significant contribution to the understanding of MCTS in a class of games that had previously lacked theoretical analysis. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the notational inconsistencies.
