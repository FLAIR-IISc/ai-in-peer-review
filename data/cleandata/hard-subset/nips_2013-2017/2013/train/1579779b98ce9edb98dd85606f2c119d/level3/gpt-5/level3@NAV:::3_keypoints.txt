- Paper proves asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS to an ε‑Nash equilibrium in two‑player zero‑sum simultaneous‑move perfect‑information games when the selection rule is ε‑Hannan consistent and ensures exploration.  
- Analysis reduces nodes to repeated matrix games with bounded error and uses induction on tree depth.  
- Empirical comparison between Regret Matching and Exp3 is presented.  
- Study of backup choice: mean‑value propagation (needed for proofs) slightly underperforms sample‑value propagation in convergence speed.  
- Complements negative results for UCB in simultaneous‑move settings and connects to online learning and prior MCTS theory.  
- Theory is careful and technically sound; bounded‑error reduction and depth induction are well executed.  
- Main limitation: results are asymptotic with no finite‑time convergence rates provided.  
- Reviewer suggests using concrete regret‑rate assumptions (e.g., O(√T)) to derive explicit exploitability rates and asks whether errors compound linearly or quadratically with depth.  
- Concern about fixed exploration γ causing a limit exploitability floor; asks if decaying/implicit exploration can be accommodated.  
- Paper is generally well written with proofs sketched and full details in appendix.  
- Notation inconsistencies and minor typos detract slightly from clarity.  
- Originality: first general asymptotic convergence guarantee for SM‑MCTS with no‑regret selection, but conceptually incremental relative to sequential‑move results.  
- Significance: provides theoretical grounding for common algorithms, but impact limited by lack of finite‑time rates and modest experimental scope; larger benchmarks recommended.  
- Pros: novel asymptotic guarantee, clean template linking online learning and MCTS, careful proofs and informative experiments.  
- Cons: no finite‑time rates or strong practical guidance, incremental vs sequential‑move literature, requirement of mean‑value backup slows empirical convergence.  
- Nitpicks: “leafs”→“leaves”; Line 144 prefer “negated” over “inverted”; Algorithm 1 has (a1,a2) vs (i,j)/(σ1,σ2) inconsistencies; Theorem 4.11 should clarify constants and dependence on ε.
