This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, a class that includes games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors prove that MCTS variants using any ε-Hannan consistent selection procedure, such as regret matching, converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium. This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for MCTS in this setting, building upon more preliminary analyses such as Lanctot et al. (2013) which explored a similar framework but lacked formal convergence guarantees.

The primary strength of this work is its novel and significant formal analysis. Providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in this game class is an important contribution, bridging a critical gap between the algorithm's practical application and its theoretical understanding. The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and the results are presented effectively, making the complex proof accessible. The theoretical claims appear technically sound and well-supported by the presented inductive argument.

However, the analysis has some limitations. The main weakness stems from the proof technique itself; its inductive nature, which assumes optimal strategies at the leaf nodes and propagates this assumption upwards, offers limited insight into the complex strategic dynamics occurring at the inner nodes of the game tree. Furthermore, while the authors claim convergence to a subgame perfect equilibrium, the analysis fails to connect this result to the rich body of established game-theoretic tools for this concept. This disconnect from the broader theory of extensive-form games makes the result less impactful than it could be.

To strengthen the paper and better situate its contribution, the authors should explicitly tie their analysis to the literature on subgame perfection in simultaneous-move games. Citing and discussing foundational work, such as Harris (1990) on the existence of such equilibria, would provide a stronger theoretical grounding for their claims.

Overall, this is a valuable theoretical paper that addresses an important open problem. While the analysis could be better contextualized within game theory, its core contribution is sound and significant for the MCTS community.
