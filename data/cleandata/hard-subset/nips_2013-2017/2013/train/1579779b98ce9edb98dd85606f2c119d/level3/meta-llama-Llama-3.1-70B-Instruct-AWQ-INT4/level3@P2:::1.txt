This paper presents a study on the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games, which can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as a function for selecting the next actions to be sampled. They formally prove that if the algorithm uses an -Hannan consistent selection function, which assures attempting each action infinitely many times, the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game.

The paper relates to previous work on MCTS in sequential games and extends the analysis to simultaneous move games. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their ideas, making it easy to follow their arguments. The use of regret-matching and Exp3 as selection methods is well-motivated, and the empirical evaluation on randomly generated games and worst-case games provides valuable insights into the performance of the proposed algorithm.

The paper's main contribution is the formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under certain conditions. However, the proof technique may not be particularly novel, and the authors could have provided more context and comparison with existing work in the field. Additionally, the paper does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms, which is a notable omission given the existing analyses of these methods.

The authors' decision to focus on propagation of the mean in their theoretical result is well-justified, but the fact that their experiments show current sample-value propagation also works is not fully explained. This discrepancy between theory and practice could be explored further to provide a more complete understanding of the algorithm's behavior.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and detailed presentation of their ideas. However, the contribution appears incremental, and the paper may not be highly outstanding in terms of novelty or impact.
