Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors define a general SM-MCTS template that can use any selection function that is ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) in matrix games and ensures exploration. They prove almost-sure, asymptotic convergence of the induced SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game, with a depth-dependent error bound (Theorem 4.11). The analysis proceeds by (i) establishing convergence properties for repeated matrix games under ε-HC, (ii) extending these to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error (to model nonstationarity and exploration bias), and (iii) lifting the guarantees to the game tree by induction over depth. Empirical results with regret matching (RM) and Exp3 on random and adversarially selected games support the convergence claims; additionally, the paper compares propagating mean returns (required by the theory) vs. propagating immediate sample returns (as is common in practice), finding that mean propagation converges slightly slower.

This contributes formal guarantees to a setting where UCT-style algorithms lack convergence to NE (e.g., [17]) and where exact backward-induction algorithms with alpha-beta pruning exist but require domain knowledge or costly LP/double-oracle steps [8,9]. The work is conceptually related to no-regret learning leading to equilibria in repeated games [15,16,19] and to convergence results for sequential-move MCTS [14], but focuses on simultaneous moves, for which, to my knowledge, no prior MCTS convergence guarantees existed.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The proof roadmap is clear and plausible: ε-HC implies approachability to NE in repeated matrix games; bounded errors preserve approximate convergence; error accumulation across depths yields a subgame-perfect approximate NE. The assumptions (ε-HC, guaranteed exploration, eventual full-tree expansion) are stated and justified. The empirical section, while small, is consistent with the claims and thoughtfully explores worst cases and the effect of mean vs. sample propagation.
- Limitations: The guarantees are asymptotic; no finite-time convergence rates are given. This is a meaningful limitation for practice. Given that many ε-HC algorithms admit regret bounds of O(1/√T), it is natural to ask whether these translate into explicit depth-dependent finite-time exploitability bounds for SM-MCTS under additional regularity assumptions. The paper briefly acknowledges this as future work.
- Carefulness: Minor notation and editorial issues (listed below) could be cleaned up, but they do not affect the substance.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The algorithmic template is clear; the roles of selection, update, and return value are well explained, including the rationale for using sample means in the analysis. The theoretical section is dense but readable; key lemmas are stated with intuition. Some notational inconsistencies and small typos should be fixed (see Minor comments).

Originality
To the best of my knowledge, this is the first general asymptotic convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information games. The reduction to ε-HC selection rules and the careful handling of exploration-induced errors are novel in this context. While the building blocks (HC, repeated games, induction over depth) are known, their combination in an MCTS setting with simultaneous moves is original and timely.

Significance
The result fills a gap between practical success of SM-MCTS and the lack of theoretical guarantees, and it helps justify the use of regret-based selection in such games. The theoretical template is modular and could influence algorithm design beyond RM/Exp3. The absence of finite-time rates tempers immediate impact, but the contribution is still significant for the community interested in game-theoretic planning and online learning.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Finite-time rates: Can the authors derive explicit finite-time bounds by assuming concrete regret rates (e.g., O(√(log A/T)) for Exp3 variants or O(1/√T) for RM) and tracking error propagation across depth? Even a coarse bound of the form exploitability ≤ poly(D)·max_h Regret_h(T_h)/T_h would be valuable.
- Exploration schedule: The analysis uses a fixed γ exploration. Could γ be annealed (e.g., γ_t→0) to reduce asymptotic bias while preserving guaranteed exploration, and would the proofs adapt?
- Return value choice: The theory requires propagating means, whereas practice often uses immediate samples. Is there a weaker condition (e.g., exponential moving averages) that would suffice for the proofs, balancing bias/variance and aligning better with empirical performance?
- Subgame perfection: The theorem states subgame-perfect approximate NE. It would help to explicitly quantify how the approximation parameter depends on depth and ε, and to discuss whether the bound is tight.
- Tree growth: The analysis conditions on eventual full-tree expansion. A brief argument bounding the expected time to full expansion under guaranteed exploration would strengthen the presentation.

Pros
- First convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad ε-HC selection rules.
- Clean, modular analysis bridging repeated games with error and tree induction.
- Empirical validation on random and adversarial games; useful observations on mean vs. sample propagation.
- Clear exposition of algorithmic template; relevant to practitioners.

Cons
- No finite-time convergence rates; asymptotic-only result limits direct practical guidance.
- Some statements (e.g., constants in Theorem 4.11) could be more explicit; minor notation inconsistencies.
- Experiments are relatively small-scale; comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., alpha-beta with pruning in this setting) are not reported.

Minor comments and typos
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Around Algorithm 1: line 10 likely returns a joint action (i,j); ensure consistency between (i,j) and (a1,a2) notation throughout.
- Section 3: “Player 2 does the same except using negated utilities” (not “inverted”).
- Eq. (4): clarify the definition of wh,i (subtracting the max for stability is fine; state both forms once).
- Definition 4.3: clarify that exploratory moves do not update A’s internal state; briefly justify that Lemma 4.4 covers this off-policy exploration.
- Theorem 4.11: spell out the exact dependence of the approximation parameter on depth D and ε to avoid ambiguity.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and timely theoretical contribution with supportive experiments. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that adding even coarse finite-time bounds (or a clear discussion of how regret-rate assumptions would translate) would substantially strengthen the impact.
