

# Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games, which can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They prove that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games and satisfies additional exploration requirements, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The paper empirically validates this result using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The work builds on prior MCTS research (e.g., UCT by Kocsis and Szepesvari) and game theory literature (e.g., Hart and Mas-Colell on regret minimization). It addresses a gap in the literature by providing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, where such results were previously unavailable.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous theoretical analysis. The proofs are well-structured and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. However, the proof technique primarily relies on established results about Hannan consistency and repeated games, which may lack novelty for the main theoretical contribution. The authors correctly acknowledge that their proof technique builds on existing literature (citing Hart and Mas-Colell, Blum and Mansour). The empirical evaluation is thorough, though it would benefit from a more detailed analysis of convergence rates.

**Clarity:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and provides sufficient detail for an expert to reproduce the results. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures effectively illustrate the key findings. The distinction between propagation of the mean versus current sample values is clearly explained, though the paper could better address why the empirical results match the theoretical analysis despite the proof only covering mean propagation.

**Originality:** The paper makes a useful contribution by extending MCTS convergence guarantees to simultaneous move games, which is a significant gap in the literature. However, the sufficient condition for convergence (using ε-Hannan consistent selection methods) is not highly novel, as such methods have been well-studied in online learning. The paper does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms (which are prevalent in MCTS applications), and the authors should address this omission. The paper could strengthen its originality by comparing their approach to existing MCTS variants and explaining why their theoretical framework applies to them despite not being explicitly covered.

**Significance:** The work is technically interesting and provides valuable theoretical grounding for MCTS in a specific class of games. However, the contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. The results are important for researchers studying MCTS in game theory contexts, but they may not significantly advance the broader field of machine learning. The paper correctly notes that their theoretical result only proves convergence for propagation of the mean, while experiments show current sample-value propagation also works—this discrepancy needs better explanation.

**Strengths:**
- Clear presentation of a useful theoretical result
- Rigorous empirical validation of the theoretical claims
- Appropriate comparison to existing work in game theory
- Well-structured and readable manuscript

**Weaknesses:**
- Proof technique lacks novelty for the main theoretical contribution
- Does not analyze widely used UCB/UCT algorithms, which are prevalent in practice
- Theoretical result only proves convergence for propagation of the mean, while experiments use current sample-value propagation without explanation
- Contribution appears incremental rather than highly innovative

**Recommendation:** The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on MCTS in game-theoretic settings, but its incremental nature and lack of analysis for widely used algorithms prevent it from being highly significant. I recommend a borderline decision between acceptance and rejection. The paper should be accepted if the authors can address the concerns about the proof's novelty, provide a clear explanation for the discrepancy between the theoretical analysis (mean propagation) and empirical results (current sample propagation), and discuss UCB/UCT variants more thoroughly.

The paper's main value lies in providing a theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous move games, but it could be strengthened by addressing the gaps noted above. For a NIPS paper, the contribution is technically sound but not sufficiently novel or impactful to be considered outstanding. The reviewers should consider whether the incremental contribution is worth publishing in NIPS given the conference's high standards.
