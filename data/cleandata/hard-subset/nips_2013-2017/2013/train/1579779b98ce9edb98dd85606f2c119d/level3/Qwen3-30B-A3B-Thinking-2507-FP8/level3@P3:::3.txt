

**Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"**

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing selection method, and prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the algorithm converges asymptotically to an ε-Nash equilibrium. This work addresses an important gap in the literature, as prior MCTS convergence results primarily focused on sequential games, with no guarantees for simultaneous-move settings.

The paper builds upon foundational work in online learning (e.g., regret matching [15] and Exp3 [16]) and extends it to the MCTS framework. It properly contextualizes its contribution relative to prior NIPS work, particularly noting that UCT-based MCTS (which works well in sequential games [14]) fails to converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move games [17], positioning their work as addressing this specific limitation.

**Strengths:**
1. The theoretical contribution is significant and well-executed. The proof of asymptotic convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium represents a clear advancement over prior work.
2. The paper is technically correct and well-written, with a clear presentation of the formal analysis.
3. The empirical evaluation provides valuable insights, particularly the comparison between propagating mean rewards versus current sample values. The finding that mean-value propagation slightly underperforms sample-value propagation is useful for practitioners.
4. The authors are appropriately cautious about limitations, explicitly acknowledging the asymptotic nature of their convergence result.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The absence of convergence rates is a notable limitation. While the authors note this may be inevitable under only ε-Hannan consistency, it would have been valuable to explore whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., the √T rate from Exp3) yield explicit convergence rates, as suggested by the reviewer.
2. The impact is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the contribution given existing sequential-move MCTS results. While the simultaneous-move setting is important, the asymptotic convergence result without rates reduces its practical significance compared to prior work with stronger guarantees.
3. The paper contains several minor issues: Figure 1 uses "leafs" instead of "leaves"; Line 144 uses "negated" instead of "inverted"; Algorithm 1 line 10 uses (a1,a2) instead of (i,j) or (σ1,σ2); and there are other notation inconsistencies between (i,j) and a.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a rigorous proof of asymptotic convergence. The empirical evaluation adequately supports the theoretical claims, though it doesn't provide explicit convergence rates.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though the notation inconsistencies noted above slightly reduce clarity.

**Originality:** The paper addresses a distinct gap in the literature regarding MCTS in simultaneous-move games, differentiating it from prior NIPS work on sequential games. The authors properly cite related work and clearly articulate their contribution.

**Significance:** The work provides a crucial theoretical foundation for using MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which is relevant for applications in security domains and game-playing. However, the asymptotic nature of the result without convergence rates limits its immediate practical significance compared to other MCTS work.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a solid theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. The paper is well-written, technically correct, and provides valuable insights into MCTS for simultaneous-move games. While the asymptotic convergence result without rates is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and frame it as a direction for future work.

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a clear and important theoretical contribution to understanding MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The work is well-executed, properly contextualized within the literature, and addresses a meaningful gap. The minor limitations noted (particularly the absence of convergence rates) are acknowledged by the authors and do not undermine the paper's technical contribution. The paper represents a necessary step toward formalizing MCTS in a broader class of games and will inform future research in the field.
