- Studies convergence of MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, where standard selection policies like UCB can fail to converge.  
- Proposes a general MCTS template that uses any regret-minimizing procedure for the local matrix games at each node.  
- Main theoretical result: if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (e.g., regret matching, Exp3), MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium.  
- Proof technique: induction from leaf nodes upward, treating subgame values as payoffs with bounded error; the argument is clever and technically sound.  
- Supports theory with experiments on random and worst-case games, comparing regret matching and Exp3, which empirically show convergence.  
- Quality: technically solid, clear assumptions, and the formal analysis convincingly supports the central claim.  
- Clarity: very well written and organized, with consistent notation and an easy-to-follow experimental section.  
- Originality: provides the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS to an approximate NE in this game class—a significant novel contribution.  
- Significance: important for MCTS research and practice, laying theoretical foundations and enabling future work (e.g., finite-time bounds, imperfect-information extensions).  
- Strength: broad applicability—result holds for any ε-Hannan consistent selection with guaranteed exploration, not a single algorithm.  
- Strength: empirical validation and practical insights (e.g., mean vs. sample value propagation).  
- Weakness: the treatment of subgame perfection is limited; the paper could better contextualize and discuss implications with existing literature.  
- Weakness: the black-box handling of inner-node values gives limited insight into strategic dynamics of policy evolution at internal nodes.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, significant, rigorous result that addresses an important open problem and is well-presented.  
- Arguments against acceptance: weaker connection to game-theoretic literature on subgame perfection and limited deeper insight into inner-node dynamics.  
- Overall: strengths substantially outweigh weaknesses; the paper is a high-quality, important contribution.
