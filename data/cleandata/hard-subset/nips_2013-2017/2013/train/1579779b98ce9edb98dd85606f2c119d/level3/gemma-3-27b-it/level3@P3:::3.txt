This paper analyzes a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms employing any no-regret selection method, proving asymptotic convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium for simultaneous-move games. The work is technically sound and well-written, presenting a significant, though incremental, contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS.

The authors correctly position their work within the context of algorithmic game theory and simulation-based game playing, referencing key works such as those on Goofspiel, general game-playing, and previous MCTS applications. They clearly articulate the gap in theoretical understanding regarding MCTS in simultaneous-move games, highlighting the limitations of backward induction approaches and the need for analysis of algorithms like MCTS that rely on simulation. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the paper's contributions.

The strength of this paper lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors establish a general template for SM-MCTS and demonstrate, under the assumption of ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration, that the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof strategy, leveraging repeated matrix games with bounded error and inductive reasoning, appears sound. The careful definitions and notation, while dense, are necessary for the mathematical rigor.

However, the lack of explicit convergence rates is a notable weakness. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest it may be inherent given the generality of their assumptions (specifically, only requiring ε-Hannan consistency), the reviewer’s query regarding whether concrete regret rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP3) could yield explicit rates remains pertinent. Addressing this, even with a negative result demonstrating the difficulty of obtaining such rates, would strengthen the paper. The empirical analysis, while providing some insight into convergence behavior, doesn't fully compensate for the absence of theoretical rates.

The experimental section offers a reasonable comparison between propagating mean reward values versus current sample values. The finding that mean-value propagation slightly underperforms sample-value propagation is interesting, although the magnitude of the difference seems modest. The attempt to identify empirically "worst-case" games is commendable, but the search space is limited and doesn't guarantee true worst-case identification.

Minor issues detract from an otherwise polished presentation. Figure 1 incorrectly labels “leafs” as “leaves”. Line 144 contains a stylistic error ("negated" should be "inverted"). Algorithm 1, line 10, uses (a1,a2) which is inconsistent with the rest of the notation and should be (i,j) or (σ1,σ2). Several other instances of inconsistent use of (i,j) vs. a notation are present and should be standardized. While these are minor, they indicate a lack of meticulous attention to detail.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantee for a general class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and the proofs appear correct.
*   **Well-Written:** The paper is clearly structured and the authors effectively communicate complex ideas.
*   **Relevance:** The work addresses an important open problem in the field of game-theoretic algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Convergence Rates:** The absence of explicit convergence rates limits the practical impact of the theoretical result.
*   **Incremental Nature:** The contribution, while valuable, is incremental given existing results on sequential-move MCTS.
*   **Minor Presentation Issues:** Typographical errors and inconsistencies in notation detract from the overall quality.

**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper is a solid contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS. Addressing the minor presentation issues and acknowledging the limitations regarding convergence rates would further strengthen the work. While the lack of rates and the incremental nature of the contribution are concerns, the theoretical result is significant enough to warrant publication at NIPS, especially given the growing interest in applying MCTS to complex game-playing scenarios. The authors should specifically consider discussing (even briefly) why obtaining tighter rates seems difficult under their general framework.
