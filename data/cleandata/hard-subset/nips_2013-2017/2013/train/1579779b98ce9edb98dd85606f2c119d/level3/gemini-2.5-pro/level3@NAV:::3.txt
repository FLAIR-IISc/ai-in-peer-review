This paper analyzes the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS framework that can be instantiated with any selection method that is ε-Hannan consistent, such as Regret Matching or Exp3. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof of asymptotic convergence to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This work extends the theoretical understanding of MCTS, which has been largely confined to sequential-move games, to the simultaneous-move setting, building upon the well-established theory of no-regret learning.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The inductive proof structure—building from matrix games to repeated games with bounded error and finally to the full game tree—is rigorous. The authors are commendably transparent about the limitations of their analysis, particularly the asymptotic nature of the result. The empirical evaluation validates the theory and offers useful insights, such as the performance comparison between propagating mean rewards versus current sample values.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. However, several minor notational inconsistencies and typos should be addressed. For example, in Figure 1, "leafs" should be "leaves"; on Line 144, "negated" is more standard than "inverted"; and the action notation `(a1,a2)` in Algorithm 1, line 10, should be consistent with `(i,j)` used elsewhere.

**Originality and Significance:** The main strength lies in providing the first formal convergence guarantee for a broad class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games. This is a novel and significant step, formally justifying the use of popular no-regret learners within MCTS for this game class.

However, the impact of this contribution is somewhat mitigated by two key points. The most significant weakness is the lack of explicit convergence rates. While asymptotic convergence is proven, the absence of rates limits the practical implications, as it provides no guidance on the number of simulations needed to achieve a desired approximation quality. I would strongly encourage the authors to discuss whether assuming a specific regret rate for the underlying learner (e.g., O(√T)) could yield a concrete convergence rate for the overall MCTS algorithm. Secondly, while important, the work could be viewed as an incremental extension of MCTS theory from the sequential domain. The experimental finding that the theoretically-analyzed mean-value propagation is slightly outperformed by standard sample-value propagation also creates a minor disconnect between the proof and empirical best practice.

In summary, this is a solid paper with a valuable theoretical result. Its primary weakness is the lack of convergence rates, which tempers its overall impact but does not invalidate the contribution.
