

This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this setting. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games, proving that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The theoretical results build upon existing work on Hannan consistency in repeated games [15, 16] and extend it to the multi-stage game setting through careful error analysis in the tree structure.

The paper makes a clear contribution by establishing a sufficient condition for convergence that explains why certain MCTS variants work in practice. The authors empirically validate their theoretical claims using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated games and worst-case examples, demonstrating that both propagation methods (mean vs. current sample) converge to approximate Nash equilibria, though the theoretical analysis requires mean propagation. This empirical finding is particularly valuable as it suggests practitioners may use the simpler current-sample propagation without sacrificing convergence.

Strengths of the paper include its clear problem formulation, well-structured theoretical analysis, and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The formal analysis is technically sound, with careful handling of the multi-stage nature of the problem through inductive arguments about convergence in subgames. The paper also properly contextualizes its work against previous game-theoretic approaches to MCTS [12, 14, 17], noting the lack of convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games in prior literature. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing both random and worst-case games, and the implementation details are sufficiently precise for reproducibility.

However, the paper has several notable weaknesses. First, the theoretical contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core convergence proof relies heavily on existing Hannan consistency results [15, 16] and extends them to multi-stage games, which is a reasonable but not particularly novel approach. The authors might have strengthened their contribution by discussing how their approach differs from related work in game theory (e.g., [18, 19]) or by providing a more detailed comparison to recent advances in regret minimization for extensive-form games.

Second, the paper does not address UCT/UCB, which are the most widely used MCTS variants in practice for sequential games and have been adapted for simultaneous move games [12, 17]. The authors should have at least discussed why UCT variants don't satisfy their Hannan consistency requirements, as this would provide important context for practitioners. In fact, Shafiei et al. [17] already showed that UCT fails to converge in simple simultaneous move games, which suggests the authors' framework could explain this failure.

Third, the paper presents a theoretical result requiring propagation of the mean value, yet the empirical experiments show that propagation of the current sample value also converges well. The authors do not adequately explain this discrepancy, which weakens the practical relevance of their theoretical result. A more nuanced discussion of the trade-offs between theoretical guarantees and practical implementation would strengthen the paper.

Regarding significance, the work provides a valuable theoretical foundation for understanding why certain MCTS variants work in simultaneous move games, which is useful for practitioners. However, the paper doesn't advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way beyond the specific convergence guarantee, and the contribution seems more like a natural extension of existing work than a major breakthrough.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written with clear definitions and logical flow. The technical content is accessible to readers familiar with game theory and online learning, and the experimental setup is well-described. The authors have properly referenced related work, though they might have included more recent advances in regret minimization for extensive-form games.

The quality of the paper is good, with sound theoretical arguments and thorough empirical validation. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach, and the experiments adequately support their claims. However, the incremental nature of the contribution and the lack of analysis of widely used UCT variants somewhat diminish the paper's impact.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound paper that provides a useful sufficient condition for MCTS convergence in simultaneous move games. While the contribution is not revolutionary, it fills an important gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in this specific game class. The paper would benefit from addressing the UCT/UCB gap and explaining the mean vs. current sample propagation discrepancy more thoroughly. Given the current state of the field, this paper is a solid contribution to the literature but not one of the most significant papers at NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical-experimental discrepancy and to discuss UCT variants more fully.
