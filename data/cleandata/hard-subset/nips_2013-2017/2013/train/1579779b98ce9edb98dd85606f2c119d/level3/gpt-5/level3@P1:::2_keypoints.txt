- Problem: Analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search in two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”).
- Main contribution: A general SM-MCTS template with any ε-Hannan-consistent selection rule and guaranteed exploration, proving almost-sure convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE.
- Key assumptions: Backups must use means of returns (not single samples), each joint action is tried infinitely often (full-tree eventuality), and selection rules have guaranteed exploration.
- Central theoretical result: Average strategies converge to a subgame-perfect (D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium (for any δ>0) under the above assumptions.
- Proof sketch: (i) convergence in repeated matrix games with ε-Hannan consistency, (ii) extend to bounded-payoff-error matrix games, (iii) induction over tree depth to bound error propagation to root.
- Explicit constants: The analysis gives concrete constants (e.g., 4(c+1) in matrix games) and a D^2 dependence on depth.
- Empirical validation: Experiments on random and adversarial instances compare regret matching vs Exp3 and mean vs sample backups, showing consistency with theory.
- Empirical finding: Mean-backups modestly slow convergence but reach the exploitability floor imposed by forced exploration.
- Relation to prior work: Advances beyond empirical SM-MCTS studies and known negative UCT results by providing the first general convergence guarantee under regret-based selection.
- Strength — clarity: Well-organized exposition, precise definitions, consistent pseudocode and explicit parameters.
- Strength — novelty: First broad convergence analysis for SM-MCTS with ε-Hannan-consistent selection and explicit exploration.
- Strength — proof: Insightful reduction to repeated games and depth induction, making assumptions and limitations transparent.
- Strength — experiments: Appropriate methodology comparing selection rules and backup types on multiple instance types.
- Practical takeaway: Enforce explicit exploration and use mean backups to guarantee asymptotic convergence.
- Weakness — loose bounds: D^2 dependence and constant blow-ups appear pessimistic; no finite-time convergence rates provided.
- Weakness — strong assumptions: Reliance on mean backups and full-tree eventuality limits applicability to common sample-backup and large-action implementations.
- Weakness — interior-node analysis: Limited structural insight into how simultaneous-move interactions at inner nodes affect convergence speed/constants.
- Weakness — exposition: Could better contextualize subgame-perfection in game-theoretic literature and clarify proven vs conjectured variants.
- Suggestions: Provide finite-time/iteration complexity guidance, tighten constants or justify D^2, analyze sample-backup variants, and relate results to subgame-perfect equilibrium theory.
- Suggestions (practical): Discuss scalability, progressive widening, and function approximation implications for large action spaces.
- Arguments for acceptance: First principled convergence guarantee under practically relevant selection rules, clean theory, and solid empirical support.
- Arguments against acceptance: Lack of finite-time rates, dependence on mean backups/full-tree, and limited practical applicability to common MCTS variants.
- Overall recommendation: Lean toward acceptance due to novelty, clarity, and relevance, subject to addressing suggested clarifications and extensions.
