

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors develop a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any no-regret selection method, proving that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the algorithm converges to an ε-Nash equilibrium. The work extends previous MCTS analyses that primarily focused on sequential games, addressing an important gap in the literature.

The paper's main strength lies in its technically sound theoretical contribution, establishing a convergence guarantee for a broad class of MCTS algorithms. The formal proof is well-structured and builds upon established concepts from online learning and game theory. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical analysis by comparing mean-value propagation (required for the proof) with sample-value propagation (common in practice), finding only minor performance differences.

However, the absence of convergence rates represents a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this is likely inevitable under only ε-Hannan consistency, the reviewer questions whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., √T from EXP3) could yield explicit convergence rates, which would substantially strengthen the theoretical contribution. The incremental nature of the work, building upon known sequential-move MCTS results, also limits its perceived significance.

The paper is well-written and technically correct, though it contains several minor notation inconsistencies and typos (e.g., "leafs" instead of "leaves," "negated" instead of "inverted," and inconsistent (a1,a2) vs. (i,j) notation). These should be easily corrected in revision.

In summary, the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to MCTS analysis in a previously unaddressed game class. While the asymptotic convergence result is important, the lack of convergence rates diminishes its impact for practical applications. The work is technically sound and well-presented, but its incremental nature and missing rates prevent it from being a groundbreaking contribution. 

Pro: Solid theoretical analysis, well-written, addresses an important gap in MCTS theory for simultaneous-move games. Con: No convergence rates, incremental contribution, minor notational issues. The paper is suitable for acceptance at a high-quality conference but would benefit from addressing the rate question or discussing limitations more thoroughly.
