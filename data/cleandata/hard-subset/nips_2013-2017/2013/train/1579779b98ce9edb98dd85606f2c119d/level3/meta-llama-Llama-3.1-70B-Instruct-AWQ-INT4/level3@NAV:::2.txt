This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of game theory and artificial intelligence, providing a formal analysis of the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors prove that any -Hannan consistent algorithm can be used to create a MCTS algorithm that provably converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the game.

The paper is well-written, clear, and well-organized, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments and understand the technical details. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem, explaining the context and the significance of the research. They also provide a comprehensive review of the related work, highlighting the contributions of their paper.

The technical contributions of the paper are substantial. The authors introduce a general template of MCTS algorithms for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, which can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as a function for selecting the next actions to be sampled. They formally prove that if the algorithm uses an -Hannan consistent selection function, which assures attempting each action infinitely many times, the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium of the extensive form game.

The authors also provide empirical evaluations of their claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods on randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games. The results show that the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on their template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms for these games.

The strengths of the paper include:

* A clear and well-written introduction to the problem and the technical contributions
* A comprehensive review of the related work
* A significant technical contribution, providing a formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves
* Empirical evaluations of the claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the results and the potential applications of the research
