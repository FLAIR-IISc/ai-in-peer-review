This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a setting relevant to games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors establish convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under the condition that the selection method employed within MCTS is -Hannan consistent and satisfies additional exploration requirements. They instantiate this general template with regret matching and Exp3, providing empirical validation of their theoretical results.

The work builds upon prior efforts in applying MCTS to these types of games, notably the work of Lanctot et al. (2013), which offered preliminary analysis in this area. This paper significantly advances this line of research by providing formal convergence guarantees. The problem addressed is important; while MCTS is widely used in practice, theoretical understanding of its behavior in simultaneous-move games has been lacking. The presentation is clear and well-structured, making the core ideas accessible. The empirical evaluation convincingly supports the theoretical claims, demonstrating convergence on both randomly generated and specifically constructed “worst-case” games.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The formal analysis is a key contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for the practical success of MCTS in this domain. The generality of the template – allowing instantiation with different selection methods – is a valuable aspect. The empirical results are well-executed and bolster confidence in the theoretical findings. The authors thoroughly acknowledge related work and position their contributions effectively within the existing literature. The writing is exceptionally clear, facilitating understanding of both the technical details and the broader context.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the convergence guarantee is significant, the analysis appears to focus largely on the leaf nodes of the game tree. The inductive proof relies on assuming optimal strategies at the leaf nodes, which while a common technique, leaves open questions about the behavior within the deeper levels of the tree. The connection between the approximate Nash equilibrium achieved and subgame perfect Nash equilibria is not explicitly articulated.  A more direct engagement with subgame perfect concepts would strengthen the results. The authors could benefit from citing Harris (1990), which establishes the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games, and discussing how their results relate to this established framework. 

Furthermore, the paper does not extensively explore the implications of the convergence rate. While the empirical results provide some insight, a more detailed theoretical analysis of the rate of convergence – even under simplifying assumptions – would be valuable. Exploring the impact of different exploration parameters (γ) on convergence speed would also be beneficial. The analysis also does not delve deeply into the specific characteristics of games that make them easier or harder for this MCTS approach to solve.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The general template allows for flexibility in the choice of selection method.
*   Empirical results validate the theoretical findings.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Addresses an important open problem in the field.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The analysis primarily focuses on leaf nodes and does not provide detailed insight into the behavior of MCTS in the deeper levels of the game tree.
*   The connection to subgame perfect equilibria is not explicitly established.
*   Limited analysis of convergence rate and factors influencing performance.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses outlined above, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The formal analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move games is a significant achievement, and the empirical results provide strong support for the theoretical claims. The weaknesses do not detract substantially from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised regarding the connection to subgame perfect equilibria and potentially expanding the discussion of convergence rate. The clarity of the paper and the importance of the results make it a suitable addition to NIPS 2013.
