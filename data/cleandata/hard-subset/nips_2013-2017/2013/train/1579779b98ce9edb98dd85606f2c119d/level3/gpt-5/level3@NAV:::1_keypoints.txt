- Studies SM-MCTS in two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves; proposes a generic SM-MCTS using any ε–Hannan-consistent selector with exploration.  
- Main theorem: almost-sure convergence to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium with approximation O(D^2 ε) in depth D when mean returns are propagated.  
- Empirical instantiation with regret matching and Exp3; validates convergence on random and hand-crafted hard games.  
- Observes that practical variants that propagate sample returns also empirically appear to converge.  
- Connects online-learning regret guarantees to stacked matrix games and complements CFR results by addressing SM-MCTS specifically.  
- Strength: Clear problem statement and a useful sufficient condition based on ε–Hannan consistency and exploration.  
- Strength: Clean inductive proof via repeated matrix games and careful treatment of empirical vs average strategies.  
- Strength: Solid and reproducible empirical validation across varied instances.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty in proof technique—relies on standard online-learning reduction and induction.  
- Weakness: Theoretical result requires mean-propagation, but experiments suggest sample-return propagation works—this discrepancy is unexplained.  
- Weakness: No analysis or experimental comparison of UCB/UCT variants, which are widely used in practice.  
- Weakness: Bounds (D^2 dependence and constants like 4(c+1)) seem loose; no finite-sample rates provided.  
- Quality assessment: Generally sound but some assumptions (guaranteed exploration, complete-tree eventuality) should be explicitly quantified.  
- Clarity assessment: Well organized and readable, though notation is heavy in places.  
- Originality assessment: Incremental; sufficient condition is useful but proof ideas are standard.  
- Significance assessment: Moderate—likely informative for practitioners choosing selection policies, but missing UCT analysis limits practical reach.  
- Suggestion: Theorize or provide conditions/counterexamples explaining why sample-return propagation appears to converge.  
- Suggestion: Include UCT variants in experiments and discuss mitigation of prior negative results.  
- Suggestion: Tighten theoretical bounds or provide finite-time rates and analyze dependence on exploration γ.  
- Overall verdict: Technically interesting and well presented but incremental, with a notable theory–practice gap.
