This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. This class of games, which can be viewed as "stacked matrix games," includes well-known examples like Goofspiel. The central contribution of the work is a formal proof of convergence for a general template of MCTS algorithms. Specifically, the authors demonstrate that if the action selection mechanism within a node is an ε-Hannan consistent procedure with guaranteed exploration (such as variants of Regret Matching or Exp3), then the strategies derived from the MCTS search will converge to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively, first establishing convergence for single matrix games, then extending this to matrix games with bounded errors (to model the noisy value estimates from child nodes), and finally applying this framework to the entire game tree. The theoretical results are complemented by an empirical analysis on randomly generated and empirically-derived worst-case games, which confirms the convergence and compares different MCTS variants.

This work builds upon prior applications of MCTS to simultaneous-move games (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013), but moves significantly beyond by providing the first formal convergence guarantees, a crucial step in establishing the theoretical validity of these methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel theoretical contribution. While MCTS has been widely and successfully used in sequential games, its application to simultaneous-move games has largely been based on heuristics and empirical success. This paper fills a critical theoretical gap by formally proving that MCTS, when instantiated with regret-minimizing algorithms, is not just a powerful heuristic but a provably convergent method for finding approximate Nash equilibria. This is a significant result for both the MCTS and algorithmic game theory communities.

2.  **Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is high. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The inductive proof strategy—moving from simple matrix games to games with bounded error and then to the full extensive-form game—is elegant and sound. The definitions of ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration are precise and appropriate for the problem. The claims are well-supported by the provided proofs, and the empirical section, while secondary to the theory, provides good validation of the formal results.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of defining the problem setting, introducing the necessary game-theoretic background, and clearly presenting their SM-MCTS algorithm template. The flow of the formal analysis section is logical and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Insights into MCTS Dynamics:** The main weakness of the formal analysis is that it relies on a significant simplification: it assumes the entire game tree is built out and analyzes the convergence of the selection strategies within this fixed structure. While the authors correctly argue this is sufficient to prove *eventual* convergence, it sidesteps the core dynamic of MCTS, which is the process of incrementally growing the tree. Consequently, the proof provides limited insight into the algorithm's behavior at the inner nodes during the search process, which is arguably where the most complex interactions occur. The analysis feels more like a proof of convergence for repeated play in a fixed hierarchy of matrix games rather than a full analysis of the MCTS search algorithm itself.

2.  **Weakened Connection to Subgame Perfection Literature:** Although the main theorem claims convergence to a *subgame-perfect* ε-Nash equilibrium, the paper could strengthen its connection to the broader game theory literature on this topic. The discussion does not fully contextualize this result within the established theory of subgame perfection in games with simultaneous moves. For instance, citing and discussing foundational work on the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in such games (e.g., Harris, "On the Existence of Subgame-Perfect Equilibrium in Games of Perfect Information," Econometrica 1990) would provide valuable context and better ground the paper's contribution.

3.  **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation serves its purpose in validating the theory but is somewhat limited. The "worst-case" games are found via a brute-force search over a small class of games, which provides interesting examples but does not guarantee they are truly the most difficult instances. This is a minor weakness, however, as the paper's primary contribution is theoretical.

### Summary and Recommendation

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable and novel contribution to the field. It provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, placing a popular and practical algorithm on a solid theoretical foundation. The paper is technically sound and clearly written. While the analysis simplifies away some of the dynamics of the tree search, the core result is significant and likely to be influential. The weaknesses identified are primarily areas for improvement and future work rather than fatal flaws. I recommend acceptance.
