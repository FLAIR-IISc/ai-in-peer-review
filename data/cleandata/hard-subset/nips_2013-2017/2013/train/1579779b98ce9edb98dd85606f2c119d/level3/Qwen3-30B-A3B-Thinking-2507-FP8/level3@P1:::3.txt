

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for simultaneous-move games, a class of zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any no-regret selection method, and prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and satisfies exploration guarantees, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the game. This work extends the theoretical foundation of MCTS beyond sequential games (where convergence guarantees exist for algorithms like UCT) to the more challenging simultaneous-move setting, which has received less theoretical attention despite practical applications in games like Goofspiel and general game-playing systems.

The paper's main theoretical contribution is a rigorous proof of asymptotic convergence to subgame perfect ε-NE for zero-sum simultaneous-move games. The authors establish this through a layered proof strategy: first analyzing matrix games (leaf nodes), then repeated matrix games with bounded error, and finally composing these results for the full game tree. The proof leverages established results from online learning theory, particularly the properties of Hannan-consistent algorithms like regret matching and Exp3. The formal analysis is sound, well-structured, and addresses a clear theoretical gap in the literature. The empirical evaluation complements the theory by confirming convergence on randomly generated and worst-case games, with experiments comparing mean-value propagation (required for the theoretical analysis) against the more common sample-value propagation. The results show that while mean propagation slightly underperforms sample propagation in practice, both converge to approximate NE.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The mathematical presentation is precise, the proofs are well-motivated, and the experimental design is appropriate. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the complex theoretical arguments accessible. The authors appropriately contextualize their work against prior MCTS research (e.g., UCT in sequential games) and related game-theoretic literature. Minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., "leafs" instead of "leaves" in Figure 1, inconsistent use of (i,j) vs. (a1,a2), and "negated" instead of "inverted" on line 144) are easily fixable but do not detract from the paper's overall quality.

The primary limitation is the asymptotic nature of the convergence result, with no finite-time convergence rates provided. The authors acknowledge this limitation, noting that the rate depends on the specific regret minimization algorithm used. The reviewer is particularly interested in whether concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., O(√T) for Exp3) could yield explicit convergence rates, which would strengthen the paper's practical relevance. While the asymptotic result is theoretically important, the absence of rates makes it less immediately applicable for practitioners seeking to understand MCTS performance in specific time constraints. This limitation is compounded by the fact that MCTS convergence has already been established for sequential games (e.g., UCT), so the incremental contribution here is primarily theoretical.

The significance of the work lies in its theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which is valuable for algorithmic game theory. While the practical impact may be limited by the asymptotic nature of the result, the work provides a crucial stepping stone for future research on finite-time bounds and extensions to imperfect-information games. The empirical evidence that other MCTS variants (not covered by the proof) also converge to approximate NE suggests the theoretical framework may be more broadly applicable than currently proven.

Strengths:
- Solid theoretical contribution addressing a clear gap in the literature
- Rigorous proof of asymptotic convergence with careful handling of exploration requirements
- Well-executed empirical validation on both random and worst-case games
- Clear writing and good organization
- Appropriate contextualization against prior work

Weaknesses:
- Asymptotic convergence without finite-time rates limits practical applicability
- Incremental nature of the contribution compared to existing sequential-game MCTS work
- Minor notational inconsistencies that slightly affect readability

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games, though its practical impact is somewhat limited by the asymptotic nature of the convergence guarantee. The theoretical framework is sound and well-supported, and the empirical results confirm the theoretical claims. The authors have addressed a meaningful gap in the literature, and the work provides a strong foundation for future research on convergence rates and extensions to more complex game classes.

For acceptance considerations: The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a clear theoretical contribution to a relevant problem area. While the absence of convergence rates is a limitation, the asymptotic result is significant for algorithmic game theory. The work advances the state of the art by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which is a notable contribution to the field. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and is appropriate for the conference. The minor notational issues are easily fixable and do not affect the technical content.
