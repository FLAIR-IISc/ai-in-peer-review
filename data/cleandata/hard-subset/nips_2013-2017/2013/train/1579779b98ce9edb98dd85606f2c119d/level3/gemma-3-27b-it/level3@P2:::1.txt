This paper studies the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games and formally prove that, under the condition of using a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration guarantees, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The theoretical results are supported by empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods on both randomly generated and specifically designed worst-case games.

The work builds upon a growing body of research applying game theory to algorithmic problems, and particularly leverages the success of MCTS in domains like Go [2]. While MCTS has seen practical success in simultaneous-move games [10, 11, 12, 13], this paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of its behavior. The connection to Hannan consistency, a well-established concept in online learning [15, 16], provides a solid foundation for the convergence proof. The authors appropriately relate their work to existing algorithms for solving simultaneous-move games, such as backward induction [7, 8, 9], and highlight the limitations of those approaches that motivate the use of MCTS.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The problem addressed is well-defined and relevant to the NIPS community, bridging game theory and machine learning. The technical contribution – the convergence proof – is significant, providing a theoretical justification for the practical success of MCTS in this game class. The clarity of the presentation is excellent; the paper is well-organized, and the notation is consistently used. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating the convergence of the algorithm on different game instances and comparing the performance of different selection methods. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the use of approximate Nash equilibrium and the reliance on specific selection criteria.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The proof technique, relying on Hannan consistency and inductive reasoning, while sound, feels somewhat incremental. Hannan consistency and its implications have been extensively studied in the online learning literature, and its direct application to MCTS, though correct, doesn’t present a fundamentally novel proof strategy. A deeper discussion of the novelty of the proof within the context of MCTS convergence analysis would strengthen the paper. 

A significant omission is a discussion of Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) based selection policies (UCT) [14]. While the authors mention that UCB does not necessarily converge to a Nash equilibrium [17], they do not explore related UCB variants or compare their theoretical properties to the approaches presented in the paper. Given the widespread use of UCB in MCTS, a more comprehensive comparison, even if negative, would be valuable.

Another point of concern arises from the fact that the theoretical result specifically applies to propagating the *mean* reward value. However, the experimental results show that propagating the *current sample* value also leads to convergence. This discrepancy requires further explanation. Why does the theory not cover the empirically observed behavior? Is the empirical convergence faster or slower with current sample propagation? Addressing this inconsistency would significantly improve the paper.

Finally, while the paper demonstrates convergence, it does not provide tight bounds on the convergence rate. The rate of convergence is an important practical consideration, and further analysis in this area would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and correct theoretical analysis of MCTS convergence in a challenging game setting.
*   The work bridges a gap in the understanding of MCTS, providing a theoretical foundation for its practical success.
*   The experimental results validate the theoretical findings and provide further insights into the algorithm's behavior.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution, while solid, may lack significant novelty in terms of proof techniques.
*   The omission of a discussion of UCB-based algorithms is a noticeable weakness.
*   The discrepancy between theoretical guarantees (mean propagation) and experimental observations (current sample propagation) needs to be addressed.
*   The paper does not provide convergence rate analysis.

**Overall assessment:**

This is a technically sound and well-presented paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of MCTS. While the theoretical novelty is perhaps modest, the convergence proof is a significant result. The concerns regarding UCB and the propagation method discrepancy should be addressed, but do not fundamentally undermine the quality of the work. I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. It is a good paper, but not a groundbreaking one. Addressing the weaknesses could easily elevate it to a stronger acceptance.
