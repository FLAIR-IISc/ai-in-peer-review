This paper presents a formal analysis of the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated by various selection methods, and formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper starts by providing a clear and concise introduction to the problem, explaining the context and relevance of the research. The authors then present the necessary background and definitions, including the concept of -Hannan consistency, which is a crucial property for the convergence of the MCTS algorithm. The main contribution of the paper is the formal analysis of the MCTS algorithm, which is presented in a clear and well-structured manner.

The authors use an inductive proof technique, assuming optimal strategies at leaf nodes, to show that the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate NE. The proof is well-presented and easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the key steps and insights. The paper also includes empirical evaluations of the MCTS algorithm, which demonstrate its effectiveness in practice.

One of the major strengths of the paper is its clarity and readability. The authors have done an excellent job of presenting complex ideas and technical results in a clear and concise manner. The paper is well-organized, and the writing is engaging and easy to follow.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the major weaknesses is that the analysis fails to connect to subgame-perfect concepts and other game-theoretic tools. The authors could have strengthened their analysis by tying it to these concepts and citing relevant literature, such as Harris (1990) on the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games.

Another limitation is that the analysis provides limited provable insights about inner nodes of the game tree. The authors could have explored this aspect further, providing more detailed analysis and insights into the behavior of the MCTS algorithm at inner nodes.

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of game theory and MCTS. The formal analysis of the MCTS algorithm is a major achievement, and the paper provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

In terms of originality, the paper builds on prior work by Lanctot et al. (2013), but provides a more comprehensive and rigorous analysis of the MCTS algorithm. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, and their work is likely to have a lasting impact.

The significance of the paper is also high, as it provides a formal analysis of the MCTS algorithm in a important class of games.
