

# Review for "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as the selection method. They prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures exploration, then MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The paper evaluates this claim empirically using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated games and worst-case examples.

The paper builds on prior work by Lanctot et al. (2013), who studied similar problems but provided only preliminary analysis. The authors' formal analysis is a significant contribution to the field, as it provides the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games. The authors correctly identify that while backward induction can solve these games exactly, it requires domain knowledge and is computationally expensive for practical applications, making MCTS a more appealing approach.

The paper excels in clarity and organization. The mathematical definitions are precise, the proofs are well-structured, and the empirical evaluation is thorough. The authors present their main theorem (Theorem 4.11) with a clear inductive approach, starting from leaf nodes and working upward. The empirical results (Section 5) demonstrate that the MCTS variants with mean propagation converge as expected, with the RMM variant converging slightly slower than RM but still effectively.

The main strengths of the paper are:
1. The formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which fills a significant theoretical gap
2. The well-structured presentation of the mathematical analysis
3. The comprehensive empirical evaluation that supports the theoretical claims
4. The clear distinction from prior work, particularly Lanctot et al. (2013)

However, the paper has two significant weaknesses that need to be addressed:

First, the analysis fails to adequately connect to subgame perfect Nash equilibria, which is a critical concept for extensive-form games. The paper states that the convergence is to a "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium" (Theorem 4.11), but the proof doesn't properly establish this connection. The authors should explicitly relate their results to subgame perfection and cite relevant game theory literature (e.g., Harris, 1990) that establishes the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games.

Second, the paper provides limited provable insights about the inner nodes of the game tree. While the inductive proof assumes optimal strategies at leaf nodes, it doesn't sufficiently address how the error propagates through the tree structure. The bound in Theorem 4.11 (D² + δ) seems somewhat arbitrary, and the authors should provide more intuition about how the depth D affects the convergence rate.

The paper's significance is strong as it provides theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous-move games from an algorithmic game theory perspective. This has practical implications for applications in security domains and general game-playing, where MCTS variants are commonly used but lack theoretical guarantees.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and weaknesses of their approach. The clarity is excellent, with sufficient detail for reproduction. The originality is strong as this is the first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in this specific class of games.

The paper meets NIPS criteria as follows:
- Quality: High (sound technical contribution with theoretical and empirical validation)
- Clarity: High (well-written with clear organization)
- Originality: High (novel theoretical contribution to the field)
- Significance: High (provides theoretical foundation for practical MCTS applications)

Arguments for acceptance:
- First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move games
- Strong empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Clear, well-structured presentation
- Practical significance for game-theoretic applications

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited connection to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts
- Insufficient analysis of inner node error propagation
- Theorem 4.11's bound (D² + δ) lacks intuitive justification

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCTS in a practically important class of games. The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the two weaknesses noted above, particularly by properly connecting the analysis to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts. As it stands, it is a valuable contribution that would be of interest to the NIPS community, particularly those working on algorithmic game theory and reinforcement learning. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the theoretical connections to subgame perfect equilibrium.
