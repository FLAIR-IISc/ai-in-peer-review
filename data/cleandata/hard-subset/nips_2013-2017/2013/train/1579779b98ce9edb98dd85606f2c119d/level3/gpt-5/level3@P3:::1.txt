Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template in which the per-node selection rules are any no-regret (“ε-Hannan-consistent”) algorithms with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching (RM), Exp3). The main theoretical result shows that, if nodes back up mean returns, SM-MCTS with such selection rules converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE); the approximation error scales as O(ε D^2) in the depth D of the game tree. The proof reduces each node to a repeated matrix game, invokes standard no-regret-to-equilibrium arguments for repeated games (Hart & Mas-Colell; Blum & Mansour), and then composes bounded errors inductively up the tree. Empirically, the paper validates convergence on random and adversarially selected depth-2 games using RM and Exp3, comparing mean-backup (RMM, Exp3M) to the commonly used current-sample backup (RM, Exp3).

The work fills a gap between: (i) practical success of SM-MCTS variants in simultaneous-move games (e.g., Tron, general game playing), and (ii) lack of convergence guarantees for such settings, especially since UCB/UCT is known to fail to converge in simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al.). It complements exact/backward-induction methods with pruning (Saffidine et al.; Bosansky et al.) by providing asymptotic guarantees for simulation-based search without domain heuristics, and relates closely to the classical “no-regret implies (approximate) equilibrium” literature. While CFR and variants address imperfect-information EFGs, this paper focuses on perfect-information simultaneous moves, where the per-node objects are matrix games.

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and well-scoped setting (perfect information, simultaneous moves).
- A useful and general sufficient condition: any ε-Hannan consistent selection method with guaranteed exploration yields convergence to an approximate, subgame-perfect NE under mean backups.
- Clean, modular analysis: matrix games → matrix games with bounded error → inductive composition across tree depth.
- Empirical validation with two canonical no-regret algorithms (RM, Exp3) on random and adversarial game instances; consistent with theory and with informative observations (mean backups converge slightly slower).
- The paper is clearly written and well organized; definitions and assumptions are explicit.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Novelty of the proof technique: The theoretical core leverages well-known facts (no-regret → approximate NE in repeated games) plus an error-propagation induction across depth. This is technically sound but feels incremental. The new contribution is largely the composition argument and the specialization to SM-MCTS. To improve perceived novelty, the authors could sharpen the error analysis (e.g., reduce D^2 to D, or isolate constants), or provide finite-time bounds that connect regret rates to exploitability rates.
- UCB/UCT not analyzed or compared: UCT is the most widely used SM-MCTS selection rule; although prior work shows it can fail to converge, practitioners still deploy it. The paper opts to “not discuss UCB any further.” At minimum, an empirical comparison to UCT baselines (including known remedies such as explicit exploration mixing, or adversarial-bandit variants like Exp3.P) would make the empirical section more informative. A brief discussion of why UCT’s optimism-in-the-face-of-uncertainty fails in simultaneous-move settings, and whether simple fixes (e.g., independent action selection + joint estimation, or mixing with uniform exploration) can salvage convergence, would broaden impact.
- Backup rule mismatch: The convergence proof requires mean backups, yet the experiments show that the common “current sample” backup also appears to converge. The paper should explain this discrepancy. Is the sample-backup estimator asymptotically unbiased at each node under guaranteed exploration? Can one bound the additional bias/variance and extend the proof to sample backups (perhaps via martingale arguments or concentration)? Alternatively, a counterexample would clarify the necessity of mean backups.
- Loose/asymptotic guarantees: The O(ε D^2) bound (and factors like 4(c+1) in intermediate lemmas) are likely conservative. No finite-sample rates are provided, and empirical results require up to 10^6 iterations to reach the exploration-induced floor. Providing (even coarse) iteration–exploitability curves with confidence intervals, or bounds translating regret rates of RM/Exp3 to exploitability as a function of visits per node, would strengthen the contribution.
- Scope and generality: While the focus on perfect-information simultaneous moves is appropriate, the paper could better situate the result relative to CFR-style methods in imperfect-information games and to double-oracle/backward-induction methods in this domain, including a runtime/practicality discussion. A small head-to-head comparison on depth-limited instances (e.g., alpha-beta with SM pruning) would add context.
- Experimental details: Please report variability across seeds, sensitivity to exploration rate γ and learning-rate choices (e.g., η in Exp3), scaling with branching factor/depth, and wall-clock performance. Since forced exploration lower-bounds exploitability, plotting the theoretical floor and showing convergence to it is helpful (you do this in worst-case games; extending to random games would be useful).

Quality
- Technically sound with careful definitions; proofs appear correct and align with established online-learning theory.
- Empirical evaluation is consistent but modest in scope; additional baselines (esp. UCT variants) and sensitivity analyses would improve it.
- Authors are appropriately cautious about assumptions and limitations.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well structured. Minor suggestions: explicitly state the ε factor in Theorem 4.11 (typesetting seems to omit ε before D^2); summarize the error-propagation intuition near the theorem; clarify when empirical vs average strategies are evaluated, and unify notation in the experiments.

Originality
- Moderate. The sufficient condition and its instantiation to SM-MCTS is useful, but the underlying ingredients are standard. The work reads as a careful, principled consolidation rather than a fundamentally new technique.

Significance
- Useful for researchers/practitioners building SM-MCTS agents who want convergence guarantees and guidance on selection rules. The result is incremental rather than groundbreaking. Broader impact would increase with analysis of commonly used UCT variants and finite-time guidance.

Pros for acceptance
- First formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under broad and practical conditions (no-regret + exploration).
- Clear, correct, and general framework; subgame-perfect guarantee is appealing.
- Empirical validation across two canonical no-regret algorithms.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty of proof technique; bounds likely loose; no finite-time rates.
- Omits analysis/experiments with UCT/UCB variants despite their prevalence.
- Theory requires mean backups; mismatch with common practice left unexplained.
- Experiments are relatively small-scale with limited baselines and sensitivity analysis.

Questions for the authors
- Can you tighten the D^2 dependence, or identify conditions yielding O(D)?
- Can the analysis be extended to current-sample backups, or can you provide a counterexample showing why mean backups are necessary?
- How do your results change with annealed exploration (γt → 0) to avoid an exploration-induced exploitability floor?
- Could you include empirical comparisons to UCT (and simple fixes), and discuss why they do/don’t converge?
- Are finite-time exploitability bounds attainable by combining regret rates of RM/Exp3 with your error-propagation analysis?

Recommendation
Technically interesting and clearly presented, with a useful sufficient condition for convergence, but incremental and missing analysis of widely used UCT baselines. I lean toward weak accept if the community values principled consolidation in this niche; otherwise, borderline.
