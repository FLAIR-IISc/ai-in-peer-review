- Presents SM-MCTS, a general MCTS template for simultaneous-move, zero-sum, perfect-information games, instantiable with any ε-Hannan consistent selection rule that guarantees exploration.  
- Main theoretical contribution: formal proof that such algorithms asymptotically converge to a subgame-perfect ε‑Nash equilibrium.  
- This is the first known convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, extending prior results from purely sequential settings.  
- Proof technique: builds from single matrix games to repeated games with bounded error and inductively to the full extensive-form game—elegant and rigorous.  
- Empirical evaluation with Regret Matching and Exp3 supports theory and examines random and worst-case instances.  
- Strength: technically sound and addresses an important open theoretical question.  
- Strength: exceptionally clear writing and organization; definitions, lemmas, and Algorithm 1 are well presented.  
- Strength: significant foundation linking MCTS convergence to no-regret learning, useful for future algorithm design.  
- Weakness: result is purely asymptotic—no convergence rates are provided, limiting practical guidance on simulation requirements.  
- Question: can known regret bounds for RM/Exp3 (e.g., O(√T)) be leveraged to derive concrete exploitability convergence rates for SM-MCTS?  
- Weakness: contribution may be seen as an incremental synthesis of existing no-regret and MCTS ideas, despite nontrivial extension to the simultaneous-move case.  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a literature gap with the first formal convergence proof, provides a powerful general framework, and is well supported by experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: lack of convergence rates and perceived incremental novelty relative to established no-regret/MCTS theory.  
- Minor suggestions: explain why the proof requires propagating mean values vs. sample values; fix "leafs"→"leaves"; use "negated utilities" instead of "inverted utilities"; make joint-action notation `(i,j)` consistent in Algorithm 1.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper recommended for acceptance, with encouragement to address convergence-rate questions in the final version.
