This paper studies the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. This class of games, which includes examples like Goofspiel, presents a challenge for standard MCTS algorithms, as selection policies like UCB are known not to converge to a Nash Equilibrium (NE). The authors present a general template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for the local matrix games at each node. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration (properties held by algorithms like regret matching and Exp3), then the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate-NE of the full extensive-form game. This formal analysis is a significant step forward from prior work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013), which explored similar setups but provided more preliminary or empirical analysis. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments on randomly generated and empirically-derived worst-case games, comparing variants of regret matching and Exp3.

### **Evaluation**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The central claim—that an MCTS framework built on ε-Hannan consistent selection converges to an approximate NE—is well-supported by the provided formal analysis. The proof technique, proceeding by induction from the leaf nodes upwards and treating the values from subgames as payoffs with bounded error, is clever and effective. The authors are careful to outline their assumptions and definitions clearly. The empirical evaluation further corroborates the theoretical findings, confirming that the proposed algorithms do converge in practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of first introducing the necessary background on extensive-form games and MCTS, then defining their specific algorithm template, and finally building the theoretical argument step-by-step from simple matrix games to the full multi-stage setting. The notation is consistent and the formal claims are stated precisely. The experimental section is also easy to follow, with clear figures and descriptions of the setup.

**Originality:** The main contribution of this paper—the formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this class of games—is highly original. While MCTS has been applied to simultaneous move games before, this work provides the first theoretical guarantee that strategies derived from the search will converge to a game-theoretic equilibrium. This addresses a critical open question and places the use of these popular heuristic search algorithms on a much stronger theoretical footing.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. MCTS is a cornerstone algorithm in AI for games, and extending its theoretical guarantees to a broader and more complex class of games is a significant achievement. This work provides formal justification for using regret-minimization techniques within MCTS for simultaneous move games and opens the door for further theoretical analysis, such as deriving finite-time bounds or extending the results to games with imperfect information. Researchers and practitioners working on general game playing and multi-agent systems will find this result both interesting and useful.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Guarantee:** The paper provides the first formal proof of convergence for an MCTS algorithm to an approximate Nash Equilibrium in perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. This is a strong and important contribution.
2.  **Generality:** The proof is not tied to a single algorithm but applies to a general class of selection methods (any ε-Hannan consistent algorithm with guaranteed exploration), which makes the result broadly applicable.
3.  **Clarity and Soundness:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, and the theoretical argument is presented rigorously and is technically sound.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the convergence in practice and provide useful insights, such as the performance comparison between propagating mean values versus current sample values.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Connection to Subgame Perfection:** While Theorem 4.11 claims the resulting equilibrium is "subgame perfect," the analysis does not deeply engage with this core game-theoretic concept. The proof demonstrates convergence at each subgame via induction, which implies the property, but the paper would be stronger if it explicitly discussed the implications and connections. For instance, citing and relating the work to the literature on the existence of subgame-perfect equilibria in such games (e.g., Harris, 1990) would better situate the contribution.
2.  **Limited Insight into Inner Node Dynamics:** The proof technique treats the value of a subgame as a payoff from a matrix game with bounded error. While this is a valid and effective simplification for proving convergence, it offers limited insight into the strategic dynamics of how policies evolve at the inner nodes of the game tree.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   A novel, significant, and technically sound theoretical result addressing an important open problem.
*   The paper is very well-written, making a complex topic accessible.
*   The findings provide a solid theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms in a challenging domain.
*   The analysis is general and applies to a family of MCTS variants.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The connection to established game-theoretic concepts like subgame perfection could be stronger and better contextualized.
*   The analysis provides a "black-box" view of subgames that, while correct, somewhat limits deeper game-theoretic insight.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. The primary contribution is a novel and important convergence guarantee that advances our understanding of MCTS. The weaknesses are largely matters of framing and potential extensions rather than fundamental flaws. This is a high-quality paper that represents a clear contribution to the field.
