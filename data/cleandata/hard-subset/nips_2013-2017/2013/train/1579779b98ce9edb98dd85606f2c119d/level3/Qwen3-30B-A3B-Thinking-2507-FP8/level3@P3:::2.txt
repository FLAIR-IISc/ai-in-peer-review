

# Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, which includes notable examples like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors introduce a general MCTS template for these games and prove that when instantiated with an ε-Hannan consistent selection method (like regret matching or Exp3), the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The paper builds upon Lanctot et al. (2013), which explored similar setups but provided only preliminary analysis, and extends the theoretical understanding of MCTS in this specific game class.

The main contribution is a rigorous formal analysis demonstrating convergence to approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The proof technique is inductive, starting from the leaf nodes (where the game reduces to matrix games) and working upward through the game tree. The authors establish that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, the MCTS algorithm will eventually converge to an approximate equilibrium. They also provide empirical validation using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games, confirming their theoretical claims.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The mathematical definitions are precise, the proofs are carefully constructed, and the experimental results are well-presented. The authors effectively connect their work to the broader context of game theory and MCTS applications, citing relevant prior work such as the double-oracle method for imperfect information games.

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical contribution is significant as it provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
- The paper is technically sound, with well-supported claims through both rigorous proofs and empirical validation.
- The writing is exceptionally clear, making complex concepts accessible to readers.
- The connection to online learning (Hannan consistency) provides a valuable theoretical bridge.
- The experimental section thoughtfully examines both the theoretical requirements (mean propagation vs. sample values) and worst-case performance.

**Weaknesses:**
- The analysis provides limited provable insights about inner nodes of the game tree, as the inductive step relies on the assumption that leaf nodes have optimal strategies, but the error propagation through the tree is not fully characterized.
- The paper fails to explicitly connect its results to subgame perfect Nash equilibria (SPE), despite claiming convergence to "subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium." While the induction does establish subgame perfection, the paper could better leverage established game-theoretic concepts.
- The authors should cite Harris (1990), which proves the existence of SPE in simultaneous-move games, to strengthen their theoretical foundation.
- The formal analysis assumes complete tree building, which may not hold in practice where tree growth is limited by computational constraints.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the experimental results corroborate the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the focus on simultaneous-move games rather than general extensive-form games).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical definitions are clear, the proof structure is logical, and the experimental results are presented effectively. The authors provide sufficient detail for experts to reproduce their results.

**Originality:** This work makes a significant contribution by providing the first formal convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While prior work (Lanctot et al., 2013) explored similar settings, this paper offers a complete theoretical framework with rigorous proofs, distinguishing it from previous preliminary analyses.

**Significance:** The results have substantial significance for the field. They provide a theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous-move games, which is widely applied in practice (e.g., in Goofspiel, Tron, and general game-playing). The connection to Hannan consistency bridges game theory and online learning, opening potential for future work on more efficient algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move games
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear inductive proof technique
- Well-executed empirical validation on relevant game examples
- Clear writing and strong connection to established game theory concepts

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited connection to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts (though the induction implies it)
- Failure to cite Harris (1990) on existence of SPE in simultaneous-move games
- Some theoretical assumptions (complete tree building) may not hold in practical implementations

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a practically important class of games. The minor weaknesses are easily addressable in revision (particularly the connection to subgame perfect equilibrium), and the paper's strengths far outweigh its limitations. I strongly recommend acceptance.
