This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting, called SM-MCTS, which can be instantiated with any selection method that is ε-Hannan consistent in a single matrix game and guarantees exploration. The primary contribution is a formal proof that any such algorithm asymptotically converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. This work provides the first known convergence guarantee for MCTS in this class of games, extending theoretical understanding beyond the well-studied domain of purely sequential games where variants like UCT are known to converge. The theoretical analysis is supported by an empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated games and on empirically identified worst-case instances.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Quality and Originality:** The paper is technically sound and addresses an important and open theoretical question. While MCTS has been applied to simultaneous-move games in practice, a formal understanding of its convergence properties has been lacking. The authors provide the first proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium in this setting. The framework is general, showing that convergence is guaranteed for a broad class of MCTS algorithms based on the principle of no-regret learning (specifically, ε-Hannan consistency). The proof strategy, which builds from single matrix games to repeated games with bounded error and finally to the full extensive-form game via induction, is elegant and rigorous.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is clearly defined, and the theoretical development is presented in a logical and understandable progression. The definitions of ε-Hannan consistency and the related lemmas concerning repeated games with errors provide the necessary building blocks for the main theorem. Algorithm 1 clearly outlines the general SM-MCTS procedure, and the descriptions of the regret matching and Exp3 instantiations are sufficient for an expert to understand the implementation.

3.  **Significance:** This work provides a crucial theoretical justification for using MCTS-style algorithms in an important class of games beyond the sequential setting. By connecting MCTS convergence to the well-understood properties of no-regret online learning algorithms, it opens a principled path for designing and analyzing MCTS variants for more complex game structures. The empirical results are also valuable, as they confirm the theoretical convergence and provide practical insights, such as the observation that propagating the current sample value (common in practice) leads to slightly faster convergence than propagating the mean value (required for the proof).

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Lack of Convergence Rates:** The most significant limitation of the paper is that the convergence result is purely asymptotic. The main theorem guarantees that the algorithm will *eventually* converge to an ε-NE, but provides no information about the rate of convergence—that is, how the quality of the approximation improves as a function of the number of simulations. This limits the practical impact of the result, as a practitioner needs to know how many simulations are required to reach a desired level of accuracy.

    While proving rates for the general case of any ε-Hannan consistent algorithm might be difficult, the specific instantiations considered (Regret Matching and Exp3) have known regret bounds, typically on the order of O(√T) in matrix games. Can these specific regret rates be leveraged to derive a concrete convergence rate for the overall SM-MCTS algorithm? For example, could one show that the exploitability of the computed strategy decreases as O(T^-α) for some α > 0? Deriving such a rate, even for a specific algorithm like SM-MCTS with Exp3, would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution and impact.

2.  **Incremental Nature:** While the result is novel and important for this game class, the core idea of using no-regret algorithms to find equilibria in games is well-established. Similarly, MCTS convergence proofs in sequential games have been known for some time. The contribution here is the non-trivial synthesis of these ideas and the extension to the simultaneous-move setting, which involves carefully handling the error propagation up the game tree. This is a solid contribution, but its scope is arguably an incremental, albeit necessary, step forward.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides the first formal proof of MCTS convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for simultaneous-move games, filling a significant gap in the literature.
*   The general framework based on ε-Hannan consistency is powerful and provides a solid foundation for future work.
*   The paper is technically sound, very clearly written, and the experiments effectively support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The primary theoretical result is asymptotic and lacks convergence rates, which limits its practical significance.
*   The contribution could be viewed as an incremental extension of existing concepts from no-regret learning and MCTS theory.

**Minor Comments and Suggestions:**

*   The experiments show that propagating the mean value (RMM) converges slightly slower than propagating the current sample (RM). This is an interesting finding. It would be valuable to briefly discuss why the proof requires propagating the mean and what the technical barrier is to analyzing the more common sample-propagation approach.
*   In Figure 1's caption, "leafs" should be corrected to "leaves".
*   In the paragraph describing player 2's perspective (around line 144), it is stated that player 2 uses "inverted utilities." For a zero-sum game, the standard and clearer term would be "negated utilities."
*   There is a minor notational inconsistency in Algorithm 1. Line 10 uses `(i, j) ← Select(h)`, while other parts of the text use `a` to denote an action. Using `(i, j)` consistently for joint actions selected in a state `h` would improve clarity.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution. While the lack of convergence rates is a notable limitation, the foundational result of guaranteed asymptotic convergence is an important step forward for the MCTS community. I recommend acceptance, but strongly encourage the authors to address the question of convergence rates in their final version if possible, as this would significantly elevate the paper's impact.
