## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) applied to zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors establish a general MCTS template and prove its convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, given the use of a -Hannan consistent selection method combined with appropriate exploration. This work builds upon prior analysis (Lanctot et al., 2013), extending it with a more rigorous and complete theoretical treatment. 

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The definitions are precise, and the inductive proof technique, based on assuming optimal strategies at leaf nodes, is presented logically. The empirical evaluation, using regret matching and Exp3, provides valuable validation of the theoretical results, demonstrating convergence in both randomly generated and specifically constructed “worst-case” games. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a clear path for future research, specifically extending the analysis to imperfect-information games and tighter convergence bounds. 

A key strength lies in the formalization of MCTS within this specific game class and the resulting convergence guarantee. This is a significant contribution, providing a theoretical foundation for the practical success observed with MCTS in these games. The comparison to previous work highlights the advancement over preliminary analysis, solidifying the novelty of the contribution.

However, a significant weakness is the limited insight into the inner nodes of the game tree. While convergence is proven, the analysis doesn't fully leverage subgame perfect Nash equilibria or related game-theoretic tools to understand *how* the algorithm navigates and refines its strategy within the branching structure of the game. The lack of connection to subgame perfection feels like a missed opportunity. The proof relies heavily on the complete tree assumption and the impact of building the tree incrementally is not thoroughly addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contribution establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in a non-trivial class of games.
*   Clear, well-written paper with a logical presentation of the proof.
*   Empirical validation supporting the theoretical results.
*   Builds on prior work, providing a significant extension of existing analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Limited insight into the behavior of MCTS within the game tree's inner nodes.
*   Lack of connection to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts.

**Recommendation:** Accept. Despite the weakness regarding inner nodes, the paper presents a substantial and rigorously proven theoretical result that significantly advances our understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. Addressing the suggested citation (Harris, 1990) and tying the analysis to subgame-perfect concepts would further strengthen the work, but the current contribution is already of high quality and suitable for NIPS.
