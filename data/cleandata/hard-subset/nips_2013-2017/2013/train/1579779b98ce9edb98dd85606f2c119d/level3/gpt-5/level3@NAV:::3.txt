Summary and relation to prior work: The paper analyzes a broad class of Simultaneous-Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) algorithms that use essentially any no-regret selection method. The main contribution is an asymptotic proof that, in two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, SM-MCTS converges to an ε-Nash equilibrium (subgame perfect) provided the selection rule is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration. The analysis proceeds via repeated matrix games with bounded error and an inductive argument up the tree depth. Empirically, the paper compares regret matching and Exp3, and studies a practical design choice: propagating mean returns versus current sample values, finding that mean-value propagation (needed for the proof) slightly underperforms sample-value propagation in convergence speed. This work complements negative results for UCB in simultaneous-move settings and connects to online learning (RM, Exp3) and prior MCTS theory in sequential-move games, as well as exact game-solving via backward induction/alpha–beta for simultaneous moves.

Quality: The theory appears careful and technically sound; the “bounded error” reduction and depth-induction are standard but well executed. A limitation is that convergence is asymptotic: no finite-time rates are provided. Given ε-Hannan consistency alone this may be inevitable, but concrete regret-rate assumptions (e.g., O(√T) for Exp3/RM variants) might yield explicit exploitability rates; can the authors derive O(poly(D))/√T rates, and clarify how errors compound (linear vs quadratic in depth)? Also, with fixed exploration γ the empirical limit exploitability matches the γ-induced mixture; can guaranteed exploration be achieved with γt→0 (or implicit exploration) to remove this floor while preserving the proofs?

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized; proofs are sketched with details in the appendix. Some notation inconsistencies and minor typos (see Nitpicks).

Originality: Novel convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS with general no-regret selection. Conceptually incremental relative to known sequential-move MCTS analyses and no-regret-to-equilibrium folklore, but the simultaneous-move setting is nontrivial and previously open.

Significance: Useful theoretical grounding for widely used algorithms; impact is tempered by the absence of finite-time rates and modest experimental scope. Adding larger benchmarks (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel variants) and ablations would strengthen the case.

Pros for acceptance:
- First general asymptotic convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS with no-regret selection
- Clean, broadly applicable template connecting online learning and MCTS
- Careful proofs; informative experiments and design insights

Cons against acceptance:
- No finite-time convergence rates; unclear practical guidance
- Incremental w.r.t. known sequential-move results; limited experimental breadth
- Proof requires mean-value backup, which empirically slows convergence
