- Main contribution: Proves almost-sure convergence of SM-MCTS with Hannan-consistent selectors (e.g., regret matching, Exp3) to an approximate subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move perfect-information games.
- Proof strategy: (i) Hannan-consistent learners in one-shot matrix games, (ii) extend to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff error, (iii) inductively compose bounds up the game tree.
- Empirics: Experiments on random and adversarial small games show convergence for regret matching and Exp3 and explore mean vs. sample backup effects.
- Relation to prior work: Strengthens Lanctot et al. (2013) by providing formal convergence guarantees and situates results relative to negative UCT findings.
- Strength: Clear, well-written exposition with a clean SM-MCTS algorithmic template.
- Strength: New formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS using regret-minimizing selectors, yielding subgame-perfect approximate equilibria.
- Strength: Modular, principled proof technique linking matrix-game learning to tree induction.
- Strength: Practical relevance—validates a widely used approach and admits multiple selection rules.
- Weakness: Limited provable insight into inner-node behavior; analysis treats internal nodes via error propagation rather than structural properties.
- Weakness: Theoretical bounds are asymptotic and likely loose (e.g., D^2 dependence, large constants), with no finite-sample rates.
- Weakness: Subgame-perfection exposition is insular to the induction and underconnected to classical game-theoretic literature and refinements.
- Weakness: Experimental scope is modest (small depth/branching) and lacks comparisons to stronger baselines like CFR-style methods.
- Technical quality: Assumptions and reductions are clear; proofs match claims; necessity of mean backups is explicitly discussed.
- Clarity suggestions: Emphasize the subgame-perfect ε-NE result early, clarify role/necessity of mean vs. sample backups, and streamline notation for empirical vs. average strategies.
- Originality: Novel formal analysis for SM-MCTS and the simple but original template “MCTS + any Hannan-consistent selector.”
- Significance: Advances theoretical understanding of a commonly used heuristic in simultaneous-move games and has practical implications for algorithm design.
- Suggested improvements: Connect to subgame-perfect equilibrium literature (e.g., Harris 1990), discuss equilibrium selection and refinements.
- Suggested improvements: Provide at least high-level finite-time guidance or heuristics relating convergence to depth, branching, exploration, and selector regret rates.
- Suggested improvements: Expand experiments to larger games and compare to other regret-minimization tree algorithms (CFR variants).
- Suggested improvements: Discuss practical gap between mean and sample backups and when sample backups might suffice asymptotically.
- Pros for acceptance: First rigorous convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to approximate subgame-perfect NE, clear theory, and supportive experiments.
- Cons against acceptance: Asymptotic-only results with loose constants, limited inner-node structural insight, and missing connections to broader game-theoretic refinements.
- Overall recommendation: Strong and timely theoretical contribution; recommend accept (borderline-to-strong) with minor revisions to strengthen literature connections and finite-time discussion.
