## Review of “Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games”

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a class of games including Goofspiel and variants of pursuit-evasion games. The core contribution lies in proving that MCTS, when instantiated with -Hannan consistent selection methods like regret matching and Exp3, converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and the results are presented in a straightforward manner. 

The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader context of algorithmic game theory and highlight the growing interest in applications of game-theoretic models. They correctly identify the gap in theoretical understanding of MCTS performance outside of sequential, two-player perfect-information games, contrasting it with the success of MCTS in practice.  The introduction effectively motivates the need for a formal analysis, especially given the limitations of backward induction and the computational expense of domain knowledge requirements in those methods. The definitions and background section is thorough and provides the necessary foundation for understanding the subsequent technical contributions.

A significant strength of this paper is the rigorous formal analysis. The proof technique, employing induction and leveraging the properties of -Hannan consistency, appears sound. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of related concepts such as regret minimization and the connection to Nash equilibrium. The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, provides supporting evidence for the theoretical results, showing convergence behavior comparable to existing MCTS algorithms. The inclusion of experiments with both Regret Matching and Exp3 strengthens the generality of the findings.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. While the convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium is a valuable result, the analysis primarily focuses on the leaf nodes of the game tree, with limited provable insights into the convergence behavior of inner nodes. This reliance on assuming optimal strategies at the leaves, while enabling the inductive proof, feels somewhat limiting. It would be beneficial to explore methods for bounding the errors introduced by imperfect play in those inner nodes more directly. 

Furthermore, the connection to established game-theoretic concepts like subgame perfect Nash equilibria feels underdeveloped. While the authors mention subgame perfection, the analysis doesn't explicitly demonstrate convergence *to* a subgame perfect equilibrium.  Considering that simultaneous move games can be viewed as stacked matrix games, referencing existing work on subgame perfect equilibria in this context would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, the authors should consider citing Harris (1990), which demonstrates the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games, and discuss how their convergence guarantees relate to achieving such equilibria. 

Another minor point is the somewhat limited scope of the empirical evaluation. While the worst-case game analysis is helpful, expanding the empirical testing to a wider range of game instances and comparing performance against other state-of-the-art algorithms in more detail would further bolster the claims.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and theoretically sound analysis of MCTS convergence in a challenging game setting.
*   Clear and well-written presentation of a complex topic.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature regarding the theoretical understanding of MCTS.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Analysis is limited by its reliance on optimal strategies at leaf nodes.
*   The connection to subgame perfect Nash equilibria and related concepts could be stronger.
*   Empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some limitations, this paper represents a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The rigorous analysis and clear presentation make it a valuable addition to the NIPS conference.  The paper is of good quality, and the strengths outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised regarding subgame perfection and potential for expanding the empirical evaluation.
