This paper presents a significant contribution to the field of game theory and artificial intelligence, providing the first formal analysis of convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors prove that any ε-Hannan consistent algorithm can be used to create an MCTS algorithm that provably converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the game.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with a clear and concise presentation of the main ideas and results. The authors provide a thorough review of the relevant literature and clearly explain the context and significance of their contribution.

The main theoretical contribution of the paper is the proof of asymptotic convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium for simultaneous-move games. This result is significant because it provides a foundation for the use of MCTS algorithms in this class of games, which are common in many real-world applications.

The experiments presented in the paper provide additional insight into the empirical convergence rates of the algorithms and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The comparison between propagating mean reward values versus propagating current sample values is particularly interesting and provides valuable insights into the trade-offs between these two approaches.

One limitation of the paper is the lack of convergence rates, which may be inevitable under ε-Hannan consistency. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest potential avenues for future research.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of techniques from game theory and artificial intelligence, and the results are not a straightforward extension of previous work. The authors clearly explain how their contribution differs from previous research and provide a thorough discussion of the related work.

The significance of the paper is high, as it provides a foundation for the use of MCTS algorithms in a wide range of applications, including game-playing, security, and decision-making under uncertainty. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in these fields, and the paper has the potential to make a significant impact on the development of new algorithms and applications.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper for publication, subject to minor revisions to address some typos and notation issues.
