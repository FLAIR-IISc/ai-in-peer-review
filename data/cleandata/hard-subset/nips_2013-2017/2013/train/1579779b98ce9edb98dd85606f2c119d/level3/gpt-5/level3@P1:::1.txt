This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move extensive-form games (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which the within-node selection policy is any algorithm that is ε-Hannan consistent (HC) in matrix games and guarantees sufficient exploration. The main theoretical result shows that, under these conditions and with mean-value backups, SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium; the approximation error scales with the game depth (Theorem 4.11). The analysis proceeds by (i) standard regret arguments in repeated matrix games (Lemma 4.6), (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff error (Lemma 4.9, Theorem 4.10), and (iii) an induction over tree depth to aggregate error across levels. Empirically, the paper instantiates the template with Regret Matching and Exp3, evaluates on random and empirically hard games, and observes convergence of both mean-backup and sample-backup variants; mean backups converge slightly slower.

Relation to prior work: Prior MCTS successes (e.g., UCT) lack guarantees in simultaneous-move settings; Shafiei et al. previously observed UCB can fail to converge even in one-step simultaneous games. Exact backward induction with LP/double-oracle is possible but expensive. Regret-minimization (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) provides the HC building blocks. This paper’s novelty is to compose no-regret dynamics across the game tree under simultaneous moves and show eventual convergence of SM-MCTS to approximate NE.

Strengths
- Clear sufficient condition: any ε-HC selection rule with guaranteed exploration yields convergence to an approximate subgame-perfect NE in this class. This is practically useful and conceptually clean.
- Sound and modular analysis: the reduction to repeated games (with and without bounded error) and the depth-wise induction are transparent and easy to build upon.
- Empirical validation with two canonical HC algorithms (Regret Matching, Exp3), including stress tests on hard instances; consistent with theory.
- Clarity: definitions, algorithm template, and assumptions are well spelled out; proofs seem careful and reference standard results.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty of the proof technique: the main theoretical contribution feels incremental, relying on standard no-regret to equilibrium guarantees and a straightforward induction over tree depth. The final bound (quadratic in depth) seems loose; tighter constants or depth dependence would be valuable.
- Gap between theory and practice: the proofs require mean-value backups and a specific exploration scheme (A* that does not update on exploration), but experiments also show convergence with sample-value backups and on-policy exploration. The paper should explain why sample-backups empirically work (e.g., bounded-variance martingale arguments) and whether the proofs can be extended to cover them.
- Limited scope of empirical study: mostly random and small worst-case games; no comparisons to exact backward induction (LP/DO) where feasible, or to CFR-based methods for simultaneous-move games. Confidence intervals and computational cost are not reported.
- UCT/UCB discussion: since UCT is a de facto baseline, it would help to include either a negative result replicated under this setup or a discussion of what modifications (e.g., adversarial bandit variants) would be needed to recover guarantees.
- No finite-time rates: the paper proves eventual convergence but gives no sample complexity. Even loose high-probability bounds would strengthen the significance.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The results are carefully stated and appear correct, leveraging well-known HC properties. The assumption that the tree is eventually fully expanded and all joint actions are sampled infinitely often is crucial; the guaranteed exploration formalization addresses this, but the mismatch with common on-policy exploration warrants clarification.
- Empirics support the claims but are limited in breadth and reporting (no error bars; modest domains).

Clarity
- Generally well-written and organized. Suggestions: (i) unify the use of empirical vs average strategies earlier, in light of Lemma 4.5; (ii) explicitly state the δ quantification when presenting asymptotic equilibrium claims; (iii) emphasize the precise exploration mechanism used in experiments vs theory.

Originality
- The high-level idea of composing no-regret dynamics up a tree is not new, but adapting it to simultaneous-move perfect-information games with explicit convergence of MCTS is a useful and, to my knowledge, first formalization in this setting.

Significance
- Provides the first general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate NE under widely used HC selection rules. Practical impact is tempered by the need for mean backups, lack of rates, and exclusion of UCT, but the result is still valuable for guiding algorithm design.

Suggestions for improvement
- Theoretical: (i) close the gap between mean and sample-value backups (e.g., via concentration bounds controlling backup bias/variance); (ii) sharpen the dependence on depth; (iii) discuss whether off-policy exploration (A*) is essential or artifacts of the proof.
- Empirical: (i) include exact baselines where possible and CFR-based methods; (ii) report error bars and runtime; (iii) evaluate on named simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel variants) and deeper trees; (iv) analyze sensitivity to γ and selection parameters; (v) separate the effect of uniform exploration by reporting exploitability after stripping forced exploration from the output strategies.
- UCT: add experiments illustrating the known failure modes, and discuss whether adversarial-bandit UCT variants (e.g., Exp3-based trees) recover guarantees consistent with this theory.

Arguments for acceptance
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate NE under broad, practical conditions.
- Clear, correct, and modular analysis; results likely to be cited and extended.
- Empirical confirmation across two standard HC selection rules.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental theoretical novelty and loose bounds; no finite-time rates.
- Theory–practice mismatch (mean backups vs sample backups) not resolved.
- Limited empirical breadth and lack of comparison to strong baselines; no UCT analysis.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a clean, useful sufficient condition and a solid theoretical foundation for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move games, with reasonable empirical support, despite the incremental nature and missing finite-time analysis.
