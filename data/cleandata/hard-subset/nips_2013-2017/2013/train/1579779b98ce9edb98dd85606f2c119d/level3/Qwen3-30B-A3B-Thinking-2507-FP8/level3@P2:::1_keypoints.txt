- Addresses an important theoretical gap: convergence of MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.  
- Proposes a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with different selection methods.  
- Proves that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games and satisfies exploration, MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium.  
- Empirically validates theory using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games.  
- Provides the first formal convergence guarantees for this class of games, linking online-learning Hannan consistency to MCTS convergence.  
- Technical quality is solid: clear, well-structured proofs using induction from matrix games to multi-stage games.  
- Empirical results support the main claim but lack finite-time convergence rates and broader comparisons with other MCTS variants.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with effective use of definitions, lemmas, theorems, and visualizations.  
- Needs a better explanation for why mean propagation (required by theory) performs slightly worse than sample-value propagation in practice.  
- Originality is moderate: application of known online-learning results to a new context rather than novel proof techniques.  
- Does not analyze widely used UCT/UCB methods, limiting practical relevance given existing negative results for UCB in some simultaneous-move games.  
- Significance is meaningful but limited: useful sufficient condition for practitioners but not a major leap forward and not shown superior in convergence speed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a theoretical gap, provides sound analysis, clear writing, and supporting experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: contribution is incremental, uses standard techniques, omits popular MCTS variants, and leaves a theory–practice discrepancy unexplained.  
- Overall assessment: solid paper recommended for weak acceptance; should address UCT variants and the mean vs. sample propagation discrepancy to strengthen impact.
