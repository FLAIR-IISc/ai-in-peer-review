## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves – a challenging setting where theoretical guarantees are lacking. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, under the condition of using a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The results are empirically validated on randomly generated and worst-case games, demonstrating convergence even with selection methods beyond those explicitly covered by the proof. 

The paper is well-written and clearly presents a complex topic. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches like backward induction and the practical success but theoretical gaps of MCTS. The definitions are precise, and the presentation of the algorithm and proof outline is logical. Relating the work to existing literature, particularly in the context of regret minimization and game theory, is done effectively, citing relevant papers like those by Hart & Mas-Colell and Auer et al. The empirical evaluation provides reasonable support for the theoretical claims, and the inclusion of worst-case game analysis is commendable.

However, while the work is solid, its originality feels incremental. The proof technique, relying on -Hannan consistency, appears to be a fairly standard approach in online learning and regret minimization, and its application to this specific MCTS context, while valuable, doesn’t represent a significant theoretical leap. A key weakness is the lack of analysis of widely used UCB/UCT algorithms. Given the existing analyses of UCB in related settings, a discussion of why these algorithms are not covered by the theoretical framework and a comparison to their empirical performance would strengthen the paper. 

Furthermore, the theoretical result focuses on propagation of the mean, while the experiments demonstrate that propagating the current sample value also works. The authors acknowledge this discrepancy but don’t provide a satisfying explanation. A deeper investigation into why the empirical results deviate from the theoretical guarantees is needed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a valuable, formally proven sufficient condition for convergence of MCTS in a challenging game setting.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Empirical validation supports the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Theoretical contribution lacks significant novelty.
*   Missing analysis and comparison to UCB/UCT algorithms.
*   Discrepancy between theoretical guarantees and empirical results regarding mean vs. sample value propagation needs further explanation.



**Overall Assessment:** Technically sound and a worthwhile contribution, but not outstanding. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points above, particularly the discussion of UCB variants and the explanation for the empirical success of sample value propagation.
