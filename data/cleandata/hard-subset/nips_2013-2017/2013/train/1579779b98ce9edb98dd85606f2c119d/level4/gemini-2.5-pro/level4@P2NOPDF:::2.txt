This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for the class of zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games, a category that includes interesting examples like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors integrate regret-matching into the MCTS framework and provide a proof demonstrating that this approach converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition and well-presented results. Its primary contribution lies in providing a new formal analysis for regret-matching within MCTS for this specific class of games. This work significantly extends the more preliminary analysis found in prior research, such as Lanctot et al. (2013) [20]. The inductive proof, which establishes convergence by reasoning from optimal strategies at the leaf nodes up through the game tree, is a notable technical achievement.

The main weakness of the paper, in its current form, is the disconnect between its computational analysis and established concepts in game theory, specifically the notion of Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria (SPNE). The current proof demonstrates convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for the overall game, treating the tree as a whole and building up from the leaves. However, this approach does not characterize the properties of the learned strategies within the internal nodes of the game tree.

An analysis of SPNE would be particularly relevant for this class of games, as it provides a stronger solution concept that ensures rational play at every decision point, not just at the outset. While the current results are interesting from a computational standpoint, connecting the convergence properties of MCTS with regret-matching to the attainment of an approximate SPNE would substantially elevate the paper's contribution. Such a connection would bridge the gap between the computational AI literature and the extensive work on this topic by game theorists. The authors may find foundational work on this topic, such as the following, to be a useful reference:

*   Harris, C. (1990). "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves".

In summary, this paper presents a valuable computational result, but its impact could be significantly enhanced by situating the analysis within the broader context of game-theoretic solution concepts like SPNE. Addressing this would strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation and broaden its appeal to both the machine learning and game theory communities.
