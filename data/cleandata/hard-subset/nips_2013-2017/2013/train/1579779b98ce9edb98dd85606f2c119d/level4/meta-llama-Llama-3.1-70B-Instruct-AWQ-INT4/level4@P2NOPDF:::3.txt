The authors present an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using essentially any no-regret selection method, with some mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash in the case of simultaneous moves.

One weakness of the paper is that the convergence is asymptotic, with no rates provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis, as the selection method is assumed to be only epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this may not be detrimental, as there are no known proofs of convergence for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of rates is important. Given that backwards induction can provide an exact equilibrium, it would be useful to know the rates attained when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method. For example, using the \sqrt{T} guaranteed by exp3, do the authors know what rates are achieved?

The authors conclude with some experiments comparing the approach of propagating mean reward values used in their analysis to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. The authors also attempt to experimentally derive some insight into the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper that is technically correct. However, I have a mild reservation regarding the ultimate significance of the work given the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step. On the other hand, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, making this result feel somewhat incremental.

Some minor corrections are needed:

- Figure 1: "leafs" should be "leaves".
- Line 144: "negated" is the correct term, not "inverted".
- Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation should be (i,j) or (\sigma1,\sigma2), rather than (a1,a2), to avoid confusion with the previously introduced notation a_{i,j} for payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
- Line 172: The notation seems to switch from (i,j) to a's, which may continue throughout the paper. These notation inconsistencies should be edited.

Overall, this was a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading, but I have some reservations about its impact due to the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
