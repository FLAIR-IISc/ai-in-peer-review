- Paper analyzes SM-MCTS for two-player zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games and proposes a general SM-MCTS template using ε-Hannan-consistent selection with guaranteed exploration.  
- Main theoretical result: with mean-value backups, SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect ε-approximate Nash equilibrium; error scales as O(D^2 ε) plus arbitrarily small δ.  
- Proof approach: (i) convergence in matrix games with ε-Hannan-consistent learners, (ii) extension to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors, (iii) inductive lifting over the game tree.  
- Authors instantiate the template with regret matching and Exp3 and empirically compare mean-value vs. sample backups and investigate hard instances.  

- Exact backward-induction solutions exist but can be expensive (LP/double-oracle) and require evaluators.  
- UCT lacks convergence guarantees for simultaneous moves and can fail even in simple cases.  
- Prior convergence analyses covered sequential-move games; this work fills the gap for simultaneous moves.  

- Clear, general template covering broad no-regret selection methods (e.g., regret matching, Exp3).  
- Formal subgame-perfect convergence guarantees to approximate Nash equilibria in simultaneous-move games.  
- Principled, modular proof strategy isolating matrix-game learning and composing errors across depths.  
- Experiments validate asymptotic claims and compare mean vs. sample backups.  

- Only asymptotic convergence is provided; no finite-time rates or sample complexity are given.  
- Analysis requires mean-value backups while practice often uses current-sample backups; theoretical justification for sample backups is missing.  
- Depth dependence O(D^2 ε) may be conservative; tighter bounds might be possible.  
- Open question: translate standard regret rates (e.g., O(√T)) into finite-time exploitability rates at the root accounting for exploration bias and error propagation.  
- Practical guidance lacking on choosing exploration parameter γ or adaptive schemes compatible with the proof.  

- Theoretical results appear technically sound and the empirical evaluation aligns with the claims.  
- Generally well written and organized, but recurring notation inconsistencies (e.g., (i,j) vs. aij) and algorithm notation confusion.  
- Original contribution: first convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS under broad no-regret selection, meaningfully extending sequential-move results.  
- Significance: provides theoretical backing for SM-MCTS in practice, though impact is tempered by missing finite-time bounds.
