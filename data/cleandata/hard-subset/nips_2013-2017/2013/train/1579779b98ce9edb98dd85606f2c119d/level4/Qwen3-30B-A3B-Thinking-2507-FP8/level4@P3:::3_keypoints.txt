- Main contribution: defines a general MCTS template for simultaneous-move perfect-information games that uses any epsilon-Hannan consistent selection method and proves convergence to a subgame-perfect epsilon-Nash equilibrium.
- Novelty: first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, filling a gap beyond prior sequential-game (UCT) results.
- Selection methods: framework applies to algorithms like regret matching or Exp3, provided they include guaranteed exploration.
- Theoretical result: asymptotic convergence to approximate equilibria is rigorously proven using careful definitions and technical proofs.
- Relation to literature: builds on regret-minimization results (e.g., Hart & Mas-Colell, Auer et al.) and contrasts with exact but costly backward induction.
- Empirical validation: experiments confirm theoretical claims, compare mean-propagation vs current-sample propagation, and identify empirically challenging games.
- Empirical finding: mean propagation converges slightly slower in practice, but both propagation methods reach approximate Nash equilibria.
- Limitation: only asymptotic convergence is shown—no finite-time or convergence-rate bounds are provided.
- Practical impact concern: lack of convergence rates limits practical significance since exact backward-induction solutions exist and rates may be slow.
- Notational issues: inconsistencies (e.g., joint-action notation (i,j) vs (a1,a2) vs a_ij) could confuse readers and should be fixed.
- Significance assessment: contribution is important theoretically but somewhat incremental given existing MCTS work in sequential games and practical use of MCTS for these games.
- Recommendation: accept—technically sound and novel foundation; future work should target finite-time convergence bounds to strengthen practical relevance.
