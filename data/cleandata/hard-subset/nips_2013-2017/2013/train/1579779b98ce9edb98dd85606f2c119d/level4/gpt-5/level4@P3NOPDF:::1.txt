Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It establishes sufficient conditions under which MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium and provides empirical results consistent with the theory. The topic is timely and relevant, the main claims are clearly stated, and the presentation is generally clear.

Strengths
- Provides a formal convergence result for MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information settings, a less commonly analyzed regime.
- States a clear sufficient condition for convergence and supports it with empirical evidence.
- Writing and organization are clear, making the contributions accessible.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty of the proof technique: Since the central contribution is theoretical, the paper should more clearly articulate how its proof techniques differ from, or improve upon, prior analyses of MCTS/UCT or related learning dynamics in games. As written, it is difficult to assess the originality beyond the setting considered.
- UCB/UCT omission: UCB-style selection (e.g., UCT) is the de facto standard in MCTS and has received prior theoretical scrutiny. The current paper does not analyze UCB algorithms, nor does it compare them empirically. This limits the practical relevance and makes it harder to position the contribution within the existing literature.
- Backup/propagation rule gap: The theory establishes convergence when propagating means, yet the experiments show that propagating the current sample value also performs well under several criteria. This mismatch calls for explanation—are there conditions under which sample-value backups are expected to behave well (e.g., due to reduced bias or particular noise structures), or is the good performance incidental to the chosen domains and parameters?
- Scope and realism of assumptions: The “certain conditions” needed for convergence should be made explicit and discussed in terms of practicality. Which explore/exploit schedules, independence assumptions, or regularity conditions are required? Do they hold in typical simultaneous-move games used in practice? How sensitive are the results to violations?
- Strength of guarantees: Are there finite-sample bounds or convergence rates, and how do they scale with game depth, branching factor, or payoff ranges? If only asymptotic results are available, clarifying the constants and dependencies in the asymptotics would help practitioners interpret the results.
- Empirical evaluation details: More specifics on domains, baselines, parameter tuning, and statistical robustness (e.g., error bars, number of seeds) would strengthen the empirical claims and their alignment with the theory.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify novelty and positioning: Include a dedicated related-work comparison that precisely situates the result relative to prior analyses of MCTS/UCT in trees and to learning dynamics in simultaneous-move games (e.g., how your assumptions, proof ingredients, and guarantees differ). If the proof technique draws from known tools, highlight what is genuinely new (e.g., handling of simultaneity, backup dynamics, or selection policies).
- Address UCB/UCT: Either (i) extend the analysis to cover UCB-style selection, or (ii) provide a clear rationale for why the current proof framework does not directly extend to UCB, identifying the technical obstacles. In either case, add empirical comparisons with well-tuned UCB/UCT baselines to contextualize performance and convergence behavior.
- Explain the backup discrepancy: Provide intuition or partial analysis for why propagating current sample values can still perform well empirically, and delineate when it might fail. A small theoretical lemma, a counterexample, or a bias–variance discussion comparing mean vs. sample backups would be valuable. Alternatively, broaden experiments to show regimes where sample-value backups underperform, to align practice with theory.
- Make assumptions explicit: List all convergence assumptions upfront (exploration schedules, tie-breaking, payoff boundedness, independence/mixing assumptions, etc.), discuss their necessity, and indicate which are artifacts of analysis versus intrinsic to the problem. Include guidance on how to set hyperparameters to satisfy the conditions in practice.
- Tighten the empirical section: Add standard benchmarks, error bars over multiple runs, ablations on backup rules and exploration parameters, and comparisons against UCB/UCT. Report computational costs and sensitivity to hyperparameters to support practical takeaways.
- Improve theorem statements: If possible, include rates or at least qualitative dependence on problem parameters. Clarify the metric for “approximate Nash equilibrium” (e.g., exploitability) and how it is estimated in experiments to ensure consistency between theory and practice.

Minor comments
- Define “propagation of the mean” and “propagation of current sample value” precisely when first introduced, and keep the terminology consistent throughout.
- Ensure all acronyms (MCTS, UCB, UCT) are defined on first use and that notation is summarized in a short table or appendix.
- Add citations to prior analyses of UCT and to relevant work on simultaneous-move game search to better frame the contribution.
- Improve figure readability (axis labels, font sizes) and include confidence intervals.
- Consider releasing code or pseudocode to aid reproducibility.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important problem and provides a useful sufficient condition for convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information games. The results are interesting and the writing is clear. However, the theoretical novelty is not fully convincing as presented, the omission of UCB/UCT analysis and comparisons weakens the contribution’s practical impact, and the gap between the proven backup rule and the empirically effective one is insufficiently explained. With stronger positioning, a fuller treatment of UCB/UCT, and a clearer explanation of the backup-rule behavior, the paper would be significantly strengthened. In its current form, I view it as interesting but technically incremental.
