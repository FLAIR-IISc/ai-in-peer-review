The authors present a convergence analysis for a general class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms. The key feature of their framework is its compatibility with a wide range of selection methods, requiring only that they satisfy the no-regret property (specifically, epsilon-Hannan consistency), with some mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium for extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A key limitation of the analysis is that the convergence is purely asymptotic; no convergence rates are provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' general assumption about the selection method. While this is a significant weakness, it is not necessarily a fatal flaw. To my knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, making it a novel contribution.

However, the question of convergence rates is critical for understanding the practical utility of this approach, especially when compared to methods like backward induction that can compute an exact equilibrium (albeit often intractably). Could the authors comment on what convergence rates might be achievable if a specific selection method with a known regret rate, such as one with O(sqrt(T)) regret, were incorporated into their analysis?

The paper concludes with an empirical evaluation. The authors compare the performance of propagating mean reward values (the method analyzed in the paper) against the standard approach of propagating the most recent sample value. Interestingly, the experiments show that using mean values, while necessary for the theoretical analysis, results in slightly worse empirical performance. Finally, they use experiments to gain some insight into the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a well-written and technically correct paper. My main reservation concerns the ultimate impact of the work, given the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods are known to perform well empirically, so establishing their asymptotic convergence in this more general setting is a valuable first step. On the other hand, since convergence results for MCTS in sequential-move games are already established, this extension to simultaneous-move games feels somewhat incremental without a characterization of the convergence rate. Despite these reservations, I enjoyed reading the paper.
