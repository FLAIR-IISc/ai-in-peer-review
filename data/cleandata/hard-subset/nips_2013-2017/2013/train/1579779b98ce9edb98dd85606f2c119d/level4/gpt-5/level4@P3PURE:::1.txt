This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It proves that, under certain conditions, MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium, and the empirical results support this theoretical claim. The presentation is clear, and the findings are interesting. However, given that the main contribution is theoretical, I have concerns about the novelty and strength of the proof techniques, and overall the paper is not technically outstanding.

Detailed comments:
1. The paper provides a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS, which is useful and clearly presented. However, since the theoretical results are positioned as the central contribution, I am concerned about the novelty of the proof approach. Please clarify what aspects of the analysis are genuinely new relative to prior work, and highlight the key technical ideas that enable your convergence result.

2. UCB-type algorithms are widely used in the literature, yet they are not analyzed here. Given that there has already been analysis of UCB for Trees, it would strengthen the paper to include UCB-based variants in your theoretical and empirical comparisons, or to explain clearly why they are excluded and how your results relate to that line of work.

3. The theory establishes good convergence for the propagation of the mean, but the experiments show that propagating the current sample value also performs well under several criteria. Please provide an explanation for this empirical behavior, and, if possible, discuss whether your analysis could be extended (or why it cannot) to cover the sample-value propagation scheme.

In summary, the paper addresses an interesting problem and presents useful sufficient conditions for MCTS convergence with supportive experiments. Nonetheless, the technical novelty appears limited, and important algorithmic baselines (notably UCB variants) are missing from the analysis and comparison.
