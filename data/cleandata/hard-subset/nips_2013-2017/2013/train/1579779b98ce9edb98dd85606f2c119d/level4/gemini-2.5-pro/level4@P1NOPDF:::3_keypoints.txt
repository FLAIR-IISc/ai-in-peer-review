- Presents a convergence analysis for a broad class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms.  
- Framework requires only no-regret (epsilon-Hannan consistency) selection methods with mild restrictions, making it widely compatible.  
- Main theoretical result: proof of asymptotic convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.  
- Major limitation: convergence is purely asymptotic — no convergence rates are provided.  
- Lack of rates stems from the generality of the selection-method assumptions.  
- Novelty: appears to be the first convergence proof for MCTS in extensive-form simultaneous-move games.  
- Open question posed: what convergence rates could be derived if a selection method with known regret (e.g., O(√T)) is used?  
- Empirical comparison between propagating mean reward values (theoretically analyzed) and propagating most recent sample values.  
- Empirical finding: mean-value propagation (needed for theory) yields slightly worse performance than recent-sample propagation.  
- Experiments offer some insight into convergence behavior but do not fully characterize rates.  
- Overall assessment: well-written and technically correct, but impact is limited by absence of rate characterization and feels incremental over existing sequential-move results.  
- Nitpicks: typographical and notational issues — "leafs"→"leaves", prefer "negated" over "inverted", inconsistent action notation (a1,a2 vs i,j vs σ1,σ2) and other notational inconsistencies.
