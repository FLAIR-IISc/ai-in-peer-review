This paper analyzes the class of zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games, which includes interesting examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors present a Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm that incorporates regret-matching and prove its convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for this class of games.

The paper is clearly written, and its results are well-presented. The main contribution is a novel formal analysis that builds upon prior work (e.g., Lanctot et al. [20]), which explored a similar setup but with more preliminary results. The authors' proof of convergence is inductive, proceeding from the assumption of optimal strategies at the leaf nodes of the game tree.

However, the primary weakness of this approach is that the analysis provides limited guarantees regarding the strategies at the inner nodes of the game tree. Consequently, the paper misses an opportunity to connect with the powerful game-theoretic concept of Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria (SPNE). The analysis could be significantly strengthened by relating the properties of the algorithm's learned strategies to subgame perfection, a topic well-studied by game theorists. For exploring this connection, the authors may find the following work relevant:

C. Harris (1990). "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves".

In summary, while this paper presents a novel and interesting analysis of MCTS with regret-matching from a computational standpoint, its contribution would be enhanced by tying the analysis to the powerful tools of Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria for this class of games.
