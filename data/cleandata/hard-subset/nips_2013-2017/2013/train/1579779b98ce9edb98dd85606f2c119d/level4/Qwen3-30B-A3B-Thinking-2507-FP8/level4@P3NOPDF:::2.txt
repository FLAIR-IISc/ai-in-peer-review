

This paper investigates zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, including notable examples like Goofspiel and Tron, and analyzes the application of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with regret-matching to converge to approximate Nash equilibria. The work presents a technically sound inductive proof, building from optimal strategies at leaf nodes, and offers a clear, well-structured exposition of its results. The contribution is valuable for formalizing regret-matching within this specific game class, extending prior preliminary analysis by Lanctot et al. (2013). The writing is precise, and the results are effectively communicated, making the paper accessible to the NIPS audience.

However, the analysis exhibits a significant limitation in scope: it focuses exclusively on terminal nodes and fails to leverage the recursive structure of subgames, thereby missing a critical connection to subgame perfect Nash equilibrium (SPE), a cornerstone concept in game theory for sequential decision-making. While the paper establishes convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, it does not address how this relates to the stronger solution concept of SPE, which is particularly relevant for games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. As Harris (1990) demonstrated, SPE is well-defined and existent in such settings, providing a richer analytical framework for understanding equilibrium behavior across the entire game tree. 

The current proof strategy—centered on leaf-node optimality—does not naturally extend to inner nodes or subgame recursion. This omission is consequential because SPE requires equilibrium strategies to be optimal in every subgame, not merely at the root. For instance, in Tron or Goofspiel, optimal play in a subgame (e.g., a specific board configuration) should inform the equilibrium strategy for the broader game, but the paper’s approach does not capture this dependency. Consequently, the analysis remains computationally oriented without grounding its results in established game-theoretic tools that could deepen the interpretation of the convergence guarantees.

The authors should explicitly engage with SPE to strengthen their contribution. A revised analysis could: (1) formalize how regret-matching strategies induce SPE when applied recursively, (2) compare the approximate equilibrium found via MCTS to SPE in terms of stability and strategic depth, and (3) cite Harris (1990) as a foundational reference for SPE existence in simultaneous-move games with perfect information. This would position the work not just as a computational advance, but as a bridge between algorithmic methods and core game-theoretic principles. 

While the paper’s technical results are valid and well-presented, this gap prevents it from fully realizing its potential impact. The connection to SPE is not merely academic—it would clarify why the convergence results matter beyond computational efficiency, aligning the work with decades of game-theoretic scholarship. For example, Harris (1990) proved that SPE exists for such games, and integrating this perspective would allow the authors to discuss whether their regret-matching MCTS approach converges to SPE or a weaker equilibrium, thereby answering a more substantive theoretical question. 

In summary, the paper makes a solid contribution to the computational analysis of specific game classes, but its theoretical depth is constrained by the absence of SPE. With a focused revision to incorporate this established framework, the work would offer a more compelling synthesis of algorithmic and game-theoretic insights. As a reviewer, I recommend acceptance pending this critical enhancement. The suggested integration of Harris (1990) and SPE analysis would significantly elevate the paper’s significance for both the machine learning and game theory communities.
