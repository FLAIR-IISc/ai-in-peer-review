

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing selection method for matrix games. Their main theoretical contribution is a proof demonstrating that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and satisfies exploration requirements, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The authors empirically validate their theoretical results using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games, confirming convergence to approximate Nash equilibria.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a class of games where such guarantees were previously unavailable. This addresses an important gap in the literature, as MCTS variants have been widely used in practice for such games without theoretical justification. The technical content is sound, with well-structured proofs that build from matrix game analysis to extensive-form games through induction. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical results by showing that the mean-propagation variant (required for the theoretical analysis) converges slightly slower than the standard current-sample propagation, though both methods converge to approximate Nash equilibria.

The primary limitation of the work is that the convergence is asymptotic without explicit rates. As the authors note, this is a necessary consequence of their analysis, which only assumes ε-Hannan consistency without specifying the regret rate. While this is not inherently problematic (since no prior convergence proofs exist for MCTS in this domain), it raises questions about the practical relevance of the result. Specifically, the authors do not address whether known regret rates for specific selection methods (e.g., the O(√T) rate for Exp3) would translate to meaningful convergence rates for the MCTS algorithm. This is particularly relevant since backward induction can compute exact equilibria in polynomial time, making the practical advantage of MCTS unclear without rate information.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the contribution. While convergence guarantees for sequential-move MCTS (e.g., UCT) have been established, this paper extends such guarantees to simultaneous-move games, which is an important but not groundbreaking extension. The paper would be stronger with a more compelling discussion of why these theoretical guarantees matter for practical applications, especially given that MCTS is already used successfully in many domains without such guarantees.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, with clear exposition of the mathematical framework and algorithms. I have a few minor suggestions for improvement: Figure 1 should say "leaves" instead of "leafs"; the term "negated" would be more precise than "inverted" on line 144; and the notation in Line 10 of Algorithm 1 (using (a1,a2) instead of (i,j)) is confusing given the paper's existing notation for payoffs as a_{i,j}. These are minor issues that could be easily addressed in revision.

In summary, this is a technically strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While the asymptotic nature of the convergence result limits its practical impact, it represents an important first step toward formal justification of MCTS methods in this domain. The work would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the convergence result and by addressing the question of convergence rates that arise from specific regret-minimizing selection methods.

Strengths:
- First formal analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move games
- Technically sound proofs with clear theoretical framework
- Well-executed empirical evaluation supporting the theoretical claims
- Clear writing and organization

Weaknesses:
- Asymptotic convergence without explicit rates limits practical relevance
- Significance could be better justified given existing MCTS work on sequential games
- Limited discussion of practical implications for real-world applications

Overall, I believe this paper is suitable for publication at NIPS. While the contribution is incremental rather than transformative, it provides important theoretical grounding for a widely used algorithmic approach in game theory. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games, and their work provides a foundation for future research on convergence rates and practical applications.
