Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that uses a selection method at each node based on a regret-minimizing procedure for the corresponding matrix game. The main theoretical result shows that if the selection method is ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) and guarantees exploration (so each joint action is tried infinitely often), then SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. Specifically, when using mean-backups, the average strategies converge to a subgame-perfect (ε·D^2 + δ)-NE, where D is the game-tree depth and δ > 0 is arbitrary. The analysis proceeds by (i) establishing properties of HC dynamics in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending these to repeated matrix games with bounded evaluation error, and (iii) inducting over depth to handle stacked (simultaneous-move) matrix games.

Empirically, the paper evaluates regret matching (RM) and Exp3 as selection methods on random games and small worst-case instances. Both RM and Exp3 converge empirically; using mean-backups converges slightly more slowly than using current-sample backups. The experiments also identify “hard” games (depth 2, branching factor 2) where convergence is slow; in these, the observed exploitability floor matches the effect of the uniform exploration rate γ.

Relation to prior work: Exact algorithms for simultaneous-move games based on backward induction exist (with LP or double oracle at cutoffs), but they can be impractical without domain heuristics. UCT is widely used but is known not to converge in simultaneous-move settings (e.g., Shafiei et al.). To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence analysis of an MCTS-style method for simultaneous-move perfect-information games under regret-minimizing selection.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and a clean algorithmic template tying SM-MCTS to online learning in matrix games.
- A general convergence theorem covering any ε-HC selection method with guaranteed exploration, yielding subgame-perfect approximate equilibria.
- Sensible empirical evaluation with two standard HC algorithms (RM and Exp3), random and adversarial instances, and discussion of practical backup choices (mean vs current sample).
- The paper is clearly written overall, with well-defined notation and a helpful decomposition of the analysis (matrix games → bounded-error → stacked games).

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty of proof technique: The main ingredients—HC implies approachability to NE in matrix games, handling bounded evaluation error, and an induction over depth—are standard ideas combined in a straightforward way. The contribution is solid but not particularly surprising; more emphasis on what is technically new versus known would help.
- UCB/UCT not analyzed: UCB-type selection is ubiquitous in MCTS. While prior work shows UCT can fail to converge in simultaneous-move games, the paper omits any theoretical or empirical comparison with UCB-based variants. Given existing analyses of UCT in trees (albeit for sequential moves), a discussion or negative result for UCB in this setting, or at least an empirical comparison, would strengthen the paper.
- Backup rule gap: The convergence proof requires mean-backups, but experiments show that current-sample backups also behave well and often converge faster. Can the authors explain why current-sample backups appear to work (e.g., martingale arguments, diminishing bias under guaranteed exploration), or provide a counterexample where they fail? Even a partial rationale or conditions under which they are safe would be valuable.
- Bound quality and rates: The asymptotic guarantee scales as O(D^2 ε) and provides no finite-time rates. This limits practical guidance. Any discussion of how the error accumulates, opportunities to tighten the D^2 factor, or empirical convergence-rate estimates as functions of D, branching factor, and γ would improve significance.
- Experimental scope: Worst-case enumeration is limited to depth-2, BF-2 games, and random games use small sizes. Including a larger benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel instances mentioned in the paper) or reporting convergence versus time (not just iterations) would make the empirical section more convincing.
- Exploration/exploitability: The experiments note the exploitability floor induced by uniform exploration γ. A brief analysis of this trade-off (e.g., decaying γ, or using exploration that preserves HC while reducing asymptotic exploitability) would be helpful.

Quality
- The theoretical results are sound and carefully structured. Some proofs are deferred to the appendix, which is acceptable. The empirical methodology is reasonable but somewhat limited in scope.

Clarity
- The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Minor suggestions: clearly separate the two backup variants in Algorithm 1; explicitly state where mean-backups are required in the proofs; and summarize all assumptions (HC, exploration, mean-backups) before the main theorem.

Originality
- Moderate. The setting (simultaneous moves) and the conclusion (convergent SM-MCTS under HC selection) are novel for MCTS, but the proof components largely adapt known ideas.

Significance
- The result provides the first formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS under broad conditions, justifying the use of regret-minimizing selection in practice. However, the lack of finite-time bounds, the D^2 dependence, and the omission of UCB comparisons limit immediate impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss the novelty relative to known HC-to-NE results and prior MCTS analyses more explicitly.
- Include a theoretical or empirical treatment of UCB/UCT variants in simultaneous-move games.
- Provide intuition or partial theory for why current-sample backups work in practice, or present a counterexample.
- Explore decaying exploration schedules to reduce the exploitability floor while maintaining exploration guarantees.
- Expand experiments to larger domains or established benchmarks.

Arguments for acceptance
- First formal convergence result for SM-MCTS under broad and practical selection rules (RM, Exp3).
- Clear presentation and a useful unifying template linking MCTS to online learning.
- Empirical results consistent with theory and informative about practical choices.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical novelty in techniques and loose asymptotic bound (O(D^2 ε)) without rates.
- Lack of analysis or comparison with UCB/UCT, despite their prevalence.
- Modest experimental scope.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and clearly written paper with an interesting and useful convergence result for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While I find the contribution worthwhile, the technical novelty is moderate and the empirical section is limited. If space permits addressing the UCB question and clarifying the backup rule behavior, the paper would be significantly stronger.
