Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (stacked matrix games). It introduces a general SM-MCTS template whose selection policy can be any Hannan-consistent (HC) algorithm with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching and Exp3). The central theoretical result shows that, under these assumptions and when mean values are backpropagated, SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE); the approximation error scales with game depth. The analysis proceeds by (i) characterizing convergence in repeated matrix games with HC learners, (ii) extending to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff noise due to exploration and sampling, and (iii) inductively composing error bounds across tree depth to obtain subgame-perfect guarantees.

This connects to several lines of work. Backward induction with alpha–beta pruning for simultaneous-move games (Saffidine et al.; Bosansky et al.) provides exact solutions but is computationally heavy and requires evaluation functions/cutoffs. The empirical success of UCT in many domains is tempered by negative results for simultaneous moves (Shafiei et al.). The paper’s guarantees rely on online learning theory: Hannan consistency (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.), convergence of no-regret play to NE in zero-sum games (e.g., Blum & Mansour), and specific algorithms (regret matching, Exp3). Lanctot et al. (2013) previously explored SM-MCTS empirically; this work provides the first rigorous convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move perfect-information games within an MCTS framework. The subgame-perfect nature of the limit strategies is explicitly addressed and aligns with classical game-theoretic notions; citing the existence results for subgame-perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move settings (e.g., Harris, 1990) could further situate the contribution.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The assumptions are clearly stated (HC selection, guaranteed exploration, eventual full-tree expansion). The reduction from SM-MCTS nodes to repeated (noisy) matrix games is well motivated. Lemmas on HC play in repeated games, their noisy extensions, and the induction across depth culminate in Theorem 4.11, which asserts almost-sure convergence to subgame-perfect ε-NE with an error scaling as O(D^2). The requirement to backpropagate mean returns (rather than single-sample returns) is crucial to the proofs and is clearly called out.
- Empirical support: Experiments compare mean vs sample return propagation and assess convergence on random games and enumerated depth-2 worst cases. They validate the asymptotic claims qualitatively and show comparable convergence for regret matching and Exp3 variants, with mean-propagation slightly slower.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The abstract and introduction motivate the setting and limitations of prior work. The algorithm template and selection/update rules are described precisely, though the RetVal choice (current sample vs mean) could be emphasized earlier as a central modeling decision; a short subsection explicitly stating which variant is covered by theorems would help. The distinction between empirical frequencies and average strategies is handled carefully; Lemma 4.5 justifies interchangeability asymptotically. A brief intuitive sketch of the induction that yields the O(D^2) dependence would aid readability.

Originality:
To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence analysis of MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, for a broad class of selection policies (any HC algorithm with exploration). The composition of no-regret learning guarantees with MCTS over stacked matrix games, including handling exploration-induced noise via repeated games with bounded error, is novel.

Significance:
The work advances the theoretical foundations of SM-MCTS, a practically relevant algorithmic family for domains with simultaneous moves (e.g., Tron, Goofspiel, pursuit-evasion, general game playing). The subgame-perfect guarantee is particularly meaningful for sequential games. The results are asymptotic and place conditions (mean backpropagation, exploration) that differ from standard practice, so further work is needed to bridge the gap to widely used variants and to provide finite-time rates.

Strengths:
- First rigorous convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS in this setting; convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad HC selection rules.
- Clean reduction from tree search to repeated (noisy) matrix games; clear, modular proof structure.
- Empirical validation that complements theory and covers multiple HC algorithms (regret matching, Exp3).
- Clear writing and useful contextualization with prior algorithmic game theory and MCTS literature.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumptions vs practice: The proof requires backpropagating mean returns, whereas standard MCTS typically backpropagates single samples. The experiments suggest both variants converge empirically, but theory for the standard variant is missing. Suggest extending the analysis (or providing counterexamples) for sample-return backpropagation.
- Rates and constants: The guarantees are asymptotic with potentially loose depth dependence (O(D^2)) and no finite-time bounds. Providing high-probability convergence rates (in terms of iterations, depth, branching factor, and exploration γ) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
- Exploration mixing: The need for guaranteed exploration (and the A* construction) inflates the effective regret and sets a floor on achievable exploitability. An analysis of exploration schedules (e.g., decaying γ) and their impact on both theory and practice would be valuable.
- Empirical scope: Experiments focus on small random games and enumerated worst cases at depth 2. Including larger structured benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron variants) and reporting exploitability over both players would better demonstrate practical impact.
- Exposition: Consider (i) elevating the subgame-perfect aspect of Theorem 4.11 in the introduction, (ii) summarizing all assumptions immediately before the main theorem, and (iii) clarifying early that “average strategies” and “empirical frequencies” become equivalent asymptotically. A brief discussion linking your subgame-perfect guarantee to game-theoretic existence results (e.g., Harris, 1990) would further ground the contribution.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and general theoretical guarantee for an important class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games.
- Subgame-perfect convergence result is nontrivial and relevant.
- Solid, careful presentation with empirical corroboration.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Gap between the theoretical variant (mean backpropagation, fixed exploration) and standard SM-MCTS practice; no finite-time/sample complexity bounds.
- Limited empirical scale and domains.

Overall recommendation:
Lean accept. The paper offers a clear, substantive theoretical contribution that will interest both the MCTS and computational game theory communities. Addressing the practical gap (or clarifying its limits) and expanding empirical validation would further strengthen the work.
