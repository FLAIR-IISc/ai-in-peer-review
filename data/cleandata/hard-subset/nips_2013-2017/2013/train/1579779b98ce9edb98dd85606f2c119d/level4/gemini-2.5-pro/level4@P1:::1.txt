This paper studies the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with different action selection methods. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration (e.g., Regret Matching or Exp3), the resulting SM-MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. This formal result is supported by an empirical study on randomly generated and worst-case games, which confirms the convergence of the proposed methods.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an important and previously open theoretical question: the convergence of MCTS in games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS has been successfully applied to these games in practice, a formal guarantee of convergence to a Nash equilibrium was lacking. Providing such a proof is a significant contribution.
*   The work is well-structured and clearly presented. The theoretical analysis is developed systematically, starting from repeated matrix games and building up to the full extensive-form setting with an inductive proof.
*   The generalization of the proof to any ε-Hannan consistent algorithm with guaranteed exploration provides a useful framework for designing and analyzing future MCTS variants for this game class.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**
1.  **Novelty of the Proof Technique:** The primary contribution of this work is theoretical. While the convergence result is valuable, the proof seems to be constructed by applying established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency, regret minimization) to the recursive structure of the game tree. The core ideas, such as analyzing repeated games with bounded error and using induction over the tree depth, are sound but may not be entirely novel in themselves. Could the authors comment on the specific technical novelty of their proof techniques, beyond the application to this MCTS setting? For instance, does the analysis in Section 4.3 introduce new tools for handling bounded errors in repeated games that could be of independent interest?

2.  **Exclusion of UCB-based Algorithms:** The paper briefly mentions and then dismisses UCB-based selection policies, citing prior work showing they fail to converge to a Nash equilibrium. While this is a valid reason for focusing on regret-minimizing procedures, UCB and its variants are arguably the most popular and empirically successful methods in many MCTS applications. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion on this point. For example, could the authors elaborate on *why* the theoretical framework presented here does not apply to UCB? What specific properties of UCB-style exploration cause the convergence proof to fail? Including UCB-based methods in the empirical comparison, even if they are not expected to converge theoretically, would also provide a valuable baseline and better contextualize the performance of the proposed algorithms.

3.  **Discrepancy Between Theory and Empirical Results:** The formal analysis requires that nodes propagate the *mean* of all rewards from their children's subtrees (the RMM and Exp3M variants) to ensure the error in the value estimates is bounded. However, the experiments show that the more standard MCTS approach of propagating the single, current sample value also converges robustly and, in some cases, slightly faster. This is an interesting and practically relevant finding. The paper would be more complete if it offered some intuition or discussion as to why this standard update rule performs so well, despite not being covered by the formal proof. Could the theoretical analysis potentially be extended to also cover the propagation of single sample values?

**Summary:**
This paper provides the first formal proof of convergence for a general class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous-move games, addressing an important gap in the literature. The result is useful, and the presentation is clear. However, the overall technical contribution feels somewhat incremental, relying on a careful application of existing theoretical tools. The paper could be improved by clarifying the technical novelty of the proof, expanding the discussion to better situate the work relative to popular UCB-based methods, and providing insight into the strong empirical performance of standard MCTS variants not covered by the theory. Overall, this is a solid paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of MCTS.
