The authors analyze a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithms that, with mild restrictions, can use any no-regret selection method. Their main theoretical result is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in games with simultaneous moves.

A key limitation is that the convergence guarantee is purely asymptotic—there are no rates. This seems unavoidable under their assumptions, since the selection method is only required to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this is not necessarily a serious drawback—especially given that, to my knowledge, there are no existing convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves—it does leave open an important question.

In particular, rates matter because backward induction already yields an exact equilibrium. Can the authors characterize convergence rates when the selection method has a known regret rate—e.g., when it achieves O(sqrt(T)) regret as in exponential-weights methods?

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values (as in the analysis) against the standard approach of propagating the most recent sample value. The mean-based variant performs slightly worse empirically. The authors also attempt to infer convergence rates empirically.

Overall, this is a clear and technically sound paper. I have a mild concern about the ultimate significance given the absence of convergence rates. On the one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step. On the other, convergence results already exist for MCTS with sequential moves, which makes the contribution feel somewhat incremental.
