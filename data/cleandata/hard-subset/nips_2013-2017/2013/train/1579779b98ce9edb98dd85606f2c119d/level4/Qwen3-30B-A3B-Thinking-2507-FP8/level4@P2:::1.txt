

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games and formally prove that when the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games with sufficient exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. They empirically validate this claim using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games, confirming both the theoretical convergence and the practical effectiveness of their approach.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games, which is particularly significant given that MCTS variants are widely used in practice for games with simultaneous moves (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron, Urban Rivals). Previous work has established convergence guarantees for MCTS in sequential games but not in games with simultaneous moves, where the theoretical foundation has been lacking. The authors effectively connect their work to established results in online learning (Hannan consistency) and game theory (Nash equilibrium), demonstrating how existing techniques from these fields can be adapted to provide theoretical guarantees for MCTS in this broader class of games.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution: The paper provides the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, filling an important theoretical gap. The proof technique, which extends Hannan consistency to multi-stage extensive-form games through induction, is sound and well-executed.

2. Empirical validation: The experiments on randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios provide convincing empirical support for the theoretical claims. The comparison between propagating mean values versus current sample values (Figure 2) offers valuable practical insights.

3. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow from simple matrix games to complex extensive-form games, and detailed mathematical proofs. The presentation of algorithms (Algorithm 1, 3.1, 3.2) is precise and accessible.

4. Significance: The result provides theoretical justification for using MCTS in practical applications involving simultaneous-move games, which are common in security domains and general game-playing competitions. The paper's findings are directly applicable to real-world implementations.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited scope of empirical analysis: The paper does not address UCB (Upper Confidence Bound), which is the most widely used selection strategy in MCTS for sequential games (and has been applied to simultaneous-move games in prior work). The authors note that UCB does not converge to Nash equilibrium in simple simultaneous-move games (citing Shafiei et al.), but they could have provided a more thorough comparison between UCB and the Hannan-consistent approaches they analyze.

2. Theoretical scope: While the proof covers the convergence to approximate Nash equilibrium, the paper does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it would be valuable to include some discussion of the limitations of their current bounds.

3. Experimental design: The worst-case analysis (Section 5.2) identifies games with high exploitability but does not provide a systematic explanation for why these games are particularly challenging. A deeper analysis of the structural properties of these difficult games would enhance the empirical contribution.

Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:

Pro:
- First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, addressing a significant theoretical gap
- Well-executed theoretical analysis connecting online learning and game theory
- Empirical results strongly support theoretical claims
- Clear, well-structured presentation that advances understanding of MCTS in a practically important class of games
- Theoretical framework provides justification for existing practical MCTS implementations

Con:
- Does not analyze UCB, which is the most commonly used MCTS selection strategy in practice
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds, limiting the practical applicability of the theoretical contribution
- The paper's scope is limited to zero-sum games with perfect information, while many practical applications involve imperfect information or non-zero-sum games
- The experimental comparison of propagation methods (mean vs. current sample) lacks a thorough explanation for the observed differences

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that provides valuable insights into the convergence properties of MCTS in a practically relevant class of games. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and provides sound theoretical analysis supported by empirical evidence. While the authors could have addressed UCB more thoroughly and provided finite-time bounds, these are reasonable limitations that do not undermine the significance of their core contribution. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory and MCTS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points about UCB and finite-time bounds.
