This paper provides a theoretical analysis for a general class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms that employ a no-regret selection method at each node, subject to some minor constraints. The central theoretical result is a proof demonstrating that these algorithms converge to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A notable limitation is the asymptotic nature of the convergence proof, which does not provide explicit rates. This is an inherent consequence of the analytical framework, as it relies on the general assumption that the selection method is merely epsilon-Hannan consistent. However, this is not a major flaw, as to the best of my knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for MCTS in this particular game setting. Nevertheless, the question of rates remains important, particularly since other methods like backward induction can compute an exact equilibrium. Could the authors comment on whether specific rates can be derived if a concrete regret bound is assumed for the selection module? For instance, what would the convergence rate be if one were to use a method with a known O(\sqrt{T}) regret guarantee?

The paper includes an experimental section that compares the propagation of mean reward values (as used in the theoretical analysis) against the more conventional approach of propagating single sample values. The results indicate that using mean values leads to slightly weaker empirical performance. Further experiments aim to provide empirical insights into the convergence rate of the proposed algorithms.

In summary, the paper is well-written and the technical claims appear to be correct. My primary reservation concerns the work's overall impact, which is tempered by the absence of convergence rates in the theoretical results. On one hand, establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step and a solid theoretical foundation for MCTS methods, which are known for their strong empirical performance. On the other hand, since convergence proofs for MCTS in sequential-move games already exist, this extension to simultaneous moves could be viewed as an incremental contribution.
