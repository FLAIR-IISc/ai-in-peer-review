This paper presents a formal analysis of the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with various selection methods and prove that if the selection method is -Hannan consistent and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper is well-written, and the results are clearly presented. The main contribution is the new formal analysis, which builds upon previous work in this area. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the MCTS algorithm, including a detailed explanation of the selection and update policies. The use of regret-matching as a selection strategy is particularly interesting, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how this strategy is used in the MCTS algorithm.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to provide a formal guarantee of convergence to an approximate NE. This is a significant contribution, as previous work in this area has been largely empirical. The authors' use of -Hannan consistency as a measure of the algorithm's performance is also a strength, as it provides a clear and well-defined metric for evaluating the algorithm's convergence.

The paper also includes an empirical evaluation of the MCTS algorithm, which provides further evidence of its effectiveness. The authors evaluate the algorithm on a set of randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games, and the results show that the algorithm converges to an approximate NE in all cases.

However, the paper could be improved by providing a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's performance in terms of subgame perfect Nash equilibria. While the authors mention this concept in the introduction, they do not provide a thorough analysis of how the MCTS algorithm performs in this regard. This is a significant omission, as subgame perfect Nash equilibria are a key concept in game theory, and a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's performance in this area would be valuable.

In terms of the quality of the paper, the writing is clear and concise, and the results are well-presented. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the MCTS algorithm and its components, and the empirical evaluation is well-designed and clearly presented. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's performance in terms of subgame perfect Nash equilibria.

Overall, I would recommend this paper for publication, but with the suggestion that the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's performance in terms of subgame perfect Nash equilibria.
