The authors present an analysis for a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using essentially any no-regret selection method, with some mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash in the case of simultaneous moves.

One weakness of the paper is that the convergence is asymptotic, with no rates provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis, as the selection method is assumed to be only epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this may not be detrimental, given the lack of existing proofs of convergence for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of rates is important. Specifically, we know that backwards induction will yield an exact equilibrium anyway. It would be interesting to know what rates are attained when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method. For example, using the \sqrt{T} guaranteed by exp3, do the authors know what rates can be achieved?

The authors conclude with some experiments that compare the approach of propagating mean reward values, used in their analysis, to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. Additionally, they attempt to derive some insight into the convergence rate experimentally.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper that is technically correct. However, I have a mild reservation regarding the ultimate significance of the work, given the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step, even if rates do not follow from the authors' analysis. On the other hand, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, making this result feel somewhat incremental.

Minor corrections and suggestions:

* Figure 1: "leafs" should be "leaves".
* Line 144: "negated" is the correct term, rather than "inverted".
* Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1,a2) should be (i,j), or possibly (\sigma1,\sigma2), to avoid confusion with the previously introduced notation a_{i,j} for payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
* Line 172: There appears to be a notation inconsistency, switching from (i,j) to a's. This should be edited for clarity and consistency throughout the paper.

Overall, this was a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading, despite having some reservations about its impact due to the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
