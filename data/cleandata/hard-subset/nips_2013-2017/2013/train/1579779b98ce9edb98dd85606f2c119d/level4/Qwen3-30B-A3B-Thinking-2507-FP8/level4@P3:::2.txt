

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as a selection function. They prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The authors empirically validate this claim using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated games and worst-case examples.

The paper builds on important prior work in the field. Lanctot et al. (2013) previously examined MCTS in simultaneous move games but provided only preliminary analysis, while Shafiei et al. (2013) demonstrated that UCT fails to converge to Nash equilibrium in simple simultaneous move games. The authors correctly cite these works and position their contribution as the first formal convergence analysis for MCTS in this specific game class. Their approach extends the well-established connection between regret minimization and Nash equilibrium in matrix games to the more complex setting of extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

The strengths of this paper are substantial. The formal analysis is technically sound, with a clear inductive proof structure that carefully handles the error propagation through the game tree. The authors properly address the challenge of bounded error in repeated matrix games (Lemma 4.9) and successfully establish the convergence to subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium (Theorem 4.11). The empirical evaluation is thorough, including both random games and worst-case analysis, with appropriate metrics (exploitability) and careful comparison between different variants of the algorithm (RMM vs. RM). The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and provides sufficient detail for replication.

The paper also makes an important contribution to the understanding of MCTS by demonstrating that the convergence guarantee depends critically on the selection function's Hannan consistency and exploration properties. This provides theoretical justification for the practical use of MCTS in this class of games, which has been employed in applications like Goofspiel and Tron.

However, the paper has some weaknesses that merit consideration. The convergence guarantee is asymptotic, with no finite-time bounds provided, limiting its immediate practical utility. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not adequately address how the theoretical guarantee translates to practical performance. The assumption that the complete tree is built before convergence (Section 4) is somewhat unrealistic for practical implementations where tree growth is limited by computational resources.

The paper also misses an opportunity to more deeply connect with game theory concepts. While it mentions subgame perfect equilibria in the title and abstract, the analysis could have better leveraged established results on subgame perfection in simultaneous move games. For instance, the 1990 paper by Harris on "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves" would have provided valuable theoretical grounding for the authors' approach to subgame perfectness in their analysis. The connection to this body of work would have strengthened the theoretical contribution.

The significance of the paper is moderate. While the theoretical analysis is novel and valuable for algorithmic game theory, the practical impact is somewhat limited to the specific class of games considered (zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games). The paper does not address how these results might extend to imperfect information games, which represent a much broader class of practical applications.

In terms of the NIPS criteria:
- Quality: Strong technical contributions with well-supported claims, though the asymptotic nature of the convergence result is a limitation
- Clarity: Excellent organization, clear definitions, and well-structured arguments
- Originality: First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games, though the core technique is an extension of existing regret minimization theory
- Significance: Provides theoretical justification for MCTS in this game class, with limited practical impact due to the specific game structure

Pro-acceptance arguments:
- First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games
- Rigorous theoretical contribution with clear inductive proof structure
- Thorough empirical evaluation with both random and worst-case games
- Well-written with clear organization and sufficient detail for replication

Con-acceptance arguments:
- Asymptotic convergence guarantee without finite-time bounds
- Limited practical impact due to focus on a specific game class
- Underdeveloped connection to established game theory concepts of subgame perfection
- Assumption of complete tree building before convergence is unrealistic

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution that advances our understanding of MCTS in a specific but important class of games. The paper's strength lies in its rigorous formal analysis, which fills a significant gap in the literature. While the practical implications are somewhat limited by the specific game class and asymptotic nature of the results, the theoretical contribution is valuable for the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the connection to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts and to better articulate the limitations of the asymptotic convergence guarantee.
