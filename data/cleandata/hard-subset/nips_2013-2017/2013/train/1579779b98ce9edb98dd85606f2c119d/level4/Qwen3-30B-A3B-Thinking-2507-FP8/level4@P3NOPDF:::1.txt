

This paper investigates Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, establishing conditions under which MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The theoretical analysis is supported by empirical validation, demonstrating alignment between the formal results and practical performance. While the work presents a clear and well-structured contribution to the literature, its technical novelty and completeness warrant careful consideration.  

**Strengths:** The paper successfully identifies a sufficient condition for MCTS convergence in the specified game class, filling a gap in the analysis of MCTS for simultaneous-move games. The presentation is logically organized, with the theoretical results clearly articulated and the empirical evaluation well-designed to corroborate the claims. The focus on zero-sum games with perfect information—a relevant and challenging domain—adds practical significance to the findings.  

**Key Concerns:**  
1. **Limited Novelty of Proof Technique:** The theoretical contribution hinges on proving convergence under specific conditions, but the proof technique appears to rely on established methods (e.g., martingale convergence arguments adapted from prior MCTS analyses). The authors do not sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing work on MCTS convergence (e.g., in sequential games). For instance, the core arguments resemble those in recent literature on UCT-style algorithms, yet the paper does not engage with this body of work to clarify its novelty. A more explicit comparison would strengthen the claim that the proof technique is novel or substantially advances the state of the art. Without this, the theoretical contribution risks being perceived as incremental rather than foundational.  

2. **Omission of UCB Analysis:** The paper focuses on a specific MCTS variant but neglects to address UCB (Upper Confidence Bound) algorithms, which are widely adopted in practice and have been rigorously analyzed for tree search (e.g., in the UCT framework). Given the prominence of UCB in the literature, the absence of a comparative analysis—particularly regarding how UCB-based methods perform under the same conditions—is a significant oversight. The authors should either extend their analysis to include UCB variants or explicitly justify why this omission is justified (e.g., due to fundamental incompatibility with the game structure). Without this, the empirical claims about algorithmic performance lack contextual depth.  

3. **Unexplained Empirical Behavior:** The theoretical analysis proves convergence for mean propagation, yet the experiments show that propagation of the current sample value also achieves good results under various criteria. The authors provide no explanation for this discrepancy. This is a critical gap, as it suggests either the theoretical conditions may be overly conservative or that the empirical method leverages properties not captured by the current analysis. A discussion of this phenomenon—e.g., whether it stems from finite-sample effects, game-specific structure, or the interplay of exploration/exploitation—would significantly enhance the paper’s credibility and utility.  

**Overall Assessment:** The paper makes a competent contribution to the MCTS literature, with sound theory and empirical validation. However, its technical impact is tempered by the lack of clear novelty in the proof framework, the omission of UCB analysis, and the unresolved empirical inconsistency. While the results are interesting and the presentation is clear, the work does not advance the field in a way that distinguishes it from existing studies. The theoretical foundation is solid but not sufficiently innovative to justify a major contribution to NIPS.  

**Recommendation:** I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the paper would benefit from substantial revisions addressing the concerns above. Specifically, the authors must: (i) clarify the novelty of their proof technique relative to prior work; (ii) incorporate analysis of UCB algorithms or justify their exclusion; and (iii) explain the empirical behavior of sample-value propagation. Without these, the paper’s significance remains limited. As currently written, it offers a useful but incremental step in the analysis of MCTS for simultaneous-move games.
