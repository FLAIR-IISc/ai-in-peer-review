The authors analyze a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms that can use, with mild restrictions, essentially any no-regret selection method. Their main theoretical result is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move settings.

A limitation is that the convergence is asymptotic (no rates are given), which follows from the analysis since the selection method is only assumed to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. This may not be a serious drawback, as, to the best of my knowledge, there are no existing convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

That said, rates matter, since backward induction yields an exact equilibrium anyway. If one assumes a specific regret rate for the selection method, do corresponding convergence rates follow—for example, using the sqrt{T} guarantee provided by exp?

The paper concludes with experiments. The authors compare propagating mean rewards, as in their analysis, against the standard practice of propagating the current sample, finding that mean values perform slightly worse empirically. They also attempt to extract empirical insight into the convergence rate.

To conclude: This is a well-written and technically sound paper. I have a mild concern about its ultimate significance given the absence of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods work well in practice, so even if rates do not emerge from the analysis, establishing asymptotic convergence is a solid first step. On the other hand, convergence results already exist for MCTS with sequential moves, so the contribution feels somewhat incremental.
