This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms, demonstrating convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium for a broad class of algorithms employing any no-regret selection method, under the assumption of simultaneous moves. This is a valuable contribution, particularly given the scarcity of convergence guarantees for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous play. 

The primary strength of the work lies in its generality. By leveraging the framework of no-regret learning, the authors demonstrate a convergence result applicable to a wide range of MCTS implementations. However, a key limitation is the asymptotic nature of the convergence proof; the analysis, constrained by the epsilon-Hannan consistency of the assumed selection method, does not provide explicit convergence rates. 

While the lack of rates isn't necessarily a fatal flaw – especially considering the difficulty of the problem – it does raise the question of practical implications. Given that backwards induction can compute exact equilibria, understanding the rate at which this MCTS approach converges becomes crucial for assessing its utility. The authors are encouraged to investigate whether specific regret bounds of the underlying selection method (e.g., the \sqrt{T} bound offered by Exp3) can be translated into quantifiable convergence rates for the algorithm.

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values – as used in the theoretical analysis – to the standard practice of propagating current sample values. The results indicate a slight performance disadvantage for the mean value approach, providing a useful empirical observation. The authors also attempt to glean insights into convergence speed through experimentation.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. My main reservation concerns the incremental nature of the contribution. While establishing asymptotic convergence is a positive step, existing results already demonstrate convergence for MCTS with sequential moves. The absence of convergence rates further tempers the potential impact. Nevertheless, MCTS is known to perform well in practice, and this work provides a solid theoretical foundation for understanding its behavior in simultaneous-move games.

**Minor Comments/Typos:**

*   Figure 1: “leafs” should be “leaves.”
*   Line 144: “negated” is more appropriate than “inverted.”
*   Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1, a2) is unclear. Using (i, j) or, perhaps more descriptively, (\sigma1, \sigma2) would improve readability, especially given the earlier definition of a_{i,j} to represent payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
*   Line 172 and elsewhere: There appears to be inconsistency in the notation, switching between (i, j) and ‘a’ representations. A careful review and consistent notation throughout the paper would be beneficial.

Despite these minor issues, I enjoyed reading this paper and believe it represents a worthwhile contribution to the field. The lack of convergence rates, however, does somewhat limit its overall impact.
