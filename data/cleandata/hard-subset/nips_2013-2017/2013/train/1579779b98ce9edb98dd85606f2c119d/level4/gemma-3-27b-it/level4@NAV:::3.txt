The authors provide a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms employing a broad class of no-regret selection methods, subject to minor restrictions. Their central theoretical result is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in the setting of simultaneous moves.

A limitation of this work is the asymptotic nature of the convergence guarantee; the analysis does not provide specific convergence rates. This is a natural consequence of the authors’ assumption of only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. While not necessarily a flaw – to the best of my knowledge, existing literature lacks convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves – the question of achievable rates remains important. Given that backward induction yields exact equilibria, understanding the convergence speed relative to specific regret bounds (e.g., the \sqrt{T} bound offered by Exp3) would strengthen the contribution.

The paper includes experimental validation, comparing the propagation of mean reward values (as used in the analysis) against the standard practice of propagating current sample values. The experiments demonstrate a slight performance disadvantage for the mean value approach. The authors also attempt to empirically assess convergence rates.

In summary, this is a technically sound and clearly written paper. My primary reservation concerns the overall significance of the work given the absence of convergence rate results. Establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable initial step, particularly given the practical success of MCTS. However, the result feels somewhat incremental considering existing convergence results for MCTS with sequential moves.

Minor comments:

Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1, a2) is unclear and should be replaced with (i, j) or, preferably, (\sigma1, \sigma2). This is particularly confusing given the earlier definition of a_{i,j} as the payoff in a single-stage matrix game.
Line 172: Inconsistencies in notation between (i, j) and the use of ‘a’ appear to persist throughout the paper. While I will not enumerate further instances, careful editing is warranted.

Overall, this was a well-written paper that I found enjoyable to read. Despite my reservations regarding its potential impact due to the lack of convergence rates, it represents a solid contribution to the field.
