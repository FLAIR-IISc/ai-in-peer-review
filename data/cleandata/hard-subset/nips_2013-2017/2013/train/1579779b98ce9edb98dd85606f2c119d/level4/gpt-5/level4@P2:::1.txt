This paper analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template where the selection policy at each node is any ε-Hannan-consistent (HC) procedure with guaranteed exploration (e.g., regret matching or Exp3). They prove eventual convergence to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with an approximation bound that scales with game depth (quadratically in D in their statement). The proof proceeds by (i) establishing convergence in matrix games under HC selection, (ii) extending this to repeated games with bounded payoff error, and (iii) inducting up the game tree to account for error compounding across depths. Experiments on random games and empirically hard instances support the theory and suggest that both regret matching and Exp3 variants converge, with mean backups converging slightly slower than sample backups.

Relation to prior work: The paper positions itself against exact solvers for simultaneous moves (backward induction with alpha-beta pruning or double oracle) and the practical success of MCTS/UCT in games with sequential moves. It highlights negative results for UCB/UCT in simultaneous-move settings (e.g., Shafiei et al.), and leverages established results on Hannan consistency of regret matching and Exp3.

Strengths
- First formal convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate NE in this game class.
- General and modular template: any ε-HC selection rule with exploration suffices.
- Clear separation of concerns: matrix-game analysis, bounded-error extension, and tree induction.
- Empirical evidence aligns with theory; includes random and adverse test cases.
- Establishes subgame-perfect convergence, not only root-level equilibrium.

Weaknesses and questions
- Novelty of proof technique: The core argument (HC ⇒ approachability of NE in repeated games, plus an induction over depth with error accumulation) is conceptually standard. Clarify precisely which technical steps go beyond known results (e.g., your Lemma 4.9 on bounded-error games and how it differs from existing approachability/online-learning analyses).
- Approximation bound and tightness: The final bound scales as O(D^2 ε) (the statement appears to miss ε; please fix). This seems loose; can sharper bounds be obtained (e.g., linear in D) or are there lower bounds justifying quadratic growth?
- UCB/UCT: Given UCT’s prevalence and existing analyses for trees, the paper would benefit from a more thorough treatment. At minimum, add a principled explanation of why standard UCB/UCT fails your conditions (non-HC in simultaneous moves) and whether simple modifications (forced exploration, unbiased backups) could bring UCB within your framework. An empirical comparison on the same benchmarks would strengthen the paper.
- Mean vs sample backups: The theory requires mean returns, but experiments show that sample backups also converge and often faster. Provide intuition (bias–variance trade-offs, martingale properties, or stability of selection updates) and discuss whether the analysis can be extended to sample backups. Even a partial result or a proposition identifying conditions under which sample backups inherit convergence would significantly increase impact.
- Experimental scope: The search over depth-2, BF=2 games and random instances is a good start, but the empirical section is small for a theory-plus-experiments paper. Consider adding:
  - Scaling with depth and branching (beyond D≤2) and exploration rates γ, with confidence intervals.
  - Wall-clock comparisons and memory overhead of regret tables at each node.
  - Comparisons against other regret-based selection rules (e.g., Hedge/Exp4 variants, RM+).
  - Sensitivity to rollout policies and stochastic returns.
- Presentation issues:
  - Theorem 4.11 seems to omit ε in the (·)-NE bound; please correct and restate clearly.
  - Unify terminology between empirical frequencies and average strategies; you prove they coincide asymptotically—state which is used in the main theorem.
  - Clarify whether forced-exploration iterations update the learner’s state (A* definition suggests no); align this with the implementation details in Section 3.
  - Improve Algorithm 1 readability (line references, consistent RetVal usage).

Quality
- The theoretical core appears sound and complete for eventual convergence, with appropriate assumptions (HC and guaranteed exploration). The reduction that ignores tree growth is justified for eventual convergence, though finite-time bounds are absent.
- Proofs rely on known HC properties but contribute a careful bounded-error extension and an induction across tree depth. More discussion of tightness would help.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. A few notation slips (missing ε) and minor ambiguities can be addressed. The empirical section could report variability (error bars) and operational details (hyperparameters, γ schedules).

Originality
- Moderate. The key insight is to import HC-based learning guarantees into SM-MCTS and manage error accumulation through depth. While the building blocks are standard, the assembled result for simultaneous-move EFGs is new and useful.

Significance
- Meaningful for researchers working on search in simultaneous-move domains and for the broader MCTS community. The template guides algorithm design (use HC selection with exploration). Impact would grow with tighter bounds and a theory covering sample backups and popular UCT variants.

Pros for acceptance
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate NE; subgame-perfect result.
- General and implementation-friendly template (RM, Exp3 satisfy conditions).
- Solid empirical support on random and hard instances.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty of techniques; loose depth-dependent bounds and no finite-time rates.
- Excludes UCT/UCB from both theory and experiments; limited experimental scale.
- Theory requires mean backups, while practice often uses sample backups; gap remains.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and tighten the final bound; discuss tightness and possible improvements.
- Expand experiments (scale, sensitivity, baselines) and include UCT with commentary.
- Provide intuition and, if possible, partial theory for sample backups.
- Clarify exploration handling and unify average vs empirical strategy usage.

Overall, the paper contains interesting and useful results for SM-MCTS. The contribution is solid, though not technically groundbreaking. Strengthening the discussion of UCT, expanding experiments, and tightening bounds would substantially improve the paper.
