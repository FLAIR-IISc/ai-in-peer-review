This paper analyzes zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games, a class that includes interesting examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors study MCTS with regret-matching in this setting and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The paper is clear, well-written, and the results are presented cleanly. The main contribution lies in the formal analysis, which extends prior work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013 [20]) beyond preliminary results. The proof proceeds inductively, with optimal strategies assumed at the leaf nodes and properties then propagated up the tree.

However, the main weakness of this approach is that it yields limited insight about strategies at internal nodes of the game tree. As a result, it does not connect to key game-theoretic concepts such as subgame-perfect Nash equilibria. Strengthening the connection to subgame perfection would significantly improve the paper’s relevance to game theory and help clarify what the convergence guarantees imply for behavior in all subgames, not just at the root. In particular, it would be valuable either to characterize when the proposed procedure yields subgame-perfect outcomes or to explain why such guarantees are out of reach in this framework.

I recommend adding a discussion and, where possible, results that relate the analysis to subgame-perfect equilibria in simultaneous-move games. A useful reference is: Harris, C. (1990), “Existence of subgame-perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.” This literature could help situate the contribution within established equilibrium refinements and clarify the implications of the inductive proof strategy for subgame play.

Overall, the paper provides novel and interesting convergence results for regret-matching within MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games. From a computational standpoint, the analysis is valuable, but it would benefit from a tighter connection to subgame-perfect equilibrium concepts that have been extensively studied in the game theory community.
