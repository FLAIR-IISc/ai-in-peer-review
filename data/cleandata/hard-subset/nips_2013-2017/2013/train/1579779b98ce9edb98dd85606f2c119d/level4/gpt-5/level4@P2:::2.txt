Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron-like games). The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses any selection rule that is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration. They prove that, under these conditions, the induced dynamics converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE), with a subgame-perfect guarantee whose approximation error grows with game depth. The proof proceeds inductively on the game tree, starting from leaf matrix games and propagating bounded errors upward. They instantiate the template with regret matching and Exp3, and empirically verify convergence on random and adversarially chosen small games, also comparing mean vs. sample-value backups.

This extends prior SM-MCTS work that lacked convergence guarantees. In particular, it complements results showing UCB/UCT may fail to converge in simultaneous-move settings (e.g., Shafiei et al., 2013) and goes beyond earlier preliminary analyses (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013). It also connects to online learning results on Hannan consistency (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear formal contribution: a general convergence result for SM-MCTS under broad selection conditions (ε-Hannan consistency with exploration).
- Sound proof strategy using repeated matrix games with bounded error and an induction over depth; assumptions are stated and reasonable for this setting.
- Empirical results support the theory and illuminate practical choices (e.g., mean vs. sample backups; regret matching vs. Exp3).
- Presentation is clear and well organized; algorithms and assumptions are explicit.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Finite-time performance is not analyzed; bounds are asymptotic and the approximation scales with depth, which may be loose for practical games. Suggest quantifying finite-time rates or at least discussing how the error constants depend on exploration γ and depth D.
- The empirical evaluation focuses on small, synthetic games. Adding benchmarks from established simultaneous-move domains or comparisons to alternative solvers (e.g., backward induction with LP/double oracle, CFR-style baselines) would strengthen significance.
- The proofs temporarily assume the full tree is built; while addressed by exploration, more discussion on how tree growth and nonstationary payoffs interact would improve clarity.
- The paper claims subgame-perfect approximate equilibrium; it would be helpful to make the link to classical subgame-perfect equilibrium concepts more explicit and to situate the result within that literature. Suggested citation: Harris (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves,” and related work on extensive-form perfection.
- Practical guidance on selecting γ and the impact of mean backups on convergence speed vs. bias would be valuable.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and general convergence guarantee for an important class of games where UCT fails.
- Conceptually clean reduction tying MCTS selection to online learning guarantees (Hannan consistency).
- Clear writing and reproducible algorithmic template; results likely to influence practitioners using SM-MCTS.

Arguments against acceptance:
- No finite-time bounds; asymptotic guarantees may be too weak for practice, especially with depth-dependent error.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and scale.
- The practical performance implications of mean vs. sample backups and exploration choices are not fully characterized.

Evaluation by criteria:
- Quality: Strong on theory (correct, well-supported); empirical validation is suggestive but limited; finite-time analysis is missing.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well-structured; could better emphasize the subgame-perfect aspect and its relation to classical game theory.
- Originality: High; first general convergence proof for SM-MCTS under ε-Hannan consistent selection with exploration.
- Significance: Meaningful for both theory and practice in simultaneous-move games; broader impact would increase with finite-time results and larger-scale experiments.

Overall, this paper presents a solid theoretical advance with supportive experiments. Clarifying the connection to subgame-perfect equilibrium and expanding empirical breadth would strengthen the submission. Recommended citation to aid this connection: C. Harris (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.”
