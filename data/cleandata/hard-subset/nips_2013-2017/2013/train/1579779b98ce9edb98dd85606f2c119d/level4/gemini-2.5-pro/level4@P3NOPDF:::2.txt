This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for the class of zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games. The authors specifically investigate an MCTS variant that employs regret matching at each decision node and provide a proof of its convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. This class of games is notable as it includes well-known examples such as Goofspiel and Tron.

The paper is clearly written, and its results are well-presented. The primary contribution is a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of the regret-matching MCTS algorithm in this specific game setting. This work significantly extends previous, more preliminary analyses (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013 [20]) by providing a formal convergence proof. The core of this proof is an elegant inductive argument that establishes optimal strategies at the leaf nodes and propagates them up the game tree.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its theoretical scope. The analysis focuses exclusively on convergence to a Nash equilibrium for the entire game. While this is a valuable result, the inductive nature of the proof, which builds from the leaves upwards, seems to miss an opportunity to connect with more refined and powerful solution concepts from game theory, namely Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria (SPNE).

SPNE is a crucial concept for perfect-information games because it requires strategies to be optimal not just for the game as a whole, but also within every possible subgame. The current analysis does not provide guarantees about the optimality of play at the inner nodes of the game tree, which is the central requirement of subgame perfection. A discussion of how the proposed algorithm's behavior relates to SPNE would substantially strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution and bridge the gap between this computational approach and established game-theoretic principles. For relevant background, the authors might consider citing foundational work on this topic, such as "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves" by C. Harris (1990).

In summary, this paper presents a solid and novel analysis of a regret-matching based MCTS algorithm for an important class of games. The convergence proof is a definite contribution to the computational game theory literature. However, the paper could be significantly improved by situating its findings within the broader context of game-theoretic solution concepts, particularly by addressing the relationship between the algorithm's output and Subgame Perfect Nash Equilibria.
