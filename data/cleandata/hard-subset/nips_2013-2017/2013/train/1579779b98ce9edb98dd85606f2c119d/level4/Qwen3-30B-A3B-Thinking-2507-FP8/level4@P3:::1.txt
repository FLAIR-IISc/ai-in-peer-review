

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. While MCTS has been successfully applied in practice to such games (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), formal convergence guarantees have been lacking. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games, prove that it converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium when instantiated with ε-Hannan consistent selection methods with proper exploration, and empirically validate their claims using regret matching and Exp3. This work builds on established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency) and game theory (Nash equilibrium), but extends them to a previously unanalyzed class of games.

The paper's strengths are significant. The theoretical contribution is solid and well-executed, providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The proof technique is sound, leveraging existing results on Hannan consistency and extending them to the multi-stage setting through careful inductive arguments. The empirical evaluation is thorough, testing both randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios, and demonstrates that the theoretical results hold in practice. The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from prior work, particularly noting that UCT-based methods (which have been widely used) lack convergence guarantees as shown by Shafiei et al. (2011). The writing is generally clear and well-organized, with proper technical details provided for the theoretical analysis.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. While the theoretical contribution is valuable, the novelty of the proof technique is somewhat limited. The core approach—reducing the problem to Hannan consistency in matrix games and then extending to extensive-form games via induction—is conceptually straightforward and builds directly on established results in online learning (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000; Auer et al., 2002). The authors could better articulate how their extension to simultaneous move games represents a meaningful technical advance beyond these prior results.

The empirical section could be strengthened. While the authors compare the mean propagation variant (RMM) with the current sample value variant (RM), they do not adequately address why RM works well in practice despite the theoretical requirement for mean propagation. The paper states that both methods converge but RMM converges slightly slower, yet fails to explain this empirical observation in relation to the theoretical analysis. This is a significant gap, as the practical implications of the theoretical requirement are unclear. Additionally, the paper omits analysis of UCT, which is widely used in practice for similar games (as noted in the introduction), despite acknowledging its prevalence in the literature.

Regarding significance, the work provides important theoretical justification for using MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is valuable for practitioners. However, the immediate applicability is somewhat limited, as the convergence guarantees are asymptotic and the paper does not provide finite-time bounds. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their conclusion, suggesting it as future work, but this represents a missed opportunity to strengthen the paper's impact.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, but some technical details would benefit from more elaboration. For instance, the connection between the regret matching algorithm and the MCTS template could be more explicitly explained. The formal analysis is sound, but the transition from matrix games to extensive-form games could be made more transparent.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by addressing a specific gap in MCTS theory for simultaneous move games. While the proof technique isn't entirely novel, its application to this particular class of games represents a meaningful advance. The authors correctly cite relevant prior work (Shafiei et al., 2011; Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000), and their results are distinct from previous NIPS papers on MCTS (e.g., Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006; Browne et al., 2012).

In summary, this is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a specific but important class of games. The main strengths are the formal convergence proof and empirical validation. The main weaknesses are the limited novelty of the proof technique and the lack of explanation for the empirical success of the current sample value propagation method. While the paper is not groundbreaking, it provides a solid theoretical foundation for future work and is well-suited for NIPS as a quality contribution to the field.

Strengths:
- First formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Sound theoretical analysis using established concepts
- Thorough empirical evaluation supporting theoretical claims
- Clear distinction from prior work (e.g., UCT's lack of convergence guarantees)

Weaknesses:
- Proof technique builds directly on existing results without sufficient novelty
- Fails to explain why current sample value propagation works empirically
- Omits analysis of UCT despite its prevalence in practice
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds

Recommendation: Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a specific but important class of games, and the quality of the work meets NIPS standards. While not groundbreaking, it provides valuable theoretical justification for MCTS applications in simultaneous move games and would be a useful addition to the NIPS proceedings.
