

Review:  
The authors present an analysis of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms that employs any no-regret selection method (with mild restrictions). Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium for games with simultaneous moves.  

A notable limitation is that the convergence is asymptotic, with no rates provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' assumption that the selection method is only ε-Hannan consistent. While this is not inherently problematic—given the absence of prior convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves—I believe the question of convergence rates remains important. Specifically, since backwards induction yields exact equilibria, it is pertinent to ask whether the authors can derive rates under specific regret guarantees (e.g., the √T rate from exponential weighting algorithms like Exp3).  

The paper concludes with experiments comparing the propagation of mean reward values (used in the analysis) against the standard propagation of current sample values. The mean-reward approach slightly underperforms experimentally. The authors also attempt to extract empirical insights into convergence rates, though the results are inconclusive.  

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound paper. My reservation concerns the work’s ultimate significance, given the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step for MCTS in this setting, especially since such methods are known to perform well in practice. On the other hand, convergence results for MCTS with sequential moves are already established, making this contribution somewhat incremental.

- Line 172: Notation inconsistencies (e.g., transitions from (i,j) to a's) persist throughout the paper; these require editing.  

This is a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading, though its impact is tempered by the absence of convergence rates in the analysis.
