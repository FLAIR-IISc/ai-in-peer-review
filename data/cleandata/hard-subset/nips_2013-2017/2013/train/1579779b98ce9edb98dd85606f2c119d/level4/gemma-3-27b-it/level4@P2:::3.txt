This paper analyzes Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The core contribution is a proof establishing convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, leveraging any regret-minimizing selection method satisfying certain consistency requirements. This is a valuable result as, to the authors’ knowledge, no prior convergence guarantees exist for MCTS in this setting. The paper clearly presents the theoretical framework and supports it with empirical validation.

The work builds upon and relates to a substantial body of prior research. The introduction effectively situates the problem within non-cooperative game theory and highlights the limitations of traditional methods like backward induction, particularly regarding domain knowledge requirements. The authors appropriately cite relevant work on MCTS (e.g., UCT [14]), security games [1], and general game playing [6], demonstrating awareness of the existing literature. They correctly position their contribution as addressing a gap in theoretical understanding for MCTS specifically in simultaneous-move games.

A key strength of the paper is its technical soundness. The formal analysis, employing the concept of -Hannan consistency, provides a solid foundation for the convergence result. The authors clearly define their notation and progressively build their argument, culminating in Theorem 4.11. The empirical evaluation, while not providing convergence *rates*, serves to confirm the theoretical findings and explore the practical implications. The comparison between propagating mean rewards and current sample values is a thoughtful addition, and the attempt to identify worst-case games provides valuable insight.

However, the lack of convergence rates is a notable limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, and its connection to the -Hannan consistency assumption, it does diminish the practical impact of the result. Backward induction provides an exact solution, and understanding how quickly MCTS converges relative to this optimal solution would be highly beneficial. Investigating the relationship between regret rates of the selection method (e.g., the √T bound for Exp3) and the MCTS convergence rate would be a fruitful direction for future work.

Further minor weaknesses exist. There are some notational inconsistencies throughout the paper, particularly concerning the use of (i,j) versus a’s to denote actions, which could cause confusion. Additionally, the proof in Section 4 is quite dense and relies on substantial background knowledge. While thorough, it might benefit from more intuitive explanations of key steps.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** This is the first theoretical convergence result for MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and based on well-established concepts from online learning theory.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates its contributions and limitations.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments support the theoretical findings and provide useful insights.
*   **Significance:** Even without convergence rates, establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable step towards understanding the behavior of MCTS in this challenging game class.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Convergence Rates:** The absence of convergence rates limits the practical impact of the result.
*   **Incremental Advance:** Compared to existing MCTS convergence results for sequential games, this work feels somewhat incremental.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** Minor notational issues detract from the clarity of the presentation.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper, despite the limitation concerning convergence rates. The technical contribution is significant, the analysis is sound, and the presentation is clear. While the result may not be revolutionary, it represents a valuable addition to the theoretical understanding of MCTS and opens up avenues for future research focused on obtaining tighter convergence bounds. The authors have produced a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading and believe will be of interest to the NIPS community.

**Specific suggestions for improvement:**

*   Address the notational inconsistencies, particularly the use of (i,j) and a’s.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential approaches for deriving convergence rates, even if only speculatively.
*   Review and potentially clarify the dense proof in Section 4 to improve its accessibility.
*   Correct “leafs” to “leaves” in Figure 1.
*   Replace "inverted" with "negated" on line 144.
*   Clarify the notation on line 10 of Algorithm 1 to use either (i,j) or a consistent alternative.
