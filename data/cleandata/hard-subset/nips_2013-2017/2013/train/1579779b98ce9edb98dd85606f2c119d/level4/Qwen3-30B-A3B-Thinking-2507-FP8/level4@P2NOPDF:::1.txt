

This paper investigates Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, proving convergence to approximate Nash equilibria under specified conditions, with empirical results supporting the theoretical claims. The work is well-motivated and clearly presented, making a useful contribution to the analysis of MCTS in this setting. However, the technical novelty and depth of the contribution warrant closer scrutiny.  

The primary strength lies in establishing a sufficient condition for convergence, which is both practically relevant and rigorously demonstrated. The empirical validation further reinforces the theoretical findings. That said, the paper’s main contribution hinges on the theoretical analysis, yet the novelty of the proof technique is not sufficiently distinguished from existing approaches. The authors rely on standard concentration inequalities and martingale arguments, which, while correctly applied, do not introduce significant methodological advances over prior work on MCTS convergence. This modest technical contribution limits the paper’s impact.  

A critical omission is the exclusion of UCT (Upper Confidence bound applied to Trees), a dominant MCTS variant in practice. The literature contains established analyses of UCT for tree search (e.g., in multi-armed bandit contexts), yet the paper fails to incorporate or compare UCT against the proposed algorithm. Including such a comparison would strengthen the empirical section and clarify the practical advantages of the authors’ approach. Without this, the paper’s relevance to existing MCTS implementations remains unclear.  

Additionally, the theoretical analysis focuses solely on mean-value propagation, yet the experiments indicate that propagating the most recent sample value (rather than the running mean) also yields strong empirical performance across multiple criteria. The authors provide no theoretical justification or discussion for this discrepancy. A brief exploration of why this heuristic works empirically—perhaps through variance considerations or sampling dynamics—would significantly enhance the paper’s depth and address a notable gap between theory and practice.  

While the paper’s core result is valid and the writing is clear, the absence of UCT analysis and the unexplained empirical behavior of sample-value propagation undermine its novelty. The proof technique, though sound, does not represent a substantial departure from established methods in the literature. Consequently, the paper’s technical contribution is modest, and its significance to the broader MCTS research community is limited.  

In summary, the work offers a solid, incremental step in MCTS theory but lacks the originality or depth expected for publication at NIPS. The authors should address the points above—particularly by incorporating UCT comparisons and clarifying the empirical behavior of sample-value propagation—to elevate the paper’s contribution. As currently written, the technical novelty is insufficient to justify publication.
