The authors provide a theoretical analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms, demonstrating convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move games under the assumption of an epsilon-Hannan consistent, and more generally, any no-regret selection method (with mild restrictions). A primary contribution is this convergence proof for simultaneous moves, a setting where such guarantees are currently lacking in the literature.

A limitation of the analysis is that the convergence result is asymptotic, without providing specific rates. This is a natural consequence of the authors’ choice to assume only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. While not necessarily a flaw given the existing gap in theoretical results, understanding the achievable rates is an important question, particularly considering that backward induction provides a method for finding exact equilibria. The authors should comment on whether rates can be determined for specific regret bounds of the selection method, such as the \sqrt{T} bound guaranteed by Exp3.

The paper includes experimental validation, comparing propagation of mean reward values – as used in the theoretical analysis – to the standard practice of propagating current sample values. The experiments indicate that propagating mean values results in slightly lower performance. The authors also attempt to experimentally investigate the convergence rate.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. My main reservation concerns the potential impact of the work due to the absence of convergence rates. Establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step, given the empirical success of MCTS methods. However, the result feels somewhat incremental, as convergence guarantees are already known for MCTS in the context of sequential moves.

I found this to be a well-written paper and an enjoyable read. Despite my reservations regarding the lack of convergence rates, I believe it represents a solid contribution to the field.
