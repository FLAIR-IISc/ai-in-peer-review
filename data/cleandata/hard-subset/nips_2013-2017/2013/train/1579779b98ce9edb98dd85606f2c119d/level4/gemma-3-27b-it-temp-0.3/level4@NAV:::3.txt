The authors provide a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms employing a broad class of no-regret selection methods, subject to minor restrictions. Their key contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in the context of simultaneous moves.

A limitation of this work is the asymptotic nature of the convergence result, as no specific rates are established. This is a natural consequence of the analysis, given the assumption of only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. While not necessarily a flaw – to the best of my knowledge, existing proofs of convergence for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves are absent – the question of convergence rates remains important. Given that backwards induction provides an exact equilibrium, understanding the rates achieved with specific regret bounds (e.g., the \sqrt{T} bound guaranteed by Exp3) would be valuable.

The paper includes experimental validation, comparing the performance of propagating mean reward values – as used in the theoretical analysis – to the standard practice of propagating current sample values. The experiments indicate a slight performance disadvantage for the mean value approach. The authors also attempt to empirically investigate convergence rates.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. My primary reservation concerns the potential significance of the work in the absence of convergence rates. Establishing asymptotic convergence is a positive step, particularly given the practical success of MCTS methods. However, convergence results are already available for MCTS with sequential moves, making this contribution somewhat incremental.
