- Primary contribution: Proves asymptotic convergence of a class of MCTS algorithms using any no-regret selection method (with mild restrictions) to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move games.

- Main weakness: Result is asymptotic only—no convergence rates are provided.

- Cause of weakness: Analysis relies on epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method, which precludes direct rate bounds.

- Reviewer question: Asks whether the authors can relate convergence rates to specific regret bounds (e.g., Exp3’s O(√T)) and provide insights on attainable rates.

- Empirical evaluation: Compares propagating mean reward values (theoretically consistent) to propagating current sample values, finding only slight performance differences.

- Empirical attempt: Authors try to experimentally assess convergence rate, though theoretical rates are absent.

- Overall assessment: Well-written and technically sound, but significance is tempered by the lack of rate analysis and existing results for sequential-move MCTS.

- Incrementality concern: Contribution seen as somewhat incremental given prior convergence results for sequential-move games.

- Acceptance arguments—novelty: First theoretical convergence guarantee for MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form simultaneous-move games.

- Acceptance arguments—soundness and clarity: Technical analysis is rigorous and the paper is clear and well-organized.

- Acceptance argument—significance: Asymptotic convergence in a challenging setting is a valuable theoretical step.

- Rejection arguments—lack of rates: Absence of convergence-rate analysis limits practical implications.

- Rejection arguments—incremental nature: Improvements over existing sequential-move results are limited.

- Minor issues to fix: Typo “leafs”→“leaves”; replace “inverted” with “negated”; clarify notation (use (i,j) or (σ1,σ2) instead of (a1,a2)); resolve inconsistencies between (i,j) and ‘a’.
