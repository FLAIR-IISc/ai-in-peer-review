This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum, perfect-information, extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any selection method satisfying ε-Hannan consistency (a standard no-regret property). The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this family of algorithms is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The analysis is supported by experiments comparing different algorithm variants on randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is that it provides what appears to be the first formal proof of convergence for an MCTS-based method in this game setting. This is a valuable contribution, as it offers a solid theoretical justification for applying these popular simulation-based algorithms to games with simultaneous moves, where such guarantees were previously lacking. The proof technique, which proceeds by induction from the leaves of the game tree, is elegant and the generalization to any ε-Hannan consistent selection method is a nice feature.

A key limitation of the analysis, however, is that the convergence result is asymptotic and does not provide any explicit convergence rates. This is an understandable consequence of the proof's generality, which relies only on the high-level property of ε-Hannan consistency rather than on a specific rate of regret minimization for the underlying selection algorithm. While establishing asymptotic convergence is an important first step, the practical significance is somewhat tempered by this lack of rates. For instance, since backward induction can find an exact equilibrium in polynomial time for these games, a key advantage of an MCTS approach lies in its performance as an anytime algorithm. Without convergence rates, it is difficult to theoretically characterize this practical advantage.

The experimental section provides a useful comparison between the theoretically-justified approach of propagating mean reward values and the more standard approach of propagating the current sample value. The finding that the standard approach converges slightly faster is interesting. The empirical investigation into convergence rates on "worst-case" games also offers valuable insight.

**Clarity and Quality**

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the proofs provided.

**Originality and Significance**

To my knowledge, this is the first work to prove the convergence of MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. In this respect, the work is original and significant. It provides a solid theoretical underpinning for a widely used class of algorithms and bridges a gap in the literature.

However, my main reservation concerns the ultimate impact of the work. While the result is novel for this specific class of games, it can also be viewed as an extension of known convergence results for MCTS in purely sequential games. From this perspective, the contribution might feel somewhat incremental. Establishing the asymptotic convergence is a good step, but the lack of rates makes it difficult to assess how this method compares theoretically to existing exact solvers or to understand its finite-time behavior.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   Could the authors' analysis be extended to provide explicit convergence rates if a specific selection method with a known regret bound is assumed? For example, what rate could be proven if one were to instantiate the framework with a specific algorithm like Exp3, which has a known regret bound of O(√T)? This would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong, technically correct paper that establishes an important foundational result. The convergence proof is a valuable and novel contribution to the MCTS literature. While I have a mild reservation regarding the significance due to the lack of convergence rates and the incremental feel relative to prior MCTS theory, the paper successfully provides the first formal justification for using these methods in simultaneous-move games. I lean towards acceptance.

**Minor Comments**

*   Figure 1: The caption uses "leafs," which should be "leaves."
*   In Algorithm 1 (line 10), the selected action is denoted `(i, j)`. The surrounding text and later sections sometimes use `a` to denote an action. For consistency and clarity, it would be best to use `(i, j)` throughout when referring to a joint action pair, especially since `a_{ij}` is already defined as a payoff.
*   Line 144 mentions that Player 2 uses "negated utilities." This is the correct term. Please ensure this wording is used consistently throughout the paper, as alternatives like "inverted utilities" would be ambiguous.
