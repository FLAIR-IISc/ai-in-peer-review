Summary
The paper studies zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, a class that includes well-known examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. It analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) when combined with regret matching at simultaneous-move nodes, and proves convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis proceeds inductively on game depth, assuming optimal strategies at terminal nodes.

Strengths
- Clarity and presentation: The paper is clearly written, and the technical results are presented in an accessible manner.
- Novelty: The formal convergence guarantees for regret-matching MCTS in this specific class of games go beyond prior work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013), which offered more preliminary analysis.
- Relevance: Simultaneous-move perfect-information games sit at a useful intersection of game theory and search, and the paper’s results are of interest to the computational game theory and RL communities.

Main concerns
The primary limitation is conceptual rather than technical: the analysis focuses on convergence of play but says little about the equilibrium refinement induced at interior nodes. In perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, the notion of subgame-perfect equilibrium (SPE) is central, as backward-induction arguments do not apply straightforwardly. The current results—framed around approximate Nash equilibrium of the overall profile—do not clearly establish whether the algorithm’s limit strategies satisfy an ε-SPE property (i.e., are approximately optimal in every subgame). This is an important omission because:
- Simultaneous-move nodes can generate coordination issues that equilibrium refinements address.
- Convergence of play along the root may not imply consistency or credibility of strategies in off-path subgames.
Connecting the convergence proof to subgame perfection would substantially strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Suggested directions to strengthen the paper
- Explicitly define the equilibrium concept achieved at each history. Does the algorithm induce a behavior strategy profile that is an ε-SPE, or only an ε-NE at the root? If only the latter, characterize the gap and conditions under which ε-SPE might be attained.
- Relate the inductive proof to subgame analysis. The current induction assumes optimality at leaves; discuss how this assumption interacts with subgame-perfect reasoning. Can the base case be relaxed to ε-optimal leaves and still propagate guarantees that align with an SPE notion?
- Discuss equilibrium refinements. If SPE is too strong for the given algorithm, clarify which refinement (if any) is appropriate in this setting—e.g., trembling-hand perfection or subgame-perfect correlated equilibrium—and whether regret matching at nodes is compatible with it.
- Position relative to prior work. Deepen the comparison to Lanctot et al. (2013): What additional assumptions does your proof avoid or require? Are there differences in how regret is decomposed across nodes? What new insights about simultaneous-move structure drive your result?
- Cite relevant game-theoretic literature on existence and characterization of equilibria in simultaneous-move subgames. In particular: Harris, C. (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.”

Technical questions and clarifications
- Assumptions for convergence: Specify exploration strategies, tie-breaking rules, and learning-rate schedules required for the guarantees. Are the results asymptotic, or do you obtain finite-time bounds?
- Error metrics: Define the exact notion of “approximate” Nash equilibrium used (e.g., exploitability at the root, max deviation gain across all histories). If results are root-based, state this explicitly.
- Dependence on depth and branching factor: Indicate how error or regret accumulates across levels in the induction. Are there depth-dependent constants or mixing-time effects?
- Leaf handling: In MCTS, leaves are typically rollouts to terminal states or evaluation functions. How do the guarantees extend when leaves are solved approximately rather than optimally?
- Strategy representation: Is regret computed independently at each simultaneous-move node, and if so, how is consistency across the tree ensured? Does the algorithm effectively learn a profile over all subgames or primarily along sampled paths?
- Practicality: If experiments are included, report exploitability over time in Goofspiel and Tron, not only win rates, to empirically validate the theoretical claims.

Minor comments
- Tighten the formal model section by precisely defining histories, simultaneous action sets, and the information structure at nodes (even though the game is perfect-information, simultaneous moves imply nontrivial information sets about the opponent’s current choice).
- Provide a brief proof roadmap to highlight the inductive structure and the role of leaf optimality.
- Where multiple equilibria exist, clarify whether the algorithm selects a particular equilibrium (e.g., via exploration or tie-breaking) and whether selection affects subgame consistency.

Overall assessment
This paper offers a clear and useful theoretical contribution on regret-matching MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, extending earlier analyses and providing convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. Its main shortcoming is the limited treatment of interior-node incentives and equilibrium refinement. Strengthening the connection to subgame-perfect reasoning—conceptually and, where possible, formally—would significantly increase the impact and interpretability of the results. With an expanded discussion along these lines and clearer statements of assumptions and error metrics, I would view the paper as a solid contribution. Recommended citation to situate the work within game-theoretic foundations: Harris (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.”
