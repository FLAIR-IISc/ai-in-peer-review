This paper analyzes the class of zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. Several interesting games fall into this class, such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), implemented with regret-matching, for this class of games and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. 

The paper is clear and well-written, and the results are well-presented. The main contribution lies in its new formal analysis. Previous work, such as Lanctot et al. [20], looks at a similar setup but offers more preliminary analysis. The proof employs an inductive approach, starting with optimal strategies at the leaf nodes. 

A potential weakness of this approach is that it may not yield particularly insightful results concerning the inner nodes of the game tree. This overlooks some key concepts in game theory, specifically subgame perfect Nash equilibria. The paper could be strengthened by explicitly connecting the analysis to these concepts. A relevant citation is Harris [1990], which presents analyses of regret-matching strategies in the context of subgame perfect Nash equilibria. 

This paper presents novel analyses of regret-matching strategies with MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. While interesting from a computational standpoint, the analysis could benefit from a stronger connection to established tools like subgame perfect Nash equilibria, which have been extensively studied by game theorists.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proof appears rigorous and builds upon established concepts in online learning and game theory.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the subject matter.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The focus on zero-sum perfect-information games may limit the broader impact of the results.
*   **Missed Connection:** The lack of explicit connection to subgame perfect Nash equilibria weakens the theoretical contribution from a game-theoretic perspective.
*   **Convergence Rate:** The paper does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, leaving open questions about the practical efficiency of the algorithms.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While there is room for improvement, particularly in connecting the work to established game-theoretic concepts, the formal analysis and empirical validation are compelling. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the comment regarding subgame perfection in a revised version.
