This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice for such games, a formal guarantee of their convergence to a Nash equilibrium has been an open problem. The authors' primary contribution is to provide such a proof. They introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with any no-regret learning algorithm for action selection in the constituent matrix games. The core theoretical result demonstrates that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and satisfies a guaranteed exploration property, the resulting MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The paper supports this theoretical claim with an empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection mechanisms, comparing their performance on both randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The main strength of this paper is its novelty. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS algorithms in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. This is a significant theoretical contribution that bridges an important gap between the widespread practical application of MCTS in this domain and its formal understanding. It places these heuristic methods on a more solid theoretical foundation from the perspective of algorithmic game theory.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound and rigorous. The proof is built up logically, starting from the analysis of repeated matrix games, extending to games with bounded payoff errors, and finally using an inductive argument to establish convergence for the multi-stage, extensive-form game. This structured approach makes the argument clear and convincing. The work is a complete contribution, combining a general theoretical framework with specific instantiations and empirical validation.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of defining the problem setting and presenting the general SM-MCTS algorithm before diving into the formal analysis. This clarity is crucial for a paper with a significant theoretical component.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Convergence Rates:** The primary weakness of the analysis is that the convergence guarantee is asymptotic; no finite-time convergence rates are provided. This is an inherent consequence of the general assumption that the selection algorithm is merely ε-Hannan consistent. While establishing asymptotic convergence is an important first step, the practical significance is somewhat limited without understanding how quickly the algorithm approaches an equilibrium. Since exact methods like backward induction exist for this game class, the key advantage of MCTS is its performance in games that are too large to be solved exactly. A rate of convergence would help quantify this advantage. Could the authors' analysis be extended to provide explicit rates if one assumes a specific selection algorithm with a known regret bound, such as the O(√T) regret achieved by Exp3? Such an extension would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.

2.  **Incremental Nature:** While the result is novel for simultaneous move games, convergence results for MCTS are already established for the purely sequential case. From this perspective, the contribution could be viewed as an incremental, albeit technically non-trivial, extension of existing MCTS theory to a more complex class of games.

3.  **Discrepancy Between Theory and Practice:** The experiments reveal that propagating the mean reward value, a requirement for the theoretical analysis, leads to slightly slower empirical convergence than propagating the single-sample reward, which is more common in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, it raises a question about whether the analysis could be adapted to cover the empirically superior variant, or if there is a fundamental reason for this gap.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Figure 1 Caption:** The word "leafs" should be corrected to "leaves".
*   **Player 2 Utilities (Page 4):** The description of player 2's algorithm states they use "negated" utilities. This is correct for a zero-sum game framework.
*   **Notation in Algorithm 1:** The notation in line 10, `(i, j)← Select(h)`, is inconsistent with other parts of the paper where `a` is used for action. For clarity, it would be best to consistently use `(i, j)` for the joint action throughout. This notational inconsistency appears to persist in Section 3. Consistent notation would improve readability.

In conclusion, this is a well-written and technically correct paper that presents a valuable and novel theoretical result. The proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games is a strong contribution. My main reservation concerns the ultimate impact of the work, given the absence of convergence rates, which makes it difficult to assess its practical implications against existing algorithms. However, establishing asymptotic convergence where no proof previously existed is a commendable achievement and a solid foundation for future work. The paper is a clear candidate for acceptance.
