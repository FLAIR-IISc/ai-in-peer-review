This paper studies Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games and prove that, under specific conditions – namely, -Hannan consistency of the selection method and appropriate exploration requirements – the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE). Empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games supports this theoretical result, and suggests convergence for other MCTS variants as well.

Overall, this is a solid paper that provides a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS algorithms in a challenging game setting. The result is valuable, and the presentation is generally clear and well-organized. However, considering the core contribution lies in the theoretical analysis, I have some reservations regarding the novelty of the proof technique employed. The paper builds upon established concepts in online learning and game theory, and while the application to simultaneous-move games is significant, a more detailed discussion of how this work extends existing theoretical frameworks would strengthen the contribution.

Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithms are frequently used in practice and represent a natural baseline for comparison. While the authors acknowledge previous work showing UCB’s lack of convergence guarantees in this context [Shafiei et al., 2017], a discussion of why the theoretical tools used in this paper cannot be applied to UCB, or a brief exploration of potential modifications to UCB that *could* lead to convergence guarantees, would be beneficial. Existing analyses of UCB for Trees [e.g., Kocsis & Szepesvári, 2006] provide relevant background and could inform this discussion.

The paper demonstrates convergence with propagating the mean reward, as required by the theoretical analysis. However, the experimental results suggest that propagating the current sample value also performs well under the evaluated criteria. Providing a potential explanation for this empirical observation – perhaps related to the specific properties of the games tested or the impact of the exploration parameter – would enhance the understanding of the algorithm’s behavior. 

In summary, while this paper doesn’t present a radically new theoretical breakthrough, it offers a valuable and rigorous analysis of MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move games. The convergence guarantee provides a solid foundation for future research, and the empirical results are encouraging. Technically, the paper is a sound contribution to the field, though not exceptionally groundbreaking.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical convergence guarantee for MCTS in a challenging game setting (simultaneous moves).
*   The proof is sound and well-supported by empirical results.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   Addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The proof technique, while correct, may not be substantially novel.
*   Limited discussion of UCB algorithms and potential avenues for extending the theoretical results to include them.
*   Lack of explanation for the observed performance of propagating the current sample value.

**References:**

*   Shafiei, M., Sturtevant, N. R., & Schaeffer, J. (2017). Comparing UCT versus CFR in simultaneous games. *Proceedings of the IJCAI Workshop on General Game-Playing (GIGA)*.
