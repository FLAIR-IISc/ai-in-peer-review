This paper presents a compelling analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms, demonstrating convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium for a class of algorithms employing any no-regret selection method in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof of this convergence, a notable achievement given the absence of existing convergence guarantees for MCTS in such settings.

The strength of the paper lies in its generality. By framing the analysis around the broad class of no-regret learning algorithms, the authors provide a powerful and flexible foundation for understanding MCTS behavior. The technical presentation is clear and the proof appears sound.

However, a significant limitation is the asymptotic nature of the convergence result. While acknowledged by the authors as a consequence of assuming only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method, the lack of convergence *rates* raises questions about the practical significance of the result. Backwards induction offers a method for computing exact equilibria, and understanding how quickly MCTS converges—particularly in relation to the computational cost of backwards induction—would considerably strengthen the contribution. I would be interested to know if the authors have investigated the convergence rates achievable when specific regret bounds are assumed for the selection method. For instance, what rates can be attained using the well-established \sqrt{T} regret bound of Exp3? 

The experimental evaluation provides some empirical insight, comparing the propagation of mean reward values—consistent with the theoretical analysis—to the standard practice of propagating current sample values. The observation that mean values slightly underperform in practice is intriguing and suggests potential avenues for refinement. The attempt to experimentally estimate the convergence rate is also appreciated, though more conclusive evidence would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. My primary reservation concerns the impact of the work given the lack of concrete convergence rates. On one hand, the established success of MCTS in practice makes establishing *any* form of asymptotic convergence a valuable step. On the other hand, convergence results already exist for MCTS in the sequential-move setting, which makes this result feel somewhat incremental without a clearer understanding of the convergence speed. The paper is a solid theoretical contribution but could be made more compelling with further investigation into achievable rates.

**Minor Points:**

*   Figure 1: Please correct “leafs” to “leaves.”
*   Line 144: “Negated” is more appropriate than “inverted.”
*   Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1, a2) is confusing, especially given the earlier introduction of a<sub>i,j</sub> to denote payoffs in a single-stage matrix game. I recommend using (i, j) or, alternatively, (\sigma1, \sigma2) to represent action profiles.
*   Lines 172 and throughout: There appears to be some inconsistency in notation, with shifts between (i, j) and ‘a’ notation. A thorough review to ensure consistent notation throughout the paper is recommended.

Despite these minor points, I enjoyed reading this paper and believe it represents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS.
