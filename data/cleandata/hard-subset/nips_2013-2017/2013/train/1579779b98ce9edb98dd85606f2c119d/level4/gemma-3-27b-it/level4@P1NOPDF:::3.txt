The authors present an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms, demonstrating convergence using any no-regret selection method with mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in the case of simultaneous moves.

A potential weakness is that the convergence result is asymptotic, lacking specific rates. However, this is a natural consequence of their analysis, which relies on the assumption that the selection method is only epsilon-Hannan consistent. This limitation isn’t necessarily a drawback, as, to the best of my knowledge, no existing proofs demonstrate convergence for MCTS methods applied to extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. 

Nonetheless, the question of convergence rates is important. Given that backwards induction can compute an exact equilibrium, it’s natural to ask about the rates achievable when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method—for example, the \sqrt{T} rate guaranteed by Exp3.

The authors complement their theoretical work with experiments, comparing the propagation of mean reward values (as used in their analysis) to the standard approach of propagating current sample values. They observe that propagating mean values results in slightly lower performance experimentally. They also attempt to experimentally assess the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. My main reservation concerns the ultimate significance of the work, stemming from the absence of convergence rates. While MCTS methods perform well in practice, and establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step even without rates, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves. Therefore, this result feels somewhat incremental.

*   Line 10 of Algorithm 1: I believe (a1, a2) should be (i, j), or possibly (\sigma1, \sigma2). This is especially confusing given that a_{i,j} is used to denote payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
*   Line 172: There appear to be inconsistent notations, switching between (i, j) and the letter ‘a’ throughout the paper. I’ll refrain from pointing out every instance, but these inconsistencies should be addressed.

Overall, this was a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading. Despite my reservations about its overall impact, given the lack of convergence rates in the analysis, I believe it represents a solid contribution to the field.
