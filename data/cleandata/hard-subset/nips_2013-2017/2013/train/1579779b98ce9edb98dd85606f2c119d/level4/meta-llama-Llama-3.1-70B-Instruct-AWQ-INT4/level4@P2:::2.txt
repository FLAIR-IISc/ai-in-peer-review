This paper provides a thorough analysis of the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated by various selection methods, and formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning. The main contribution of the paper is the formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a significant improvement over previous work in this area. The authors also provide empirical evaluations of their claims, which support their theoretical results.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to tie the analysis of MCTS to game-theoretic concepts, such as Nash equilibrium and subgame perfection. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the underlying game theory and provide a clear explanation of how their results relate to these concepts.

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach. While the authors acknowledge that their analysis is limited to the leaf nodes of the game tree, they do not fully explore the implications of this limitation. In particular, they could discuss how their results relate to the concept of subgame perfect Nash equilibria, which is a well-studied concept in game theory.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel analysis of regret-matching strategy with MCTS in zero-sum perfect information simultaneous move games. While the authors build on previous work in this area, their contribution is significant and provides new insights into the convergence of MCTS in these games.

Overall, I would recommend this paper for publication, but with some revisions to address the limitations of the approach and provide a more detailed discussion of the implications of the results.
