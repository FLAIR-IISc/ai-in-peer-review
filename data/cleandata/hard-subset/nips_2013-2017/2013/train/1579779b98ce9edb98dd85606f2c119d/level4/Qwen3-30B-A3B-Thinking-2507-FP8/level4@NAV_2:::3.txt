

The authors present a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. Their primary contribution is a rigorous proof that MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium when using an epsilon-Hannan consistent selection method with guaranteed exploration. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as previous MCTS research focused primarily on sequential games without simultaneous moves, with no existing convergence guarantees for games with simultaneous moves.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured, with a clear theoretical framework and thorough empirical validation. The authors establish their convergence result through a careful inductive proof over the game tree structure, demonstrating that the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect epsilon-Nash equilibrium. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical analysis by comparing mean reward propagation (required for the proof) with standard current sample propagation, and by examining convergence rates on worst-case games.

Strengths:
- First formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move extensive-form games
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with careful handling of the induction and error bounds
- Well-executed empirical experiments that validate the theoretical claims
- Clear writing and logical organization of the material

Weaknesses:
- Convergence is established only asymptotically without rate bounds, which is a significant limitation given that backward induction provides exact solutions for these games
- The paper does not explore how convergence rates would change with specific regret minimization algorithms (e.g., Exp3 with its known sqrt(T) regret bound)
- The significance is somewhat limited compared to existing MCTS convergence results for sequential games, making the contribution somewhat incremental

The paper's significance is tempered by the lack of convergence rates, which is particularly relevant when considering that backward induction can solve these games exactly. While establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step, the practical impact would be greater with finite-time bounds. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their conclusion, suggesting future work on finite-time convergence.

I recommend minor revisions to address the following issues:
- Figure 1: "leafs" should be corrected to "leaves"
- Line 144: Use "negated" instead of "inverted" (as the authors correctly use elsewhere)
- Line 10 of Algorithm 1: Replace (a1,a2) with (i,j) to avoid confusion with payoff notation (a_{i,j})
- Address notational inconsistencies between (i,j) and a's throughout the paper

Overall, this is a well-written, technically correct paper that makes a meaningful contribution by establishing the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move extensive-form games. The theoretical foundation is solid, and while the lack of convergence rates limits practical impact, the work represents an important step forward for the field.
