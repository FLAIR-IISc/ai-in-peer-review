This paper studies zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, including illustrative examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) with regret matching in this setting and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The presentation is clear, the results are well organized, and the main contribution lies in the formal analysis. Compared with prior work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013), which provides more preliminary insights, this paper advances the theoretical understanding. The core proof proceeds inductively, with optimal strategies established at the leaves and extended upward through the game tree.

The principal weakness is that the analysis offers limited insight into behavior at internal nodes. As a result, the work does not fully engage with subgame-level solution concepts central to game theory, especially subgame-perfect Nash equilibria. Strengthening the connection between the regret-based MCTS analysis and subgame-perfect equilibria would significantly enhance the paper’s impact. For instance, it would be valuable to clarify whether the convergence guarantees extend to produce approximately subgame-perfect strategies in every subgame, identify conditions under which they do or do not, or provide examples where the approximate Nash equilibrium produced by MCTS with regret matching fails to be subgame perfect. Such discussion would better situate the results within the broader game-theoretic literature and address the limitations of focusing primarily on leaf-based optimality and global equilibrium guarantees.

Recommended citation to support these points:
C. Harris (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.”

Overall, this is a well-written paper that delivers novel formal analysis of regret-matching within MCTS for zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games and establishes convergence to approximate Nash equilibria. While the computational and algorithmic contributions are solid, the paper would be stronger if it explicitly tied its results to subgame-perfect equilibrium concepts and analyzed implications for internal nodes and subgames.
