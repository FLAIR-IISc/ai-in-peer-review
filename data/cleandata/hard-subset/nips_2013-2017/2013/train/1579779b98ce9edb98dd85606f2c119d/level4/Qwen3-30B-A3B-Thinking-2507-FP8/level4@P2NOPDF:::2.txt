

This paper examines zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, including notable examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) augmented with regret-matching, establishing convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium within this game class. The work is clearly written, with results presented cohesively, and the primary contribution lies in its novel formal analysis. While prior work (Lanctot et al., 2013) explores similar setups, it offers only preliminary theoretical insights; this paper advances the field with a rigorous inductive proof that leverages optimal strategies at terminal nodes.

The main strength of the paper is its computational contribution, demonstrating how regret-matching can effectively guide MCTS in these specific games. However, a significant limitation is the analysis's failure to engage with subgame perfect Nash equilibria (SPE), a foundational concept in game theory for sequential decision-making. The authors focus exclusively on the game's root-level equilibrium, overlooking how SPE—derived through backward induction on the game tree—provides deeper structural insights. This omission is critical because SPE addresses strategic consistency across all subgames, including inner nodes, which the current analysis neglects. Consequently, the work misses an opportunity to connect its computational results to well-established theoretical frameworks that explicitly handle sequential rationality in simultaneous-move contexts.

The paper would be substantially strengthened by integrating SPE into its theoretical foundation. Harris (1990) provides a rigorous treatment of SPE existence in simultaneous-move games, offering a natural bridge for the authors to contextualize their convergence results. Explicitly linking the regret-matching dynamics to SPE would not only enhance theoretical depth but also clarify the scope and implications of the approximate equilibrium achieved. For instance, the authors could investigate whether the MCTS-regret-matching approach implicitly satisfies SPE conditions or how deviations from SPE might arise in practice. This refinement would elevate the paper from a computational study to one that meaningfully advances the intersection of algorithmic game theory and strategic analysis.

While the computational perspective is valuable, the current framing risks isolating the work from the broader game-theoretic literature. The recommendation to cite Harris (1990) is not merely a suggestion for references but a pathway to align the paper with decades of established theory on strategic consistency. Without this connection, the analysis remains confined to a narrow computational viewpoint, potentially limiting its impact for readers familiar with game theory. The authors should explicitly address how their approach relates to or diverges from SPE, as this would clarify the novelty and scope of their contribution.

In summary, the paper makes a solid technical contribution to MCTS analysis in a specific game class, with clear exposition and sound methodology. Its most significant opportunity lies in deepening the theoretical grounding by engaging with subgame perfect equilibria. Addressing this gap would transform the work from a competent computational study into a more compelling interdisciplinary contribution that resonates with both machine learning and game theory communities. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions focused on this theoretical integration.
