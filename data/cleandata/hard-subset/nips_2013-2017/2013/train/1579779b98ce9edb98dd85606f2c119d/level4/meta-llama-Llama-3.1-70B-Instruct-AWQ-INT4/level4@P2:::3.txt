The authors present an analysis for a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using essentially (with some mild restrictions), any no-regret selection method. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash in the case of simultaneous moves.

The paper provides a comprehensive and well-written analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated by various selection methods. They formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

One of the strengths of the paper is its thoroughness in presenting the background and definitions necessary to understand the problem and the proposed solution. The authors provide a clear explanation of the game-theoretic concepts, such as Nash equilibrium, Hannan consistency, and regret matching, which are essential to understanding the paper's contributions.

The authors also provide empirical evaluations of their claims using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods on randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games. The results confirm the formal analysis and show that additional MCTS variants also converge to approximate NE on the evaluated games.

However, one weakness of the paper is that the convergence is asymptotic (no rates are provided), which is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis since the selection method is assumed only to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. This limitation raises questions about the practical significance of the result, as backwards induction can provide an exact equilibrium anyway.

To strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial for the authors to explore the question of convergence rates, for example, by assuming a specific regret rate for the selection method. This could provide more insight into the practical implications of the result and help to address the concern about the lack of convergence rates.

The experimental evaluation is also a valuable contribution of the paper. The authors compare the approach of propagating mean reward values used in their analysis to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. Additionally, they attempt to experimentally derive some insight on the convergence rate, which provides a useful starting point for future research.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper that is technically correct. While it has some limitations, it provides a valuable contribution to the field of game theory and MCTS algorithms. The authors' analysis and empirical evaluations provide a solid foundation for future research on the convergence properties of MCTS algorithms in extensive-form games.
