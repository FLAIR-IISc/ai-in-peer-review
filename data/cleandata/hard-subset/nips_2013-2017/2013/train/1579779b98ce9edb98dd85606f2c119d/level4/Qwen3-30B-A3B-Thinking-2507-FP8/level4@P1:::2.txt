

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms applicable to this class of games, which can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. They formally prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection method with sufficient exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games confirms the theoretical claims, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria with comparable speed to existing MCTS variants.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this specific class of games. Previous work by Lanctot et al. (2013) examined similar setups but lacked the rigorous theoretical foundation presented here. The authors' inductive proof strategy—starting with optimal strategies at leaf nodes and extending to higher levels of the game tree—provides a clear theoretical framework for understanding MCTS convergence in these games.

The writing is clear and well-structured, with a logical flow from problem definition through formal analysis to empirical validation. The technical content is sound, with appropriate mathematical notation and careful handling of the theoretical concepts. The experimental design is thorough, with well-chosen benchmarks including both randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case scenarios. The results consistently support the theoretical claims, demonstrating that both regret matching and Exp3 variants converge to approximate Nash equilibria.

The paper's main strength lies in its formal analysis, which fills a critical gap in the literature. The connection between Hannan consistency in matrix games and convergence to Nash equilibria in extensive-form games is particularly well-executed, with careful attention to the nuances of bounded error in repeated matrix games. The authors' treatment of exploration requirements (ensuring each action is attempted infinitely often) and the adaptation of standard online learning algorithms to the MCTS framework are particularly impressive.

However, I have a significant concern regarding the paper's connection to established game-theoretic concepts. The authors claim convergence to "an approximate Nash equilibrium" but fail to properly address the more refined concept of subgame perfect Nash equilibrium, which is crucial for extensive-form games with perfect information. While the paper mentions subgame perfection in its definition, the theoretical analysis does not explicitly leverage this concept to strengthen the convergence guarantees. The proof structure (starting from leaf nodes and working upward) is conceptually aligned with subgame perfection, but the paper misses an opportunity to explicitly connect its results to this well-studied game-theoretic concept.

The omission is particularly notable given that Harris (1990) established the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves—a foundational result for this class of games. A proper connection to subgame perfection would have strengthened the paper's theoretical contribution and provided deeper context for the MCTS analysis. While the paper does state that the equilibrium is "subgame perfect" in the definition, the theoretical development and discussion would have been more compelling if it had explicitly engaged with the game-theoretic literature on this topic.

This is not a fundamental flaw in the technical content but rather a missed opportunity to position the work within the broader game theory literature. The authors could easily strengthen their paper by citing Harris (1990) and discussing how their convergence results relate to the existence and computation of subgame perfect equilibria.

In terms of significance, the paper provides a theoretically grounded justification for using MCTS in this class of games, which has practical applications in security domains and general game-playing. The results are important for algorithmic game theory, as they establish MCTS as a valid approximation method for these games with convergence guarantees.

To summarize:
- Strengths: Clear writing, sound technical content, thorough empirical validation, novel formal analysis, strong connection between online learning and game theory.
- Weaknesses: Insufficient engagement with the subgame perfect Nash equilibrium concept in the theoretical discussion, despite the paper's use of the term.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of computational game theory. The formal analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights for both theoretical and applied researchers working with MCTS in game-theoretic settings. The connection to practical applications in security domains and general game-playing is well-motivated.

While the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly connecting to the subgame perfect Nash equilibrium literature, the core technical contribution is solid and represents a meaningful advance in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in this specific class of games. I recommend acceptance with the suggestion that the authors make a stronger connection to subgame perfect equilibrium concepts in the revised version.
