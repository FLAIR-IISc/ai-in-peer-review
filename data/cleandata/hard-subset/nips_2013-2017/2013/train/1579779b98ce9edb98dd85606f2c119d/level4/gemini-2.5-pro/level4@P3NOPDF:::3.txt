This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a general class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for two-player zero-sum extensive-form games. The key novelty is the extension of MCTS analysis to games with simultaneous moves. The authors' framework is general, accommodating any node selection method that is Hannan consistent (i.e., has no-regret in the long run). The main theoretical contribution is a proof that these algorithms converge to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium.

The paper is well-written, and the theoretical analysis appears to be sound. A major strength of the work is its generality; by leveraging the concept of Hannan consistency, the analysis applies to a broad family of MCTS variants. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for MCTS methods in extensive-form games featuring simultaneous moves, which is a significant and non-trivial extension of prior work that focused on purely sequential games.

However, a primary weakness of the analysis is its asymptotic nature; no convergence rates are provided. As the authors note, this is a direct consequence of assuming only Hannan consistency for the selection method, which does not itself guarantee a specific rate. While establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step, the absence of rates raises questions about the algorithm's practical utility compared to existing methods. For instance, backwards induction can compute an exact Nash equilibrium for games of this type. The motivation for using a sampling-based method like MCTS is often to find good approximate solutions in games that are too large for exact methods. Without convergence rates, it is difficult to quantify how many simulations are needed to achieve a desired level of approximation (i.e., a given epsilon). Given that convergence results for MCTS in sequential games are already established, this work could be perceived as an incremental, though technically challenging, extension to the simultaneous move setting.

This leads to an important question for the authors: What convergence rates can be established if a specific selection method with a known regret bound is used? For example, if one were to employ a method like Hedge (Exponential Weights), which guarantees O(√T) regret, would it be possible to derive a corresponding convergence rate for the overall MCTS algorithm to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium? Providing such an analysis, even for a specific instance of the general framework, would substantially strengthen the paper's contribution and impact.

The paper concludes with a set of experiments that provide some interesting insights. The comparison between propagating the mean reward (as assumed in the analysis) and propagating the direct sample value (a common heuristic) is noteworthy. The finding that the standard heuristic slightly outperforms the variant supported by the theory highlights a small but relevant gap between the theoretical model and practical implementations. The experimental investigation into convergence rates is a welcome addition, though it also underscores the importance of the missing theoretical guarantees.

In conclusion, this is a well-executed and technically sound paper that addresses an important gap in the MCTS literature by proving convergence in games with simultaneous moves. My primary reservation concerns the asymptotic nature of the result. While MCTS algorithms are known to perform well in practice, a theoretical analysis should ideally provide quantitative guarantees that help explain this performance. On the one hand, establishing any form of convergence in this setting is a valuable contribution. On the other, the lack of rates limits the paper's ultimate impact. If the authors could augment their analysis with a discussion of rates under more specific assumptions, it would make for a much stronger and more complete paper.
