The paper analyzes a family of Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithms that, under mild conditions, use any no-regret (Hannan-consistent) selection rule. The central theoretical result is a proof of asymptotic convergence to an epsilon–Nash equilibrium in perfect-information, zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A key limitation is that the guarantees are purely asymptotic: no finite-time rates are derived. This follows from the analysis relying only on epsilon–Hannan consistency of the selection rule. While this is understandable, the lack of rates is noteworthy given that, to my knowledge, there are no prior convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

That said, rates are important in light of the fact that backward induction can compute an exact equilibrium. Can the authors translate specific regret rates into convergence rates for the overall algorithm? For instance, if the selection method achieves the standard O(sqrt(T)) regret (e.g., Exp3 or exponentiated weights), what convergence rate for exploitability in the tree would follow?

The empirical section compares propagating mean rewards (as required in the analysis) against the more common practice of propagating the current sample. The results indicate that using mean values converges slightly more slowly. The authors also explore difficult instances to glean empirical convergence behavior.

In summary, the paper is clearly written and technically sound. I have a minor concern about the overall significance due to the absence of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS often works well in practice and establishing asymptotic convergence in this setting is a valuable first step. On the other hand, convergence for MCTS in sequential-move settings is known, so the contribution feels somewhat incremental.
