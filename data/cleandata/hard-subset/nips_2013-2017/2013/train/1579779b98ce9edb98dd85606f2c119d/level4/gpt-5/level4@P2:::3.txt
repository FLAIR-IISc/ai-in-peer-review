Summary
The paper analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that uses any selection rule that is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration. The main theoretical result is that, under these conditions and with mean-value backups, SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium; the error scales with game depth (O(D^2 ε) plus an arbitrarily small δ). The analysis proceeds by (i) showing convergence in matrix games with ε-Hannan-consistent learners, (ii) extending this to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors, and (iii) lifting the result inductively over the game tree. The authors instantiate the template with regret matching and Exp3, and empirically compare mean-value propagation (required by the proof) against the standard current-sample backup, as well as examining difficult instances to gauge convergence speed.

Relation to prior work
- Exact solutions for simultaneous-move perfect-information games exist via backward induction, with recent improvements (e.g., α-β pruning), but they can require costly LP or double-oracle subroutines and domain evaluators.
- UCT is successful in practice but lacks convergence guarantees in simultaneous-move settings and can fail even in simple cases.
- Prior convergence analyses cover MCTS in sequential-move perfect-information games; to my knowledge, no rigorous guarantees existed for simultaneous moves. This paper fills that gap.

Strengths
- Clear, general template that covers a broad class of no-regret selection methods, including regret matching and Exp3.
- Formal convergence to approximate Nash equilibria, with subgame perfection, in a class of games where guarantees were missing.
- The proof strategy is principled and modular: it isolates matrix-game learning, handles bounded errors due to exploration/backup noise, and composes error across depths.
- Experiments validate the asymptotic claim and examine practical differences between mean and sample backups.

Weaknesses and questions
- Only asymptotic convergence is established; no finite-time rate or sample complexity is provided. Because exact equilibria are achievable by backward induction, rates are important for assessing when SM-MCTS is preferable in practice.
- The analysis requires mean-value backups, while standard practice often propagates the current sample; although experiments show both variants converge and sample backups can be faster, a theoretical explanation of when sample backups are safe would strengthen the contribution.
- The dependence on depth via O(D^2 ε) can be conservative. Can tighter depth dependence be obtained, perhaps by refining the error compounding argument?
- Finite-time rates: If the selection rule has regret O(√T) (e.g., Exp3 or regret matching under standard conditions), can the authors translate that into a convergence rate for exploitability at the root, accounting for the exploration bias and error propagation across depths?
- Practical guidance: How should one choose the exploration parameter γ to balance guaranteed exploration, bias, and convergence speed? Are there adaptive schemes compatible with the proof?

Quality
- The theoretical results appear technically sound under the stated assumptions, and the empirical evaluation aligns with the claims. The proof sketch and supporting lemmas are coherent and connect well to online learning theory.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. However, there are recurring notation inconsistencies between (i, j) indices and aij payoffs, and some confusion in algorithm notation. Addressing these would improve readability.

Originality
- Provides the first convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to approximate NE under broad no-regret selection. While related in spirit to known results for sequential-move games, extending this to simultaneous moves with a general template is a meaningful step.

Significance
- The result gives theoretical backing for widely used SM-MCTS methods and should be of interest to both theory and practice. The absence of finite-time rates tempers the impact but leaves a clear and important direction for follow-up work.

Pros
- First convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information games.
- Broad applicability via ε-Hannan-consistent selection methods with exploration.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee.
- Empirical study supports claims and informs practice (mean vs sample backups).

Cons
- No finite-time convergence rates; depth dependence may be loose.
- Proof requires mean backups, diverging from common practice.
- Notation issues and minor ambiguities.

Minor comments (nitpicks)
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Line 144: “negated,” not “inverted.”
- Algorithm 1, line 10: (i, j) is more consistent than (a1, a2); avoid overloading aij already used for payoffs.
- Line 172 and elsewhere: ensure consistent use of (i, j) versus aij throughout.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and technically correct contribution that establishes asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS with no-regret selection. While incremental relative to sequential-move results, it fills a real gap for simultaneous moves. The main reservation concerns the lack of rates; deriving finite-time bounds under standard regret rates would significantly enhance the impact.
