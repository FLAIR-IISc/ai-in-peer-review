This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, providing a convergence guarantee to an approximate Nash equilibrium under specified conditions. The theoretical results are supported by empirical validation. Overall, this is a solid contribution that clarifies the conditions under which MCTS can be expected to converge, and the presentation is generally clear. However, while the result is useful, the novelty of the proof technique warrants further discussion.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making the core ideas accessible.
*   **Useful Result:** Establishing a sufficient condition for MCTS convergence is a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of the algorithm, particularly in the context of game theory.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results provide supportive evidence for the theoretical findings, enhancing confidence in the analysis.

**Weaknesses & Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Novelty of Proof:** While the convergence result is positive, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how the proof technique differs from, and builds upon, existing approaches in related literature. A more explicit comparison to existing analyses would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Limited Algorithm Coverage:** The paper focuses on a specific MCTS variant, omitting analysis of Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithms which are commonly used in practice (e.g., UCT – UCB applied to Trees). Given existing analyses of UCB for Trees, it would significantly enhance the work to either incorporate UCB algorithms into the analysis or to provide a clear justification for their exclusion and a discussion of how the results might (or might not) extend to UCB-based MCTS. 
*   **Discrepancy Between Theory and Experiment:** The theoretical analysis centers on the convergence of mean propagation. However, the experimental section suggests that propagating the current sample value also performs well under various criteria. This observation raises a question regarding the theoretical underpinnings of this empirical success. The authors should address this discrepancy by either extending the theoretical analysis to encompass sample value propagation or providing a reasoned explanation for why this seemingly different approach yields comparable performance. Exploring the implications of this divergence between theory and practice would add depth to the work.
*   **Technical Depth:** While technically sound, the paper doesn’t represent a substantial breakthrough in theoretical methodology. The results, while correct and valuable, do not push the boundaries of the field in terms of novel theoretical tools or techniques.



**Specific Questions/Suggestions for the Authors:**

*   Please elaborate on the novelty of the proof technique compared to existing literature on MCTS convergence.
*   Can you discuss why UCB algorithms were not analyzed and how the results might relate to MCTS variants utilizing UCB?
*   What is the explanation for the good empirical performance observed when propagating the current sample value, given that the theoretical analysis focuses solely on mean propagation? Is there potential to extend the theoretical results to include this propagation method?
*   Consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the current work and potential directions for future research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in zero-sum games. The convergence result is useful, and the presentation is clear. However, the paper's impact is somewhat limited by the novelty of the proof technique and the exclusion of widely used algorithms like UCB. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
