This paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms applied to zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The core contribution is a proof establishing convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under the condition that the selection method employed is ε-Hannan consistent. This is a valuable contribution, as convergence guarantees for MCTS in such games are currently lacking.

The paper effectively positions its work within the broader context of game theory and algorithmic game playing, referencing relevant prior work, including advancements in security domains [1] and large-scale game playing [2], as well as the challenges of applying backward induction [7, 8, 9] and the existing successes, but limited theoretical understanding, of MCTS [10-14]. The authors clearly articulate the novelty of their approach and its potential to address a gap in the theoretical foundations of MCTS. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The formal proof of convergence is the primary strength of the paper. Establishing this guarantee for simultaneous-move games is a significant step forward.
*   **Generality:** The framework is general, allowing for the instantiation of MCTS with various ε-Hannan consistent selection methods.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and methodology. The algorithm (Algorithm 1) is presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of experimental results provides some validation of the theoretical findings and demonstrates the practical applicability of the approach.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors correctly identify and relate their work to existing literature on regret minimization [15, 16] and MCTS [14].

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Asymptotic Convergence:** The convergence result is asymptotic, without providing specific convergence rates. While acknowledging this is a consequence of the ε-Hannan consistency assumption, the absence of rates limits the practical insight gained from the analysis. Investigating the relationship between the regret rate of the selection method (e.g., the √T guarantee of Exp3) and the resulting convergence rate of the MCTS algorithm would be a valuable extension.
*   **Incremental Contribution:** While significant, the result feels somewhat incremental. Convergence results already exist for MCTS in sequential-move games, and the practical performance of MCTS is well-established. The theoretical guarantee for simultaneous moves is important, but its impact might be limited without a more detailed understanding of the convergence speed.
*   **Notational Inconsistencies:** Several notational inconsistencies exist, particularly regarding the use of (i, j) versus a’s to represent actions and payoffs, creating potential confusion. Careful editing is needed to ensure clarity and consistency throughout the paper.
*   **Minor Clarifications:** The motivation for propagating mean values versus current sample values could be explained more thoroughly. The experimental result showing slightly worse performance with mean propagation warrants further discussion.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   The formal proof of convergence is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   The generality of the framework is a strength, allowing for the use of various selection methods.
*   The empirical results provide some support for the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of convergence rates limits the practical impact of the analysis.
*   The contribution, while important, might be considered incremental given existing results for sequential-move games.
*   The notational inconsistencies detract from the clarity of the presentation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. While the lack of convergence rates and the incremental nature of the result are drawbacks, the theoretical guarantee of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a significant achievement. The paper is well-written and clearly presented, and the empirical results provide some validation of the theoretical findings. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the notational inconsistencies and provide a more thorough discussion of the implications of the asymptotic convergence result. Exploring the potential relationship between the regret rate of the selection method and the convergence rate of the MCTS algorithm would further strengthen the paper.
