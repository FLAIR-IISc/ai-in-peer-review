The authors present a thorough analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The paper provides a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games, which can be instantiated using various selection methods. The authors formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game.

The paper's main contribution is the proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash in the case of simultaneous moves. While this result is significant, it is worth noting that the convergence is asymptotic, and no rates are provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis, as the selection method is assumed only to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. However, it would be interesting to explore the question of rates, especially when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method.

The authors also present empirical evaluations of their approach, comparing the propagation of mean reward values used in their analysis to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. Additionally, they attempt to derive some insight on the convergence rate through experiments. However, these results are not conclusive, and more work is needed to understand the convergence rate of MCTS in these games.

The paper is well-written and technically correct. However, I have some reservations about its impact, given the lack of convergence rates in the analysis. On the one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so even if rates don't follow from the authors' analysis, establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step. On the other hand, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, so the result feels a little incremental.

The paper would benefit from some minor revisions to address notation inconsistencies and minor typos. For example, "leafs" should be replaced with "leaves" in Figure 1, and "inverted" should be replaced with "negated" on Line 144. Additionally, the notation in Line 10 of Algorithm 1 should be clarified to avoid confusion with the payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.

Overall, this is a solid paper that provides a significant contribution to the understanding of MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While it may not be a groundbreaking result, it is a well-written and technically correct paper that is worth publishing.

Strengths:

* The paper provides a thorough analysis of MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.
* The authors formally prove the convergence of MCTS to an approximate Nash equilibrium in these games.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:

* The convergence is asymptotic, and no rates are provided.
* The result feels a little incremental, as convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves.
* Minor revisions are needed to address notation inconsistencies and minor typos.
