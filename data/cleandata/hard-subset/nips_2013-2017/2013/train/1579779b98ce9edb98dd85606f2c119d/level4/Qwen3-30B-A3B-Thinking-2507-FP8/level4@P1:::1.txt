

This paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template applicable to this class of games, which can be instantiated using any regret minimization procedure for matrix games as the selection function. They formally prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. This theoretical contribution fills a critical void, as prior work on MCTS has primarily focused on sequential games without convergence guarantees for simultaneous move settings. The empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games confirms the theoretical findings, demonstrating that the algorithms converge to approximate Nash equilibria.

The paper makes several noteworthy contributions. First, it provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a substantial theoretical advance over previous empirical work. Second, the authors establish a general template that can accommodate various selection methods, making the framework broadly applicable. Third, the empirical results effectively validate the theoretical claims, with experiments showing that both regret matching and Exp3 variants converge to approximate equilibria at comparable rates to other MCTS approaches for these games.

The paper excels in quality and clarity. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with careful definitions and well-structured proofs that build upon established concepts from online learning and game theory. The exposition is clear and well-organized, with sufficient detail for an expert reader to follow the technical arguments. The experimental design is sound, using both randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios to test the convergence properties.

The originality of this work is significant within the context of NIPS. While the core techniques (Hannan consistency, regret minimization) draw from established literature, their application to MCTS in simultaneous move games represents a novel contribution to the field. The authors appropriately contextualize their work against prior NIPS papers on MCTS (e.g., the UCT algorithm), making clear distinctions between their theoretical guarantees and previous empirical approaches. The paper does not claim novelty for the underlying algorithms but rather for their application to a previously unaddressed class of games.

The significance of this work is substantial for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretically, it provides a rigorous foundation for understanding MCTS behavior in a broader class of games than previously analyzed. Practically, it justifies the use of MCTS in applications where simultaneous moves are common (such as security games, card games, and general game-playing competitions), offering algorithmic guarantees that were previously unavailable. While the convergence rates shown in the experiments are not dramatically better than existing methods, the theoretical guarantees provide a new perspective on why MCTS works well in practice for these games.

I have a few concerns regarding the paper's presentation and scope. First, the paper does not address UCB-based selection methods, which have been widely used in MCTS for sequential games (e.g., UCT [14]). While the authors acknowledge UCB's practical success, they note that it does not converge to Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games [17], but they could strengthen their argument by explicitly comparing UCB with their methods in the empirical evaluation. Second, the paper proves that propagating mean values (RMM) leads to convergence, but the empirical results show that using current sample values (RM) also converges well. The authors could provide more insight into why both methods work despite the theoretical requirement for mean propagation.

In terms of the four NIPS criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis and well-designed experiments. The authors carefully address both strengths and limitations of their approach.
- Clarity: The paper is well-written and organized, with clear explanations of technical concepts. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined.
- Originality: The application of Hannan consistency to MCTS in simultaneous move games represents a meaningful extension of prior work, though the core techniques are not entirely novel.
- Significance: The theoretical guarantees provide important insights for practitioners using MCTS in simultaneous move games, advancing both the theoretical understanding and practical application of these algorithms.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The empirical results confirm the theoretical claims, demonstrating practical relevance. The general template framework is valuable for future work in this area.

Con arguments for acceptance: The novelty of the proof technique is somewhat limited, as it builds upon established concepts in online learning and game theory. The paper does not address UCB-based methods, which are widely used in practice, and the experimental comparison with these methods would strengthen the paper.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. While the novelty of the proof technique may be somewhat constrained, the application to a previously unaddressed class of games is significant. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and makes a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the UCB comparison and provide more explanation for the empirical convergence of the current sample value method.
