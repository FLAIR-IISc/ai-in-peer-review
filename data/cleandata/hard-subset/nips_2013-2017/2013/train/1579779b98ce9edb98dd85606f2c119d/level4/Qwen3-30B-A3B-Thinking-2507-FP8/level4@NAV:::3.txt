

The authors analyze a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms that employ any no-regret selection method (with minor constraints). Their primary theoretical contribution establishes convergence to epsilon-Nash equilibrium for games with simultaneous moves.  

A limitation is that convergence is asymptotic without rate guarantees, which is inherent to the analysis due to the assumption of only epsilon-Hannan consistent selection. This limitation is not inherently problematic, as no prior convergence proofs exist for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. However, the absence of convergence rates is significant, particularly since backward induction yields exact equilibria. The authors should clarify whether specific regret rates (e.g., the √T bound from exponential weights) yield concrete convergence rates.  

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values (used in the analysis) against standard sample-value propagation, finding mean values slightly underperform. The authors also attempt to infer convergence rates experimentally.  

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound paper. I have a mild reservation about its significance due to the lack of convergence rates. While asymptotic convergence represents a valuable first step—especially given MCTS’s practical efficacy—the result feels incremental compared to established convergence guarantees for sequential-move MCTS.
