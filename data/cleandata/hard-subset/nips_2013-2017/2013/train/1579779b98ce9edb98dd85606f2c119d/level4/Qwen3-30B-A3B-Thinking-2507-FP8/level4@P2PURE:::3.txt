

The authors present an analysis of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using any no-regret selection method (with mild restrictions). Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash equilibrium in the case of simultaneous moves.  

A key limitation is that the convergence is asymptotic (no convergence rates are provided), which is inherent to the analysis due to the assumption of only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. While this is not necessarily detrimental—since, to my knowledge, no prior proofs of convergence exist for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves—the absence of rates is significant. Specifically, backward induction already yields an exact equilibrium; thus, it is important to determine whether rates can be derived when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method (e.g., the √T guarantee from exponential weights).  

The authors conclude with experiments comparing propagation of mean reward values (used in their analysis) to the standard approach of propagating current sample values. They find that mean propagation slightly underperforms experimentally. They also attempt to derive empirical insights on convergence rates.  

In summary, this is a well-written, technically sound paper. I have a mild reservation regarding its ultimate significance due to the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step given MCTS’s strong empirical performance. On the other hand, convergence results for MCTS with sequential moves are already established, making this contribution somewhat incremental.
