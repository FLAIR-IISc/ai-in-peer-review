This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) employing regret-matching in the context of zero-sum, perfect-information simultaneous-move games, exemplified by games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors demonstrate convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium within this game class, building upon and extending preliminary analyses such as those presented in Lanctot et al. [20]. The presentation is clear, well-structured, and the results are effectively communicated. The core contribution lies in this new formal analysis, providing theoretical underpinnings for the observed performance of regret-matching MCTS. The proof technique relies on induction, leveraging the assumption of optimal strategies at the leaf nodes of the search tree.

However, a significant limitation of this approach, and consequently of the paper, is the relative lack of insight it offers regarding the behavior of the algorithm at inner nodes of the game tree. This is a notable shortcoming given the importance of subgame perfect Nash equilibria in game theory. While the analysis successfully establishes convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, it does not directly address or connect to the crucial concept of subgame perfection. This omission overlooks a rich body of work, particularly within game theory, concerning the analysis of simultaneous-move games through the lens of subgame perfection. 

Strengthening the paper would involve explicitly linking the findings to these established concepts. Specifically, exploring how the regret-matching dynamics within MCTS relate to the existence and computation of subgame perfect equilibria would significantly enhance the theoretical contribution. I recommend the authors consider citing Harris’s 1990 paper, "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves," as a relevant reference point within this broader theoretical landscape. 

In summary, this paper provides a valuable and novel analysis of regret-matching MCTS in a specific class of games. While interesting from a computational perspective, the lack of connection to the well-developed theory of subgame perfect Nash equilibria limits its theoretical impact. Addressing this gap would considerably strengthen the work and broaden its appeal to the game theory community.
