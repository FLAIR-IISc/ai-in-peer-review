This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. It proves that, under specific conditions, MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium, and the empirical results are consistent with the theory.

Detailed comments:
1. Overall assessment: The paper is well-written and establishes a clear sufficient condition for MCTS convergence, which is useful for the community. However, since the main contribution is theoretical, I am concerned about the novelty of the proof technique. Please clarify how your approach differs from or advances existing analyses, and identify which parts are genuinely new versus adaptations of known techniques.

2. Missing analysis of UCB-based methods: Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithms are widely used in tree search (e.g., UCT), yet they are not analyzed here. Given prior analyses of UCB for Trees, it would strengthen the paper to either:
   - provide theoretical guarantees for UCB-style selection within your framework,
   - compare UCB variants empirically against the algorithms you study, or
   - at least discuss the challenges of extending your analysis to UCB and why it may or may not satisfy your conditions.

3. Mean vs. current-sample backups: Your theory establishes convergence for mean-value backups, but the experiments suggest that using the current sample value for propagation also performs well across several criteria. Please explain this discrepancy. For example:
   - Under what conditions might current-sample propagation behave similarly to mean backups?
   - Is there an implicit averaging effect due to repeated visits or exploration that reduces variance?
   - Are there game structures or parameter regimes where the current-sample backup would fail, and can you relate this to your assumptions?

Overall, the results are interesting and the presentation is clear. However, the technical contribution appears incremental rather than groundbreaking. Strengthening the novelty claim, addressing UCB-based methods, and reconciling theory with the observed success of current-sample propagation would significantly improve the paper.
