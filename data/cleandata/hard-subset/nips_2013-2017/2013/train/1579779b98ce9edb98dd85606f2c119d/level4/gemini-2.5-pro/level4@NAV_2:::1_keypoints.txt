- Studies convergence of MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.  
- Proposes a general MCTS template parameterized by in-node selection strategies.  
- Main theoretical result: if in-node selection is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium.  
- Bridges online learning (Hannan consistency) with global convergence guarantees for tree search.  
- Empirical evaluation with Regret Matching and Exp3 confirms convergence on random and worst-case games.  
- Provides a novel theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous-move games where formal guarantees were previously lacking.  
- Strength — Significance: resolves an important open question and rigorously justifies MCTS as a principled approximation method in this domain.  
- Strength — Quality: technically sound, logically structured analysis using repeated matrix games, bounded-error propagation, and induction over tree depth.  
- Strength — Clarity: well-organized, clearly written, and communicates proof strategy accessibly.  
- Weakness — Originality: core tools (Hannan-consistent convergence in repeated games) are established; contribution is mainly the nontrivial synthesis and application to hierarchical MCTS.  
- Weakness — Theory/practice gap: proof requires mean-reward propagation, but experiments show single-sample propagation converges faster; lacks intuition or analysis for the sample-based variant.  
- Weakness — UCB/UCT discussion: could better explain why UCB fails in simultaneous-move games and motivate regret-minimizing choices.  
- Weakness — Asymptotic focus: results are asymptotic with no finite-time convergence bounds and should discuss challenges in deriving such bounds.
