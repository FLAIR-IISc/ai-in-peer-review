This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with various action selection methods. The central contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration (i.e., every action is tried infinitely often), then the overall SM-MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. This theoretical result is evaluated empirically using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods, confirming the convergence claims on both randomly generated and worst-case games.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is that it provides, to my knowledge, the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous move games. While MCTS is widely used in practice for such games, its theoretical guarantees outside of purely sequential settings have been lacking. This work fills an important theoretical gap by connecting the online learning property of Hannan consistency in matrix games to the convergence of a tree search algorithm in the corresponding extensive-form game. This provides a solid theoretical justification for using regret-minimizing algorithms within MCTS for this game class.

2.  **Sound and Rigorous Analysis:** The formal analysis is well-structured and appears technically sound. The authors build their argument methodically, starting from known results in repeated matrix games, extending them to handle games with bounded payoff errors, and finally applying this framework inductively to the multi-stage structure of the extensive-form game. This step-by-step approach makes the proof strategy clear and convincing.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The problem is clearly defined, the algorithm is presented cleanly, and the theoretical sections are well-motivated. The empirical evaluation directly addresses the claims made in the theoretical section, particularly the requirement of propagating mean values, which provides a cohesive link between theory and practice.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Novelty of the Proof Technique:** While the result itself is novel and significant, the proof technique appears to be a careful and skillful combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach. The core concepts—Hannan consistency, regret minimization, and inductive arguments over game trees—are well-established in the fields of online learning and algorithmic game theory. The paper's contribution lies in the novel application of these tools to prove MCTS convergence, which is valuable, but the authors could be more precise in framing the novelty to avoid overstating the originality of the underlying technical machinery.

2.  **Limited Scope Regarding UCB Methods:** The paper explicitly mentions and dismisses UCB-based methods, citing prior work showing their failure to converge to a Nash Equilibrium in this setting. While this is a valid reason for focusing on regret-minimizing approaches, UCB variants remain highly popular and effective in practice. The paper would be more comprehensive and have a greater impact if it included an empirical comparison against a UCB-based SM-MCTS variant. This would allow readers to better understand the practical trade-offs between the provably convergent methods proposed here and the widely-used heuristics, for instance, in terms of convergence speed or final solution quality in a finite number of iterations.

3.  **Gap Between Theory and Experimental Results:** The formal analysis requires propagating the mean of all previous samples from a node (the "M" variants, e.g., RMM) to guarantee convergence. However, the experiments show that propagating the value of only the current sample (the standard approach, e.g., RM) not only converges but appears to do so slightly faster. This discrepancy between the theoretical requirements and empirical performance is interesting and important. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of this gap. Why does the standard method work so well in practice despite not being covered by the proof? Could the proof be extended, or are there theoretical reasons (e.g., the noise from single samples providing a different form of exploration) that might explain this phenomenon? Addressing this would provide deeper insight and connect the theory more tightly to practical algorithm design.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on why your proof requires propagating the mean value? Is it because the bounded error assumption in Lemma 4.9 relies on the law of large numbers to ensure the estimated payoffs at child nodes stabilize around their true values?
*   Following up on the previous point, do you have any intuition as to why propagating the single-sample return works well, and even slightly better, in your experiments? This seems to be a key practical finding that is at odds with the theoretical machinery, and a discussion of it would be very valuable.
*   The bounds on the approximation quality in Theorem 4.11 depend on the depth of the game tree, D. This seems intuitive, as errors accumulate. Does this suggest that for very deep games, the ε required for the base selection algorithm must be impractically small to achieve a reasonable overall equilibrium approximation? A brief comment on the practical implications of this dependency on D would be helpful.

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable and much-needed theoretical contribution to the MCTS literature. The proof of convergence is a significant result. The main weaknesses relate to the framing of the technical novelty and a missed opportunity to fully explore the gap between the formal theory and practical implementations. Addressing these points would further enhance the quality and impact of the work.
