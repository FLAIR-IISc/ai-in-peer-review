This paper studies Monte Carlo tree search in zero-sum extensive form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, providing a formal analysis of its convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The authors present a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games, which can be instantiated using various selection methods, and prove that if the selection method is -Hannan consistent and satisfies additional exploration requirements, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The paper also includes empirical evaluations of the proposed approach using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in practice.

The paper is well-written and clearly presents its main contributions, which are the formal proof of convergence and the empirical evaluation of the proposed approach. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the problem and the proposed solution, and the paper is well-structured and easy to follow.

However, I have some concerns regarding the novelty of the proof technique used in the paper. While the authors provide a clear and detailed proof, it is not entirely clear what new insights or techniques are introduced in the paper. The use of -Hannan consistency and the analysis of repeated matrix games with bounded error are not new concepts, and it is not clear how the authors' approach differs from previous work in this area.

Additionally, the paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other algorithms, such as UCB, which are widely used in the literature. Given the existence of previous analyses on UCB for trees, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with these algorithms to provide a more complete understanding of the proposed approach.

The empirical evaluation of the paper is thorough and provides interesting insights into the performance of the proposed approach. However, the authors only prove the convergence of the propagation of the mean, while the experiments also show good performance for the propagation of the current sample value. It would be beneficial to provide some explanation for this discrepancy and to discuss the implications of these findings.

Overall, while the paper provides some interesting insights and results, it is not technically outstanding, and the authors could benefit from providing more context and comparisons with other work in the area.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper provides a formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.
* The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides interesting insights into the performance of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presents its main contributions.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The novelty of the proof technique used in the paper is not entirely clear.
* The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with other algorithms, such as UCB.
* The empirical evaluation only proves the convergence of the propagation of the mean, while the experiments also show good performance for the propagation of the current sample value.
