The authors analyze a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms that, with mild restrictions, can use any no-regret selection method. Their main theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move settings.

A limitation is that the guarantee is purely asymptotic—no convergence rates are provided—because the selection method is assumed only to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. This may be acceptable, since, to my knowledge, there are no existing convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

That said, rates are important, since backward induction yields an exact equilibrium anyway. Can the authors derive explicit rates when the selection method has a known regret bound, for example O(sqrt(T)) as guaranteed by algorithms like Exp3? Clarifying how a selection method’s regret rate translates into the overall convergence rate would significantly strengthen the contribution.

The paper concludes with experiments comparing the propagation of mean reward values (as used in the analysis) to the standard propagation of the most recent sample value. Mean values perform slightly worse in practice. The authors also attempt to empirically explore convergence rates.

Overall, this is a well-written and technically sound paper. I have mild reservations about its ultimate significance due to the lack of rate guarantees. On the one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, and establishing asymptotic convergence in the simultaneous-move case is a valuable first step. On the other hand, convergence results already exist for MCTS with sequential moves, so the contribution feels somewhat incremental.
