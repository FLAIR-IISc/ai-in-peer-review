Here's a paraphrased version of the review:

The authors provide an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms, incorporating any no-regret selection method with minor restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is proving convergence to epsilon-Nash in scenarios with simultaneous moves.

One limitation of the analysis is the asymptotic nature of convergence, with no provided rates. This outcome is an inherent consequence of assuming the selection method is only epsilon-Hannan consistent. Although this might not be detrimental, given the lack of existing convergence proofs for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of convergence rates is crucial. Specifically, it would be valuable to investigate the rates achieved when a particular regret rate is assumed for the selection method, such as the \sqrt{T} guaranteed by exp.

The authors supplement their analysis with experimental results, comparing the approach of propagating mean reward values to the standard method of propagating current sample values. They found that mean values slightly underperformed in experiments. Additionally, they attempted to experimentally derive insights into the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. However, I have some reservations about its significance due to the absence of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods are known to perform well in practice, making the establishment of asymptotic convergence a valuable first step. On the other hand, convergence results for MCTS with sequential moves are already established, making this result feel somewhat incremental.

Overall, the paper is well-written and enjoyable to read, but its impact is somewhat diminished by the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
