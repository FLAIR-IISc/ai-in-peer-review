

The authors present a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. They introduce a general MCTS framework that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing selection method for matrix games, and prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the algorithm converges to an ε-Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as previous MCTS analyses have primarily focused on sequential games without simultaneous moves.

The paper makes a strong contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games. The authors' proof technique is sound, using induction to show convergence from leaf nodes up to the root of the game tree while carefully handling the bounded errors introduced by MCTS's stochastic nature. The empirical evaluation complements the theoretical results, demonstrating that their approach with mean value propagation converges to approximate Nash equilibria, with convergence rates comparable to existing MCTS variants.

The paper is technically robust. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with clear proofs addressing the challenges of simultaneous moves in extensive-form games. The empirical results validate the main theoretical claims, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria on both randomly generated and worst-case games. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their analysis, particularly regarding convergence rates.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear technical exposition suitable for the NIPS audience. There are a few minor notational inconsistencies (e.g., switching between (i,j) and (a1,a2) for joint actions), but these do not significantly impact readability.

The originality of the work is strong. While MCTS has been extensively studied for sequential games, this paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for games with simultaneous moves. The authors thoroughly review the relevant literature, including previous work on MCTS for sequential games [14, 20] and the absence of convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games [17]. The paper clearly differentiates its contributions from these prior works.

Regarding significance, the paper establishes a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in a broader class of games. This is valuable as MCTS is widely used in practice for games with simultaneous moves, but without formal guarantees. The work provides a theoretical justification for practitioners' empirical success with MCTS in these settings. However, the lack of convergence rates is a notable limitation, as the authors acknowledge, since practitioners often care about how quickly an algorithm approaches the equilibrium.

I have two specific concerns:
1. The paper doesn't provide convergence rates, which is a limitation given that backward induction can solve these games exactly. While the authors correctly note that asymptotic convergence is a necessary first step, it would be valuable to explore how convergence rates depend on the regret rate of the selection method (e.g., the √T rate for Exp3).
2. The experiments comparing mean propagation to current sample value show that mean propagation slightly underperforms, which is counterintuitive given the theoretical analysis. The authors could provide more insight into this discrepancy.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes an important contribution to the theory of MCTS in games with simultaneous moves. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported, and the empirical evaluation provides good validation of the main claims. While the lack of convergence rates is a limitation, the asymptotic convergence result is valuable as a first step, especially since no previous convergence guarantees exist for MCTS in this class of games.

Pros:
- First formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move extensive-form games
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear proofs
- Good empirical validation of theoretical claims
- Well-written with appropriate technical depth

Cons:
- Convergence rates not provided (asymptotic convergence only)
- Theoretical analysis requires mean propagation, but experiments show mean propagation slightly underperforms
- Minor notational inconsistencies

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it addresses an important theoretical question in a domain with significant practical applications. The work advances the state of the art by providing formal guarantees for an important class of games that was previously unaddressed in the MCTS literature.

I recommend acceptance, as the theoretical contribution is significant and fills an important gap in the literature, even if the convergence rates are not provided.
