

The authors analyze a class of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms using any no-regret selection method (with mild restrictions). Their main theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash equilibrium for simultaneous-move games.  

A key limitation is the asymptotic nature of the convergence result (no rates are provided), which is inherent to their analysis due to the assumption of only epsilon-Hannan consistent selection. This limitation is not necessarily problematic, as I am unaware of prior convergence proofs for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. However, the absence of rates is significant, given that backwards induction yields exact equilibria. The authors should clarify whether specific regret rates (e.g., the √T guarantee from exponential weights) imply concrete convergence rates for their method.  

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values (used in the analysis) against the standard approach of propagating current sample values. Mean propagation slightly underperforms experimentally. The authors also attempt to infer convergence rates empirically, though the results appear preliminary.  

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound paper. My primary reservation concerns the work’s significance: the lack of convergence rates weakens the contribution, especially since MCTS is known to perform well in practice. While establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step, the result feels somewhat incremental given existing convergence guarantees for sequential-move MCTS.

The paper is clearly written and engaging. While the asymptotic result is a solid foundation, the absence of rate analysis limits its impact for a venue like NIPS.
