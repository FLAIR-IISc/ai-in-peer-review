### Summary

This paper investigates the theoretical convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with various selection methods. The main contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent (a concept from online learning) and guarantees sufficient exploration, the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash Equilibrium. The paper relates this to prior work by noting the practical success but theoretical deficiency of MCTS in this domain, particularly highlighting that popular methods like UCT are known not to converge. The authors empirically validate their claims using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection functions, confirming convergence on both randomly generated and empirically-derived worst-case games.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

This is a well-written and valuable paper that addresses an important theoretical gap in the MCTS literature. The strengths and weaknesses are discussed below, organized by the review criteria.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by a rigorous, step-by-step proof that builds from simple repeated matrix games to the full extensive-form game setting. The inductive argument, which treats parent nodes as playing a matrix game with bounded error from their children, is sound and clearly presented. The experiments are carefully designed to test the core assumptions of the proof (e.g., propagating the mean reward) and to assess the empirical performance of the proposed methods. The authors are honest about the scope of their work, focusing on a specific class of algorithms.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The progression from background definitions to the formal analysis and finally to the empirical results is logical and easy to follow. The introduction effectively situates the work within the broader context of game theory and MCTS. The notation is consistent, and the formal definitions (e.g., ε-Hannan consistency, games with bounded error) are introduced precisely when needed. Algorithm 1 provides a clear template for the class of algorithms under consideration.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is the novel application of concepts from online learning (specifically, Hannan consistency and regret minimization) to establish a convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games. To my knowledge, this is the first such formal proof for this game class. While the underlying concepts of regret minimization and inductive proofs on game trees are not new in isolation, their synthesis to analyze the error propagation and convergence of a simulation-based search algorithm like MCTS is a novel and significant contribution. The work is clearly differentiated from previous research, which has either been empirical or has shown non-convergence for other MCTS variants (e.g., UCT).

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for both the algorithmic game theory and MCTS communities. By providing a formal justification for using MCTS as an approximation algorithm in this game class, the paper solidifies the theoretical foundations of a widely used practical method. The general template allows researchers to reason about and design new MCTS variants with provable guarantees. This work is likely to be built upon, for example, by seeking tighter convergence bounds, extending the analysis to other game classes (e.g., imperfect information games), or analyzing other selection algorithms within this framework.

Despite the paper's strengths, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1.  **Novelty of the Proof Technique:** While the application is novel, the core proof techniques (regret bounds in matrix games, induction on game tree depth) are adapted from the online learning literature. The main contribution is the bridge built between these two areas, rather than the invention of fundamentally new mathematical tools. This is a minor point, as the contribution is still substantial, but it is worth noting the intellectual heritage of the approach.

2.  **Exclusion of UCB Algorithms:** The paper explicitly sets aside UCB-based methods, which are arguably the most popular and empirically successful variants of MCTS. The authors justify this by citing prior work showing UCT does not converge even in simple one-stage games. However, a more detailed discussion on *why* the current proof framework fails for UCB would be beneficial. Does it violate Hannan consistency, or is there a different reason? Contextualizing the presented results against this dominant family of algorithms would strengthen the paper's impact.

3.  **Discrepancy between Theory and Practice:** The formal analysis requires propagating the mean of all rewards from a child node (`RetVal` = `Xh/nh`). The experiments (Figure 2) show that this theoretically-grounded approach converges slightly slower than the more standard MCTS heuristic of propagating only the current sample's value. The paper presents this interesting empirical finding but does not offer an explanation or intuition. A discussion on why the theoretically sound method is empirically slower, and whether the faster heuristic might also be provably convergent under a different analysis, would be a valuable addition.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for a general class of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games, addressing a significant open problem.
*   The work is technically sound, with a rigorous proof and well-designed experiments that support the main claims.
*   The paper is clearly written and situates its contributions effectively within the existing literature.
*   The results are significant and likely to inspire future theoretical work on MCTS and its applications in game theory.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The analysis omits UCB-based algorithms, which are the most common in practice, somewhat limiting the direct applicability of the theoretical insights.
*   The core mathematical tools are drawn from existing literature on online learning, with the main novelty being their application to MCTS.

In conclusion, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a foundational contribution to the understanding of MCTS. The minor weaknesses identified are primarily suggestions for improvement or future work and do not detract from the core value of the paper.
