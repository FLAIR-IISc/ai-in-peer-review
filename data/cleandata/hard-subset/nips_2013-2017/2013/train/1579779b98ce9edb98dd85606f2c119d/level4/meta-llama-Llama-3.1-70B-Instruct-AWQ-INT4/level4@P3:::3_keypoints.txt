- Primary theoretical contribution: proof that MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.  
- Addresses a literature gap: no prior convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move or general extensive-form games.  
- Method: presents a general MCTS template that can use any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix-game action selection.  
- Theorem: if the selection function is ε-Hannan consistent (ensuring every action is tried infinitely often), MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect ε-Nash equilibrium.  
- Empirical evaluation: experiments with regret matching and Exp3 show empirical convergence speed comparable to recent MCTS algorithms.  
- Conjecture: many existing MCTS variants may also converge to ε-NE and the analysis could be extended to include them.  
- Writing and presentation: paper is well-written, clear, and concise.  
- Main limitation: no convergence rates are provided, limiting practical significance.  
- Incrementality concern: result may feel incremental because convergence for sequential-move MCTS is already known.  
- Minor fixes suggested: replace "leafs" with "leaves," use "negated" instead of "inverted," fix Algorithm 1 notation (a1,a2 → (i,j) or (σ1,σ2)), and ensure consistent notation at line 172.  
- Quality assessment: technically sound with a well-executed proof and thorough empirical work.  
- Clarity assessment: generally clear but with some minor notation issues.  
- Originality assessment: significant for simultaneous-move games though not revolutionary.  
- Significance assessment: provides an important foundation for understanding MCTS convergence in simultaneous-move extensive-form games.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical contribution, clear presentation, and thorough experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: absence of convergence rates and perceived incrementality relative to known sequential-move results.
