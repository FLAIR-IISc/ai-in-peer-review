The authors present an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms, demonstrating convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium under mild restrictions and utilizing any no-regret selection method. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence for simultaneous-move games. 

A potential weakness lies in the asymptotic nature of the convergence result – no explicit rates are provided. However, this is a natural consequence of the authors' analysis, which assumes only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. While not necessarily a fatal flaw, particularly given the current lack of convergence proofs for MCTS methods applied to extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of convergence rates remains important. Since backwards induction guarantees an exact equilibrium, understanding the rates achieved with a specific regret bound for the selection method – for example, the \sqrt{T} bound provided by Exp3 – would be valuable.

The authors complement their theoretical work with experiments, comparing the propagation of mean reward values (as used in their analysis) to the standard approach of propagating current sample values. They observe that propagating mean values results in slightly lower performance. They also attempt to experimentally assess convergence rates.

In conclusion, this is a well-written and technically sound paper. My main reservation concerns the ultimate significance of the work, given the absence of convergence rates. Establishing asymptotic convergence is a solid first step, particularly considering the practical success of MCTS methods. However, convergence results are already available for MCTS with sequential moves, making this result feel somewhat incremental. 

Nitpicks:

*   Figure 1: “leafs” should be “leaves.”
*   Line 144: “negated” is more accurate than “inverted.”
*   Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1, a2) is confusing and should be changed to (i, j), or possibly (\sigma1, \sigma2). This is especially problematic as a_{i,j} is already used to denote payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
*   Line 172: Notation inconsistencies between (i, j) and the ‘a’ notation appear throughout the paper. While I will not detail further instances, these should be carefully reviewed and edited for consistency.

Overall, this was an enjoyable paper to read. Despite my reservations about its impact due to the lack of convergence rates in the analysis, I believe it represents a valuable contribution to the field.
