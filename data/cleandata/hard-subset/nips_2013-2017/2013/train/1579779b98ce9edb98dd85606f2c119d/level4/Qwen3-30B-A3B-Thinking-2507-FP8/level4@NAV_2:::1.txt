

This paper examines Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS algorithms under specific conditions. The authors prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration, MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. Empirical evaluations on randomly generated and worst-case games confirm these theoretical findings.

The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this specific class of games, which has not been previously analyzed in the literature. The technical approach is sound, with clear theoretical analysis supported by empirical validation. However, I have the following concerns regarding the contribution and presentation:

1. While the paper establishes a sufficient condition for convergence, the novelty of the proof technique is limited. The approach builds on established results in regret minimization and online learning for matrix games, and it would be helpful to more clearly articulate how this differs from prior theoretical work in game theory and MCTS. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with recent NIPS papers on similar topics (e.g., Lanctot et al. 2014 on MCTS in Goofspiel).

2. The paper does not analyze the UCT algorithm, which is widely used in practice for MCTS in sequential games. Given that UCT has been the subject of prior theoretical analysis for sequential games (e.g., Kocsis and Szepesvári 2006), it would strengthen the paper to include a discussion of UCT's applicability to simultaneous-move games and to compare its empirical performance with the algorithms analyzed.

3. The theoretical analysis focuses on the propagation of mean values, but the experiments show that propagation of current sample values also achieves good convergence. The paper does not provide a clear explanation for why both approaches work well in practice, despite the theoretical requirement for mean propagation. This discrepancy between theory and practice should be addressed, as it affects the practical relevance of the theoretical results.

Overall, the paper presents technically sound work that makes a meaningful contribution to understanding MCTS in simultaneous-move games. The results are significant for the field, particularly given the practical importance of MCTS in game-playing applications. However, the theoretical novelty could be strengthened by more clearly positioning the work within the existing literature, and the practical implications would be better supported by addressing the concerns noted above.
