This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) within the context of zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors establish a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS to an approximate Nash equilibrium, and validate this result empirically. 

Overall, this is a solid contribution that clarifies the theoretical underpinnings of MCTS convergence. The result is valuable, and the paper is generally well-presented. However, my primary concern lies with the novelty of the proof technique employed, given that the theoretical component constitutes the core contribution. 

Specifically, the paper focuses on algorithms propagating the mean value, while Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithms – widely used in practice – are not analyzed. Considering the existing literature on UCB for Trees, a comparison with these algorithms would strengthen the paper and provide a more complete analysis. Including such a comparison would contextualize the presented results and better highlight their advantages and limitations.

Furthermore, the paper demonstrates convergence for mean-value propagation, yet the experimental results suggest that propagating the current sample value also performs well under the tested criteria. The authors should provide an explanation for this observation. Does the convergence guarantee extend, perhaps under modified conditions, to sample-value propagation? Addressing this discrepancy between the theoretical result and the empirical findings would significantly enhance the paper’s impact.

In summary, while the findings are interesting, the technical aspects of the paper, while correct, do not represent a substantial breakthrough. The theoretical result is a positive step, but further analysis, particularly regarding commonly used UCB variants and a justification for the good empirical performance of sample-value propagation, is needed to elevate this work to a higher level.
