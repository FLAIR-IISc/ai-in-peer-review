This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with various selection methods. The central contribution of the work is a proof that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the resulting MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The paper specifically analyzes regret matching and Exp3 as instances of such selection methods. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical evaluation on randomly generated games and on games designed to be worst-case scenarios, comparing the performance of different MCTS variants.

This work builds upon a growing body of research applying MCTS to games beyond the traditional sequential, perfect-information setting. While prior work, such as Lanctot et al. (2013), has empirically explored MCTS in simultaneous move games, this paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees, bridging a significant gap between the practical application of MCTS and the theoretical foundations of algorithmic game theory.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The main contribution—the convergence proof to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium—is highly significant. It provides a strong theoretical justification for using MCTS in this class of games. Establishing this link between a practical, simulation-based search algorithm and a core game-theoretic solution concept is a novel and important result for the NIPS community.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and well-structured. The proof is built logically, starting from the known properties of ε-Hannan consistent algorithms in single-stage matrix games and extending the analysis inductively to the multi-stage (extensive-form) setting by carefully bounding the error propagated from the subgames. The formal definitions and lemmas are precise and lead rigorously to the main theorem.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The authors do an excellent job of defining the problem domain, presenting the general SM-MCTS algorithm, and breaking down the formal analysis into understandable components (repeated matrix games, games with bounded error, and finally extensive-form games). The experimental section is well-designed to validate the theoretical claims and explore practical implementation choices.

4.  **Completeness:** The work is a complete research contribution. It presents a general algorithmic framework, provides a rigorous theoretical analysis with formal proofs, and includes a solid empirical evaluation that not only supports the theory but also provides practical insights into the performance of different algorithm variants.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Asymptotic Nature of the Convergence Proof:** The proof guarantees *eventual* convergence but does not provide finite-time bounds on the convergence rate or the number of simulations required to achieve a specific ε-approximation of the equilibrium. While establishing asymptotic convergence is a critical first step, a discussion on the prospects or challenges of deriving concrete convergence rates would strengthen the paper's impact. As the authors note in their conclusion, this is a valuable direction for future work.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practical Performance:** The formal analysis requires propagating the *mean* of accumulated rewards (the 'M' variants in the experiments) to bound the error. However, the empirical results consistently show that propagating the single value from the current simulation (the standard approach) converges slightly faster. This creates a minor disconnect where the variant with theoretical guarantees is empirically outperformed by a variant whose convergence is not formally proven here. While the authors rightly conjecture that their analysis could be extended, it would be beneficial to elaborate on the specific theoretical challenges that prevent the direct application of the proof to the standard, faster-converging variants.

3.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is confined to the perfect information setting. While this is a necessary and standard simplification for a foundational proof of this nature, many complex real-world applications involving simultaneous moves also feature imperfect information. A brief discussion on how the presence of imperfect information would break the current proof structure and what new tools might be needed could provide valuable context and highlight further avenues for research.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and novel theoretical contribution to the field. It successfully bridges the gap between the heuristic-driven world of Monte Carlo Tree Search and the formal world of algorithmic game theory. The proof that SM-MCTS with an ε-Hannan consistent selection rule converges to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium is a foundational result. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and well-supported by empirical evidence. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to the scope and potential extensions of the work rather than any fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The paper is of high quality and significance and should be accepted.
