### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) applied to two-player zero-sum extensive-form games characterized by perfect information and simultaneous moves. The primary contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that a specific class of MCTS algorithms converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium, provided certain conditions on the tree policy are met. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical study that illustrates the convergence behavior of the algorithm.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the presentation is clear, making the core arguments and results easy to follow.
2.  **Theoretical Contribution:** The main theoretical result—establishing a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS to an approximate Nash equilibrium in this game class—is a valuable contribution to the literature. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for applying MCTS in this domain.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Novelty of the Proof Technique:** My primary concern revolves around the novelty of the proof technique. While the result itself is useful, the paper's main contribution is theoretical. The authors should more clearly situate their proof methodology within the existing literature and highlight what aspects of their technique are genuinely novel. If the proof relies heavily on established techniques from related areas (e.g., stochastic approximation or regret minimization), the paper's technical contribution might be more incremental than it first appears. A more detailed comparison with prior work in the discussion would help clarify the significance of the technical advance.

2.  **Omission of UCB-based Policies:** A significant omission is the lack of analysis for UCB-based tree policies. UCB algorithms are arguably the most widely used and successful selection strategies within MCTS. Given that prior theoretical work exists for "UCB for Trees," its exclusion from this analysis is surprising and limits the paper's impact. Including an analysis of a UCB-based policy, or at least a detailed discussion of why the current proof framework does not apply to it, would substantially strengthen the paper. A comparative analysis, both theoretically and empirically, against UCB would provide a much clearer picture of the proposed algorithm's practical relevance and advantages.

**Questions and Minor Comments**

1.  **Discrepancy Between Theory and Experiments:** The theoretical analysis focuses exclusively on the convergence properties of value propagation using the mean of the samples. However, the empirical results suggest that propagating the current sample value also performs well across different criteria. The paper would benefit from a discussion on this observation. Could the authors provide some intuition or a hypothesis explaining why this alternative propagation method works well in practice, even though it is not covered by the current theoretical guarantees?

**Overall Assessment**

This paper addresses an important problem and provides an interesting theoretical result for MCTS convergence in a specific class of games. However, its technical contribution feels somewhat limited due to potential concerns about the novelty of the proof technique and the notable absence of an analysis of UCB, the most common MCTS variant. While the findings are valuable, the paper could be significantly improved by addressing these major points.
