- Addresses a significant theoretical gap: MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games.  
- Proposes a general MCTS template and proves convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium when the selection method is ε‑Hannan consistent with sufficient exploration.  
- Empirically validates the theory using regret matching and Exp3 on random and worst-case games, confirming convergence and practical effectiveness.  
- Delivers the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class, linking online learning (Hannan consistency) to game-theoretic Nash equilibrium results.  
- Proof technique: extends Hannan consistency to multi-stage extensive-form games via induction; proof is sound and well-executed.  
- Experiments convincingly support theoretical claims and provide useful comparison of mean-value propagation vs. current-sample propagation.  
- Paper is very clear and well-organized, with precise algorithm descriptions and detailed mathematical proofs.  
- Results have practical significance, providing theoretical justification for MCTS use in many simultaneous-move applications.  
- Weakness: Does not analyze UCB, the most commonly used MCTS selection strategy, nor provide a thorough empirical comparison to it.  
- Weakness: Lacks finite-time convergence bounds; only asymptotic/approximate guarantees are given.  
- Weakness: Worst-case experimental analysis highlights hard games but lacks systematic explanation of structural causes.  
- Pro-accept: First formal proof, strong theoretical-empirical linkage, clear presentation, and direct practical relevance.  
- Con-accept: Omits UCB analysis, no finite-time bounds, limited to zero-sum perfect-information setting, and incomplete explanation of propagation differences.  
- Overall: Strong theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address UCB and finite-time bound concerns.
