This paper studies Monte Carlo tree search in zero-sum extensive form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated by various selection methods. They formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game.

The paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the problem, the proposed solution, and the theoretical analysis. The empirical evaluation is also well-conducted, and the results confirm the formal analysis.

However, I have some concerns regarding the novelty of the proof technique. The authors rely heavily on existing results in the literature, and it is not entirely clear what new insights are provided by this paper. While the application of -Hannan consistency to MCTS in simultaneous move games is new, the underlying proof technique seems to be a straightforward extension of existing results.

Furthermore, the authors do not analyze UCB algorithms, which are widely used in the literature. Given the existing analysis on UCB for Trees, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with other algorithms in the paper.

The empirical evaluation shows that the propagation of the mean has good convergence properties, but the propagation of the current sample value also seems to work well under different criteria. The authors should provide some explanation for this observation.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, but it is not very outstanding. The results are interesting, but they do not provide a significant breakthrough in the field. The paper is well-suited for a conference like NIPS, but it may not be suitable for a top-tier journal.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, and the presentation is clear. However, some of the notation and definitions could be improved for better readability.

In terms of originality, the paper provides a new application of -Hannan consistency to MCTS in simultaneous move games. However, the underlying proof technique is not new, and the authors rely heavily on existing results in the literature.

In terms of significance, the results are interesting, but they do not provide a significant breakthrough in the field. The paper is well-suited for a conference like NIPS, but it may not be suitable for a top-tier journal.
