Summary
This paper analyzes Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors propose a general SM-MCTS template that uses any selection rule that is ε-Hannan consistent (with guaranteed exploration), and prove that the resulting algorithm converges asymptotically to an approximate, subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium. The proof builds up from repeated matrix games, including the case with bounded payoff errors induced by stochastic selection and exploration, and then applies an inductive argument over the game tree depth. Empirically, the paper instantiates the template with regret matching and Exp3, verifies convergence on random and empirically challenging games, and compares propagating mean returns (required for the proof) versus the standard propagation of the current sample.

The work fills a gap: while MCTS (notably UCT) has strong empirical performance and some analyses in sequential perfect-information settings, its behavior in simultaneous-move extensive-form games has lacked convergence guarantees, and UCB-based selection is known not to converge in this setting. Providing such a convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS with no-regret selection is useful to the community.

Quality
- The theoretical results appear sound and carefully structured: matrix games → matrix games with bounded error → stacked (simultaneous-move) extensive-form games via induction. The assumptions (ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration) are standard and satisfied by regret matching and Exp3 (with explicit exploration).
- The analysis is asymptotic. No finite-time convergence rates are provided, which follows from relying only on ε-Hannan consistency. The empirical section is appropriate for validating convergence qualitatively and for comparing mean vs sample propagation.
- The experiments are clear and relevant to the theory, including random and worst-case game families, and show that mean propagation slightly underperforms in practice.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written. A few notational inconsistencies and minor wording issues (noted below) should be fixed. The overall structure (definitions, algorithms, theory, experiments) is clear.

Originality
- To my knowledge, this is the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves under broad no-regret selection. This extends beyond prior analyses focused on purely sequential settings. While the high-level approach (reduce to no-regret dynamics in stage games) is familiar, its adaptation to simultaneous-move MCTS with exploration and mean propagation is nontrivial.

Significance
- The result provides theoretical justification for SM-MCTS with a broad class of selection rules and confirms empirical observations that such methods work well. However, the lack of rates limits immediate practical impact: backward induction already yields exact equilibria (albeit with domain knowledge and higher computational costs), and practitioners often need guidance on sample complexity/exploitability vs iterations. As an asymptotic first step, the contribution is still meaningful.

Main weaknesses and questions
- No finite-time rates: Can the authors translate selection-level regret bounds (e.g., the O(√T) regret guarantees of Exp3 or regret matching variants) into exploitability bounds at the root as a function of iterations and depth? Even a loose depth-dependent bound would significantly strengthen the contribution.
- Mean vs sample propagation: The formal proof requires propagating mean returns, yet empirically this underperforms slightly. Can the authors comment on whether the proof can be adapted to the standard sample propagation, or on conditions under which the two are equivalent in the limit?
- Practical guidance: How sensitive are the results to the exploration parameter γ (both theoretically and empirically)? Since exploration determines guaranteed visitation and introduces bias, a short discussion would help practitioners.

Pros
- Provides the first asymptotic convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS with simultaneous moves under broad, standard assumptions (ε-Hannan consistency and exploration).
- General template applicable to multiple selection methods (regret matching, Exp3).
- Clear inductive proof structure and a thoughtful reduction to repeated matrix games with bounded error.
- Empirical results corroborate the theory and examine worst-case convergence behavior.

Cons
- No finite-time convergence rates; practical sample-complexity guidance is missing.
- The requirement to propagate means for the proof conflicts with the empirically stronger sample propagation.
- Incremental relative to known convergence results for MCTS-like methods in sequential perfect-information games, though still novel for simultaneous moves.

Minor comments and typos
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Line 144: “negated,” not “inverted.”
- Algorithm 1, line 10: Use (i, j) instead of (a1, a2); alternatively, be explicit if (σ1, σ2) is intended. This is especially important since aij denotes matrix payoffs elsewhere.
- Line 172 and nearby: There are additional notation switches between (i, j) and a’s; please standardize throughout.
- Throughout: Replace “exp” with “Exp3” when referring to the algorithm; if “exp” denotes the exponential function in equations, make that distinction clear.

Conclusion
This is a well-executed paper that establishes asymptotic convergence of SM-MCTS with no-regret selection in simultaneous-move games and validates the claim empirically. I have mild reservations about impact due to the absence of finite-time rates, especially given that exact equilibria are available via backward induction. Nonetheless, the theoretical foundation for a widely used class of algorithms in a previously unanalyzed setting is valuable, and this work is a solid first step toward more refined, rate-based analyses.
