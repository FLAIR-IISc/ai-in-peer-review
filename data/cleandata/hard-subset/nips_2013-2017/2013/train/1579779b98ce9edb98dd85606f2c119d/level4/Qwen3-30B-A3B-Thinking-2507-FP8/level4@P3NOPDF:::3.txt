

The paper presents a theoretical analysis of Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms using general no-regret selection methods, establishing asymptotic convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium for games with simultaneous moves. This constitutes a meaningful contribution, as prior convergence guarantees for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves are absent. The authors’ proof leverages the ε-Hannan consistency of the selection method, which inherently precludes non-asymptotic rates—a necessary limitation given their assumptions.

The absence of convergence rates, however, significantly impacts the paper’s significance. While asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step, the lack of quantitative rates raises questions about practical relevance. Backward induction provides exact equilibria in finite games, so the utility of MCTS hinges on its computational efficiency relative to exact methods. The authors should address whether specific regret bounds (e.g., the √T guarantee of exponential weights) yield concrete convergence rates. Without this, the theoretical result remains abstract, and it is unclear how the approach compares to existing MCTS variants in sequential-move games, where convergence rates are known. This gap diminishes the paper’s incremental contribution, as asymptotic convergence for sequential-move MCTS has already been established.

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values (used in the analysis) against standard sample-value propagation. The mean-value approach underperforms slightly, consistent with prior empirical observations. The authors also attempt to estimate convergence rates experimentally but provide no rigorous analysis. This effort is limited by the absence of theoretical rates to validate against, further underscoring the main theoretical gap.

The paper is clearly written and technically sound. The core analysis is correct, and the experiments are appropriately designed. However, the lack of convergence rates weakens the impact. While MCTS is widely used in practice, the theoretical justification here does not advance beyond the asymptotic guarantee—especially when established methods (e.g., sequential-move MCTS) already achieve similar convergence properties. The work feels incremental rather than transformative, as it extends existing frameworks without resolving the critical question of computational efficiency.

In summary, the paper offers a technically valid but incremental contribution. The asymptotic convergence result is a useful foundation, but the absence of rates prevents meaningful comparison to exact solution methods or sequential-move MCTS. The authors should explicitly address whether their framework can yield rates under specific regret bounds (e.g., √T) to strengthen the theoretical impact. Without this, the work’s significance is limited to confirming convergence in a previously unaddressed setting, rather than advancing practical or theoretical understanding of MCTS. The experiments are adequate but do not compensate for the theoretical gap. The paper would benefit from a sharper discussion of the trade-offs between asymptotic guarantees and practical efficiency.
