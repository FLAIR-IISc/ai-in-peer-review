

This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, establishing conditions under which MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The theoretical results are corroborated by empirical validation.  

The paper presents a clear and well-structured argument, making a useful contribution to the understanding of MCTS convergence. However, the primary theoretical novelty of the proof technique warrants deeper scrutiny. While the sufficient conditions for convergence are valuable, the approach appears to build incrementally on existing frameworks rather than introducing a substantially new analytical perspective.  

A significant gap concerns the omission of UCB-based algorithms, which are prevalent in MCTS literature. The authors should address this by incorporating a comparison with tree-based UCB variants—particularly given prior analyses of UCB for tree search—and discussing how their convergence guarantees relate to or differ from these established methods. This would strengthen the paper’s positioning within the broader MCTS research landscape.  

Additionally, the theoretical analysis focuses solely on mean-value propagation, yet the experiments indicate that propagating raw sample values also yields strong performance across multiple evaluation criteria. The authors should provide a theoretical or empirical explanation for this discrepancy: why does the sample-value propagation succeed empirically despite the absence of formal guarantees, and how does this align with the established mean-value convergence framework?  

Overall, the paper delivers a solid, technically sound contribution that advances the practical understanding of MCTS in the specified game settings. However, the theoretical novelty is limited, and the omission of UCB comparisons weakens the paper’s claim to a significant methodological advance. While the results are interesting and the presentation is clear, the work does not substantially exceed the current state-of-the-art in theoretical MCTS analysis. The authors should address these points to elevate the paper’s impact.
