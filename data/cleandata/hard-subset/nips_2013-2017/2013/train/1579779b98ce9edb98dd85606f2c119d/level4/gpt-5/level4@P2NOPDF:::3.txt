This paper analyzes a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms that employ, with mild restrictions, arbitrary no-regret selection methods. The main theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A key limitation is that the convergence guarantee is purely asymptotic: no finite-time rates are provided. This appears inherent to the analysis since the selection method is assumed only to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this is not necessarily a fatal drawback—indeed, I am not aware of prior convergence proofs for MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games—the absence of rates raises questions about practical implications. In particular, since backward induction can compute an exact equilibrium in these settings, it would be valuable to understand how fast MCTS approaches equilibrium. Can the authors translate specific regret rates of the selection rule (e.g., O(√T) for algorithms like EXP3) into convergence rates for exploitability or approximation error at the root?

The experimental section compares two backup strategies: propagating mean rewards (used in the analysis) versus propagating the most recent sample. The results indicate that mean-value backups perform slightly worse empirically. The authors also attempt to infer convergence rates experimentally, which provides some qualitative insight but leaves open the theoretical question above.

Overall, this is a clear and technically sound paper. Establishing asymptotic convergence for simultaneous-move MCTS is a meaningful first step, given the lack of prior results in this setting. That said, because convergence guarantees for MCTS with sequential moves already exist, the contribution feels somewhat incremental. My main reservation about impact stems from the lack of finite-time convergence rates.

Nitpicks:

- Line 144: likely “negated,” not “inverted.”
- Algorithm 1, line 10: (a1, a2) should probably be (i, j), or possibly (σ1, σ2). This is especially confusing given that a_{i,j} denotes payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
- Line 172: The notation seems to switch inconsistently between (i, j) and a’s; similar issues appear elsewhere and should be edited.

This was a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading. I remain mildly concerned about its overall impact given the absence of convergence rates.
