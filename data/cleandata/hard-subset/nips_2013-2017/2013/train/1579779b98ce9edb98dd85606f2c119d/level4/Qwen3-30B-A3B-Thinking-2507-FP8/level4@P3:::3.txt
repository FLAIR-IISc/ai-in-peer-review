

This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors develop a general template for MCTS algorithms that can be instantiated with any epsilon-Hannan consistent selection method (e.g., regret matching or Exp3). Their main theoretical result proves that when using such a selection method with guaranteed exploration, the MCTS algorithm converges to a subgame perfect epsilon-Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. This fills a critical gap in the literature, as prior work on MCTS has primarily focused on sequential games (e.g., the UCT algorithm), with no known convergence guarantees for simultaneous move games.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with careful definitions and rigorous proofs addressing the key challenges in simultaneous move games. The authors effectively relate their work to the broader literature by noting that while backward induction can solve these games exactly, it requires domain knowledge and is computationally expensive, making MCTS a practical alternative. Their formal analysis is novel in the context of MCTS for simultaneous move games, building upon but extending previous work on regret minimization in matrix games (e.g., Hart & Mas-Colell, 1996; Auer et al., 2002).

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical flow from problem statement through formal analysis to empirical validation. The experimental section provides useful empirical confirmation of the theoretical results, comparing the mean propagation approach required for the analysis against the standard current-sample propagation approach. The results show that while mean propagation converges slightly slower in practice (as expected from the theoretical analysis), both approaches ultimately converge to approximate Nash equilibria. The authors also identify empirically challenging games to test convergence behavior.

However, the paper has a notable limitation in its theoretical contribution: it establishes asymptotic convergence without providing convergence rates. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' assumption that the selection method is merely epsilon-Hannan consistent (without specifying a regret rate), but it limits the practical significance of the result. The authors acknowledge this gap and suggest future work on finite-time convergence bounds, but the lack of rates is a significant point to consider, especially since exact solutions (via backward induction) are available for these games. The reader must question whether the asymptotic guarantee offers meaningful advantages over existing practical MCTS implementations, particularly given that the convergence rates of the analyzed algorithms are likely to be slow.

Regarding notational clarity, the paper contains some inconsistencies. For example, Algorithm 1 uses (i,j) for joint actions but later refers to them as (a1,a2), which creates confusion with the payoff matrix notation a_ij used elsewhere. These notational issues should be resolved in the final version.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by the fact that MCTS has already been successfully applied in practice for these games, and convergence results for sequential MCTS games (e.g., UCT) have been established previously. However, the authors' contribution is valuable as it provides the first formal justification for using MCTS in simultaneous move games from a theoretical perspective, which is an important step toward understanding why these methods work in practice.

Strengths:
- First formal convergence analysis for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Technically sound theoretical contribution
- Clear writing and organization
- Empirical validation supports theoretical claims
- Addresses a significant gap in the literature

Weaknesses:
- Asymptotic convergence without rates (limiting practical significance)
- Some notational inconsistencies that could confuse readers
- Incremental contribution compared to prior MCTS work in sequential games

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS in a previously unanalyzed class of games. While the lack of convergence rates is a notable limitation, the authors' work represents an important first step in establishing theoretical foundations for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The result is valuable for both theoretical understanding and practical applications where domain knowledge for backward induction is limited.

Given the technical soundness, novelty in addressing a clear gap, and the authors' careful acknowledgment of limitations, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the theoretical contribution is foundational but not yet complete. Future work on finite-time convergence bounds would strengthen the paper's significance, but the current work is sufficiently novel and technically rigorous for NIPS. The paper makes a solid contribution to the literature on game-theoretic algorithms and MCTS.
