This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, a class that includes games such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze MCTS instantiated with regret matching for action selection and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds inductively, establishing optimal behavior at the leaf nodes and working upward. The paper is clearly written, and the results are presented well. Relative to prior work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013 [20]), which considered a similar setup but offered only preliminary analysis, the main contribution here is a stronger, more complete formal analysis.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation for simultaneous-move extensive-form games and a clean template for SM-MCTS.
- A formal convergence result to approximate Nash equilibrium when using regret matching (and with conditions on exploration), which advances the theoretical understanding of MCTS in this setting.
- The analysis approach (induction from leaves) is easy to follow and is consistent with established techniques in game-tree reasoning.
- The experimental results are well-presented and support the theoretical claims.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The main limitation of the approach is that it provides little insight into the behavior at inner nodes of the game tree. The inductive structure focuses on leaf optimality and propagates guarantees upward, but this yields limited interpretability about strategic structure within subgames.
- This misses an opportunity to connect the results to core concepts in game theory, notably subgame-perfect Nash equilibria in games with simultaneous moves. Anchoring the analysis in the framework of subgame perfection would strengthen the theoretical contribution and situate the results more directly within the game-theory literature.
- Recommended citation to help bridge this gap: C. Harris (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.”

Quality: The paper appears technically sound and careful in its assumptions and claims. The convergence proof is well-structured and the inductive argument from leaf nodes is clearly articulated. The empirical results are consistent with the theory and are easy to interpret.

Clarity: The manuscript is clearly written and well-organized. Definitions and notation for simultaneous-move extensive-form games and repeated matrix games are clear, and the algorithmic variants are explained succinctly.

Originality: The formal convergence analysis of MCTS with regret matching in simultaneous-move extensive-form games is novel relative to prior work that focused on more preliminary or heuristic analyses in this setting.

Significance: From a computational perspective, the result meaningfully contributes to the theory of MCTS beyond purely sequential perfect-information games. Its impact would be further increased by tying the analysis to subgame-perfect equilibrium concepts and related game-theoretic results for simultaneous moves.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, well-written paper with a solid formal contribution.
- Provides convergence guarantees for MCTS in an important but under-analyzed game class.
- Empirical results are consistent with the theory.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited insight into inner-node behavior; the inductive proof largely defers interpretation of subgame structure.
- Lacks a strong connection to subgame-perfect equilibrium concepts and relevant game-theoretic literature in simultaneous-move games.

Overall assessment: This paper presents a useful and well-argued theoretical advance on MCTS in simultaneous-move games, supported by clear experiments. I recommend strengthening the link to subgame-perfect equilibrium and citing Harris (1990) to better connect with existing game-theoretic results.
