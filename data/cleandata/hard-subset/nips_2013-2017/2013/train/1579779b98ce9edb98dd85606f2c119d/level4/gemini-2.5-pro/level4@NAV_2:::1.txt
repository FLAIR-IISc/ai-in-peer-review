This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with various in-node selection strategies. The central contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection strategy is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration (visiting each action pair infinitely often), then the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. This theoretical result connects the well-studied property of Hannan consistency from online learning to the convergence of the broader tree search algorithm. The paper supports its theoretical claims with an empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods, confirming convergence on both randomly generated and worst-case games. This work provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for MCTS in a game class where, despite practical applications, formal convergence guarantees were previously lacking.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a significant and previously open question regarding the theoretical guarantees of MCTS in games with simultaneous moves. By proving convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, it provides a rigorous justification for using MCTS as a principled approximation algorithm in this domain, bridging an important gap between the algorithm's practical success and its theoretical understanding.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is the paper's main strength. It is technically sound and logically structured, proceeding carefully from repeated matrix games to repeated games with bounded error, and finally using an inductive argument over the game's depth to establish the main result. The connection established between the local, in-node learning algorithm's properties (ε-Hannan consistency) and the global convergence of the tree search is elegant and insightful.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The problem setting, definitions, and the general MCTS algorithm are explained effectively. The theoretical sections are accessible, and the high-level proof strategy is communicated well, making a complex analysis easier to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Originality of Technique:** While the application of the theoretical tools to MCTS convergence is novel and significant, the core concepts themselves—namely, that Hannan-consistent players converge to a Nash equilibrium in repeated matrix games—are well-established in the online learning literature. The authors could strengthen the paper by more explicitly framing their contribution as the novel synthesis of these established tools and their non-trivial application to the hierarchical structure of MCTS, particularly in how subgame values are modeled as payoffs in a repeated game with bounded error.
*   **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The formal analysis requires propagating the mean of collected rewards up the tree to ensure the error bounds hold. However, the empirical results show that propagating the value of the current sample—a more standard and computationally simpler approach in MCTS—not only converges but does so slightly faster. This creates a disconnect between the proven theory and best practice. It would greatly enhance the paper if the authors could offer some intuition as to why the single-sample variant also converges, and discuss whether the current analytical framework could be extended to cover this case or if a fundamentally new approach would be needed.
*   **Discussion of UCB/UCT:** The authors correctly state that UCB-based selection strategies do not converge in simultaneous-move games and provide a relevant citation. This point could be further leveraged to motivate their work. A brief discussion on *why* UCB fails (e.g., its exploration strategy is designed for a stationary bandit problem, which is violated when both players are adapting) would provide valuable context and highlight more clearly why regret-minimizing approaches like Regret Matching and Exp3 are a more suitable theoretical foundation in this multi-agent learning context.
*   **Asymptotic Nature of Results:** The analysis is asymptotic and does not provide finite-time bounds on the convergence rate. While this is acknowledged as future work, a brief discussion of the challenges in deriving such bounds (e.g., the compounding of errors through the depth of the game tree, the dependence on game-specific properties) would add valuable depth to the paper and better frame the contribution.
