This paper analyzes the class of zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, which include interesting examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS), implemented with regret-matching, for this class of games and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The paper is clear and well-written, and the results are well-presented. The main contribution lies in the new formal analysis of MCTS in this game setting. Previous work, such as Lanctot et al. [20], explored this setup but offered more preliminary analysis. The proof technique, utilizing an inductive approach with optimal strategies at the leaf nodes, is sound. 

However, a potential weakness of this approach is that it provides limited insights into the inner nodes of the game tree. This overlooks key concepts in game theory, namely subgame perfect Nash equilibria. The paper could be strengthened by explicitly tying the analysis to these concepts. A relevant citation to consider is Harris [1990], which provides analyses of regret-matching strategy and its relationship to subgame perfect equilibria. 

In summary, this paper presents novel analyses of regret-matching with MCTS in zero-sum perfect information simultaneous move games. While interesting from a computational standpoint, the analysis could benefit from a stronger connection to established game-theoretic tools like subgame perfect Nash equilibria, concepts that have been extensively studied by game theorists.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games.
*   The analysis is technically sound and well-motivated.
*   The experimental results support the theoretical findings.
*   The work builds on existing literature in both MCTS and game theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The connection to subgame perfect Nash equilibria is not fully explored.
*   The practical significance of the convergence rate (although empirically evaluated) remains unclear.
*   The analysis is limited to zero-sum perfect-information games, and generalization to other game classes is not discussed.

**References:**

*   Harris, C. (1990). Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves. *Games and Economic Behavior, 2*(3), 255–271.
*   Lanctot, M., Lisý, V., & Winands, M. H. M. (2013). Monte Carlo tree search in simultaneous move games with applications to Goofspiel. *Workshop on Computer Games at IJCAI*.
