- Studies MCTS in two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves and proposes an SM-MCTS template using no-regret per-node selectors.  
- Main theorem: with ε-Hannan consistent selection and sufficient exploration, SM-MCTS with mean-value backups converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate NE; approximation scales O(ε D^2).  
- Analysis uses induction on depth, reducing internal nodes to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors.  
- Empirically evaluates RM and Exp3 on random and worst-case shallow games, comparing mean vs. sample-value backups and confirming convergence.  
- Positions work between practical MCTS (e.g., UCT) and formal EFG methods; fills a gap by giving convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move MCTS.  
- Builds on standard no-regret results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) but assembles them in a novel tree-setting application.  
- Theoretical reduction and inductive argument are sound and well-motivated; use of ε-Hannan consistency and exploration is appropriate.  
- Some constants/assumptions (dependence on ε, γ, reward range, and D) are insufficiently explicit in the final exploitability bound.  
- Proofs crucially require mean backups; unclear if running means or sample backups satisfy the same guarantees.  
- Experiments align with theory and show sample-value backups often work in practice despite no proof.  
- Experimental scope is limited (small depths, symmetric branching, synthetic payoffs) and omits UCB-based selection comparisons.  
- No finite-sample convergence rates are provided, though known RM/Exp3 regret bounds could yield iteration complexity.  
- Presentation is generally clear with good algorithm descriptions but has notational ambiguities (e.g., “( D2 + δ)”) and consistency issues between average strategies vs. empirical frequencies.  
- Suggestions: state main theorem with explicit bound, clarify which backup rule is used in each experiment, and unify exploration treatment.  
- Originality: novel application of no-regret selection to simultaneous-move trees; technical novelty moderate since tools are standard.  
- Significance: first convergence guarantee for MCTS in this game class; useful template for practitioners but practical impact tempered by mean-backup and finite-time limitations.  
- Strengths: clear SM-MCTS formalization, almost-sure convergence under transparent assumptions, useful decomposition, and supportive empirical validation.  
- Weaknesses/questions: clarify which technical hurdles are specific to simultaneous moves, explain/empirically compare UCB failures, and examine extending analysis to sample backups.  
- Additional weaknesses: lack of finite-time bounds, sensitivity to exploration rate γ, unclear update behavior on exploration steps, and limited experimental breadth.  
- Recommended improvements: derive conservative finite-time bounds from known regrets, clarify exploration/update protocol, test larger/structured games, and compare to backward-induction or double-oracle baselines.  
- Pros for acceptance: first convergence guarantee in this setting, general template with common no-regret algorithms, clear exposition, and matching experiments.  
- Cons for acceptance: moderate technical novelty, no finite-time guarantees, limited experiments, and reliance on mean backups.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept, contingent on clarifying theorem/bounds, exploration/backup details, and expanding analysis or experiments.
