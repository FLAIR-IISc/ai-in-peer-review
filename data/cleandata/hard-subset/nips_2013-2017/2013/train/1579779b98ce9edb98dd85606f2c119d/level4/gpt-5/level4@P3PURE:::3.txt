This paper analyzes a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithms that employ, with mild restrictions, any no-regret selection method. The main theoretical contribution is a proof of asymptotic convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A key weakness is that the convergence guarantee is purely asymptotic—no rates are provided. This appears unavoidable under the current analysis, since the selection method is assumed only to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this limitation is understandable, rates matter in practice, especially given that backward induction yields an exact equilibrium in these settings. Can the authors characterize convergence rates when the selection method has a specific regret rate—for example, when using a method with the typical sqrt(T) regret rate (as in exp)?

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values (as assumed in the analysis) versus the standard approach of propagating the most recent sample. The results indicate that mean values slightly underperform. The authors also attempt to glean empirical insight into convergence rates.

Overall assessment: The paper is well written and technically sound. I have a mild reservation about its ultimate impact due to the lack of convergence rates. On the one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence in the simultaneous-move setting is a worthwhile first step, particularly given the absence of prior proofs for this case (to the best of my knowledge). On the other hand, convergence guarantees for MCTS with sequential moves already exist, so the contribution feels somewhat incremental without rate results.

Minor comments and nitpicks:
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Line 144: “negated,” not “inverted,” correct?
- Algorithm 1, line 10: (a1, a2) should likely be (i, j), or possibly (σ1, σ2). This is especially confusing since a_{i,j} was introduced as the payoff notation for a single-stage matrix game.
- Line 172: The notation appears to keep hiccuping from (i, j) to a’s; please standardize throughout.

In sum, this is a well-executed and enjoyable paper to read. Clarifying or deriving convergence rates under standard regret assumptions would substantially strengthen its impact.
