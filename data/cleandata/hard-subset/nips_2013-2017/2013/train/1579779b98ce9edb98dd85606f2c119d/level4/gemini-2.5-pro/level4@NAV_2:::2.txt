This paper studies the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. This class of games, which includes examples like Goofspiel, has been successfully approached with MCTS empirically, but a formal understanding of the algorithm's convergence has been lacking. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with various selection mechanisms. The main contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the average strategies produced by MCTS converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The proof is built inductively, treating nodes as repeated matrix games where subgame values introduce bounded error. The paper validates these theoretical claims with experiments using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods, comparing performance on random and empirically-derived worst-case games. This work extends preliminary empirical studies, such as Lanctot et al. (2013), by providing the first formal convergence guarantees.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper's primary contribution is a novel and important theoretical result. It addresses a significant open question by providing the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in this class of games. This places a widely-used heuristic algorithm on a firm theoretical foundation from the perspective of algorithmic game theory.
*   **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-constructed. The argument is developed clearly, starting from foundational results in repeated matrix games and carefully extending them to handle the bounded errors that arise from subgame estimations in a multi-stage setting. The empirical evaluation is thorough, not only confirming the main theoretical claim but also providing valuable insights into practical variants, such as the effect of propagating mean values versus current sample values.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The problem setting, algorithm template, and theoretical analysis are presented with precision, making the work accessible and the line of reasoning easy to follow.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The analysis focuses on asymptotic convergence, proving that the algorithm eventually converges to an approximate equilibrium. However, the work does not provide theoretical bounds on the *rate* of convergence. While the empirical results suggest a reasonable convergence speed, finite-time performance guarantees would significantly strengthen the paper's impact. As the authors note this is future work, a brief discussion on the challenges in deriving such bounds would be a welcome addition.
*   The bound on the approximation quality for the subgame perfect equilibrium is shown to have a quadratic dependency on the game's maximum depth, D. For games with significant depth, this bound may be quite loose. It would improve the paper to include a short discussion on whether this quadratic dependency is believed to be an artifact of the current proof technique or a more fundamental property of the problem.
*   The formal proof makes the simplifying assumption that the game tree is complete to analyze the long-term collection of statistics. While this is a standard and acceptable technique for an asymptotic proof, a brief comment on the pre-asymptotic regime, where statistics are collected concurrently with the tree's expansion, could provide additional practical context.

In summary, the paper presents a valuable and novel contribution to the understanding of MCTS algorithms.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   The theoretical framework is general and applicable to any ε-Hannan consistent selection algorithm.
*   The claims are well-supported by both a rigorous proof and a solid empirical evaluation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis is asymptotic and lacks finite-time bounds on the convergence rate.
*   The derived approximation bound's dependence on game depth may be loose, limiting its practical predictive power for deep games.
