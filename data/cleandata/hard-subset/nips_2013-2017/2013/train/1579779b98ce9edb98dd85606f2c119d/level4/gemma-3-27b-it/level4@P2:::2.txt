This paper analyzes zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, a class that includes games like Goofspiel and Tron. The authors investigate Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) implemented with regret-matching, and demonstrate convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. 

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with results presented effectively. The primary contribution lies in the formal analysis of MCTS in this game class. While prior work, such as Lanctot et al. (2013) [20], has explored this area, this paper provides more rigorous analytical results. The proof strategy, employing induction with optimal strategies at the leaf nodes, is a sound approach. 

However, a key limitation of this type of analysis is the difficulty in deriving strong results about the inner nodes of the game tree. This overlooks a central concept in game theory: subgame perfect Nash equilibria. Strengthening the connection between the analysis and subgame perfection would significantly enhance the paper’s theoretical grounding. I recommend the authors consider citing Harris (1990), “Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves,” which provides relevant background.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper presents new formal analysis of MCTS convergence for simultaneous-move games.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Soundness:** The proof strategy appears valid and well-explained.
*   **Relevance:** The work addresses a relevant problem in algorithmic game theory and has connections to practical applications.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis doesn’t fully leverage the concept of subgame perfect Nash equilibria, a core element of game-theoretic analysis.
*   **Theoretical Depth:** While the convergence result is valuable, further exploration of the convergence *rate* or more refined approximation bounds would increase the contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the understanding of MCTS in a challenging game setting. While the analysis could be strengthened by more explicitly addressing subgame perfection, the existing results are valuable and well-supported. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors considering the suggested citation and potentially addressing the limitations outlined above. The work is a positive step towards bridging the gap between algorithmic game theory and classical game-theoretic concepts.



**References:**

*   Lanctot, M., Lisý, V., & Winands, M. H. M. (2013). Monte Carlo tree search in simultaneous move games with applications to Goofspiel. *Workshop on Computer Games at IJCAI*.
