The authors present an analysis for a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using essentially any no-regret selection method, with some mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash in the case of simultaneous moves.

One weakness is that the convergence is asymptotic, with no rates provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis, since the selection method is assumed to be only epsilon-Hannan consistent. Although this might not be detrimental, as there appear to be no existing proofs of convergence for MCTS methods for extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of rates is important. Given that backwards induction yields an exact equilibrium anyway, it would be valuable to know the rates attained when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method. For instance, what rates are achieved when using the \sqrt{T} guaranteed by exp3?

The authors conclude with some experiments, comparing the approach of propagating mean reward values used in their analysis to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. Additionally, they attempt to experimentally derive some insight into the convergence rate.

To conclude, this is a well-written paper that is technically correct. I have a mild reservation regarding the ultimate significance of the work, given the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step, even if rates do not follow from the authors' analysis. On the other hand, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, making the result feel somewhat incremental.

Nitpicks:
Figure 1: "leafs" should be "leaves".
Line 144: The term "negated" is more accurate than "inverted".
Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation should be (i,j) or possibly (\sigma1,\sigma2), rather than (a1,a2), to avoid confusion with the previously introduced notation for payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
Line 172: There appears to be a notation inconsistency, switching from (i,j) to a's, which should be edited throughout the paper. Despite this, the paper is well-written and enjoyable to read. However, I have some reservations about its impact due to the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
