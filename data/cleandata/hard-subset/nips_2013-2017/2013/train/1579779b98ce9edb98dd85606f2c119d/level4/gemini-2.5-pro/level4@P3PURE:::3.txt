This paper presents a theoretical analysis for a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms that can employ any selection method with the property of no-regret, subject to some mild restrictions. The primary contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium for extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A key limitation of the analysis is that the convergence is asymptotic; no convergence rates are provided. This is an inherent consequence of the general approach, which only assumes that the selection method is epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this is a notable weakness, the contribution is still significant, as to my knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for any MCTS method in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

The question of convergence rates is critical, however, as algorithms like backward induction can compute an exact equilibrium. The practical utility of an MCTS approach often lies in its anytime performance. Could the authors comment on the convergence rates that might be achievable if a specific selection method with a known regret bound is used? For instance, what rate would their analysis yield if one assumes a selection method with a regret of O(√T), such as the one guaranteed by the EXP3 algorithm?

The paper includes experiments comparing the propagation of mean reward values (as used in the theoretical analysis) against the standard approach of propagating single sample values. The results indicate that using mean values leads to slightly worse empirical performance. The authors also present experiments aimed at providing empirical insight into the algorithm's convergence rate.

In summary, this is a well-written and technically sound paper. The main contribution, a proof of asymptotic convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, is a valuable first step, especially given that MCTS algorithms are known to perform well in practice. However, I have a mild reservation about the ultimate impact of the work due to the lack of convergence rates. Because convergence results are already established for MCTS in sequential-move games, this contribution, while novel, may be viewed as somewhat incremental.
