

This paper examines zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, including notable examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) implemented with regret-matching, proving convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for this class. The paper is clearly written, with results presented effectively. The primary contribution lies in its formal analysis, extending prior work by Lanctot et al. (2013) [20], which provided only preliminary analysis of the same setup. The proof employs induction, relying on optimal play at terminal nodes.

However, the main limitation of this approach is its failure to yield meaningful insights about intermediate game states. By focusing solely on terminal convergence, the analysis overlooks a fundamental concept in game theory: subgame perfect Nash equilibria. This omission is significant, as subgame perfection provides a rigorous framework for analyzing strategic behavior at every decision point in the game tree—a cornerstone of game-theoretic analysis for sequential and simultaneous-move games alike. The authors would substantially strengthen their work by explicitly connecting their regret-matching/MCTS convergence results to subgame perfect equilibria. 

The seminal paper "Existence of Subgame Perfect Equilibria in Games with Simultaneous Moves" by Harris (1990) establishes this connection for simultaneous-move games and offers a well-developed theoretical foundation. Integrating this perspective would elevate the paper from a computational contribution to a more theoretically grounded analysis, aligning it with established game theory methodology. While the computational novelty of applying regret-matching to MCTS in this context is noteworthy, the current analysis remains disconnected from the powerful tools game theorists have long used to analyze such games. The authors should address this gap to fully realize the paper’s potential.
