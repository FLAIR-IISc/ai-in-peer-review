- Paper proposes a general SM-MCTS template using any ε-Hannan consistent selection rule with sufficient exploration for simultaneous-move, perfect-information games.  
- Main theoretical result: induced dynamics converge to an approximate (subgame-perfect) Nash equilibrium, with approximation error growing with game depth.  
- Proof strategy: inductive argument over the game tree, starting from leaf matrix games and propagating bounded errors upward.  
- Instantiations and experiments: authors implement regret matching and Exp3, and empirically evaluate convergence on random and adversarial small games, comparing mean vs. sample-value backups.  
- Relation to prior work: complements negative results for UCB/UCT in simultaneous-move games and builds on online learning (Hannan consistency) literature.  
- Strength: clear, general convergence guarantee under broad selection conditions; assumptions and algorithms are explicit.  
- Strength: sound, well-organized proof strategy and reasonable assumptions for the setting.  
- Strength: empirical results support theory and provide insights into practical choices (mean vs. sample backups; algorithm comparisons).  
- Weakness: no finite-time bounds; results are asymptotic and approximation scales with depth, possibly loose for practical games.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited to small, synthetic games and lacks benchmarks from established simultaneous-move domains.  
- Weakness: proofs assume the full tree is built temporarily; interaction between tree growth and nonstationary payoffs needs more discussion.  
- Suggestion: quantify finite-time rates or discuss how error constants depend on exploration γ and depth D.  
- Suggestion: add comparisons to alternative solvers (e.g., backward induction with LP/double oracle, CFR-style baselines) and larger benchmarks.  
- Suggestion: clarify link to classical subgame-perfect equilibrium literature (e.g., cite Harris 1990) and make the subgame-perfect claim more explicit.  
- Suggestion: provide practical guidance on choosing exploration γ and discuss mean backups’ impact on speed vs. bias.  
- Argument for acceptance: novel, general convergence proof for an important class of games where UCT can fail; conceptually clean reduction to online learning guarantees.  
- Argument against acceptance: lack of finite-time bounds and limited empirical scope may reduce practical significance.  
- Quality assessment: strong theoretical contribution; empirical validation is suggestive but limited; missing finite-time analysis.  
- Clarity assessment: generally clear and well-structured but should emphasize the subgame-perfect aspect and its classical context.  
- Originality assessment: high—first general convergence proof for SM-MCTS under ε-Hannan consistent selection with exploration.  
- Significance assessment: meaningful for theory and practice, though broader impact depends on finite-time results and larger experiments.
