This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) combined with regret-matching for solving zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games, a class that includes notable examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium within this game class, building upon and extending prior work, such as the preliminary analysis presented in Lanctot et al. (2013) [20]. The paper is clearly written and the results are well-presented, making the core arguments accessible. The main contribution lies in these new analytical results regarding the behavior of regret-matching within the MCTS framework.

The proof relies on an inductive argument, leveraging the assumption of optimal strategies at the leaf nodes of the search tree. While this approach provides valuable insights, a key limitation – and, in my view, the primary weakness of this line of reasoning – is the difficulty in drawing strong conclusions about the behavior of the algorithm at the *inner* nodes of the game tree. This omission represents a significant disconnect from core concepts in game theory, specifically the notion of subgame perfect Nash equilibria. 

Zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games are naturally amenable to subgame perfect equilibrium analysis. Focusing solely on the overall convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, without relating it to subgame optimality, feels incomplete. A richer analysis would explore how the regret-matching dynamics and MCTS structure influence the emergence of subgame perfect strategies. Establishing connections to this existing theoretical framework would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution and broaden its appeal to the game theory community. 

To that end, I recommend the authors consider citing Harris (1990), "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves.” This work offers a relevant perspective on the broader theoretical landscape of simultaneous-move games and could inform a discussion of how the presented MCTS-regret matching approach relates to established solution concepts.

In summary, this paper offers a novel and valuable analysis of regret-matching with MCTS in a specific class of games. While the results are interesting from a computational perspective, their impact would be substantially enhanced by explicitly addressing and incorporating the well-developed theoretical tools of subgame perfect Nash equilibria. Despite this weakness, the clarity and rigor of the presentation are commendable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the connection to subgame perfect equilibria.
