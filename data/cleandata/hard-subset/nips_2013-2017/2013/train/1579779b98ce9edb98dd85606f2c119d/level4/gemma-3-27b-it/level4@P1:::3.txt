The authors present an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms, showing that using essentially any no-regret selection method leads to convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in games with simultaneous moves. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of this convergence, filling a gap in the existing literature.

One potential weakness is that the convergence result is asymptotic, without providing specific rates. This is a natural consequence of assuming only epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. Nevertheless, given that convergence proofs for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves are currently lacking, this result is valuable.

However, the question of convergence rates is important. While backward induction can yield an exact equilibrium, understanding the rate at which MCTS approaches an approximate equilibrium is crucial. Do the authors have any insights into the rates attained when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method, such as the √T guarantee provided by Exp3?

The authors complement their theoretical analysis with experiments. They compare propagating mean reward values (as used in their analysis) to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value, finding a slight performance difference experimentally. They also attempt to assess convergence rates empirically.

In conclusion, this is a technically sound and well-written paper. My main reservation concerns the ultimate significance of the work given the absence of convergence rates in the analysis. On the one hand, MCTS methods are known to perform well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step. On the other hand, convergence results exist for MCTS with sequential moves, making this result somewhat incremental. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Fills a gap in the literature by providing the first convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-presented.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The work is clearly written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The convergence result is asymptotic and lacks specific rates.
*   The incremental nature of the result, given existing work on sequential-move games, could limit its impact.

**Nitpicks:**

*   Figure 1: "leafs" should be "leaves".
*   Line 144: “negated” is more appropriate than “inverted”.
*   Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1, a2) is confusing, and should be (i, j) or potentially (σ1, σ2) for clarity, especially given the earlier introduction of a<sub>i,j</sub> as payoffs in a matrix game.
*   Throughout the paper, there are some inconsistencies in notation (switching between (i, j) and a's). These should be carefully reviewed and edited for consistency.

Despite these minor points, this was an enjoyable paper to read, and I believe it represents a solid contribution to the field. I have some reservations about its overall impact, given the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
