- Core contribution: proves convergence of MCTS to an approximate Nash equilibrium in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous-move games using regret-minimizing selection rules meeting consistency requirements.
- Novelty: first theoretical convergence guarantee for MCTS in this specific simultaneous-move setting.
- Theoretical foundation: rigorous analysis built on ε-Hannan consistency and culminates in Theorem 4.11.
- Empirical support: experiments confirm theoretical findings, compare mean vs sample reward propagation, and explore worst-case games.
- Literature positioning: well-situated within prior work (UCT, security games, general game playing) and clearly motivates the gap addressed.
- Strength — technical soundness: formal proofs are solid and grounded in online learning theory.
- Strength — clarity: generally well-written with clear notation and progressive argument build-up.
- Major limitation: no convergence rates provided, reducing practical impact and leaving open how quickly MCTS approaches backward-induction solutions.
- Suggested future direction: relate selection-method regret rates (e.g., Exp3’s √T) to MCTS convergence rates.
- Weakness — presentation: some notational inconsistencies (use of (i,j) vs a’s) that can confuse readers.
- Weakness — accessibility: Section 4 proofs are dense and assume substantial background; more intuitive explanations would help.
- Minor issues: typos and wording errors (e.g., “leafs”→“leaves”, “inverted”→“negated”) and unclear notation in Algorithm 1.
- Acceptance arguments: novelty, technical rigor, clarity, empirical validation, and significance justify acceptance despite limitations.
- Rejection arguments: lack of convergence rates, perceived incremental advance over sequential-game MCTS results, and minor notation problems.
- Overall recommendation: accept — valuable theoretical advance that opens avenues for tighter convergence bounds and further work.
