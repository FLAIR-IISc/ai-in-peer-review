The authors present an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using essentially any no-regret selection method, with some mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in the case of simultaneous moves.

One weakness of the paper is that the convergence is asymptotic, with no rates provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis, as the selection method is assumed to be only epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this may not be detrimental, given the lack of existing proofs of convergence for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of rates is important. Notably, backwards induction can provide an exact equilibrium, making the rate of convergence a relevant consideration. It would be interesting to know whether the authors can derive rates of convergence when a specific regret rate is assumed for the selection method, such as the \sqrt{T} rate guaranteed by exp3.

The authors also present experimental results, comparing the approach of propagating mean reward values used in their analysis to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. Additionally, they attempt to experimentally derive some insight into the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper that is technically correct. However, I have a mild reservation regarding the ultimate significance of the work due to the lack of convergence rates. On one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step, even if rates do not follow from the authors' analysis. On the other hand, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, making this result feel somewhat incremental.

Despite these minor issues, the paper is well-written and enjoyable to read. My reservations about its impact stem from the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
