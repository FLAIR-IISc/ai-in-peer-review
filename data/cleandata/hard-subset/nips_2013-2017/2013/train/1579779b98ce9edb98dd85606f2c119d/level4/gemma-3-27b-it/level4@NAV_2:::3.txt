The authors present an analysis for a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms utilizing, with some mild restrictions, any no-regret selection method. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in the context of simultaneous moves.

A potential weakness lies in the asymptotic nature of the convergence result—no convergence rates are provided. This is a natural consequence of the authors’ analysis, which relies on the assumption of epsilon-Hannan consistency for the selection method. However, establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves remains an open problem, and this work addresses it. 

Nevertheless, the question of convergence rates is important. While backward induction provides an exact equilibrium, understanding the rates attainable with specific regret bounds for the selection method (e.g., the \sqrt{T} guarantee of Exp3) would be valuable. Do the authors have insights into this matter?

The authors complement their theoretical analysis with empirical evaluations. They compare propagating mean reward values—consistent with their analysis—to the standard practice of propagating the current sample value, finding a slight performance difference. They also attempt to experimentally assess the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a well-written and technically sound paper. A reservation regarding the overall significance of the work arises from the lack of convergence rate analysis. MCTS methods are known to perform well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step. However, convergence results already exist for MCTS with sequential moves, making the contribution somewhat incremental. 

Minor points for consideration:

*   Figure 1: “leafs” should be “leaves”.
*   Line 144: “negated” is more appropriate than “inverted.”
*   Line 10 of Algorithm 1: The notation (a1, a2) is potentially confusing and should be (i, j) or, perhaps, (\sigma1, \sigma2), to align with the earlier introduction of a_{i,j} as payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
*   Line 172 and elsewhere: Notation inconsistencies between (i, j) and ‘a’ should be reviewed and rectified throughout the paper.

Overall, this was a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading. My reservations about its potential impact stem from the absence of convergence rates within the analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides the first theoretical convergence guarantee for a class of MCTS algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.
*   **Soundness:** The technical analysis appears rigorous and well-justified.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized, making the complex theoretical concepts accessible.
*   **Significance:** Establishing even asymptotic convergence is a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of MCTS, particularly in a challenging setting like simultaneous-move games.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of rates:** The absence of convergence rate analysis limits the practical implications of the theoretical result.
*   **Incremental nature:** Given existing convergence results for sequential-move games, the contribution feels somewhat incremental, though still valuable.
*   **Minor notational issues:** Several inconsistencies in notation could hinder readability.
