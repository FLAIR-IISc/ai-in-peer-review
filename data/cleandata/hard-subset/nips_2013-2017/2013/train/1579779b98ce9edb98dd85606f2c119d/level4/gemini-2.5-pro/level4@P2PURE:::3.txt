This paper provides a convergence analysis for a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms that can employ, with mild restrictions, any no-regret selection method. The primary theoretical contribution is a proof that this approach converges to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.

A key limitation of the analysis is that the convergence is asymptotic; no convergence rates are provided. This is an understandable consequence of the general framework, which only assumes the selection method to be epsilon-Hannan consistent. To my knowledge, this may be the first convergence proof for MCTS in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, making this a valuable contribution despite the lack of rates.

Nevertheless, the question of convergence rates is important, particularly since alternative methods like backwards induction can compute an exact equilibrium. The practical utility of MCTS often hinges on its convergence speed. Could the authors comment on whether convergence rates can be derived if a specific selection method with a known regret bound is assumed? For instance, what rate would be achieved by employing a method with a regret of O(√T)?

The paper includes experiments that compare two value propagation strategies: using the mean reward (as in the theoretical analysis) versus using the current sample value (the standard approach). The results show that propagating mean values slightly underperforms the standard method. The authors also present experiments aimed at providing empirical insight into the convergence rate.

In summary, this is a well-written and technically sound paper. My primary reservation concerns the work's ultimate significance due to the absence of convergence rates. On one hand, establishing asymptotic convergence is a valuable first step, as MCTS methods are known to perform well in practice. On the other hand, since convergence results already exist for MCTS in sequential-move games, this contribution feels somewhat incremental.
