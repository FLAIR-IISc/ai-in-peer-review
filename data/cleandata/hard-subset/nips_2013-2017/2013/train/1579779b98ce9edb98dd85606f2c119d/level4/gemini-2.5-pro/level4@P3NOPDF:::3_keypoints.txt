- Presents theoretical analysis of a general class of MCTS algorithms for two-player zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves.  
- Key novelty: extends MCTS convergence analysis to games with simultaneous moves.  
- Framework is general and accommodates any node selection method that is Hannan consistent (no-regret).  
- Main theoretical contribution: proof that these algorithms converge asymptotically to an ε‑Nash equilibrium.  
- Paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis appears sound.  
- Major strength: broad applicability due to leveraging Hannan consistency, covering many MCTS variants.  
- To the reviewer’s knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games.  
- Primary weakness: results are purely asymptotic—no convergence rates are provided.  
- Absence of rates limits practical utility and makes it hard to compare to exact methods (e.g., backward induction).  
- Without rates, one cannot quantify how many simulations are needed to reach a target ε.  
- Important suggested extension: derive convergence rates under specific selection methods with known regret bounds (e.g., Hedge with O(√T) regret).  
- Experiments compare mean-reward propagation (theory) vs direct sample-value heuristic; the heuristic slightly outperforms the theory-supported variant.  
- Experimental analysis of convergence rates is useful but underscores the missing theoretical guarantees.  
- Overall: technically sound and addresses an important gap, but lack of quantitative rates limits impact; adding rate analyses under stronger assumptions would strengthen the paper.  
- Minor comments/typos: plural of "leaf" is "leaves"; use "negated" instead of "inverted" for opponent payoffs; notation clashes (a1,a2 vs i,j) and inconsistent action notation throughout.
