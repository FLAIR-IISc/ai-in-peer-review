This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for the class of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with any no-regret learning algorithm as the action selection method.

The main contribution is a proof that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. This provides a significant theoretical justification for using MCTS in this class of games, for which, to my knowledge, no prior convergence guarantees existed. The authors support their theoretical claims with an empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods. The experiments confirm the convergence and also investigate a practical variant of MCTS (propagating the current sample value instead of the mean) that is not covered by the proof but is shown to converge empirically.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. It provides the first formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While MCTS and its variants are widely used in practice, their theoretical underpinnings, especially outside of sequential, perfect-information games, are not well-established. This work makes a solid contribution by closing an important theoretical gap.

2.  **Generality of the Framework:** The theoretical analysis is not tied to a single algorithm but applies to a general class of selection methods based on ε-Hannan consistency. This provides a robust and extensible framework for designing and analyzing future MCTS algorithms for this game class.

3.  **Technical Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound and very well-written. The proof is built up logically, starting from the known properties of no-regret learning in single-stage matrix games and extending them inductively to the multi-stage, extensive-form setting. The argument is clear and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance):**

1.  **Lack of Convergence Rates:** The main weakness of the analysis is that it is purely asymptotic; no convergence rates are provided. The practical utility of an approximation algorithm like MCTS is largely determined by how quickly it converges to a good solution. For the class of games studied, exact solvers based on backward induction exist. The motivation for using MCTS is to tackle games that are too large for these exact methods. Without convergence rates, it is difficult to theoretically assess the trade-off between computation time and solution quality compared to other methods or to understand how the complexity of the game (e.g., depth, branching factor) affects performance.

2.  **Incremental Nature of the Contribution:** While the result is new for this specific game class, the analytical approach combines established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency, no-regret dynamics) with the MCTS framework. The extension of convergence results from sequential to simultaneous-move games is a valuable step, but it could be viewed as an incremental advance rather than a breakthrough.

**Questions for the Authors:**

The lack of convergence rates appears to be a necessary consequence of assuming only generic ε-Hannan consistency for the selection method. Could your analysis be extended to provide explicit convergence rates if a specific selection algorithm with a known regret bound is used? For instance, by using Exp3, which guarantees an O(√T) regret bound in the underlying matrix games, would it be possible to derive a convergence rate for the overall MCTS algorithm in the extensive-form game? Providing such a result would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Conclusion:**

This is a well-written and technically correct paper that addresses an important theoretical question. Establishing the asymptotic convergence of MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a valuable first step and a good contribution to the literature. My main reservation is about the significance of an asymptotic result without convergence rates, especially when exact solvers are available for this game class.

Despite this limitation, the novelty of being the first to prove any form of convergence in this setting is a strong argument for acceptance. The work provides a solid foundation upon which future research into finite-time bounds can be built.
