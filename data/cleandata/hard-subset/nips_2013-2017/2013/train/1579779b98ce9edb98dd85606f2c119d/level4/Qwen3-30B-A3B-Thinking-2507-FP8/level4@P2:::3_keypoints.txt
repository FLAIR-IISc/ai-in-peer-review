- Presents a formal analysis of MCTS for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves.  
- Introduces a general MCTS template that can use any regret-minimizing selection method for matrix games.  
- Main theoretical result: if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures exploration, MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium.  
- Empirically validates theory using regret matching and Exp3 on random and worst-case games, confirming convergence to approximate equilibria.  
- First formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, filling a gap in the literature.  
- Technical content is sound with well-structured proofs that extend from matrix games to extensive-form games via induction.  
- Empirical finding: mean-propagation (required by theory) converges slightly slower than standard current-sample propagation, though both converge.  
- Primary limitation: convergence is asymptotic with no explicit convergence rates provided.  
- Authors do not show whether known regret rates (e.g., O(√T) for Exp3) translate into meaningful MCTS convergence rates.  
- Practical relevance is unclear because backward induction computes exact equilibria in polynomial time, so rates are needed to justify MCTS use.  
- Contribution is incremental—extends known sequential-move MCTS guarantees to simultaneous-move games but is not groundbreaking.  
- Paper would be stronger with a more compelling discussion of practical implications and applications of the theoretical guarantees.  
- Minor revisions suggested: change "leafs" to "leaves"; use "negated" instead of "inverted" on line 144; fix confusing notation in Algorithm 1 line 10.  
- Strengths: first formal analysis for simultaneous-move MCTS, technically sound proofs, solid empirical support, and clear writing/organization.  
- Weaknesses: asymptotic-only convergence, limited justification of significance over existing MCTS results, and limited discussion of real-world impact.  
- Overall recommendation: suitable for publication (NIPS); valuable theoretical foundation that motivates future work on convergence rates and practical applications.
