This paper provides a convergence analysis for a class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms designed for extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. The framework is general, accommodating any node selection method that is Hannan consistent (i.e., has no regret), subject to some mild restrictions. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that this class of algorithms converges to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium.

A key strength of this work is that it appears to be the first to establish a convergence guarantee for MCTS in this game setting. However, a significant limitation of the analysis is that the convergence is purely asymptotic; no convergence rates are provided. This is an understandable consequence of the general assumption that the node selection method is merely Hannan consistent, without specifying a particular rate of regret.

The absence of convergence rates raises questions about the practical utility of the algorithm compared to alternatives. For instance, backward induction can compute an exact Nash equilibrium for games of this type. The primary appeal of a sampling-based method like MCTS is its potential to find good approximate solutions much faster in large games. Without convergence rates, it is difficult to quantify this advantage. This leads to a crucial question: What convergence rates can be established if a specific online learning algorithm with a known regret bound is used for node selection? For example, if one were to use an algorithm like EXP3, which guarantees regret on the order of O(sqrt(T)), could a corresponding rate of convergence to an epsilon-Nash equilibrium be derived for the overall MCTS procedure?

The experimental section evaluates two aspects of the proposed method. First, the authors compare the performance of propagating mean reward values (the variant analyzed in the paper) against the standard MCTS approach of propagating the terminal sample value from a single playout. The results show that using mean values, while necessary for the theoretical analysis, slightly underperforms the standard method in practice. Second, the authors provide an empirical investigation into the convergence rate, offering some initial insights where the theory is silent.

In summary, this is a well-written and technically sound paper that addresses an important gap in the MCTS literature. Establishing asymptotic convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a valuable first step, especially given the empirical success of these methods. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by a reservation about the work's overall impact. The lack of convergence rates makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's efficiency, and since convergence results for sequential-move games already exist, this contribution feels somewhat incremental. An analysis that incorporates specific regret bounds to derive explicit convergence rates would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
