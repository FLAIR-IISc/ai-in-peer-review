This paper analyzes the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors introduce a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for action selection. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection method is epsilon-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, the average strategies produced by the algorithm converge to a subgame perfect epsilon-Nash equilibrium. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as prior to this, formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games were not known, despite their practical application. The theoretical analysis is complemented by an empirical study on randomly generated and worst-case games, comparing different MCTS variants.

**Strengths and weaknesses of the paper:**

The primary strength of this work is its novelty and theoretical contribution. It provides the first formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games. This is an important and non-trivial result that provides a solid theoretical foundation for using MCTS in this domain. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and the proof structure, which relies on induction over the game tree, is clearly explained. The generalization to any epsilon-Hannan consistent selection algorithm is a particularly strong aspect of the analysis.

The main weakness, however, concerns the significance of the result, stemming from the fact that the convergence proof is purely asymptotic and provides no finite-time convergence rates. For this class of games, exact equilibria can be computed in polynomial time via backward induction. The primary motivation for using an approximation algorithm like MCTS is to find a good solution faster. Without convergence rates, it is difficult to theoretically assess the trade-off between MCTS and exact methods. This raises the question: can the authors' analysis be extended to provide explicit rates if the selection method has a known regret bound (e.g., the O(√T) regret of Exp3)? Providing such an analysis would substantially elevate the paper's impact.

The experimental section is a valuable addition. The authors empirically confirm their theoretical claims and astutely compare the performance of propagating mean rewards (as required by their proof) versus propagating the current sample's value (a common heuristic). The finding that the theoretically grounded approach is slightly slower in practice is an interesting result.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games, a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly written, and the analysis is general, applying to a wide class of no-regret selection methods.
*   The paper addresses a clear gap between the practical use of MCTS in these games and the lack of theoretical guarantees.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The convergence is only shown asymptotically, with no finite-time rates. This limits the ability to compare its performance against existing exact algorithms and thus tempers the work's practical significance.
*   Given that convergence results for MCTS in sequential games are already known, this contribution could be viewed as a somewhat incremental, albeit important, extension to the simultaneous-move setting.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a high-quality paper that presents a valuable and technically correct theoretical result. I enjoyed reading it. While I have reservations about its ultimate impact due to the lack of convergence rates, establishing asymptotic convergence is a crucial first step and a solid contribution to the field.
