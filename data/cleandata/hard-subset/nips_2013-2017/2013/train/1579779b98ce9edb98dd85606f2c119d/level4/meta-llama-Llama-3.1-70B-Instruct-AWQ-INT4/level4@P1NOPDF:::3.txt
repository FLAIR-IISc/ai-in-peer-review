The authors present an analysis of a class of Monte Carlo tree search algorithms using essentially any no-regret selection method, with some mild restrictions. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to epsilon-Nash in the case of simultaneous moves.

One weakness is that the convergence is asymptotic, with no rates provided. This is a necessary consequence of the authors' analysis, as the selection method is assumed to be only epsilon-Hannan consistent. While this may not be detrimental, given the absence of existing convergence proofs for MCTS methods in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, the question of rates is important. We know that backwards induction yields an exact equilibrium, so it is worth exploring whether specific regret rates for the selection method, such as the \sqrt{T} guarantee provided by exponential weights, would yield corresponding convergence rates.

The authors conclude with some experiments comparing the approach of propagating mean reward values, as used in their analysis, to the standard approach of propagating the current sample value. They find that mean values slightly underperform experimentally. Additionally, they attempt to experimentally derive some insight into the convergence rate.

In conclusion, this is a well-written paper that is technically correct. However, I have a mild reservation regarding the ultimate significance of the work due to the lack of convergence rates. On the one hand, MCTS methods are known to work well in practice, so establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step, even if rates do not follow from the authors' analysis. On the other hand, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, making the result feel somewhat incremental.

Overall, this was a well-written paper that I enjoyed reading, but I have some reservations about its impact due to the lack of convergence rates in the analysis.
