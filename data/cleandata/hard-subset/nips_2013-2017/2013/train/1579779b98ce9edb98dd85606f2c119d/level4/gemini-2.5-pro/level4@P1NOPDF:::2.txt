This paper analyzes the class of zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games, which includes interesting examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors investigate Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) implemented with regret-matching for this class of games and prove its convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium.

The paper is clear, well-written, and the results are presented effectively. The main contribution is a novel formal analysis that builds upon previous work, such as Lanctot et al. [20], which explored a similar setup but offered a more preliminary analysis. The convergence proof proceeds by induction, assuming optimal strategies at the leaf nodes of the game tree.

However, a significant weakness of this approach is its limited ability to yield strong theoretical guarantees for the inner nodes of the game tree. As a result, the analysis does not engage with key concepts from game theory, specifically subgame perfect Nash equilibria. For games with perfect information, subgame perfection is a crucial refinement that ensures strategies remain optimal throughout the entire game, not just from the outset.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by connecting its analysis to these powerful, established concepts. The current contribution is framed primarily from a computational standpoint, but its impact would be greater if it were situated within the broader context of game-theoretic solution concepts. For example, the authors may wish to consider citing and discussing relevant literature, such as "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves" by C. Harris (1990).

In summary, this paper presents a valuable computational analysis of MCTS with regret-matching in an important class of games. While the results are interesting, the analysis misses an opportunity to connect with the well-studied theory of subgame perfect equilibria, which would make the contribution more complete and impactful.
