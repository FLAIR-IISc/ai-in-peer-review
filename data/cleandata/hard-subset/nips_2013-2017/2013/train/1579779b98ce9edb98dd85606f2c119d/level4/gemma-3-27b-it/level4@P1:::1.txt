This paper studies Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. It proves that MCTS algorithms converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium under certain conditions, and empirical studies support this formal result. 

Overall, I believe this is a solid paper that provides a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS algorithms. The result is valuable and the presentation is generally clear. However, given that the main contribution lies in the theoretical analysis, I have some reservations regarding the novelty of the proof technique.

Here are some detailed comments:

1. The paper establishes a convergence condition for MCTS, which is a significant contribution. The results are clearly presented and well-motivated within the context of game theory and MCTS. However, exploring how this result compares to existing convergence analyses (even in related domains) would strengthen the paper.

2. Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithms are widely used in MCTS. While the authors focus on algorithms guaranteeing Hannan consistency, given the existing analysis of UCB for Trees, it would be beneficial to discuss UCB algorithms and compare their performance to the methods explored in this paper. A brief discussion explaining *why* UCB is not covered by the theoretical framework would also be useful.

3. The formal analysis demonstrates the benefit of propagating the mean value during the MCTS simulation. However, the experiments suggest that propagating the current sample value also performs well under various criteria. An explanation for this empirical observation would be valuable. Does the convergence rate differ significantly? Is the difference more pronounced in specific game structures?

4. The paper does a good job of relating the work to prior research in game theory and MCTS. However, more specific citations to related work on convergence analysis of tree search algorithms (even outside of game playing) could further position the contribution.

5. While the paper provides a strong theoretical foundation, further exploration of practical implications and limitations would be helpful. For example, how sensitive is the convergence rate to the choice of the Hannan consistency parameter ε? What types of games pose the greatest challenges for this approach?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in a challenging game setting (simultaneous moves).
*   The empirical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The work builds upon and connects to important research areas in game theory and artificial intelligence.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The novelty of the proof technique is not fully established.
*   The analysis does not cover widely used algorithms like UCB.
*   The empirical analysis could be expanded to provide deeper insights into the practical performance of the algorithms.

**Overall assessment:**

This paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field. While not groundbreaking, it offers a valuable theoretical result and provides empirical validation. I would lean towards acceptance, provided the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty of the proof and the omission of UCB algorithms, and perhaps expand the discussion of the empirical results.

This paper studies Monte Carlo tree search in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The findings are interesting, but technically speaking, the paper is not exceptionally outstanding.
