This paper analyzes a broad class of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, assuming the selection rule at each node is any no-regret (ε–Hannan consistent) algorithm under mild conditions. The main theoretical result is asymptotic convergence to an ε–Nash equilibrium.

Strengths
- Generality: The analysis applies to essentially any no-regret selection method, which makes the result modular and relevant to many MCTS variants.
- Novelty in setting: To my knowledge, this provides the first proof of asymptotic convergence for MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games, where the usual sequential-move analyses do not directly apply.
- Technical correctness and clarity: The core arguments are well structured, and the paper is overall clearly written.

Weaknesses and questions
- Finite-time rates: The convergence guarantee is asymptotic, and no nonasymptotic rates are provided. This appears inherent to relying only on ε–Hannan consistency. However, given that backward induction yields an exact equilibrium in these games, rates are critical for understanding when and why one would prefer MCTS in practice.
  - Can the authors derive finite-sample exploitability bounds when the selection rule has a known regret rate, e.g., O(√T) for Hedge/EXP3 or O(log T) in favorable settings? In particular, how does the tree structure (depth, branching factor, number of simultaneous-move nodes) compound the regret into exploitability at the root?
  - Is there a separation between using mean-value backups versus single-sample backups for rates? The experiments suggest mean backups slightly underperform; theory explaining when mean backups help or hurt would be informative.
  - What role do exploration parameters play in any finite-time bound (e.g., uniform exploration to control bias in value propagation)?
- Scope and incremental feel: Since convergence results exist for sequential-move MCTS, the contribution can feel incremental without rates or clear conditions under which simultaneous-move structure makes the result qualitatively different. Highlighting concrete classes of games where the new analysis enables algorithms that were previously unamenable would strengthen the impact.

Experimental evaluation
- The comparison between mean-value propagation and single-sample backups is useful; the finding that mean values slightly underperform is interesting and somewhat at odds with the analysis emphasis. It would help to:
  - Report exploitability over time (or a proxy) rather than just payoffs, to connect experiments to the theoretical goal.
  - Include confidence intervals and variability across seeds.
  - Test specific no-regret algorithms (e.g., EXP3, regret matching, Hedge) to see whether empirical convergence rates track their regret rates.
  - Vary tree depth and branching factor to probe how structure affects convergence.
  - If possible, include a domain where backward induction is infeasible at realistic scales, to motivate MCTS despite the existence of exact methods.

Clarity and presentation
- Clearly state all assumptions required for the convergence theorem (e.g., bounded payoffs, independent randomization, exploration requirements, how ties are broken).
- Define “ε–Hannan consistent” precisely on first use and relate it to the selection rules instantiated in experiments.
- Make explicit whether the guarantee is to an ε–Nash equilibrium of the full extensive-form game or of the restricted game induced by the explored subtree, and how averaging/averaging windows are used to form the strategies.

Nitpicks and minor edits
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Line 144: “negated,” not “inverted.”
- Algorithm 1, line 10: (a1, a2) should likely be (i, j), or possibly (σ1, σ2). This is especially confusing since a_{i,j} denotes payoffs in a single-stage matrix game.
- Line 172: There appear to be continuing notation switches between (i, j) and a’s; please standardize throughout.
- Several places: ensure consistency between action notation at matrix-game nodes and action profiles at simultaneous-move states.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and technically sound paper that advances our understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games. Establishing asymptotic convergence under broad no-regret selection is a valuable first step, and the analysis should be of interest to the community. My main reservation is the lack of finite-time rates, which limits practical guidance and makes the contribution feel incremental relative to existing results for sequential moves. If the authors can (i) provide finite-sample bounds derived from known regret rates of standard algorithms, or (ii) delineate structural conditions under which their approach yields provably faster or more scalable convergence than exact dynamic programming, the impact would be significantly strengthened. Even short, interpretable bounds that make the dependence on depth, branching factor, and regret explicit would go a long way.

I enjoyed reading the paper and believe it is close to being ready, pending attention to the rates question and the noted presentation issues.
