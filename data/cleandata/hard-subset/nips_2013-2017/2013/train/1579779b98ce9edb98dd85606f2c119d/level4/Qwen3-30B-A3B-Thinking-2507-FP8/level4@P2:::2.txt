

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a class of games that includes examples such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors introduce a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with various selection methods, and prove that when using an ε-Hannan consistent selection method with sufficient exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The empirical evaluation demonstrates convergence using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games, which is an important gap in the literature. Previous work (Lanctot et al., 2013) addressed similar settings but lacked rigorous theoretical analysis. The authors' proof proceeds by induction, establishing convergence at leaf nodes and then building up to higher levels of the game tree, with careful handling of error propagation from stochastic selection policies. This theoretical contribution is particularly valuable as it provides a solid foundation for using MCTS in practical applications where exact solutions may be computationally infeasible.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a thorough exposition of the problem setup, definitions, and algorithmic framework. The formal analysis is technically sound, with careful treatment of the relationship between Hannan consistency and Nash equilibrium convergence. The empirical results complement the theoretical analysis well, showing that the algorithm converges to approximate Nash equilibria in practice, and the experimental design effectively tests both random and worst-case game structures. The authors also thoughtfully address the distinction between using mean values versus current sample values in the MCTS update procedure, which is crucial for the theoretical guarantees.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a setting where prior theoretical results were lacking. While the general approach builds on Hannan consistency from online learning, the specific application to extensive-form games with simultaneous moves represents a novel combination of techniques. The authors properly contextualize their work within the NIPS proceedings by comparing with recent related work on game theory and MCTS.

The significance of this work is substantial for both theoretical and practical aspects of algorithmic game theory. The formal guarantees provide a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in practical applications involving simultaneous move games, which are common in security domains, game playing, and other strategic interactions. The results also advance the theoretical understanding of MCTS, which has seen widespread practical success but lacks rigorous theoretical underpinnings in many game settings.

The main weakness of the paper is that while it establishes convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium, it does not explicitly connect this result to the stronger concept of subgame perfect Nash equilibrium, which the authors define in the paper but do not leverage in their analysis. The paper acknowledges subgame perfection in the problem statement but the proof framework does not explicitly build toward this stronger solution concept. A more thorough analysis of how the MCTS algorithm converges to subgame perfect equilibria would strengthen the theoretical contribution. For example, the authors could explicitly show that the convergence result holds for each subgame in the tree, rather than just for the overall game.

Additionally, while the paper shows that the algorithm converges to approximate NE, the paper does not provide finite-time convergence bounds, which would be valuable for practitioners seeking to understand how many iterations are required to reach a desired approximation level. The empirical results demonstrate convergence rates but lack theoretical guarantees on these rates.

Strengths:
- First formal convergence analysis of MCTS in simultaneous move extensive-form games
- Careful theoretical treatment of error propagation in the game tree
- Well-designed empirical evaluation on both random and worst-case games
- Clear exposition and well-organized presentation
- Addresses an important gap in the literature for practical applications

Weaknesses:
- Does not explicitly leverage subgame perfect equilibrium concept in the theoretical analysis (though it's defined in the paper)
- Lacks finite-time convergence bounds for practical application
- The connection between the theoretical result and the practical implementation could be more explicitly drawn

Pro-con acceptance arguments:
Pro: This is a significant theoretical contribution that provides the first convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move extensive-form games. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the literature. The results are important for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of MCTS in strategic settings.

Con: The paper does not fully exploit the subgame perfect equilibrium concept, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution. Additionally, the absence of finite-time convergence bounds limits the immediate practical utility of the theoretical results.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of MCTS in simultaneous move games. While the connection to subgame perfect equilibrium could be strengthened, the paper provides a solid foundation for future work in this area. The empirical results effectively support the theoretical claims, and the paper is well-suited for publication at NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
