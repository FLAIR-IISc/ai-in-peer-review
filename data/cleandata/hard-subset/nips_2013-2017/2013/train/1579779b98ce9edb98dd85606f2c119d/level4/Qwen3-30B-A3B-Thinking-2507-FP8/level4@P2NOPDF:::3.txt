

The authors analyze Monte Carlo tree search (MCTS) algorithms using any no-regret selection method (with minor restrictions), establishing convergence to ε-Nash equilibrium for simultaneous-move games. Their primary theoretical contribution is this asymptotic convergence proof, which appears novel for extensive-form games with simultaneous moves—a gap in existing MCTS literature.

A key limitation is the absence of convergence rates. As the authors note, this is inherent to their analysis, which assumes only ε-Hannan consistency of the selection method. While asymptotic convergence remains valuable as a foundational step (given MCTS' practical success), the lack of rates weakens the work's impact. Specifically, exact equilibria are obtainable via backwards induction, making the practical relevance of asymptotic convergence unclear without rate guarantees. It would be valuable to explore whether specific regret rates (e.g., the √T bound from exponential weights) yield concrete convergence rates for the MCTS framework. The authors do not address this, leaving open whether their analysis provides meaningful guidance for practical implementation.

The experimental section compares propagating mean reward values (used in the analysis) against standard sample-value propagation. Results show mean propagation yields marginally inferior performance. The authors also attempt to empirically estimate convergence rates but provide no conclusive insights. While the experiments are well-executed and support the theoretical claims, they do not resolve the core concern about rates.

The paper is well-written and technically sound. However, the significance is tempered by two factors: (1) the asymptotic nature of the convergence result, and (2) the incremental contribution relative to existing MCTS work for sequential-move games (where convergence results are established). While establishing asymptotic convergence for simultaneous moves is a necessary step, the absence of rates diminishes the work's novelty and practical utility for NIPS, where algorithmic guarantees are highly valued.

In summary, this is a technically correct and clearly written paper making a modest contribution to MCTS theory. The asymptotic convergence result for simultaneous-move games is a useful first step, but the lack of convergence rates limits its significance. The work feels incremental given prior results for sequential games, and without rate analysis, it does not substantially advance the state of the art for practical MCTS applications. The experiments, while supportive, do not address the core limitation. For NIPS, the work would benefit from either a rate analysis or stronger justification for prioritizing asymptotic convergence over rates.
