Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. It introduces a general SM-MCTS template that can use any selection rule that is ε–Hannan consistent (HC) in matrix games and guarantees sufficient exploration. The main theoretical result proves eventual convergence of SM-MCTS to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium, with a bound that scales with game depth. The analysis proceeds by (i) establishing convergence properties for repeated matrix games under ε-HC, (ii) extending these to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors (arising from sampling and exploration), and (iii) propagating these bounds inductively up the game tree. Empirical evaluations with regret matching (RM) and Exp3 on random and “difficult” instances support the theory and compare two backpropagation choices: returning the current sample vs. the running mean.

This fills a gap between the strong empirical performance of UCT-style methods and the lack of guarantees in simultaneous-move settings; prior work even shows UCB can fail to reach a Nash equilibrium in simple simultaneous-move games. In contrast to exact methods (backward induction, alpha-beta pruning, double oracle) that require evaluation functions and/or linear programming and can be costly, the proposed template justifies the use of simulation-based methods in this class.

Evaluation:
- Quality: The main proof strategy is sound and well-motivated: convert selection guarantees in single-stage matrix games (ε-HC with exploration) into convergence of SM-MCTS in stacked matrix games. The induction across depths and the treatment of bounded payoff errors are carefully set up, and the subgame-perfectness claim is clear. Empirical results are consistent with the theory. A limitation is that the theoretical guarantees are asymptotic and potentially loose (e.g., dependence on depth), and the paper does not provide finite-time rates.

- Clarity: The paper is generally clear and well organized, with useful definitions and a readable algorithmic template. However, there are several notational inconsistencies between (i, j) indices and payoff entries aij, and a few small terminology slips. The algorithm pseudocode would benefit from aligning symbols with the notation in the text, and from more explicit statements about what is stored/updated at each node when returning mean vs. sample values.

- Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first general convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games. While convergence for sequential moves is known and UCT has extensive empirical success, the simultaneous-move case lacked theory and included negative results for popular choices like UCB. The paper’s template and proof conditions are broadly applicable (RM, Exp3, and other ε-HC learners).

- Significance: Establishing asymptotic convergence for a widely used class of algorithms in a natural and practically relevant game family is valuable. That said, exact polynomial-time methods exist for this setting; thus the main impact is on large-scale or knowledge-poor domains where MCTS is preferred over exact search. The absence of finite-time guarantees and the depth dependence in the bound temper the immediate practical impact.

Questions and suggestions for the authors:
- Finite-time rates: Given that RM and Exp3 satisfy O(√T) regret (or better in some variants), can you translate these into explicit finite-time exploitability bounds at the root as a function of T, depth D, and exploration γ? Even if loose, such bounds would materially strengthen the contribution.
- Depth dependence: The final bound scales with D (stated as O(D) per level and D^2 overall). Is this tight? Could using variance-reduction or weighted backups improve the dependence on D?
- Backpropagation choice: Your proof requires returning the mean, whereas standard SM-MCTS returns the current sample. Are there classes of selection rules (or averaging schemes) under which sample backpropagation still yields convergence? The experiments suggest similar behavior; any partial theory?
- Exploration: The guarantees rely on guaranteed exploration. Can you quantify the bias floor induced by γ (observed empirically) and incorporate it explicitly in the asymptotic bound? Could adaptive or decaying exploration preserve guarantees yet reduce the exploitability floor?
- Scope: Do any parts of the analysis carry over to non–zero-sum or imperfect-information settings (e.g., via regret minimization in information sets)? A brief discussion would help position the work.

Pros:
- First general asymptotic convergence proof for MCTS in simultaneous-move, perfect-information, zero-sum games.
- Broadly applicable template using standard no-regret learners (RM, Exp3); subgame-perfect equilibrium guarantee.
- Clear connection between matrix-game learning properties and tree-level convergence; solid empirical support.

Cons:
- No finite-time convergence rates; asymptotic bounds may be loose and depth-dependent.
- Theoretical analysis requires mean backpropagation, differing from common practice.
- Some notational inconsistencies and minor presentation issues.
- Result is limited to perfect information and zero-sum; exact methods exist in this regime.

Minor comments and typos:
- Figure 1: “leafs” → “leaves.”
- Line 144: “negated,” not “inverted.”
- Algorithm 1, line 10: the selected joint action should be denoted (i, j); (a1, a2) is confusing given aij denotes payoffs.
- There are several places where notation switches between (i, j) and a’s; please standardize throughout.
- Clarify RetVal: explain earlier that RetVal may return u1 or Xh/nh, and when each is used.
- Specify that both players select actions independently in Exp3, and remind the reader that values are normalized to [0, 1].

Overall recommendation:
A solid and technically correct paper addressing an important theoretical gap for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that adding even coarse finite-time bounds or a clearer discussion of depth dependence would materially enhance the significance.
