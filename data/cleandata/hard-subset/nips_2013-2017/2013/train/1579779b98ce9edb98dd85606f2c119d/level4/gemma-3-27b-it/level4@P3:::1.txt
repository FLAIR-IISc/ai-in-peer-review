## Review: Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games

This paper investigates the convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves – a challenging and important area. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, under the condition of employing a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. Empirical results on randomly generated and worst-case games validate the theoretical findings. Overall, this is a solid contribution that provides a valuable theoretical foundation for MCTS in these game settings.

The paper begins by clearly framing the problem and its relevance to areas like security and general game playing, referencing key prior work [1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6]. It effectively highlights the limitations of traditional backward induction approaches [7, 8, 9] and motivates the use of MCTS [10, 11, 12, 13]. The authors correctly identify the lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, establishing a clear gap in the literature that this work addresses. The introduction effectively sets the stage for the core contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength lies in the formal proof of convergence for a general MCTS template. This provides a rigorous understanding of when and why MCTS can be expected to succeed in simultaneous-move games.
* **Generality:** The framework is general, allowing for instantiation with various -Hannan consistent selection methods. This broad applicability enhances the impact of the work.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation, using regret matching [15] and Exp3 [16] as selection methods, lends credence to the theoretical results and demonstrates practical performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the key ideas accessible. Definitions and notation are clearly presented.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors appropriately cite and relate their work to previous research in game theory and MCTS.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Novelty of Proof Technique:** While the convergence result is valuable, the novelty of the proof technique itself could be more thoroughly discussed.  A more detailed comparison to existing convergence proofs for MCTS in sequential games would strengthen this aspect.
* **Limited Consideration of UCB:** The paper acknowledges the widespread use of UCB algorithms [14] but does not analyze them. Given the existing research on UCB for Trees, a discussion of why UCB is not covered (e.g., known non-convergence issues [17] as mentioned briefly) and a potential direction for future work would be beneficial.
* **Propagation of Mean vs. Current Sample:** The experimental results suggest that propagating the current sample value performs comparably to propagating the mean, despite the theoretical analysis relying on the latter. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of this discrepancy and possible explanations, potentially exploring the practical impact of variance in the simulations. 
* **Limited Scope of Empirical Analysis:** While the empirical analysis is a good starting point, exploring a wider range of game structures and parameters could further validate the robustness of the findings.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
* The convergence proof is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a solid foundation for future research.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical applicability of the proposed framework.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides adequate references to related work.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The novelty of the proof technique is not sufficiently emphasized.
* The exclusion of UCB algorithms, a widely used approach, without more detailed justification is a limitation.
* The discrepancy between theoretical requirements (mean propagation) and observed empirical performance (current sample value) requires further investigation.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a worthwhile contribution to the NIPS community. While there are minor weaknesses, the strengths – particularly the theoretical convergence result – outweigh them. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem in the field of game theory and artificial intelligence. I recommend **acceptance**, with the caveat that the authors address the concerns regarding the novelty of the proof technique and the discrepancy between theoretical and empirical findings in a revision.
