This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The central contribution is a proof establishing conditions under which MCTS algorithms converge to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The authors validate this theoretical result with empirical evaluation.

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a useful convergence condition for MCTS in a challenging game setting. The presentation is clear and well-organized. However, the novelty of the proof technique warrants further consideration, as the theoretical contribution is the paper’s primary strength.

The authors acknowledge the widespread use of Upper Confidence Bound (UCB) algorithms in the literature. Given existing analyses of UCB for Trees, it would strengthen the paper to discuss why UCB was not analyzed here and to provide a comparative analysis against the presented algorithms. Specifically, addressing the convergence properties, or lack thereof, of UCB in this context would be valuable. The paper could benefit from a discussion of related work on UCB and its limitations in simultaneous-move games.

The paper demonstrates convergence with mean propagation, but the experiments suggest that propagating the current sample value also yields good results. Further explanation of this observation is needed. Are there specific game characteristics where one method outperforms the other? Exploring this discrepancy could offer additional insights into the behavior of MCTS algorithms. The empirical analysis would be improved by providing statistical significance tests for comparing these different propagation methods.

While the empirical results support the theoretical findings, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive exploration of the convergence rate and scalability of the proposed algorithms. Analyzing performance on larger, more complex games would provide a better understanding of the practical implications of the theoretical results. 

In summary, this paper presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, providing a theoretical foundation for MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While the theoretical novelty could be strengthened, the clarity of the presentation and the empirical validation make this a valuable addition to the NIPS workshop. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel theoretical analysis of MCTS convergence in a challenging game setting.
*   Provides a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS algorithms.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Novelty of the proof technique is not fully established.
*   Lack of analysis of widely used UCB algorithms.
*   Limited explanation for the effectiveness of current sample value propagation.
*   Convergence rate and scalability require further investigation.
