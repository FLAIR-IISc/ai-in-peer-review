This paper addresses the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with various action selection methods. The main contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the overall MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The authors then empirically validate this theoretical claim using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods on a series of randomly generated and worst-case games. This work builds upon previous empirical studies, such as Lanctot et al. [20], by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel theoretical contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS to an approximate Nash equilibrium in simultaneous move games. This is a significant result, as it provides a solid theoretical foundation for using MCTS, a popular and practical algorithm, as a principled approximation method for this game class.
*   **Quality:** The technical analysis appears to be sound. The proof is built carefully, starting with convergence guarantees for ε-Hannan consistent algorithms in simple repeated matrix games, extending this to games with bounded error, and then applying this framework inductively to the multi-stage structure of the extensive-form game. The inclusion of empirical results complements the theory well, confirming the convergence in practice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally clear, well-written, and logically structured. The authors do a good job of defining the problem setting, explaining the algorithm, and outlining the structure of their formal analysis. The experimental section is also well-presented and supports the main claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The main area for improvement lies in strengthening the connection between the paper's formal analysis and established concepts in game theory, specifically that of subgame perfection.

*   The paper's main result (Theorem 4.11) claims convergence to a *subgame perfect* approximate Nash Equilibrium. However, the discussion of why the resulting equilibrium is subgame perfect feels underdeveloped. The inductive proof relies on the idea that nodes at a higher level of the tree face a matrix game where payoffs (from child subgames) have a bounded error. While the proof shows that play will converge to an approximate NE at each level, the link to the stronger condition of subgame perfection—that the strategy profile constitutes a Nash equilibrium in *every* subgame—could be made more explicit. A more detailed discussion of how the algorithm's behavior at inner nodes guarantees optimal play from that point forward would significantly bolster this claim.
*   To strengthen this aspect of the paper, the authors could benefit from more thoroughly connecting their work to the existing game theory literature on subgame perfection in games with simultaneous moves. For example, discussing their result in the context of foundational work such as the following could be valuable:
    *   Harris, C. (1990). "Existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves".

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and important theoretical result for MCTS. While the analysis is valuable from a computational standpoint, its impact could be increased by more explicitly tying the convergence proof to the powerful game-theoretic tool of subgame perfect Nash equilibria.
