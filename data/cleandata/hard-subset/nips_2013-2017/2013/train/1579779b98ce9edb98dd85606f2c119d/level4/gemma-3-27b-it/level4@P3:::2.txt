This paper analyzes the class of zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games, a class that includes interesting games such as Goofspiel and Tron. The authors analyze Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) implemented with regret-matching for this class of games and prove convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. 

The paper is clear and well-written, and the results are well-presented. The main contribution lies in the formal analysis of MCTS in these games, a topic previously lacking rigorous guarantees. While Lanctot et al. [20] explored a similar setup, their analysis appears more preliminary. The inductive proof, building from optimal strategies at the leaf nodes, is a reasonable approach. 

However, a significant weakness is that the analysis provides limited insights into the inner nodes of the game tree. This neglects key concepts from game theory, particularly subgame perfect Nash equilibria. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by mentioning the subgame perfect equilibrium in the introduction but do not fully integrate this framework into their analysis. Connecting the convergence results to the broader theory of subgame perfection would significantly strengthen the paper. A relevant citation would be Harris [1990], which presents analyses of regret-matching strategies and their relationship to subgame perfect equilibria. Furthermore, the assumption of perfect information seems restrictive; exploring the robustness of the results to imperfect information scenarios could be valuable.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, with a clear presentation of the results and methodology.
*   **Soundness:** The technical analysis appears rigorous, with a well-defined framework and a logical inductive proof.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical evaluation provides supporting evidence for the theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope:** The focus on simultaneous-move games with perfect information is restrictive and may not generalize to more complex scenarios.
*   **Lack of Connection to Subgame Perfection:** The analysis does not fully leverage the framework of subgame perfect Nash equilibria, a central concept in game theory.
*   **Assumptions:** The assumption of perfect information might limit the practical applicability of the results.
*   **Convergence Rate:** While the paper proves convergence, it does not provide detailed insights into the rate of convergence.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important open problem in the field of game theory and artificial intelligence.
*   The theoretical results are novel and potentially impactful.
*   The empirical evaluation provides supporting evidence for the theoretical claims.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The scope of the paper is relatively narrow.
*   The analysis does not fully connect to established concepts in game theory, such as subgame perfection.
*   The lack of convergence rate analysis limits the practical value of the results.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field. While the limitations mentioned above are present, the novelty and theoretical rigor of the work outweigh these concerns. The paper’s focus on establishing formal guarantees for MCTS in a challenging game setting is commendable. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors consider addressing the weaknesses identified above in future work.
