### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the theoretical underpinnings of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general algorithmic template for MCTS in this setting, which can be instantiated with different action selection mechanisms. The central contribution is a formal proof demonstrating that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent (a concept from online learning ensuring bounded regret) and guarantees sufficient exploration, then the MCTS algorithm is guaranteed to converge to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game.

The proof proceeds inductively. It first establishes convergence to an approximate Nash Equilibrium (NE) in single-stage matrix games (the leaves of the game tree) using ε-Hannan consistent procedures. It then shows that the values propagated up the tree from these subgames can be treated as payoffs with bounded error. The analysis is extended to show that ε-Hannan consistent algorithms still converge to an approximate NE in these "games with bounded error." By applying this reasoning recursively up the game tree, the authors establish convergence for the entire game. The paper's theoretical claims are supported by empirical evaluations on randomly generated games and carefully selected worst-case scenarios, using regret matching and Exp3 as instantiations of the selection mechanism. This work builds upon previous empirical applications of MCTS in such games (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013), but provides the first formal convergence guarantees, which has been a notable open question.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novel theoretical contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games. This is a significant result, as it bridges a gap between the widespread practical use of MCTS in this domain and the lack of theoretical guarantees. It provides a solid foundation for the use of MCTS as a principled approximation algorithm for this class of games from the perspective of both machine learning and algorithmic game theory.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The proof technique is elegant, building from established results in online learning for matrix games and carefully extending them to the multi-stage, extensive-form setting by bounding the error propagated from subgames. The formal definitions of ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration are clear and appropriate for the analysis.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of defining the problem setting, presenting the general SM-MCTS algorithm, and then systematically building the theoretical argument from simple to complex cases. The empirical results are presented clearly and effectively support the theoretical claims.

4.  **Completeness:** The work is a complete research contribution, combining a novel theoretical proof with a solid empirical evaluation. The experiments not only validate the main theorem but also provide useful insights, such as the comparison between propagating the mean value versus the current sample value, which is relevant to practitioners.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Connection to Game Theory Literature:** While the paper proves convergence to a *subgame perfect* approximate NE (Theorem 4.11), the discussion is framed almost entirely from an online learning and computational perspective. The paper could be strengthened by more explicitly connecting its findings to the classical game theory literature on subgame perfection in games with simultaneous moves. Briefly discussing how this algorithmic approach provides a constructive method for finding equilibria whose properties have been studied in foundational game theory could broaden the paper's impact and better situate the contribution.

2.  **Convergence Rate and Bounds:** The main theorem guarantees eventual convergence but does not provide a finite-time convergence rate. The final approximation quality bound appears to have a strong dependence on the depth of the game (D). While deriving tight finite-time bounds is likely beyond the scope of a single paper and is noted as future work, a brief discussion in the main body regarding the tightness of the derived ε'-bound and its practical implications for deep games would be a valuable addition.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation of Subgame Perfection:** The experiments measure exploitability from the root of the game, which demonstrates convergence to an approximate NE. Since the main theoretical claim is about subgame perfection, the empirical analysis could be made more compelling by including a measure of exploitability in representative subgames, not just the root. This would provide direct empirical evidence for the subgame perfection aspect of the convergence guarantee.

### **Overall Evaluation**

This is a strong, well-executed paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of Monte Carlo Tree Search. The authors tackle an important open problem—the convergence of MCTS in simultaneous move games—and provide a rigorous and satisfying proof. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and provides a much-needed theoretical justification for using MCTS in this game class. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to suggestions for strengthening the paper's framing and extending the experiments. The strengths, particularly the originality and significance of the theoretical result, substantially outweigh these minor points. The paper is a clear contribution to the NIPS community and should be accepted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous move games.
*   The theoretical analysis is rigorous, novel, and closes an important gap in the literature.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and presents a complete piece of research with both theory and experiments.
*   The results are significant for both researchers and practitioners using simulation-based methods in game theory and multi-agent systems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   (Minor) The connection to classical game theory literature could be more developed.
*   (Minor) The paper does not provide finite-time convergence rates, though this is a standard limitation for initial convergence proofs.
