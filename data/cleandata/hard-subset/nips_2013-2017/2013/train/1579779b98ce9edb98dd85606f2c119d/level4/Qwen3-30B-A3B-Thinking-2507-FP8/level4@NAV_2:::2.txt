

This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, presenting a formal convergence analysis to approximate Nash equilibria. The authors develop a general MCTS framework that can be instantiated with any -Hannan consistent selection method, proving that under appropriate conditions, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium. This work extends prior preliminary analysis by Lanctot et al. (2013) by providing the first rigorous theoretical foundation for MCTS convergence in this game class.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and effectively presents its technical contributions. The main strength lies in the quality of the formal analysis, which is technically sound and carefully supported by both theoretical arguments and empirical validation. The authors provide thorough experimental evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods, confirming their theoretical claims on randomly generated and worst-case games.

Quality: The paper delivers a rigorous proof of convergence with appropriate theoretical grounding and well-designed experiments that complement the theoretical claims. The authors are careful to address both strengths and limitations of their approach.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written with a logical flow from problem statement to formal analysis to empirical validation. The technical content is presented accessibly to the NIPS audience, with sufficient detail to allow reproduction of the results.

Originality: This represents the first formal convergence proof for MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with simultaneous moves, addressing a significant gap in the literature. The theoretical contribution is substantially novel compared to prior work.

Significance: The results provide important theoretical justification for using MCTS as an approximation algorithm for this game class, with potential applications in security domains and other game-theoretic problems. The work advances the state of the art in computational game theory.

The primary weakness concerns the limited integration of subgame perfect Nash equilibrium concepts, which are central to game-theoretic analysis of extensive-form games. While the paper mentions subgame perfect equilibria in the main theorem statement, the analysis would be strengthened by more explicitly connecting to this key concept and citing relevant game theory literature, such as Harris (1990) on the existence of subgame perfect equilibria in games with simultaneous moves. The paper could benefit from deeper theoretical integration of this established game theory framework.

Pro arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with rigorous convergence guarantees for MCTS in an important game class, supported by thorough empirical validation.

Con arguments for rejection: The connection to subgame perfect equilibrium theory could be more deeply integrated into the analysis to strengthen the theoretical foundation.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field with its formal convergence analysis. The main weakness is a missed opportunity to more fully leverage subgame perfect equilibrium theory, which could be addressed with minor revisions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
