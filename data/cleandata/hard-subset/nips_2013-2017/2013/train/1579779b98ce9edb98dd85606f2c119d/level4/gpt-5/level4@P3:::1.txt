Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games (EFGs) with perfect information and simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template in which the per-node selection mechanism is any no-regret algorithm for matrix games. The main theoretical result shows that if the selection rule is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, then SM-MCTS—when using mean-value backups—converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE), with an approximation factor that scales with depth (O(ε D^2) as written). The analysis proceeds by induction on depth: base cases are repeated matrix games; internal nodes are treated as matrix games with bounded payoff errors due to stochastic rollouts and exploration. Empirically, the paper evaluates regret matching (RM) and Exp3 on random and “worst-case” shallow games, confirming convergence and comparing mean vs. sample-value backups.

This work fits between practical MCTS successes (e.g., UCT) and theoretical understanding in more general EFGs. Prior work has shown UCT performs well in some simultaneous-move settings but may fail to converge to NE (Shafiei et al.). There are exact methods for simultaneous moves via backward induction with pruning and double oracle, but they require domain knowledge or expensive LPs. No previous convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move EFGs are known to the best of my knowledge; the paper leverages standard no-regret learning results (Hart & Mas-Colell; Auer et al.) and extends them to the tree setting with bounded-error propagation. This is a natural and useful contribution.

Quality
- Theory: The reduction from tree games to repeated matrix games with bounded errors is sound and well-motivated. The use of ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration is standard and appropriate, and the inductive argument explains the O(D^2) error accumulation. However, some constants and assumptions could be made more explicit; e.g., the precise dependence on ε, exploration rate γ, reward range, and depth D in the final exploitability bound. The requirement to back up mean returns rather than sampled returns is crucial for the proofs; it would help to clarify whether weaker averaging (e.g., running means) suffices and whether similar guarantees hold with sample backups under additional conditions.
- Experiments: The empirical section aligns with the theory and explores random and selected hard cases. It also observes that sample-value backups often work comparably in practice, despite lacking a proof. The scope is limited (small depths, symmetric branching, synthetic payoffs), and comparisons omit widely used UCB-based selection, though the paper cites a negative result for UCB in simultaneous moves. No finite-sample convergence rates are given, even though RM and Exp3 have known regret rates that could be propagated to derive iteration complexity bounds.
- Presentation: Overall clear, with useful algorithm descriptions. A few notational issues remain (e.g., the theorem statement “( D2 + δ)” is ambiguous; please state the bound explicitly as a function of ε, γ, and D). The relationship between empirical frequencies and average strategies is discussed (Lemma 4.5); ensure consistency when reporting which one is used in experiments.

Clarity
The manuscript is well organized. Suggestions:
- State the main theorem with an explicit bound, e.g., “the average strategies form a subgame-perfect (C(ε+γ)D^2 + δ)-NE,” and define C.
- Clearly specify the backup rule used in each experiment and its relation to the theoretical requirement (mean vs. sample).
- Unify the treatment of exploration: Definition 4.3’s A* does not update on exploration steps, while Section 3.1 seems to mix in exploration and then update. Clarify which version is analyzed/implemented.

Originality
The core idea—instantiating SM-MCTS with no-regret selection and proving convergence to approximate NE in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs—appears novel and addresses a gap in the literature. The technical tools (Hannan consistency, bounded-error games, induction on depth) are standard, so the novelty is more in the problem setting and assembly of known components than in fundamentally new proof techniques.

Significance
The result provides the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in this class of games and offers a general template that practitioners can instantiate with RM or Exp3. The lack of finite-time bounds and the mean-backup requirement temper immediate practical impact, but the work is a solid step toward principled MCTS in simultaneous-move settings and may catalyze further theoretical and empirical work.

Specific strengths
- Clear formalization of SM-MCTS with general no-regret selection.
- Almost-sure convergence to subgame-perfect approximate NE under transparent assumptions.
- Useful decomposition via repeated matrix games with bounded error.
- Empirical validation on random and hard cases; observation that sample backups can still work well.

Specific weaknesses and questions
- Novelty of technique: The analysis leverages known no-regret results and a standard induction; clarify what technical hurdles are specific to simultaneous-move trees and how they are resolved.
- UCB/UCT: While prior work shows UCB can fail, it is a major baseline. A brief empirical comparison or a more detailed discussion of when/why UCB fails in this setting would strengthen the paper.
- Backup rule: Theory requires mean backups, but experiments show sample backups often converge. Can the analysis be extended to sample backups (e.g., via concentration and bias control)? What are the minimal conditions?
- Finite-time rates: Given known regret bounds for RM/Exp3, can the authors derive explicit iteration complexity bounds for reaching ε-NE, with dependence on D, branching factors, and γ?
- Exploration: Please clarify whether exploration steps update the learner. How sensitive are results to γ? Can adaptive or diminishing exploration be used while preserving convergence?
- Scope of experiments: Consider adding larger or structured games (e.g., Goofspiel instances) and comparisons to alpha-beta with simultaneous-move pruning or double oracle to contextualize practical performance.
- Notation/typos: Make the final approximation bound explicit; check consistency between “average strategies” and “empirical frequencies” in statements and experiments.

Pros for acceptance
- First convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs.
- General, simple template applicable with widely used no-regret algorithms.
- Clear exposition and connection to online learning theory.
- Empirical results align with theory and offer useful practical insights.

Cons for acceptance
- Technical novelty is moderate; relies on assembling known tools.
- No finite-time guarantees; practical guidance on iterations needed is missing.
- Experiments are limited in scope and omit UCB comparisons beyond citing its shortcomings.
- Dependence on mean backups may limit direct applicability to common MCTS implementations.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The theoretical guarantee in an under-explored but relevant setting, coupled with a clear presentation and reasonable experiments, makes this a meaningful contribution, though with modest technical novelty and limited empirical breadth. Strengthening the theorem statement, clarifying exploration/backup details, and adding either a brief finite-time analysis (even with conservative constants) or a broader empirical comparison would significantly enhance the paper.
