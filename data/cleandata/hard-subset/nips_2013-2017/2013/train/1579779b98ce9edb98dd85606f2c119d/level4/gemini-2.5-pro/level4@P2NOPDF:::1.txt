This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a theoretical analysis proving that the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium under specific conditions. This formal result is subsequently corroborated by an empirical study.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that presents a useful result. The establishment of a sufficient condition for the convergence of MCTS in this game class is a valuable contribution, and the presentation is clear and easy to follow. However, my primary enthusiasm is tempered by a few concerns, particularly regarding the theoretical novelty and the scope of the analysis.

Detailed comments are as follows:

1.  **Novelty of the Proof Technique:** The main contribution of the paper is its theoretical analysis. Given this focus, the novelty of the proof technique itself is a critical factor in evaluating its impact. The paper would be stronger if the authors could more explicitly situate their technical approach within the existing literature. Specifically, how do the methods used here differ from or significantly extend prior proof techniques for MCTS convergence in simpler settings? Clarifying the key technical innovations would help in assessing the paper's contribution beyond the specific result.

2.  **Omission of UCB-based Algorithms:** The analysis notably omits a discussion of UCB (Upper Confidence Bound) selection strategies. Given the prevalence and success of UCB algorithms in MCTS, particularly the widely-used UCT (UCB for Trees) algorithm, their absence is a significant gap. As there is already a body of theoretical work on UCT, the paper would be substantially strengthened by either extending the analysis to cover UCB-based methods or providing a detailed discussion on why the current framework does not apply. A comparison with existing UCT convergence results would also be highly beneficial for context.

3.  **Discrepancy Between Theory and Experiments:** There appears to be a disconnect between the scope of the theoretical proof and the empirical findings. The formal analysis only proves convergence for the method that propagates the *mean* of the sample values up the tree. However, the experiments show that propagating the *current sample value* also achieves strong performance. The authors should address this observation. Could they offer some intuition as to why this second method works so well in practice despite lacking a formal guarantee in this paper? This might suggest that the theoretical conditions could be relaxed, or perhaps another factor explains the strong empirical performance. A brief discussion on this point would enrich the paper.

In summary, the paper presents interesting findings and a useful convergence result. However, its technical significance is not fully established due to questions about the novelty of the proof and the exclusion of standard UCB-based approaches. Addressing these points would significantly improve the quality and impact of the work.
