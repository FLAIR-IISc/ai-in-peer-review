The authors present a comprehensive analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. Their primary theoretical contribution is a proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) in the case of simultaneous moves. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as no convergence guarantees were previously known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general extensive-form games.

The authors provide a general template of MCTS algorithms for these games, which can be instantiated using any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games as a function for selecting the next actions to be sampled. They formally prove that if the algorithm uses an -Hannan consistent selection function, which ensures attempting each action infinitely many times, the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to a subgame perfect -Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game.

The authors also empirically evaluate their claim using two different -Hannan consistent procedures: regret matching and Exp3. In experiments on randomly generated and worst-case games, they show that the empirical speed of convergence of the algorithms based on their template is comparable to recently proposed MCTS algorithms for these games. They conjecture that many of these algorithms also converge to -Nash equilibrium and that their formal analysis could be extended to include them.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise presentation of their results. However, I have some reservations about the ultimate significance of the work given the lack of convergence rates. While establishing asymptotic convergence is a good first step, the absence of convergence rates is a notable limitation. Additionally, convergence results are already known for MCTS with sequential moves, which may make this result feel somewhat incremental.
