

This paper addresses an important gap in the theoretical understanding of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in games with simultaneous moves, a class of zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information. The authors present a general MCTS template for these games, showing that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games with guaranteed exploration, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. They formally prove this convergence and empirically validate it using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods.

The main strengths of the paper are its clear theoretical contribution and thorough empirical validation. The formal analysis is well-structured and addresses a gap in the literature, as the authors correctly note that "no convergence guarantees are known for MCTS in games with simultaneous moves or general EFGs." Their proof technique, using induction over game depth and bounding errors in repeated matrix games, is elegant and well-executed. The paper makes a significant contribution to algorithmic game theory by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this specific game class. The empirical evaluation is also thorough, with careful analysis of both randomly generated games and worst-case scenarios, showing that the theoretical predictions hold in practice.

The paper is well-written with clear definitions and logical flow. The authors appropriately reference related work, including previous MCTS applications in games like Goofspiel and Tron, and correctly situate their theoretical contribution within the broader context of regret minimization algorithms. The notation is consistent and well-motivated, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with game theory and online learning.

Some minor weaknesses to note: The theoretical analysis focuses on asymptotic convergence without providing finite-time bounds, which would be valuable for practical applications. The empirical evaluation, while comprehensive, is limited to relatively small games (depth 2 with branching factor 2 in worst-case analysis), and it would be beneficial to see how the algorithms perform on larger games. The paper could also strengthen its connection to practical applications by discussing how the theoretical guarantees translate to real-world game-playing scenarios.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by bridging the gap between theoretical guarantees and practical MCTS implementations in simultaneous move games. The authors successfully show that the template they propose is theoretically sound, and their empirical results confirm the formal claims. The connection to regret minimization algorithms (regret matching and Exp3) is particularly well-executed, as these methods are known to be Hannan consistent, which is a key requirement for their convergence proof.

I believe this paper would be of interest to the NIPS community, as it provides rigorous theoretical analysis of a widely used algorithm (MCTS) in a specific but important class of games. The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature and makes a clear contribution to the understanding of algorithmic game theory. The empirical validation adds substantial weight to the theoretical claims.

For future work, the authors might explore extending their results to more general game classes (e.g., imperfect information games), or providing finite-time convergence bounds. They also mention conjecturing that other MCTS variants converge to approximate NE, which would be an interesting direction for future research.

Overall, this is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field. The results are significant for both theoretical understanding and practical applications of MCTS in game-playing scenarios. The paper meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS.

Strengths:
- First formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games
- Clear theoretical analysis with well-structured proofs
- Thorough empirical validation
- Appropriate connection to related work in regret minimization

Weaknesses:
- Limited to zero-sum perfect information games
- Asymptotic convergence without finite-time bounds
- Empirical evaluation restricted to relatively small games
