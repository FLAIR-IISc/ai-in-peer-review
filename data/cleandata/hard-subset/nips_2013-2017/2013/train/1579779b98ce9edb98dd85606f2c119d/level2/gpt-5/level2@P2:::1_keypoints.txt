- Studies SM-MCTS with per-node online learners and proves convergence to an (ε·D^2+δ)-approximate NE under ε–Hannan consistency and sufficient exploration.  
- Analysis builds from HC properties in matrix games, extends to bounded-payoff-error repeated games, and bootstraps errors up the tree.  
- Empirical instantiation with regret matching and Exp3 shows convergence on random and small adversarial games and compares mean vs. sample backups.  
- Fills a gap in prior work: first general convergence proof for SM-MCTS to approximate NE under broad per-node HC learners, contrasting with UCT failures and costly exact methods.  
- Theory uses standard online-learning tools and natural tree induction, but constants are loose, yielding O(ε·D^2) error that might be improvable to O(ε·D).  
- Proof crucially requires mean-value backups rather than sample backups, diverging from typical practical implementations.  
- Requires guaranteed exploration via constant γ, which forces a mixed limiting strategy and a nonzero exploitability floor.  
- Suggests need to analyze decaying exploration schedules that still ensure every joint-action is visited infinitely often.  
- Assumes eventual full expansion of finite tree; formal details deferred and this weakens practical implications for large trees.  
- Experiments confirm qualitative convergence but are limited in depth/scale, lack standard benchmarks and strong baseline comparisons (CFR, LP/DO), and omit finite-sample rates and ablations.  
- Empirical finding: mean backups are slightly slower and exploitability plateaus at the exploration floor, consistent with theory.  
- Paper is generally clear with a clean algorithmic template, but some constants/notation and proof sketches (error propagation, ε in theorem) need clarification.  
- Originality: novel application of repeated matrix games with bounded error and upward induction to prove SM-MCTS convergence.  
- Significance: provides theoretical justification and practical guidance (use HC learners, ensure exploration, mean backups), but impact limited by asymptotic results and loose constants.  
- Pros: first general convergence guarantee, modular analysis, RM/Exp3 instantiations, and subgame-perfect guarantee.  
- Cons: asymptotic-only with loose O(ε·D^2) bound, requirement of mean backups, constant-exploration exploitability floor, reliance on full-tree expansion, and limited experiments.  
- Key suggestions: tighten constants/depth dependence, analyze decaying exploration, handle sample backups via concentration techniques, derive finite-sample rates, and expand experiments/benchmarks.  
- Minor fixes: correct theorem typesetting for ε, ensure consistent citation years, and complete references.  
- Overall: solid theoretical contribution addressing an important gap; with tighter analysis and stronger experiments the reviewer leans toward acceptance.
