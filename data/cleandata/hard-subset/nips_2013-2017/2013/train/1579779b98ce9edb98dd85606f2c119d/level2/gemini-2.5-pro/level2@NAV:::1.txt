This paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing the first formal proof of convergence for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for action selection. The main theoretical contribution is a proof that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration, the MCTS search will converge to a subgame perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. This work connects the practical success of simulation-based methods like MCTS with the rich theoretical literature on online learning and regret minimization, particularly building on work by Hart & Mas-Colell and Auer et al.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty and importance. Proving convergence for MCTS outside of sequential, perfect-information games is a challenging and open problem. This result provides a strong theoretical foundation for using MCTS in a broader and more complex class of games, justifying its use from a game-theoretic perspective.
*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound. The proof strategy, which proceeds by induction from the leaf nodes of the game tree, is elegant and well-reasoned. The paper supports its theoretical claims with a solid empirical evaluation on both randomly generated and empirically-derived worst-case games, confirming the convergence of MCTS variants using Regret Matching and Exp3.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors clearly define the problem setting, present the algorithm, and outline the structure of their formal analysis before diving into the details.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Gap between Theory and Practice:** The formal proof relies on propagating the mean value of rollouts back up the tree, whereas experiments show that the more common approach of propagating the current sample’s value converges slightly faster. While the authors acknowledge this and conjecture their proof could be extended, this represents a minor gap between the proven theory and best practice.
*   **Asymptotic Results:** The analysis proves eventual convergence but does not provide finite-time bounds on the convergence rate. While this is acknowledged as future work, the absence of rate bounds limits the practical applicability of the theoretical result for determining the number of simulations needed to achieve a desired approximation quality (ε).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first formal convergence guarantee for MCTS in this important game class.
*   The theoretical analysis is novel and technically sound.
*   The work is significant and likely to be built upon by future research in MCTS and algorithmic game theory.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The theoretical result does not cover the empirically superior variant.
*   The convergence analysis is asymptotic and lacks finite-time bounds.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—particularly its significant and novel theoretical contribution—far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It represents a clear and valuable advancement for the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.
