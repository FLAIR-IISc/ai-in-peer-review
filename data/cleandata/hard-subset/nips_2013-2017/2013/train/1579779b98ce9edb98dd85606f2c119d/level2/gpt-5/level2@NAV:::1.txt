This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) for two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). The main claim is a general SM-MCTS template that, when instantiated with any ε–Hannan consistent (HC) selection method with guaranteed exploration, converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium; the bound scales as O(ε D^2) in game depth D. The proof reduces node selection to repeated matrix games with bounded error, bootstrapping from leaves upward. Empirically, the authors evaluate regret matching and Exp3 on random and empirically hard depth-2 games, measuring exploitability and contrasting “mean-propagation” versus sample-return updates. Results corroborate convergence and show mean-propagation is slightly slower but consistent with the theory. This connects prior negative results for UCT in simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al.) with positive guarantees for no-regret selection rules; it complements exact solvers (backward induction with LP/double-oracle) by offering sampling-based asymptotics.

Quality: The theoretical development is careful and builds on standard online learning facts (HC, exploration) and a clear bounded-error framework. The induction argument to subgame perfection is well-motivated. However, guarantees are asymptotic with loose constants (D^2, factors of 4), and the practical requirement to propagate mean returns (rather than samples) is only partly justified empirically. Experiments are correct but limited in scale and do not probe sample complexity.

Clarity: The paper is generally clear and organized. A few places could be streamlined (dual RetVal choices, notational overload in Section 4). Pseudocode is helpful; more detail on rollout policies and hyperparameters (γ, learning rates) would aid reproducibility.

Originality: To my knowledge, this is the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs, and the HC-to-NE lifting under bounded error is a neat synthesis. Related work is mostly well covered.

Significance: The result provides theoretical footing for widely used SM-MCTS variants and a unifying template for bandit-based selection. Practical impact is tempered by asymptotic nature and small-scale experiments.

Pros for acceptance:
- First rigorous convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS under broad HC selectors.
- Clean, modular analysis with subgame-perfect conclusion.
- Empirical confirmation and insights on mean-propagation vs sample returns.

Cons for acceptance:
- No finite-time/sample-complexity bounds; constants are loose.
- Experiments small and narrow; limited comparisons (e.g., alpha-beta + LP/DO, MCCFR-style baselines).
- Dependence on fixed exploration induces an exploitability floor; decay schedules unstudied.

Constructive suggestions:
- Derive finite-time bounds and tighten D-dependence; study decaying exploration.
- Evaluate on larger games and compare to practical baselines (α–β with DO, MCCFR variants).
- Analyze when sample-return propagation still converges; quantify bias/variance trade-offs.
- Provide code and full hyperparameter/reporting details; clarify rollout policy and RetVal choice.
