

This paper presents a formal analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a significant gap in the literature. The main claim is that a general MCTS template using ε-Hannan consistent selection methods (with guaranteed exploration) converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the extensive-form game. The authors provide a formal proof of this convergence (Theorem 4.11) and empirically validate it using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper demonstrates strong theoretical support for its main claim through careful formal analysis, moving beyond the common practice of empirical evaluation alone. The proof correctly builds upon established concepts in online learning (Hannan consistency) and game theory (Nash equilibrium), with appropriate citations to relevant literature (e.g., [15, 16, 18, 19]). The empirical evaluation (Section 5) effectively complements the theoretical results, showing that the MCTS variants converge to approximate Nash equilibria as predicted, with the mean propagation variant (RMM) performing consistently but slightly slower than the current sample value variant.

The work makes a clear contribution to the field by providing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games, which have been widely used in practice without theoretical justification. The paper adequately addresses related work, including Shafiei et al.'s finding that UCB does not converge to Nash equilibrium [17], and correctly positions its contribution relative to prior research.

A minor limitation is that the formal analysis assumes the complete game tree is eventually built (Section 4), which may not hold in finite time for practical applications. The paper acknowledges this by stating "we disregard the incremental building of the tree" but doesn't discuss the implications for finite-time convergence. Future work could address this by providing finite-time bounds, as mentioned in the conclusion.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient details for reproducibility. The empirical methods are sound, and the results are convincing. The theoretical contribution is significant for the field, as it provides a rigorous foundation for using MCTS in a broad class of games where it has been widely applied without theoretical justification.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding MCTS in simultaneous move games. The formal analysis is sound, the empirical validation is appropriate, and the results are significant for both theoretical and practical aspects of game-playing algorithms. I recommend acceptance.
