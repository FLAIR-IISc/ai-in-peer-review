

The paper presents a general template for Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, establishing the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in this class of games. The authors prove that if a selection method is ε-Hannan consistent in matrix games and satisfies additional exploration requirements, the MCTS algorithm converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The proof proceeds through a series of lemmas establishing convergence for repeated matrix games with and without bounded error, culminating in an inductive argument for the full extensive-form game. Empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games confirms the theoretical results.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by addressing a critical gap in the literature. Prior work on MCTS in simultaneous move games lacked convergence guarantees, despite the algorithm's widespread practical use. The connection between online learning theory (Hannan consistency) and MCTS is particularly elegant, providing a new theoretical lens for understanding these algorithms. The formal analysis is technically sound, with careful definitions, well-structured proofs, and appropriate citations to related work in online learning and game theory.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a comprehensive background section that defines all necessary concepts. The algorithm descriptions are precise and easy to follow, and the empirical evaluation appropriately supports the theoretical claims. The authors make a compelling case for the practical relevance of their work, noting that many applications in security domains and general game-playing involve simultaneous move games.

The main weakness is the lack of discussion regarding the tightness of the convergence bound (D² + δ). While the authors acknowledge this bound is likely loose, they do not provide intuition for why this might be the case or suggest directions for improvement. For practical applications, the quadratic dependence on game depth could be prohibitive, making this an important consideration. A brief discussion of potential approaches to tighten the bound would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

Another minor limitation is the empirical evaluation, which primarily focuses on games of depth 2 with branching factor 2. While the worst-case games provide useful insight, expanding the empirical analysis to include deeper games or games with varying branching factors would better demonstrate the algorithm's scalability and practical utility. The paper mentions that "many of these algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium" but does not provide additional empirical evidence for this claim.

The significance of the work is high, as it provides a theoretical foundation for using MCTS in a class of games where it has been widely applied but lacked theoretical justification. The results will be valuable to researchers working on game-theoretic AI applications, particularly in domains like security games and general game-playing where simultaneous move games are common. The connection between online learning and MCTS also opens new research directions at the intersection of these fields.

The paper is well-positioned for NIPS as it makes a strong technical contribution to a fundamental problem in AI. The work connects several important areas: online learning, game theory, and search algorithms, which is highly relevant to the conference's focus on machine learning and AI. The theoretical contribution is substantial, with clear implications for both the theory and practice of game-theoretic AI.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and novel contribution to the field. The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-executed, and the empirical evaluation supports the theoretical claims. The main opportunity for improvement is addressing the tightness of the convergence bound and expanding the empirical evaluation to better demonstrate the algorithm's practical utility across a broader range of game structures. Despite these minor limitations, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field and should be accepted.
