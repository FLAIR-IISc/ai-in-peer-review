This paper addresses the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS is a popular practical algorithm for game tree search, its theoretical guarantees outside of purely sequential games are lacking. Notably, popular MCTS variants like UCT have been shown to fail to converge to a Nash Equilibrium (NE) even in simple single-stage simultaneous move games.

The main contribution of this work is to provide the first formal proof that a class of MCTS algorithms does converge to an approximate NE in this game setting. The authors present a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) where the in-tree selection strategy can be instantiated by any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games. The core theoretical result states that if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees sufficient exploration (i.e., every action pair is tried infinitely often), then the average strategy played by SM-MCTS converges to a subgame perfect ε'-Nash equilibrium. The proof is built up constructively, starting from convergence in single matrix games, extending the result to matrix games where payoffs are observed with bounded error (to model the noisy estimates from subgame simulations), and finally using induction on the depth of the game tree.

The authors experimentally validate their claims using Regret Matching (RM) and Exp3 as instantiations of ε-Hannan consistent selection functions. The experiments, conducted on randomly generated and empirically identified worst-case games, confirm that the proposed algorithms converge as predicted by the theory.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Significant Theoretical Result:** The primary strength of this paper is its theoretical contribution. It is the first work to formally establish convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous move games. This fills a critical gap between the practical application of MCTS-style algorithms and their theoretical understanding, justifying their use as principled approximation algorithms for this class of games.

2.  **Generality of the Framework:** The proof is not specific to one particular selection algorithm (e.g., Regret Matching). Instead, it relies on the general property of ε-Hannan consistency. This makes the result powerful and broadly applicable to a family of online learning algorithms that satisfy this condition, providing a clear recipe for designing provably convergent MCTS variants.

3.  **Sound and Clear Proof Structure:** The theoretical analysis is well-structured and presented in a clear, incremental fashion. By first analyzing repeated matrix games with perfect information, then extending this to games with bounded errors, and finally applying these lemmas inductively to the extensive-form game, the authors make a complex argument accessible and convincing.

4.  **Supportive Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments effectively support the theoretical claims. They not only demonstrate the convergence of the theoretically-backed variants (RMM, Exp3M) but also investigate the behavior of more standard MCTS variants that are not covered by the proof. The inclusion of empirically-found worst-case games is a good step towards understanding the practical convergence rates and robustness of the algorithms.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The formal proof requires that the value propagated up the tree is the *mean* of the rewards observed at a child node. However, standard MCTS implementations and the empirically faster-converging variants in the paper's own experiments propagate the value from the *single current sample*. While the authors acknowledge this and show that the standard variant also converges empirically, the fact that the proof does not cover the empirically superior method is a limitation.

2.  **Lack of Convergence Rate Analysis:** The paper proves asymptotic convergence but does not provide any finite-time bounds on the rate of convergence. The final approximation quality of the equilibrium depends on the depth of the game, D (Theorem 4.11 suggests a dependence on D^2), which could be a very loose bound for deep games. While providing tight finite-time bounds is a hard problem, their absence means the practical utility for a given computational budget is hard to predict from the theory alone.

3.  **Slower Convergence of the Proven Variant:** The experiments show that propagating the mean value (the RMM and Exp3M variants) leads to slightly slower convergence than propagating the single sample value. This makes the theoretically-grounded algorithm less attractive for practical applications. A deeper discussion on why this slowdown occurs would strengthen the paper. For example, is it because the mean value provides a more stable but less aggressive learning signal to the regret calculations in the parent node?

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions:**

*   The presentation of the "A*" algorithm in Definition 4.3 could be clarified. The phrase "without updating any of the variables belonging to A" should be made more explicit, e.g., stating that when a uniform random action is chosen, the internal state of the base algorithm A (such as cumulative regrets for RM) is not modified for that timestep.
*   In the discussion of Theorem 4.11, the paper states that the bound comes from "summing the possible gain of deviations on each level". A sentence or two of additional intuition in the main text on how the errors from subgames accumulate through the levels of the tree would be helpful for the reader, even if the formal details are in the appendix.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of Monte Carlo search. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for a class of algorithms that were previously only justified by empirical success. The weaknesses identified are largely avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current contribution. The paper is well-written, the claims are well-supported, and the results are important for researchers in game theory, reinforcement learning, and general game playing.
