- Addresses convergence of MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, a setting lacking theoretical guarantees.

- Main contribution: first formal proof that a class of SM-MCTS converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium using ε-Hannan consistent selection and sufficient exploration.

- Provides a general SM-MCTS template where any regret-minimizing matrix-game procedure can be used as the in-tree selection strategy.

- Proof strategy: build from single matrix-game convergence → extend to matrix games with bounded payoff noise → induct over game tree depth.

- Empirical validation uses Regret Matching and Exp3 variants, confirming predicted convergence on random and worst-case games.

- Strength: novel, significant theoretical result bridging practical MCTS use and formal guarantees for simultaneous-move games.

- Strength: framework is general—results apply to any ε-Hannan consistent online-learning selection algorithm.

- Strength: proof is well-structured and accessible, with clear incremental lemmas and induction.

- Strength: experiments support theory and examine standard MCTS variants and worst-case behaviors.

- Weakness: proof requires propagating mean child rewards, whereas standard MCTS uses single-sample propagation that converges empirically but is not covered theoretically.

- Weakness: no finite-time/convergence-rate bounds; asymptotic result and D-dependent approximation may be loose for deep games.

- Weakness: the theoretically-backed mean-propagation variants converge slower than single-sample variants; lack of deeper analysis on this slowdown.

- Suggestion: clarify the "A*" algorithm description to state exactly which internal variables are not updated during uniform exploration.

- Suggestion: add intuitive explanation in main text of how subgame errors accumulate across tree levels (Theorem 4.11).

- Overall: strong, well-written paper with important theoretical contributions; weaknesses are opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws.
