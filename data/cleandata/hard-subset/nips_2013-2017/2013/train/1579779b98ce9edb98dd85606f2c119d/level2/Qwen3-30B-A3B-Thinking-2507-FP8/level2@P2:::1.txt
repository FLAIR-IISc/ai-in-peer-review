

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first formal convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors present a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with any regret-minimizing procedure for matrix games, and prove that if the selection method is ε-Hannan consistent with guaranteed exploration, the algorithm converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium. The empirical evaluation using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games confirms the theoretical claims.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses a critical gap in the literature, as previous convergence guarantees for MCTS applied only to sequential games [14, 20], while simultaneous move games are common in applications like security domains and general game-playing [1, 6, 10]. The authors properly contextualize their work against prior research.

2. The theoretical contribution is strong and well-executed. The proof strategy using induction over the game tree structure and leveraging results on repeated matrix games with bounded error is elegant and logically sound. The formalization of required properties (ε-Hannan consistency and guaranteed exploration) is precise.

3. The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors systematically test the influence of propagating mean values versus current samples, and identify worst-case games to evaluate convergence rates. The results confirm theoretical claims and provide practical insights about MCTS variants.

4. The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. Notation is consistent, definitions are precise, and proofs are logically structured. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproduction of results.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper makes a significant simplifying assumption that the complete game tree is built before convergence analysis. While the authors state the tree will eventually be built with probability 1, this weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical guarantees. A more detailed analysis of tree-building convergence rates would strengthen the paper.

2. The bound D² + δ in Theorem 4.11 is quite loose for practical games. The paper doesn't provide empirical evidence of how convergence rates actually scale with game depth, nor does it discuss how this bound affects the practical applicability of their results for deeper games.

3. The paper mentions that "many of these algorithms also converge to ε-Nash equilibrium" but doesn't specify which algorithms might fall outside their theoretical framework. Identifying specific MCTS variants (e.g., UCT) that might be covered by the framework and explaining why they're not included would strengthen the paper.

4. The empirical evaluation of worst-case games is limited to depth-2 games with branching factor 2. Testing on larger games would better demonstrate how the theoretical guarantees scale with game complexity.

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. The paper is well-written, the theory is sound, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed with straightforward revisions. The paper makes a substantial contribution to understanding MCTS for a class of games with wide practical applications, and its results provide theoretical justification for MCTS usage in security domains and other applications.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's theoretical contribution is substantial, and it addresses a clear gap in the literature. The empirical validation provides strong support for the theoretical claims. The minor weaknesses identified above are not critical to the main contribution but would strengthen the paper if addressed.
