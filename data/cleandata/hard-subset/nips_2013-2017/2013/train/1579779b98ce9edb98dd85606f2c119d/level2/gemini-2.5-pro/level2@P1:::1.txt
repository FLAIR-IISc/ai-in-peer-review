This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for two-player, zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The primary contribution is a formal proof that MCTS converges to a subgame perfect ε-Nash equilibrium in this class of games, provided that the in-node action selection method is ε-Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration. The paper presents a general template for Simultaneous Move MCTS (SM-MCTS) and shows that instantiating it with no-regret learning algorithms like Regret Matching (RM) or Exp3 satisfies the conditions for the proof. This work addresses a significant gap in the literature, as prior applications of MCTS in such games lacked theoretical convergence guarantees. The theoretical claims are supported by an empirical evaluation on randomly generated and empirically-selected worst-case games, comparing the convergence of different algorithm variants.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The main strength of this paper is its novel theoretical contribution. To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence for MCTS to a Nash equilibrium in extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. This is a significant result that provides a solid theoretical foundation for applying simulation-based search methods to a broader class of games than was previously understood. The connection established between the ε-Hannan consistency of the local selection policy (within a matrix game at a node) and the global convergence to an equilibrium is elegant and insightful.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The formal analysis is well-structured and appears sound. The proof is built up carefully, starting from the known properties of Hannan-consistent algorithms in single matrix games, extending the analysis to matrix games with bounded errors in payoffs, and then using induction over the depth of the game tree to prove the main theorem for the full extensive-form game. This step-by-step approach makes the argument clear and convincing.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. It begins with clear definitions of the game class and relevant concepts like ε-Nash equilibrium. The general SM-MCTS algorithm is presented clearly, and the specific instantiations with Regret Matching and Exp3 are well-explained. The separation of the formal analysis from the empirical evaluation makes the paper easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Simplifying Assumptions in Analysis:** The formal analysis makes a key simplification by assuming a complete game tree has been built and then analyzing the convergence of the value estimates within that fixed tree. While the authors argue that the tree will eventually be fully explored with probability 1, this sidesteps the complex interplay between the tree growth phase and the value estimation process. In practice, early, biased estimates from a partially grown tree can influence which parts of the tree are explored, and a more complete analysis would account for this dynamic.

2.  **Lack of Convergence Rate Analysis:** The paper proves eventual convergence but does not provide any analysis of the convergence rate. While an asymptotic guarantee is valuable, a finite-time bound on the exploitability (ε) as a function of the number of simulations (t) would be significantly more impactful for practitioners. The provided bound on the final approximation quality (ε depends on D^2) also appears quite loose.

3.  **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments confirm the theoretical convergence but also reveal that the variant required for the proof (propagating the mean value, "RMM") converges slightly slower than the more standard MCTS variant (propagating the last sample's value, "RM"). While this is an honest and interesting result, it somewhat diminishes the practical guidance offered by the theory. Furthermore, the experiments are conducted on small, randomly generated or brute-force-searched "worst-case" games. It would be beneficial to see how these algorithms perform on larger, more structured simultaneous-move games of interest (e.g., a larger instance of Goofspiel).

**Questions and Suggestions for Authors:**

*   Could you comment on the looseness of the O(D^2) factor in the approximation bound of the equilibrium? Is this an artifact of the inductive proof technique, and do you believe a tighter bound is possible?
*   The analysis requires propagating the mean value (Xh/nh) from a child node, whereas standard MCTS propagates the value from the single simulation (u1). Your experiments show the standard approach works slightly better. Do you have any intuition on why the proof requires the mean, and could the theoretical analysis be extended to cover the standard value propagation method?
*   In Section 4, you state "we disregard the incremental building of the tree and assume we have built the complete tree." Could you elaborate in the paper on why the statistics collected during the tree-building phase do not prevent eventual convergence? It seems plausible that early, high-variance estimates could lead the search astray for a very long time.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and novel theoretical result that provides the first convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous move games. The connection it draws between no-regret learning and MCTS convergence is a significant conceptual contribution. While the analysis relies on some simplifying assumptions and lacks a discussion of convergence rates, the core result is an important step forward for the field. The strengths in originality and significance strongly outweigh the weaknesses.
