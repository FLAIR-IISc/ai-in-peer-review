## Review of "Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games"

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a challenging area with limited theoretical understanding. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, under certain conditions – specifically, the use of a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration – the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant gap in the theoretical foundations of MCTS, and empirically validates the theoretical results using Regret Matching and Exp3.

The paper’s strength lies in its rigorous mathematical analysis. The proof of convergence, building from repeated matrix games with bounded error to the full extensive-form game, is a substantial contribution. The clear definitions and lemmas leading to the main theorem enhance the paper’s clarity and allow for easier verification of the results. The empirical evaluation, while not extensive, supports the theoretical findings, demonstrating convergence on both randomly generated and specifically constructed “worst-case” games. The comparison of propagating the mean versus the current sample value is a useful addition, providing practical insight.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The convergence rate, while guaranteed, isn’t explicitly quantified, leaving open the question of practical efficiency. The empirical evaluation could be expanded to include a wider range of game structures and branching factors to better assess the algorithm’s scalability and robustness. The discussion of the limitations is brief; a more thorough exploration of scenarios where the assumptions (e.g., perfect information, zero-sum) don’t hold would be valuable. While the authors acknowledge that other MCTS variants likely converge, they don’t provide a clear roadmap for extending the analysis to these cases.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical contribution establishing convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the mathematical analysis.
*   Empirical validation supporting the theoretical results.
*   Addresses a significant gap in the literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of explicit convergence rate analysis.
*   Limited scope of the empirical evaluation.
*   Brief discussion of limitations.

**Overall:**

This is a solid theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of MCTS. While the empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive, the rigorous analysis and clear presentation justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically encouraging the authors to expand the empirical evaluation and provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of their work.
