Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move extensive-form games. It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that can be instantiated with any selection policy that is epsilon-Hannan consistent (HC) in matrix games and ensures sufficient exploration. The main theoretical result shows almost-sure eventual convergence of the induced average strategies to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE). Specifically, if the game has depth D, the average strategies converge to an (epsilon·D^2 + delta)-NE (for arbitrarily small delta), assuming a “mean backup” (returning node means rather than the last sample) and that each joint action is explored infinitely often. The analysis proceeds by (i) establishing convergence properties of HC learners in repeated matrix games, (ii) extending these results to repeated matrix games with bounded payoff errors, and (iii) lifting them inductively to the extensive-form setting. Empirically, the paper evaluates regret matching and Exp3 variants on random and “empirically worst-case” small games, measuring exploitability. Results confirm convergence and show that mean backups converge slightly slower than sampling the latest value.

Relation to prior work
MCTS and UCT have been successful in many domains, but guarantees in simultaneous-move settings have been lacking; UCB-based selection can fail to converge to NE in simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al., 2013). The work leverages HC selection rules (regret matching, Exp3) known to minimize regret in matrix games (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000; Auer et al., 2002). Backward induction with LP or double oracle can solve these games exactly (Saffidine et al., 2012; Bosansky et al., 2013), but is often impractical. The contribution appears to be the first formal proof that an MCTS-style algorithm converges to approximate subgame-perfect NE in simultaneous-move extensive-form games, complementing empirical SM-MCTS successes reported in game-playing (e.g., Tron, general game-playing).

Quality
Strengths:
- The theoretical framework is well-structured: clear definitions of HC with guaranteed exploration, careful stepping from repeated matrix games to bounded-error games, and then to the tree via induction.
- The “bounded-error” lens is a natural and useful device to capture noise from exploration and non-stationarity at inner nodes.
- The subgame-perfect guarantee is stronger than root-only convergence and is relevant for adversarial planning.
- Empirical results are consistent with the theory and also probe additional variants (e.g., Exp3) beyond those strictly covered.

Weaknesses:
- The guarantees are asymptotic only; no finite-time rates are provided. This limits practical guidance for resource-bounded search.
- The final error bound scales as O(D^2), which may be pessimistic; discussion of whether O(D) is achievable, or where the quadratic accumulation is intrinsic vs. proof-artifact, would be valuable.
- The proof requires mean backups, whereas standard MCTS often propagates the last sample. The experiments suggest both variants converge, but the theory does not cover the more common backup rule.
- The analysis assumes the entire tree is eventually built and argues this does not prevent eventual convergence; details of how incremental tree growth and the chosen expansion policy interact with the selection and backup assumptions are mostly deferred.
- Experiments are on small synthetic games and “empirically worst cases.” There are no results on canonical benchmarks in this class (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), nor comparisons to specialized solvers (backward induction with LP/pruning, double oracle) where tractable.

Clarity
- The paper is generally clear, but notation is heavy. The distinction between empirical frequencies σ̂ and average strategies σ̄ could be signposted earlier with intuition for why they coincide asymptotically (Lemma 4.5).
- The interaction between guaranteed exploration (Definition 4.2), the A* modification (off-policy uniform exploration without updating), and the on-policy γ-mixing used in regret matching should be unified and clarified. As written, selection/exploration differs across sections.
- The necessity of mean backups and why last-sample backups break the proof should be made explicit in the main text (currently mostly implicit in Section 5.1).

Originality
- The convergence proof for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under HC selection appears novel. Prior work has either empirical demonstrations or negative results for UCB in these settings.
- The reduction pathway (HC in matrix games → bounded-error repeated games → extensive-form induction) is an elegant synthesis of online learning and game-tree analysis.

Significance
- The results give a theoretical foundation for a widely used heuristic in a class of games that appear in security and general game playing. This is useful to both theory and practice.
- The absence of finite-time bounds and reliance on mean backups somewhat limit immediate practical impact, but the framework opens promising directions for tighter analyses and broader classes (e.g., imperfect information).

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Clarify and unify the exploration mechanism across sections. State precisely which exploration scheme is assumed in the main theorem and whether on-policy γ-mixing suffices for guaranteed exploration and the proofs.
- Provide a short, self-contained argument in the main text (not only the appendix) that incremental tree growth and expansion criteria do not interfere with eventual convergence; specify minimal assumptions on the expansion policy.
- Discuss whether the D^2 factor can be improved, or isolate where in the inductive argument the quadratic accumulation arises.

Nice-to-have:
- Extend experiments to include at least one established benchmark (e.g., Goofspiel), and compare against an exact solver (LP-based backward induction or double oracle) at small scales to validate exploitability estimates and convergence behavior.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for the exploration parameter γ and its contribution to the final approximation error (noting constants like 4(c+1)).
- Add a brief intuition for why mean backups are required in the analysis and whether alternative unbiased backup schemes could be analyzed.
- Clarify novelty relative to contemporaneous workshop work (e.g., Lanctot et al., 2013) to preempt confusion.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- First formal convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to subgame-perfect approximate NE under broad HC conditions.
- Clean, modular analysis linking online learning and MCTS.
- Empirical results support the claims and explore robust variants.

Cons:
- Asymptotic-only guarantees; no finite-time rates.
- Error bounds likely loose (D^2) and rely on mean backups not standard in practice.
- Limited empirical scope; no benchmark domains or comparison to exact/known solvers where feasible.

Overall assessment
A solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. With clarifications on exploration/backup assumptions and modest empirical extensions, the work would make a valuable addition to the NeurIPS program.
