This paper presents a formal convergence analysis of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors establish a general template for SM-MCTS algorithms and prove that, under certain conditions (specifically, using a -Hannan consistent selection method with appropriate exploration), the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The analysis is complemented by empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 as selection methods on both randomly generated and “worst-case” games.

The paper is well-motivated. Simultaneous-move games pose a significant challenge for traditional game-solving techniques like backward induction, making MCTS a pragmatic alternative. The lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in this setting is a clear gap in the literature, which this paper addresses. The connection to Hannan consistency is insightful, providing a strong theoretical foundation. The inclusion of both theoretical results and empirical validation is a strong point.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The formal analysis is rigorous and provides valuable insights into the convergence behavior of SM-MCTS.  The generality of the template, allowing instantiation with various selection methods, is a significant advantage.  The empirical results support the theoretical findings, showing convergence to approximate Nash equilibria. The discussion of related work (particularly referencing Shafiei et al.’s work demonstrating UCB’s lack of convergence) is appropriate. The authors acknowledge the limitations and suggest avenues for future research, such as extending the analysis to imperfect information games and deriving finite-time bounds. The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The clear definition of notation and the detailed explanations of the algorithms (Algorithm 1 and the variants) are commendable.

However, there are also weaknesses. While the theoretical results are strong, the constant factors involved in the approximation guarantees (specifically, the D^2 term in Theorem 4.11) might be large in practice, potentially limiting the practical applicability. The empirical evaluation, while helpful, feels somewhat limited. The focus on worst-case games and randomly generated games doesn't necessarily represent the types of simultaneous-move games encountered in real-world applications (e.g., security games, negotiation scenarios). It would be beneficial to see the algorithm tested on more realistic game instances, even if they are smaller in scale.  The discussion of the exploration parameter γ could be more extensive. The impact of different values of γ on convergence speed and the quality of the resulting equilibrium isn’t thoroughly explored. The empirical comparison between propagating the mean versus the current sample value (Section 5.1) is interesting, but the difference in convergence speed appears small, and the reason for this difference isn’t fully elucidated. The empirical evaluation lacks a comparison to existing SM-MCTS algorithms other than briefly mentioning them – a direct performance comparison would be very valuable. The connection between the theoretical error bounds and the empirical results could be strengthened.  For example, do the observed convergence rates align with the theoretical predictions?  The long proofs are largely relegated to the appendix (and arXiv link) which, while acceptable, can hinder accessibility for reviewers and potentially readers.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper provides the first formal convergence guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games, a significant contribution to the field.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The analysis is rigorous and based on established concepts from online learning (Hannan consistency).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments support the theoretical results and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed template.
*   **Generality:** The algorithm template is flexible and can be instantiated with various selection methods.
*   **Significance:**  The results have the potential to advance the theoretical understanding of MCTS and its applicability to a broader range of game-theoretic problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Practicality of Bounds:** The constant factors in the convergence guarantees might be large, limiting practical applicability.
*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments lack diversity and don't fully represent real-world applications.
*   **Accessibility:** Proofs are relegated to the appendix making it harder for readers to quickly verify.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on MCTS and game theory. The theoretical results are significant, and the empirical validation provides further support. While the practical implications of the convergence bounds and the limited empirical evaluation are concerns, they do not outweigh the paper’s strengths. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Discuss the practical implications of the D^2 term in Theorem 4.11 and potential strategies for mitigating its impact.
2.  Consider including a comparison with existing SM-MCTS algorithms in the empirical evaluation.
3.  Elaborate on the impact of the exploration parameter γ and provide guidelines for its selection.
4.  Strengthen the connection between the theoretical bounds and the empirical observations.
