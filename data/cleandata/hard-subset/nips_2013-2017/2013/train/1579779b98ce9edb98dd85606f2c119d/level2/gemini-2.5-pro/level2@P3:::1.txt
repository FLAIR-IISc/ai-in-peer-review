### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms in the context of two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves. While MCTS is a popular practical approach for such games, a formal guarantee of its convergence to a Nash Equilibrium (NE) has been a significant open question. Previous work has even demonstrated that popular MCTS variants like UCT do not converge in this setting.

The main contribution of this work is a formal proof that a general template for MCTS in simultaneous move games converges to an approximate subgame perfect Nash equilibrium. The key condition for this convergence is that the action selection mechanism within each node must be an ε-Hannan consistent procedure, such as regret matching (RM) or Exp3, and must guarantee sufficient exploration. The proof is structured inductively on the depth of the game tree. It establishes that convergence in the leaf nodes (which are standard matrix games) provides bounded-error value estimates to their parent nodes. The parent nodes are then treated as matrix games with noisy payoffs, and the analysis shows that ε-Hannan consistent algorithms still converge to an approximate NE in this noisy setting. This reasoning is extended recursively to the root of the game tree.

The theoretical results are complemented by an empirical evaluation on randomly generated and empirically selected worst-case games. The experiments confirm the convergence of MCTS variants using RM and Exp3. They also compare the practical performance of propagating the mean value of rollouts (as required by the proof) versus propagating the value of the most recent rollout (a common heuristic).

### Quality and Technical Soundness

The paper presents a complete and technically sound piece of work. The claims are well-supported by a rigorous formal analysis. The connection drawn between the online learning literature on Hannan consistency and the game-tree search setting is insightful and forms a solid foundation for the main theorem. The proof sketch provided in the main text is clear, and the inductive argument is well-conceived.

The experimental section is carefully designed to validate the theoretical claims. The inclusion of empirically-derived "worst-case" games is a valuable addition, providing insight into the practical convergence rates in challenging scenarios. The paper is honest about the limitations of its analysis, noting that the proof requires propagating mean values, while experiments show that propagating the current sample value converges slightly faster in practice.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, background definitions, proposed algorithm, and theoretical analysis are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. Algorithm 1 provides a clear template for Simultaneous Move MCTS, and the descriptions of the regret matching and Exp3 instantiations are sufficient for understanding. The notation is consistent and standard for the field.

A potential point for improvement in clarity relates to the convergence bound in Theorem 4.11. The bound's dependence on the square of the game depth (D^2) is a key outcome. A brief discussion on whether this quadratic dependency is believed to be tight or is an artifact of the specific proof technique (e.g., a loose bounding of the accumulating error) would provide additional insight for the reader.

### Originality

The work is highly original and addresses a significant gap in the literature. To the best of this reviewer's knowledge, this is the first work to provide a formal proof of convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium for any MCTS-based algorithm in simultaneous move games. While the constituent parts of the algorithm (MCTS, regret matching) are not new, their combination and the accompanying theoretical analysis are novel and non-trivial. The paper correctly positions its contribution with respect to prior work, highlighting the known non-convergence of UCT and the lack of guarantees for other heuristic approaches.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are of high significance to the fields of machine learning, computational game theory, and artificial intelligence.
1.  **Theoretical Foundation:** It provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for using MCTS in a large and important class of games, moving the approach from a successful heuristic to a provably correct approximation algorithm.
2.  **Methodological Contribution:** The analytical technique of treating nodes in the game tree as repeated matrix games with bounded error from subgame estimates is a powerful one. This methodology could potentially be adapted to analyze algorithms in more complex settings, such as games with imperfect information.
3.  **Practical Implications:** The work validates the use of regret-minimization techniques within MCTS and provides guidance on algorithm design for practitioners aiming to solve large-scale simultaneous move games. It directly advances the state of the art by providing a family of convergent algorithms for this problem class.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Presents the first formal proof of convergence for MCTS in simultaneous move games, a significant and long-standing open problem.
*   The work is technically sound, with a rigorous proof that connects online learning theory to recursive game solving.
*   The results are highly significant, providing a theoretical justification for a practical class of algorithms and offering a new analytical framework.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and supported by relevant experiments.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   The theoretical analysis covers a variant of the algorithm (propagating mean rewards) that is shown to be slightly slower empirically than a more common heuristic (propagating the last sample). However, this is a minor point and is transparently addressed by the paper.

### Suggestions for Improvement (Optional):
*   As noted under "Clarity", a short discussion on the tightness of the D^2 term in the main convergence theorem would be welcome.
*   The paper shows that the theoretically-backed "mean propagation" is empirically slower. It would be interesting to briefly comment on why the current proof technique does not apply to "sample propagation" and what challenges a formal analysis of that more common variant would entail. This could spur valuable future work.
*   For the empirically found worst-case games, a brief characterization of what structural properties make these games difficult for the algorithms could provide useful intuition.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of game-playing algorithms. It is well-executed, clearly presented, and highly significant. It is a strong candidate for acceptance.
