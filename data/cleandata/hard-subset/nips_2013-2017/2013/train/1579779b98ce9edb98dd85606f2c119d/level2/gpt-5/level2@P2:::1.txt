Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player zero-sum, perfect-information, simultaneous-move games (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template where each node hosts an online learner that selects actions. The main theoretical contribution is a convergence guarantee: if the per-node selection method is ε–Hannan consistent (HC) in matrix games and ensures sufficient exploration, then SM-MCTS with mean-value backups converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium (NE). Specifically, the induced average strategies converge to an (ε·D^2+δ)-NE, where D is game-tree depth and δ>0 is arbitrary. The analysis proceeds by: (i) recalling properties of HC algorithms in repeated matrix games; (ii) extending to repeated games with bounded payoff errors; and (iii) bootstrapping up the tree to bound error accumulation. Empirically, the paper instantiates the template with regret matching (RM) and Exp3, showing convergence on random games and on empirically difficult small games, and compares mean-backup vs. sample-backup variants.

Relation to prior work is sound. UCT is known to fail to converge in some simultaneous-move games (Shafiei et al.), while exact methods (backward induction with LP/double-oracle) can be costly or require domain knowledge. Counterfactual regret minimization (CFR) targets imperfect-information EFGs; guarantees for SM-MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs have been lacking. The paper connects established HC learners (RM, Exp3) to MCTS and provides the first proof of eventual convergence to approximate NE in this setting.

Quality
- Theory: The high-level argument is reasonable and builds on standard online learning facts. Lemma 4.6 and its extension (Lemma 4.9) adapt regret-based convergence to approximate NE under bounded payoff error. The induction up the game tree is natural. However, constants appear loose: the matrix-game result yields (4+δ)-NE under ε-HC, error terms scale with level d, and the final bound is O(ε·D^2). It seems plausible that with tighter tracking of bias/variance and best-response deviations this could be reduced to O(ε·D), or at least with improved constants (e.g., why 4(c+1) rather than 2(c+1)?). The requirement to back up means (rather than samples) is critical to the proof; this diverges from common SM-MCTS implementations.
- Assumptions: “Guaranteed exploration” is required (Definition 4.2). The paper ensures it via a constant γ “off-policy” exploration (Definition 4.3), yielding ε+γ–HC (Lemma 4.4). This implies the limiting strategy is necessarily mixed with γ, which empirically leads to a nonzero exploitability floor. It would be useful to analyze decaying exploration schedules that still ensure ∑t p_t(i,j)=∞ for all (i,j) but allow γ_t→0.
- Incremental tree growth: The proof assumes the finite tree is eventually fully expanded and argues that early statistics do not preclude convergence. Full formal details are deferred to the appendix. While acceptable for a convergence-in-the-limit result, this assumption weakens practical implications for large trees.
- Experiments: The empirical section confirms convergence qualitatively, compares mean vs. sample backups (mean is slightly slower), and probes small worst-case games. This is helpful but limited: (i) only small depths/branching factors; (ii) no benchmarks like Goofspiel/Tron; (iii) no comparisons to exact/heuristic baselines (e.g., alpha-beta + LP/DO), CFR variants, or prior SM-MCTS variants beyond a brief mention; (iv) no finite-sample rates or ablations (e.g., different γ schedules). The observation that exploitability plateaus to the exploration floor is consistent with the theory but underscores the need for decaying exploration.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a clean template (Alg. 1) and precise definitions. Some constants and notational choices could be sharpened (e.g., explicitly carrying ε through results; clarifying the “(ε D^2+δ)” bound in Theorem 4.11 as typesetting obscures ε). Several proofs are in the appendix; giving at least a concise sketch of the tree-level induction with explicit error propagation would help.

Originality
To my knowledge, this is the first convergence proof for SM-MCTS to approximate NE in perfect-information simultaneous-move games under broad per-node HC learners, unifying RM/Exp3 within a single template. The “repeated matrix game with bounded error” device and the upward induction are straightforward but novel in this context.

Significance
The result provides theoretical justification for regret-based SM-MCTS and gives actionable guidance (use HC learners; ensure sufficient exploration; mean backups). While asymptotic and with loose constants, it addresses a notable gap. Practical impact would be strengthened by finite-time bounds and empirical validation on larger benchmarks.

Pros
- First general convergence guarantee for SM-MCTS to approximate NE using HC selection.
- Clean, modular analysis that leverages standard online learning tools.
- Instantiation with RM and Exp3; empirical confirmation on random and adversarial small games.
- Subgame-perfect guarantee (not just root play).

Cons
- Asymptotic-only; no finite-time bounds and likely loose constants (O(ε·D^2)).
- Requires mean-value backups; in practice these are slower than sample backups.
- Constant exploration leads to a non-vanishing exploitability floor; no analysis of decaying exploration.
- Proof relies on eventual full-tree expansion; limited practical guidance for large trees.
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scale and baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Tighten constants and, if possible, reduce the depth dependence from D^2 to O(D); clarify where the factor 4 arises in Corollary 4.7 and whether 2 is achievable in zero-sum games.
- Analyze decaying exploration schedules that preserve “guaranteed exploration” (e.g., γ_t∝1/t or ∝1/√t) and characterize the resulting asymptotic exploitability.
- Explore whether sample backups can be handled via concentration or bias-control arguments (e.g., martingale techniques), to avoid the practical slowdown of mean backups.
- Provide finite-sample convergence rates under standard HC rates for RM/Exp3 and explicit dependence on depth/branching factor.
- Expand experiments: include standard simultaneous-move benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron), compare against UCB-variants known to fail (to illustrate difference), alpha-beta+LP/DO where feasible, and CFR-style baselines. Report wall-clock and sample complexity.
- Clarify the joint-action selection and update scheme for both players at each node, and how average strategies are extracted (empirical vs. time-averaged; consistency with Lemma 4.5).

Minor comments
- Fix typesetting where ε appears to be missing in “( D^2+δ)” in Thm 4.11.
- Cite years consistently in references and ensure completeness.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution addressing an important gap, with reasonable empirical support. With tightened analysis and stronger experiments, it would be a strong addition. I lean toward acceptance.
