- Studies MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, providing a general MCTS template.  
- Proves convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium under selection methods that are ε-Hannan consistent plus additional exploration.  
- Empirically validates convergence using regret matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and designated "worst-case" games.  
- Well-motivated problem: simultaneous-move games are important and previously lacked MCTS guarantees.  
- Clever use of ε-Hannan consistency gives a solid theoretical foundation.  
- Strong theoretical contribution: a formal, structured convergence proof is the paper’s main strength.  
- General template allows instantiation with different selection/regret-minimizing methods.  
- Clear definitions and consistent notation improve readability.  
- Empirical results, though limited, support the theoretical claims for both tested selectors.  
- Good literature connection and comprehensive references to related MCTS and game-theory work.  
- Empirical scope is limited: few games/iterations and no broad comparisons to other simultaneous-move MCTS variants.  
- Proof complexity and technical length may reduce accessibility for readers not expert in online learning/game theory.  
- “Worst-case” games are insufficiently explained—selection criteria and why they’re challenging are unclear.  
- Practical impact unclear: convergence rates and competitiveness versus heuristic MCTS in real tasks are not assessed.  
- Limited analysis on propagating mean vs. sample values; trade-offs and causes of observed small differences aren’t explored.  
- Arguments for acceptance: significant theoretical advance, rigorous proof, preliminary empirical support, and clear writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical evaluation, potentially inaccessible proof, and underexplored practical implications.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions—expand empirical evaluation, add proof intuition, and include comparative benchmarks (e.g., a performance table).
