- Main claim: SM-MCTS with any ε–Hannan consistent selection rule plus guaranteed exploration converges a.s. to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium; bound scales O(ε D^2).
- Proof approach: reduces node selection to repeated matrix games with bounded error and bootstraps convergence from leaves upward via induction.
- Empirical setup: evaluates Regret Matching and Exp3 on random and hard depth-2 games, measuring exploitability and comparing mean-propagation vs. sample-return updates.
- Empirical findings: experiments corroborate convergence; mean-propagation converges slightly slower but aligns with theoretical requirements.
- Relation to prior work: reconciles negative UCT results for simultaneous-move games with positive guarantees for no-regret selectors and complements exact solvers (backward induction, LP/DO) by offering sampling-based asymptotics.
- Theoretical quality: careful development using standard online learning facts (HC, exploration) and a clear bounded-error framework; induction to subgame perfection is well-motivated.
- Theoretical limitations: guarantees are asymptotic with loose constants (D^2 dependence and factors like 4) and no finite-time/sample-complexity bounds.
- Practical limitation: requirement to propagate mean returns (vs. samples) is only partly justified empirically and raises implementation concerns.
- Experimental quality: experiments are correct but limited in scale and do not probe sample complexity or larger, practical games.
- Clarity: paper is generally clear and organized; some notational overload and duplicated RetVal choices could be streamlined.
- Reproducibility: pseudocode is helpful but more detail needed on rollout policies, exploration rate γ, and learning-rate hyperparameters.
- Originality: likely first convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move perfect-information EFGs; HC-to-NE lifting under bounded error is a neat synthesis.
- Significance: provides theoretical footing and a unifying template for bandit-based selection in SM-MCTS, though practical impact is tempered by asymptotic nature and small experiments.
- Pros for acceptance: first rigorous convergence guarantees for broad HC selectors; clean modular analysis to subgame perfection; empirical confirmation and insight on update choices.
- Cons for acceptance: lacks finite-time bounds and tight constants; experiments are small and narrow with limited baseline comparisons; fixed exploration causes exploitability floor.
- Suggested theoretical work: derive finite-time/sample-complexity bounds, tighten D-dependence, and study decaying exploration schedules.
- Suggested empirical work: evaluate on larger games and compare to practical baselines (α–β + DO, MCCFR variants) and probe sample complexity.
- Suggested analysis/engineering: analyze when sample-return propagation can still converge (bias/variance trade-offs) and provide code plus full hyperparameter reporting and rollout details.
