This paper investigates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, a challenging setting where theoretical convergence guarantees are lacking. The work establishes a general template for MCTS algorithms in these games and proves that, under certain conditions (specifically, -Hannan consistency and adequate exploration of the selection method), the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The results are empirically validated using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated and worst-case games.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, extending its analysis beyond traditional sequential games. The core contribution—proving convergence to approximate NE under stated conditions—is valuable and well-motivated. The choice to focus on -Hannan consistent selection methods is sensible, providing a concrete link to established online learning theory. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing different selection methods and testing on both random and specifically designed "worst-case" games.  The connection drawn to existing MCTS algorithms, and the conjecture that many also converge, adds to the impact.

A major strength is the rigorous mathematical analysis. The proof sketch presented in the paper, while not fully detailed (with significant portions deferred to an appendix and arXiv submission), appears sound and logically structured. The careful definitions and lemmas leading up to the main theorem build a strong foundation. The separation of concerns – analyzing the repeated matrix game with error before tackling the full extensive-form game – is a good strategy.  The inclusion of the exploration guarantee alongside Hannan consistency is crucial and often overlooked.

However, the paper suffers from some weaknesses. The empirical evaluation, while reasonably comprehensive, lacks detailed statistical analysis. Reporting means and standard deviations, or confidence intervals, would strengthen the conclusions. The discussion of the performance differences between using the mean and current sample value for propagation feels somewhat inconclusive. While the theory suggests a potential slowdown with the mean, the empirical results appear marginal. A more detailed analysis of the conditions under which this slowdown is more pronounced would be useful.

The connection to existing SM-MCTS algorithms is mentioned, but could be more explicitly explored. For instance, how do the practical implementations of these algorithms (e.g., [11, 12, 20] cited in the paper) map onto the proposed MCTS template? A more detailed comparative analysis would clarify the novelty and broader applicability of the theoretical results.  Finally, the section on empirical convergence rate relies on identifying “worst-case” games through limited runs. A more systematic approach to worst-case game identification, or a theoretical justification for the selected games, would increase confidence in the empirical findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a fundamental open problem in MCTS theory, providing the first convergence guarantees for simultaneous-move games.
*   The mathematical analysis is rigorous and well-structured.
*   The empirical validation supports the theoretical results.
*   The work has potential to influence the design and analysis of MCTS algorithms for a wider range of games.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation lacks detailed statistical analysis.
*   The connection to existing SM-MCTS algorithms could be more thoroughly explored.
*   The identification of "worst-case" games is not fully justified.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper is a strong contribution to the field. While the empirical analysis could be strengthened and the connection to existing work made more explicit, the theoretical results are significant and the work is well-executed. The paper meets the NIPS criteria for originality, significance, quality, and clarity. A recommendation for acceptance is warranted, with a request for the authors to provide a more detailed statistical analysis of the empirical results and to elaborate on the relationship between their template and existing SM-MCTS implementations.
