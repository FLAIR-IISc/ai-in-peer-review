- First formal proof of convergence for MCTS in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information games with simultaneous moves.
- Presents a general MCTS template usable with any regret-minimizing action-selection procedure.
- Main theorem: if the selection algorithm is ε-Hannan consistent and ensures sufficient exploration, MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium.
- Connects MCTS convergence to online learning and regret-minimization literature (Hart & Mas-Colell, Auer et al.).
- Original and significant: fills a challenging open problem and justifies broader use of MCTS from a game-theoretic perspective.
- Technical quality: sound, elegant proof via induction from leaf nodes.
- Empirical support: experiments on random and worst-case games confirm convergence for Regret Matching and Exp3 variants.
- Clear presentation: well-written, organized, and definitions/analysis structure are easy to follow.
- Theory/practice gap: proof uses rollout mean-value propagation while practical implementations use sample-value propagation, which converges faster experimentally.
- Asymptotic-only result: no finite-time convergence rates or bounds on number of simulations to reach ε.
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, technically solid, significant impact, and likely to inspire follow-up work.
- Arguments against acceptance: does not cover empirically superior variant and lacks finite-time guarantees; overall recommendation: strong accept.
