## Review of “Convergence of Monte Carlo Tree Search in Simultaneous Move Games”

This paper investigates the convergence properties of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves – a challenging setting lacking theoretical guarantees despite practical success. The authors present a general MCTS template and prove that, under the assumption of using a -Hannan consistent selection method satisfying exploration requirements, the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. The results are empirically validated using Regret Matching and Exp3 on randomly generated and worst-case games, confirming the theoretical findings.

The paper addresses a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of MCTS, extending its known convergence properties beyond two-player sequential games. The formal analysis is a strong point, providing a rigorous justification for the use of MCTS in these complex game settings. The authors clearly define the necessary conditions ( -Hannan consistency and exploration) and relate them to existing algorithms. The empirical evaluation, while not exhaustive, supports the theoretical claims and demonstrates convergence on tested instances. The inclusion of both randomly generated and specifically constructed "worst-case" games strengthens the empirical results.

However, the paper lacks discussion of the practical implications of the convergence rate. While proven to converge, the rate might be prohibitively slow for complex real-world scenarios. The paper acknowledges this implicitly by focusing on approximate Nash equilibria but doesn't quantify the approximation error or its dependence on game parameters. Furthermore, the exploration parameter γ is somewhat arbitrarily set in the experiments; a more systematic exploration of its impact on convergence speed would be valuable. The connection between the theoretical analysis (assuming a completed tree) and the practical iterative tree-building process could also be discussed more explicitly.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant theoretical gap in MCTS research.
*   Provides a rigorous proof of convergence under well-defined conditions.
*   Empirical results support the theoretical claims.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks discussion of the practical convergence rate.
*   Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity (e.g., γ).
*   Weak connection between the theoretical model and iterative MCTS implementation.

**Overall:** This is a valuable contribution to the field, providing a solid theoretical foundation for the application of MCTS in simultaneous-move games. While some aspects could be further explored, the paper's strengths outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically requesting a discussion of practical convergence rates and the impact of key parameters.
