- Presents a formal convergence analysis and general template for SM-MCTS in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves, proving convergence to approximate Nash equilibria under -Hannan consistent selection with exploration.  
- Complements theory with empirical evaluation using Regret Matching and Exp3 on random and “worst-case” games.  
- Well-motivated: simultaneous-move games resist backward induction, making MCTS a practical alternative and filling a theoretical gap.  
- Insightful connection to Hannan consistency, providing a solid online-learning foundation for the analysis.  
- Rigorous formal analysis that illuminates SM-MCTS convergence behavior.  
- Template is general and can be instantiated with various selection methods.  
- Empirical results support the theoretical findings by demonstrating convergence to approximate equilibria.  
- Appropriate related-work discussion, including noting prior results (e.g., Shafiei et al.) showing UCB’s lack of convergence.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and propose future work (e.g., imperfect information, finite-time bounds).  
- Paper is well-written, logically organized, with clear notation and detailed algorithm descriptions.  
- Practicality concern: approximation guarantees include large constant factors (notably a D^2 term in Theorem 4.11) that may limit real-world usefulness.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope and does not test realistic application domains (e.g., security or negotiation games).  
- Exploration parameter γ is underexplored; its effect on convergence speed and equilibrium quality is insufficiently analyzed.  
- The mean-vs-sample propagation difference is only briefly observed and not fully explained or characterized.  
- Lacks direct empirical comparisons to existing SM-MCTS algorithms, reducing practical benchmarking value.  
- Connection between theoretical error bounds and empirical convergence rates is weak and should be strengthened.  
- Long proofs are placed in the appendix/arXiv, which hampers accessibility for readers and reviewers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty (first formal guarantees for SM-MCTS), theoretical soundness, empirical validation, generality, and significance.  
- Arguments against acceptance: potentially impractical bounds, limited empirical diversity, and reduced accessibility of proofs.  
- Overall recommendation: strong paper meriting acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: discuss practical impact and mitigation of the D^2 term; add comparisons with existing SM-MCTS methods; elaborate guidance on selecting γ; and better align theoretical bounds with empirical results.
