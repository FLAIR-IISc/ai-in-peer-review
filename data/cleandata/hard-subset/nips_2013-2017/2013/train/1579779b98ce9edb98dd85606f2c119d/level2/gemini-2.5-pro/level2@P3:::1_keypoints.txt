- Main contribution: proves a general MCTS template converges to an approximate subgame-perfect Nash equilibrium in simultaneous-move games.  
- Key condition: action selection at each node must be ε-Hannan consistent (e.g., RM or Exp3) and ensure sufficient exploration.  
- Proof technique: inductive argument on tree depth treating leaves as matrix games and parents as matrix games with noisy payoffs.  
- Error control: shows ε-Hannan consistent algorithms still converge to approximate NE even with bounded noisy payoffs, extending recursively to the root.  
- Experiments: empirical evaluation on random and worst-case games confirms convergence of RM/Exp3-based MCTS variants.  
- Mean vs sample propagation: theoretical proof requires propagating mean rollout values, but empirically propagating the latest sample converges slightly faster.  
- Technical soundness: analysis is rigorous, well-supported, and insightfully connects Hannan consistency from online learning to MCTS.  
- Experimental design: includes carefully constructed worst-case games to study practical convergence rates.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written and organized; algorithms and notation are clear and standard.  
- Clarity suggestion: discuss whether the D^2 dependence in Theorem 4.11 is tight or an artifact of the proof.  
- Originality: first known formal proof of convergence for any MCTS-based algorithm in simultaneous-move games.  
- Significance — theory: provides a foundational theoretical justification for MCTS in this important game class.  
- Significance — methodology: introduces a useful analytical technique of modeling nodes as repeated matrix games with bounded subgame error.  
- Significance — practice: validates regret-minimization within MCTS and offers practical algorithmic guidance.  
- Acceptance arguments: addresses a long-standing open problem with rigorous analysis, strong experimental support, and clear exposition.  
- Main downside: theoretical analysis focuses on mean-propagation variant which is empirically slightly slower than common sample-propagation heuristics.  
- Suggestions for improvement: discuss D^2 tightness, explain why proof doesn’t extend to sample propagation, and characterize structural features of worst-case games.
