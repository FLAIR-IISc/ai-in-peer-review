- Proposes an SM-MCTS template using ε-Hannan-consistent selection with guaranteed exploration and proves almost-sure convergence of average strategies to a subgame-perfect (ε·D^2 + δ)-Nash equilibrium under mean backups and infinite joint-action exploration.  
- Analysis builds from HC learners in repeated matrix games → bounded-error repeated games → inductive lifting to extensive-form trees.  
- Empirical evaluation tests regret matching and Exp3 variants on small random and worst-case games, showing convergence and that mean backups converge slightly slower than last-sample backups.  
- Positions work against prior negative results for UCB in simultaneous-move games and leverages known HC selection rules (regret matching, Exp3); contrasts with exact LP/double-oracle solvers.  
- Theoretical framework is well-structured with clear HC definitions and a useful bounded-error abstraction.  
- Subgame-perfect (not root-only) convergence guarantee is a strong, relevant result for adversarial planning.  
- Empirical results align with theory and explore variants beyond those strictly covered by proofs.  
- Guarantees are asymptotic only; no finite-time convergence rates provided.  
- Final error bound scales as O(D^2), which may be pessimistic; unclear if quadratic factor is intrinsic or proof artifact.  
- Proof requires mean (node-mean) backups while common MCTS uses last-sample backups; theory does not cover the more typical backup rule.  
- Assumes eventual full tree growth; interaction of incremental expansion policies with selection/backup assumptions is insufficiently detailed.  
- Experiments are limited to small synthetic/worst-case games; no canonical benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel, Tron) or comparisons to exact solvers.  
- Notation is heavy; distinction between empirical frequencies and average strategies should be clarified earlier with intuition for asymptotic coincidence.  
- Exploration mechanisms (guaranteed exploration, A* modification, on-policy γ-mixing) are presented inconsistently and should be unified.  
- The necessity of mean backups and why last-sample backups break the proof should be made explicit in the main text.  
- Convergence proof for SM-MCTS under HC selection appears novel and is an elegant synthesis of online learning and game-tree analysis.  
- Results provide a theoretical foundation for heuristics in simultaneous-move games, useful to theory and practice despite practical limitations.  
- Essential fixes: unify/clarify exploration assumptions; give a main-text argument that incremental tree growth doesn't prevent convergence; isolate source of D^2 factor.  
- Nice-to-have improvements: add benchmark experiments and comparisons to exact solvers; sensitivity analysis for exploration parameter γ; brief intuition or alternatives for mean backups.  
- Pros for acceptance: first formal subgame-perfect SM-MCTS convergence under broad HC conditions; modular analysis; supportive empirical results.  
- Cons for acceptance: asymptotic-only guarantees, possibly loose D^2 error bound, reliance on nonstandard mean backups, limited experimental scope.  
- Overall: solid theoretical contribution that advances understanding of MCTS in simultaneous-move games, deserving acceptance after clarifying assumptions and modest empirical extensions.
