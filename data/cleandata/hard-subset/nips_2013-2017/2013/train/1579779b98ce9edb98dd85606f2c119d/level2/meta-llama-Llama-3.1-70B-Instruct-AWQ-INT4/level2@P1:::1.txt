This paper presents a general template of Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithms for zero-sum perfect-information simultaneous move games. The authors formally prove that if a selection method is -Hannan consistent in a matrix game and satisfies additional requirements on exploration, then the MCTS algorithm eventually converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium (NE) of the extensive-form game. They also empirically evaluate this claim using regret matching and Exp3 as the selection methods on randomly generated games and empirically selected worst-case games.

The main contribution of this paper is the formal analysis of the convergence of MCTS algorithms in simultaneous move games. The authors provide a general template for MCTS algorithms and prove that any -Hannan consistent algorithm can be used to create a MCTS algorithm that provably converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the game. This is a significant contribution to the field of algorithmic game theory.

The paper is well-written and well-organized. The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and the related work. They also provide a detailed explanation of the MCTS algorithm and the selection methods used in the paper.

The empirical evaluation of the paper is also well-done. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the convergence rate of the algorithms and compare the performance of different selection methods. The results show that the MCTS algorithm with regret matching and Exp3 selection methods converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium of the game.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the formal analysis is limited to -Hannan consistent algorithms. It would be interesting to see if the authors can extend the analysis to other types of algorithms.
