This paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in zero-sum extensive-form games with perfect information and simultaneous moves. The authors provide a general template for MCTS algorithms in this setting and prove that, under certain conditions (specifically, -Hannan consistency of the selection method and additional exploration requirements), the algorithm converges to an approximate Nash equilibrium. They empirically validate this claim using regret matching and Exp3 as selection methods on randomly generated games and "worst-case" games.

The paper is well-motivated. Simultaneous-move games represent a challenging, but important, class of problems with applications in various domains. The lack of theoretical guarantees for MCTS in this setting is a significant gap that this paper addresses. The approach of leveraging -Hannan consistency is clever and provides a solid theoretical foundation. The empirical evaluation, while not exhaustive, is a good starting point and supports the theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The formal convergence proof is the main strength of the paper. Establishing guarantees for MCTS in simultaneous-move games is a valuable contribution. The proof appears well-structured, and the authors clearly outline the necessary conditions.
*   **General Template:** Presenting a general MCTS template that can be instantiated with different selection methods is beneficial. This allows for flexibility and potential application of other regret-minimizing procedures.
*   **Clear Definitions and Notation:** The paper provides clear definitions of key concepts and uses consistent notation throughout. This enhances readability and understanding.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experiments, though limited in scope, provide evidence supporting the theoretical results. Showing convergence with both regret matching and Exp3 is reassuring.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately acknowledges relevant work in MCTS, game theory, and related areas. The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Empirical Scope:** The empirical evaluation is relatively limited. While the authors test on randomly generated and "worst-case" games, the number of games and iterations appears small. A more comprehensive empirical study, including comparison with other MCTS variants specifically designed for simultaneous-move games (e.g., those cited in the introduction), would strengthen the conclusions.  Specifically, the paper mentions Shafiei et al. [17] showing UCB doesn’t converge; a comparison showing *why* their method succeeds where UCB fails would be valuable.
*   **Complexity of Proof:** While the proof is structured, its length and technicality might make it difficult for readers not deeply familiar with online learning and game theory to fully grasp. Providing more intuition or a higher-level summary of the proof steps could improve accessibility.
*   **Definition of "Worst Case" Games:** The paper refers to "worst-case" games but doesn't fully explain how these games were selected or why they are considered challenging. More details on the criteria used to identify these games would be helpful.
*    **Practical Impact:** While the theoretical guarantees are valuable, it's not entirely clear how much practical benefit this convergence result provides. Does the rate of convergence implied by the theorem make this approach competitive with existing, heuristic-based MCTS algorithms in real-world applications? This aspect is not thoroughly addressed.
*   **Propagation of Mean vs. Sample:** The comparison between propagating the mean and the current sample value seems limited. A more in-depth analysis of the trade-offs between these two approaches would be useful. The results show a small difference, but the reasons aren’t fully explored.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the convergence properties of MCTS in a challenging game setting.
*   The formal proof is rigorous and well-motivated.
*   The empirical results provide preliminary support for the theoretical claims.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in scope.
*   The complexity of the proof may hinder accessibility for some readers.
*   The practical implications of the theoretical result are not fully explored.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. The theoretical result is significant, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. While the empirical evaluation could be strengthened and the proof simplified for broader accessibility, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance. I recommend accepting the paper with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly expanding the empirical evaluation and providing more intuition for the theoretical results.  Specifically, including a comparative table to prior works' performance on benchmark games would be very helpful.
