- Main contribution: formal proof that MCTS converges to a subgame-perfect ε‑Nash equilibrium in two-player zero-sum perfect-information games with simultaneous moves, given ε‑Hannan-consistent, exploring in-node selection.

- Provides a general SM‑MCTS template and shows instantiations with no-regret algorithms (Regret Matching, Exp3) satisfy the proof conditions.

- Fills a literature gap: first theoretical convergence guarantee for MCTS in simultaneous-move extensive-form games.

- Theoretical analysis is well-structured and sound: builds from Hannan-consistency in matrix games to bounded-payoff errors and uses induction over tree depth.

- Empirical results support the theory on random and worst-case games.

- Clarity: paper is well-written, definitions and algorithm instantiations are clearly presented; analysis and experiments are cleanly separated.

- Weakness: analysis assumes the complete game tree is already built, ignoring the interaction between tree growth and value estimation (early biased estimates may misdirect exploration).

- Weakness: no finite-time convergence rates or bounds on exploitability as a function of simulation count; the provided ε bound with an O(D^2) factor appears loose.

- Weakness: empirical finding that the proof-required mean-value propagation (RMM) converges slightly slower than standard last-sample propagation (RM) reduces immediate practical guidance; experiments are limited to small or worst-case games rather than larger structured domains.

- Request for clarification: comment on whether the O(D^2) looseness is an artifact of the inductive proof and whether tighter bounds are possible.

- Request for clarification: explain why the proof requires propagating mean child values rather than standard last-sample values and whether the analysis can be extended to standard propagation.

- Suggestion: elaborate on why statistics gathered during tree-building do not prevent eventual convergence, given potential long-lasting effects of early high-variance estimates.
