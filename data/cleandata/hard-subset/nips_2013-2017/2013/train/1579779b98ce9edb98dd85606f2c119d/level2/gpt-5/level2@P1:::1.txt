Summary
The paper studies Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) in two-player, zero-sum, perfect-information extensive-form games with simultaneous moves (“stacked matrix games”). It proposes a general SM-MCTS template that uses any no-regret (ε–Hannan consistent) selection procedure at each internal matrix game, with explicit exploration. The main theoretical result shows that if the per-node selection algorithm is ε–Hannan consistent and guarantees exploration, then SM-MCTS converges almost surely to a subgame-perfect approximate Nash equilibrium. The bound scales as O(D^2 ε), where D is the depth of the game tree (Theorem 4.11). The analysis proceeds by: (i) establishing convergence of empirical strategies for repeated matrix games under ε–HC (and a version allowing bounded payoff errors), (ii) showing that backing up mean values yields bounded bias, and (iii) inducting up the tree to obtain a global approximation guarantee. Empirically, the paper validates convergence using regret matching and Exp3 on random and empirically difficult games, and studies the practical impact of backing up sample means versus single samples.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution: to my knowledge, this is the first general convergence guarantee for MCTS in perfect-information simultaneous-move games, a setting where UCT is known to fail to converge.
- Generality: the analysis applies to any selection rule that is ε–Hannan consistent with exploration, including regret matching and Exp3; the result is subgame-perfect and does not rely on domain heuristics.
- Sound proof structure: the decomposition into repeated games with bounded error, and the induction over depth, is technically clean and easy to follow. The handling of exploration-induced bias is explicit.
- Empirical support: experiments confirm convergence behavior with two distinct no-regret methods and illustrate the (modest) slowdown introduced by backing up means, as required by the proof.
- Practical insight: the paper explains why using mean backups improves analyzability and why uniform exploration causes a nonzero asymptotic exploitability floor, which matches empirical observations.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Tightness of the bound: the O(D^2 ε) guarantee appears loose. The proof sketch indicates linear accumulation of errors per level and a factor of two when passing from value bounds to ε–NE, yielding the D^2 factor. It would help to discuss whether this can be improved (e.g., O(D ε)) by sharpening the error composition, using weighted backups, or by refining the passage from best-response value gaps to ε–NE.
- Finite-time rates: since RM and Exp3 have known regret rates, it seems feasible to derive explicit finite-sample convergence bounds for exploitability as a function of T, D, branching factors, and exploration γ. Even a heuristic derivation or discussion would strengthen practical relevance.
- Mean versus sample backups: the key theorem relies on backing up means (SM-MCTS-M), while most practical MCTS variants back up samples. The experiments suggest both variants converge, but the paper stops short of a proof. Please specify conditions under which sample backups also inherit the convergence guarantee (or provide a counterexample). A brief discussion of whether Polyak/Ruppert averaging or importance weighting could bridge the gap would be useful.
- Exploration mechanism: two exploration schemes are presented (on-policy mixing and the A* wrapper that withholds updates during uniform exploration). The consequences for bias and variance differ. Unifying the presentation (and clarifying which form is used in each theorem/experiment) would improve clarity. Also, a brief tradeoff analysis between γ, convergence speed, and asymptotic exploitability would help practitioners choose γ.
- Scope and scalability: while the theoretical results assume the full tree is eventually expanded (almost surely), experiments are limited to small random and depth-2 worst-case games. Additional empirical evidence on deeper trees or on standard benchmarks (e.g., Goofspiel/Tron instances used in prior work) would better demonstrate scalability. A discussion of progressive widening or rollout policies in large branching-factor games would be welcome.
- Related work positioning: The paper could more directly compare to regret-minimization approaches in EFGs (e.g., CFR-type methods) and earlier SM-MCTS variants (decoupled UCT). Clarifying how this result complements those lines (e.g., perfect vs imperfect information, simultaneous vs sequential) would help readers situate the contribution.

Quality
The theoretical claims are well-supported and technically sound, with proofs deferred to the appendix. Assumptions are explicit (ε–HC, guaranteed exploration, mean backups). The empirical section is careful and aligns with the theory, though limited in scale.

Clarity
Overall clear and well-organized. Suggestions for clarity:
- Streamline notation for empirical versus average strategies and collect all definitions in a single subsection for quick reference.
- Make explicit early that mean backups are required for the main proof and consistently label which variant (RM/Exp3 vs RM-M/Exp3-M) is used in each experiment.
- Briefly restate the practical interpretation of Lemma 4.9 (where the 4 and c+1 factors arise) in the main text.

Originality
High in the specific setting of simultaneous-move, perfect-information games: establishing convergence of MCTS to approximate NE under no-regret selection appears new. The combination of online learning guarantees with MCTS in this setting is a novel and useful bridge.

Significance
Moderate to high. The result gives long-missing theoretical grounding for a family of algorithms used in practice for simultaneous-move games. It should influence future SM-MCTS designs and encourage principled use of no-regret learners at internal nodes. Stronger finite-time and tighter depth dependence would further enhance impact.

Questions for authors
- Can the D^2 dependence be tightened with a different backup scheme or by using confidence bounds on value estimates per node?
- Can you leverage known regret rates for RM/Exp3 to state an explicit T-dependent exploitability bound?
- Under what conditions (if any) do sample backups also converge? Is there a simple counterexample if not?
- How sensitive are results to non-uniform exploration (e.g., ε-greedy with annealing γt)?

Pros for acceptance
- First convergence guarantees for SM-MCTS to (subgame-perfect) approximate NE.
- General, modular analysis applicable to multiple no-regret algorithms.
- Clean proofs and empirical confirmation.

Cons for acceptance
- Asymptotic nature of guarantees; no finite-sample bounds.
- Loose dependence on depth; mean-backup requirement may limit immediate applicability.
- Limited experimental scale and benchmarks.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution with practical relevance. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarity and positioning suggestions.
