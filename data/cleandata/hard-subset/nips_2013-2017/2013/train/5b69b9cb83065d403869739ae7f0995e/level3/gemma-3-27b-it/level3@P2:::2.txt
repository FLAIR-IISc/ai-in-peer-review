This paper proposes a new approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work on AMP and applying it to the clustering problem by recasting it as a constrained reconstruction task. The core idea is to leverage the flexibility of the Bayesian framework and efficiently approximate the posterior distribution using an AMP-based approach. The algorithm is evaluated on both synthetic and real datasets, demonstrating competitive performance against Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in low-rank matrix completion, Bayesian inference, and approximate message passing. The authors correctly identify the benefits of the Bayesian approach in incorporating prior knowledge about matrix structure. The use of AMP, motivated by its efficiency and theoretical guarantees in compressed sensing, is a reasonable direction. Related work in variational Bayes matrix factorization [3, 4, 5] and other AMP-based methods for matrix reconstruction [8, 9, 13, 14] are acknowledged, although the discussion could be more thorough, as detailed below. The application of low-rank matrix reconstruction to clustering is also not novel [10, 11], but the authors attempt to address the constraint of exclusive cluster assignment within this framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Technically Sound:** The proposed algorithm appears to be a logically derived extension of existing AMP techniques. The mathematical formulation, while dense, is generally understandable and outlines a clear algorithmic procedure.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The numerical experiments demonstrate that the proposed AMP-based algorithm outperforms Lloyd’s K-means in certain settings, particularly for higher-dimensional data. The improvement over K-means is a positive result.
*   **Novelty (Incremental):** While not a revolutionary departure, the extension of AMP to handle the general rank case and the application to clustering with explicit cluster assignment constraints represent incremental contributions.
*   **Potential Significance:** The algorithm's efficiency and accuracy, if validated more broadly, could be valuable for applications requiring scalable low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The primary concern is the evaluation strategy. Evaluating the algorithm primarily through a clustering lens, while interesting, obscures its potential benefits in general low-rank matrix reconstruction. The lack of comparison to standard matrix completion benchmarks (e.g., on datasets like Netflix or image inpainting tasks) makes it difficult to assess its competitiveness. The authors should justify this choice or include results on standard datasets.
*   **Limited Novelty:** The algorithm is a relatively direct application of AMP principles to a known problem. The connection to expectation propagation (EP) and related Bayesian inference techniques is not discussed, missing an opportunity to situate the work within a broader context.
*   **Asymptotic Approximations:** The reliance on large-m and large-N asymptotic approximations is not sufficiently justified. A discussion of the conditions under which these approximations hold, and their potential impact on performance in practical settings, is needed.
*   **Clarity & Presentation:** The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The mathematical notation is cluttered and unnecessarily complex, hindering readability. Equations (9a, 9b, 9d) are particularly difficult to parse. Explanations of key functions (f, G) are delayed, and the role of the *m* factor in the noise variance is unclear. Exposition lacks obvious simplifications and connections that would aid understanding.
*   **Scholarship & Citation:** The paper lacks sufficient scholarship. The claim of novelty in applying matrix factorization to clustering is misleading without proper attribution to prior work. Important related methods (e.g., EP) are not cited or discussed. The VBMF baseline requires a clear citation specifying the implementation details.
*   **Derivation in Supplementary Material:** The core derivation of the algorithm is relegated to the supplementary material, making it harder for readers to follow the methodology and assess its validity. Key steps should be summarized or included in the main text.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm demonstrates improved performance compared to Lloyd’s K-means in the tested scenarios.
*   The technical derivation appears sound, and the AMP framework offers a potentially efficient approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction.
*   The application to clustering, while not novel in concept, provides a concrete demonstration of the algorithm's capabilities.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited scope of the evaluation hinders assessment of the algorithm's broader applicability.
*   The lack of novelty and the missing connections to related work reduce the paper's impact.
*   The clarity and presentation issues significantly detract from the paper's readability and understanding.
*   The reliance on asymptotic approximations without sufficient justification raises concerns about the algorithm's robustness.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. However, the current version is hampered by clarity issues, a limited evaluation scope, and insufficient contextualization within the existing literature. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit, addressing the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should: (1) evaluate the algorithm on standard matrix completion datasets, (2) clarify the mathematical presentation and notation, (3) provide a more thorough discussion of related work, (4) justify the asymptotic approximations, and (5) improve the overall clarity and readability of the manuscript.
