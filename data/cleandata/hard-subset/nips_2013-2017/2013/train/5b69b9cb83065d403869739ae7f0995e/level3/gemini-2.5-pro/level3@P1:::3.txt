This paper introduces an algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for low-rank matrix reconstruction. The problem is formulated within a Bayesian framework, allowing for the flexible incorporation of prior knowledge about the matrix factors, such as sparsity or non-negativity. The core contribution is the derivation of a new AMP algorithm for this problem, extending previous work to handle general MAP estimation, not just posterior means. The authors unify the treatment of marginalization and MAP estimation problems through a one-parameter extension (β) of the posterior distribution. As a key application, the paper reformulates K-means clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints on one of the factor matrices. The derived AMP algorithm is then specialized for this task, resulting in a novel clustering method. This method is compared experimentally against Lloyd's K-means algorithm and the k-means++ initialization scheme on both synthetic and real-world data.

### Strengths

The primary strength of this paper is the application of the powerful AMP framework to the problem of low-rank matrix factorization and, subsequently, to clustering. The resulting algorithm for K-means clustering (Algorithm 3) is particularly interesting, as it modifies the standard assignment step with bias-correction terms that have an intuitive interpretation (Eq. 18b). The experimental results, though limited in scope, are promising. The proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms consistently outperform the standard Lloyd's K-means and k-means++ on the chosen datasets, both in terms of the K-means loss and clustering accuracy. This suggests that the approach has practical merit.

The paper is also well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is well-explained, and the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is laid out effectively. The unified treatment of MAP and marginalization problems via the parameter β is an elegant theoretical device.

### Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper has significant weaknesses regarding technical soundness, originality, and the significance of its contributions.

**1. Quality and Technical Soundness:**

The mathematical derivations are extremely brief and omit many non-trivial steps, making it difficult to verify the correctness of the proposed algorithm. Several key claims and approximations lack sufficient justification.

*   **Q1:** The derivation in the supplementary material (referenced in Section 4.1) relies on approximations whose validity is not clearly established. For instance, in line 139 of the appendix, it is not obvious why the second term of equation (11) should be O(1/√m). Furthermore, the β parameter appears in equation (8) and the subsequent algorithm but is not introduced in the problem setting in Section 2, which seems inconsistent.
*   **Q2:** The asymptotic analysis used to derive the AMP algorithm appears to assume that m and N grow at the same order. These arguments are presented very tersely, and their rigor is questionable without a more detailed proof, which is essential for establishing the validity of the resulting algorithm.
*   **Q3:** In deriving the AMP update for clustering, the paper states that `m^{-2} \sum_{i=1}^m A_{ij}^2 S_i^t` is estimated by `\tau m^{-1} \sum_{i=1}^m S_i^t`. This seems to approximate `m^{-1} E[A_{ij}^2]` with the noise variance `\tau`. However, A is the observed data, not a random variable from the noise distribution; it also contains the signal component. This approximation requires much more careful justification.
*   **Q4:** The paper claims that the algorithm for finite β converges to the MAP solution in the limit β → ∞. However, this relies on exchanging the limit and the minimization of the KL divergence. This exchange of limits is not always valid and requires a formal proof.
*   **Q5:** The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief. In particular, it is not shown how equation (16) is derived from the fixed point conditions of the AMP algorithm for the MAP problem. A more detailed derivation is needed to support this claim.

**2. Originality:**

The proposed algorithm may not be as novel as presented. The derivation from belief propagation and the resulting update equations bear a strong resemblance to Expectation Propagation (EP). It is highly plausible that the proposed algorithm is equivalent to a direct application of EP to the Bayesian matrix factorization model, where the likelihood term is approximated. The update functions f and G in equation (10) are essentially moment-matching updates, which are characteristic of EP. The authors should explicitly discuss the relationship between their AMP derivation and EP, referencing relevant work such as Stern et al. (WWW 2009) on EP for matrix factorization. Without this comparison, the novelty of the technical contribution is unclear.

Additionally, the bias correction terms in the specialized clustering algorithm (Eq. 18b) are reminiscent of techniques used in EP to account for the "self-contribution" of a data point to a parameter's posterior, as seen in problems like the "clutter problem" (Minka, 2001). A discussion of this connection would strengthen the paper by placing it in a broader context.

**3. Significance:**

The significance of the work is hampered by the issues above and the limited experimental evaluation.

*   The comparison is made almost exclusively against k-means and k-means++. While demonstrating an improvement here is valuable, the clustering literature is vast, containing many more sophisticated and powerful algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering, Gaussian mixture models with EM). To claim a significant advance in clustering, a more comprehensive comparison against stronger and more diverse baselines is necessary.
*   The combination of sketchy proofs and potential overlap with prior work in EP reduces confidence in the paper's contribution. If the algorithm is a re-derivation of EP, its significance lies in the specific application and empirical results, which, as noted, are not yet benchmarked broadly enough.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and interesting application of the AMP framework to clustering.
*   The proposed clustering algorithm shows promising empirical performance, outperforming the widely used k-means++ baseline.
*   The paper is well-written and the core ideas are easy to understand.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Major concerns about technical soundness due to highly abbreviated and insufficiently justified derivations.
*   Significant questions about originality, with a strong possibility that the algorithm is a re-derivation of Expectation Propagation.
*   Limited experimental comparison makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed method in the broader context of clustering research.

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting idea with promising preliminary results, the concerns regarding its technical rigor and originality are too substantial for it to be accepted in its current form.
