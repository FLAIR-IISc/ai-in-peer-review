Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional structural priors on the factors and derives an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm from belief propagation for the “tempered” posterior p̂(U,V|A;β). By varying β, the same machinery purportedly covers both marginal inference (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞). A specialization to clustering is obtained by using a discrete one-hot prior on V; this yields an AMP variant of K-means with an assignment rule that corrects for the self-bias of the current cluster membership, while maintaining O(mN) per-iteration cost. Proposition 1 claims a coordinate-wise optimality property at fixed points for the MAP case. Experiments on synthetic data and on ORL faces indicate improvements over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ in loss and, for the marginal version, in accuracy; the AMP methods converge in a small number of iterations.

This work connects to a large literature on AMP for linear models and bilinear problems, including IterFac (rank-one), low-rank AMP, and bilinear GAMP (e.g., Rangan 2011; Matsushita & Tanaka 2012; Parker et al. 2013; Krzakala et al. 2013). It also relates to variational Bayesian matrix factorization, dictionary learning, and EP-style matrix factorization approaches (e.g., Stern et al., WWW 2009). The clustering specialization is close in spirit to K-means (Lloyd) but with corrections akin to Onsager terms in AMP.

Quality
- Derivation details: The main derivations are quite terse, even in the supplement, and several nontrivial steps are omitted.
  Q1: Appendix line 139: please justify rigorously why the second term of (11) scales as O(1/√m). What randomness and normalization are used, and where does the 1/√m arise from (e.g., central limit scaling of sums over m)? Also, β seems to be omitted in Section 2 (e.g., in the definition of the tilted factors and the covariance scaling). Please reconcile the presence of β in (8)–(11) with the subsequent equations; if β is absorbed by rescaling Λ or b, state this explicitly.
  Q2: The asymptotic arguments (including derivation of (19)) appear to assume m,N→∞ at the same order. Please state the precise limit (e.g., N/m→δ, r fixed) and the distributional assumptions required for the AMP approximation and state evolution to be valid. A step-by-step derivation of (19), with concentration statements and references to Bayati–Montanari-style proofs, would greatly improve rigor.
  Q3: Line 167: equating m τw^2 with E[Ail^2] is not justified as written because A contains both signal and noise. If A = A0 + W with Var(Wil)=mτ, then E[Ail^2] = E[A0,il^2] + mτ under independence. Using E[Ail^2] as a proxy for mτ implicitly assumes negligible signal or requires subtracting an estimate of E[A0,il^2]. In Algorithm 3 you propose using the residual ∥A−UV⊤∥F^2/(mN) to estimate τ; please make this consistent and justify the approximation (e.g., via consistency under state evolution).
  Q4: Convergence to MAP as β→∞: showing that fixed points/minimizers of the β-tempered Bethe free-energy converge to MAP solutions requires care; exchanging limit and minimization is not generally valid. Please either provide conditions under which Γ-convergence or epi-convergence holds for your objectives, or give a direct argument that your updates reduce to the β→∞ proximal/MAP updates (e.g., f∞, G∞ limits) and that fixed points correspond to stationary points of the MAP problem.
  Q5: Proposition 1: the proof is too brief. In particular, provide a clear derivation of equation (17): show that the U-update solves a penalized problem CMAP(U,Vt) minus a quadratic in U−Ut−1 with weight (1/2mτ)∑jTt j, deriving this from the stationarity conditions of the AMP updates and identifying the Onsager term. Please also specify assumptions ensuring ∑iS t i and ∑jT t j are positive semidefinite and discuss when this can fail.

- Convergence and stability: In practice AMP often benefits from damping. Please report whether damping/step-size control was used, and provide convergence diagnostics. The oscillations you observe in assignments suggest the need for damping or a tie-breaking schedule.

- Experiments: The synthetic setup is clear. For ORL, you switched to K-means++ seeding for AMP to avoid empty clusters; this is reasonable, but then AMP and K-means++ differ in both update rule and seeding. Report results for both random and K-means++ seeding for both methods for fairness, and include multiple seeds with variability bars. Also assess sensitivity to rank mis-specification (r̂≠r).

Clarity
The paper is well-organized and the main ideas are easy to follow. However, the supplement must fill in missing steps for the derivations noted above. Minor suggestions:
- Define dimensions of all matrices upon first use in Algorithm 2 and collect the functions f, G (and their β→∞ limits) in a self-contained subsection.
- Keep β consistently visible in all derivations, or state explicitly when it is absorbed.
- Provide pseudocode boxes with stopping criteria and any damping parameters for reproducibility.

Originality
The β-tempering to unify marginalization and MAP within an AMP framework is a nice touch, and specializing to one-hot priors to obtain an AMP-corrected K-means is interesting. However, the core update structure closely resembles EP/Bethe approximations with Gaussian site parameters; there is prior EP-based matrix factorization work (e.g., Stern et al., WWW 2009), and AMP has been connected to EP in large-system limits. The paper should explicitly position itself relative to EP: are your updates equivalent to EP with specific factorizations and Onsager corrections? Similarly, relation to bilinear GAMP (Parker et al., 2013) should be clarified, especially regarding MAP estimation and discrete priors. The claim of being the first to directly enforce one-hot constraints within a low-rank reconstruction framework should be tempered or carefully scoped.

Significance
The clustering gains over Lloyd’s K-means/K-means++—especially the bias-corrected assignment—are promising. Yet, the evaluation is narrow for NIPS: comparisons to stronger clustering baselines (e.g., spherical K-means, EM for GMMs with tied covariance, spectral clustering, NMF-based clustering) are missing. The theoretical contribution would be more compelling with a precise state evolution and a rigorous MAP limit. As it stands, sketchy proofs and potential overlap with EP-based methods reduce confidence in the technical advance.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation; unifies marginal and MAP inference via β.
- Elegant specialization to clustering with intuitive bias correction in assignments.
- Low per-iteration complexity and fast convergence in experiments.
- Empirically improves over K-means/K-means++ on synthetic and ORL.

Cons
- Derivations are terse and omit key steps; several claims need justification (Q1–Q5).
- Relationship to EP/bilinear GAMP is not clarified, raising originality concerns.
- Limited experimental comparison to broader clustering literature.
- No convergence guarantees; practical stabilization details (damping) not reported.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The ideas are interesting and potentially useful, but the paper needs stronger, clearer derivations (especially Q1–Q5), a precise positioning relative to EP/bilinear GAMP, and broader empirical comparisons. I encourage the authors to revise accordingly.

Constructive suggestions
- Expand the supplement to provide full derivations, explicit assumptions for the large-system limit, and a careful treatment of the β→∞ limit.
- Clarify the EP connection and differences, and add an ablation showing the effect of the Onsager terms (i.e., Algorithm 2 vs. VB/ICM).
- Broaden clustering baselines; analyze sensitivity to r̂ and to prior mis-specification.
- Document damping/line-search and include a brief state-evolution heuristic or proof sketch.
