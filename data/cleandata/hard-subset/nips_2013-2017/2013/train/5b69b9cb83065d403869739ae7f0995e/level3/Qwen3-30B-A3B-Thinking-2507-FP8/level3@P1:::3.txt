

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural properties, extending previous AMP work in compressed sensing to matrix factorization. The authors reformulate K-means clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem and apply their AMP algorithm to derive a novel clustering method. The main technical contribution is extending the AMP framework to handle general-rank matrices and both marginalization and MAP estimation within a unified framework. The paper claims the proposed algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means algorithm in numerical experiments on synthetic and real data (ORL face database).

The paper's strengths include a clear problem formulation, well-structured presentation, and strong experimental results. The algorithm is shown to achieve lower K-means loss and higher accuracy than K-means++ on both synthetic and real datasets, with comparable or faster convergence. The paper's clear articulation of the connection between matrix reconstruction and clustering provides valuable insight.

However, the paper suffers from significant quality concerns. The mathematical derivations are extremely sketchy, omitting non-trivial steps even in the supplementary material. For instance, the asymptotic analysis (including derivation of equation 19) appears to assume m and N grow at the same order without proper justification. The authors claim mτ_w^2 = E[A_il^2] (line 167), but this conflates likelihood variance with data statistics and lacks justification. The convergence of the finite-β AMP algorithm to the MAP solution is not rigorously established, and the proof of Proposition 1 is too brief to follow (equation 17 derivation is unclear).

Regarding originality, the algorithm appears to be essentially an application of Expectation-Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization, which has been previously studied (Stern et al., WWW 2009). The paper's AMP framework for matrix reconstruction resembles standard EP updates, and the authors do not adequately distinguish their approach from existing EP-based methods. The connection to Minka's "clutter" problem (which deals with similar clustering constraints) is not addressed, though this could be a promising direction for future work.

The significance of the contribution is limited by insufficient comparison to relevant literature. The authors only compare to K-means++ (Lloyd's algorithm), ignoring a substantial body of work on matrix factorization-based clustering (e.g., non-negative matrix factorization, convex matrix factorization). The paper does not sufficiently address how their method differs from or improves upon these established approaches.

The paper's clarity is excellent, making the main contribution accessible despite the technical shortcomings. However, the quality concerns significantly undermine confidence in the theoretical claims. The sketchy proofs and omitted derivations make it difficult to assess whether the algorithm's claimed advantages are theoretically sound.

In summary, while the paper presents an interesting application of AMP to matrix reconstruction and clustering with promising empirical results, the technical shortcomings significantly weaken its contribution. The algorithm appears to be a straightforward application of existing EP methods to matrix factorization without adequate novelty or theoretical justification. The limited comparison to relevant literature further reduces the perceived significance of the work.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear problem formulation and well-structured presentation
- Strong empirical results showing improved performance over K-means++
- Novel connection between matrix reconstruction and clustering

Arguments against acceptance:
- Sketchy mathematical derivations with omitted non-trivial steps
- Algorithm likely equivalent to existing Expectation-Propagation approaches
- Insufficient comparison to relevant clustering literature
- Theoretical claims lack proper justification and rigor

The paper does not meet NIPS's quality standards due to its insufficient theoretical foundation. While the experimental results are promising, the lack of rigorous mathematical justification and failure to properly contextualize the work within existing EP literature significantly diminishes its contribution. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the theoretical arguments, provide a more thorough comparison to relevant prior work, and clarify how their approach differs from standard EP techniques before this work would be suitable for NIPS.
