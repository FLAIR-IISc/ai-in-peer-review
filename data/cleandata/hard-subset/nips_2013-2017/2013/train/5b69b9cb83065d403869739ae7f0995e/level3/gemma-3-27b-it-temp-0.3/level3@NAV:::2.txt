## Review of “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing”

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work to handle MAP estimation and applying it to a clustering problem framed as matrix reconstruction. The authors demonstrate empirical benefits over Lloyd’s K-means in their experiments. While the algorithm itself is promising and the mathematics are generally followable, the paper suffers from clarity issues, limited validation, and a lack of sufficient contextualization within the broader research landscape.

The core idea of leveraging AMP for low-rank reconstruction is reasonable, building on successes in compressed sensing and related fields. Framing clustering as a matrix factorization problem is also not novel, but the application of AMP to this specific formulation is a contribution. However, the paper lacks a thorough discussion of related work. The connection between matrix factorization and clustering is presented as if it’s a new insight, without citing established literature (e.g., Ding et al., 2010). Furthermore, the relationship to Expectation Propagation (EP) and its connection to BP with continuous distributions is entirely absent, a significant oversight given the algorithm’s foundation. 

A major concern is the evaluation. While the results on the clustering task are positive, evaluating solely on this non-standard formulation makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s performance against established matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix dataset). The authors do not justify this choice, hindering a clear understanding of the algorithm’s broader applicability. The asymptotic approximations (m→∞, N→∞) are mentioned but their validity isn’t rigorously tested or justified, raising questions about the practical relevance of the theoretical framework.

The paper is hampered by significant clarity issues. The mathematical presentation is often convoluted, with confusing equations (9a, 9b, 9d, 9e) and unexplained notation. Excessive use of hats, tildes, and subscripts clutters the equations, making them difficult to parse. Definitions of functions like fβ and Gβ are unclear upon first use. The derivation, crucial to understanding the algorithm, is relegated to the supplementary material, a significant drawback. The English and phrasing throughout require polishing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The algorithm shows empirical benefits in the clustering experiments.
*   The application of AMP to this specific problem formulation is a novel contribution.
*   The mathematics, while dense, are generally sound.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of evaluation on standard matrix reconstruction datasets.
*   Limited discussion of related work and novelty.
*   Poor clarity in mathematical presentation and notation.
*   Insufficient validation of asymptotic approximations.
*   Key derivation steps are hidden in the supplementary material.



**Overall:** The paper presents a potentially valuable algorithm, but its current form falls short of NIPS standards. Significant revisions addressing the clarity, validation, and contextualization issues are necessary before it can be considered for acceptance.
