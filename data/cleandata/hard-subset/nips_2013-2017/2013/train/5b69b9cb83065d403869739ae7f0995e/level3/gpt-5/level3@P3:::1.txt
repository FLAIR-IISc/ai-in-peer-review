Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations under factor-structured priors and develops an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm to perform Bayesian inference. The model assumes a low-rank factorization A0 = U0V0^T with flexible priors on rows of U0 and V0 (e.g., sparsity, nonnegativity). The authors introduce a β-parameterized posterior to unify marginal inference (β=1) and MAP (β→∞), derive a general-rank AMP with Onsager corrections, and show how to compute posterior means, marginal MAPs, or MAP solutions via proximal-like updates f, G that depend on the chosen priors. They then reformulate K-means clustering as a special low-rank matrix reconstruction with discrete one-hot constraints on V, yielding an AMP variant (AMP-KM) that modifies Lloyd’s assignment step by bias-correction and cluster-size terms. They also propose an AMP variant for maximum-accuracy clustering (AMP-MA) with discrete priors on V. Experiments on synthetic data and the ORL Faces dataset compare against Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++.

The work connects to AMP for sparse linear models and generalized AMP (e.g., Donoho-Maleki-Montanari; Rangan; Bayati-Montanari), and to prior AMP/bilinear work (IterFac; Matsushita-Tanaka; later BiG-AMP) while emphasizing the ability to treat MAP, non-Gaussian priors, and fixed rank. The clustering-as-factorization connection is well known (e.g., NMF clustering; PCA yields a 2-approximation to K-means), but this paper’s AMP instantiation for K-means with exact one-hot constraints appears novel.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clear, general AMP derivation for low-rank matrix reconstruction with factor priors, unifying β-parametrized marginal and MAP inference; reduction to VB-like updates when Onsager terms are removed is insightful.
- Novelty: AMP for the K-means objective with principled assignment corrections is new and interesting, bridging low-rank AMP and classical clustering.
- Practicality: O(mN) per iteration; functions f and G adapt to priors (closed-form for discrete priors; convex proximal for MAP when −log prior is convex).
- Empirical evaluation: Solid comparisons to K-means and K-means++; both Frobenius residuals and clustering accuracy are reported. AMP-MA shows high accuracy and fast convergence; AMP-KM improves K-means loss substantially on synthetic and ORL data.
- Clarity of connection: The link from the AMP-MAP specialization to K-means (one-hot V, uniform priors) is clean, and the bias-correction intuition for assignments is compelling.

Weaknesses
- Theory: Convergence guarantees are weak. State evolution is mentioned but not developed; there is no general proof of convergence or global optimality, only a fixed-point optimality proposition under PSD conditions.
- Scope of baselines: Comparisons omit spectral clustering, GMM/EM, SDP/local-search approximations, NMF-based clustering, and mini-batch K-means; wall-clock times are not reported.
- Robustness/assumptions: AMP derivations rely on dense random models and large-system limits with i.i.d. Gaussian noise; behavior off-model (e.g., non-Gaussian noise, heavy-tailed data, rank misspecification, correlated factors) is not systematically explored.
- Practical guidance: Limited discussion of damping, stepsizes, and β-scheduling (e.g., annealing) to stabilize AMP; sensitivity to initialization and choice of priors is not quantified.

Quality
- Technically sound derivation with correct placement of Onsager terms and careful mapping to VB/ICM. However, the main claims rely on empirical validation; theoretical convergence and performance guarantees (e.g., via state evolution) are deferred.
- Experiments are well-executed for the chosen baselines, with appropriate metrics (residuals and accuracy), but broader baselines and runtime reporting would strengthen the empirical case.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The algorithm presentation via concise update equations is effective; the β-unification and the K-means specialization are explained cleanly with good intuition for the assignment correction terms.
- Suggestions: Provide pseudocode with damping options; move key parts of the derivation or state evolution summary from the supplement into the main text; add a paragraph on computational cost of f/G for common priors and recommended implementations.

Originality
- The general-rank AMP that handles posterior means and MAP for flexible factor priors, and its direct specialization to K-means with one-hot constraints, are novel combinations. While the connection between low-rank factorizations and K-means is classical (e.g., PCA’s 2-approximation), an AMP algorithm targeted at the K-means objective is new.

Significance
- If stabilized and adopted, the approach could impact scalable clustering and Bayesian low-rank modeling by providing faster, higher-quality solutions than Lloyd-type methods, particularly in high dimensions. Lack of convergence theory tempers the near-term impact but the empirical promise is strong.

Specific suggestions
- Theory: Include at least an outline of state evolution for the β=1 case and conditions under which the Onsager terms make ∑S_i and ∑T_j PSD; discuss damping/stepsize and convergence diagnostics; report failure modes (oscillations) and mitigations.
- Baselines: Add spectral clustering, GMM/EM with diagonal/full covariances, NMF-clustering, local-search or k-means++ with multiple restarts and minibatch updates; include wall-clock time and memory.
- Robustness: Evaluate sensitivity to r̂ ≠ r, non-Gaussian noise, correlated factors, varying N/m, and mis-specified priors; ablations on the assignment correction terms.
- References: Cite more theoretical CS work on K-means with guarantees, e.g., k-means++ approximation [Arthur & Vassilvitskii], PTAS/local-search (Kanungo et al.), smoothed analysis of Lloyd’s algorithm, stability/approximation results (e.g., Ostrovsky et al., Awasthi et al.). Clarify distinctions from contemporaneous bilinear GAMP (BiG-AMP) and earlier low-rank AMP (Matsushita-Tanaka).

Questions for the authors
- Do you observe consistent empirical state-evolution tracking for β=1? Can you report MSE trajectories vs. predictions?
- What damping or annealing schedule do you recommend to avoid oscillations in AMP-KM?
- How sensitive are results to the τ estimation heuristic and to the initialization of V?
- Can f/G be computed in closed form for common continuous priors (e.g., Gaussian, Laplacian, spike-and-slab) and what are the practical runtimes?

Pros
- Novel AMP framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible priors; clean β-unification of marginal and MAP.
- Elegant and effective AMP specialization to K-means with principled assignment corrections.
- Strong empirical results on synthetic and real data; evaluates both residuals and clustering accuracy.

Cons
- No strong theoretical convergence guarantees; state evolution omitted.
- Limited baselines and no runtime comparisons; robustness not thoroughly evaluated.

Overall assessment
A solid AMP-based contribution that advances low-rank Bayesian inference and provides a novel, effective AMP algorithm for K-means-style clustering. The experimental results are promising and suggest practical gains over K-means/K-means++. The main weakness is the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees and limited breadth of empirical baselines. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on strengthening the theory discussion (at least empirically validating state evolution) and expanding empirical comparisons and references to theoretical k-means literature.
