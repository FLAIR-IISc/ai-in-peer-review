This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix factorization. The framework is flexible, accommodating various prior distributions on the factor matrices to incorporate structural properties like sparsity. The authors derive the algorithm by applying approximations to the belief propagation algorithm on the corresponding factor graph. This general method is then specialized to the problem of clustering, which is framed as a matrix factorization problem where one factor matrix consists of one-hot indicator vectors. The resulting AMP clustering algorithm is compared experimentally against k-means++, showing superior performance in terms of the K-means loss function and clustering accuracy on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The main idea of developing an AMP algorithm for matrix factorization and applying it to clustering is easy to grasp. The experimental results, though limited in scope, are promising and suggest that the proposed algorithm is more effective at escaping poor local minima compared to standard iterative methods like Lloyd's algorithm, even when the latter is combined with sophisticated initialization (k-means++). The connection between the AMP update rules and a penalty term that discourages staying in the current configuration (Section 4.3) provides some intuition for this improved performance.

**Weaknesses**

Despite its clarity, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in its technical depth, originality, and the scope of its evaluation.

**Quality:** The primary concern is the lack of mathematical rigor. The derivations are extremely sketchy, omitting many non-trivial steps that are essential for verifying the claims. This is true for both the main paper and the appendix. Specific issues include:

*   **Q1:** In Appendix line 139, the derivation relies on the second term of equation (11) being O(1/√m). This is a crucial step in the approximation, but it is stated without any justification. Further, the derivation mentioned in Section 2, which leads to this appendix, seems to omit the inverse temperature parameter β.
*   **Q2:** The asymptotic analysis, which underpins the AMP framework, appears to assume that matrix dimensions m and n grow at the same order. This assumption is not explicitly stated or justified. The arguments are terse, and key results like the derivation of the clustering update rule in equation (18b) lack the necessary rigor to be convincing.
*   **Q3:** In line 167, the authors state that `m τ_w^2` is estimated by `E[A_{il}^2]`. This is confusing, as `A` is the observed data, not a random variable from which one can take an expectation in this context. While `m τ_w^2` is the variance of the noise in the generative model, simply replacing it with an empirical moment of the observed data is an approximation that requires careful justification, which is not provided.
*   **Q4:** The paper claims that the algorithm for finite β converges to the MAP solution as β → ∞. This claim is not proven. Such a result would require showing that the limit and the KL minimization can be exchanged, which is not necessarily valid and requires a formal argument.
*   **Q5:** The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief to be verifiable. In particular, it is not shown how equation (16), which re-expresses the update for U as a penalized optimization problem, is derived from the AMP update rules.

**Originality:** There is a strong possibility that the proposed algorithm is not novel but is instead a rediscovery of Expectation-Propagation (EP) applied to matrix factorization. The message updates derived via the "cavity method" are characteristic of EP, and the final algorithm structure closely resembles EP. Stern et al. (WWW 2009) have previously applied EP to a similar collaborative filtering problem. The authors should have thoroughly discussed the relationship between their AMP derivation and the well-established EP framework. The update functions `f` and `G` in equation (10) are essentially the moment-matching updates for an exponential family distribution, which are standard in EP.

Furthermore, for the clustering application, the problem formulation has parallels with the EP treatment of "clutter" or outlier models (e.g., Minka, 2001), where data points are assigned to either a model component or a background distribution. A discussion of this connection would have better situated the work within existing literature.

**Significance:** The paper's significance is diminished by the technical sketchiness and potential lack of originality. The experimental comparison is too narrow; focusing solely on k-means++ is insufficient given the vast literature on clustering. To demonstrate a significant practical contribution, the algorithm should have been compared against other widely used methods such as spectral clustering or Gaussian Mixture Models (fit via EM). Without a broader comparison and with the theoretical foundations being so poorly substantiated, it is difficult to be confident in the paper's practical and technical contributions.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Pros:**
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.
*   The proposed clustering algorithm shows promising empirical performance against a strong k-means baseline.
*   The application of AMP-style algorithms to clustering is an interesting direction.

**Cons:**
*   Major concerns about technical soundness due to sketchy, unverified mathematical derivations.
*   Significant concerns about originality; the algorithm appears to be equivalent to Expectation-Propagation, a connection the authors fail to make.
*   Experimental evaluation is too limited, comparing only to k-means++, which makes it hard to assess the practical significance of the results.

In conclusion, while the paper addresses an important problem and presents an interesting approach, the lack of technical detail and the unaddressed relationship to prior work in Expectation-Propagation are major flaws. The paper could be a valuable contribution if the theoretical claims were rigorously proven and the work was properly contextualized within the existing literature on approximate Bayesian inference.
