

Review text  
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work on AMP for compressed sensing to matrix factorization. The core contribution is an AMP-based algorithm (Algorithm 2) that unifies marginalization and MAP estimation via a parameter β, and applies it to K-means clustering by reformulating clustering as low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural constraints. The authors demonstrate empirical gains over Lloyd’s K-means and variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF) in clustering tasks.

**Strengths**  
The algorithm is theoretically grounded, with a clear derivation from belief propagation and a well-justified computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration). The empirical results (Fig. 1–2) show consistent improvements in K-means loss and accuracy over baselines, particularly for high-dimensional data. The algorithm’s connection to the ICM framework (Proposition 1) provides a useful theoretical anchor, and the clustering application is novel in its direct handling of cluster assignment constraints. The paper also correctly identifies the Bayesian flexibility to incorporate diverse structural priors (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity), a significant advantage over fixed-prior methods.

**Weaknesses**  
*Originality and Scholarship*: The clustering-as-reconstruction approach is not novel (Samaria & Harter, 1994; Xu et al., 2003), yet the paper omits these foundational references, misleading readers into believing the connection is original. The AMP extension builds directly on Matsushita & Tanaka (2012), but the paper fails to clarify how it differs from their work (e.g., handling non-i.i.d. row/column dependencies). The absence of citations to Expectation Propagation (EP) or related BP work (Yedidia et al., 2005) further undermines novelty claims.  

*Clarity and Presentation*: The notation is excessively cluttered (e.g., inconsistent β subscripts, overuse of hats/tildes, and ambiguous time indices). Key equations (e.g., 9a, 9b) lack intuitive explanation—e.g., why the noise variance scales with *m* (Eq. 1) or how *f* and *G* derive from the posterior. The algorithm’s symmetry (e.g., Eq. 9a vs. 9c) is missed, and the β = 1/∞ limits are not motivated. The derivation’s relegation to supplementary material (with its own clarity issues) is a major drawback.  

*Quality and Validation*: The asymptotic approximations (m,N → ∞) are central to the AMP framework but lack empirical validation (e.g., no ablation on small-scale matrices). The evaluation solely uses clustering tasks, omitting standard low-rank reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix, MovieLens), making it impossible to assess general matrix-reconstruction utility. The K-means comparison is reasonable, but the paper does not clarify why VBMF-MA (the baseline) uses the same prior as the proposed AMP-MA—this undermines the significance of accuracy gains.  

*Significance*: The clustering improvements are promising for high-dimensional data, but the non-standard evaluation framework limits broader impact. The paper claims "first" application to clustering under low-rank reconstruction, yet prior work (e.g., Ding et al., 2010) already addresses this. Without standard benchmarks or analysis of reconstruction error (e.g., Frobenius norm), the significance of the method remains unclear.  

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*: Efficient algorithm, solid theoretical grounding, empirical gains in clustering, flexible Bayesian framework.  
*Con*: Overstated novelty, poor citation of prior work, inadequate evaluation (no standard matrix datasets), unclear notation, unvalidated asymptotics.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper presents a technically sound algorithm with practical value for clustering, but its current form falls short of NIPS standards. The scholarship gaps (missing key citations on matrix factorization for clustering) and presentation issues (notational clutter, omitted derivations) are severe. While the empirical results are compelling, the lack of standard matrix-reconstruction validation weakens the significance claim. The authors should: (1) add citations to Samaria & Harter (1994), Xu et al. (2003), and Matsushita & Tanaka (2012); (2) include experiments on standard low-rank datasets (e.g., Netflix); (3) simplify notation and clarify key steps (e.g., the role of *m* in noise variance); and (4) move critical derivations to the main text. Without these revisions, the paper risks being perceived as incremental. As-is, it is not suitable for NIPS.
