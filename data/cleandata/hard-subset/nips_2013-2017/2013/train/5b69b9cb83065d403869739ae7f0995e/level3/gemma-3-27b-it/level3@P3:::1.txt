This paper presents an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work on AMP for sparse vectors and rank-one matrices. A key contribution is applying this AMP approach to clustering by reformulating the problem as a constrained low-rank reconstruction. The authors demonstrate empirically that their method outperforms Lloyd’s k-means and k-means++ in terms of speed and, in some cases, clustering quality, assessing performance using both Frobenius norm residuals and clustering accuracy.

The work builds upon a well-established line of research exploiting low-rank structure for matrix completion and related problems, notably Bayesian approaches (Salakhutdinov & Mnih, 2008; Raiko et al., 2007). While these Bayesian methods offer flexibility, they are often computationally expensive, motivating the use of approximate inference techniques. The AMP algorithm, originating in compressed sensing (Donoho et al., 2009), provides an efficient alternative, and its extension to matrix reconstruction (Rangan & Fletcher, 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012) represents a logical progression. The connection made to k-means clustering is not entirely novel (Xu et al., 2003; Ding et al., 2010), but the formulation within a constrained low-rank reconstruction framework, coupled with the novel AMP implementation, is a valuable contribution.  Recent related work on AMP for dictionary learning (Krzakala et al., 2013) and generalized observation models (Parker et al., 2013) further contextualizes this research.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction, particularly extending prior AMP algorithms to handle MAP estimation and incorporating structural properties, is a significant contribution.
* **Efficiency:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed AMP-based algorithm is faster than both k-means and k-means++.  The O(mN) computational cost is attractive for large-scale problems.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors wisely evaluate their method using both Frobenius norm residuals (measuring reconstruction quality) and clustering accuracy, offering a well-rounded assessment of performance.
* **Theoretical Justification (Partial):** The paper provides a proposition guaranteeing optimality under certain conditions, linking the AMP fixed point to a global minimum of the cost function.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the algorithm is clearly presented, with equations providing a precise description of the iterative process.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Strong Theoretical Guarantees:** The most significant weakness is the absence of robust theoretical convergence guarantees. While Proposition 1 is helpful, a more comprehensive analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties would strengthen the paper considerably. The authors acknowledge this limitation.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons to k-means and k-means++ are valuable, benchmarking against more recent and sophisticated clustering algorithms, or more advanced Bayesian matrix factorization methods, would provide a more thorough evaluation.
* **Dependency on τ Estimation:** The performance on real data relies on a heuristic for estimating the noise variance parameter τ. A more principled approach to parameter estimation, or a method less sensitive to τ, would improve robustness.
* **Citation of K-means Theory:** The authors suggest citing more theoretical computer science work that provides provable guarantees for K-means. This is a good suggestion, as the existing literature offers theoretical analyses of k-means convergence and approximation ratios (e.g., work on the Blum-Kalai-Vazirani algorithm).



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The algorithm presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate significant speed improvements over existing methods.
* The connection between low-rank reconstruction and clustering is well-motivated and effectively exploited.
* The paper is technically sound and well-written.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees is a significant concern.
* The limited comparison to state-of-the-art algorithms leaves some questions about the relative performance of the proposed method.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution with promising experimental results. The AMP-based algorithm offers a practical and efficient solution for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, particularly in scenarios where speed is critical. However, the absence of robust theoretical guarantees and the limited comparison to the broader literature temper the overall assessment. I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the theoretical limitations in future work.  I strongly encourage them to incorporate the suggested citations related to theoretical guarantees for K-means.
