

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, framed within a Bayesian inference approach. The authors extend AMP to handle low-rank matrices, which they apply to clustering by reformulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with structural constraints. The work builds on prior AMP applications in compressed sensing [6,7,8,9] and extends them to matrix reconstruction.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach that generalizes existing AMP techniques to the matrix factorization setting.
- The derivation of the AMP algorithm for low-rank reconstruction appears mathematically sound, with the authors providing a clear connection between their method and the variational Bayes matrix factorization approach.
- The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate that the proposed AMP algorithm for clustering outperforms Lloyd's K-means in terms of accuracy and convergence speed, which is a compelling empirical contribution.
- The algorithm has low computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration), making it practical for large-scale problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The paper's primary weakness is the lack of validation for the asymptotic approximations (N→∞, m→∞) that underpin the AMP derivation. These approximations are central to the algorithm's theoretical justification, yet their validity is not tested or clearly justified in the main text. The derivation is relegated to supplementary material, which is a significant drawback for a paper at NIPS where key technical contributions should be presented in the main body. The authors also fail to adequately compare against standard matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix dataset), instead using a non-standard clustering-as-factorization task that makes it difficult to assess the method's performance on conventional matrix reconstruction problems.

*Clarity:* The mathematical presentation is difficult to follow due to excessive notational clutter (excessive hats, tildes, subscripts/superscripts) and inconsistent notation (e.g., β as a global parameter with inconsistent subscripting). Equations 9a-9e contain unexplained factors and confusing notation that would benefit from simplification. The exposition misses obvious simplifications (e.g., equation 5 being the negative log of equation 2), which would significantly improve readability. The supplementary material also contains clarity issues, including unclear steps in the derivation.

*Originality:* The claimed novelty is limited. The algorithm appears to be a straightforward application of belief propagation to a problem previously addressed with variational message passing. The paper implies novelty of matrix factorization for clustering without citing prior work [10,11] that already established this connection. The paper fails to properly cite and discuss related work on Expectation Propagation (EP) and its relation to applying BP to continuous distributions, which would have strengthened the novelty claim.

*Significance:* While the algorithm shows empirical benefits in clustering experiments, the significance is diminished by the lack of comparison to standard matrix reconstruction benchmarks. The paper's focus on clustering-as-factorization, while interesting, makes it difficult to assess the method's broader applicability to low-rank matrix reconstruction tasks. The authors also fail to provide a clear citation for the VBMF-MA implementation used as a baseline.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents a promising algorithm with empirical benefits, but requires substantial revision before it can be accepted. The main issues are the lack of validation for key approximations, poor clarity in the mathematical presentation, insufficient scholarly context for the claimed novelty, and the non-standard evaluation approach that obscures the method's true significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The algorithm is theoretically grounded, shows empirical benefits over K-means in clustering tasks, and has low computational complexity. The connection between AMP and Bayesian inference is well-motivated.

**Arguments against acceptance:** The paper lacks sufficient clarity, validation of key approximations, and scholarly context to justify its claims of novelty. The evaluation methodology is non-standard and makes it difficult to assess the method's performance on conventional matrix reconstruction tasks.

This paper has potential but currently lacks the clarity, validation, and scholarly context necessary for acceptance at NIPS. With substantial revisions addressing the clarity issues, providing validation for the asymptotic approximations, and expanding the experimental evaluation to include standard matrix reconstruction benchmarks, this work could become a strong contribution to the field.
