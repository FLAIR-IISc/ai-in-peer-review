

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with structured factor priors (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity), extending prior AMP work in compressed sensing [6,8,9] to the matrix factorization setting. The key novelty lies in deriving an AMP-based approach for both marginalization and MAP estimation (previously limited to marginalization in [9]), and applying it to K-means clustering by reformulating clustering as a constrained low-rank reconstruction problem. While the connection between low-rank factorizations and clustering has been explored (e.g., PCA as a 2-approximation for K-means [10,11]), this work is the first to directly enforce exact cluster assignment constraints within the low-rank framework, avoiding the need for post-processing. The authors demonstrate that their AMP algorithm for K-means clustering (AMP-KM) outperforms Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ on both synthetic and real-world (ORL Faces) datasets.

**Strengths:**  
The paper’s experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. For synthetic data (m=800, N=1600, r=2–15), AMP-KM achieves the lowest normalized K-means loss (0.400 vs. 0.412 for K-means++) and highest clustering accuracy (69.0% vs. 63.5%) across 500 instances, with faster convergence (8.8 vs. 6.6 iterations on average for ORL). Crucially, they evaluate both loss minimization and actual cluster accuracy (via the Hungarian algorithm), avoiding the common pitfall of optimizing only for the loss function. The AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering (AMP-MA) also demonstrates superior accuracy and convergence speed compared to variational Bayes (VBMF-MA), highlighting the benefits of the AMP approach over traditional variational methods. The algorithm’s computational efficiency (O(mN) per iteration) and clear connection to existing methods (e.g., recovering ICM as a special case when β→∞) are well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**  
The primary weakness is the lack of strong theoretical guarantees for convergence or optimality. While Proposition 1 establishes that fixed points of the AMP algorithm are fixed points of the ICM algorithm (and thus locally optimal for CMAP(U,V)), it does not address global convergence or the quality of the fixed points. The paper omits state evolution analysis (standard for AMP algorithms [6,8,18]), which would provide theoretical performance predictions and convergence insights—particularly important for a method claiming practical advantages. The empirical convergence speed (e.g., AMP-MA converging faster than VBMF-MA) is noted, but without theoretical backing, the practical efficiency claims remain partially empirical. Additionally, the discussion of related work could better differentiate the paper’s AMP extension from [13,14] (e.g., [14] also handles matrix reconstruction but assumes different scaling limits).

**Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel AMP extension for MAP estimation in low-rank matrix reconstruction; first application to enforce exact cluster assignments in the low-rank framework; strong empirical results showing superiority over K-means++ in loss, accuracy, and convergence speed; well-structured experiments on synthetic and real data.  
*Con:* Theoretical analysis is incomplete (no state evolution, no global convergence proof); the connection to clustering is not entirely novel (though the constraint handling is), and the paper could better contextualize its contribution against [10,11,13,14].  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality technical contribution that advances the application of AMP to matrix factorization problems. The proposed algorithm is theoretically grounded in Bayesian inference, computationally efficient, and empirically validated on relevant tasks. While the theoretical gaps are notable, the practical impact—demonstrated by clear performance gains in clustering—makes the work significant for the NIPS community. The paper aligns with NIPS’ focus on principled, scalable methods for machine learning. The weaknesses are manageable for a conference paper, and the strengths (particularly the experimental rigor and novel algorithmic extension) outweigh the theoretical limitations. The authors have addressed key related work, but a brief mention of provable K-means guarantees [16] would strengthen the discussion of clustering significance. This work is a solid addition to the growing AMP literature at NIPS and should be accepted.
