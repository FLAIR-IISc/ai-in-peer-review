

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, with applications to clustering. The authors formulate matrix reconstruction as a Bayesian inference problem, using AMP to efficiently approximate posterior distributions. They then reformulate clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints, applying their AMP algorithm to derive a novel clustering method that outperforms Lloyd's K-means in experiments.

The paper builds on recent work in AMP for compressed sensing and low-rank matrix estimation (e.g., [6, 8, 9]), extending it to handle general-rank matrices and various structural priors. The connection between clustering and low-rank matrix factorization has been explored previously (e.g., [10, 11]), but the authors claim novelty in their direct handling of the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank framework.

**Strengths:**
- The proposed AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction shows promising empirical performance, particularly in clustering applications where it outperforms Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes approaches in terms of accuracy and convergence speed.
- The theoretical foundation of AMP is well-motivated, with the paper correctly identifying the advantages of AMP over variational Bayes methods (better handling of variable dependence).
- The paper successfully demonstrates the versatility of their approach by applying it to both matrix reconstruction and clustering problems.
- The empirical evaluation on both synthetic and real datasets (ORL faces) provides reasonable evidence of the algorithm's practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Clarity issues:** The paper suffers from excessive notational complexity (e.g., multiple hats, tildes, subscripts) that makes key equations difficult to parse. The derivation of the AMP algorithm is relegated to supplementary material, which is problematic for a main conference paper. Key simplifications (e.g., recognizing that equation 5 is the negative log of equation 2) are missed, reducing readability.
- **Originality concerns:** The claimed novelty is limited. The application of AMP to matrix reconstruction appears to be a straightforward extension of prior work (e.g., [9]), and the paper fails to properly cite or contextualize prior work on matrix factorization for clustering (e.g., [10, 11]), which makes the novelty claim questionable.
- **Quality concerns:** The asymptotic approximations (m,N→∞) are not validated with empirical evidence, and the role of the factor m in the noise variance is not clearly explained, potentially affecting row-column symmetry. The paper also lacks comparison to standard low-rank matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix dataset), making it difficult to assess performance on the core matrix reconstruction task.
- **Scholarship issues:** The paper implies novelty in applying matrix factorization to clustering without acknowledging that this connection is well-established in prior work. The BP nature of the algorithm should be stated earlier, and relevant literature on EP (Expectation Propagation) and BP for continuous distributions should be cited.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:*
- The algorithm is theoretically sound and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over K-means in clustering tasks.
- The AMP framework provides a unifying approach to both matrix reconstruction and clustering.
- The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important problem in machine learning.

*Con:*
- The paper lacks sufficient clarity and notation simplification, making it difficult for readers to follow.
- The novelty is overstated and not properly contextualized within the literature.
- The evaluation is limited to a non-standard task (clustering-as-factorization) without proper comparison to standard matrix reconstruction benchmarks.
- Key derivations are hidden in supplementary material, reducing the paper's accessibility.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising algorithm with potential utility in matrix reconstruction and clustering applications. However, the current version suffers from significant clarity issues, overstated novelty claims, and insufficient validation of the asymptotic approximations. The authors should address these concerns by simplifying notation, properly contextualizing their work within the literature, providing validation of their asymptotic assumptions, and expanding their empirical evaluation to include standard low-rank matrix reconstruction datasets. The supplementary material's clarity issues also need to be resolved.

The paper has potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but it requires substantial revisions to meet NIPS standards. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the concerns raised above. The authors have a good idea but need to refine their presentation and scholarship to demonstrate the full significance of their contribution.
