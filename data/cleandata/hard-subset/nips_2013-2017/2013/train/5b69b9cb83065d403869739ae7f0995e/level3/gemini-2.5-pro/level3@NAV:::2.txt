This paper proposes an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. The algorithm is derived as an approximation of Belief Propagation (BP) under asymptotic assumptions of large matrix dimensions (m, N → ∞). The primary application and evaluation presented is for clustering, which the authors frame as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction. The proposed method is compared empirically against Lloyd’s K-means and a Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF) approach, demonstrating superior performance on the presented tasks. This work builds upon the AMP framework, which has been successful in compressed sensing, and applies it to a problem domain commonly addressed by variational methods.

**Strengths:**
The paper presents an interesting algorithm that demonstrates strong empirical performance on the clustering tasks, outperforming standard baselines like K-means. The mathematical derivation, while dense, appears generally sound and the core idea of applying a BP-based method to this problem is compelling. The algorithm is potentially useful and its connection to the well-studied AMP framework provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper has several significant weaknesses that need to be addressed.
- **Quality:** The primary concern is the evaluation. By focusing exclusively on a non-standard "clustering-as-factorization" task, it is impossible to judge the algorithm's performance on conventional low-rank matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., collaborative filtering datasets). The authors should either include results on standard datasets or provide a strong justification for their absence. Furthermore, the validity of the central asymptotic approximations is not tested or discussed in the main paper.
- **Originality:** The novelty is somewhat limited, as the work appears to be a fairly direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed by related techniques like Variational Message Passing. The scholarship is also lacking; the paper fails to cite crucial prior work, implying novelty for the well-known connection between matrix factorization and clustering, and omits discussion of related methods like Expectation Propagation (EP).
- **Clarity:** The manuscript is very difficult to read. Mathematical notation is cluttered and inconsistent, hindering comprehension of the algorithm (e.g., equations 9a, 9b, 9d, 9e are confusing). Relegating the full derivation to supplementary material is a drawback; key steps should be summarized in the main text. The overall exposition misses opportunities for simplification and the English prose requires significant polishing.

**Conclusion:**
While the proposed algorithm is promising, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. The lack of standard evaluation, limited novelty, poor scholarship, and severe clarity issues prevent a confident assessment of its contribution. A major revision is required to address these points.
