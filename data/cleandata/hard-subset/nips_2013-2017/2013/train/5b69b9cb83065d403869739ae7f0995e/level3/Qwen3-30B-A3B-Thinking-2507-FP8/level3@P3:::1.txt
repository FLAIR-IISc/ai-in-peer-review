

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional structural properties, and applies it to K-means clustering. The authors formulate the matrix reconstruction problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing them to incorporate prior knowledge about the factor matrices beyond just low-rankness (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity). They derive an AMP algorithm that efficiently performs Bayesian inference, which is a significant computational improvement over traditional methods like variational Bayes. The key innovation lies in connecting the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem to clustering through a reformulation that incorporates the constraint that each data point must belong to exactly one cluster.

The paper builds on recent work in AMP for compressed sensing [6,7] and extends it to the matrix factorization setting. While AMP has been applied to dictionary learning [13] and matrix reconstruction with generalized observation models [14], this paper makes two distinct contributions: (1) an AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix reconstruction that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation, and (2) a novel application to K-means clustering that directly incorporates the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank reconstruction framework.

The paper's strengths are significant. The proposed AMP algorithm for matrix reconstruction is theoretically grounded and computationally efficient, with linear complexity in the number of matrix elements. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing the proposed method with K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes matrix factorization on both synthetic and real datasets. The authors evaluate not just the standard K-means loss (Frobenius norm residuals) but also clustering accuracy, which is particularly valuable. The results consistently show that the AMP-based approach outperforms traditional methods in both speed and quality, especially for high-dimensional data. The connection between low-rank factorizations and K-means (e.g., PCA giving a 2-approximation) is well-noted, demonstrating the authors' awareness of the broader context.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The main theoretical shortcoming is the lack of strong convergence guarantees for the proposed AMP algorithm. While the authors provide a proposition showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm (which is used in Lloyd's K-means), they do not establish convergence properties or provide bounds on the number of iterations required. The paper would benefit from citing more theoretical computer science work that provides provable guarantees for K-means, such as the work by Arthur and Vassilvitskii [16] on K-means++ or the recent advances on the complexity of K-means [21].

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with a well-structured presentation of the problem, algorithm, and experiments. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the supplementary material provides additional details. However, the connection between the AMP algorithm and the clustering application could be more explicitly motivated, as the well-known PCA-based connection to K-means might lead readers to question the novelty of the approach.

In terms of originality, the AMP-based approach to matrix reconstruction is novel, and the application to clustering is an interesting extension. While the connection between low-rank factorizations and clustering is not new [10,11], the specific way the authors incorporate the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank framework represents a meaningful contribution. The paper clearly differentiates itself from related work on AMP for matrix reconstruction [9,14] by focusing on fixed rank rather than rank ratio in the asymptotic limit.

The significance of the work is high, as efficient clustering algorithms are crucial in many applications. The empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages of the proposed approach over standard methods, particularly for high-dimensional data. The authors' focus on both reconstruction quality and clustering accuracy provides a more comprehensive evaluation than many competing methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel AMP-based approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional factor assumptions
- Strong experimental results demonstrating superior performance in both reconstruction quality and clustering accuracy
- Comprehensive evaluation including both Frobenius norm residuals and actual clustering quality
- Practical computational efficiency with linear complexity
- Clear connection to and extension of prior work in AMP and matrix factorization

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm
- Insufficient citation of theoretical computer science work on K-means with provable guarantees
- The connection between low-rank factorizations and K-means is well-established, potentially diminishing the novelty claim

Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field with promising empirical results. The AMP-based approach to matrix reconstruction is well-motivated and effectively applied to clustering. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the practical significance and strong experimental validation make this a valuable paper for NIPS. The main weakness is the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees, which should be addressed in a revision. Given the quality of the experiments and the practical relevance of the approach, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical discussion.
