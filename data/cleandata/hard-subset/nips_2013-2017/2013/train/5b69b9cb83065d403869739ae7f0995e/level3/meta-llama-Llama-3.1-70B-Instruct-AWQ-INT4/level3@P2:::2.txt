The paper "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing" presents a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction using approximate message passing (AMP). The authors apply this algorithm to the problem of clustering, framing it as a matrix reconstruction task, and compare its performance to Lloyd's K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).

The main idea of the paper is to use the Bayesian framework to model the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem, incorporating structural properties of matrices such as sparsity. The authors propose an efficient AMP algorithm, derived from the belief propagation algorithm, to perform Bayesian inference for matrix reconstruction. They also apply this algorithm to a clustering problem, reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with an additional structural property.

The paper's strengths include its ability to incorporate various structural properties of matrices into the reconstruction process and its efficient computational cost. The algorithm's performance is demonstrated through numerical experiments on both artificial and real data sets, showing that it outperforms Lloyd's K-means algorithm in terms of K-means loss and accuracy.

However, there are several concerns and areas for improvement. The evaluation of the algorithm on a non-standard task (clustering-as-factorization) makes it hard to judge its performance on conventional matrix-reconstruction datasets. The authors should consider using standard datasets or justifying why they did not do so. Additionally, the algorithm appears to be a fairly straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing, which limits its novelty.

The asymptotic approximations (N→∞, m→∞) are interesting but their validity is not tested or clearly justified in the main text. The paper also lacks proper citation and scholarship, implying novelty of matrix factorization for clustering without citing prior work or stating the connection is well-known. The authors should address these concerns and provide a clearer justification for their approach.

Furthermore, the mathematical presentation is hard to parse, with specific confusing equations and unexplained factors. The notation is cluttered, making the algorithm harder to read. The authors should simplify the notation and state conventions to improve readability. Additionally, the definitions and meanings of functions are not clearly explained near their first use and should be clarified.

The paper also raises questions about the competitiveness of the novel evaluation criterion against established benchmarks. The derivation of the algorithm is relegated to supplementary material, which is a drawback. Key derivation steps should appear or be summarized in the main paper. The supplementary material has clarity issues, and the authors should address these concerns.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the paper's clarity and organization could be improved. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of their approach, simplify the notation, and address the concerns mentioned above.

In terms of originality, the paper's contribution is limited by its similarity to previous work on Variational Message Passing. However, the application of the AMP algorithm to clustering is a novel aspect of the paper.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are important, and the algorithm has the potential to be useful in various applications. However, the paper's impact is limited by its lack of clarity and scholarship.

Overall, the paper is promising, but it requires significant revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. With proper revisions, the paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the field.
