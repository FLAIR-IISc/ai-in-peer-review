This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for matrix factorization and applies it to clustering. The algorithm is derived from the belief propagation algorithm and is designed to perform Bayesian inference for matrix reconstruction. The authors also demonstrate the application of the algorithm to a clustering problem by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with an additional structural property.

The paper is well-structured, and the main idea is easy to grasp. The authors provide a clear explanation of the problem setting, the proposed algorithm, and its application to clustering. The paper also includes numerical experiments to evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithm.

However, there are some concerns regarding the quality of the paper. The mathematical derivations are very sketchy, and many non-trivial steps are omitted, even in the appendix. For example, the reviewer questions why the second term of (11) is O(1/√m) and notes that β appears to be omitted in Section 2. The asymptotic analysis, including the derivation of (19), seems to assume that m and n grow at the same order, but the arguments are terse and lack rigor.

Additionally, the reviewer disputes the claim that m τ_w^2 equals E[A_{il}^2], noting that A is observed data and m τ_w^2 would only be the likelihood variance. The approximation needs justification. The reviewer also asks how the message passing algorithm for finite β converges to the MAP solution, pointing out that exchanging limit and minimization of KL is not necessarily valid.

The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief, and the reviewer asks how equation (17) was derived. Furthermore, the reviewer suggests that the algorithm may essentially be Expectation-Propagation (EP) applied to matrix factorization, pointing to Stern et al. (WWW 2009) and standard EP updates in the appendix.

The originality of the paper is also a concern. While the authors claim that their algorithm is novel, the reviewer suggests that it may be a variant of existing algorithms. The connection between the proposed clustering algorithm and the EP "clutter" problem (Minka) is also noted, but it is framed as a suggestion rather than an attack.

The significance of the paper is also limited by the fact that the authors only compare their algorithm to k-means++. A more comprehensive comparison with other clustering algorithms would be necessary to demonstrate the practical and technical contribution of the paper.

In conclusion, while the paper proposes an interesting algorithm for matrix factorization and clustering, there are concerns regarding the quality, originality, and significance of the paper. The authors need to address these concerns by providing more rigorous mathematical derivations, justifying their approximations, and comparing their algorithm to a wider range of existing algorithms.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel algorithm for matrix factorization and clustering.
* The algorithm is derived from the belief propagation algorithm and is designed to perform Bayesian inference for matrix reconstruction.
* The paper includes numerical experiments to evaluate the performance of the proposed algorithm.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The mathematical derivations are very sketchy, and many non-trivial steps are omitted.
* The asymptotic analysis lacks rigor and assumes that m and n grow at the same order.
* The approximation of m τ_w^2 equals E[A_{il}^2] needs justification.
* The convergence of the message passing algorithm for finite β to the MAP solution is not clear.
* The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief, and the derivation of equation (17) is not clear.
* The originality of the paper is a concern, and the connection to existing algorithms needs to be clarified.
* The significance of the paper is limited by the lack of comparison to other clustering algorithms.
