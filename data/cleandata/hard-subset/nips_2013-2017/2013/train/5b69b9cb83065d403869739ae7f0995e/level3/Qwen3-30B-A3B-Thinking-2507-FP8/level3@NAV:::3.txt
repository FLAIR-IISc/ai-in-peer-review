

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, specializing it to clustering problems and comparing it experimentally to k-means++. The main contribution is an AMP-based approach that treats both marginalization and MAP estimation problems within a unified framework, with promising experimental results showing improved performance over k-means++ on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper has several strengths. It is well-structured and clearly explains the connection between matrix factorization and clustering. The experimental results are thorough, demonstrating that the proposed AMP algorithm achieves better clustering accuracy and K-means loss than k-means++ on both synthetic data (with varying cluster numbers) and the ORL face dataset. The clarity of the main ideas is commendable.

However, the paper suffers from significant quality concerns that undermine its technical contribution. The mathematical derivations are extremely sketchy, with multiple non-trivial steps omitted even in the appendix. Specifically, the paper fails to justify why the second term in equation (11) is O(1/√m) (Q1), and the asymptotic analysis in Section 2 appears to assume m and n grow at the same order without rigorous justification (Q2). The claim that mτ_w² equals E[A_il²] is questionable since A is observed data (Q3), and the convergence to MAP solution for finite β lacks proper justification (Q4). Most critically, Proposition 1's proof is too brief and omits key derivation steps (Q5).

Regarding originality, the proposed AMP algorithm appears to be essentially an Expectation-Propagation (EP) application to matrix factorization, with connections to Stern et al. (WWW 2009) and standard EP updates in the appendix. The paper does not adequately distinguish itself from this prior work.

The significance is reduced by focusing solely on k-means++ comparison while omitting comparisons with other clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering, hierarchical clustering) and by the lack of rigorous theoretical justification. Given the sketchy proofs and potential overlap with EP-based methods, the technical contribution appears limited.

In summary, while the experimental results are promising, the lack of mathematical rigor significantly weakens the paper's scientific contribution. The technical claims are not properly supported, and the originality is questionable. I recommend rejection due to insufficient quality and originality.
