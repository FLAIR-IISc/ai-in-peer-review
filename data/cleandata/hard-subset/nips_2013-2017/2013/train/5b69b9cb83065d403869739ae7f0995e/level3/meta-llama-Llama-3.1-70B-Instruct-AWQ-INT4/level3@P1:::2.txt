This paper proposes a new approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction using a Bayesian framework. The algorithm is designed to incorporate additional structural properties of matrices, such as sparsity, and is shown to be effective in clustering problems. The authors evaluate the algorithm by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and compare its performance to Lloyd's K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it extends the AMP algorithm to the general-rank case, allowing for the estimation of posterior means and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimates. Second, it applies the derived AMP algorithm to K-means type clustering, obtaining a novel efficient clustering algorithm. The algorithm is shown to outperform Lloyd's K-means algorithm in numerical experiments.

However, there are several concerns and areas for improvement. One concern is that the evaluation of the algorithm is performed on a non-standard task (clustering-as-factorization), making it difficult to judge its performance on conventional matrix-reconstruction datasets (e.g., Netflix). The authors should consider using standard datasets or justifying why they did not do so.

Additionally, the algorithm appears to be a fairly straightforward application of belief propagation (BP) to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing, which may limit its novelty. The asymptotic approximations (N→∞, m→∞) are interesting but their validity is not tested or clearly justified in the main text.

The paper also has some issues with citation and scholarship. For example, it implies novelty of matrix factorization for clustering without citing prior work or stating that the connection is well-known. Furthermore, the paper does not cite or discuss Expectation Propagation (EP) and its relation to applying BP to continuous distributions.

The mathematical presentation is generally followable, but there are some confusing equations and unexplained factors. The notation is also cluttered, with excessive hats, tildes, subscripts/superscripts, and unnecessary time indices. Simplifying the notation and stating conventions would make the algorithm easier to read.

The paper also misses some obvious simplifications and connections, such as the symmetry in Algorithm 2. The definitions and meanings of functions, such as fβ and Gβ, are not clearly explained near their first use and should be clarified.

Overall, the algorithm is promising and publishable, but the paper currently lacks sufficient clarity, validation of approximations, and scholarship to be accepted as-is. The authors should address these concerns and improve the paper's clarity, notation, and scholarship before resubmitting.
