

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional structural assumptions on the factors, extending the AMP framework beyond its traditional vector reconstruction applications. The authors successfully reformulate K-means clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem, demonstrating that their AMP-based approach outperforms traditional methods in both synthetic and real-world clustering tasks.

The work exhibits strong technical quality with well-designed experiments comparing the proposed method against K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes approaches. The authors provide comprehensive evaluation metrics including Frobenius norm residuals and actual clustering accuracy, which significantly strengthens their empirical claims. The algorithm's computational efficiency (O(mN) per iteration) and faster convergence compared to existing methods represent a clear practical contribution.

The paper demonstrates good originality in applying AMP to matrix reconstruction with factor constraints, though the connection between low-rank factorizations and K-means is acknowledged as well-established (e.g., PCA provides a 2-approximation). The authors effectively situate their work within the broader context of Bayesian matrix factorization and AMP literature.

However, the main weakness lies in the lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed AMP algorithm. While the paper shows that fixed points of their AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm (proposition 1), this does not establish convergence properties or global optimality guarantees. The authors should cite relevant theoretical computer science work on provable K-means guarantees to better contextualize their contribution.

Overall, this is a technically sound contribution with compelling experimental results that demonstrates a valuable application of AMP to matrix reconstruction and clustering. The practical performance improvements are significant, though the theoretical foundation would benefit from strengthening. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, particularly for practitioners seeking efficient clustering methods, and I recommend acceptance with the understanding that addressing the theoretical gap would strengthen future work.
