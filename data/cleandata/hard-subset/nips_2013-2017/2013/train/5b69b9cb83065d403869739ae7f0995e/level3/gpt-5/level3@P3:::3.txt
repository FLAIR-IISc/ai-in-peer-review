Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix factorization with flexible (possibly improper) separable priors on the factors. A temperature-like parameter β unifies marginal inference (β=1) and MAP (β→∞), and the resulting fixed-point iterations include Onsager-like correction terms. The method is then specialized to clustering by encoding one-hot assignments in V and uniform priors, yielding a modified K-means procedure with bias-correction and cluster-size terms in the assignment step. Experiments compare the clustering instantiation primarily to Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++.

This work connects to AMP/GAMP for linear models (Donoho et al. 2009; Rangan 2011), to low-rank AMP/biG-AMP for bilinear estimation (Rangan & Fletcher 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka 2012; Parker et al. 2013), and to variational Bayes matrix factorization (Lim & Teh 2007; Raiko et al. 2007). The claimed novelty is extending AMP to handle general priors and β→∞ for MAP, and the clustering specialization that explicitly enforces one-hot constraints within the AMP framework.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and a well-written overview; the main ideas are easy to grasp.
- The algorithmic form is elegant: dropping Onsager terms recovers VB/ICM updates, clarifying the link between AMP and mean-field methods.
- The clustering specialization yields a practical, computationally light modification to K-means with intuitive corrections for center bias and cluster-size effects.
- Experiments show notable improvements over K-means++ on synthetic and ORL data, with fast convergence.

Quality and technical concerns
While the high-level derivation is plausible, many steps are too terse and skip non-trivial arguments. Specific issues:

- Q1 (Appendix line 139): Please justify why the second term of (11) is O(1/√m). What randomness and concentration assumptions are invoked (e.g., i.i.d. Gaussian A or W with appropriate scaling) and which norms are controlled? Also, β appears omitted in Section 2 when scaling terms are discussed; since q̂(u; b,Λ, p̂) explicitly depends on β, clarify where β enters the O(·) arguments and whether constants depend on β.

- Q2 (asymptotics and (19)): The derivation seems to assume m and N grow at the same order (m/N→c>0) while fixing rank r. Please state this assumption explicitly and specify the version of LLN/CLT used to pass to deterministic limits (state evolution). Under what distributional assumptions on U0,V0, and W (sub-Gaussian? bounded moments?) are the approximations valid? The step to (19) in the supplement is especially terse; a structured proof sketch via a conditioning argument or a reference to a rigorously analogous result (e.g., Bayati & Montanari 2011; biG-AMP theory) would help.

- Q3 (line 167: m τw^2 vs E[Ail^2]): A is observed and includes signal plus noise, so E[Ail^2]=E[(U0V0^T)il^2]+m τw^2 under the model. Replacing the empirical second moment by m τw^2 amounts to assuming the signal term averages out or is subtracted. Please justify this approximation, e.g., by showing the Onsager-corrected estimator of τ is consistent, or by using m−2N−1||A−U(V)^T||_F^2 with concentration bounds. As written, equating m τw^2 to E[Ail^2] is misleading without conditions.

- Q4 (β→∞ and MAP): The claim that the message passing algorithm “converges to” the MAP solution for finite β and in the limit β→∞ requires care. Exchanging the limit β→∞ with minimization of the KL/BETHE free energy is not automatically valid. Please provide conditions (e.g., convexity in each block, Γ/epi-convergence of the objective, or stability of fixed points) under which f(b,Λ; p̂)→f∞ and fixed points of the β-parametrized algorithm converge to blockwise MAP minimizers.

- Q5 (Proposition 1 and (17)): The proof is too brief. In particular, equation (17) in the main text (AMP-KM) should be derived step-by-step from Algorithm 2 by taking p̂v as a discrete one-hot prior, computing f∞ and G∞ explicitly, and showing precisely how the Onsager terms produce the bias and size corrections in (18b). Please expand the derivation and the reformulation in (16), and state assumptions ensuring ∑i S_i and ∑j T_j are PSD (e.g., via G∞=(Λ−H)^{-1} with Λ−H≽0).

Additional comments on rigor
- State evolution: the paper mentions it but provides neither equations nor a proof sketch. Even a concise SE system and a pointer to a matching proof for closely related bilinear AMP (Parker et al. 2013) would materially strengthen the work.
- Scaling: The mτ noise variance is unusual but standard in AMP scalings; please clarify its role earlier, especially since τ later becomes an estimated nuisance parameter in clustering.

Originality
The iterative updates with Onsager corrections are characteristic of AMP, but the per-factor updates f/G closely mirror Expectation Propagation (EP) site updates. Indeed, the algorithm looks like EP on a bilinear factor graph with Bethe free energy, with Onsager terms corresponding to EP cavity corrections. Please clarify how your method differs from standard EP for matrix factorization (e.g., Stern et al., WWW 2009) and from EP treatments of related “clutter” assignment problems (Minka’s EP notes). The clustering specialization also resembles EP on a mixture of Gaussians with hard one-of-r assignments; drawing and contrasting these connections would help establish novelty.

Significance
The empirical focus on K-means++ is too narrow given the clustering literature. To strengthen significance, consider comparisons to:
- EM for GMMs with tied covariance, DP-means, spectral clustering, normalized cuts, and recent scalable mixture models.
- EP-based clustering baselines if available.
In addition, tighter theory (SE, convergence conditions) would boost confidence in practical utility.

Clarity and reproducibility
The paper is well structured and easy to follow at a high level, but several key derivations are sketchy (see Q1–Q5). Provide:
- A self-contained derivation of Algorithm 3 from Algorithm 2.
- Precise asymptotic assumptions, and a short SE description.
- Pseudocode with stopping criteria and the τ estimator; detail numerical integration/minimization for f and f∞ for typical priors.
Code and seeds for the experiments would further aid reproducibility.

Pros
- Clear idea; elegant link between AMP and K-means with principled corrections.
- Competitive empirical performance and fast convergence.
- Potentially broad applicability to priors beyond Gaussians.

Cons
- Derivations are incomplete; several steps lack rigor.
- Originality relative to EP and biG-AMP is unclear.
- Limited empirical baselines for clustering.
- Justification of β→∞ MAP limit and variance approximations is weak.

Questions for authors (actionable)
1) Provide the missing β-dependent scaling and the O(1/√m) justification in Appendix line 139.
2) State formal asymptotic assumptions and expand the derivation of (19).
3) Justify the substitution leading to m τw^2 ≈ E[Ail^2], or replace it with a consistent τ estimator plus concentration.
4) Give conditions for interchanging β→∞ with KL minimization and for convergence to MAP.
5) Expand the proof of Proposition 1 and the derivation of (17)/(18b), explicitly computing f∞ and G∞ for the one-hot prior.

Recommendation
Borderline reject in current form due to rigor/originality concerns and limited evaluation. With stronger theoretical justification, clearer differentiation from EP/biG-AMP, and broader empirical comparisons, this could be a solid contribution.
