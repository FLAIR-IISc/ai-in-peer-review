

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, framed as a Bayesian inference problem, and applies it to clustering by reformulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction task. The authors derive an AMP-based algorithm that extends prior work on AMP for matrix reconstruction, with the key novelty being its application to clustering with the constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster. The paper claims empirical advantages over Lloyd's K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF) on clustering tasks.

The paper's strengths include technically sound mathematical derivations (though relegated to supplementary material), clear presentation of the algorithm's computational properties, and promising empirical results showing faster convergence and better accuracy than baselines on both artificial and real datasets (ORL faces).

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that prevent acceptance in their current form. First, the evaluation on clustering-as-matrix-reconstruction is non-standard and fails to validate performance on conventional matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix), making it difficult to assess the algorithm's general utility. Second, the novelty is questionable as the approach appears to be a straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing, without sufficient differentiation from prior work. Third, the asymptotic approximations (m,N→∞) are not validated or clearly justified in the main text, and the derivation is buried in supplementary material without key steps in the main paper. 

The paper suffers from poor scholarship: it implies novelty in matrix factorization for clustering without citing seminal work like [10,11], and fails to properly cite EP or discuss the BP nature of the algorithm in the abstract. The mathematical presentation is difficult to parse due to confusing equations (9a-9e), excessive notation (hats, tildes, subscripts), and inconsistent use of β. The role of the m factor in noise variance is unclear, and definitions of key functions (fβ, Gβ) are not explained upon first use.

While the algorithm shows promise and the mathematics are generally followable, the paper lacks sufficient clarity, validation of approximations, and scholarly rigor. The authors should address these issues by validating approximations, moving key derivations to the main paper, clarifying notation, properly citing related work, and evaluating on standard matrix reconstruction datasets. The paper is potentially publishable after substantial revision to address these concerns.
