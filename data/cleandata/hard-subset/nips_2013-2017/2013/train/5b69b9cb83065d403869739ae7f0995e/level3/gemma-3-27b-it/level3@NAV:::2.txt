## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction within a Bayesian framework, extending prior work on Iterative Factorization. A key contribution lies in applying this algorithm to the clustering problem by framing it as a specific instance of low-rank matrix reconstruction. The authors demonstrate empirical benefits over Lloyd’s K-means algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional data.

The core idea of leveraging AMP for matrix reconstruction, building on established work in compressed sensing, is interesting. The extension to clustering, while not entirely novel as acknowledged, offers a potentially efficient alternative to traditional methods. The mathematics appear generally sound, although the heavy reliance on the Supplementary Material for the full derivation is a significant drawback – key steps should be summarized within the main text for clarity. 

However, several weaknesses detract from the paper’s overall impact. Firstly, the evaluation primarily focuses on this “clustering-as-factorization” task. While reasonable as a proof of concept, this makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s performance on standard matrix reconstruction datasets like Netflix or similar, hindering comparisons with established benchmarks. Justification for this choice, or inclusion of standard benchmarks, is needed. Secondly, the novelty of the proposed approach appears somewhat limited. The application of BP to this problem feels like a fairly straightforward extension of existing Variational Message Passing techniques, and the paper fails to adequately position itself within that literature. 

Furthermore, the paper suffers from significant issues regarding citation and scholarship. Prior work on applying matrix factorization to clustering is not properly acknowledged, creating the impression of novelty where it doesn't exist. Crucially, related work on Expectation Propagation (EP) and its connection to BP for continuous distributions is completely absent. The BP nature of the algorithm should be highlighted earlier, preferably in the abstract.

The mathematical presentation is often dense and challenging to follow, with unexplained notation and confusing equations (particularly 9a, 9b, 9d, 9e). Simplifying the notation and clearly stating conventions would dramatically improve readability. The authors also miss opportunities to highlight obvious simplifications and connections between equations, further hindering understanding. The role of the 'm' factor in the noise variance also needs clarification. Finally, the English and phrasing throughout the paper require polishing.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Potentially useful algorithm for specific applications.
*   Empirical benefits demonstrated in experiments.
*   Sound mathematical foundation (though obscured by presentation).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty.
*   Evaluation on a non-standard task hinders broader impact.
*   Poor citation and lack of contextualization within related work.
*   Poor clarity in mathematical presentation and exposition.
*   Derivation relegated to supplementary material.



**Overall:** The algorithm presented has promise, but the paper requires substantial revisions to address the issues of clarity, validation, and scholarship before it can be considered for acceptance. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the reviewer concerns.
