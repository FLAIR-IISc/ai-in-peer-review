This paper introduces an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work on AMP for sparse vectors and rank-one matrices. The authors formulate the problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing for the incorporation of structural properties of the matrix factors, such as sparsity, and demonstrate an application to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a constrained low-rank reconstruction problem. The paper’s strength lies in its algorithmic contribution and promising experimental results, which outperform Lloyd’s K-means algorithm on high-dimensional data.

The paper begins with a clear motivation for developing efficient algorithms for low-rank matrix reconstruction, highlighting the computational challenges of traditional Bayesian approaches. The connection to compressed sensing and the success of AMP in that domain provides a strong justification for exploring its application to matrix reconstruction. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant papers on Bayesian matrix factorization, variational Bayes methods, and AMP algorithms. They correctly note the existing work on applying low-rank methods to clustering, while arguing that their approach is novel due to its direct handling of the single cluster assignment constraint.

The technical contribution, the derivation of an AMP algorithm capable of handling MAP estimation (beyond just posterior means as in prior work [9]), is significant. The introduction of the β-parameterized posterior distribution provides a flexible framework for balancing exploration and exploitation during the inference process. The algorithm's description is reasonably clear, though some aspects could benefit from further elaboration (discussed in the Clarity section). The link to variational Bayes methods is insightful, acknowledging the advantages and potential limitations of each approach. The proposition regarding the optimality of the algorithm's fixed points is a valuable theoretical contribution, although the assumptions required for its validity should be stated more prominently.

The experimental evaluation is a strong aspect of this paper. The authors present results on both artificial and real-world datasets (ORL faces), using metrics appropriate for assessing both reconstruction accuracy (K-means loss) and clustering quality (accuracy). Comparing the proposed algorithm against established methods like Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes-based approaches provides a comprehensive benchmark. The consistently superior performance of the AMP algorithm, particularly in terms of both speed and accuracy, is compelling. 

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed AMP algorithm. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, a more detailed discussion of potential convergence issues and avenues for future theoretical analysis would be beneficial. The reliance on empirically observed convergence behavior, while reasonable, does not provide the same level of confidence as a rigorous proof. The assumption of positive semi-definiteness of certain matrices in Proposition 1 is also a point requiring more careful consideration and justification. 

Regarding clarity, while the overall presentation is good, some sections could be improved. The notation is occasionally dense and could benefit from more explicit definitions. The explanation of the functions f and G, while mathematically correct, could be made more intuitive. The discussion of the parameter τ in Subsection 4.3 is somewhat abrupt, and the motivation for its introduction could be elaborated further.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel extension of AMP algorithms to the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem, particularly the ability to perform MAP estimation. The application to K-means clustering, while building on existing ideas, benefits from the algorithm's ability to directly enforce the single cluster assignment constraint. The extensive experimental validation and the proposition concerning fixed points further contribute to the novelty of the work.

The significance of this work is substantial. An efficient and accurate algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction has broad applications in various fields, including signal processing, machine learning, and computer vision. The demonstrated improvement over traditional K-means clustering, particularly in high-dimensional settings, is particularly noteworthy. The potential for utilizing the algorithm in other applications where low-rank structure and prior knowledge about factors are present is significant.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel extension of AMP algorithms to low-rank matrix reconstruction with MAP estimation.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods.
*   Clear connection to K-means clustering and a practical application in this domain.
*   The proposition regarding fixed point optimality provides a partial theoretical justification.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees for the proposed algorithm.
*   Some sections of the paper could benefit from improved clarity and more detailed explanations.
*   The assumption of positive semi-definiteness requires further justification.



**Overall, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.** The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction with a promising algorithm and compelling experimental results. Addressing the concerns regarding theoretical guarantees and clarity would further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact. I suggest the authors explicitly address the convergence concerns in the revised version and consider expanding the discussion of the assumptions underlying Proposition 1.
