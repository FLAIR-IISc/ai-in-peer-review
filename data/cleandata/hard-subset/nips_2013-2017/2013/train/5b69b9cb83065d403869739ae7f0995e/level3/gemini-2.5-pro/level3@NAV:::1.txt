This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations, leveraging the Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The method operates within a Bayesian formulation, which provides the flexibility to incorporate various structural priors on the matrix factors. Building on AMP's successes in compressed sensing, the authors extend the framework to general-rank matrix reconstruction. A key contribution is the application of this method to K-means clustering, which is elegantly framed as a low-rank reconstruction problem with a discrete structural constraint on one of the factors. While the link between low-rank approximation and clustering is well-known (e.g., spectral methods), the derivation of a K-means-like algorithm from the AMP formalism is original and insightful.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The core contribution—an AMP-based algorithm for matrix factorization and its specialization to clustering—is novel. The resulting clustering algorithm provides an interesting alternative to the standard Lloyd's algorithm, with update rules that intuitively correct for bias and account for cluster sizes. This is a creative and well-motivated approach.
*   **Quality of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and compelling. The proposed method is benchmarked against strong baselines like k-means and k-means++ on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The authors commendably evaluate performance using both the Frobenius norm objective and external clustering accuracy metrics, demonstrating superior results on both fronts. The findings suggest the proposed algorithm is a practically effective tool that advances the state of the art.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary limitation of the work is the lack of theoretical convergence guarantees. The authors explicitly state that convergence is assumed, which weakens the paper's technical soundness. While Proposition 1 offers some characterization of potential fixed points, it provides no assurance that the algorithm will reach them.
*   **Related Work:** The paper could be strengthened by better situating itself within the theoretical computer science literature on clustering. Citing and discussing work that provides provable approximation guarantees for K-means (e.g., via spectral relaxation or careful seeding) would provide important context for the trade-offs made by this heuristic, albeit empirically powerful, approach.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with a novel algorithm and strong experimental results, but its impact is somewhat limited by the weak theoretical foundation. The work is a good fit for the conference and will be of interest to researchers in both message passing and clustering.
