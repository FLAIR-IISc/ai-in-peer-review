This paper introduces an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work in the field and applying it to the problem of K-means clustering. The authors address the computational challenges of Bayesian approaches by leveraging AMP, which offers efficiency and scalability, particularly when dealing with large matrices and structural properties of the factors.  The work builds upon a growing body of research utilizing AMP for compressed sensing and matrix factorization, referencing key papers like Donoho et al. (2009) and Rangan & Fletcher (2012). The connection drawn between low-rank reconstruction and K-means clustering, while not entirely novel (see Ding et al., 2010, and Xu et al., 2003), is presented with a focus on directly addressing the constraint of exclusive cluster assignment within the reconstruction framework. 

The strengths of this paper lie primarily in its algorithmic contribution and thorough empirical validation. The AMP-based algorithm presented offers a potentially significant improvement over traditional methods like variational Bayes and Lloyd’s K-means, particularly in terms of computational cost. The authors demonstrate this advantage through comprehensive experiments on both artificial and real datasets (the ORL face database), showcasing superior performance in terms of K-means loss and clustering accuracy, as well as comparatively fast convergence.  The inclusion of both performance metrics – reconstruction error and clustering quality – is commendable and strengthens the evaluation. The detailed algorithmic description, including clear equations (Algorithms 2 & 3), allows for relatively straightforward implementation and reproducibility. The analysis of the proposed algorithm's properties, connecting it to variational Bayes and discussing the potential for convergence, provides valuable insight.

However, the paper does have weaknesses. The most significant is the lack of strong theoretical guarantees regarding the convergence of the proposed AMP algorithm. While the authors mention potential optimality under specific conditions (Proposition 1), a more rigorous analysis of convergence properties would significantly strengthen the contribution. Furthermore, while the connection to K-means is acknowledged as existing in prior work, the paper could benefit from a more extensive discussion of the theoretical foundations of K-means itself.  Specifically, citing results from theoretical computer science offering provable approximation guarantees for K-means (e.g., work on the Davies-Bouldin index or other quality metrics) would provide valuable context.  The handling of the noise parameter 'τ' in the practical implementation also feels somewhat ad-hoc, and further justification or theoretical underpinning would be valuable.  The reliance on the assumption of independent components within each row of U0 and V0, although explicitly stated, might limit the algorithm’s applicability to certain problem domains. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, particularly the extension to handle MAP estimation, appears to be a novel contribution.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate superior performance compared to existing methods (K-means, K-means++, VBMF) on both synthetic and real datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the algorithmic steps are clearly presented.
*   **Significance:** The potential for faster and more accurate low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is significant, with potential applications in various fields.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Theoretical Weakness:** The lack of rigorous convergence guarantees for the AMP algorithm is a major drawback.
*   **Limited Theoretical Context:** The discussion of K-means lacks a connection to established theoretical results on its performance guarantees.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The handling of the noise parameter 'τ' lacks strong justification.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and promising contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. While the lack of strong theoretical guarantees is a concern, the demonstrated empirical performance and the clear algorithmic presentation warrant serious consideration. I recommend acceptance with the caveat that the authors address the theoretical limitations in future work. The paper offers a valuable practical tool with the potential to advance research in these areas.
