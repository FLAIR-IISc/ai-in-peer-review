This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations, focusing on matrices with additional structural properties such as sparsity and non-negativity. The authors propose an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm, derived from the belief propagation algorithm, to perform Bayesian inference for matrix reconstruction. The AMP algorithm is shown to be efficient and effective, with a low computational cost and the ability to handle a wide variety of prior distributions.

The paper also explores the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, formulating the clustering problem as a special case of matrix reconstruction. The authors propose a novel AMP-based clustering algorithm, which is shown to outperform Lloyd's K-means algorithm in numerical experiments.

The strengths of the paper include its solid experimental evaluation, which compares the proposed method to k-means and k-means++ on both artificial and real data sets. The results suggest that the proposed approach is faster and more efficient than k-means and k-means++. The paper also commendably evaluates both Frobenius norm residuals and actual clustering quality.

However, the main weakness of the paper is the lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees for the AMP algorithm. While the authors provide some plausible properties of the algorithm, such as its low computational cost and the ability to handle a wide variety of prior distributions, they do not provide a rigorous proof of convergence.

In terms of related work, the paper cites several existing methods for low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, including variational Bayesian matrix factorization and Lloyd's K-means algorithm. However, the authors could benefit from citing more theoretical computer science work that provides provable guarantees for K-means, such as the work on k-means++.

Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, with a novel AMP-based algorithm and promising experimental results. However, the lack of strong theoretical convergence guarantees is a notable weakness.
