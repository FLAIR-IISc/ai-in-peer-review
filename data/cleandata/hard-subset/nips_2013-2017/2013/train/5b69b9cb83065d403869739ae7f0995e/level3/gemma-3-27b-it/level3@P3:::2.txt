This paper proposes a belief propagation (BP)-based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work on approximate message passing (AMP) to handle general rank and MAP estimation. The algorithm is then applied to a clustering problem framed as a low-rank reconstruction task. While the algorithm shows empirical benefits in experiments, particularly outperforming Lloyd’s K-means on a clustering task, the paper suffers from clarity issues, lacks sufficient validation, and exhibits shortcomings in scholarship and presentation.

The core idea – leveraging BP/AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural priors – builds upon established work in compressed sensing ([6]) and matrix factorization ([3–5]). However, the paper doesn’t adequately position itself within this landscape. The extension to MAP estimation from prior AMP work ([9]) is a contribution, but its impact feels incremental. The application to clustering, while interesting, is presented as novel without fully acknowledging existing approaches to clustering via matrix factorization ([10, 11]). This omission undermines the originality claim. Furthermore, the paper fails to discuss the relationship between the proposed algorithm and Expectation Propagation (EP), a closely related technique for applying BP to continuous distributions, a significant oversight.

The technical soundness of the algorithm is plausible, but hampered by presentation. The derivation, crucial to understanding the method, is relegated to the Supplementary Material, a major drawback. Key derivation steps should be either included in the main text or at least summarized with more detail. Equations (9a, 9b, 9d, 9e) are particularly difficult to parse, containing unexplained factors and lacking sufficient context. The extensive use of hats, tildes, and subscripts/superscripts creates notational clutter, making the algorithm harder to follow. Simplifying the notation and clearly stating conventions would significantly improve readability. Obvious simplifications and connections – such as the relationship between eq.5 and eq.2, or eq.8 and eq.2β – are missed, hindering understanding. The definitions of functions fβ and Gβ are unclear at first use and require explicit clarification. The role of the 'm' factor in the noise variance and its impact on row-column symmetry remains unexplained. Minor inconsistencies in notation (tilde for column vectors, capitalization of N vs. m) further contribute to confusion.

The asymptotic approximations (N→∞, m→∞) are mentioned but lack justification or empirical validation. The validity of these approximations, central to the algorithm's theoretical foundation, needs to be addressed. While the authors acknowledge differences from related work ([13, 14]), the comparison remains superficial. The choice of evaluating the algorithm on clustering *as* matrix factorization is problematic. While a valid testbed, it's difficult to assess performance against established matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix) without evaluation on standard datasets. The justification for not including such datasets is weak. Using K-means as a comparison is reasonable, but the novel evaluation criterion introduced raises questions about the competitiveness of the method against existing, well-established clustering algorithms.

The experimental results are encouraging, demonstrating improvements over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++. However, the VBMF-MA implementation used as a baseline lacks a clear citation, leaving the reader unsure of which variational reference was followed. The Supplementary Material also suffers from clarity issues, including suggestions for simplifying expressions (product of exponentials) and unclear steps in the derivation (eq.14 to 15). The English and phrasing throughout the paper could benefit from polishing, and the title and several sentences could be made more precise. Adding more diagrams, as suggested by the helpful Figure 1 in the Supplement, would significantly enhance understanding.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The algorithm is promising and potentially useful for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
*   The mathematics are generally followable (despite the noted issues).
*   The algorithm demonstrates empirical benefits in the experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of clarity in presentation and notation.
*   Insufficient validation of asymptotic approximations.
*   Weak scholarship and inadequate referencing of related work.
*   Evaluation on a non-standard task makes it difficult to assess generalizability.
*   Key derivation steps are relegated to supplementary material.



**Overall:** The paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution. However, the current version lacks the clarity, validation, and scholarship necessary for acceptance. Addressing the points outlined above—particularly improving the presentation, justifying the approximations, and acknowledging related work—would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend rejection in its current form, with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revision.
