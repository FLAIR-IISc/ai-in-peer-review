This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix factorization and specializes this framework to derive a novel clustering algorithm. The authors formulate clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and demonstrate experimentally that their AMP-based approach outperforms the k-means++ algorithm on both synthetic and real-world datasets. The work connects to the growing literature on AMP for inference problems, extending concepts from compressed sensing (Donoho et al., 2009) to the matrix factorization setting, and positions itself as an alternative to variational Bayes methods.

The primary strength of the paper is its clarity. The problem is well-motivated, the paper is well-structured, and the core idea of applying AMP to clustering is easy to grasp. The experimental results, while limited in scope, are promising and suggest the approach has merit.

However, the paper suffers from significant weaknesses in technical quality, originality, and significance. The main concern is the lack of mathematical rigor. The derivations are extremely sketchy, with many non-trivial steps omitted. For example: the justification for the O(1/√m) scaling in (11) is missing (Appendix, line 139); the asymptotic analysis assuming m and n grow at the same rate is terse and unconvincing; and the proof of Proposition 1 is too brief, failing to explain how equation (17) is derived. Further clarification is also needed on the claim in line 167 regarding the approximation of the noise variance and on how the algorithm is guaranteed to find the MAP solution as β → ∞, as the exchange of limit and minimization operators is not always valid.

Regarding originality, the proposed algorithm appears to be a special case of Expectation-Propagation (EP) applied to matrix factorization, a connection the authors do not discuss. The update equations bear a strong resemblance to standard EP updates, and this approach has been explored before (e.g., Stern et al., WWW 2009). The clustering algorithm may also relate to the EP "clutter problem" (Minka).

Finally, the paper's significance is undermined by these issues. The experimental evaluation is narrow, comparing only against k-means++ while ignoring the vast clustering literature. Without more rigorous proofs and a clear discussion of the relationship to prior work on EP, the confidence in the paper's technical and practical contribution is low. The work is interesting but requires a major revision to address the mathematical gaps and properly position itself within the existing literature before it can be considered a solid contribution.
