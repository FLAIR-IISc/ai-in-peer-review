- Paper derives an AMP algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, unifying marginal inference (β=1) and MAP (β→∞), and applies it to clustering as one-hot-constrained factorization (AMP-KM).  
- Empirically, AMP-KM improves K-means loss and accuracy on synthetic data and the ORL faces dataset versus Lloyd’s, K-means++, and a VBMF baseline.  
- Builds on prior AMP and bilinear AMP work (IterFac, low-rank AMP, BiG-AMP) and relates to VMP/VBMF, NMF-based clustering, spectral methods, and EP/TAP/Bethe literature.  
- Technical soundness: algorithm matches known AMP structures (Onsager corrections, state-evolution claims) but main derivations are mostly in the supplement and large-system approximations are not clearly justified in the main text.  
- Empirical support: results are encouraging for the chosen clustering framing but lack standard low-rank reconstruction/completion benchmarks, making broader competitiveness unclear.  
- Carefulness: several design choices (noise variance scaling by m, β treatment, τ estimation) are inadequately motivated and not ablated.  
- Exposition is dense and hard to parse; key equations (9a–9d) are confusing and origins of specific terms/factors are unclear.  
- Notation is cluttered and inconsistent (excessive hats/tilde/superscripts, inconsistent m/N capitalization); conventions should be established early.  
- Functions fβ and Gβ (and MAP limits) are not defined/interpreted on first use; β should be explicitly treated as a global parameter.  
- Missed simplifications: several equations and symmetries (e.g., eq.5 vs eq.2, eq.8 as p(U,V|A)β, symmetry between U and V) are not pointed out to aid readability.  
- Supplement needs clearer steps: show product-of-exponentials, clarify transition from eq.14→15, and connect approximations to TAP/Bethe and prior AMP derivations.  
- Originality is incremental: BP/Bethe-to-AMP for low-rank bilinear models and tempered posteriors are known; novelty mainly a MAP-capable extension and clustering specialization.  
- Significance: could be practically useful if consistent gains over K-means hold at small extra cost, but broader impact requires validation on standard datasets and comparisons to PMF/BPMF, VBMF variants, and BiG-AMP.  
- Pros: clear framing, β-unified inference/MAP view, derivation links to BP/AMP with O(mN) iterations, empirical gains shown, and a useful Proposition 1.  
- Cons: limited novelty vs prior bilinear AMP/VMP, opaque derivation in supplement, untested asymptotic validity/state evolution, narrow evaluation, and missing citations.  
- Suggested experiments: empirically validate large-system assumptions by varying (m,N,r), test state-evolution predictions, and add standard matrix completion benchmarks (MovieLens/Netflix, synthetic completion).  
- Suggested baselines: compare to PMF/BPMF, BiG-AMP, Gaussian Mixture/EM, spectral clustering, and NMF-based clustering, not just K-means variants.  
- Scholarship fixes: cite EP and expectation-consistent approximations (Minka; Opper & Winther) and prior clustering-via-factorization/NMF work; clarify BP/Bethe derivation in abstract/introduction.  
- Method clarifications: explain m factor in noise variance, origin of 1/(βmτ) and Onsager subtraction terms, and when Σi Si and Σj Tj are PSD as assumed.  
- Notation/readability fixes: reduce hats/tilde, remove unnecessary time indices, define f, G, q̂ at first use, and treat β as a global parameter consistently.  
- Algorithmic details: explain τ estimation in AMP-KM, test sensitivity to initialization vs K-means++, and provide implementation details for VBMF-MA baseline (citation, priors, hyperparameters).  
- Presentation: add factor graph, message-flow diagram, and illustrations of algorithm symmetry; polish English and tighten the title.  
- Questions for authors: provide concise main-text derivation of Onsager terms and 1/β scaling; sensitivity of AMP-KM to τ and initialization; evidence of approximation accuracy for moderate m,N; exact VBMF variant used.  
- Recommendation: Borderline reject—promising but needs clearer derivations, empirical validation of asymptotic claims, stronger baselines/benchmarks, and improved exposition before acceptance.
