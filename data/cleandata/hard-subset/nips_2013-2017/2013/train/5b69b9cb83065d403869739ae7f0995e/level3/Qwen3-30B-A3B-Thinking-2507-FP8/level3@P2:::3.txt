

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, which is then specialized to clustering by reformulating the problem as a matrix reconstruction task with additional structural constraints. The authors extend previous AMP work for rank-one matrices to the general rank case, enabling both marginalization and MAP estimation. They demonstrate that the proposed AMP algorithm for clustering outperforms Lloyd's K-means algorithm on both synthetic and real datasets.

The work relates to recent advances in AMP for compressed sensing [6,7] and matrix reconstruction [9], while positioning itself as the first AMP-based approach for clustering that directly handles the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster. It builds on the observation that clustering can be formulated as low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural constraints [10,11], but differs by incorporating AMP for inference.

## Strengths
The paper is well-structured and clearly presents its main contribution: a unified AMP framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction that can handle both marginalization and MAP problems. The experimental results are comprehensive, showing the proposed AMP algorithm for clustering (both K-means and maximum accuracy variants) outperforms Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ on both synthetic and real datasets (ORL faces), particularly in terms of accuracy and convergence speed. The paper's clarity is a significant strength, with well-organized sections and clear presentation of the algorithm.

## Weaknesses

### Quality
The theoretical foundation of the paper is concerning. The mathematical derivations are extremely sketchy, omitting non-trivial steps even in the supplementary material. Specifically:
- The asymptotic analysis (including derivation of (19)) assumes m and N grow at the same order but provides no rigorous justification for this assumption.
- In Section 2, the reviewer questions why the second term of (11) is O(1/√m) and notes β appears to be omitted.
- The claim that mτ_w^2 = E[A_{il}^2] (line 167) is questionable since mτ_w^2 represents likelihood variance, not data statistics.
- The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief, and the derivation of equation (17) is unclear.
- The connection between finite-β AMP and MAP solutions requires justification, as exchanging limits and minimization of KL divergence is not necessarily valid.

### Originality
The paper's main contribution appears to be a specialized application of Expectation-Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization, rather than a novel algorithm. The AMP framework closely resembles EP updates, and the authors should explicitly acknowledge prior work on EP for matrix factorization (e.g., Stern et al. [WWW 2009]) and the connection to Minka's "clutter" problem. The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing EP-based methods.

### Significance
The experimental evaluation is limited to comparisons with K-means++ and lacks broader context within the clustering literature. The paper does not address how the proposed method compares to state-of-the-art clustering algorithms beyond K-means, such as spectral clustering, hierarchical clustering, or more recent approaches. The significance is further reduced by the theoretical weaknesses and potential overlap with existing EP-based methods.

## Recommendation

**Pros for acceptance:** 
- Well-structured and clearly presented
- Comprehensive experimental results showing improved performance over K-means
- Novel application of AMP to clustering with structural constraints
- Interesting convergence properties compared to variational Bayes methods

**Cons against acceptance:**
- Theoretical foundation is weak and lacks rigorous justification
- Insufficient differentiation from existing Expectation-Propagation methods
- Experimental evaluation lacks comparison with relevant state-of-the-art clustering algorithms
- Critical mathematical steps are omitted or unjustified

The paper's main contribution appears to be an application of known techniques (AMP/EP) to clustering rather than a novel algorithmic or theoretical advance. While the experimental results are promising, the theoretical weaknesses and lack of clear distinction from prior work reduce confidence in the paper's technical contribution. The authors should either substantially strengthen the theoretical justification or more clearly position the work within the EP literature.

Given the quality concerns and the risk of overlapping with existing EP-based methods, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from a major revision addressing the theoretical gaps, providing a thorough comparison with EP-based approaches, and expanding the experimental evaluation to include more relevant clustering algorithms.
