This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible priors on the factors, introduces a one-parameter family p̂(U,V|A;β) that unifies posterior-mean and MAP inference (β=1 and β→∞, respectively), and specializes the MAP algorithm to clustering by imposing a one-hot prior on V, yielding an AMP variant of K-means with principled “Onsager” corrections. Empirically, the AMP-KM variant reduces K-means loss relative to Lloyd/K-means++ on synthetic Gaussian mixtures and the ORL dataset; an AMP-based maximum-accuracy variant (β=1) outperforms a variational Bayes matrix factorization baseline.

Relation to prior work: The framework builds on AMP for compressed sensing and bilinear models ([6–9,14]), and the clustering specialization connects to earlier uses of matrix factorization for clustering ([10–11]) and K-means ([12,16]). The main claim of novelty is: (i) general-rank AMP with β bridging marginalization and MAP (beyond [9], which focused on posterior means), and (ii) direct enforcement of one-hot constraints in V for clustering within an AMP derivation.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal and organization. The exposition makes the main idea easy to grasp, and the link between low-rank reconstruction and clustering is well motivated.
- Algorithmic contributions are practical: O(mN) per iteration, simple steps, and explicit formulas in the K-means specialization (Algorithm 3).
- The β-trick provides a unifying view of posterior mean vs MAP within one message passing scheme, and the Onsager corrections are carefully carried through to the clustering case.
- Empirical results indicate improvements over Lloyd/K-means++ on synthetic mixtures and ORL; AMP-MA converges quickly and often matches AMP-KM in speed where accuracies are similar.

Weaknesses and technical questions (quality/rigor)
- Derivations are sketchy. Several non-trivial approximations are asserted without proof or with terse arguments, even in the appendix. Specific issues:
  - Q1 (Appendix, around line 139): Please justify why the second term of your Eq. (11) is O(1/√m). What precise scaling assumptions on A, U, V, and noise are used (e.g., entries O(1), Frobenius norms O(√mN))? Also, β seems to disappear in Sec. 2 and in the scaling arguments—please reconcile with the definition p̂(U,V|A;β) and q̂(u;b,Λ,p̂), where β multiplies both the likelihood and prior potentials.
  - Q2 (asymptotics/state evolution): The analysis appears to assume m and N grow at the same rate and that the “effective fields” are asymptotically Gaussian, leading to Eq. (19). Please make the assumptions explicit (e.g., randomness of A or of U,V, independence structure across indices, bounded moments) and provide a more rigorous derivation or at least a roadmap referencing theorems (e.g., Bayati–Montanari dynamics) adapted to the bilinear, structured-prior setting. As written, the arguments are too terse to assess validity.
  - Q3 (line 167): The statement m τw^2 = E[Ail^2] confounds the noise variance with the observed data second moment. Under your model, E[W^2]=mτ while E[A^2]=E[(UV⊤)^2]+E[W^2]. Your practical estimator τ ≈ m−2N−1∥A−U(V)⊤∥^2F is reasonable ex post, but needs justification (bias/variance, dependence on current iterate), and the relationship to the asymptotic approximation should be clarified.
  - Q4 (β→∞ and MAP): You motivate the MAP algorithm as β→∞. However, exchanging the β→∞ limit with the approximate Bethe/AMP fixed-point equations is not obviously valid; neither is interchanging limit and minimization of the KL/Betha free energy. Please justify that fixed points of the β-AMP converge to stationary points of CMAP, or provide conditions/damping under which this holds (or cite a result).
  - Q5 (Proposition 1): The proof is too brief. In particular, Eq. (17) (the penalized update identity) should be derived explicitly by expanding CMAP(U,Vt), completing the square, and showing how the Onsager term yields the negative quadratic penalty. Please also discuss when ∑i S^t_i and ∑j T^t_j are positive semidefinite at fixed points (beyond the heuristic “almost always”).
- Convergence and stability: You observe oscillations in assignments and use ad-hoc stopping criteria. Please discuss damping/step-size strategies, diagnostics for divergence, and whether the theory (state evolution) predicts convergence domains.
- Sensitivity: The algorithm assumes a rank r̂ and a prior; practical guidance on robustness to r̂≠r and prior mismatch would be valuable.

Originality
- The update structure in (9) strongly resembles Expectation Propagation (EP) for matrix factorization: sitewise Gaussian approximations, moment matching to define f and G, and Onsager-like corrections can be interpreted as EP cavity/tilted moments. There is EP-based work on matrix factorization and collaborative filtering (e.g., Stern et al., WWW 2009) and Minka’s “clutter”/assignment EP examples that are conceptually close to your one-hot V. The paper should position itself more explicitly with respect to EP: is Algorithm 2 recoverable as EP under a particular factor graph approximation, and if not, what is new beyond EP (e.g., the specific Onsager form and state-evolution prediction)? Likewise, please clarify novelty relative to bilinear GAMP [14], IterFac [8], and [9] (posterior-mean AMP).

Significance and empirical evaluation
- Clustering evaluation is narrow. Comparing only to Lloyd/K-means++ (and a VBMF variant) is insufficient given the literature. For Gaussian-mixture data and ORL, natural baselines include EM for GMMs (with careful initialization), spectral clustering, NMF with orthogonality/convexity constraints, and modern K-means variants. Without these, it is hard to assess practical impact.
- Report wall-clock time, not just iterations. O(mN) with small constants is attractive, but f/G can be costly for general priors; in the K-means case, the discrete update is cheap—quantify the per-iteration cost and speedups.
- Analyze sensitivity to initialization, r̂, and τ estimation; give ablations (with/without Onsager term) to demonstrate that the advantage over Lloyd comes from the AMP corrections.

Suggestions to improve clarity
- Unify notation (m vs N scaling), consistently carry β through all formulas, and move key parts of the derivation (leading to (9) and (17)/(18)) from the appendix into the main text.
- Provide a short, self-contained proof sketch of Proposition 1 and a discussion of when the assumptions hold.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, simple AMP algorithm with promising empirical gains over K-means/K-means++.
- Unifying β-framework for marginalization and MAP in low-rank models.
- Direct treatment of one-hot constraints within an AMP derivation.

Cons for acceptance
- Incomplete and sometimes questionable derivations; key steps need justification.
- Potential overlap with EP-based matrix factorization and bilinear GAMP; novelty positioning is unclear.
- Limited empirical comparison and analysis; significance to the clustering community is not yet convincingly demonstrated.

Overall, I find the ideas promising and the algorithms potentially useful, but the paper in its current form falls short on rigor and evaluation breadth. Strengthening the theoretical justification (especially Q1–Q5), clarifying novelty vs EP/biG-AMP, and expanding the empirical comparisons would substantially improve the paper. My inclination is borderline reject at this stage.
