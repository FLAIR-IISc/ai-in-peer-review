Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional structural constraints on the factors (e.g., sparsity, nonnegativity) by deriving an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm. The posterior is “tempered” with a parameter β, which unifies marginal inference (β = 1) and MAP estimation (β → ∞). The algorithm provides update equations for factor means and scaled covariances that incorporate AMP-style Onsager corrections. A key result (Proposition 1) connects fixed points of the MAP-AMP iterations to global minima of partially minimized objectives, linking AMP to ICM-like updates.

A notable application is to K-means-type clustering. By encoding the one-hot constraint on V explicitly, the authors obtain a principled AMP variant of K-means with assignment updates that include two interpretable corrections: a leave-one-out de-biasing when reassigning a point to its current cluster, and a term that favors currently smaller clusters. The method also supports a maximum-accuracy (marginal MAP) clustering variant when discrete priors are used for V.

The work builds on AMP for compressed sensing and generalized AMP (e.g., Donoho–Maleki–Montanari, Rangan), the IterFac rank-one AMP (Rangan & Fletcher), and recent bilinear AMP variants (e.g., Matsushita & Tanaka; Parker et al.; Krzakala et al.). Compared to prior AMP for low-rank problems that primarily estimate posterior means, this paper extends to MAP, handles general-rank factors without assuming per-row independence, and applies AMP directly to one-hot-constrained clustering. The connection between low-rank factorization and K-means is well known (e.g., Ding & He, 2004), but the explicit AMP treatment of the assignment constraint and the derived correction terms appear novel.

Strengths
- Novel AMP formulation that unifies marginal and MAP inference via β and extends prior low-rank AMP to MAP estimation for general rank.
- Clear, interpretable assignment rule for K-means that reveals principled leave-one-out (Onsager-like) corrections beyond Lloyd/ICM.
- Solid empirical evaluation on synthetic data (varying rank) and on ORL faces; both K-means loss and clustering accuracy are reported.
- Good baselines: Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes. AMP methods compare favorably in loss and accuracy; convergence appears fast.
- Complexity per iteration is linear in the number of observed entries; the extra AMP terms cost only O(m+N) beyond variational updates.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical guarantees: no state evolution analysis, no convergence conditions, and no global optimality guarantees beyond Proposition 1’s fixed-point property.
- The estimator relies on a heuristic noise-variance estimate τ during clustering; robustness to τ mis-specification is not analyzed.
- Experiments primarily use r̂ = r; robustness to rank mismatch and to prior mis-specification (for U and V) is not shown.
- Real-data evaluation is limited (only ORL). No runtime or wall-clock comparisons are provided; only iteration counts.
- Some derivation details are deferred to the supplement; guidance on computing f and G for common priors (and numerical stability/damping) could be clearer.

Quality
Technically sound at the algorithmic level, with correct positioning relative to belief propagation and AMP. The link to ICM and the role of correction terms are insightful. However, claims would be strengthened by state evolution predictions and empirical verification, by a convergence analysis (even under simplified assumptions), and by robustness studies (rank/prior mismatch, τ). The K-means application is well-motivated and the empirical evidence is promising.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized. The AMP updates are carefully presented and the interpretation of the clustering corrections is clear. To improve clarity: add concrete examples of f and G for common priors (Gaussian, Laplace/sparse, nonnegative), discuss numerical integration/minimization costs for r̂ > 1, and describe damping/regularization used to prevent oscillations. Provide pseudo-code for the clustering variant and specify the τ update precisely.

Originality
Moderate to high. Extending low-rank AMP beyond posterior means to MAP, unifying with β-tempering, and handling one-hot constraints in AMP for K-means are novel contributions. The general idea of casting clustering as low-rank factorization is known, but the AMP-based assignment rule adds new insight.

Significance
Potentially significant. AMP is an important tool for large-scale inference; bringing it to constrained low-rank factorization and clustering could impact practice, especially in high dimensions. The empirical gains over K-means/K-means++ are notable, although broader benchmarks and stronger theory would bolster impact.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel AMP formulation (MAP via β → ∞) with interpretable correction terms and practical clustering gains.
- Strong empirical results on synthetic and real data; both loss and accuracy improve over standard baselines.
- Low per-iteration cost and fast convergence in practice.

Arguments against acceptance
- Weak theory: no state evolution, no convergence analysis, and limited guarantees beyond a fixed-point optimality property.
- Limited robustness and scalability analysis; sparse set of real datasets; no wall-clock runtimes.
- Relation to closely related bilinear/biG-AMP methods could be compared more thoroughly.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add state evolution equations and at least empirical validation; or provide a theoretical analysis under simplified priors/noise.
- Evaluate robustness to r̂ ≠ r and to prior mis-specification; study sensitivity to τ estimation and include damping strategies to mitigate oscillations.
- Report runtime and memory usage versus K-means/K-means++/VB, and add stronger baselines (e.g., EM for GMMs with diagonal covariances, spectral clustering).
- Expand related work: K-means theory (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007; Ostrovsky et al., 2006; Awasthi, Blum & Sheffet, 2012; Ding & He, 2004) and clarify differences from bilinear GAMP (Parker et al., 2013) and dictionary learning AMP (Krzakala et al., 2013).
- Provide code and more implementation details for f/G under common priors.

Overall recommendation
A solid AMP-based contribution that is technically interesting and practically promising, especially for high-dimensional clustering. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors strengthen the theoretical discussion (at least empirical state evolution) and broaden the empirical evaluation and comparisons.
