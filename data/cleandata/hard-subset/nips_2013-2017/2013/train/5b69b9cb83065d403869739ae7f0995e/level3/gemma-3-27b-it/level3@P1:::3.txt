This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction within a Bayesian framework. The authors extend existing AMP approaches to handle general rank cases and demonstrate its application to K-means type clustering, achieving performance improvements over Lloyd's K-means algorithm in numerical experiments. The core idea is to formulate clustering as a low-rank reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints.

The work builds upon a significant body of research in low-rank matrix completion and clustering. The Bayesian approach to matrix factorization, as noted by the authors, has been explored with methods like Monte Carlo sampling [3] and variational Bayes [4, 5]. AMP algorithms have gained prominence in compressed sensing [6] and have been adapted for matrix reconstruction [8, 9], but the presented work aims to extend these by enabling MAP estimation and applying the method to clustering. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is also not novel [10, 11], however, the paper aims to offer a new algorithm addressing the constraint of exclusive cluster assignment within this framework.

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear structure and accessible presentation of the main idea. The authors successfully demonstrate the applicability of AMP to a clustering problem, achieving promising empirical results. The formulation leveraging low-rank structure for clustering provides a potentially interesting perspective. However, several weaknesses significantly impact the overall quality of the work.

A primary concern is the lack of rigorous mathematical detail. The derivations are often “sketchy,” omitting crucial steps even in the supplementary material. For example, the justification for the O(1/√m) term in Appendix line 139, and the omission of β in Section 2 are unexplained. The asymptotic analysis, including the derivation of equation (19), relies on assumptions about the growth rates of m and N, presented tersely and without sufficient justification of their rigor. The claim linking mτ_w^2 to E[A_{il}^2] in line 167 is questionable; mτ_w^2 represents the likelihood variance of the observed data A, and equating it to the data’s second moment requires further justification. Furthermore, the paper doesn't adequately explain how the algorithm with finite β converges to the MAP solution, as exchanging the limit and minimization of the KL divergence isn't always valid. The proof of Proposition 1 is similarly brief; the derivation of equation (17) lacks sufficient detail, hindering verification.

Regarding originality, the algorithm bears a strong resemblance to Expectation-Propagation (EP) applied to matrix factorization. The resemblance to standard EP updates, as outlined in Stern et al. (WWW 2009), warrants further investigation and discussion. The authors should more thoroughly contrast their method with existing EP-based approaches.  The proposed clustering algorithm’s connection to the “clutter” problem encountered in EP, as highlighted by Minka, is a potentially relevant observation, although currently only a suggestion.  

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the narrow focus on comparison with Lloyd's K-means algorithm. Given the extensive literature on clustering algorithms, demonstrating superiority over k-means++ alone is insufficient. The combination of the sketchy proofs and potential overlap with prior EP work diminishes confidence in the practical and theoretical contributions of this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel application of AMP algorithms to clustering.
*   Empirically demonstrates performance gains over Lloyd’s K-means.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of mathematical rigor and detail in derivations.
*   Potential overlap with existing Expectation-Propagation techniques.
*   Limited comparison with other relevant clustering algorithms.
*   Questionable asymptotic analysis.
*   Insufficient justification for key approximations.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents an interesting idea but suffers from significant shortcomings in mathematical justification and a lack of thorough comparison to related work. While the empirical results are encouraging, the lack of rigor and potential overlap with existing techniques significantly reduce the confidence in the originality and impact of this work. I recommend **rejection** in its current form. The authors should address the concerns regarding mathematical clarity, rigorously justify their assumptions, and more thoroughly position their work within the broader context of matrix factorization and clustering algorithms.
