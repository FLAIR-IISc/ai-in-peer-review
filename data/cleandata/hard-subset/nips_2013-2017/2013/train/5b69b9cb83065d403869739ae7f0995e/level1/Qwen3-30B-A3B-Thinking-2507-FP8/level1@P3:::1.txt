

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that can incorporate various structural properties (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity) through appropriate prior distributions. The authors extend the AMP framework, originally developed for compressed sensing, to the matrix reconstruction problem, and apply it to K-means clustering by reformulating the clustering problem as a low-rank matrix reconstruction task with specific structural constraints. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) an AMP algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP problems in low-rank matrix reconstruction, and (2) an application of this algorithm to clustering that outperforms standard Lloyd's K-means.

The paper builds upon prior work in AMP for compressed sensing [6,7] and low-rank matrix factorization [8,9]. The authors correctly note that while [9] proposed an AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix reconstruction, it was limited to posterior mean estimation, whereas their work extends to MAP estimation and other inference problems. They also differentiate their approach from recent works on dictionary learning [13] and generalized observation models [14] by fixing the rank r rather than the ratio r/m in the asymptotic limit.

**Strengths:**
The paper's main strength is the elegant extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction, which provides a theoretically grounded framework for incorporating diverse structural priors. The algorithm derivation is technically sound, and the connection between the AMP algorithm and variational Bayes (as shown in Section 4.2) is insightful. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, demonstrating that the AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means in terms of both clustering accuracy and convergence speed on both synthetic and real datasets (ORL Faces). The authors also provide a clear explanation of how their clustering algorithm differs from standard approaches, particularly regarding the handling of cluster assignment constraints.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of theoretical justification for why the AMP approach should outperform standard methods like Lloyd's K-means. While the authors claim their algorithm "outperforms" Lloyd's method, they do not provide a clear theoretical explanation for this superiority beyond computational efficiency claims. The state evolution analysis mentioned in Section 4.1 is acknowledged as being omitted due to space constraints, but this is a critical component for understanding the theoretical guarantees of AMP algorithms.

Another concern is the paper's limited comparison with other recent low-rank matrix reconstruction methods. While the authors cite [13,14], they don't provide a detailed comparison of their approach with these methods in the context of clustering, which would strengthen their claim of novelty. The paper also assumes the rank r is known, which is a limitation in practical applications where the true rank is often unknown.

The paper's claim that "the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering achieves the highest accuracy among the five algorithms" is somewhat misleading. The comparison is made against the variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF-MA), which is known to be slower to converge than ICM-based methods. The authors acknowledge that the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering converges faster than VBMF-MA, but they don't explain why the AMP approach should be superior to standard ICM-based methods for maximum accuracy clustering.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the AMP literature and its application to clustering. The proposed algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. However, the paper could be significantly strengthened by providing a more detailed theoretical explanation of why the AMP approach outperforms standard methods, and by including a more comprehensive comparison with recent low-rank matrix reconstruction techniques.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized, with sufficient mathematical detail to allow reproduction of the results. The experimental design is appropriate, and the results are convincing. The work advances the state of the art in matrix reconstruction and clustering, particularly for high-dimensional data where the authors demonstrate clear advantages.

**Recommendation:** Accept.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural priors
- Clear theoretical foundation and rigorous experimental validation
- Demonstrated practical advantages over standard methods in clustering applications
- Well-written with appropriate comparison to prior work

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical justification for empirical improvements over standard methods
- Incomplete comparison with recent low-rank matrix reconstruction techniques
- Assumption of known rank r, which is a practical limitation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a unified framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction with diverse structural priors and demonstrating its effectiveness in clustering applications. The theoretical insights and empirical results are strong, and the work represents a significant step forward in applying AMP techniques to matrix factorization problems. The minor weaknesses do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the contribution.
