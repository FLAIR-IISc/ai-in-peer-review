

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The authors formulate the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem in a Bayesian framework, where the matrix A0 = U0V0^T is to be reconstructed from noisy observations A = A0 + W. They extend previous AMP work (e.g., [8, 9]) to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation problems, which previous AMP algorithms for matrix reconstruction could not do. The key novelty is the extension to handle general estimation objectives beyond posterior mean estimation.

The authors demonstrate that their AMP algorithm can be applied to clustering by reformulating K-means as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with structural constraints (each row of V must be a standard basis vector). This differs from previous approaches [10, 11] in that it directly handles the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster. The proposed AMP algorithm for clustering (Algorithm 3) is shown to outperform Lloyd's K-means algorithm and variational Bayes matrix factorization on both artificial and real datasets (ORL Face Database).

The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) an AMP-based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation; (2) a novel application to clustering that properly handles the assignment constraint; and (3) theoretical guarantees about the fixed points of the algorithm (Proposition 1).

Strengths:
1. Technical soundness: The paper provides a clear derivation of the AMP algorithm, with rigorous theoretical justification. The extension from previous AMP work [9] to handle MAP estimation is well-motivated and properly justified. The authors correctly identify the key theoretical advantage of AMP over variational Bayes: the Bethe approximation used in AMP can better capture dependencies between variables than the mean-field approximation used in variational Bayes.

2. Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the algorithm is presented with precise equations. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, and the results are presented clearly with appropriate statistical measures.

3. Novelty: The paper distinguishes itself from previous work by extending AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation, which previous AMP algorithms for matrix reconstruction could not do. The application to clustering with proper handling of the assignment constraint is novel and well-justified.

4. Significance: The proposed algorithm has practical significance as it provides a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The numerical experiments demonstrate clear improvements in both reconstruction error and clustering accuracy, particularly for high-dimensional data.

Weaknesses:
1. The convergence of the AMP algorithm is not guaranteed in general, though the authors assume convergence for their analysis. This is a common limitation of AMP algorithms, but the paper could have provided more discussion about when convergence is likely to occur.

2. The comparison to related AMP-based methods ([13, 14]) is somewhat superficial. The paper mentions differences in assumptions but doesn't provide a detailed technical comparison of how their approach differs in the derivation or performance.

3. The paper doesn't fully explain why the AMP approach works better than variational Bayes in practice, beyond the theoretical justification about the Bethe approximation. More detailed analysis of the performance differences would strengthen the claims.

4. The computational complexity analysis is somewhat limited. While the authors state the algorithm has linear complexity (O(mN)), they don't provide detailed analysis of the number of iterations required for convergence.

Overall, this is a strong NIPS paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and its applications. The theoretical development is sound, the experimental results are convincing, and the application to clustering is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly distinguishes its contributions from previous work and provides a theoretically grounded alternative to existing methods. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor compared to the paper's strengths.

Pro: 
- Well-motivated extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation
- Clear theoretical justification for advantages over variational Bayes
- Novel application to clustering with proper handling of assignment constraints
- Strong experimental results demonstrating improvements over existing methods

Con:
- Convergence not guaranteed in general
- Superficial comparison to related AMP-based methods
- Limited analysis of computational complexity and convergence properties

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the field. It advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix reconstruction and demonstrates practical utility through its application to clustering. The results are important for both theoretical understanding and practical applications. I recommend acceptance.
