

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering problems. The authors formulate the problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing them to incorporate structural properties of matrices (such as sparsity) in addition to low-rankness. Their key contribution is extending AMP to handle both marginalization problems (for posterior mean estimation) and MAP problems (for maximum a posteriori estimation) through a parameter β. The paper also demonstrates how to apply this algorithm to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with a structural constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster.

The work builds on the successful AMP framework developed for compressed sensing (Donoho et al., 2009) and extends it to matrix reconstruction. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related papers (Rangan & Fletcher, 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012) by highlighting their different treatment of the rank parameter and their relaxation of the statistical independence assumption among components.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a rigorous derivation of the AMP algorithm. The authors provide a convergence property (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of their AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm, which is a standard approach for this problem. The experimental results are thorough, with 500 synthetic instances and 50 trials on real data, consistently demonstrating performance improvements over baselines (Lloyd's K-means, VBMF-MA, K-means++). The authors carefully report both K-means loss and accuracy metrics, and the computational cost (O(mN) per iteration) is well-justified.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The problem formulation, algorithm derivation, and experimental setup flow naturally. The algorithm pseudocode (Algorithms 2 and 3) is clear and detailed enough for replication. The comparison with related work is well-motivated, helping contextualize the contribution within the NIPS community.

Originality: The paper makes a novel contribution by extending AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with a unified framework for marginalization and MAP estimation. While AMP has been applied to related problems (e.g., dictionary learning, Krzakala et al., 2013), the specific application to low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible structural priors is new. The authors provide a careful comparison with existing methods, highlighting how their approach differs from previous AMP-based methods.

Significance: The results demonstrate that the proposed AMP algorithm outperforms existing methods in terms of both K-means loss and accuracy, particularly for high-dimensional data. The real-world results on the ORL Database of Faces show practical utility. The computational efficiency (O(mN) per iteration) and ability to handle a wide range of structural priors make the method potentially valuable for practitioners dealing with large-scale matrix problems.

Strengths: Novel extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with a unified framework, thorough experimental validation, clear theoretical properties, practical application to clustering with demonstrated improvements, and well-written presentation.

Weaknesses: The paper could better motivate why AMP would outperform variational Bayes beyond the intuitive argument about Bethe vs. mean-field approximations. While the paper claims to handle the constraint that each datum belongs to exactly one cluster, it's not entirely clear how this differs from other low-rank matrix reconstruction approaches to clustering (e.g., Xu et al., 2003). More diverse real-world datasets would strengthen the claims.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of matrix factorization and clustering. The AMP-based approach provides a principled and efficient way to handle low-rank matrix reconstruction with various structural priors, and the application to clustering demonstrates clear practical benefits. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and well-motivated.

Pro: Novel algorithm with strong theoretical grounding, thorough experiments showing consistent improvement over baselines, clear connection to AMP literature and matrix factorization.
Con: Could better motivate AMP's advantages over variational Bayes, more diverse real-world experiments would strengthen claims.

Overall, this is a strong paper that advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The proposed AMP algorithm is theoretically sound, computationally efficient, and empirically superior to existing methods. I recommend acceptance.
