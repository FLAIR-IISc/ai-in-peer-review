

**Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending the AMP framework from compressed sensing (Donoho et al., NIPS 2009) to low-rank matrix problems. The authors formulate the reconstruction problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing them to incorporate structural properties of the factors (e.g., sparsity) beyond simple low-rankness. They develop a unified AMP algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation through a single parameter β, and apply it to K-means clustering by reformulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with additional structural constraints. The paper compares their approach with Lloyd's K-means, variational Bayes matrix factorization, and K-means++ on synthetic and real data, demonstrating superior performance in terms of accuracy and convergence speed.

**Strengths**

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper presents a technically rigorous extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction. The theoretical foundation is strong, with Proposition 1 showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm, providing a solid theoretical basis for the approach. The Bayesian framework allows for flexible incorporation of structural properties, which is a significant advantage over many existing methods.

2. **Novelty**: The paper makes several key contributions: (a) extending AMP to general-rank matrix reconstruction (beyond the rank-one case of Rangan & Fletcher, 2012), (c) providing a unified treatment of marginalization and MAP estimation through the parameter β, and (d) applying this framework to clustering in a novel way that properly handles the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster. This last contribution distinguishes the work from previous clustering approaches based on low-rank matrix reconstruction (e.g., Xu et al., SIGIR 2003).

3. **Experimental Validation**: The numerical experiments are comprehensive and well-designed. The synthetic data experiments (500 problem instances) show consistent advantages over baselines in terms of both normalized K-means loss and accuracy. The real data experiments on the ORL Database of Faces demonstrate practical utility, with the AMP algorithm outperforming K-means++ in 48/50 trials for K-means loss and 47/50 for accuracy. The convergence speed comparison is particularly convincing, showing AMP-MA converges as fast as AMP-KM despite being a more complex algorithm.

4. **Clarity**: The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, and the connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction is well-motivated. Algorithm 3 for K-means clustering is particularly well-explained, with clear comparison to Lloyd's algorithm.

**Weaknesses**

1. **State Evolution Analysis**: The paper mentions state evolution as an important property of AMP algorithms but does not provide it for their proposed method. This is a significant omission, as state evolution is a key theoretical advantage of AMP that allows for performance prediction in large-scale problems. While the authors state they omit this due to space constraints, it's a critical component of AMP's theoretical appeal.

2. **Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper states the computational cost is O(mN) per iteration, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with other methods (e.g., variational Bayes). It would be valuable to understand how this compares to the actual computational burden of other approaches in practice, especially for large-scale problems.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to the parameter β or how to choose it in practice. This is particularly relevant for the maximum accuracy clustering application, where β=1 is used.

4. **Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references Matsushita & Tanaka (2012) as prior work on AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction but doesn't provide a detailed comparison with their approach or with more recent work like Krzakala et al. (2013) on blind calibration and dictionary learning.

**Arguments for Acceptance**

- The paper presents a technically sound extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with clear theoretical foundations.
- The unified treatment of marginalization and MAP estimation through β is elegant and useful for practitioners.
- The application to clustering is novel and well-motivated, with clear advantages demonstrated in experiments.
- The numerical experiments are thorough and demonstrate consistent superiority over baselines.
- The work addresses a fundamental problem in machine learning with significant practical implications.

**Arguments Against Acceptance**

- The omission of state evolution analysis is a notable theoretical weakness.
- The computational complexity analysis is insufficient for understanding practical advantages.
- The paper doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the algorithm to key parameters.

**Overall Assessment**

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending the AMP framework to low-rank matrix reconstruction and applying it to clustering in a novel and effective way. The theoretical foundation is strong, the experimental validation is comprehensive, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. While the omission of state evolution analysis is a weakness, the paper's strengths in technical soundness, novelty, and experimental validation outweigh this limitation. The work is clearly relevant to NIPS, advancing the state of the art in a fundamental problem with practical applications.

**Recommendation**: Accept

**Additional Comments**

The authors should consider adding a brief discussion of how state evolution would work for their algorithm, even if it's just a high-level description. This would significantly strengthen the theoretical foundation of the paper. Additionally, a more detailed comparison of computational complexity with variational Bayes methods would help readers understand the practical advantages of the proposed approach. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the field.
