Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction when additional structure on the factors (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity, one-hot constraints) is known. It derives an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm starting from belief propagation on a dense factor graph, introducing a temperature parameter β to interpolate between standard Bayesian inference (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞). The algorithm handles general vector-valued priors p̂u, p̂v on rows of U and V without requiring independence among components within a row, and has O(mN) per-iteration cost. A simple specialization yields an AMP-based K-means algorithm (AMP-KM) by setting a one-hot prior on V, producing an assignment rule that includes “Onsager” (leave-one-out) corrections compared to Lloyd’s algorithm. A second specialization (β=1 with discrete prior on V) yields a “maximum accuracy” clustering method approximating marginal MAP.

The work builds on AMP for compressed sensing and generalized linear models (Donoho et al. 2009; Rangan 2011), the rank-one IterFac (Rangan & Fletcher 2012), and a prior AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction (Matsushita & Tanaka 2012) that only addressed posterior means; here the authors target both posterior means and MAP, including discrete priors. For clustering, the formulation connects K-means as Gaussian MLE with equal and known variance, similar to classical EM for spherical GMMs and matrix factorization views of clustering and NMF (Ding et al. 2010). The paper also notes contemporaneous bilinear GAMP (Parker et al. 2013) and AMP for dictionary learning (Krzakala et al. 2013).

Quality:
- Theory: The derivation of Algorithm 2 (in the supplement) follows standard AMP approximations. A useful Proposition 1 shows that fixed points (under a PSD condition typically met) are coordinate-wise global minima of CMAP, hence fixed points of ICM. This supports the claim that AMP-KM might avoid some poor local minima. The algorithm’s state evolution is not presented, and convergence is assumed rather than proved; damping/stepsize mechanisms are not discussed. The β-tempered formulation is elegant and unifies posterior mean and MAP limits. The specialization to one-hot priors is clean and yields an intuitive leave-one-out correction term in the assignment step.
- Empirics: On synthetic data, AMP-KM achieves lower K-means loss than Lloyd’s and K-means++; AMP-MA achieves highest clustering accuracy and fast convergence compared to VBMF. On ORL faces (40-way clustering), AMP-KM improves K-means loss and accuracy in most runs using K-means++ initialization, though occasional mode collapse to a single cluster occurs. The experiments are informative but somewhat limited in scope (one real dataset; no runtime plots; few baselines beyond Lloyd/K++/VBMF).

Clarity:
The paper is generally well organized and technically clear, with careful notation and explicit algorithms. The role of β, the definitions of f and G as mean and scaled covariance, and the specialization to K-means are well explained. However:
- The main text defers the AMP derivation and state evolution entirely to the supplement; at least a sketch and the key approximations would help.
- Practical details (damping, stopping, handling oscillations, empty clusters) deserve more discussion; the authors note oscillations and a two-step stopping rule for AMP-KM but do not analyze it.
- The estimation of τ during AMP-KM is motivated by large-system heuristics; more guidance on stability and sensitivity would be useful.

Originality:
The extension of AMP for bilinear matrix factorization to support general priors and both β=1 and β→∞ cases appears novel relative to [9], with an appealing, unified formulation. The direct treatment of the one-hot assignment constraint within AMP to produce a K-means solver with Onsager corrections is, to my knowledge, original and timely, and different from VB, NMF variants, and EM for GMMs. The proposition linking fixed points to ICM fixed points is also a nice insight.

Significance:
Efficient AMP algorithms for structured matrix factorization are important, and connecting AMP to K-means with demonstrable empirical gains could impact practice. The approach is flexible (arbitrary priors on rows) and computationally attractive. However, the current empirical validation is relatively narrow, and the lack of state evolution or convergence guarantees reduces theoretical impact. If the method robustly improves K-means local optima across domains, its significance would be substantial.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled AMP derivation that unifies posterior and MAP estimation via β.
- Novel and practical AMP-based K-means algorithm with intuitive leave-one-out correction.
- Empirical improvements over Lloyd’s and K-means++ on synthetic and ORL data; AMP-MA shows strong accuracy and fast convergence vs VBMF.
- Low per-iteration complexity and generality to arbitrary priors, including discrete ones.

Cons for acceptance:
- No state evolution characterization or convergence analysis; algorithm relies on heuristic stopping and ad hoc τ estimation.
- Limited comparisons: no EM for spherical GMMs, spectral clustering, orthogonal NMF, or recent robust K-means variants; no wall-clock times.
- Occasional failure mode (single-cluster collapse) in real data; mitigation strategies not fully developed.
- Sensitivity to mis-specified priors, rank mis-specification, class imbalance, and noise model not systematically studied.

Suggestions and questions:
1. Provide at least a condensed derivation or summary of the Onsager terms and the key independence/scaling assumptions; include a short state evolution for a canonical setting (e.g., Gaussian priors) and compare predictions to experiments.
2. Discuss convergence aids (damping, stepsize adaptation, annealing β) and strategies to prevent collapse/oscillations; consider re-seeding empty clusters.
3. Compare against EM for spherical GMMs (which optimizes the same likelihood), spectral clustering, and orthogonal NMF-based clustering, and report wall-clock times and iteration counts.
4. Analyze sensitivity to τ estimation and propose joint updates for τ (e.g., via EM or state evolution moments) rather than a plug-in residual estimate.
5. Ablate the Onsager corrections in AMP-KM to quantify their impact versus Lloyd’s; report distribution of achieved K-means loss across multiple initializations.
6. Explore robustness to rank mis-specification (r̂≠r), imbalanced clusters, and non-Gaussian noise; discuss extensions to missing data (a common matrix factorization setting).
7. For AMP-MA, evaluate with imperfect priors to gauge practical utility when true priors are unknown.

Overall recommendation:
This is a solid and promising contribution at the intersection of AMP and clustering. With strengthened theoretical characterization (even partial) and broader empirical evaluation, it would make a strong NIPS paper. In its current form, it demonstrates clear novelty and practical benefits but would benefit from deeper analysis and comparisons.
