### Summary of Main Ideas

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The problem is formulated within a Bayesian setting, which allows for the flexible incorporation of prior knowledge about the structure of the factor matrices (e.g., sparsity). The primary contribution is the derivation of a general AMP algorithm that can handle both marginal posterior estimation (for calculating posterior means) and Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This unification is achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter, β, into the posterior distribution, where β=1 corresponds to the marginalization problem and β→∞ corresponds to the MAP problem.

The second key contribution is the application of this framework to the problem of K-means clustering. The authors reformulate K-means as a specific instance of low-rank matrix reconstruction where one of the factor matrices is constrained to have rows that are one-hot vectors, representing cluster assignments. The derived AMP algorithm for this problem (AMP-KM) provides a novel update rule for cluster assignments that differs from the standard Lloyd's algorithm. Specifically, the AMP update includes corrective terms that account for the influence of a data point on its current cluster center and favor assignments to smaller clusters. This work builds upon the lineage of AMP algorithms developed for compressed sensing [6] and extends previous work on AMP for rank-one [8] and general-rank matrix factorization [9], notably by providing a framework for MAP estimation. While the use of low-rank factorization for clustering is not new [11], the derivation of a specific, high-performing algorithm from first principles via the AMP framework is a novel approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm is derived from the belief propagation framework, following a methodology that is well-established in the statistical physics and information theory communities for deriving AMP algorithms. The authors provide a clear connection between their algorithm and existing methods like the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), correctly identifying the additional "Onsager" terms that distinguish AMP. Proposition 1 is a valuable theoretical result, establishing that any fixed point of the AMP algorithm for MAP estimation is also a fixed point of the ICM algorithm. This suggests that the AMP algorithm is less prone to converging to certain poor local optima that are not fixed points for ICM.

The experimental evaluation is solid and effectively demonstrates the claims. On both synthetic and real-world data, the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) consistently outperform standard benchmarks like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and VBMF in terms of their respective objectives (K-means loss and accuracy). The results are presented with appropriate metrics and statistical summaries (averages and standard deviations over multiple runs).

A minor weakness is the handling of convergence. The paper explicitly states that convergence of the algorithm is not guaranteed and is assumed. While this is a known issue with AMP algorithms, a more thorough discussion of this limitation would be beneficial. The ad-hoc stopping criterion used for AMP-KM to handle oscillations is a practical workaround but points to the underlying stability issue.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setting, motivation, and contributions are laid out clearly in the introduction. Section 2 provides a precise mathematical formulation of both the general low-rank reconstruction problem and its specific application to clustering. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 2) and its specialization to K-means clustering (Algorithm 3) are presented in a clean and understandable format. The interpretation of the new terms in the AMP-KM update rule (Eq. 18b) is insightful and provides a strong intuition for why the algorithm might outperform Lloyd's. The experimental setup and results are also clearly described, making the work easy to follow and assess.

**Originality:**
The work is original and presents several novel contributions.
1.  The extension of the AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix reconstruction to handle MAP estimation via the β-parameterization is a novel and useful generalization.
2.  The application of this AMP framework to K-means clustering is highly original. While matrix factorization has been used for clustering before, the derivation of this specific algorithm with its unique update rule directly from the AMP formalism is new. This provides a principled, message-passing-based alternative to the widely used Lloyd's algorithm.
3.  The connection drawn between the AMP fixed points and ICM fixed points (Proposition 1) is a novel insight that helps theoretically situate the proposed algorithm relative to existing ones.

The authors do a good job of citing and differentiating their work from related AMP literature [8, 9, 13, 14] and classical matrix factorization and clustering methods.

**Significance:**
The results presented in this paper are significant. K-means clustering is a fundamental and widely used algorithm in machine learning and data analysis. The introduction of a new algorithm, AMP-KM, that empirically outperforms Lloyd's algorithm and K-means++ on both synthetic and real data is an important practical contribution. Given its competitive computational cost (O(mN) per iteration), the proposed method could be a valuable tool for practitioners.

Furthermore, the general Bayesian AMP framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction is a significant contribution in its own right. Its flexibility in accommodating different priors makes it a powerful tool for a variety of problems beyond clustering, such as matrix completion with sparsity constraints. The demonstration that AMP can outperform VBMF in both accuracy and convergence speed (for maximum accuracy clustering) is an important finding for the community of researchers working on approximate Bayesian inference.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, principled, and high-performing algorithm for K-means clustering, a fundamental problem in machine learning.
*   **Pro:** It presents a general and flexible AMP framework for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that unifies marginal and MAP estimation, which is of broad interest.
*   **Pro:** The experimental results are strong, showing clear improvements over widely used and established baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF) on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and provides valuable theoretical insights connecting the proposed method to existing algorithms like ICM.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   **Con:** The theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence is limited; convergence is assumed rather than proven or analyzed in detail. The mention of state evolution analysis is tantalizing but its omission (due to space) leaves the theoretical performance guarantees unexplored in the paper.
*   **Con:** The method for estimating the noise parameter τ in the K-means application is heuristic. A more detailed study of the algorithm's sensitivity to this parameter would strengthen the experimental section.
