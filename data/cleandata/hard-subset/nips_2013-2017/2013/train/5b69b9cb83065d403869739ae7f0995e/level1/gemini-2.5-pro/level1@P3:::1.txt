This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The problem is framed in a Bayesian setting, where prior distributions on the matrix factors U and V can be used to incorporate structural knowledge. The authors' first contribution is the derivation of a general AMP algorithm that can handle both the computation of posterior marginals (for posterior mean or marginal MAP estimates) and full MAP estimation. This work extends a previous AMP algorithm by Matsushita and Tanaka (2012) which was limited to posterior mean estimation. The authors unify these two estimation tasks by introducing an inverse temperature parameter β into the posterior. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 2) has a computational cost linear in the number of matrix entries and includes additional "Onsager" terms not present in related variational Bayes (VB) or iterated conditional modes (ICM) algorithms.

The second, and arguably more impactful, contribution is the application of this AMP framework to the problem of K-means clustering. By formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one factor matrix consists of one-hot cluster assignment vectors, the authors derive a new iterative clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3). This algorithm modifies the assignment step of the classic Lloyd’s algorithm by including correction terms that penalize re-assigning a data point to its current cluster and favor assigning points to smaller clusters. This provides an intuitive mechanism for escaping poor local minima. The paper presents strong empirical evidence on both synthetic and real-world datasets showing that the proposed AMP clustering algorithms (for both K-means and a "maximum accuracy" variant) outperform standard methods like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a variational Bayes-based clustering method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of AMP to derive a new K-means algorithm is highly original and significant. The resulting algorithm is not just a black box; the modified update rule (Eq. 18b) has a clear and compelling intuition—it corrects for the bias introduced when a cluster center is calculated using the very point being assigned. This provides a principled way to improve upon Lloyd's algorithm, a fundamental workhorse of machine learning. The strong empirical results, showing consistent improvements in both K-means loss and clustering accuracy over established baselines, underscore the practical importance of this contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed algorithm. The derivation from belief propagation connects the method to a well-understood framework for approximate inference. Proposition 1, which establishes that any fixed point of the MAP-AMP algorithm is also a fixed point of the ICM algorithm, is a valuable theoretical result. It suggests that the AMP dynamics may help navigate the cost function landscape more effectively to find better local optima than ICM, a claim supported by the experiments.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The problem setting is clearly defined, and the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is explained well. The final derived clustering algorithm (Eq. 18b) is presented in a form that makes its relationship to Lloyd's algorithm transparent.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Omission of State Evolution Analysis:** A major theoretical strength of AMP algorithms is that their performance in the large system limit can be precisely characterized by a low-dimensional set of recursions known as "state evolution." The authors acknowledge that such an analysis is possible for their algorithm but omit it due to space constraints. This is a significant omission, as it would provide theoretical guarantees on the performance of the algorithm and formally establish its advantages over other methods, moving the claims beyond empirical observation.
*   **Convergence Assumptions:** The paper assumes convergence of the proposed AMP algorithm, which is not guaranteed in general. In fact, the authors note that they had to modify the stopping criterion for their clustering experiments due to observed oscillations. A more thorough discussion of the algorithm's convergence properties, even if empirical, and the potential for using damping or other techniques to ensure stability would strengthen the paper.
*   **Handling of the Noise Parameter `τ`:** The derived clustering algorithm depends on a noise variance parameter `τ`, which is not part of the original K-means problem formulation. The authors propose an ad-hoc heuristic of estimating `τ` at each iteration using the current reconstruction error. This approach feels somewhat unprincipled. An analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to this parameter, or a more integrated method for its estimation, would be beneficial.
*   **Accessibility of Derivation:** The full derivation of the main algorithm is relegated to the supplementary material. While understandable given page limits, this makes it difficult for a reviewer to fully vet the technical correctness of the core method from the main paper alone.

**Summary of evaluation:**

The paper makes a strong contribution by introducing a novel and effective clustering algorithm derived from the principled framework of Approximate Message Passing. The method is theoretically motivated, intuitively appealing, and demonstrates significant empirical improvements over widely-used baselines. While the theoretical analysis could be deepened by including a state evolution analysis and a more rigorous treatment of convergence, the novelty and practical significance of the clustering algorithm are high. The work is well-suited for the NIPS audience and is likely to inspire further research into AMP-based algorithms for a variety of matrix factorization and clustering problems. I recommend acceptance.
