

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with applications to clustering. The authors extend AMP, originally developed for compressed sensing, to handle low-rank matrix reconstruction problems with flexible structural priors (e.g., sparsity), addressing both marginalization and MAP estimation problems in a unified framework. The key innovation is extending previous AMP-based low-rank reconstruction methods [8, 9] to handle MAP estimation and incorporate a wider range of structural properties. The paper then applies this framework to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with an additional constraint that each datum must belong to exactly one cluster, which the authors claim is novel compared to prior work [10, 11].

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical derivation of the AMP algorithm, including a proof of optimality properties (Proposition 1). The algorithm is computationally efficient (O(mN) per iteration) and well-motivated through comparison with variational Bayes approaches. The experimental evaluation on synthetic and real data (ORL faces) shows consistent improvements over Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes methods in terms of clustering accuracy and convergence speed. The numerical results are thorough and well-presented.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending AMP to a broader class of low-rank matrix problems and providing a theoretically grounded, efficient solution for clustering. However, the connection to very recent AMP-based matrix reconstruction work [13, 14] could be more thoroughly discussed, particularly regarding their different asymptotic assumptions. While the clustering application is novel in its direct handling of cluster assignment constraints, the paper could better clarify how it differs from other low-rank-based clustering approaches.

Strengths: Technical soundness, clear theoretical foundation, strong empirical results, practical algorithm with efficient implementation.

Weaknesses: Limited comparison with the most recent AMP-based matrix reconstruction methods, some aspects of the theoretical analysis (state evolution) omitted due to space constraints.

Pro: Novel extension of AMP to handle a broader class of low-rank reconstruction problems with structural priors; clear application to clustering with improved performance over standard methods; technically rigorous with strong empirical validation.

Con: Limited discussion of connections to very recent AMP work; the theoretical contribution could be more clearly positioned against the most relevant prior art.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of matrix factorization and clustering, with clear significance for both theoretical understanding and practical applications. I recommend acceptance.
