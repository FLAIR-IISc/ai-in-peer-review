This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction in a Bayesian framework and applies it to clustering. The core idea is to extend existing AMP algorithms to handle general rank matrices and leverage the flexibility of the Bayesian approach to incorporate prior knowledge about the matrix factors. The authors claim their algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and demonstrate this with numerical experiments.

The paper builds upon a substantial body of work in low-rank matrix completion, sparse recovery, and Bayesian inference. The use of AMP is well-motivated given its success in compressed sensing (Donoho et al., 2009) and related fields (Rangan, 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012). The application of low-rank matrix reconstruction to clustering is not new (Xu et al., 2003; Ding et al., 2010), but the proposed AMP-based approach with the explicit constraint of exclusive cluster assignment is a novel contribution. The paper correctly positions itself within the broader literature, citing key works in each relevant area. 

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of AMP to general rank matrices with a focus on flexibility in incorporating priors is a worthwhile contribution. The direct handling of exclusive cluster assignment within the low-rank framework is also a positive aspect.
* **Theoretical Motivation:** The AMP algorithm is well-grounded in the belief propagation framework and the authors allude to potential state evolution analysis (though not presented due to space constraints).
* **Performance:** The numerical results demonstrate competitive performance against standard K-means and variational Bayes methods, particularly in higher dimensions. The comparison against K-means++ is also valuable.
* **Clarity (in sections):** The problem setting and algorithm derivations are reasonably clear, particularly after the initial setup.
* **Detailed Algorithm Description:** Algorithm 2 and 3 are presented with sufficient detail, enabling implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** While the authors mention state evolution, they do not present any theoretical guarantees on convergence or performance of the AMP algorithm. This is a significant weakness, as it leaves the reader without a clear understanding of when and why the algorithm will succeed. A discussion of the limitations of the algorithm (e.g., assumptions on noise distribution, prior choices) would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of the Algorithm:** Despite the claim of low computational cost, the algorithm still involves iterative updates and potential numerical integration (or minimization) within the `f` and `G` functions. A more detailed computational complexity analysis, including the cost of these steps, would be helpful.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t sufficiently address the sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter choices, such as the noise variance `τ` and the prior distributions `p̂u` and `p̂v`. How are these parameters chosen in practice?
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other methods is somewhat limited. While K-means is a good baseline, comparing to more recent and sophisticated matrix factorization techniques (e.g., those based on alternating minimization) would strengthen the results.
* **Clarity (overall):** While the individual sections are often clear, the paper as a whole feels somewhat dense and could benefit from improved organization and transitions between ideas. The notation is also occasionally heavy.
* **Missing Details in Experiments:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. For example, how were the initial values for the algorithms chosen? What are the specific parameter settings used for each experiment?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, addressing a potentially important problem.
* The numerical results demonstrate promising performance compared to existing methods.
* The theoretical motivation is sound, and the algorithm is presented with sufficient detail for implementation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The lack of theoretical guarantees on convergence and performance is a significant drawback.
* The computational complexity analysis is insufficient.
* The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* The clarity and organization could be improved.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. The novelty and potential of the proposed algorithm are appealing, and the numerical results are encouraging. However, the lack of theoretical guarantees and the insufficient discussion of practical considerations (parameter sensitivity, computational complexity) are significant weaknesses. I believe the paper has the potential to be a strong contribution with further work addressing these issues. The authors should focus on providing theoretical insights into the algorithm’s behavior and conducting more thorough experimental evaluations.
