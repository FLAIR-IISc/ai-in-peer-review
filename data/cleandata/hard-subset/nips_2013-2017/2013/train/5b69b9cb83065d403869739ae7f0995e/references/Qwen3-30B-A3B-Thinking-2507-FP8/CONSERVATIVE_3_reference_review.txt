

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) approach for low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering. The authors extend previous AMP algorithms to handle general-rank cases and multiple estimation types (posterior mean and MAP), claiming their method outperforms existing approaches like Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes methods.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

The paper builds on the AMP framework originally developed for compressed sensing [6, 7] and extends it to low-rank matrix reconstruction. It draws on prior work including IterFac for rank-one cases [8] and a general-rank AMP algorithm [9]. The authors claim their algorithm is the first to handle general-rank cases with both marginalization and MAP estimation, and the first to directly incorporate the "exactly one cluster assignment" constraint in clustering within low-rank reconstruction.

The paper makes connections to existing work on matrix factorization [3-5, 10, 11] and clustering [12], but the distinction from [9] (which the authors cite as a predecessor) is not sufficiently clear. The authors also mention similarities to [13, 14] but don't adequately explain how their approach differs.

## Strengths

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the proposed algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented with appropriate pseudocode. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering (Section 2.2) is conceptually sound. The paper provides a theoretical justification for the AMP approach compared to variational Bayes methods, noting that AMP can better capture dependencies between variables.

## Weaknesses

**Insufficient Validation:** The most significant weakness is the lack of thorough experimental validation against established baselines. The authors claim their AMP algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes approaches, but their experiments are limited to synthetic data with only 500 instances and one real dataset (ORL Faces). The comparison is not comprehensive enough to support their strong claims of superiority.

**Limited Baseline Comparison:** The paper does not compare against the most recent and strongest baselines for clustering, such as those using deep learning approaches or more sophisticated initialization methods. The comparison with K-means++ is appropriate, but the authors fail to demonstrate how their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods beyond the limited baselines they tested.

**Overstated Claims:** The paper claims their AMP algorithm "outperforms" existing methods, but the results in Figure 1 show only marginal improvements in accuracy and K-means loss. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these differences, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are meaningful.

**Lack of Convergence Guarantees:** While the paper presents Proposition 1 about the optimality of fixed points, it does not provide convergence guarantees for the AMP algorithm in general. The authors acknowledge convergence is not guaranteed, which is a significant concern for an algorithm meant to be used in practice.

**Insufficient Distinction from Prior Work:** The paper claims to be the "first" to handle certain aspects, but it doesn't sufficiently differentiate from [9] (which they cite) and other related work [13, 14]. The distinction between their approach and [9] is particularly unclear.

## Specific Concerns

1. The paper claims their AMP algorithm for K-means clustering "achieves the smallest K-means loss among the five algorithms" (Figure 1), but the difference is marginal (e.g., 0.001-0.005 in K-means loss). This doesn't justify the claim of "outperforming" existing methods.

2. The paper states that the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering "achieves the highest accuracy" but doesn't provide statistical significance for these results.

3. The computational complexity claim (O(mN) per iteration) is standard for matrix algorithms, but the paper doesn't provide evidence that this is significantly more efficient than existing methods in practice.

4. The real-world experiment on the ORL dataset is limited to 50 trials with different initializations, which is insufficient for robust conclusions about real-world performance.

## Recommendations

1. The authors should conduct more extensive experiments with additional datasets and stronger baselines, including recent deep learning approaches to clustering.

2. They should provide statistical significance tests for their performance comparisons to demonstrate whether the observed differences are meaningful.

3. The paper should more clearly distinguish their contributions from [9] and other related work, providing a more thorough comparison.

4. The authors should provide stronger theoretical guarantees for convergence or at least discuss the conditions under which convergence is guaranteed.

5. The paper should include a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach and when it might not outperform existing methods.

## Conclusion

While the paper presents a theoretically sound extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with potential applications to clustering, it falls short of providing sufficient evidence to support its claims of superiority over established methods. The experimental validation is limited, and the paper overstates the significance of its results. As a skeptical reviewer who prefers established methods, I find the evidence presented insufficient to justify the paper's claims of significant improvement over existing approaches. The paper would benefit from more extensive validation against stronger baselines and a more nuanced discussion of the conditions under which their approach provides meaningful advantages.
