This paper presents an algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The work has two primary contributions. First, it extends a previous AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction [9] to handle both marginal posterior estimation and Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation within a unified framework. This is achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter `β` into the posterior distribution. Second, and more significantly, the paper applies this AMP framework to the problem of K-means clustering. By formulating K-means as a low-rank matrix factorization problem where one of the factor matrices consists of one-hot vectors representing cluster assignments, the authors derive a novel iterative clustering algorithm. This new algorithm, termed AMP-KM, can be seen as a principled modification of the classic Lloyd’s algorithm. The paper also presents an AMP-based algorithm for "maximum accuracy clustering" derived from the marginal estimation setting. The proposed methods are evaluated on synthetic and real-world data, demonstrating improved performance over standard baselines like Lloyd's K-means and K-means++.

This work builds upon the well-established line of research on AMP, which originated in compressed sensing [6] and has been extended to various statistical estimation problems. The connection between matrix factorization and clustering is also not new [10, 11]. However, the direct application of state-of-the-art AMP machinery to derive a new K-means-type algorithm appears to be a novel contribution. The proposed method is also compared to Variational Bayes (VB) methods for matrix factorization [4, 5], with the authors correctly noting that AMP is based on the more accurate Bethe approximation, whereas VB typically relies on a simpler mean-field approximation.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the AMP algorithm appears to follow standard techniques from the statistical physics literature. The introduction of the `β` parameter to unify marginalization and MAP estimation is an elegant approach. Proposition 1, which establishes that any fixed point of the proposed AMP algorithm for the MAP problem is also a fixed point of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm, is a valuable theoretical result. This provides insight into the algorithm's behavior and its relationship to simpler alternating minimization schemes. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with comparisons to multiple relevant baselines on both synthetic and real data. The claims of superior performance are well-supported by the empirical results presented.

However, there are some areas where the technical presentation could be stronger. The paper states that the algorithm's convergence is assumed but not guaranteed, which is a known issue for AMP in general. While the authors mention that a state evolution analysis is possible, its omission (even from a supplement) leaves questions about the theoretical performance guarantees and conditions for convergence unanswered. Furthermore, the handling of the noise variance parameter `τ` in the K-means application (Section 4.3) is heuristic. The algorithm is derived for a fixed `τ`, but in practice, it is estimated at each iteration. The sensitivity of the algorithm to this estimation strategy is not discussed, which is a potential weakness.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the connection between the general low-rank reconstruction problem and the specific application to clustering is explained well. The key equations for the proposed algorithms (Algorithm 2 and 3) are presented, and the interpretation of the new terms in the AMP-KM algorithm (Eq. 18b) compared to Lloyd's algorithm is insightful.

A minor weakness in clarity is that the full derivation of the main AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is deferred to supplementary material. While understandable due to page limits, this makes the paper less self-contained and harder to verify for a reader interested in the core technical details.

**Originality:** The main novelty of the paper lies in the successful application of the AMP framework to derive a new and effective algorithm for K-means clustering. While the general AMP algorithm for matrix factorization is an extension of prior work [9], its specialization to clustering with discrete constraints is non-trivial and leads to a new algorithm with a clear interpretation. This is a novel combination of existing techniques to solve a classic problem. The paper also does a reasonable job of citing related work, though the comparison with other recent AMP-based matrix factorization papers [13, 14] is brief and identified by the authors as an area for future work. A more detailed comparison of the assumptions and technical differences would have better contextualized the contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented in the paper are potentially significant. K-means is a fundamental and widely used algorithm, and the proposed AMP-KM algorithm shows a consistent and demonstrable improvement over K-means++ (a strong baseline) in terms of both the K-means objective and clustering accuracy. The performance gains, especially on the high-dimensional real-world dataset, suggest that the algorithm could be of practical use. The general AMP framework itself is also a useful contribution, offering a flexible and efficient alternative to MCMC and VB methods for a broad class of Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction problems. Researchers in both clustering and signal processing are likely to build upon these ideas.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel derivation of a K-means-type algorithm from first principles using AMP, resulting in a method that empirically outperforms strong baselines on a fundamental machine learning task.
2.  **Technical Contribution:** The paper provides a general AMP framework for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and includes a useful theoretical result (Proposition 1) connecting the algorithm's fixed points to those of ICM.
3.  **Empirical Strength:** The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating clear performance improvements on both synthetic and real-world data.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, and the core ideas are communicated effectively.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Incomplete Theoretical Analysis:** The paper lacks a theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence, relying instead on an assumption. The state evolution analysis, a standard tool for AMP, is mentioned but not provided.
2.  **Heuristic Parameter Estimation:** The adaptive estimation of the noise variance `τ` in the clustering algorithm is not theoretically justified, and its impact on performance is not analyzed.
3.  **Self-Containedness:** The full derivation of the central algorithm is relegated to the supplement, making it difficult to fully assess the technical contribution from the main paper alone.
