This paper presents an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and extends it to K-means clustering. The authors aim to provide a flexible reconstruction method that can incorporate prior knowledge about the factors, and to offer an efficient clustering algorithm, particularly effective in high-dimensional spaces. While the ideas are connected to a strong body of work in AMP, Bayesian matrix factorization, and clustering, the presentation suffers from a plethora of minor flaws and a lack of rigorous justification for several claims, ultimately leading to a negative assessment.

The introduction establishes the motivation well, outlining the benefits of Bayesian approaches and the computational challenges they pose. However, the connection between the general problem setting and the specific contributions feels somewhat disjointed. The claim of extending the [9] algorithm to handle MAP estimation is stated as the first contribution, but the extent of this extension is not immediately clear from the introduction. The formulation of clustering as low-rank matrix reconstruction is standard, but the novelty of the proposed algorithm in handling the "exactly one cluster" constraint isn't convincingly demonstrated.

Section 2 provides a detailed problem setup. The notation is generally clear, but the introduction of the factor *m* in the noise variance (N(0, mτ)) is perplexing. While the authors state it's for proper scaling in the limit, this scaling isn't explicitly utilized or justified throughout the subsequent derivations.  The assumption of the conditional distribution p(A|U0, V0) feels arbitrary; a discussion of the implications of deviating from this assumption would be beneficial.  The restriction to product forms for the priors, p̂U and p̂V, is noted, but the rationale for this choice, beyond computational convenience, is weak.

The core of the paper, Section 4, details the proposed AMP algorithm. The attempt to unify marginalization and MAP estimation through the β parameter is a clever idea, but the derivation of the algorithm itself lacks sufficient detail. Equations (9a)-(9d) are presented without adequate explanation of the intermediate steps. The functions *f* and *G* are defined, but their properties and how they relate to specific prior distributions aren’t thoroughly discussed. The claim that Algorithm 2 is "almost symmetric" is trivial and doesn't add value. The leap from the formulation to Proposition 1 requires considerable justification; the connection between positive semidefiniteness and global optimality needs more rigorous proof, and the claim that it holds in "almost all cases" feels unsubstantiated. The statement about the AMP algorithm avoiding local minima by having no more fixed points than ICM is not logically sound; having fewer fixed points doesn’t *prevent* getting stuck, it simply changes the set of possible stuck points.

The discussion of computational cost is superficial. While stating O(mN) complexity is useful, a more detailed breakdown of the cost associated with calculating *f* and *G* would be more informative. The comparison to variational Bayes is also weak. The assertion that the AMP algorithm “intuitively” performs better because it reflects dependence between U and V is not a strong argument; a more rigorous analysis is needed. The assertion that the state evolution analysis is not included due to space constraints is weak. A key aspect of AMP is the ability to predict performance *via* state evolution, and omitting this analysis significantly diminishes the paper's contribution.

Section 5 presents numerical experiments. The experimental setup is generally sound, but the choice of parameters (τ = 0.1) seems arbitrary and lacks justification. The presentation of results is clear, and the claim that the AMP algorithm outperforms Lloyd’s K-means is supported by the data. However, the comparison to K-means++ is less convincing; the gains are marginal, and the benefits of the proposed algorithm don't seem to justify its complexity. The fact that the AMP algorithm yielded empty clusters in some trials is a significant issue that needs further investigation. The "parameterisation" of τ is ad-hoc, and the justification for it is weak.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   **Lack of Rigor:** Derivations are incomplete, justifications are weak, and claims are often unsubstantiated.
*   **Superficial Analysis:** The computational cost analysis and the comparison to related work lack depth.
*   **Minor Flaws:** Numerous small inconsistencies and imprecisions throughout the paper erode confidence in the authors' attention to detail.
*   **Weak Novelty:** While the combination of AMP and low-rank reconstruction is interesting, the contributions are incremental and don't represent a significant advance in the field.
*   **Unconvincing Experimental Results:** The gains over existing algorithms are marginal, and the limitations of the proposed algorithm are not adequately addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Interesting Idea:** The application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is a potentially promising direction.
*   **Clear Presentation (in parts):** The problem setup and overall structure of the paper are reasonably clear.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed algorithm can outperform existing algorithms in some cases.

**Overall:**

Despite the potentially interesting ideas, the paper is marred by a lack of rigor, superficial analysis, and numerous minor flaws. The claims made are often unsubstantiated, and the contributions don’t appear significant enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. A major revision addressing the points raised above, including a more detailed derivation, a more rigorous analysis of computational cost, and a more thorough experimental evaluation, might be considered for future submission.
