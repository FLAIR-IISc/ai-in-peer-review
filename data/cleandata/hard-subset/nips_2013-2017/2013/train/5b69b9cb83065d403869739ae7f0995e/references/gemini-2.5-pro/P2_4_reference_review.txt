### Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

#### Summary
This paper presents an algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. The core idea is to approximate the posterior distribution of the matrix factors (U, V) to enable efficient inference. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they extend a previous AMP algorithm for this problem [9] to handle not only posterior mean estimation but also Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is elegantly achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter (β) into the posterior, unifying the treatment of marginalization (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞). Second, and more significantly, they apply this framework to the problem of K-means clustering. By formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with a discrete prior on one of the factors, they derive a novel iterative clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3). This algorithm is an intuitive and effective modification of Lloyd's algorithm, incorporating "memory" terms that correct for the bias a data point introduces when calculating its own cluster's center. The authors provide compelling numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world data (ORL faces) showing that their AMP-based clustering algorithms outperform standard methods like Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF) in terms of both clustering accuracy and the final objective value.

#### Quality
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the AMP algorithm appears to be a solid extension of established techniques in the AMP literature. The theoretical analysis, while concise, provides a valuable insight in Proposition 1. This proposition connects the fixed points of the proposed MAP algorithm to those of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm (of which Lloyd's algorithm is an instance), suggesting that the AMP algorithm may have a more favorable optimization landscape and be less prone to getting stuck in poor local minima. This theoretical claim is well-supported by the empirical results.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The comparison against relevant and strong baselines on both synthetic data across a range of difficulties (varying number of clusters `r`) and a standard real-world dataset is well-executed. The performance measures (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, iterations) are appropriate and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed methods.

A minor weakness is the treatment of the noise variance parameter τ in the K-means application (Section 4.3). The theoretical AMP framework is derived under an explicit additive Gaussian noise model, but the standard K-means problem has no such parameter. The authors propose an ad-hoc iterative estimation of τ based on the current reconstruction error. While this appears to work well in practice, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of this step's justification and its sensitivity. For example, does this connect to a particular noise model assumption being implicitly made about the data?

#### Clarity
The paper is, for the most part, clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, and the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is explained effectively in Section 2.2. The derivation of the AMP-based K-means algorithm (culminating in Eq. 18b) is a highlight, as it provides a clear and powerful intuition for why the proposed method should outperform standard K-means: the extra terms act as a form of self-correction, preventing a data point from being too strongly attracted to the cluster it currently belongs to.

The primary point of unclarity is the derivation of the main AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2). The paper states that it is "derived in a way similar to that described in [9], as detailed in the Supplementary Material." While deferring derivations to supplementary material is common, given that this algorithm is the technical core of the paper, the main text would benefit from at least a high-level sketch or intuitive justification of the update equations (9a-9d). This would make the paper more self-contained and the mechanics of the algorithm less of a "black box" for the reader.

#### Originality
The work combines existing ideas in a novel and impactful way. While AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction is not new [8, 9], the extension to handle general priors and MAP estimation via the β-parameterization is a useful contribution.

The main originality lies in the application of this machinery to K-means clustering. While formulating clustering as matrix factorization is a known idea [10, 11], the authors are, to my knowledge, the first to derive a clustering algorithm from the principled, high-dimensional statistical physics perspective of AMP. The resulting algorithm is not simply a re-derivation of an existing method but a genuinely new algorithm (Algorithm 3) with a clear interpretation and demonstrably superior performance. The comparison with related work [13, 14] is brief but correctly identifies the key differences in the underlying assumptions and asymptotic regimes.

#### Significance
The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners. The derived AMP-K-means algorithm offers a practical, computationally efficient, and high-performing alternative to one of the most widely used algorithms in machine learning. Its simple, interpretable update rule (Eq. 18b) could inspire further research into "momentum" or "memory" effects in iterative clustering algorithms.

Furthermore, the general AMP framework (Algorithm 2) provides a flexible and powerful tool for a wide range of problems that can be cast as low-rank matrix factorization with structured priors (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity, discreteness). This significantly broadens the applicability of AMP-based methods in matrix factorization and provides a strong alternative to MCMC or standard Variational Bayes methods, often with benefits in both speed and solution quality, as demonstrated by the experiments. The work advances the state of the art in clustering and provides a valuable algorithmic contribution to the broader field of latent variable modeling.

#### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  **Novel and Effective Clustering Algorithm:** The paper derives a new K-means-type algorithm from first principles (AMP) that empirically outperforms standard, widely-used methods.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are backed by solid experiments on both synthetic and real data, with clear metrics showing improved performance over strong baselines.
3.  **Useful General Framework:** Beyond clustering, the paper provides a general and flexible AMP algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that unifies posterior mean and MAP estimation.
4.  **Theoretical Insight:** Proposition 1 provides a valuable theoretical link between the fixed points of the AMP and ICM algorithms, offering a partial explanation for the improved performance.

#### Arguments against Acceptance:
1.  **Clarity of Derivation:** The core technical derivation of the main algorithm is absent from the main paper, which hinders full understanding and verification without recourse to the supplement.
2.  **Ad-hoc Parameter Estimation:** The handling of the noise parameter τ in the clustering application feels heuristic and could benefit from a more rigorous justification.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and empirically successful approach to a fundamental problem. I recommend acceptance.
