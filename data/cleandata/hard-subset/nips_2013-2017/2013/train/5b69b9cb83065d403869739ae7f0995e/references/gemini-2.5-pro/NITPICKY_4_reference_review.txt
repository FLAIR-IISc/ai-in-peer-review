**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents an algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations. The primary contributions are twofold. First, it extends a previous AMP algorithm for this problem [9], which was limited to posterior mean estimation, to a more general framework that can handle other Bayesian estimation tasks, most notably Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter β into the posterior distribution. Second, the paper demonstrates a novel application of this generalized algorithm to K-means type clustering. By formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one factor matrix has rows constrained to be standard basis vectors, the authors derive a new iterative clustering algorithm. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world data suggest that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard methods like Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and a Variational Bayes baseline in terms of both clustering accuracy and the K-means loss function.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Algorithm for Clustering:** The primary strength of this work is the novel application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering. The derivation leads to a new update rule (Eq. 18b) that is distinct from Lloyd's algorithm. The interpretation of the additional terms—as a correction for the cluster center's bias and a preference for smaller clusters—is insightful and provides a plausible intuition for the algorithm's improved performance.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The numerical experiments demonstrate a clear and significant improvement over established baselines. On synthetic data, the proposed AMP-KM and AMP-MA algorithms consistently achieve lower K-means loss and higher accuracy, respectively, compared to K-means, K-means++, and VBMF. The results on the ORL face database further suggest that these advantages translate to real-world, high-dimensional data.

3.  **Generalization of Existing Work:** The extension of the AMP algorithm from [9] to handle MAP estimation via the β → ∞ limit is a sound and useful generalization, unifying the treatment of different Bayesian estimation problems within the same framework.

**Weaknesses**

The paper, while presenting promising ideas and results, suffers from a substantial number of issues related to clarity, methodological rigor, and presentation that detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The key theoretical tool that underpins the performance guarantees and predictive power of AMP algorithms is the state evolution analysis. The authors mention this ("we can present the state evolution... but we do not discuss... due to the limited space"), but its omission is a major weakness. Without this analysis, the proposed algorithm appears as a complex heuristic, and its excellent performance remains an empirical observation lacking the rigorous theoretical support expected for AMP-based work at NIPS. The precise assumptions on the data model (e.g., on U0, V0) required for the AMP approximation to be accurate are also not clearly articulated.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper's clarity is hampered by several factors:
    *   **Omitted Derivations:** Key equations are presented without derivation. For instance, the transition from the general AMP update (17c) to the interpretable K-means update (18b) is non-trivial, yet the steps are completely omitted, making it difficult to verify and fully understand.
    *   **Poor Algorithm Presentation:** Algorithms are described as a set of coupled equations within the main text. Standard pseudocode blocks would be far clearer and would explicitly state inputs, initializations, and the iterative loop structure. The current format is confusing.
    *   **Terse Explanations:** Critical steps in the derivation are justified with overly terse statements. For example, the claim that `G∞(b,Λ; p̂v)` is "O almost everywhere" for the discrete prior is central to simplifying the algorithm to Algorithm 3, but the reasoning is not adequately explained.
    *   **Ambiguous Terminology:** In Section 2.2, the description of the generative model for clustering is slightly imprecise. Proposition 1 is presented as a key property, but it merely states that AMP fixed points are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm, which is more of a sanity check than a guarantee of superiority. The claim that this "may help" avoid bad local minima is speculative.

3.  **Methodological Details:**
    *   **Convergence:** The authors acknowledge that convergence is not guaranteed. The ad-hoc stopping criterion for AMP-KM (`V t = V t−1` or `V t = V t−2`) suggests the presence of 2-cycles, which is a practical issue that is noted but not addressed.
    *   **Parameter Estimation:** The noise parameter `τ` is critical to the algorithm, but it is not part of the original K-means problem. The paper proposes an ad-hoc iterative scheme for its estimation without justification or analysis of its effect on convergence and solution quality.
    *   **Bias Towards Balanced Clusters:** The update rule in (18b) includes a term `− m/ntl` that implicitly penalizes larger clusters. While this may be beneficial in some cases, it could also be a significant drawback for datasets with intrinsically unbalanced clusters. This potential weakness is not discussed or evaluated.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** There are numerous minor formatting and typographical errors. Reference formatting is inconsistent (e.g., trailing commas). In reference [20], an extraneous "9" appears at the end of the URL line. The figures are referenced in the text but were not included in the manuscript provided for review. These issues collectively suggest a lack of careful proofreading.

**Recommendation**

While the paper introduces a novel and empirically effective algorithm for clustering, its significant weaknesses in theoretical justification, clarity, and methodological detail prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form. The omission of the state evolution analysis is particularly critical for a paper leveraging the AMP framework. The numerous small flaws in presentation and explanation collectively create a barrier to understanding and verification. Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. The authors have a strong foundation for a good paper, but it requires a major revision to address the issues of rigor and clarity before it meets the high standards of NIPS.
