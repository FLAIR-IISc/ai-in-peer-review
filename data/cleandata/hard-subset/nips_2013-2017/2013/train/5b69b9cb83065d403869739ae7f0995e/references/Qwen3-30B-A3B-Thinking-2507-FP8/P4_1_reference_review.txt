

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work to handle both marginalization and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation problems. The authors present a unified framework that generalizes the IterFac algorithm for rank-one matrices and the AMP approach for general-rank matrices. The paper then applies this framework to K-means clustering, demonstrating that their method directly incorporates the constraint that each datum belongs to exactly one cluster, unlike previous approaches. The paper compares the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm with standard methods like Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes matrix factorization, showing improved performance on both synthetic and real datasets.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The proposed AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction is rigorously derived, with clear theoretical justification through the use of a one-parameter extension of the posterior distribution. The authors provide a convergence analysis (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm correspond to fixed points of the ICM algorithm, with additional guarantees of optimality under certain conditions. The numerical experiments are comprehensive, using both synthetic data with controlled parameters and the ORL face database for real-world validation. The paper carefully evaluates multiple metrics (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, iteration count) across 500 synthetic instances and 50 trials on real data, with proper statistical reporting (averages and standard deviations). The authors acknowledge limitations, such as the need for appropriate initialization to avoid empty clusters, and provide practical solutions.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setting is clearly defined in Section 2, with precise mathematical formulations of both the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem and the connection to clustering. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented in a clear, step-by-step manner, with detailed explanations of the functions f and G. The relationship between the AMP algorithm and existing methods (variational Bayes matrix factorization, ICM, Lloyd's algorithm) is well-explained, and the paper effectively distinguishes itself from related work (e.g., Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012; Krzakala et al., 2013; Parker et al., 2013). The supplementary material provides additional details on the AMP derivation, which enhances the paper's reproducibility.

## Novelty
The paper makes a clear contribution to the field. The extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP problems in low-rank matrix reconstruction is novel, as previous AMP-based methods for matrix reconstruction (e.g., Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012) only handled posterior mean estimation. The application of this framework to clustering is particularly novel, as it directly addresses the constraint that each datum belongs to exactly one cluster, which previous low-rank matrix reconstruction approaches to clustering (e.g., Xu et al., 2003; Ding et al., 2010) did not properly handle. The paper also distinguishes itself by fixing the rank r rather than the ratio r/m in the asymptotic analysis, which is a meaningful technical difference from recent related work.

## Significance
The significance of this work is demonstrated through compelling experimental results. The AMP-based clustering algorithm consistently outperforms Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ in terms of normalized K-means loss and accuracy on both synthetic and real datasets, particularly for higher-dimensional data. The computational efficiency of the AMP algorithm (O(mN) per iteration) is advantageous for large-scale problems, and the paper demonstrates faster convergence than variational Bayes methods. The unified framework for both marginalization and MAP estimation provides a flexible tool for practitioners dealing with different types of low-rank matrix reconstruction problems.

## Strengths
- Clear theoretical contribution extending AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP problems
- Novel application of low-rank matrix reconstruction to clustering with proper handling of cluster assignment constraints
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with both synthetic and real data
- Well-organized and clearly written presentation
- Strong theoretical justification (Proposition 1) showing optimality properties

## Weaknesses
- The connection to the state evolution framework (a key theoretical property of AMP algorithms) is mentioned but not developed, which is a significant omission for a NIPS audience
- The comparison with the most recent related work (e.g., Parker et al., 2013 on bilinear generalized AMP) could be more detailed
- The paper could provide more intuition on why the AMP approach outperforms variational Bayes beyond the Bethe approximation argument

## Recommendation
The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The technical soundness is excellent, the presentation is clear, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The novelty of the approach is well-justified, and the significance is demonstrated through both theoretical insights and empirical results. While the omission of a detailed state evolution analysis is a minor weakness, it does not undermine the paper's strong technical contributions.

Pro: Clear theoretical extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP problems, strong experimental validation, novel application to clustering with proper handling of cluster assignment constraints, well-written and organized.

Con: Limited discussion of state evolution framework, could provide more detailed comparison with very recent related work.

The paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission and would be a valuable contribution to the field.
