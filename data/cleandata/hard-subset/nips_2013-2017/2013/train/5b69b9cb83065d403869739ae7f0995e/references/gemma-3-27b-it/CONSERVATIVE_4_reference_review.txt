This paper presents an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The core idea is to extend existing AMP formulations to handle MAP estimation and leverage the connection between low-rank reconstruction and clustering. While the approach builds upon established work in AMP and matrix factorization, its novelty lies in the extension to MAP estimation within the AMP framework and the application to a constrained clustering problem.

The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical derivations appear sound, though a deeper dive would be needed to fully verify all claims. The authors clearly articulate the problem setting and the motivation for using AMP. The connections to related work, including variational Bayes and existing AMP algorithms, are also reasonably well-established. However, the presentation occasionally feels dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations of the algorithm’s steps. 

My primary concern centers on the *empirical* validation. The results presented, while demonstrating improvement over Lloyd’s K-means, feel insufficient to justify acceptance to NIPS. The numerical experiments, even with the inclusion of both artificial and real datasets, lack a comprehensive comparison against stronger, established baselines. For example, the comparison against the variational Bayes method (VBMF-MA) is present, but a more thorough evaluation against state-of-the-art clustering algorithms beyond K-means++ (e.g., spectral clustering, mini-batch k-means) and more recent matrix factorization techniques would be critical. The claims of superior performance, particularly in the real-data experiment, require more robust support. The fact that the AMP algorithm occasionally fails and yields a trivial solution (one cluster containing all data) is concerning and warrants further investigation and discussion. 

Furthermore, the analysis regarding the theoretical properties (e.g., Proposition 1) is presented without substantial empirical corroboration. While theoretical guarantees are valuable, their practical relevance needs to be demonstrated through experimentation. The discussion of the state evolution, though mentioned, is omitted, which hinders a complete understanding of the algorithm’s theoretical underpinnings. The justification for the scaling factor 'm' in the noise variance also feels somewhat hand-waved and could benefit from a more rigorous explanation.

The extension of the AMP algorithm to handle MAP estimation is a contribution, but its significance is diminished without a more compelling demonstration of its practical advantages over existing MAP estimation techniques. The claim that the proposed algorithm addresses a limitation of previous AMP methods (handling non-independent components) is stated but not convincingly demonstrated.

**Strengths:**

*   Well-motivated problem and clear presentation of the approach.
*   Extension of AMP to MAP estimation is a potential contribution.
*   Theoretical analysis (Proposition 1) provides some guarantees about the algorithm’s behavior.
*   Demonstrated improvement over Lloyd’s K-means in the provided experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficient empirical validation against strong baselines.
*   Limited explanation of the theoretical results and omitted state evolution analysis.
*   Occasional algorithm failures and lack of detailed investigation into these cases.
*   The significance of the extension to MAP estimation is not fully established.
*   Justification of the noise variance scaling is unclear.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel application of AMP to a relevant problem.
*   The theoretical analysis offers some insights into the algorithm’s properties.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The empirical evaluation is limited and does not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   The theoretical claims require stronger empirical support.
*   The lack of comparison to more state-of-the-art baselines weakens the paper’s impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the ideas presented are interesting and the technical work appears solid, the lack of thorough empirical validation and the absence of a compelling demonstration of practical significance prevent me from recommending acceptance to NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental evaluation and provide more convincing evidence that their approach offers a substantial improvement over existing techniques before this work would be suitable for this venue. I encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit to a future conference.
