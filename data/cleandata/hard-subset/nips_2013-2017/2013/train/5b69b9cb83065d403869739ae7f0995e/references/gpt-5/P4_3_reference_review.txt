Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction when the target matrix A0 = U0V0^T has factors with known structure (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity), and proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm that accommodates general priors and both posterior-mean (marginalization) and MAP estimation via a temperature parameter β. The algorithm generalizes prior AMP approaches for bilinear models: it extends IterFac (rank-1) and the general-rank AMP of Matsushita & Tanaka (2012) by enabling MAP estimation (β→∞) in addition to estimation of posterior means (β=1), and by allowing non-separable intra-row priors (the rows are i.i.d., but components within a row can be dependent). The paper then instantiates the framework to clustering, showing that K-means arises as a special case with a one-hot prior on the rows of V and a uniform prior on U. This yields a new AMP-based K-means algorithm that modifies the assignment step with Onsager-like corrections that depend on current assignments and cluster sizes, and an AMP-based “maximum accuracy” clustering method (β=1) using the true priors when available.

The work is positioned relative to variational Bayes (VB) matrix factorization (Lim & Teh 2007; Raiko et al. 2007), AMP for compressed sensing (Donoho et al. 2009; Rangan 2011), rank-1 bilinear AMP (Rangan & Fletcher 2012), and later bilinear generalized AMP (BiG-AMP; Parker et al. 2013) and related dictionary learning work (Krzakala et al. 2013). Compared to VB/ICM, the proposed AMP includes Onsager corrections that account for dependencies in the Bethe approximation and, in principle, allow state evolution analysis. In clustering, the paper contrasts with prior low-rank/NMF-based clustering that does not directly enforce the one-hot constraint.

Strengths
- Methodological contribution: A unified β-parametrized AMP for bilinear matrix factorization that covers posterior-mean and MAP estimation, with explicit update equations and discussion of f and G operators for general priors.
- K-means instantiation: A concrete algorithm (Algorithm 3) that yields an interpretable modification to Lloyd’s assignments (leave-one-out style correction and balancing by current cluster sizes).
- Theoretical property: Proposition 1 gives a fixed-point optimality statement (global optimality in each block given the other) under PSD conditions, clarifying the relation to ICM.
- Empirical evidence: On synthetic data, AMP-KM reduces K-means loss versus Lloyd/K-means++ as dimension/clusters grow; AMP-MA improves accuracy versus VBMF-MA. On ORL, AMP-KM often outperforms k-means++ in both loss and accuracy with few iterations.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope: Only one synthetic setting (Gaussian centers, equal cluster priors, fixed noise) and one real dataset. No comparisons to EM for GMMs, spherical K-means, spectral clustering, or Hartigan-Wong variants, which are common K-means baselines. No wall-clock runtime comparisons or scalability experiments.
- Convergence and robustness: AMP convergence is assumed; damping/annealing not discussed. The K-means variant sometimes collapses to a single cluster; handling of empty clusters (nt_l=0 in denominators) is not specified. Oscillations are empirically addressed by ad hoc termination.
- Theory omissions: State evolution is not provided, and the PSD conditions in Proposition 1 are asserted to hold “in almost all cases” without precise assumptions. MAP with improper priors is used; conditions for posterior propriety could be clarified.
- Practicality for general priors: For β<∞, f and G may require r̂-dimensional integration; for β→∞, a nontrivial optimization and Hessian inversion per row are needed. Guidance on choices, numerical stability, and computational costs is limited.

Quality
- The algorithmic derivation is consistent with AMP literature and clearly states the β-parameterization, fixed-point interpretation, and roles of f and G. Proposition 1 is plausible; proof relegated to the supplement. Practical considerations (τ estimation, oscillations) are addressed heuristically. More rigorous convergence analysis, state evolution, and safeguards (e.g., damping) would strengthen soundness. Experimental methodology is clear, with metrics and stopping criteria, but comparative breadth and robustness analyses are limited.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with explicit update equations and intuitive explanations for the K-means corrections. Notation is dense; some symbols (e.g., use of mτ in noise scaling, handling of empty clusters) could be clarified. Including pseudocode boxes and explicit damping/regularization options would help practitioners. References are appropriate, though some clustering baselines are missing.

Originality
- Extending bilinear AMP from posterior-mean to MAP via a β-scheme in the general-rank case appears novel relative to cited work. The AMP-based K-means with one-hot constraints and Onsager correction is a new algorithmic variant. The allowance for intra-row dependent priors is a modest modeling extension.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could impact matrix factorization with structured priors and large-scale clustering, offering improved minima for K-means-like objectives with low iteration counts. Current evidence is promising but limited; broader evaluations and theoretical guarantees would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled extension of AMP to MAP estimation in bilinear models.
- Novel K-means algorithm with interpretable corrections and improved losses in high dimensions.
- Low per-iteration complexity and few iterations empirically.
- Useful theoretical fixed-point property (Proposition 1).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited experiments (datasets, baselines, ablations, runtimes) and occasional failure modes (cluster collapse).
- Missing state evolution and convergence safeguards; conditions for PSD terms not fully specified.
- Practical computation of f/G for general priors underexplored.
- Handling of empty clusters and sensitivity to τ estimation and initialization not fully analyzed.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you provide damping or stepsize strategies and report their effect on convergence and stability (especially for the K-means variant)?
2. How do you handle empty clusters (ntl=0) in Algorithm 3? Do you reseed or regularize denominators?
3. Can you compare against Hartigan-Wong K-means, EM for spherical GMMs, and spectral clustering on synthetic and ORL datasets, and report wall-clock times?
4. What are the precise conditions under which ∑i S∞i and ∑j T∞j are PSD at fixed points? Any counterexamples?
5. Can you include a brief state-evolution description for a basic Gaussian-prior setting?
6. How sensitive are results to τ estimation, r̂ mis-specification, and initialization? Any annealing from β=1 to β→∞?

Minor suggestions
- Add pseudocode and implementation details (damping, stopping, τ update, numerical stability).
- Discuss computational costs of f/G under common priors and provide examples.
- Clarify the impact of fixing rank r (vs r/m) on the asymptotics and AMP validity.

Reproducibility
- Algorithms are specified, datasets are public, and metrics/stopping criteria are given. Code availability and more implementation details would further aid reproducibility.
