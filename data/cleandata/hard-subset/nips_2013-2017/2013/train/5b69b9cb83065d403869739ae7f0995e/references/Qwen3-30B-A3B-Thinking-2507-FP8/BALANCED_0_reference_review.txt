

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work for rank-one cases [8, 9]. The authors develop a unified framework that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation problems, then apply it to K-means clustering by recognizing clustering as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction. While previous work has applied low-rank matrix reconstruction to clustering [10, 11], this paper claims to be the first that directly handles the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank framework. The paper contrasts with related AMP work [13, 14] by fixing the rank r rather than the rank ratio r/m in the asymptotic limit.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper successfully extends AMP to general-rank matrix reconstruction, which is a significant theoretical advance. The derivation of Algorithm 2 for both marginalization and MAP problems in a unified framework is well-executed.

2. **Practical Application**: The application to clustering is well-motivated and demonstrates clear practical value. The AMP-based clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3) outperforms Lloyd's K-means in both synthetic and real-world experiments, particularly for high-dimensional data.

3. **Theoretical Insights**: Proposition 1 provides valuable theoretical insight by showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm, suggesting potential advantages in avoiding bad local minima.

4. **Experimental Validation**: The numerical experiments are comprehensive, using both synthetic data (with 500 problem instances) and real data (ORL face database), with multiple performance metrics (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, iteration count).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis**: The paper assumes convergence of Algorithm 2 without providing conditions for convergence or discussing cases where convergence might fail. While the authors mention "we assume convergence," this is a significant limitation for a theoretical contribution.

2. **Computational Complexity Comparison**: The paper states the computational cost is O(mN) per iteration but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with alternative methods (e.g., variational Bayes, ICM) in terms of actual runtime or number of operations. This makes it difficult to assess the practical computational advantage.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of β parameter (which controls the transition between marginalization and MAP estimation) or how to select it in practice.

4. **Comparison with Related Work**: While the paper claims to be the first to handle the "exactly one cluster" constraint directly, it doesn't provide a detailed comparison with how other methods (like variational Bayes) handle this constraint, making it difficult to fully assess the novelty claim.

5. **Motivation for Scaling**: The introduction of mτ in the noise variance (for proper scaling in the limit m,N→∞) lacks sufficient explanation. Why is this scaling necessary, and how does it affect the practical implementation?

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand Theoretical Analysis**: Add a section discussing convergence conditions for Algorithm 2, including examples where convergence might fail and potential remedies.

2. **Include Computational Complexity Comparison**: Add a table comparing the computational complexity (in terms of floating-point operations) of the proposed AMP algorithm with variational Bayes and ICM methods for different problem sizes.

3. **Clarify Parameter Selection**: Discuss how to choose β in practice for different applications (e.g., marginalization vs. MAP), and provide experimental results showing sensitivity to β.

4. **Strengthen Comparison with Related Work**: Explicitly compare the proposed clustering algorithm with other low-rank matrix reconstruction approaches to clustering, detailing how the "exactly one cluster" constraint is handled in each method.

5. **Improve Explanation of Scaling**: Provide a clearer explanation for the mτ scaling in the noise variance, including how it affects the practical implementation of the algorithm.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a strong theoretical foundation and promising experimental results. The extension of AMP to general-rank matrix reconstruction is a meaningful contribution to the field. The application to clustering demonstrates practical utility, and the results show clear improvements over baselines.

The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, with sound theoretical development and comprehensive experiments. The clarity is generally good, though some technical sections might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with AMP. The originality is strong, with clear differentiation from prior work. The significance is demonstrated by the practical improvements in clustering performance, particularly for high-dimensional data.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The theoretical framework is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. The weaknesses identified are important but do not undermine the core contributions. With the suggested improvements (particularly regarding convergence analysis and computational complexity comparison), this paper would be even stronger.

The paper is well-positioned to advance the state of the art in Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
