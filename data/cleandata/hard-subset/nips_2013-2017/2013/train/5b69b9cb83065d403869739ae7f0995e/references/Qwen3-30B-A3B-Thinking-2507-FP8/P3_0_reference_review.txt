

# Peer Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that can handle both marginalization (posterior mean) and MAP estimation problems. The authors extend previous AMP work for low-rank matrices to incorporate a flexible parameter β that unifies these two estimation approaches. They then apply this framework to K-means clustering, deriving a novel algorithm that directly handles the constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster. The paper demonstrates through experiments that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes methods on both synthetic and real datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a well-motivated extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction. The introduction of the parameter β to unify marginalization and MAP problems is elegant and clearly explained. The theoretical connection between their AMP algorithm and the ICM algorithm (Proposition 1) is particularly insightful, showing that AMP fixed points are also ICM fixed points, which helps explain AMP's ability to avoid poor local minima.

2. **Algorithmic Innovation**: The proposed AMP algorithm for clustering (Algorithm 3) is novel in directly handling the cluster assignment constraint, which previous low-rank reconstruction approaches for clustering did not properly address. The paper correctly identifies this as a key difference from prior work [10, 11].

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The algorithm has a low computational cost of O(mN) per iteration, which is linear in the matrix size. This is significantly more efficient than variational Bayes approaches, which typically require O(mNr̂) operations.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments are comprehensive, including both synthetic data (with varying rank r) and real-world face clustering. The results consistently show that the AMP-based clustering outperforms Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes methods in terms of both clustering loss and accuracy, especially for high-dimensional data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Prior Work**: While the paper claims novelty in handling the cluster assignment constraint, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from related work [9, 14]. The key difference (fixing r rather than r/m in the limit) is mentioned but not adequately explained or justified. A more thorough comparison with [9] would strengthen the novelty claim.

2. **Theoretical Gaps**: The paper mentions state evolution but omits it due to space constraints. While this is understandable, the absence of theoretical performance guarantees (beyond Proposition 1) weakens the theoretical contribution. The convergence analysis is also limited to an "assumption of convergence" without rigorous justification.

3. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper introduces a parameter τ in the clustering algorithm that doesn't exist in the standard K-means problem. While they propose a heuristic for estimating τ, the paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the algorithm is to this parameter or how to select it optimally. This could be a practical concern for implementation.

4. **Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper compares with Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes, but doesn't compare with more recent clustering methods that might be relevant to the AMP framework, such as those based on convex optimization or deep learning.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by extending AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with a unified framework for different estimation problems and applying it to clustering. The algorithmic innovation is significant, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate practical advantages over existing approaches.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and technically sound. The theoretical foundations are strong, though they could be further strengthened by addressing the theoretical gaps noted above. The empirical results are comprehensive and support the paper's claims.

## Recommendation

**Accept**. The paper presents a novel and well-executed extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with clear applications to clustering. The method is theoretically grounded, computationally efficient, and empirically validated on both synthetic and real data. While there are some theoretical gaps and opportunities for better differentiation from prior work, these do not undermine the paper's overall contribution. The results are significant for the clustering community, particularly for high-dimensional data where traditional methods struggle.

The key strength is the unified AMP framework that handles both marginalization and MAP problems, with the clustering application being a particularly compelling demonstration of its utility. The connection between AMP and ICM (Proposition 1) provides valuable theoretical insight into why AMP might outperform traditional methods. For NIPS, this represents a meaningful advancement in scalable Bayesian inference for matrix problems with practical applications in clustering.

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it combines theoretical insights with practical applications, addresses a significant problem in machine learning (clustering), and demonstrates clear advantages over existing methods. The computational efficiency of the proposed algorithm makes it particularly relevant for modern large-scale data analysis.
