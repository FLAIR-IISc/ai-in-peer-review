Summary
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) framework for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that flexibly incorporates structure on the factors through arbitrary (possibly improper) priors. The key technical device is a “temperature” parameter β that interpolates between posterior inference (β=1) and MAP (β→∞), yielding a single algorithm (Algorithm 2) that can produce posterior means, marginal MAP estimates, or MAP estimates depending on β. The algorithm includes Onsager-type corrections missing from standard variational Bayes (VB), and the authors show that removing these terms recovers VB/ICM updates.

A second contribution instantiates the framework for clustering by taking a discrete prior over one-hot assignments, yielding a novel AMP-based K-means procedure (Algorithm 3) that enforces the hard assignment constraint directly. The assignment step includes principled corrections that down-weight the current cluster (to debias the center) and favor smaller clusters, both derived rather than heuristically added. On synthetic high-dimensional data and the ORL faces dataset, the AMP-based K-means reduces K-means loss (and often improves accuracy) versus Lloyd and K-means++ and converges in few iterations. An AMP-based maximum-accuracy (marginal MAP) clustering variant also shows strong performance on synthetic data.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AMP for sparse linear models [Donoho et al., Rangan] and prior bilinear AMP/“matrix AMP” developments: rank-one AMP (IterFac), an AMP for general rank that focused on posterior means (Matsushita & Tanaka 2012), and recent bilinear/generalized AMP for dictionary learning and bilinear inference (e.g., Krzakala et al. 2013; Parker, Schniter, Cevher 2013). Compared to these, the paper (i) unifies posterior mean, marginal MAP, and MAP via β; (ii) allows arbitrary within-row dependencies in the priors; and (iii) fixes rank r when taking the large-system limit used in derivation. The clustering formulation is classical (K-means as constrained matrix factorization), but, to my knowledge, this is the first AMP instantiation that enforces one-hot assignments exactly and yields derived bias/size corrections in the assignment step.

Strengths
- Novelty and scope: A clean, general AMP derivation for bilinear low-rank models that covers MAP (β→∞) and marginal MAP (β=1) in one framework is a meaningful conceptual step. The hard-constraint clustering variant is particularly elegant.
- Technical clarity: The algorithmic equations are explicit, with clear definitions of f and G as mean/covariance operators under tilted priors; the β→∞ limit is well handled via an implicit differentiation identity for G∞. The connection to VB/ICM through removing Onsager terms is instructive.
- Interpretability: The K-means assignment rule’s extra terms have intuitive interpretations (self-bias correction and size regularization), lending credibility to the improved optimization behavior.
- Empirical promise: On synthetic high-d data, AMP-KM attains strictly lower K-means loss than Lloyd/K-means++ and AMP-MA attains the best assignment accuracy with fast convergence. On ORL, AMP-KM improves K-means loss and accuracy in most trials with comparable iteration counts.

Weaknesses and questions
- Theory is underdeveloped in the paper: The authors assert that state evolution (SE) can be given but omit it for space, and convergence is assumed. Given the centrality of SE to AMP, even a concise SE statement with a sanity-check experiment would help. What is the precise large-system regime (m,N→∞, fixed r) under which the Onsager terms are correct? Are there known failure modes when r is not negligible?
- Comparisons are narrow: For clustering, competing baselines are mainly Lloyd/K-means++. Given the probabilistic framing, EM for spherical GMMs (with equal covariance), spectral clustering, and NMF-based clustering would be natural and informative baselines. For bilinear inference, comparison to BiG-AMP/P-BiG-AMP would help clarify the algorithmic/empirical differences.
- Use of true priors in synthetic maximum-accuracy experiments inflates performance and obscures robustness. How sensitive is AMP-MA to prior mismatch (e.g., heavy-tailed centers, nonuniform cluster sizes)?
- Practicalities: The paper notes oscillations and sometimes collapse to a single cluster on ORL without careful seeding. Are damping, adaptive step sizes, or β-annealing helpful? The τ estimation heuristic is reasonable; please quantify sensitivity to τ misestimation.
- Runtime reporting is absent: Complexity is O(mN) per iteration (with hidden r̂), but wall-clock times vs. baselines would build confidence in practical efficiency.
- Missing ablations: Since the VB/AMP connection is emphasized, an ablation removing the Onsager corrections in Algorithm 3 would isolate their contribution to optimization quality.

Quality
The derivation appears technically sound and consistent with AMP lore; the fixed-point proposition gives a useful blockwise optimality guarantee under PSD assumptions. However, the lack of SE or convergence diagnostics in the main paper is a gap. Experiments support the claims but are limited in breadth; still, the improvements on high-d synthetic data and ORL are encouraging.

Clarity
Overall well written and organized. The algorithmic specification is clear. Minor suggestions: (i) explicitly state computational cost as O(mNr̂) to avoid confusion; (ii) briefly summarize the SE setting and assumptions; (iii) clarify when Λ is guaranteed PSD in the β→∞ limit; (iv) move the τ-estimation discussion earlier in the clustering section and report the final τ values used.

Originality
High. The β-unified AMP for bilinear models that handles MAP (including hard constraints via priors) is, to my knowledge, new relative to prior general-rank AMP papers focused on MMSE/posterior means. The resulting AMP-K-means with principled assignment corrections is also novel.

Significance
Moderate to high. If adopted, the framework could influence both low-rank Bayesian inference with structured priors and practical clustering in high dimensions. The hard-constraint AMP view is likely to inspire extensions (e.g., semi-supervised labels, missing data, non-Gaussian noise).

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually bold unification (β) and extension of bilinear AMP to MAP/hard constraints.
- Simple, efficient algorithms with interpretable corrections.
- Empirical gains on synthetic and a real dataset; fast convergence.

Cons against acceptance
- Missing state evolution and limited theoretical guarantees in the paper.
- Narrow empirical baselines; sensitivity/robustness not deeply probed.
- Some instability in practice (oscillations, cluster collapse) without careful initialization.

Recommendation
Accept (borderline, novelty-driven). This paper introduces a significant and elegant extension of AMP with a compelling application to clustering; the shortcomings are mainly breadth of validation and omitted theory details, which are addressable in the camera-ready.

Confidence
Moderate.
