

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering problems. The authors extend previous AMP work to handle MAP estimation in the general-rank case, which they claim is novel, and apply their algorithm to K-means clustering.

**Quality Issues:**

The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine its quality. The most significant issue is the noise model: the authors define W with components Wi,j ~ N(0, mτ), where the noise variance scales with m. This is problematic because as m increases, the noise variance increases, making the problem harder, not easier. The justification provided ("to allow a proper scaling in the limit where m and N go to infinity") is incorrect; typically, noise variance would scale with 1/m to maintain a constant signal-to-noise ratio as the problem size grows.

In Equation (5), the CMAP objective function lacks proper scaling for the log prior terms. The data term is scaled by 1/(mτ), but the log prior terms are not scaled by any factor related to problem size, creating an imbalance that could affect optimization behavior.

The paper contains multiple notational inconsistencies. In Section 2.2, the clustering formulation uses "r" for the true number of clusters but then switches to "r̂" in Equation (6) without clear explanation. The notation for the matrix A is also inconsistent: it's defined as m×N, but later treated as having rows aj ∈ R^m when they should be in R^N.

In Proposition 1, the authors claim U∞ is a global minimum of CMAP(U, V∞), but CMAP(U, V∞) is not convex in U, so this claim is unsupported without additional assumptions.

**Clarity Issues:**

The paper suffers from poor notation and ambiguous expressions. In Algorithm 2, the update for Btu contains "U^{t-1} N∑j=1 T^tj" which is ambiguous - is this matrix multiplication or element-wise operation? The paper should clarify this.

Equation (10) contains a typo: "f(b,Λ; p̂) := ∫ uq̂(u; b,Λ, p̂)du" should be "f(b,Λ; p̂) := ∫ u q̂(u; b,Λ, p̂) du" to indicate the integral is with respect to u.

In Algorithm 3, the expression "Λtu = mτ (V^t)^T V^t" is dimensionally inconsistent. (V^t)^T V^t is N×N, but Λtu should be r̂×r̂.

**Experimental Issues:**

The numerical experiments are poorly designed. The paper compares algorithms using different initialization methods: K-means++ uses sophisticated initialization, while other methods use random initialization. This creates an unfair comparison, as K-means++ is known to perform better with good initialization.

The paper claims the AMP algorithm for K-means clustering "achieves the smallest K-means loss," but provides no statistical significance tests for these results. The improvements reported (0.412 vs. 0.400 for K-means loss) are marginal and may not be statistically significant.

**Originality and Significance:**

While the extension of AMP to handle MAP estimation for general-rank matrices appears novel, the application to clustering is not particularly innovative. The paper cites [10, 11] as previous work on low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering, but fails to clearly distinguish how their approach differs from these existing methods.

The claimed advantage of "directly dealing with the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster" is misleading - the standard K-means algorithm already enforces this constraint.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper has a novel algorithmic contribution but is undermined by multiple technical errors, notational inconsistencies, and poorly designed experiments. The noise model scaling is fundamentally flawed, which casts doubt on the entire theoretical foundation. The experimental results do not sufficiently demonstrate the claimed advantages over existing methods.

The paper's claims about performance improvements are not strongly supported, and the numerical experiments are not properly designed for fair comparison. The technical errors in the mathematical formulation are too significant to overlook for a NIPS paper, which requires rigorous mathematical treatment.

**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and has some novel elements, the technical errors and poor experimental design significantly undermine its scientific contribution. The noise model scaling issue alone is sufficient to warrant rejection, as it fundamentally misrepresents the problem setting. The paper would require substantial revision to address these critical issues before it could be considered for publication at NIPS.
