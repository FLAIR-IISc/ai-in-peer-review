This paper presents two main contributions. First, it develops a general algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. This framework is flexible, allowing for various structural priors on the factor matrices, and unifies the problems of computing posterior marginals (marginalization) and finding the joint maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate via an inverse temperature parameter. Second, it applies this framework to K-means clustering by formulating it as a MAP estimation problem with a one-hot-vector constraint on one of the factor matrices. This leads to a novel, efficient clustering algorithm (AMP-KM) that modifies the standard Lloyd's algorithm assignment step. The paper provides experimental results on synthetic and real-world data showing that the proposed clustering algorithms outperform strong baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Framework:** The paper successfully brings the powerful AMP framework, originally from statistical physics and compressed sensing, to the general problem of low-rank matrix factorization with structured priors. This is a significant contribution. Unlike previous AMP approaches for this problem which were limited to posterior mean estimation, this work provides a unified treatment for both marginalization and MAP estimation. This framework has the potential to be applied to a wide range of problems beyond clustering, such as non-negative matrix factorization or sparse PCA, by simply plugging in the appropriate prior functions.

2.  **Insightful Application to Clustering:** The application to K-means clustering is particularly compelling. By deriving the algorithm from first principles within the AMP framework, the authors provide a new perspective on the classic K-means problem. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3 / eq. 18) is a simple but non-trivial modification of Lloyd's algorithm. The interpretation of the additional terms—as a correction for the bias a data point exerts on its own cluster center and a mechanism to favor smaller clusters—is intuitive and explains why the algorithm might be better at escaping the poor local minima that plague Lloyd's algorithm.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The proposed methods (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) are compared against appropriate and widely used baselines. On synthetic data, the results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the AMP-based methods, with AMP-KM achieving a lower K-means loss and AMP-MA achieving higher clustering accuracy, especially in the more challenging high-rank regime. The fast convergence of the AMP algorithms is also a notable practical advantage. The results on the ORL faces dataset confirm that these benefits translate to real-world problems.

4.  **Sound Theoretical Connection:** The paper establishes a clear connection between the proposed AMP algorithm and the standard Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm (of which Lloyd's algorithm is an instance). Proposition 1, which states that any fixed point of the AMP-MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of ICM, is an important result. It formally supports the intuition that AMP is not just another iterative heuristic but a principled method that explores the solution space differently and potentially more effectively than ICM, likely by having fewer "bad" fixed points.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Lack of Derivation Details:** The derivation of the AMP algorithm, which is central to the paper's contribution, is entirely deferred to the supplementary material. While this is common due to page limits, the paper would be substantially stronger and more self-contained if it included a brief, high-level sketch of the derivation. Specifically, explaining how the crucial "Onsager reaction terms" (the last terms in (9a) and (9c)) arise from the cavity method would provide readers with a better intuition for why AMP differs from and improves upon variational methods.

2.  **Clarity on Underlying Assumptions:** The AMP framework's theoretical guarantees typically rely on assumptions of large systems with random, unstructured matrices (e.g., i.i.d. entries). The paper should briefly discuss these assumptions and comment on their validity in the context of the experiments, particularly for the structured ORL face dataset. While AMP is often empirically successful beyond its proven theoretical regime, acknowledging this gap would make the paper more rigorous.

3.  **Omission of State Evolution:** A key advantage of AMP is the existence of "state evolution" equations, which can precisely predict the algorithm's performance (e.g., MSE) in the large-system limit. The authors mention this but omit it for space. This is a missed opportunity. Including even a brief description of the state evolution equations and the insights they could provide (e.g., predicting phase transitions in reconstruction accuracy) would have significantly highlighted the power of the chosen methodology.

4.  **Practicality of the Noise Parameter τ:** The derived AMP-KM algorithm depends on a noise variance parameter τ, which is not part of the original K-means problem. The paper proposes an adaptive estimation scheme, but the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to this choice is not explored. A brief discussion or experiment on this sensitivity would be valuable for practitioners looking to apply the method. How robust is the algorithm to the estimation of τ?

**Summary of Pros and Cons for Acceptance:**

**Pros:**
*   Presents a novel and general AMP framework for Bayesian low-rank matrix factorization.
*   Derives a new, principled, and high-performing algorithm for K-means clustering.
*   Provides clear intuition for why the new algorithm improves upon the classic Lloyd's algorithm.
*   Demonstrates strong empirical performance against relevant baselines on both synthetic and real data.

**Cons:**
*   Key algorithmic derivation is entirely in the supplement, making the main paper less self-contained.
*   Discussion of the underlying theoretical assumptions of AMP and their applicability to the tested problems is limited.
*   Misses the opportunity to discuss state evolution, a major feature of AMP.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions in terms of both a general machine learning framework and a specific, practical algorithm. The strengths in novelty, empirical performance, and insightfulness strongly outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to presentation and could be addressed in a final version. The work advances the state of the art in clustering and provides a powerful tool for a broader class of matrix factorization problems.
