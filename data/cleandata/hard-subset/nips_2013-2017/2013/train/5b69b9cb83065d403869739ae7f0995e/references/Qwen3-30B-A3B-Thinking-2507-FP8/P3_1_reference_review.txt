

# Peer Review: Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing

## Summary and Context

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that extends previous work to handle not only posterior mean estimation but also maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors then apply this framework to K-means clustering, demonstrating how the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem can directly incorporate the constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster. This builds upon the connection between low-rank matrix factorization and clustering established in [10, 11] but provides a novel algorithmic approach that outperforms standard methods.

## Quality

The paper makes a technically sound contribution that extends the AMP framework to a broader class of problems. The theoretical derivation of Algorithm 2 is well-justified, with clear connections to belief propagation and the Bethe approximation. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach by noting that convergence is assumed without proof, though this is common in AMP literature.

The numerical experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, comparing against multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA) on both synthetic and real data. The results consistently show that the AMP-based clustering algorithm achieves lower K-means loss and higher accuracy than alternatives, with faster convergence. The use of 500 problem instances for synthetic data and 50 trials on real data provides strong statistical evidence.

However, the paper would benefit from:
1) A clearer explanation of how the proposed AMP algorithm differs from [9] (which the authors cite as the most relevant prior work)
2) A more detailed analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties
3) A discussion of how the algorithm scales with the assumed rank r̂ (critical for practical applications)

## Clarity

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem definition to algorithm derivation to experimental validation. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is particularly well-explained. The notation is generally consistent, though the dual use of U/V for both true and estimated matrices could be confusing.

The presentation of Algorithm 2 is clear, with well-structured equations that make the algorithm easy to follow. The authors provide valuable intuition about the properties of their approach (e.g., the similarity to variational Bayes methods, the computational advantages).

## Originality

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by extending AMP to handle MAP estimation (beyond just posterior mean estimation), which is a significant limitation of previous AMP algorithms for matrix reconstruction [9]. The authors also correctly identify that their approach differs from recent related work [13, 14] in how they handle the asymptotic limit (fixing rank r rather than rank ratio r/m) and in not assuming statistical independence among components.

The application to clustering is novel in that it directly handles the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank framework, unlike previous approaches that required additional modifications. This represents a clean and theoretically grounded extension of the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for the machine learning community. The proposed algorithm provides a more efficient and effective approach to two fundamental problems: low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over established baselines, with improvements in both accuracy and convergence speed.

The paper's contribution to the theoretical understanding of AMP algorithms is also significant, showing how the framework can be applied to a broader class of problems than previously considered. This has potential implications for other problems in low-rank matrix factorization and Bayesian inference.

## Critical Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical gaps**: While the authors assume convergence of the AMP algorithm, they don't provide conditions for convergence or analyze the algorithm's behavior when convergence isn't achieved. The state evolution analysis that would strengthen the theoretical foundation is mentioned but not provided.

2. **Limited comparison to recent work**: The authors cite [13, 14] as related work but don't clearly explain how their approach differs from these more recent papers, which would strengthen the novelty claim.

3. **Practical considerations**: The paper doesn't address how the algorithm would perform with noisy or incomplete data, which is common in real-world applications. The connection to K-means is strong theoretically, but the paper doesn't fully explain why AMP would be better than standard K-means for the exact problem they're solving.

4. **Lack of parameter sensitivity analysis**: The paper doesn't explore how the algorithm's performance varies with different values of the key parameter τ, which could be critical for practical implementation.

## Strengths

1. The extension of AMP to handle MAP estimation is a significant theoretical contribution.
2. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-justified and the application is novel.
3. The numerical experiments are thorough and demonstrate clear advantages over baselines.
4. The algorithm has low computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration) with fast convergence.

## Recommendation

This paper presents a strong contribution that makes a meaningful extension to the AMP framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction and demonstrates its practical utility for clustering. While there are some theoretical gaps that could be addressed in future work, the overall contribution is significant, well-executed, and would be of interest to the NIPS community.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel approach that is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is particularly compelling, and the proposed algorithm offers clear advantages over existing methods.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing the theoretical gaps mentioned above, but these are not critical flaws that would prevent acceptance. The work represents a valuable addition to the literature on approximate inference methods and their applications to machine learning problems.
