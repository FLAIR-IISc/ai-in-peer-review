

# Peer Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that handles both marginalization (posterior mean estimation) and MAP estimation. The authors extend previous AMP work for low-rank matrix reconstruction to incorporate a wider range of structural priors. The key contribution is applying this framework to clustering, where the authors derive a novel AMP-based K-means algorithm that differs from standard Lloyd's algorithm in its cluster assignment mechanism. The paper demonstrates through experiments that their AMP-based clustering outperforms Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes approaches in terms of accuracy and convergence speed.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness**: The paper provides a rigorous extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with proper theoretical grounding. The derivation of Algorithm 2 is well-motivated, and Proposition 1 establishes a meaningful theoretical guarantee about the algorithm's convergence properties. The connection between the AMP framework and the variational Bayes approach (which they show is a special case when removing certain terms) is well-explained.

2. **Practical Significance**: The application to clustering is compelling. The paper demonstrates that the AMP-based clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3) outperforms standard K-means in both synthetic and real-world experiments, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The improved convergence speed compared to variational Bayes methods (which typically converge more slowly) is a notable practical advantage.

3. **Clarity and Organization**: The paper is well-written and logically structured, with clear problem statements, algorithm descriptions, and experimental results. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is made explicit, which helps contextualize the work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Related Work**: The paper claims novelty in extending AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation, but fails to adequately distinguish its approach from the recent work in [9] (which also addresses general-rank cases) and [14] (bilinear generalized AMP). The authors mention these papers but don't clearly articulate how their algorithm differs or improves upon them. For instance, [9] already handles the general-rank case, and the paper's claim that it's "the first" to handle MAP estimation seems inaccurate.

2. **Limited Practical Considerations**: The paper assumes the true rank r is known, which is a significant limitation in real-world applications. While this is common in theoretical work, it would strengthen the paper to discuss how the algorithm could be extended to handle rank selection or to include a rank-estimation component.

3. **Insufficient Theoretical Analysis**: The paper claims "the first" to handle MAP estimation, but the theoretical analysis of the MAP version (β→∞) is minimal. The connection to the ICM algorithm is made, but it's unclear why AMP would avoid local minima better than ICM (the paper mentions this but provides no theoretical justification).

4. **Experimental Limitations**: The synthetic experiments only test a limited range of parameters (r < 9), and the real-world experiment on the ORL database uses a fixed number of clusters (r̂=40) that matches the number of subjects. This doesn't fully demonstrate the algorithm's robustness to different cluster configurations. The comparison with K-means++ is limited to one initialization method (random), without exploring how AMP performs with better initializations.

## Critical Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the AMP literature by providing a unified framework for both marginalization and MAP estimation in low-rank matrix reconstruction. The application to clustering is particularly interesting, as it directly addresses a constraint (exact cluster assignment) that previous low-rank approaches to clustering often overlook.

However, the paper's claim of novelty is overstated given the recent work in [9] and [14]. The authors should clarify how their algorithm differs from these earlier approaches, particularly regarding the treatment of the rank parameter (they fix r rather than r/m in the limit), which is a subtle but important distinction.

The experimental results are promising but could be strengthened by:
1. Including more comprehensive comparisons with recent AMP-based matrix factorization methods
2. Testing the algorithm on more diverse clustering scenarios
3. Providing more insight into why the AMP algorithm converges faster than variational Bayes

## Recommendation

This paper presents a technically sound contribution that extends the AMP framework to a broader class of low-rank matrix reconstruction problems. While the novelty claim is somewhat overstated, the work has significant practical value, particularly for high-dimensional clustering problems. The paper would be strengthened by clearer differentiation from recent related work, more thorough experimental validation, and addressing the rank selection limitation.

**Recommendation: Accept**

*Pro:*
- Solid theoretical foundation connecting AMP to Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction
- Practical algorithm with demonstrated advantages over standard K-means in high dimensions
- Clear derivation of the AMP-based clustering algorithm
- Good experimental validation on both synthetic and real data

*Con:*
- Insufficient differentiation from recent AMP work (e.g., [9], [14])
- Overstated novelty claim regarding MAP estimation
- Limited discussion of rank selection (assumes true rank known)
- Experimental results could be more comprehensive

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the AMP literature and its application to clustering. While it could be improved by addressing the weaknesses noted, it represents a valuable addition to the field that advances the state of the art in scalable Bayesian matrix factorization.
