

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work from rank-one cases to general rank. The authors frame low-rank matrix reconstruction as a Bayesian inference problem where the matrix factors U0 and V0 have known structural properties (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity), and derive an AMP-based algorithm that handles both marginalization (posterior mean estimation) and MAP estimation through a parameter β. The paper then applies this framework to K-means clustering by recognizing clustering as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with specific structural constraints on V0. The authors develop an AMP-based clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3) that differs from Lloyd's K-means by incorporating cluster size information in the assignment step.

The work builds on previous AMP applications in compressed sensing [6,7] and extends the rank-one AMP algorithm [8] to the general-rank case [9]. It relates to Bayesian matrix factorization methods [3-5] and the variational Bayes approach [4,5], while distinguishing itself by handling the rank constraint differently (fixing rank r rather than ratio r/m) and not assuming statistical independence among row components. The connection to clustering is related to prior work [10,11] but differs in directly handling the exact cluster assignment constraint.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The theoretical derivation of the AMP algorithm is rigorous, with clear connections to existing AMP literature. The authors provide Proposition 1 establishing the relationship between their AMP algorithm and the ICM algorithm (used in Lloyd's K-means), which is an important theoretical contribution. The experimental section is comprehensive, including both artificial data (with 500 trials across varying ranks) and real data (ORL face database). The results clearly demonstrate the AMP-based clustering algorithm's superiority in terms of clustering loss and accuracy over competing methods (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA), with statistically significant improvements.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem formulation is precise, the algorithm is presented with clear mathematical notation, and the connection between matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-explained. The supplementary material (mentioned in the text) would provide additional details on the state evolution analysis, which the authors omit due to space constraints. The paper provides sufficient mathematical detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The paper makes several significant contributions. The extension of AMP to general-rank matrix reconstruction (previously limited to rank-one cases) is novel. The application of AMP to clustering within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework, specifically handling the exact cluster assignment constraint, is new. The paper provides a detailed comparison with related work, clearly differentiating their approach from [9,13,14]. The AMP-based clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3) is the first to directly incorporate cluster size information into the assignment step, which explains its superior performance.

**Significance:** The proposed AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction has potential significance for large-scale matrix factorization problems due to its computational efficiency (O(mN) per iteration). The application to clustering is significant as it provides a new perspective on K-means that improves upon standard methods. The experimental results demonstrate clear practical advantages, with the AMP clustering algorithm outperforming Lloyd's K-means in both artificial and real-world data, while converging faster than variational Bayes methods. The paper advances the state of the art in both matrix reconstruction and clustering.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper provides Proposition 1 establishing a relationship with ICM, it does not thoroughly analyze the convergence properties of the AMP algorithm or provide a detailed state evolution analysis (which the authors mention they omit due to space constraints). This limits the theoretical understanding of the algorithm's performance guarantees.

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper references [13,14] as related work but does not provide a detailed comparison of their AMP algorithm with these recent methods. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the position of the paper in the literature.

**Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper uses a parameter τ that is estimated during the algorithm, but it does not analyze how sensitive the algorithm is to this parameter or provide guidance on optimal selection. The experimental section uses a heuristic estimate (m⁻²N⁻¹‖A - Uᵗ(Vᵗ)⊤‖²F), but a more systematic analysis would be valuable.

**Real Data Experiment Scope:** The ORL database experiment is limited to r̂ = 40 clusters (matching the number of subjects), which is a relatively simple clustering task. More challenging real-world clustering problems would better demonstrate the algorithm's general applicability.

## Recommendation

**Pro (Acceptance):**
- Novel extension of AMP to general-rank matrix reconstruction
- First AMP-based approach to K-means clustering that directly handles exact cluster assignment constraints
- Strong theoretical foundation with Proposition 1
- Comprehensive experimental validation showing clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

**Con (Rejection):**
- Limited theoretical analysis of convergence properties
- Incomplete comparison with recent AMP-based matrix reconstruction methods
- Insufficient analysis of parameter sensitivity
- Real-world experiment limited to a simple clustering problem

The paper makes significant contributions to both low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The AMP-based approach for clustering is particularly innovative and well-supported by experimental evidence. While the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the paper's technical soundness, clarity, and significant empirical results make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The paper advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way and addresses a difficult problem (efficient clustering with structural constraints) better than previous research. The results are important for both practitioners and researchers working on matrix factorization and clustering.
