

# Peer Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with structured factors, extending previous AMP work to handle both marginalization (posterior means) and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors demonstrate that the problem of K-means clustering can be viewed as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with binary constraints on the factors, and they develop an AMP-based algorithm for clustering that outperforms traditional methods like Lloyd's algorithm and K-means++. The paper makes two main contributions: (1) an AMP algorithm that generalizes previous work to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction, and (2) a novel AMP-based clustering algorithm that directly handles the constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster.

The paper relates to prior work in AMP (Donoho et al., 2009; Rangan, 2011), low-rank matrix reconstruction (Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012), and clustering via matrix factorization (Xu et al., 2003; Ding et al., 2010). It distinguishes itself from related work by fixing the rank r rather than the ratio r/m in the asymptotic limit and by not assuming statistical independence among components of the factor matrices.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound with a clear mathematical formulation. The theoretical analysis (Proposition 1) provides a convergence guarantee for the MAP problem, showing that any fixed point of the AMP algorithm is also a fixed point of the ICM algorithm, which has important implications for avoiding bad local minima. The experimental results are thorough and well-designed, including both synthetic data (with varying rank r) and a real-world dataset (ORL faces). The AMP algorithm consistently outperforms baseline methods in terms of clustering accuracy and K-means loss, with faster convergence than variational Bayes methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-explained. The proposed algorithms are presented in a clear, step-by-step manner, making them easy to implement. The supplementary material (referenced in the paper) appears to provide the necessary theoretical details.

**Originality:** The extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction is novel. While Matsushita & Tanaka (2012) proposed an AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix reconstruction, their approach was limited to posterior mean estimation. The paper's unified treatment of marginalization and MAP via the β-parameter extension is a significant contribution. The application of AMP to clustering as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction is also novel, as the authors correctly note that previous approaches did not directly handle the constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster.

**Significance:** The problem of low-rank matrix reconstruction with structured factors is important in machine learning, and the proposed AMP algorithm offers a computationally efficient alternative to variational Bayes methods. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods for clustering, which is a fundamental machine learning task. The approach could potentially be extended to other problems involving structured low-rank matrix factorization.

## Weaknesses

**Comparison with Recent Work:** The paper mentions "bilinear generalized approximate message passing" by Parker et al. (2013) but doesn't provide a detailed comparison. This is a significant oversight, as this recent work is directly related to the authors' contribution and could have clarified the novelty of their approach. The paper should explicitly contrast their algorithm with this and other recent AMP-based approaches.

**Rank Selection:** The paper assumes the true rank r is known, which is a limitation in practical applications where the rank is often unknown. While the authors mention that the rank can be estimated in practice, they don't discuss how this affects the performance of their algorithm or provide any experimental results with estimated rank.

**Computational Complexity:** While the paper states the computational cost is O(mN) per iteration, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the rank r affects this complexity. The cost of calculating f(·, ·; p̂) and G(·, ·; p̂) depends on r, and this should be quantified to better understand the algorithm's scalability.

**State Evolution Analysis:** The paper mentions state evolution in Section 4.1 but doesn't develop it further, despite noting that "analysis with the state evolution also shows that required iteration numbers are O(1) even when the problem size is large." This is a key strength of AMP algorithms that should be elaborated upon to demonstrate the theoretical advantages of their approach.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the area of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The proposed AMP algorithm is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-motivated and leads to a novel algorithm that outperforms traditional approaches.

The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed comparison with recent related work, a discussion of rank selection, and a more thorough analysis of the state evolution. However, these are minor issues that could be addressed in a revised version.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The contributions are significant, the methodology is sound, and the experimental results are convincing. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field of low-rank matrix factorization and clustering, with potential applications in many areas of machine learning and data analysis.

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation
- Clear theoretical analysis with convergence guarantees
- Strong empirical results demonstrating advantages over existing methods
- Well-motivated application to clustering

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited comparison with the most recent related work (Parker et al., 2013)
- Assumption of known rank (r) in practice
- Insufficient analysis of computational complexity in terms of rank r
- Brief treatment of state evolution analysis

The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it makes a theoretically sound contribution to a fundamental problem in machine learning with clear empirical validation.
