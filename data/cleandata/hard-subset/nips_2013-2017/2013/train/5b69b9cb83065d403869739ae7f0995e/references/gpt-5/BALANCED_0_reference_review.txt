Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction when a matrix A0 = U0 V0^T is observed through additive Gaussian noise. The core idea is to exploit prior structure on the factors U0 and V0 via an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm. The authors introduce a one-parameter family of posteriors p̂(U,V|A; β) ∝ p̂(U,V|A)^β to unify marginalization (β = 1) and MAP (β → ∞), derive a bilinear AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) that outputs approximate posterior means or MAP estimates depending on β, and show that it reduces to variational-Bayes/ICM-style updates if the Onsager terms are removed. They further instantiate the framework for clustering by using discrete one-hot priors on V and a flat prior on U to obtain an AMP variant of K-means (Algorithm 3). The AMP-KM update resembles Lloyd’s algorithm but with principled correction terms akin to leave-one-out adjustments. Numerical experiments on synthetic Gaussian cluster data and the ORL faces dataset suggest lower K-means loss and higher accuracy than Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ in high dimensions; the marginal-accuracy AMP variant (β = 1) also outperforms a VBMF baseline.

This work builds on and extends notable AMP/GAMP lines for compressed sensing and bilinear models [6,7,8,9], relates to bilinear generalized AMP (BiG-AMP) and dictionary learning/blind calibration via AMP [13,14], and contrasts with variational Bayes matrix factorization [4,5]. The clustering connection fits within low-rank/NMF-based clustering [10,11] and the classical K-means literature [12,16].

Strengths
- Unifying construction with β that treats both MMSE-style marginalization and MAP within a single AMP derivation; clear algorithmic form with explicit f/G denoisers.
- Insightful connection to K-means, yielding an AMP-KM update with correction terms that account for current assignments and cluster sizes; intuitive leave-one-out interpretation.
- Solid synthetic evaluation across many instances showing consistent benefits over Lloyd’s and K-means++ in high dimension; real-data experiment on ORL demonstrates practical promise.
- Useful discussion of how the Onsager terms distinguish AMP from VB/ICM and a proposition linking AMP fixed points to blockwise optimality.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical development: state evolution is claimed possible but omitted; convergence is assumed and not guaranteed; damping/stability and conditions for validity are not discussed.
- Comparisons are narrow. For bilinear inference, there is no experimental comparison to BiG-AMP/max-sum variants or to other recent AMP/VAMP approaches. For clustering, there is no comparison to EM for GMMs, spectral or spherical K-means, or robust/mini-batch K-means.
- The maximum-accuracy experiments use the true prior; robustness to prior misspecification is not evaluated. Sensitivity to rank mis-specification r̂ ≠ r is not explored.
- Practical issues: the need to estimate τ on the fly, occasional oscillations and collapse to a single cluster on ORL, and no runtime reporting per iteration versus baselines. The per-iteration cost of f/G may be significant for continuous priors and larger r̂.
- Some claims of novelty relative to [9,14] remain qualitative; the differences (e.g., fixed r vs r/m scaling, independence assumptions) are not empirically dissected.

Quality
The derivation appears plausible and consistent with standard AMP recipes. The proposition about blockwise optimality at fixed points is helpful, though many proofs are deferred. Experimental methodology on synthetic data is thorough in averaging over many trials, but the baselines are limited, and performance under model mismatch (e.g., non-Gaussian noise, uneven cluster sizes) is not studied. The observed oscillations and need for ad-hoc stopping criteria suggest missing stabilization tools (e.g., damping, adaptive steps, annealing).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The algorithmic equations are explicit, and the link to VB/ICM is well explained. However, the derivation details, conditions for convergence, and precise assumptions for the state-evolution regime are deferred. Pseudocode and a brief guide for implementing f/G for common priors would help practitioners.

Originality
Moderate. Extending bilinear AMP to a β-parameterized family that covers MAP and applying it directly to one-hot constrained K-means is interesting. However, similar max-sum AMP/GAMP formulations exist in the literature; a more careful positioning versus BiG-AMP and max-sum GAMP and a head-to-head empirical comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

Significance
Moderate. If robust and broadly applicable, an AMP-based K-means variant that consistently outperforms Lloyd’s in high dimensions would be impactful. As it stands, the empirical evidence is promising but limited in scope, and theoretical assurances are missing. The broader low-rank reconstruction algorithm could be useful, but its advantages over existing AMP/GAMP methods need clearer demonstration.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled AMP formulation with a unifying β parameter.
- Appealing and intuitive AMP-K-means algorithm with competitive results.
- Bridges matrix factorization, AMP, and clustering communities.

Cons for acceptance
- Insufficient theory (no state evolution, convergence conditions, or stability guidance).
- Limited and potentially unfair comparisons; robustness not demonstrated.
- Practical issues (oscillations, τ estimation, collapse on ORL) not fully addressed.
- Novelty relative to existing bilinear AMP/GAMP not convincingly established.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide state evolution (even empirically validated) and discuss conditions under which it accurately tracks performance; include damping/stepsize/annealing strategies to ensure convergence.
- Compare against BiG-AMP/max-sum GAMP and recent VAMP-style bilinear methods on the same tasks; for clustering, include EM for spherical GMMs, spectral/spherical K-means, mini-batch K-means, and robust variants.
- Study robustness: prior misspecification, non-Gaussian noise, uneven cluster sizes, outliers, and r̂ ≠ r. Include ablations showing effect of the Onsager terms.
- Report wall-clock time and per-iteration cost; quantify overhead of f/G for continuous priors; provide implementation details and code.
- Mitigate oscillations and empty-cluster issues (damping, soft assignments with β annealing, penalties or priors favoring balanced clusters). Analyze sensitivity to τ estimation and propose an EM-like update.
- Expand real-data evaluation beyond ORL.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The ideas are promising and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger theoretical grounding, broader and fairer empirical comparisons, and more robust treatment of practical issues to meet the NeurIPS bar.
