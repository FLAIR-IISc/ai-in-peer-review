**Summary:**
This paper proposes an algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The method is formulated within a Bayesian framework, allowing for flexible incorporation of prior knowledge on the matrix factors. The authors derive a unified algorithm that can handle both the calculation of marginal posterior distributions (for posterior mean or marginal MAP estimates) and the joint MAP estimation problem by introducing an inverse temperature parameter β. The primary contributions are twofold: first, an extension of a previous AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction to handle MAP estimation, and second, the application of this new algorithm to K-means clustering. By framing clustering as a low-rank reconstruction problem with a one-hot constraint on one of the factor matrices, the authors derive a novel clustering algorithm (AMP-KM) that modifies the standard Lloyd's algorithm update rule. The paper presents numerical experiments on synthetic and real data, suggesting that the proposed AMP-based clustering methods outperform standard K-means (Lloyd's and K-means++) and a variational Bayes baseline in terms of K-means loss and clustering accuracy.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel Application and Interpretation:** The application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering is a notable strength. The derivation results in an intuitive and interesting modification to the classic Lloyd's algorithm (Eq. 18b). The additional terms can be interpreted as correcting for the bias of a cluster center towards its current members and favoring assignments to smaller clusters. This provides a principled, if theoretically motivated, alternative to the standard greedy assignment step.
2.  **Theoretical Connection:** The algorithm is motivated by the belief propagation message passing scheme, which is known to be based on the Bethe approximation. This is often a more accurate approximation than the mean-field assumption underlying variational Bayes methods, providing a solid theoretical justification for why AMP might outperform VBMF, as observed in the experiments.
3.  **Promising Initial Results:** The experimental results, particularly for the clustering application, are promising. On the synthetic dataset, the AMP-based methods consistently outperform their respective baselines (AMP-KM vs. K-means/K-means++, and AMP-MA vs. VBMF-MA). The improved performance on the ORL face database also suggests the method has practical potential.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The empirical evaluation, while positive, is not comprehensive enough to substantiate the claims of superiority.
    *   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison for clustering is made primarily against variants of K-means and a variational Bayes method. However, for a problem cast as low-rank matrix factorization, a comparison against established spectral clustering methods is essential. Spectral methods are a powerful and standard baseline for this class of problems, and their absence is a significant omission.
    *   **Limited Datasets:** The evaluation is performed on one synthetic setup and one small real-world dataset (ORL faces, N=400). To demonstrate the robustness and general applicability of a new clustering algorithm, testing on a wider variety of datasets with different sizes, dimensionalities, and cluster structures is necessary.
    *   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The algorithm introduces a noise variance parameter τ, which does not exist in the original K-means problem. The paper proposes an ad-hoc scheme to estimate it iteratively, but provides no analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to this parameter or the estimation method. This is a critical practical detail that is not adequately addressed.

2.  **Weakened Theoretical Guarantees in Practice:** The theoretical power of AMP, including its precise characterization via state evolution, relies on large-system limits and assumptions of randomness (e.g., i.i.d. Gaussian matrices) that are rarely met in practice. The authors mention state evolution but defer its analysis, and do not discuss the gap between the theory's assumptions and the practical settings of the experiments. Without this, the algorithm appears more as a well-motivated heuristic than a theoretically guaranteed procedure for general-purpose clustering. Furthermore, the paper notes that convergence of the algorithm is not guaranteed in general, which is a major practical drawback.

3.  **Clarity and Relation to Prior Work:** While the paper mentions related AMP works ([9], [13], [14]), the discussion is brief and concludes with "A detailed comparison among these algorithms remains to be made." This is insufficient for a conference submission. A clear positioning of the work requires a more thorough discussion of how it differs from, and improves upon, these closely related methods. The core derivation of the algorithm is also relegated to supplementary material, making it difficult to fully assess the technical soundness of the main contribution from the paper alone.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents an interesting connection between the AMP framework and the classic problem of K-means clustering, resulting in a novel algorithm with an intuitive form. However, the work in its current state feels preliminary. The central weakness is the lack of a rigorous and comprehensive empirical evaluation. For a new clustering algorithm to be considered a significant advance, it must be validated against a stronger and wider set of established baselines (especially spectral methods) on a more diverse collection of datasets. Furthermore, the practical aspects of the algorithm, such as its convergence properties and sensitivity to hyperparameters like τ, are not sufficiently explored.

While the ideas are promising, the paper does not yet provide the extensive evidence needed to be confident in the proposed method's superiority and robustness. It presents a new approach but falls short of demonstrating that this new approach constitutes a clear and demonstrable advance over well-established techniques.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and principled derivation of a new K-means-type algorithm.
*   The connection between AMP and clustering provides a fresh perspective.
*   Initial experimental results are encouraging.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   Experimental validation is insufficient; baselines are weak (missing spectral clustering) and datasets are limited.
*   Practical concerns like convergence and hyperparameter tuning are not adequately addressed.
*   The theoretical justification relies on assumptions that may not hold in practice, and the gap is not discussed.
*   Insufficient comparison to closely related, concurrent work in the AMP literature.
