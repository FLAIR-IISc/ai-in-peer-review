### Review ###

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. The core idea is to reconstruct a matrix `A0 = U0*V0^T` from a noisy observation `A`, leveraging prior knowledge on the factor matrices `U0` and `V0`. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they extend a previous AMP algorithm for this problem [9], which was limited to posterior mean estimation, to a more general framework that can handle both marginal posterior estimation and Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter `β`, where `β=1` corresponds to marginalization and the limit `β→∞` corresponds to the MAP problem. Second, they demonstrate that this framework can be specialized to solve K-means type clustering problems. By setting the prior on the rows of `V` to be a uniform distribution over one-hot vectors, they derive a novel clustering algorithm from first principles. This algorithm resembles Lloyd's K-means but includes insightful correction terms that account for the influence of a data point on its current cluster center and the relative sizes of clusters. The paper provides a theoretical result (Proposition 1) connecting the fixed points of their MAP algorithm to those of the standard Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm. Finally, numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world data (ORL faces) show that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms outperform strong baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a Variational Bayes approach, in terms of both clustering accuracy and final objective value.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Elegant Application of AMP to Clustering:** The main strength of this paper is the principled derivation of a new clustering algorithm. Instead of proposing an ad-hoc modification to K-means, the authors show how a powerful inference framework (AMP) naturally leads to an improved algorithm when applied to the low-rank matrix formulation of clustering. The resulting update rule (Eq. 18b) is particularly insightful, providing an intuitive explanation for its improved performance through bias-correction and cluster-size balancing terms. This is a significant contribution that connects the fields of graphical models/inference and unsupervised learning in a meaningful way.

2.  **General and Flexible Framework:** The proposed AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is general. It can accommodate a wide variety of structural priors on the factors `U` and `V` (by changing the functions `f` and `G`) and can tackle both marginal inference and MAP estimation within a unified framework. This flexibility makes the proposed method a powerful tool for various low-rank matrix reconstruction tasks beyond clustering.

3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is convincing. The proposed methods (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) consistently outperform standard and well-regarded baselines (K-means, K-means++, VBMF) on synthetic data across a range of difficulties (varying `r`). The performance improvement on the real-world ORL face dataset further validates the practical utility of the proposed algorithm. The fact that AMP-KM achieves a lower K-means loss than K-means++ (which is specifically designed to find better local minima of the same objective) is a strong piece of evidence for its effectiveness.

4.  **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear connection to existing methods like Variational Bayes and ICM, correctly identifying the crucial "memory" terms that distinguish AMP and are responsible for its improved performance. Proposition 1, which states that any fixed point of the AMP-MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of ICM, is a valuable result that helps characterize the algorithm's behavior and suggests it explores the solution space more effectively.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Derivations and Theoretical Analysis in the Main Text:** The derivation of the core AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is relegated to the supplementary material. While this is common due to space constraints, it makes the paper less self-contained. More critically, the paper mentions the state evolution analysis—a key theoretical tool that guarantees the performance of AMP algorithms in the large system limit—but provides no details. This analysis would have significantly strengthened the paper by providing theoretical guarantees on performance and convergence, backing up claims like "required iteration numbers are O(1)".

2.  **Clarity on Practical Implementation Details:** The paper proposes using an estimate of the noise variance `τ` for the K-means algorithm, as `τ` is not a natural parameter of the original K-means problem. The update `m−2N−1∥A − U t(V t)⊤∥2F` seems reasonable, but the impact of this online estimation on the algorithm's convergence and performance is not discussed. A more detailed justification or analysis of this choice would be beneficial.

3.  **Sensitivity to Initialization:** In the real data experiment, the authors state that random initialization often failed and that they used the K-means++ initialization scheme. This is an important finding that slightly contradicts the intuition that AMP is better at avoiding bad local minima. While the algorithm still outperforms K-means++ given the same good starting point, its sensitivity to initialization is a practical limitation that should be discussed more explicitly in the main text.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper with clear contributions to the field. The application of AMP to derive a new, principled, and effective K-means clustering algorithm is a significant achievement. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and supported by strong experimental evidence. The weaknesses are primarily related to the omission of details (derivations, state evolution) and a deeper discussion of practical implementation choices, which could be addressed in a final version. The paper's strengths in originality, significance, and quality far outweigh these weaknesses. It presents ideas that are likely to be influential and built upon by other researchers in both machine learning and signal processing.

I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper.
