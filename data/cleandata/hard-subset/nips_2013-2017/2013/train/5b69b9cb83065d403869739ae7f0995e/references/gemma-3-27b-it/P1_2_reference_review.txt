## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary:**

This paper presents an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending existing work to handle general rank and MAP estimation. The authors then apply this algorithm to K-means clustering, framing it as a specific case of low-rank reconstruction, and propose a novel clustering algorithm that outperforms Lloyd’s K-means in high-dimensional settings according to their experiments. The core contribution lies in extending AMP to a broader set of estimation problems beyond posterior means and demonstrating its effectiveness in clustering.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work leveraging AMP for various inference tasks, particularly in compressed sensing and sparse recovery [6, 7]. It acknowledges prior AMP approaches for low-rank matrix reconstruction [8, 9] and extends them to MAP estimation, a crucial addition. The connection to clustering via low-rank matrix factorization is not new [10, 11], but the paper distinguishes itself by directly addressing the hard assignment constraint inherent in K-means within the low-rank framework.  The work also situates itself relative to recent advancements in AMP for dictionary learning and blind calibration [13, 14], highlighting key differences in scaling and assumptions about factor independence.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality of the AMP Framework:** The paper successfully extends the AMP algorithm to address both marginalization and MAP estimation in low-rank matrix reconstruction, broadening its applicability.
* **Novel Clustering Algorithm:** The application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering offers a promising alternative to traditional Lloyd’s algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional spaces, as demonstrated in the experiments.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors acknowledge and mention the possibility of utilizing state evolution for performance prediction, although a detailed presentation is omitted due to space constraints.  Proposition 1 provides some theoretical grounding for the algorithm’s optimality under certain conditions.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The experiments on both synthetic and real (ORL faces) datasets demonstrate the algorithm's effectiveness, showcasing performance gains over Lloyd’s K-means in terms of loss and accuracy.
* **Clear Connection to Existing Literature:**  The paper provides a good overview of related work and clearly articulates its contributions within that context.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited State Evolution Discussion:** While acknowledging the importance of state evolution for analyzing AMP algorithms, the lack of a detailed presentation limits the reader's ability to fully understand the algorithm’s theoretical properties and scaling behavior.
* **Computational Complexity Analysis:** The statement about computational cost being O(mN) is a simplification. While each iteration scales linearly with the data size, the cost of calculating f and G (especially with numerical integration for non-trivial priors) is not fully addressed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The experiments don’t thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to parameter choices (e.g., the noise variance τ). A more in-depth analysis of parameter tuning would strengthen the results.
* **Convergence Guarantee:** The paper states the convergence of the algorithm but does not provide a formal proof.  Mentioning conditions under which convergence is guaranteed or likely to occur would be valuable.
* **Clarity of Notation:**  The notation can be dense, and some definitions (e.g., the purpose of the β parameter) could benefit from more explanation.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 4.1:** The introduction of the β parameter feels somewhat abrupt. A more intuitive explanation of its role in controlling the trade-off between fitting the data and the prior would be helpful.
* **Algorithm 2:**  The functions *f* and *G* are central to the algorithm, but their specific form depends heavily on the chosen prior *p̂*.  Providing more concrete examples of *f* and *G* for commonly used priors (e.g., Gaussian, Laplacian) would enhance clarity.
* **Proposition 1:** The condition that  ∑m i=1 S ∞ i and ∑N j=1 T ∞ j are positive semidefinite seems critical. Are there scenarios where this condition might not hold, and what implications would that have?
* **Section 4.3:** The justification for using the estimated τ in Algorithm 3 is somewhat hand-wavy. A more rigorous justification or discussion of the potential impact of estimation error would be appreciated.
* **Experimental Results:** The experiments are encouraging, but it would be beneficial to see results on a wider range of datasets and with different parameter settings.  Comparing performance with other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms beyond Lloyd’s K-means would also strengthen the conclusions.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel extension of the AMP algorithm with broad applicability to low-rank matrix reconstruction and related problems.
* The application of AMP to K-means clustering offers a potentially significant improvement over existing algorithms, particularly in high dimensions.
* The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions within the context of relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a detailed analysis of state evolution and computational complexity limits the reader's understanding of the algorithm’s theoretical properties and practical limitations.
* The empirical evaluation, while promising, could be more comprehensive and robust.
* The convergence guarantee is not formally stated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The extension of the AMP algorithm and its successful application to clustering are noteworthy. While there are some areas for improvement (particularly regarding theoretical analysis and experimental validation), the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  I recommend accepting the paper with the expectation that the authors will address the concerns raised above, specifically by:

1.  Providing a more detailed discussion of state evolution (even if relegated to the supplementary material).
2.  Elaborating on the computational complexity analysis, especially the cost of evaluating *f* and *G*.
3.  Conducting a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the algorithm’s parameters.
4.  Adding more discussion regarding the convergence conditions.
