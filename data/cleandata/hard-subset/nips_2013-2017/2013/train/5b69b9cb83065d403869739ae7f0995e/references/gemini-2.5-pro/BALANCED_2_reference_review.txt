This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The problem is formulated in a Bayesian setting, where prior distributions on the factor matrices U and V can be used to incorporate structural knowledge. The key contributions are twofold. First, the paper presents a general AMP algorithm that extends previous work by unifying the treatment of posterior marginalization (for calculating posterior means) and Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation through a single inverse temperature parameter, β. This framework is flexible enough to accommodate a wide variety of priors. Second, the paper applies this algorithm to the problem of K-means clustering. By formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one factor matrix consists of one-hot vectors representing cluster assignments, the authors derive a novel iterative clustering algorithm. This new algorithm, termed AMP-KM, is shown to be a principled modification of the classic Lloyd's algorithm.

The proposed work builds upon the well-established theory of AMP, originally developed for compressed sensing, and extends it to the general-rank matrix factorization setting. It differentiates itself from prior variational Bayes (VB) approaches by being derived from the Bethe approximation, which is known to be more accurate than the mean-field approximation underlying VB. While the idea of formulating clustering as matrix factorization is not new, the derivation of a novel, high-performing clustering algorithm from the first principles of AMP is a significant and original contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance of the Clustering Algorithm:** The application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering is the paper's main strength. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3, Eq. 18) is not an ad-hoc heuristic but emerges naturally from the principled AMP derivation. The intuitive interpretation of the new terms in the assignment step (Eq. 18b) as a bias correction and a penalty that favors smaller clusters is insightful and clearly explained. Given the ubiquity of K-means, a new algorithm that demonstrably outperforms Lloyd's algorithm and K-means++ on the same objective function is a significant practical contribution.

2.  **Flexible and General Framework:** The proposed AMP algorithm for general Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction (Algorithm 2) is a valuable contribution in its own right. The unification of marginalization and MAP estimation is elegant, and the ability to incorporate arbitrary priors on the factors makes the framework powerful and applicable to a wide range of problems beyond clustering (e.g., non-negative or sparse matrix factorization).

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. On synthetic data, the proposed AMP-KM and AMP-MA algorithms consistently outperform standard baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF) in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy. The experiments on the ORL face dataset demonstrate that these advantages translate to real-world problems. The fast convergence reported for the AMP methods is also a notable practical advantage.

4.  **Theoretical Insight:** Proposition 1 provides a valuable connection between the fixed points of the proposed AMP algorithm for the MAP problem and the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm. The result that any AMP fixed point is also an ICM fixed point (but not vice versa) offers a compelling argument for why AMP might be more effective at avoiding poor local minima.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of State Evolution Analysis:** A major theoretical component of the AMP literature is the "state evolution" (SE), a set of simple scalar equations that provably track the macroscopic behavior of the algorithm in the large system limit. SE analysis provides performance guarantees and is a key justification for the algorithm's specific form (including the crucial "Onsager" terms). The authors mention SE but defer it entirely due to space limitations. This is a significant omission. Without it, the claims of asymptotic exactness and the theoretical superiority over VB remain largely assertive rather than fully substantiated within the paper.

2.  **Clarity of Derivation:** The core algorithm of the paper (Algorithm 2) is presented without any derivation in the main text. While full details are expected in the supplement, a high-level sketch of the derivation (e.g., outlining the factor graph representation and the key approximations made) would greatly improve the paper's clarity and allow readers to better appreciate the technical contribution.

3.  **Convergence and Stability:** The paper assumes convergence of the algorithm, but this is not guaranteed. The authors note that for K-means, they had to use a modified stopping criterion to handle oscillations. This suggests potential stability issues that are not analyzed. A more detailed discussion of the algorithm's convergence properties, even if empirical, and potential remedies for oscillations (such as damping) would strengthen the work.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

1.  I strongly recommend that the authors include the state evolution equations in the main paper and briefly discuss their implications. For example, a plot comparing the performance predicted by SE with the empirical MSE from simulations would provide powerful evidence for the theory and significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  To improve clarity, a short paragraph sketching the derivation of Algorithm 2 could be added. This would provide valuable context for the reader without requiring extensive mathematical detail, which can remain in the supplement.

3.  The discussion of the observed oscillations in the AMP-KM algorithm could be expanded. Is this a known phenomenon in this type of AMP algorithm? Does it affect the quality of the final solution? This would add practical depth to the analysis.

4.  The estimation of the noise parameter `τ` in the K-means experiments is an important practical detail. A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the algorithm to this parameter would be a useful addition.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper with significant contributions. The derivation of a superior K-means algorithm from the principled AMP framework is both novel and impactful. The empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. The primary weakness is the omission of the state evolution analysis, which is a cornerstone of modern AMP theory. Despite this, the strengths of the paper, particularly its originality and practical significance, are compelling. With revisions to address the theoretical and clarity-related weaknesses, this could be an excellent paper.
