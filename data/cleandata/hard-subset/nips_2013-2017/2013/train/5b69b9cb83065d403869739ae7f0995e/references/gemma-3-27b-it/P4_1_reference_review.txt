This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The core idea is to extend existing AMP algorithms to handle general rank cases and MAP estimation, and to leverage the connection between low-rank reconstruction and clustering. The work builds on a substantial body of research in AMP, Bayesian matrix factorization, and clustering. It correctly identifies the computational cost of traditional Bayesian methods and positions AMP as a potentially efficient alternative.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the AMP algorithm appears logically structured, building upon previous work in the field (specifically citing [9] and extending it). The extension to MAP estimation is a clear contribution. The proposition regarding the optimality of the fixed point in the MAP problem provides a valuable theoretical grounding. However, the explanation of the connection between the parameters and assumptions in the algorithm and their impact on performance is not always fully fleshed out. The claim of "first" application of AMP to clustering with a hard assignment constraint needs stronger justification against related work like spectral clustering or other constrained matrix factorization methods. The numerical experiments demonstrate improvements over standard K-means and variational Bayes methods. However, the justification of the parameter τ, and its impact on the results, warrants further clarification. The limitations of the algorithm’s convergence guarantee are acknowledged, although a more thorough discussion of potential failure modes would be beneficial.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but can be dense in certain sections, particularly the derivation of the algorithm. The notation is consistent, but the numerous equations can be challenging to follow without careful reading. While the authors attempt to explain the intuition behind the AMP algorithm, the explanation could be more accessible to readers not deeply familiar with the method. The presentation of Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 3 is clear. Providing a high-level overview of the key steps and the logic behind them before diving into the equations would improve readability. The organization is logical, with a clear progression from problem setting to proposed algorithm and then to experimental results.

**Originality:** The novelty lies in the extension of AMP algorithms to the general rank case for low-rank matrix reconstruction and the application of this extended algorithm to clustering with a hard assignment constraint. While AMP has been applied to related problems (as cited in the paper), the specific extensions and the connection to clustering constitute a novel contribution. The use of a one-parameter extension of the posterior distribution to unify marginalization and MAP problems is a clever approach. The advantage of potentially avoiding local minima by leveraging the AMP properties compared to ICM is a noteworthy aspect. However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing clustering algorithms beyond K-means, particularly those that also leverage low-rank structure.

**Significance:** The results suggest that the proposed AMP algorithm offers improvements in both efficiency and accuracy compared to traditional methods for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The potential for faster convergence and better performance on high-dimensional data is significant. The ability to incorporate various prior distributions provides flexibility. The theoretical guarantee on the optimality of the fixed point is a valuable contribution. The empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets support the claim of improved performance. The work has the potential to be useful to researchers and practitioners working on problems involving low-rank matrix reconstruction, clustering, and Bayesian inference. However, the practical impact would be enhanced by providing more detailed implementation guidance and exploring the algorithm’s performance on a wider range of datasets.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and builds on established research in AMP and matrix factorization.
*   Novel extension of AMP to the general rank case and application to K-means clustering.
*   Empirically demonstrates improvements over existing methods.
*   Provides a theoretical guarantee for the MAP problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The explanation of the algorithm and its theoretical foundations could be more accessible.
*   The justification of the novelty compared to existing methods in constrained clustering is not sufficiently strong.
*   The parameter selection (e.g., τ) and its impact are not fully explored.
*   The discussion of potential limitations and failure modes could be more comprehensive.
