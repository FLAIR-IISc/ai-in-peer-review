This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The authors extend previous AMP work to handle MAP estimation and demonstrate that their algorithm outperforms Lloyd’s K-means when data are high-dimensional. The core idea is to leverage the flexibility of the Bayesian framework combined with the computational efficiency of AMP. The paper is well-motivated, building on existing literature in compressed sensing, Bayesian matrix factorization, and AMP algorithms.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension of AMP:** Extending the AMP algorithm to handle MAP estimation in the low-rank matrix reconstruction setting is a valuable contribution. Prior work ([9]) was limited to posterior mean estimation.
* **Connection to Clustering:** Formulating K-means clustering as a specific case of low-rank matrix reconstruction is a clever insight and provides a strong application for the proposed algorithm. The direct constraint enforcing exactly one cluster assignment is also a positive aspect.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical framework and connect their algorithm to existing theoretical results related to AMP and variational Bayes, including a proposition demonstrating optimality under certain conditions. The discussion of the state evolution (although not presented in detail due to space constraints) hints at a deeper theoretical understanding.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents results on both artificial and real datasets (ORL faces) showing that the proposed algorithm outperforms Lloyd’s K-means algorithm. The results on the ORL dataset are particularly encouraging, as it highlights the performance gain in a real-world application.
* **Clear Mathematical Formulation:** The mathematical notation and derivations are generally clear and easy to follow, particularly for researchers familiar with Bayesian methods and AMP.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a thorough overview of related work, including references to key papers in matrix factorization, AMP, and clustering.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Algorithm:** While the authors claim low computational cost (O(mN)), the practical implementation and the dependence on parameters like *τ* and the specific functions *f* and *G* could introduce significant overhead.  More detailed computational analysis, including the cost of calculating *f* and *G*, would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance seems dependent on the choice of noise variance parameter *τ*, which is estimated empirically. A more robust and theoretically grounded approach for determining *τ* would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Clustering:**  While the comparison to Lloyd’s K-means is valuable, it would be beneficial to compare the proposed algorithm to more recent and sophisticated clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering, DBSCAN, or other deep learning based clustering methods) to better establish its relative performance.
* **Convergence Guarantee:** The authors acknowledge that convergence is not guaranteed. Providing conditions under which convergence can be ensured or discussing the impact of non-convergence on the empirical results would be helpful.
* **Explanation of the Benefits of AMP over Variational Bayes:** The paper suggests that AMP might outperform variational Bayes due to its ability to capture dependencies between *U* and *V*. However, this point is not fully elaborated, and a more detailed explanation of the advantages of AMP in this specific context would be appreciated.
* **Clarity in Algorithm 3:** The reasoning behind the use of the temporary estimate for *τ* in Algorithm 3 is not completely clear. More detail on the derivation or justification for this estimate would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel extension of AMP to a relevant problem (low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering).
* The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is insightful.
* The empirical results demonstrate a clear performance improvement over a standard baseline (Lloyd's K-means).
* The theoretical justification and connection to existing literature are solid.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The practical complexity of the algorithm and the sensitivity to parameters like *τ* raise concerns about its usability.
* The comparison to other clustering algorithms is limited.
* The convergence guarantee is not addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated contribution. The extension of AMP to MAP estimation and its application to K-means clustering are significant. While there are some weaknesses related to the algorithm’s complexity, parameter sensitivity, and limited comparison to state-of-the-art methods, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, including the cost of calculating *f* and *G*.
* Investigate more robust methods for determining the noise variance parameter *τ*.
* Compare the proposed algorithm to a wider range of clustering algorithms, including more recent and sophisticated methods.
* Discuss the convergence properties of the algorithm and potential remedies for non-convergence.
* Elaborate on the advantages of AMP over variational Bayes in this specific context.
* Provide further explanation regarding the choice of the temporary estimate of *τ* in Algorithm 3.
