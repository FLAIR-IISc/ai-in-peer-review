

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work for rank-one cases to the general-rank scenario. The authors derive an AMP algorithm that can handle both marginalization and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation problems within a unified framework. They then apply this algorithm to K-means clustering, showing how the clustering problem can be formulated as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction. The paper demonstrates that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard methods like Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ on both artificial and real-world datasets (ORL Faces).

The work builds upon Donoho et al.'s AMP framework for compressed sensing, Rangan & Fletcher's IterFac for rank-one cases, and Matsushita & Tanaka's previous AMP work for low-rank matrix reconstruction. The authors distinguish their contribution by handling general-rank cases with a unified framework, addressing the MAP problem directly, and applying it to clustering with the constraint that each data point must belong to exactly one cluster.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed AMP algorithm, with clear derivation of the update equations and a theoretical justification for the algorithm's properties (Proposition 1). The connection to variational Bayes methods is well-articulated.

2. **Novel Application**: The application of AMP to clustering is novel in that it directly handles the constraint that each data point must belong to exactly one cluster within the low-rank reconstruction framework. This addresses a limitation in previous approaches that used low-rank reconstruction for clustering.

3. **Computational Efficiency**: The algorithm has a linear computational cost (O(mN) per iteration), making it suitable for large-scale problems. The authors correctly identify the computational advantages over variational Bayes methods.

4. **Comprehensive Experiments**: The numerical experiments on both artificial and real data sets are thorough and well-designed. The results clearly demonstrate that the AMP-based clustering algorithm achieves better K-means loss and accuracy than baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and VBMF-MA.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the problem setting, algorithm derivation, and experimental results. The connection between the matrix reconstruction problem and clustering is particularly well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of State Evolution Analysis**: While the paper mentions state evolution as a key property of AMP algorithms, it does not provide the corresponding analysis for the proposed algorithm. State evolution is crucial for understanding AMP's performance in large-scale problems and for guiding parameter selection.

2. **Limited Theoretical Convergence Analysis**: The paper only provides a fixed-point analysis (Proposition 1) but lacks a comprehensive theoretical analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties. It would be valuable to know under what conditions the algorithm converges to a global optimum.

3. **Comparison with Recent Clustering Methods**: The paper compares with standard K-means variants but does not compare with more recent clustering algorithms that have been developed in the past few years (e.g., spectral clustering with different kernels, deep clustering methods).

4. **Sensitivity Analysis**: The paper does not investigate the sensitivity of the algorithm to the choice of the parameter β or the initialization. Given that the AMP algorithm's performance could depend on these choices, a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper.

5. **Real-World Dataset Limitation**: The real-world experiment on the ORL database uses only a single dataset with a fixed number of clusters (r̂ = 40). A more comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets and cluster numbers would better demonstrate the algorithm's general utility.

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a significant contribution to the intersection of AMP algorithms, low-rank matrix reconstruction, and clustering. The proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm is theoretically well-motivated, computationally efficient, and empirically superior to standard approaches on the tested datasets. The paper is well-written and makes a clear case for the novelty and significance of the work.

The primary weakness is the lack of state evolution analysis, which is a key theoretical component of AMP algorithms. While the authors mention state evolution, they do not provide the corresponding analysis, which would strengthen the theoretical foundation of their work. Additionally, the limited convergence analysis and sensitivity study are notable omissions.

## Recommendations

1. Include the state evolution analysis for the proposed AMP algorithm. This would provide theoretical guarantees about the algorithm's performance in large-scale settings, which is a hallmark of AMP algorithms.

2. Add a more comprehensive convergence analysis, including conditions under which the algorithm converges to a global optimum.

3. Expand the experimental section to include comparisons with more recent clustering algorithms and to test the algorithm on multiple real-world datasets with varying numbers of clusters.

4. Add a sensitivity analysis to investigate how the algorithm's performance depends on the choice of β and initialization.

5. Clarify the role of the parameter τ in the clustering algorithm and provide a more principled method for its estimation.

While the paper has some theoretical gaps, the empirical results are strong, and the proposed algorithm is both novel and practically useful. With the suggested improvements, particularly the addition of state evolution analysis, this paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS. The authors have presented a solid foundation for their AMP-based approach, and with some additional theoretical and experimental validation, it would be a valuable addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
