This paper presents an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and its application to K-means clustering. The core idea of extending existing AMP algorithms to handle MAP estimation and leveraging the connection between low-rank reconstruction and clustering is potentially interesting. However, the paper suffers from a significant number of shortcomings, ranging from presentation and justification of methodological choices to a lack of rigorous experimental validation and insufficient comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods.

The introduction is adequate but lacks focus. While it mentions the flexibility of the Bayesian approach, it doesn't clearly articulate *why* AMP is a superior choice compared to existing Bayesian methods (variational Bayes, MCMC) beyond a vague claim of computational efficiency. The motivation for fixing the rank 'r' rather than 'r/m' is insufficiently explained, and the statement about independence of components within U0 and V0 feels like a missed opportunity to discuss how the algorithm handles dependencies or their impact.

Section 2 is mathematically dense, which isn't inherently bad, but the justification for scaling the noise variance with 'm' feels ad-hoc and lacks a clear explanation of its purpose beyond allowing for the limit m,N → ∞.  The formulation of the Bayesian approach is standard, but the authors repeatedly state they are making *assumptions* about the prior distributions without adequately justifying *why* those assumptions are made. While acknowledging they may not be true, there’s no discussion of sensitivity analysis or the impact of different prior choices. The transition from the general problem setting to the clustering formulation is somewhat abrupt and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation.

The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 2) is presented without sufficient detail regarding the functions 'f' and 'G'. While defined in equation (10), the practical implications and computational cost of evaluating these functions, particularly for non-Gaussian priors, aren't addressed. The claim that f(b,Λ; p̂) need not be differentiable everywhere is a red flag. A rigorous analysis of the algorithm's convergence properties is absent, and the statement about positive semi-definiteness of ∑m i=1 S ∞ i and ∑N j=1 T ∞ j feels like a hand-waving argument rather than a proof component. The discussion of Proposition 1 feels disconnected from the preceding explanation of the algorithm; the link between the fixed-point condition and avoidance of local minima isn't compellingly made.

The numerical experiments are a significant weakness. The experiments on artificial data sets are incomplete. The results are presented visually (Figure 1, referenced but not included), and crucial details, like the range of ‘r’ values tested, the specific parameter settings used, and the precise definitions of accuracy and K-means loss are missing.  The claim of outperforming Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ is unsubstantiated without a detailed comparison table of results, including standard deviations.  The results on the ORL face database are equally weak. A comparison against standard clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering) is missing, and the justification for using the K-means++ initialization for the AMP algorithm suggests the standalone AMP algorithm may not be robust. The phrase "almost all data were assigned to one cluster" indicates a significant instability issue.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* **Lack of Rigor:** The paper relies heavily on intuition and lacks rigorous theoretical analysis, especially concerning convergence and the impact of prior choices.
* **Insufficient Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are incomplete, lack detailed reporting, and fail to provide a convincing comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
* **Weak Justification of Methodological Choices:** The rationale behind scaling the noise variance and fixing the rank 'r' is unclear. The assumptions regarding prior distributions are arbitrary and lack justification.
* **Presentation Issues:** The paper is dense and lacks clarity in several places, hindering understanding and reproducibility.
* **Missing Details:** Critical details regarding the implementation and computational cost of the proposed algorithm are absent.

**Arguments for Acceptance (Weak):**

* **Novelty:** The extension of AMP algorithms to MAP estimation and the application to clustering are potentially novel.
* **Potential for Efficiency:** The AMP algorithm has the potential for computational efficiency, although this isn’t convincingly demonstrated.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper correctly identifies the relationship between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential novelty of the approach, the paper suffers from too many shortcomings to warrant acceptance. The lack of rigorous analysis, insufficient experimental validation, and weak justification of methodological choices significantly detract from its contribution. The paper reads more like a preliminary investigation than a complete and well-supported research contribution. I recommend **rejection**. The authors need to significantly expand the theoretical analysis, conduct more comprehensive and well-documented experiments, and provide a more compelling justification for their choices before resubmitting.
