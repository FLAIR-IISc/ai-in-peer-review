This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The core idea is to model the matrix `A` as the product of two factor matrices `U` and `V` plus additive Gaussian noise, and then perform Bayesian inference on the factors. The proposed AMP algorithm provides an efficient, deterministic approximation for both marginal posterior estimation (e.g., for posterior means) and Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors unify these two tasks through a single algorithm parameterized by an inverse temperature `β`. A significant contribution is the application of this framework to K-means clustering, where the cluster assignment matrix `V` is constrained to have one-hot-encoded rows. This results in a new clustering algorithm, AMP-KM, that modifies the standard Lloyd's algorithm update rule with additional "self-correction" terms. The paper's claims are supported by numerical experiments on synthetic and real-world data, showing superior performance over standard K-means and Variational Bayes (VB) counterparts.

This work builds upon the growing literature on AMP, which originated in compressed sensing [6] and has been extended to various statistical estimation problems. The key innovation of AMP over more standard iterative methods like Variational Bayes (or Iterated Conditional Modes for MAP) is the inclusion of "Onsager reaction terms" which correct for dependencies induced during the message-passing iterations. This paper extends the work of [9] on AMP for low-rank matrices to handle a wider class of priors and to tackle the MAP problem, not just posterior mean estimation. The connection to clustering is clever and provides a principled derivation for a new algorithm in a well-established field.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel and Principled Algorithm Derivation:** The application of AMP to this general low-rank matrix factorization setting is a strong contribution. The derivation of a new K-means-like algorithm (AMP-KM) from this general framework is particularly elegant. Unlike heuristic modifications to K-means, Eq. (18b) provides a theoretically-grounded update rule that accounts for the bias introduced by a data point's influence on its own cluster center. This provides a clear and intuitive explanation for the algorithm's improved performance.

2.  **Unification of Estimation Tasks:** The use of the parameter `β` to smoothly interpolate between marginal inference (`β=1`) and MAP estimation (`β -> ∞`) is a nice theoretical feature. It allows for a single algorithmic framework to address multiple common statistical goals.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach. AMP-KM consistently finds better local optima (lower K-means loss) than Lloyd's algorithm and K-means++, even when K-means++ uses a sophisticated initialization. Similarly, the AMP-based algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering (AMP-MA) outperforms its VB counterpart (VBMF-MA), highlighting the practical benefit of the more accurate Bethe approximation underlying AMP compared to the mean-field approximation of VB. The convergence speed is also shown to be competitive.

4.  **Theoretical Insight:** Proposition 1 provides a valuable piece of analysis, showing that any fixed point of the AMP-MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of the simpler ICM (or alternating minimization) algorithm. This supports the intuition that AMP is a more sophisticated search procedure that can potentially escape poor local minima where ICM would get stuck.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Clarity on Originality vs. Related Work:** The paper mentions recent, highly relevant work on Bilinear GAMP [14], but dismisses a detailed comparison. This is a significant omission. The problem setting in this paper appears to be a special case of the one considered in Bilinear GAMP (BGAMP). The authors should clarify the precise relationship. Is their algorithm a specific instance of BGAMP, or are there fundamental differences? The stated difference—fixing rank `r` versus a fixed ratio `r/m`—is a subtle but important point from the perspective of the random matrix theory that underpins AMP analysis. A more detailed discussion is needed to clearly delineate this paper's contribution from [14].

2.  **Convergence and Practicalities:** The paper correctly notes that convergence of AMP is not guaranteed. The experiments reveal some practical issues that deserve more discussion:
    *   The ad-hoc stopping criterion for AMP-KM (`V t = V t−2`) is used to handle oscillations. This is a known phenomenon in AMP-like algorithms (limit cycles) but contrasts with the monotonic decrease in the objective function guaranteed by Lloyd's algorithm. A brief discussion of these dynamics would strengthen the paper.
    *   The algorithm failed in 2 out of 50 trials on the ORL dataset. What are the failure modes? Is the algorithm sensitive to initialization or the estimation of the noise parameter `τ`?
    *   The noise variance `τ` is assumed known in the derivation but is estimated heuristically during the K-means experiments. The performance of AMP can be sensitive to the accuracy of this parameter. A comment on the robustness of the algorithm to this estimation would be valuable.

3.  **Derivation Details:** While page limits are a real constraint, the complete relegation of the algorithm's derivation to supplementary material makes the paper less self-contained. A short, intuitive sketch of the derivation (e.g., starting from the graphical model and explaining how the belief propagation messages are approximated in the large system limit) would greatly improve the clarity and impact of the main paper.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

Overall, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and effective algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The connection between the sophisticated AMP framework and the classic K-means problem is insightful and leads to a practical algorithm with superior performance. The empirical results are strong and well-presented. The main weaknesses are the insufficient comparison with very recent and closely related work, and a somewhat brief treatment of the practical convergence behavior and parameter estimation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel, principled, and effective algorithm for K-means clustering derived from the AMP framework.
*   Provides a flexible framework for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that unifies marginal and MAP estimation.
*   Demonstrates superior performance over standard baselines (Lloyd's K-means, VB) in well-designed experiments.
*   Offers interesting theoretical insights (Proposition 1) into the relationship between AMP and ICM fixed points.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The novelty relative to very recent work, particularly Bilinear GAMP [14], is not clearly established.
*   Practical issues like convergence guarantees, failure modes, and heuristic parameter estimation are not fully explored.

I lean towards acceptance, as the application to clustering is novel and the results are compelling. However, I would strongly urge the authors to address the relationship with Bilinear GAMP [14] in a revised version, as this is critical for correctly positioning the paper's contribution.
