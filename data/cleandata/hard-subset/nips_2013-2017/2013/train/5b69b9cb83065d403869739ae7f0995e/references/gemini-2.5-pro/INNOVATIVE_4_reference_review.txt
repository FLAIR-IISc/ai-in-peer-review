### Summary

This paper presents two primary contributions. First, it develops a general algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). This algorithm extends previous work by introducing a unified framework, controlled by an inverse temperature parameter `β`, that can handle both the calculation of posterior marginals (for `β=1`) and MAP estimation (in the limit `β → ∞`). The framework is flexible, accommodating various prior distributions on the matrix factors to incorporate structural knowledge. Second, and more significantly, the paper applies this MAP-AMP algorithm to the problem of K-means clustering, which is framed as a specific instance of low-rank matrix factorization with one-hot vector constraints. This derivation yields a novel, iterative clustering algorithm that is distinct from the classic Lloyd's algorithm and demonstrates superior performance.

The work builds upon the established line of research on AMP, originally developed for compressed sensing and later extended to other matrix and tensor problems. The key novelty here is not the application of AMP to matrix factorization per se, but rather the elegant generalization to a flexible Bayesian framework and its subsequent specialization to produce a new, principled, and highly effective algorithm for the canonical K-means problem.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Bold Approach:** The most impressive aspect of this paper is the fresh perspective it brings to the well-trodden field of K-means clustering. Instead of proposing an incremental improvement or a new initialization heuristic, the authors derive a fundamentally new update rule from the first principles of statistical physics-based message passing. The resulting algorithm (eq. 18b) contains intuitive correction terms that penalize a point for staying in its current cluster (correcting for the centroid's bias) and favor smaller clusters. This is a non-obvious and elegant result that stems directly from the principled AMP derivation. This kind of bold reconceptualization of a classic problem is precisely what the community should value.

2.  **Strong and Unified Framework:** The general AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a valuable contribution in its own right. Unifying marginal inference and MAP estimation through the `β` parameter is elegant and provides a powerful tool for a wide range of low-rank matrix problems beyond clustering. The connection to and distinction from Variational Bayes/ICM methods is clearly articulated, with the "Onsager" correction terms being the key difference that theoretically grounds the algorithm's expected superior performance.

3.  **Compelling Empirical Validation:** The experiments, though not exhaustive, are well-designed and convincingly support the paper's claims. On both synthetic and real-world data (ORL faces), the proposed AMP-KM algorithm consistently outperforms strong baselines like K-means and K-means++, not just in terms of clustering accuracy but also in finding better local minima of the K-means objective function itself. This demonstrates that the algorithm is not just different, but practically better. The fast convergence reported is also a significant practical advantage.

4.  **Interesting Theoretical Insight:** Proposition 1, which establishes that any fixed point of the MAP-AMP algorithm is also a fixed point of the standard ICM (alternating minimization) algorithm, is a nice theoretical result. It suggests that the AMP dynamics are more constrained and may be able to navigate the energy landscape more effectively to avoid the poor local minima that often trap ICM/Lloyd's algorithm.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Assumed Convergence:** The paper assumes convergence of the proposed iterative algorithms. It is well-known that AMP-style algorithms do not have guaranteed convergence in all cases, and their dynamics can sometimes be unstable. While the empirical results suggest the algorithm works well in practice, a discussion of the conditions for convergence or potential failure modes would have strengthened the paper. However, given the novelty of the algorithm itself, this is a minor and acceptable omission for a conference paper.

2.  **Heuristic Parameter Estimation:** The proposed K-means algorithm introduces a noise variance parameter `τ` that is not part of the original problem formulation. The authors propose a reasonable heuristic to estimate this online. While this works empirically, it slightly detracts from the otherwise principled derivation. A more rigorous treatment of this parameter would be a welcome addition in future work.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Related AMP Literature:** The paper acknowledges other recent work on AMP for related problems like dictionary learning and bilinear recovery but defers a detailed comparison. A slightly more in-depth discussion situating their fixed-rank derivation against these other large-system-limit approaches would have been helpful for experts in the field.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work presents a bold and highly novel approach to the fundamental problem of K-means clustering, deriving a new and superior algorithm from the sophisticated framework of Approximate Message Passing. The conceptual leap of connecting this advanced inference machinery to a classic ML problem and producing a simple, intuitive, and effective new algorithm is a significant achievement. The strengths, particularly the novelty and significance of the contributions, far outweigh the minor weaknesses related to convergence proofs and heuristic parameter settings. This paper is an excellent example of how ideas from other fields can be used to shed new light on old problems, and it has the potential to inspire a new line of research into principled, message-passing-based algorithms for a wide range of optimization problems in machine learning.
