### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The core contributions are twofold. First, it extends a previous AMP algorithm for this problem [9] to a more general framework that can handle not only posterior mean estimation but also Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is elegantly achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter `β` into the posterior distribution, where `β=1` corresponds to marginal inference and `β→∞` recovers the MAP case. Second, the paper demonstrates a powerful application of this framework by formulating K-means clustering as a specific instance of low-rank matrix reconstruction with discrete constraints on one of the factor matrices. This leads to a new AMP-based algorithm for K-means (AMP-KM) and for a probabilistic variant termed "maximum accuracy clustering" (AMP-MA). The authors provide theoretical connections between their AMP-MAP algorithm and the standard Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm, and present compelling numerical experiments on synthetic and real-world data showing that their clustering algorithms outperform established methods like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a Variational Bayes approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Principled Algorithm Derivation:** The paper's main strength lies in its elegant derivation of a unified AMP framework for both marginal and MAP estimation in low-rank matrix factorization. The use of the `β` parameter is a clean and principled way to connect these two inference tasks. This extends the applicability of AMP in this domain beyond simple posterior mean estimation.

2.  **Innovative Application to Clustering:** The application to K-means clustering is a significant contribution. While the idea of linking clustering to matrix factorization is not new, the authors' approach appears to be the first to directly incorporate the one-hot cluster assignment constraint into an AMP framework. The resulting update rule for cluster assignment (Eq. 18b) is non-trivial and includes intuitive "self-correction" terms that account for a data point's influence on its own cluster center, a feature absent in Lloyd's algorithm.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are convincing. On synthetic data, the proposed AMP-KM and AMP-MA algorithms consistently outperform their respective counterparts (K-means++/Lloyd's and VBMF-MA) in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy, especially as the number of clusters increases. The results on the ORL face database further demonstrate the practical advantage of the proposed method over the widely used K-means++ on a real-world, high-dimensional problem.

4.  **Insightful Connections to Existing Methods:** The paper does a good job of relating the proposed algorithm to existing methods. The identification of the AMP updates as a standard variational/ICM update plus an "Onsager" correction term (the last terms in Eqs. 9a and 9c) is insightful. Proposition 1, which establishes that any fixed point of the AMP-MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of ICM, provides a useful theoretical link and suggests that AMP may navigate the optimization landscape more effectively to find better local optima, which is supported by the experiments.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Omission of State Evolution Analysis:** A major weakness is the absence of a state evolution (SE) analysis. The authors mention SE but omit it due to space constraints. For AMP-style algorithms, the SE analysis is not just a theoretical add-on; it is the core justification for the algorithm's correctness in the large system limit and the primary tool for predicting its performance. Without it, the algorithm's claimed properties (e.g., asymptotic exactness, O(1) convergence time) are unsubstantiated assertions. Including at least the final SE equations and a brief discussion of their implications would have significantly strengthened the paper's theoretical foundation.

2.  **Understated Computational Complexity:** The paper claims O(mN) complexity per iteration, which is true for the matrix-vector products. However, it glosses over the computational cost of the non-linear functions `f` and `G` (Eq. 10). For general priors, calculating these functions involves an `r̂`-dimensional integration or optimization for each of the `m+N` rows. This can be computationally prohibitive if the rank `r̂` is not very small. While this is tractable for the specific case of K-means (a discrete search over `r̂` options), the paper presents the general algorithm as being broadly applicable, and this potential bottleneck for more complex priors should be more explicitly discussed.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Related AMP Literature:** The authors briefly mention recent related work on AMP for bilinear problems ([13], [14]) but state that "a detailed comparison... remains to be made." This is a missed opportunity. It is crucial to position the current work more clearly with respect to these highly relevant papers. For instance, how does the fixed-rank `r` assumption in this paper's derivation differ in its results and applicability from the fixed-ratio `r/m` assumption in other works? A more detailed discussion would help clarify the specific contributions and novelty of this paper's formulation.

4.  **Ad-hoc Parameter Estimation:** The noise variance parameter `τ` is a key part of the model, but in the clustering experiments, it is estimated heuristically at each iteration. This is a pragmatic choice, but it deviates from the theoretical derivation which assumes `τ` is known. The authors should discuss the potential impact of this ad-hoc estimation on the algorithm's convergence and performance guarantees. A more principled Bayesian treatment might involve placing a prior on `τ` and incorporating its update into the message-passing scheme.

### Detailed Comments

*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The derivation of the AMP-KM algorithm from the general framework is particularly clear.
*   **Proposition 1:** The statement that AMP has "not more fixed points than the ICM algorithm" is very interesting. It suggests AMP is a more restrictive, and potentially better, iterative scheme. This is a strong selling point that is well-supported by the empirical results.
*   **Initialization:** The paper notes that for the real data experiment, the K-means++ initialization was needed to avoid poor solutions. This is an important practical detail. It suggests that while AMP-KM is better at local search, it is still sensitive to initialization, similar to other non-convex optimization algorithms.

In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper with a novel algorithmic contribution and strong empirical validation. The unified AMP framework for matrix factorization is elegant, and its application to clustering yields a new state-of-the-art algorithm. The primary weaknesses are the lack of a formal state evolution analysis and a somewhat incomplete discussion of computational complexity and related work. These weaknesses are significant but could potentially be addressed in a final version. The strengths of the paper, particularly the novel clustering algorithm and its impressive performance, are substantial.
