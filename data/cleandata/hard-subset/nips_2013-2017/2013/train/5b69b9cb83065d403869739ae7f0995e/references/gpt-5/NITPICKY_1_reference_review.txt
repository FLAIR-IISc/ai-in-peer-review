Summary
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with two main claims: (i) a general β-parameterized AMP framework that covers both posterior mean (β=1) and MAP (β→∞) estimation, extending prior AMP work that focused on posterior means; and (ii) an AMP-based K-means-type clustering algorithm that enforces one-hot assignments and allegedly improves upon Lloyd’s algorithm, especially in high dimensions. The AMP derivation yields an iterative scheme (Algorithm 2) that depends on functions f and G tied to the assumed priors on U and V. For clustering, the paper specializes to an improper uniform prior on U and a discrete prior on V over {e1,…,er̂}, leading to Algorithm 3, which resembles Lloyd’s updates but with AMP-style (Onsager-like) corrections in the assignment step. Synthetic and ORL face experiments suggest lower K-means loss than Lloyd/K-means++ and higher accuracy than a variational Bayes matrix factorization baseline when true priors are known.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on AMP/GAMP for linear models [Donoho et al. 2009; Rangan 2011], rank-one bilinear AMP (IterFac), and general-rank bilinear AMP [Matsushita & Tanaka 2012; Parker et al. 2013]. It argues novelty in enabling MAP estimation within an AMP framework and in handling exact one-hot constraints for clustering via low-rank factorization.

Strengths
- Unifying β-parameterization is conceptually appealing, providing a single algorithmic template spanning posterior means and MAP.
- Identifies K-means as a special case of the bilinear model with a discrete prior on V and derives a principled AMP variant with bias/size corrections in assignments.
- Empirically promising: on synthetic data, AMP-KM reduces K-means loss relative to Lloyd/K-means++; AMP for maximum-accuracy clustering attains high accuracy and fast convergence. On ORL, AMP-KM outperforms K-means++ in most trials in both K-means loss and accuracy.
- Clear intuition for the correction terms (de-biasing current cluster center and preferring smaller clusters) is helpful.

Weaknesses and detailed issues
Technical correctness and derivation clarity
- Missing state evolution and convergence theory. The paper asserts SE can be derived “like” prior work but omits it entirely and provides no stability guidance (e.g., damping, step-size control), despite noting oscillatory behavior and ad-hoc stopping (Vt=Vt−2). For a NeurIPS paper on AMP, at least a sketch of SE or a rigorous high-level justification is expected.
- Proposition 1 appears overstated or underspecified. Claiming U∞ (resp. V∞) is a global minimizer of CMAP(·,V∞) (resp. CMAP(U∞,·)) requires convexity in U (resp. V). This demands −log p̂u (resp. −log p̂v) convex; otherwise global optimality does not follow. The proposition and its assumptions should explicitly state convexity of the negative log-priors and clarify PSD conditions and the role of the “negative pseudometric” term in (16), which makes the minimand smaller and does not regularize in the usual (majorization) sense.
- Algorithm 3 scaling inconsistencies. Equations (17a)–(17b) for Λtu and the subsequent uses of St appear to have missing or inverted factors of m and τ relative to the specialization of (9). As written, Λtu = mτ (Vt)⊤Vt conflicts with the Λ form in (9) (which would give Λtu = (1/(mτ))(Vt)⊤Vt when T terms vanish). The later rewrite (18) yields assignment penalties proportional to m/nl and independent of τ, which is plausible, but the path from (17) to (18) is opaque and dimensionally confusing. This needs correction and a self-contained derivation.
- Limit β→∞ and the use of G∞. The paper uses G∞ = (Λ − H)−1 via the implicit function theorem “under some assumptions” without stating them (smoothness, strict convexity near the minimizer, etc.). For discrete p̂v this limit is singular; the paper hand-waves that G∞ is O almost everywhere but does not reconcile this with the β<∞ case or with the needed continuity assumptions.

Experimental design and reproducibility
- The “maximum accuracy” experiments use the true priors, which gives AMP-MA and VBMF-MA an advantage but is unrealistic; there is no robustness study under mis-specified priors, despite this being a stated motivation. Moreover, how VBMF incorporates discrete p̂v over one-hots is not specified; if [4] assumes Gaussian priors only, what exact VB baseline was implemented?
- Important baselines are missing: no comparison to BiG-AMP/BiG-AMP-MAP (Parker et al. 2013) or to EM-VAMP-style methods, which are highly relevant for bilinear models and can produce MAP/ML estimates. For clustering on high-dimensional data, spectral K-means (PCA+K-means) and spherical K-means are standard baselines; using raw pixels on ORL is a weak baseline.
- ORL failure modes: AMP-KM collapsed to a single cluster in 2/50 runs—this is a serious failure for a clustering method. The paper should describe mitigation (e.g., damping, re-seeding empty clusters) and report failure rates across settings.
- Stopping and initialization: For synthetic experiments, AMP methods use random initialization, while K-means++ benefits from careful seeding; but on ORL, AMP-KM is given K-means++ seeding because random init was unstable. This inconsistency complicates fairness. Provide results with identical seeding schemes across methods where possible.
- Reporting: No run-time or wall-clock comparisons; only iteration counts are given. The per-iteration cost of computing f/G (esp. for general priors) can be significant; report actual times. Parameter choices (e.g., damping, β used for MAP approximation if finite) are not documented. The threshold 1e−15 for convergence is unrealistically strict and may be numerically meaningless.

Claims and positioning
- The claim of being “the first to directly deal with the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster” within low-rank reconstruction is too strong. There is a large literature on matrix factorization formulations of K-means and hard assignments (e.g., equivalences of K-means to trace maximization / discrete factorization; hard clustering via NMF variants). Please precisely delimit the novelty relative to semi-/convex NMF, discrete assignment MF, and BiG-AMP-based bilinear inference.
- The discussion of differences from [13,14] is brief. Since BiG-AMP is designed for bilinear problems, a more careful comparison (modeling assumptions, limits, estimators supported) is necessary.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typos and formatting errors: “low-rankedness,” inconsistent transposes (V ⊤0 vs V ⊤ 0), inconsistent spacing and punctuation, misspellings (“informaion”), broken reference formatting (“, ,”), odd date ranges (ICML “Jul. 5– Aug. 9, 2008”). Some references lack full details. These detract from clarity.
- Key derivations are deferred to the Supplementary Material, which is essential to judge correctness but not included here. The main text should at least present a self-contained specialization to K-means with correct constants.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros
- Interesting extension of AMP to MAP via β-parameterization.
- Novel AMP correction terms for K-means with intuitive interpretation.
- Promising empirical improvements in K-means loss and accuracy on synthetic data; strong performance on ORL in most runs.

Cons
- Derivation gaps and likely typos in core equations; lack of state evolution or convergence analysis.
- Overstated proposition without clear assumptions.
- Incomplete and somewhat unfair experimental evaluation; serious failure modes not addressed.
- Weak baselines for clustering; missing comparisons to closely related bilinear AMP methods.
- Many presentation/formatting issues.

Recommendation
Reject. While the ideas are promising, the paper in its current form has too many technical and presentation deficiencies: unclear/incorrect constants in the specialized algorithm, missing theoretical justification, incomplete experimental validation against the most relevant baselines, and nontrivial failure modes. I encourage the authors to (i) fix the derivations (especially Algorithm 3 and its link to (18)), (ii) include at least a sketch of state evolution or rigorous convergence conditions, (iii) compare to BiG-AMP/EM-BiG-AMP and stronger clustering baselines, (iv) address stability (damping, re-seeding) and robustness to prior mis-specification, and (v) substantially clean up the writing and references. With these improvements, the paper could make a solid contribution. Confidence: high.
