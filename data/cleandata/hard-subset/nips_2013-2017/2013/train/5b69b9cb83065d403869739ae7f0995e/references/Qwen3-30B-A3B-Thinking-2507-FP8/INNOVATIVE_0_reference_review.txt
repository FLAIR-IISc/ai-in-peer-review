

# Review: Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing

This paper proposes a novel application of approximate message passing (AMP) to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, extending previous AMP work in compressed sensing to matrix factorization problems. The authors develop an AMP-based algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction, which they then apply to K-means clustering. The key novelty lies in their extension of the AMP framework to handle MAP estimation (previously only posterior mean estimation was possible), and their application to clustering with a direct handling of the single-cluster-assignment constraint.

The paper makes two significant contributions. First, it extends the AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction to handle MAP estimation (previously only posterior mean estimation was possible in [9]), which is a substantial advancement. Second, it presents a novel clustering algorithm that directly addresses the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework, which the authors convincingly argue is the first such approach.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong theoretical foundation. The authors derive their algorithm by introducing a one-parameter extension of the posterior distribution, allowing unified treatment of marginalization and MAP problems. They provide a detailed analysis of the algorithm's properties, including computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration), its relationship to variational Bayes methods, and a theoretical guarantee for the MAP case (Proposition 1). The numerical experiments on both synthetic and real data (ORL Face Database) demonstrate that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ in terms of both clustering accuracy and K-means loss, with comparable or faster convergence.

The paper's novelty is substantial. While low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering has been explored before (citing [10, 11]), the authors' approach of directly handling the single-cluster-assignment constraint within the AMP framework represents a significant conceptual advance. The extension of AMP to MAP estimation for low-rank matrices is also novel, as [9] only handled posterior mean estimation. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from related approaches like [13, 14], particularly in their treatment of rank scaling and the relaxation of independence assumptions between factors.

The significance of this work is high. Clustering is a fundamental problem in machine learning with widespread applications, and the proposed AMP-based approach offers a more theoretically grounded and computationally efficient alternative to existing methods. The experimental results on high-dimensional data (where traditional K-means struggles) demonstrate the practical value of the approach.

Weaknesses include some limited convergence analysis (though the authors note it's beyond the scope) and relatively modest experimental validation (only one real dataset). However, as a reviewer prioritizing novelty over methodological perfection, I'm willing to overlook these limitations given the paper's significant conceptual contributions.

The paper's main strength is its bold, novel approach that extends the successful AMP framework to a new domain with a meaningful generalization (MAP estimation). This represents a clear conceptual advance rather than an incremental improvement. The authors demonstrate how AMP can be fruitfully applied to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering in a way that addresses a key constraint (single-cluster assignment) that previous approaches struggled with.

The paper's contributions align well with NIPS' focus on novel algorithms and theoretical advances. The work builds on but significantly extends the AMP framework that has been successfully applied to compressed sensing [6, 7, 8, 9], demonstrating its broader applicability to matrix factorization problems. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the practical results on clustering are compelling.

I find this paper highly significant for the machine learning community. It introduces a novel algorithmic framework that advances the state of the art in both low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, with clear theoretical and empirical support. The authors' extension of AMP to handle MAP estimation for low-rank matrices is particularly noteworthy, as it opens up new possibilities for Bayesian inference in matrix factorization problems.

While the paper could benefit from more extensive experiments on additional datasets and a deeper theoretical analysis of convergence properties, these are minor limitations compared to the substantial novelty and significance of the core contributions. The work clearly represents a significant step forward in the field, offering both theoretical insights and practical algorithmic improvements.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel extension of AMP to handle MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction
- First algorithm that directly handles the single-cluster-assignment constraint within low-rank matrix reconstruction framework
- Strong theoretical foundation with meaningful guarantees
- Demonstrated superior performance in numerical experiments, especially for high-dimensional data
- Clear connection between theoretical framework and practical application

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Limited convergence analysis beyond Proposition 1
- Relatively modest experimental validation (one real dataset)
- Some technical details could be clarified (e.g., the role of parameter τ)

Given the paper's significant novelty, strong theoretical foundation, and compelling empirical results, I recommend acceptance. The work represents a clear conceptual advance that will likely influence future research in matrix factorization and clustering, which aligns perfectly with NIPS' focus on novel, impactful contributions to machine learning.
