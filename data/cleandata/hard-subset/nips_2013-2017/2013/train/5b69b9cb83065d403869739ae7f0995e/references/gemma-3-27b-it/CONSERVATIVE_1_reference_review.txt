This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The core idea is to extend existing AMP approaches to handle the broader problem of low-rank decomposition with potentially complex priors on the factor matrices, and to leverage this for clustering by formulating it as a specific instance of the reconstruction problem. The authors claim improvements over standard K-means, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

The work builds upon established foundations in AMP and Bayesian matrix factorization. The connection to compressed sensing and prior work on Iterative Factorization and AMP for low-rank problems (references [8, 9]) is clearly stated.  The attempt to unify marginalization and MAP estimation within a single framework via a beta parameter is a reasonable approach. The connection to clustering is fairly standard – it’s well-known that K-means can be framed as a low-rank problem with specific constraints – but the authors aim to improve upon existing algorithms within that framework.

However, my overall assessment is skeptical. While the ideas are not *incorrect*, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and, crucially, lacks convincing evidence of substantial improvement over established methods. The proposed algorithm’s advantages appear marginal, and the presentation doesn't sufficiently justify the complexity introduced by the AMP approach compared to simpler, well-understood alternatives like Lloyd's K-means or variational Bayesian methods. 

A major weakness is the limited experimental validation.  The numerical experiments, while present, are not entirely persuasive. The comparison to K-means++, a well-tuned variant of standard K-means, is crucial, yet the reported improvements are relatively small.  The claim of outperforming K-means++ in 48/50 trials on the ORL faces dataset is interesting, but the magnitude of the improvement (a few percentage points in accuracy) doesn’t feel substantial enough to warrant significant attention given the added algorithmic complexity. The reliance on a single dataset and the lack of discussion around statistical significance of the observed improvements are concerning. A more thorough comparison against state-of-the-art clustering algorithms and a wider range of datasets would be necessary.

The theoretical justification, while alluded to through mention of state evolution (but not presented due to space constraints), feels incomplete.  The authors state that Proposition 1 guarantees optimality under certain conditions but do not fully explain how these conditions are verified in practice.  The discussion of the beta parameter and its role in transitioning between marginalization and MAP estimation is reasonable, but more detailed analysis of its impact on performance would be valuable.

The paper's clarity suffers somewhat from the density of the mathematical notation and the lack of intuitive explanations for some of the algorithmic steps.  The connection between the theoretical derivations and the practical implementation could be made more explicit.  For example, the justification for scaling the noise variance by 'm' feels somewhat arbitrary and is not fully explained in a way that a reader unfamiliar with the related literature can easily grasp.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound algorithm based on established principles (AMP and Bayesian inference).
* It attempts to unify different problems (marginalization, MAP estimation, clustering) within a single framework.
* The experiments suggest some improvement over standard K-means, especially in specific settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The novelty is limited – the application of AMP to this problem is a natural extension of existing work.
* The experimental results are not compelling enough to demonstrate a significant advantage over established methods, particularly K-means++.
* The theoretical analysis is incomplete and lacks practical validation.
* The clarity of the presentation could be improved.
* The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to relevant state-of-the-art algorithms.



**Overall, I recommend rejection.** While the paper is not fundamentally flawed, it lacks the originality, significance, and convincing empirical evidence required for acceptance at NIPS. The incremental improvements do not justify the added complexity of the proposed algorithm, and the theoretical contributions are not fully developed. I would suggest that the authors conduct a more extensive experimental evaluation, provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis (including the state evolution analysis mentioned), and clearly demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods before resubmitting this work.
