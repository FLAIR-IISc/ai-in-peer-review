## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable extension of Approximate Message Passing (AMP) to Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and its application to clustering. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and builds upon a strong foundation of existing AMP literature. While not a revolutionary breakthrough, the contribution is significant, offering a flexible and efficient algorithm with promising theoretical properties and demonstrated empirical advantages, particularly in high-dimensional settings. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of reconstructing a low-rank matrix from noisy observations, leveraging a Bayesian framework.  It extends existing AMP algorithms for low-rank reconstruction (specifically building on [9]) to handle MAP estimation alongside posterior mean estimation. Crucially, the authors connect this reconstruction problem to the K-means clustering problem, proposing a novel clustering algorithm based on AMP.

The paper accurately positions itself within the existing literature. It correctly identifies the limitations of traditional Bayesian methods (computational cost) and highlights the benefits of AMP for large-scale problems. The connections to variational Bayes methods, particularly ICM, are well-articulated. The authors acknowledge recent advancements in AMP for related problems (dictionary learning, blind calibration) and appropriately distinguish their work by focusing on a fixed rank *r* and the non-independence assumption within the matrix factors, which is a key difference. The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Generality and Flexibility:** The Bayesian approach, coupled with the AMP implementation, provides significant flexibility in incorporating prior knowledge about the matrix factors (sparsity, non-negativity, etc.).  This is a key advantage over more rigid methods.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The extension of the existing AMP algorithm is mathematically sound, and the discussion of the state evolution (though not fully presented due to space constraints) demonstrates a strong theoretical understanding. The proposition regarding optimality adds further rigor.
* **Efficiency:** AMP’s computational efficiency (linear in the matrix size) is a major strength, particularly for large-scale problems where traditional Bayesian methods become intractable.
* **Novel Clustering Algorithm:** The application of the AMP framework to clustering is novel and addresses a key limitation of existing methods – the hard constraint of exclusive cluster assignment.
* **Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments demonstrate the superiority of the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm over Lloyd’s K-means, especially in high-dimensional settings. The results on the ORL face database further validate the practical effectiveness of the method.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized.  The notation is consistent, and the algorithm descriptions are clear and concise.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **State Evolution Omission:** While the authors acknowledge the importance of state evolution analysis for predicting performance, its absence from the paper is a notable omission. Including a sketch of the key results would significantly strengthen the theoretical contribution.
* **Convergence Guarantee:** The assumption of algorithm convergence is stated, but a formal proof or discussion of conditions for convergence is lacking.  This is a common issue with AMP algorithms, but acknowledging the potential for non-convergence would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The role of the noise parameter τ and its estimation are somewhat glossed over. A more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the algorithm to this parameter, and the robustness of the estimation method, would be valuable.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms (beyond K-means and K-means++) is limited.  Comparing against spectral clustering or more advanced matrix factorization-based clustering methods would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Computational Cost of 'f' and 'G':** While the overall computational complexity is low, the authors acknowledge the cost of computing the functions 'f' and 'G'. A more detailed discussion of strategies to mitigate this cost, especially for complex priors, would be helpful.

**4. Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and original contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
* The proposed algorithm is efficient and demonstrates promising empirical performance.
* The connection between low-rank reconstruction and clustering is novel and insightful.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a detailed state evolution analysis weakens the theoretical contribution.
* The convergence guarantee is not explicitly addressed.
* The limited comparison to other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms.



**5. Recommendations for Improvement:**

* **Include a brief sketch of the state evolution results.** Even a qualitative description of the scaling behavior would be beneficial.
* **Discuss conditions for convergence, or acknowledge the potential for non-convergence.**
* **Provide a more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity, particularly regarding the noise parameter τ.**
* **Expand the comparison to include more state-of-the-art clustering algorithms.**
* **Offer practical strategies for reducing the computational cost of functions 'f' and 'G' for complex priors.**




In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its impact and solidify its place in the NIPS proceedings.  I confidently recommend acceptance.
