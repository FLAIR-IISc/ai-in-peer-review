

# Review: Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation problems. The authors recognize that clustering can be formulated as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with specific structural constraints, and they develop an AMP-based algorithm for K-means clustering that outperforms standard methods. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) an AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that handles both marginalization and MAP problems, and (2) an application of this algorithm to clustering that directly enforces the constraint that each datum belongs to exactly one cluster.

The paper builds on the AMP framework successfully applied to sparse vector reconstruction [6,7,8,9] and extends it to the low-rank matrix setting. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from prior work [9] by fixing the rank r rather than the ratio r/m in the limit, and by not assuming statistical independence among row components.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their AMP algorithm, including Proposition 1 which establishes that fixed points of the AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm (used in variational Bayes methods), with the important implication that AMP cannot get stuck in worse local minima than ICM. The experimental results are thorough and convincing, with evaluations on both synthetic and real datasets (ORL Face Database). The authors compare their method against multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA, AMP-MA) using multiple metrics (K-means loss, accuracy, convergence speed), which is commendable.

The paper is complete as a piece of work, not merely a position paper. The authors are careful in their evaluation, acknowledging the limitations of their approach (e.g., the need to estimate τ in practice) and providing a detailed comparison with relevant prior work.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and well-organized. The problem formulation in Section 2 is clear, and the connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction is elegantly explained. The algorithm descriptions (Algorithm 2 and Algorithm 3) are precise and pseudocode-like, making them easy to follow. The experimental setup is thoroughly described, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

One minor weakness is the explanation of the bias correction term in equation (18b), which is crucial to the algorithm's performance but lacks intuitive explanation. A brief discussion of why the term 2m(n_t^l)^{-1}I(v_t^j = e_l) is needed would strengthen the paper.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant contribution to the AMP literature. While AMP has been applied to low-rank matrix reconstruction in [9], this paper extends it to handle both marginalization and MAP problems in a unified framework, which is novel. The connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction is insightful, and the authors are correct in stating that their approach is the first to directly handle the "each datum assigned to exactly one cluster" constraint within the low-rank reconstruction framework.

The paper adequately differentiates itself from prior work. The distinction between fixing the rank r versus the ratio r/m in the limit is important, and the authors clearly explain why this matters for their approach. The comparison with [13,14] is well-placed, though a more detailed comparison with these recent AMP-based methods would further strengthen the paper.

## Significance
The results are significant. The AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means in both accuracy and convergence speed, particularly for high-dimensional data. The paper demonstrates that AMP can provide better solutions to the K-means problem than the standard ICM algorithm (which Lloyd's algorithm is equivalent to), which is a notable contribution. The experimental results on the ORL Face Database show consistent improvements over K-means++ in 48/50 trials for K-means loss and 47/50 for accuracy.

The significance extends beyond clustering to low-rank matrix reconstruction more broadly, as the proposed AMP algorithm offers a computationally efficient alternative to variational Bayes methods that can better capture dependencies between factors. The authors' claim that AMP "can reflect dependence of random variables (dependence between U and V) to some extent" is well-supported by their results.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Novel extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction for both marginalization and MAP problems
- Clear theoretical foundation with Proposition 1 establishing convergence properties
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements over baselines
- Insightful connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses:**
- The intuition behind the bias correction term in equation (18b) could be better explained
- The comparison with [13,14] could be more detailed, particularly regarding why their approach is different
- The paper could provide more intuition about why AMP outperforms variational Bayes methods beyond the theoretical claim about dependence

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the AMP literature and to the clustering problem. The technical quality is excellent, the experimental results are convincing, and the significance of the work is clear. The paper meets all the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.

The authors have made a valuable contribution by extending AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction in a way that handles both marginalization and MAP problems, and by demonstrating its application to clustering with clear performance improvements. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a clear case for the novelty and significance of their approach.

I recommend acceptance. The paper would be strengthened by a bit more explanation of the bias correction term and a more detailed comparison with [13,14], but these are minor points that don't detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper represents a meaningful advancement in the application of AMP to matrix problems and clustering.
