

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The authors claim to extend previous AMP work for matrix reconstruction to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation, and to provide a novel clustering algorithm that outperforms traditional methods.

The paper's main contribution appears to be an AMP-based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that can handle both marginalization and MAP estimation through a parameter β. The authors then apply this to clustering by treating it as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction. They claim their AMP-based clustering algorithm is the first to directly handle the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank framework.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a logical progression from problem formulation to algorithm derivation to experimental validation. The theoretical justification for the AMP approach is somewhat limited, with the authors acknowledging they omit the state evolution analysis due to space constraints. The connection between the AMP framework and clustering is presented as novel, though the paper acknowledges prior work on low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering [10, 11].

The main weaknesses of the paper are significant:

1. **Insufficient novelty claim**: The paper builds on existing AMP work for compressed sensing and matrix reconstruction [6, 8, 9], but doesn't convincingly argue why their extension is necessary or superior to established methods. The claim that their algorithm is "the first" to handle the cluster assignment constraint is questionable, as standard K-means algorithms already satisfy this constraint.

2. **Inadequate experimental validation**: The numerical experiments show marginal improvements over existing methods. In the artificial data experiments, the AMP-KM algorithm achieves slightly lower K-means loss (0.012 difference) and AMP-MA achieves higher accuracy (0.055 difference) than the next best method. In the real data experiments, the improvements are even smaller (0.012 in K-means loss, 0.055 in accuracy). These modest improvements don't justify the complexity of the AMP approach, especially when the computational cost per iteration is O(mN) - the same as standard K-means algorithms.

3. **Missing relevant baselines**: The authors compare to Lloyd's K-means and K-means++, but fail to include more recent clustering algorithms or the variational Bayes approach they reference as a baseline. The paper claims to outperform variational Bayes matrix factorization for maximum accuracy clustering, but doesn't provide a fair comparison with the most recent implementations of this method.

4. **Theoretical justification**: While the paper presents Proposition 1 about the optimality of fixed points, it doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the AMP algorithm actually converges to better solutions than existing methods. The claim that AMP avoids getting stuck in bad local minima is not substantiated by the experimental results.

5. **Mischaracterization of problem**: The paper claims clustering is a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction, but this is a standard approach in the literature [10, 11]. The novelty claim would be stronger if they demonstrated a fundamental difference in the problem formulation that justifies the AMP approach.

The paper has some strengths: the algorithm derivation is technically sound, the connection between the AMP framework and Bayesian inference is well-explained, and the experimental design is reasonable. However, the marginal improvements over established methods, combined with the lack of compelling theoretical justification for why AMP is better suited for this problem, significantly weaken the paper's contribution.

In my view, this paper represents an incremental extension of existing AMP work rather than a significant advance. The improvements claimed are modest and not convincingly justified by the experimental results. For a NIPS paper, the significance of the contribution appears insufficient to warrant acceptance.

The paper would be stronger with:
1. A more thorough comparison to relevant baselines (including recent clustering algorithms)
2. A clearer theoretical justification for why AMP is superior to existing approaches for this specific problem
3. More compelling evidence that the marginal improvements are meaningful in practical applications
4. A more accurate characterization of how their method differs from prior work on low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering

I am skeptical about the significance of the proposed approach. While AMP has shown promise in other domains, this application to clustering doesn't demonstrate sufficient advantages over established methods to justify its novelty claim. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison to the most relevant baselines and a clearer explanation of why AMP is better suited for this problem than existing approaches. As it stands, the paper doesn't make a sufficiently strong case for its contribution to the field.
