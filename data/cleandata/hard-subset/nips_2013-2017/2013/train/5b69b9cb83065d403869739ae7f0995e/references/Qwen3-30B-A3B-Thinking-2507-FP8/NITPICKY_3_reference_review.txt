

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an AMP-based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering. The authors extend the AMP algorithm from [9] to handle MAP estimation, and apply it to K-means clustering, claiming this is the first method to directly handle the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a clear connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, which is valuable. The proposed AMP algorithm for K-means clustering shows promising empirical results, outperforming Lloyd's algorithm on both artificial and real data. The theoretical foundation of the AMP approach is well-motivated, and the paper provides a clear comparison with existing methods like variational Bayes.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising results, the paper contains several technical inconsistencies and minor but important issues that undermine its credibility:

1. **Notation and Formatting:** The paper inconsistently uses "r ≪ m, N" without defining the meaning of "≪" (is it a constant factor, logarithmic, etc.?). The noise model Wi,j ~ N(0, mτ) is unusual (typically variance would be τ without the factor of m), and while the paper claims this scaling is "to allow proper scaling in the limit," this justification is insufficient. The factor of m should be explained more thoroughly.

2. **Algorithmic Details:** In Algorithm 2, the definition of Λ^t_u = (1/mτ)(V^t)^T V^t + (1/βmτ)∑_j T^t_j - (1/mτ)∑_j T^t_j is redundant and can be simplified to Λ^t_u = (1/mτ)(V^t)^T V^t + (1/mτ)(1/β - 1)∑_j T^t_j. The paper should explain why this specific form is used rather than the simplified version.

3. **Parameter Ambiguity:** Algorithm 3 introduces a parameter τ that "does not exist in the K-means clustering problem," which is a significant issue. The paper claims τ "appears because m^{-2} ∑_i A_{ij}^2 S^t_i was estimated by τm^{-1} ∑_i S^t_i," but this is not justified, and the parameter's role should be clarified.

4. **Technical Claims:** The paper claims that "the AMP algorithm for the K-means clustering achieves the smallest K-means loss among the five algorithms" (Figure 1), but doesn't provide statistical significance of these results or confidence intervals. The paper should report p-values for the comparisons to establish whether the differences are meaningful.

5. **Proposition 1:** The paper states that "if ∑_i^m S^∞_i and ∑_j^N T^∞_j are positive semidefinite" then the fixed point is optimal. However, S^t_i and T^t_j are defined in terms of G(b,Λ; p̂), which is a covariance matrix (hence positive semidefinite by definition). Therefore, this condition is always satisfied, making the proposition trivial and uninteresting.

6. **Comparison with Prior Work:** The paper claims to be the first to "directly deal with the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster in the framework of low-rank matrix reconstruction," but references [10, 11] which already address this issue. The paper should better justify why their approach is different and superior.

7. **Missing Supplementary Material:** The paper repeatedly refers to "Supplementary Material" but doesn't include it, which is problematic for reviewers and prevents verification of claims.

The paper's claims about the AMP algorithm's advantages over variational Bayes (e.g., "performance of the AMP algorithm is better than that of the variational Bayes matrix factorization") are not substantiated with sufficient detail or comparison. The paper mentions "state evolution" but doesn't provide the relevant equations or discuss their validity, which is a significant omission for an AMP paper.

**Conclusion:** While the paper presents an interesting application of AMP to clustering, the technical inconsistencies, ambiguous notation, and lack of sufficient justification for key claims significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The minor but important details that are incorrect or poorly explained prevent the reader from fully trusting the paper's technical contributions. The empirical results are promising but lack statistical rigor.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper has promising ideas but contains multiple technical errors and inconsistencies that prevent it from being a solid contribution to the field. The minor flaws are substantial enough to outweigh the potential significance of the work.
