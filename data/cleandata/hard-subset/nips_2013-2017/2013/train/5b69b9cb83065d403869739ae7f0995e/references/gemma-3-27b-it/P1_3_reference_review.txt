## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary:**

This paper presents an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending existing AMP approaches to handle general rank and MAP estimation. The authors also demonstrate an application of this algorithm to K-means type clustering, framing it as a special case of the low-rank reconstruction problem with specific constraints. They claim performance improvements over Lloyd’s K-means, especially in high-dimensional settings, and offer a theoretical justification for the algorithm's optimality in certain conditions.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds on the growing body of work utilizing AMP for various inference problems, particularly stemming from Donoho et al. (2009) and Rangan (2011, 2012). It acknowledges prior AMP applications in low-rank settings (Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012; Rangan & Fletcher, 2012) and contrasts its contributions – enabling MAP estimation and a novel clustering application – with these. The work also references and positions itself relative to variational Bayesian matrix factorization methods (Salakhutdinov & Mnih, 2008; Lim & Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007) and the classic Lloyd’s K-means algorithm (Lloyd, 1982).  The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader landscape of low-rank methods and clustering algorithms, acknowledging connections to non-negative matrix factorization approaches (Xu et al., 2003; Ding et al., 2010).

**Strengths:**

* **Generality:**  Extending AMP to MAP estimation is a valuable contribution, increasing the algorithm's utility beyond posterior mean estimation.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The attempt to provide theoretical grounding, especially Proposition 1 regarding optimality under certain conditions, is commendable.  The connection to belief propagation and the potential for state evolution analysis (though not fully presented due to space constraints) suggest a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Novel Application to Clustering:**  Framing K-means clustering as a low-rank reconstruction problem with constraints is an insightful approach, and the authors’ adaptation of the AMP algorithm to this setting is a significant contribution.
* **Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments, particularly demonstrating performance gains over Lloyd's K-means and K-means++, especially with high-dimensional data, provide strong evidence for the algorithm's effectiveness.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the algorithm is clearly presented with a step-by-step breakdown, even if the derivation details are relegated to supplementary material.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited State Evolution Discussion:** The authors mention the potential for state evolution analysis but do not include it, hindering a complete theoretical understanding of the algorithm’s performance guarantees. This feels like a missed opportunity, given the emphasis on theoretical analysis.
* **Convergence Discussion:** While the paper touches on convergence, a more rigorous discussion of convergence properties would be beneficial. The ad-hoc stopping criterion used in the experiments (V_t = V_{t-1} or V_t = V_{t-2}) feels somewhat arbitrary.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The role and tuning of the parameter τ are not fully explored. Its estimation method, while described, feels a little superficial. A more detailed sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** The claim of O(mN) complexity per iteration is accurate, but a comparison to the complexity of alternative algorithms (e.g., variational Bayes methods or standard K-means) would provide more context.
* **Limited Comparison to Modern Clustering Algorithms:** While comparison to Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ is good, a comparison to more recent, sophisticated clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering, DBSCAN) would be informative, particularly on more complex datasets.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper presents a technically sound algorithm with reasonable theoretical justification and strong experimental support. The extension to MAP estimation and the novel clustering application increase the value of the contribution.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is mostly clear, although some sections (e.g., the discussion of Proposition 1) could benefit from further elaboration. The algorithmic presentation is excellent.
* **Originality:** The combination of extending AMP to MAP estimation and applying it to K-means clustering is original and presents a novel approach to these problems.
* **Significance:** The results are significant, demonstrating improvements over existing methods in certain scenarios. The algorithm has the potential to be useful in a variety of applications where low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering are important.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Presents a novel and theoretically grounded algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
* Demonstrates empirically superior performance compared to established methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
* Extends the applicability of AMP algorithms to a broader range of problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a full state evolution analysis limits the theoretical understanding.
* Convergence analysis is somewhat superficial.
* A more thorough sensitivity analysis of the key parameter τ is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The authors present a well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. While some aspects of the theoretical analysis could be strengthened, the originality and significance of the work justify acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend the authors expand the discussion on convergence, provide a more thorough analysis of parameter sensitivity, and consider including a more detailed discussion of computational complexity relative to competing methods.  Adding a brief explanation of the expected limitations of the algorithm would also be beneficial.
