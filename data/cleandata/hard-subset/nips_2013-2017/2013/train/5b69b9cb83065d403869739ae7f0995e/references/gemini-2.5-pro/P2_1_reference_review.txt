This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The authors make two primary contributions. First, they develop a general AMP framework for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that unifies the problems of marginal posterior estimation (for posterior means) and Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation through a single inverse temperature parameter, β. This extends previous AMP work which focused only on posterior mean estimation. Second, and more significantly, they apply this framework to the problem of K-means clustering. By formulating K-means as a specific instance of MAP estimation in a low-rank model, they derive a new, efficient clustering algorithm (AMP-KM) that demonstrably outperforms standard methods like Lloyd's algorithm.

### Quality
The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the AMP algorithm appears to be a valid extension of previous work in the field, building upon the principles of belief propagation on dense graphs. The theoretical justification for the AMP approach over alternatives like Variational Bayes (VB) or Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) is well-articulated. Specifically, the connection to the Bethe approximation (which captures more dependencies than the mean-field approximation used in VB) provides a strong rationale.

Proposition 1 is a key theoretical result, establishing that any fixed point of the proposed AMP algorithm for the MAP problem is also a fixed point of the ICM algorithm (of which Lloyd's algorithm is an instance). This suggests that the AMP algorithm has a potentially more favorable optimization landscape and may be less susceptible to poor local minima, a claim that is well-supported by the experimental results.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The use of both synthetic and real-world datasets provides strong evidence for the algorithm's effectiveness. The comparisons are made against appropriate and widely-used baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF). The performance metrics chosen (K-means loss, clustering accuracy, number of iterations) are standard and clearly demonstrate the advantages of the proposed method in the tested scenarios.

One minor weakness is the handling of the noise variance parameter τ in the clustering application. Since the K-means problem formulation does not inherently include noise, τ is an artifact of the AMP derivation. The authors propose a reasonable ad-hoc estimation scheme, but the paper does not analyze the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to this parameter, which could be an important practical consideration. Furthermore, the paper assumes convergence of the algorithm, noting that a proof via state evolution is beyond the scope. While this is acceptable for a conference paper, a brief discussion of potential convergence issues (e.g., oscillations, which are hinted at in the experimental setup) would have made the work more complete.

### Clarity
The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of first presenting the general low-rank matrix reconstruction problem, then showing how K-means clustering is a special case. This structure makes the motivation for the second part of the paper clear. The key equations for the proposed algorithms (Algorithm 2 and 3) are laid out clearly.

The interpretation of the AMP update rule for K-means clustering (Equation 18b) is particularly insightful. The explanation of the two additional "correction" terms—one that prevents a data point from being overly influenced by its own contribution to a cluster center, and another that favors assignment to smaller clusters—provides a clear intuition for why the AMP approach might escape the local minima that trap Lloyd's algorithm. This intuitive explanation is a significant strength of the paper.

The main text defers the full derivation of the AMP algorithm to the supplementary material. While this is standard practice, it makes the core algorithm (Algorithm 2) feel somewhat prescriptive. A bit more high-level intuition in the main text about how the specific form of the "correction terms" (the last terms in (9a) and (9c)) arises from the belief propagation framework would have been beneficial.

### Originality
The work is highly original. While AMP has been applied to matrix factorization before, this paper's contributions are novel and significant. The unification of marginal and MAP estimation via the β parameter is a valuable extension. The primary novelty lies in the application to clustering. To my knowledge, this is the first work to derive a K-means-like algorithm directly from the principled AMP framework. The resulting algorithm is not a minor heuristic modification of K-means; it is a fundamentally different update rule derived from first principles, which sets it apart from other works that simply connect clustering to low-rank factorization.

The paper adequately references related work in both Bayesian matrix factorization (VB, MCMC) and AMP, and it clearly delineates its contributions from prior AMP algorithms like [9], [13], and [14] by highlighting differences in problem settings and assumptions.

### Significance
The results presented are important and likely to have a significant impact. K-means clustering is a foundational and widely used algorithm, and improvements to it are of broad interest to the machine learning community. The proposed AMP-KM algorithm offers a compelling alternative that is computationally efficient (same O(mN) complexity per iteration) and empirically superior, particularly in the challenging high-dimensional settings tested. Researchers and practitioners working on clustering problems are likely to be interested in trying this method.

Furthermore, the paper's general framework (Algorithm 2) is flexible and can incorporate various structural priors on the factors U and V. This opens the door for developing novel AMP-based algorithms for other problems that can be framed as structured low-rank matrix reconstruction, such as sparse or non-negative factorizations. This work, therefore, not only provides a specific solution for clustering but also a template for future research in this area.

### Summary of Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel and Principled Clustering Algorithm:** Derives a new, highly effective K-means algorithm from the AMP framework, with a clear intuitive explanation for its improved performance.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Justification:** The connection to the Bethe free energy and the fixed-point analysis (Proposition 1) provide solid theoretical grounding for the algorithm's superiority over standard methods like ICM/Lloyd's.
3.  **Compelling Empirical Results:** Extensive experiments on synthetic and real data clearly demonstrate that the proposed algorithms achieve lower objective loss and higher clustering accuracy than strong baselines.
4.  **General and Flexible Framework:** The general algorithm for low-rank reconstruction is applicable beyond clustering and can incorporate a wide variety of priors.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Handling of the `τ` Parameter:** The noise parameter `τ` required by the algorithm is not part of the original K-means problem, and its estimation and the algorithm's sensitivity to it are not fully explored.
2.  **Convergence Not Formally Addressed:** The paper assumes convergence of the iterative algorithm, and a discussion of potential failure modes or oscillations is limited.
3.  **Derivation Details Omitted:** Key derivations are in the supplementary material, which makes the main paper slightly less self-contained.

Overall, this is an excellent paper. It presents a novel, well-motivated, and empirically powerful algorithm for a fundamental machine learning problem. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, which are largely avenues for future work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
