

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work on AMP for compressed sensing. The authors present a unified framework that can handle both marginalization problems and MAP estimation through a single parameter β. They then apply this framework to clustering by formulating K-means clustering as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction. The paper claims their AMP-based clustering algorithm is the first to directly handle the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. The authors provide theoretical analysis, derive the algorithm, and demonstrate its effectiveness on both artificial and real datasets.

## Strengths
1. **Technical Contribution**: The paper successfully extends AMP to general-rank low-rank matrix reconstruction, handling both marginalization and MAP estimation in a unified way. The theoretical properties (Proposition 1) provide valuable insights into the algorithm's behavior.

2. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the problem setting, algorithm derivation, and theoretical properties. The algorithm descriptions are precise and the mathematical formulations are well-motivated.

3. **Empirical Results**: The numerical experiments demonstrate that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes methods in terms of accuracy and convergence speed on both artificial and real datasets (ORL Faces). The comparison with multiple baselines (K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA, AMP-MA) is comprehensive.

4. **Practical Relevance**: The algorithm's linear computational complexity (O(mN)) and fast convergence make it attractive for practical applications, particularly in high-dimensional settings where the authors demonstrate superior performance.

## Weaknesses
1. **Novelty Claims**: The claim that their clustering approach is "the first that directly deals with the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster in the framework of low-rank matrix reconstruction" lacks sufficient justification. Previous work like [10, 11] already formulates clustering as low-rank matrix reconstruction, and the paper doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs from these in terms of handling the cluster assignment constraint.

2. **Limited Scalability Analysis**: While the paper claims linear computational complexity, it doesn't provide concrete runtime comparisons with other methods on large-scale datasets. The artificial data experiments (m=800, N=1600) are relatively small by modern standards, making it difficult to assess scalability to truly massive datasets.

3. **Parameter Sensitivity**: The paper doesn't thoroughly analyze how sensitive the algorithm is to parameter choices (particularly β in the clustering context where β=1 or β→∞), which could be critical for practical implementation.

4. **Rank Estimation**: The approach assumes the rank r is known, which is a significant limitation in real-world applications where the true rank is typically unknown. The paper doesn't discuss how to estimate the rank or handle rank mismatch.

5. **Convergence Analysis**: While the paper mentions convergence is not guaranteed in general, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of convergence properties or conditions under which convergence is likely.

## Assessment

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with well-justified theoretical contributions. The numerical experiments provide strong empirical support for the proposed methods. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations. However, the claimed novelty of the clustering approach requires stronger justification.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written with logical organization and precise mathematical formulations. The algorithm descriptions are particularly well-structured. The supplementary material appears to contain additional details that would enhance the main paper.

**Originality**: The AMP-based approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction is novel and well-motivated. However, the application to clustering is somewhat incremental given existing work on low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with [10, 11] to clarify the novelty.

**Significance**: The proposed algorithm addresses an important problem with practical relevance, and the empirical results suggest it could be useful in high-dimensional clustering applications. The linear computational complexity and faster convergence compared to existing methods are significant advantages.

## Recommendation
The paper presents a technically sound contribution with clear theoretical foundations and promising empirical results. While the novelty claim for the clustering application needs strengthening, the core AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction is novel and well-justified. The paper is suitable for NIPS with minor revisions.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement
1. Strengthen the novelty claim for the clustering application by providing a detailed comparison with [10, 11] that explicitly explains how the authors' approach handles the cluster assignment constraint differently.

2. Include a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's sensitivity to parameter choices (particularly β in the clustering context) and how to select appropriate values.

3. Discuss how to handle the case when the rank r is unknown, perhaps by including a brief discussion of rank estimation techniques.

4. Provide more detailed scalability analysis with larger datasets to better demonstrate the linear complexity claim.

5. Add a brief discussion of the convergence conditions and behavior in the paper, rather than relegating it to the supplementary material.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a solid technical contribution that would be valuable to the NIPS community, particularly for researchers working on low-rank matrix methods and clustering. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
