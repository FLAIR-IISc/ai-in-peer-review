### Summary

This paper presents an algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. The core contributions are twofold. First, it generalizes a previous AMP algorithm for this problem, extending it from posterior mean estimation to a broader framework that includes MAP estimation, unified via an inverse temperature parameter β. Second, it applies this generalized algorithm to K-means clustering by formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one of the factor matrices consists of one-hot vectors representing cluster assignments. The resulting AMP-based clustering algorithm is shown empirically to outperform standard Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ on both synthetic and real-world high-dimensional data.

### Review

This paper tackles an important problem and the proposed AMP-based approach for clustering shows promising results. The connection between the fixed points of the proposed MAP algorithm and the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm is an interesting theoretical insight. However, the paper is marred by a significant number of flaws, ranging from notational inconsistencies and presentational weaknesses to a lack of technical rigor in key areas. These issues collectively undermine the quality of the work and prevent a confident recommendation for acceptance.

#### Strengths

*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its experimental results. The proposed AMP-K-means algorithm consistently outperforms Lloyd's K-means and the well-regarded K-means++ initialization method in terms of the final K-means loss on both synthetic and real datasets. This suggests the algorithm is effective at navigating the non-convex optimization landscape and finding better local minima.
*   **Novel Algorithmic Contribution:** The derivation of a K-means-like algorithm from the principled AMP framework is novel. The resulting update rule (Eq. 18b) provides an intuitive modification to the standard assignment step, incorporating terms that can be interpreted as correcting for the bias of a cluster center towards its current members, potentially helping the algorithm escape poor local optima.
*   **Flexible Framework:** The general AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is flexible due to its Bayesian formulation, capable of incorporating various structural priors on the factor matrices U and V, making it applicable beyond the specific clustering application demonstrated.

#### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish and clarity in several key areas.
    *   **Notational Ambiguity:** There is a critical inconsistency in the notation for the matrix `U0`. In Section 2.1, `U0` is defined as `(u0,1, . . . ,u0,m)⊤`, where `u0,i` are `r`-dimensional vectors, implying `u0,i` are the *rows* of `U0`. However, in the clustering formulation in Section 2.2, `ũ0,l` is defined as the l-th *column* of `U0` and represents a cluster center in `R^m`. These definitions are contradictory and create significant confusion.
    *   **Parameter Definition:** The noise variance is defined as `mτ`, with `τ` being called a "noise variance parameter." This is unconventional and confusing. It is unclear if the variance is `τ` and is scaled by `m`, or if the variance is `mτ`. This should be clarified.
    *   **Deferred/Omitted Derivations:** The derivation of the main AMP algorithm is entirely deferred to the supplementary material, leaving the reader with little intuition. Furthermore, the crucial step of simplifying Algorithm 2 to obtain the K-means variant in Eq. (18) is not shown at all. This hinders the paper's self-containedness and reproducibility.
    *   **Comparison to Prior Work:** The comparison to related AMP literature [13, 14] in the introduction is cursory. The statement "A detailed comparison among these algorithms remains to be made" is an admission of incomplete scholarship and is not acceptable for a top-tier publication.
    *   **Reference Formatting:** The bibliography is exceptionally sloppy. Multiple entries contain stray commas (e.g., [1], [2], [3]), formatting is inconsistent, and a URL is appended unformatted to reference [20]. This lack of care in presentation reflects poorly on the manuscript as a whole.

2.  **Technical Rigor and Justification:**
    *   **Convergence:** The paper explicitly states that convergence of the algorithm is not guaranteed and is simply assumed. The experiments reveal this is a real practical problem, forcing the authors to employ an ad-hoc stopping criterion (`V t = V t−2`) to handle oscillations. This is a major weakness. A principled discussion of AMP convergence issues and potential remedies (e.g., damping) is missing.
    *   **The `τ` Parameter in K-means:** The K-means problem is deterministic, yet the proposed algorithm depends on a noise variance parameter `τ`. The authors propose an ad-hoc, iteration-dependent estimation for `τ`. This feels unprincipled and lacks theoretical justification. The sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to this estimation scheme is not analyzed.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** The paper mentions that the algorithm's performance can be predicted by "state evolution" equations, a key strength of the AMP framework. However, these equations are never presented, nor are they used to analyze or validate the experimental results. This is a significant missed opportunity to add theoretical weight to the empirical findings.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   While the comparison to K-means variants is positive, the scope is limited. To claim a significant advance in clustering, the proposed method should be compared against a broader set of strong baselines, such as Gaussian Mixture Models (via EM) or spectral clustering, especially on the real-world dataset.

#### Conclusion

While the paper presents a promising direction for clustering algorithms, its execution is flawed. The strong empirical results are not sufficiently supported by clear presentation and rigorous technical justification. The numerous issues—ranging from critical notational errors and sloppy formatting to the lack of convergence guarantees and unprincipled parameter estimation—prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper requires a major revision to address these shortcomings before it can be considered a solid contribution to the field.

**Recommendation:** Reject.
