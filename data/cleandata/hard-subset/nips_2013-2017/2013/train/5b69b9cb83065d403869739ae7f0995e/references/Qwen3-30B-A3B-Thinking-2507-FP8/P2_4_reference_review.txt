

# Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that extends previous work to handle both marginalization problems and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors derive a unified framework that can solve both the marginalization and MAP problems through a single parameter β. They then apply this framework to K-means clustering, creating a novel clustering algorithm that directly addresses the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster. The paper includes theoretical analysis, algorithm derivation, and numerical experiments on both artificial and real datasets demonstrating superior performance compared to existing methods like Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes matrix factorization.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound and presents a well-motivated extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction. The theoretical foundation is solid, with the derivation of Algorithm 2 following established AMP principles. The authors provide Proposition 1, which establishes a useful optimality property of their algorithm for the MAP problem, showing that fixed points of their AMP algorithm correspond to fixed points of the ICM algorithm. The numerical experiments are thorough, including both artificial data (with 500 problem instances across various ranks) and real data (ORL Database of Faces). The performance metrics (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, convergence speed) are well-chosen and the results consistently demonstrate advantages of the proposed method.

The paper acknowledges limitations appropriately, noting that convergence is not guaranteed in general and that the algorithm requires an initial value. The comparison with existing methods is comprehensive, including Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, variational Bayes matrix factorization, and the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem setting is precisely defined, and the relationship to previous work is carefully explained. The algorithmic description (Algorithm 2) is detailed and well-structured, with clear explanations of the components and their relationships. The connection between the general AMP framework and the specific application to clustering (Algorithm 3) is logically presented and easy to follow.

The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the supplementary material (mentioned but not included in the main paper) likely provides additional detail for the derivation. The paper effectively uses figures (Figure 1 and Figure 2) to present experimental results, which complement the textual description of findings.

## Originality
The paper makes two significant original contributions. First, it extends the AMP framework to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction, whereas previous work [9] only treated posterior mean estimation. Second, it applies this framework to clustering in a novel way that directly handles the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster, which the authors claim is the first such method in the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

The paper distinguishes itself clearly from related work [13, 14] by explaining their different approach to the rank parameter (fixing r rather than r/m) and by not assuming statistical independence among components of the factor matrices. The comparison with related work is thorough, and the authors appropriately cite and position their work within the literature.

## Significance
The paper addresses a practically important problem (low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering) with a method that offers both theoretical guarantees and empirical improvements over existing approaches. The demonstrated superiority in accuracy and convergence speed (particularly for high-dimensional data) makes this work potentially impactful for practitioners working on clustering and matrix factorization problems.

The AMP framework is known for its computational efficiency and theoretical properties, so extending it to this broader class of problems is significant. The results on the ORL Database of Faces show that the method has practical utility beyond synthetic data, which adds to its significance.

## Weaknesses
While the paper is strong overall, it has a few limitations. First, the theoretical analysis is somewhat limited—while Proposition 1 provides a useful property, the paper doesn't provide a full convergence analysis or error bounds for the AMP algorithm. Second, the numerical experiments, while comprehensive, could be expanded to include more real-world datasets to better demonstrate generalizability. Third, the paper doesn't fully explain why their algorithm converges faster than the variational Bayes approach despite noting that variational Bayes typically shows slower convergence than ICM.

## Recommendation
This paper makes a solid contribution to the field of low-rank matrix factorization and clustering. The proposed AMP-based algorithm is theoretically grounded, well-motivated, and empirically validated. It offers a meaningful extension to existing AMP methods and provides a novel application to clustering that addresses a key constraint not handled by previous approaches.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a significant contribution that advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. While the theoretical analysis could be expanded, the empirical results provide strong evidence of the method's effectiveness.

I recommend acceptance at NeurIPS as this work is of high quality, original, and significant to the community.

## Summary of Arguments
**For Acceptance:**
- Significant extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation
- Novel application to clustering that directly handles cluster assignment constraints
- Strong theoretical foundation with Proposition 1
- Comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating superior performance
- Clear distinction from and comparison to related work
- Practical utility demonstrated on real-world dataset

**Against Acceptance:**
- Limited theoretical analysis (convergence analysis not provided)
- Only one real-world dataset used for validation
- Some details about implementation (e.g., choosing τ) could be expanded

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at NeurIPS.
