This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The key contributions are twofold: first, extending a previous AMP framework for this problem from only estimating posterior means to a more general Bayesian inference setting that includes MAP estimation; second, applying this framework to the problem of K-means clustering, which results in a new, high-performing clustering algorithm. The authors demonstrate through numerical experiments that their proposed clustering algorithm outperforms standard methods like Lloyd's K-means and K-means++.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a well-founded extension of prior work in the AMP literature. The use of an inverse temperature parameter `β` to unify the treatment of marginalization (`β=1`) and MAP estimation (`β→∞`) is an elegant and standard technique from statistical physics that is appropriately applied here.

The derivation of the specialized K-means clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3 and its intuitive form in Eq. 18) from the general framework is a strong point of the paper. It showcases the flexibility of the proposed method. Proposition 1, which relates the fixed points of the proposed AMP algorithm for the MAP problem to those of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm, is an important theoretical result. It provides a formal justification for the intuition that AMP may avoid some of the poor local minima that can trap ICM-based methods like Lloyd's K-means.

The experimental evaluation is solid. The authors compare their method against relevant and strong baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and Variational Bayes) on both synthetic and real-world data. The choice of metrics (K-means loss and clustering accuracy) is appropriate and provides a comprehensive view of performance. The results convincingly support the paper's claims, showing a clear advantage for the proposed AMP-based methods, particularly in higher-dimensional or more challenging scenarios. The authors are also honest about the failure modes of their algorithm in the real-data experiment, which is commendable.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, and the problem setting is defined precisely. A major strength is the clarity with which the authors explain the connection between their algorithm and previous methods. For instance, the discussion in Section 4.2 explicitly identifies the "extra terms" in the AMP updates that differentiate it from Variational Bayes/ICM.

Furthermore, the reformulation of the AMP K-means update rule in Eq. (18b) and the accompanying intuitive explanation are excellent. This translation of a complex mathematical formula into an understandable algorithmic behavior (i.e., correcting for a data point's own influence on its cluster center and favoring smaller clusters) is a high point of the paper and makes the contribution much more accessible. The paper provides enough detail for an expert to understand the method and likely reproduce the results.

**Originality:** The work is original and builds upon existing literature in a meaningful way. While AMP for low-rank matrix recovery is not new, the extension to handle general priors and MAP estimation in this specific formulation appears to be a novel contribution. The key originality lies in the application to K-means clustering. While formulating clustering as matrix factorization is a known idea, deriving a new, principled clustering algorithm directly from the AMP framework is a novel approach. This resulting algorithm is distinct from Lloyd's algorithm and, as the authors show, superior in practice. The paper does a good job of situating itself with respect to prior work, including other recent AMP-based papers on related problems, and clearly delineates its contributions.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are significant for several reasons.
1.  It provides a new, high-performance algorithm for K-means clustering, a fundamental and widely used unsupervised learning task. The demonstrated improvement over K-means++ is particularly noteworthy, as K-means++ is a strong and popular baseline.
2.  It showcases the power and flexibility of the AMP framework, suggesting it as a viable and powerful tool for deriving algorithms for a broader class of latent variable models beyond its traditional applications in compressed sensing.
3.  The connection established between AMP and ICM/Variational Bayes, highlighted by the "correction terms," provides valuable insight into the relationship between these classes of algorithms and offers a potential explanation for AMP's strong empirical performance. This could inspire further research into improving other iterative algorithms.

Researchers and practitioners working on clustering and matrix factorization are likely to find these ideas useful and build upon them.

**Strengths:**
*   **Novel and effective clustering algorithm:** The AMP-KM algorithm is the main highlight, offering a new perspective on K-means and demonstrating superior performance.
*   **Strong theoretical intuition:** Proposition 1 and the intuitive explanation of the AMP-KM update rule provide solid reasoning for why the algorithm works well.
*   **Clear connection to existing methods:** The paper clearly explains how AMP enhances standard iterative methods like ICM/VB, which is insightful.
*   **Convincing empirical validation:** The experiments are well-designed and the results strongly support the claims.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Derivation deferred:** The full derivation of the AMP algorithm is in the supplementary material, making it difficult to fully vet the technical details from the main paper.
*   **Convergence not guaranteed:** Like many AMP algorithms, convergence is assumed but not theoretically guaranteed. The authors acknowledge this implicitly by using a modified stopping criterion for AMP-KM to handle oscillations.
*   **Introduction of a new parameter `τ`:** The AMP formulation introduces a noise variance parameter `τ` that is not present in the original K-means problem. While the authors propose a reasonable heuristic for estimating it, this adds a layer of complexity compared to the parameter-free nature (aside from K) of Lloyd's algorithm.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and significant contribution. The weaknesses are minor and are common limitations in this line of research. The strengths, particularly the development of a new, principled, and high-performing clustering algorithm, are substantial. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and empirically convincing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
