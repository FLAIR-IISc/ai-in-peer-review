

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation problems. The authors then apply their algorithm to clustering problems, claiming it outperforms traditional methods like Lloyd's K-means.

**Summary and Context**
The paper builds on the growing body of work applying AMP algorithms to matrix problems, particularly extending the recent work of [9] to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering (Section 2.2) is well-established in prior literature [10, 11], though the authors claim their approach uniquely handles the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster. The proposed AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is derived as a natural extension of existing AMP frameworks [6-9].

**Strengths**
- The paper provides a clear and well-organized presentation of the theoretical framework.
- The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is logically developed.
- The numerical experiments on both synthetic and real data (ORL faces) provide some empirical evidence supporting the proposed approach.
- The computational complexity analysis (O(mN) per iteration) is reasonable and consistent with existing AMP approaches.

**Weaknesses and Concerns**

1. **Insufficient Justification for AMP Advantage**: The paper claims AMP has advantages over variational Bayes methods due to "reflecting dependence of random variables," but provides no rigorous evidence or theoretical analysis to support this claim. The comparison with variational Bayes (Section 4.2) is superficial, and the paper fails to demonstrate why AMP would outperform the well-established variational Bayes approach [4, 5] in practice. The claim that AMP "has not more fixed points than the ICM algorithm" (Section 4.2) is interesting but doesn't translate to practical advantages.

2. **Marginal Experimental Results**: The numerical experiments show only marginal improvements over existing methods. For example, Figure 1 shows AMP-KM achieves slightly lower K-means loss than Lloyd's K-means for r ≥ 5, but the differences are small (typically less than 5% improvement). The accuracy gains for AMP-MA are similarly modest. Crucially, the paper lacks statistical significance testing for these differences, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are meaningful.

3. **Incomplete Comparison with Established Methods**: The paper compares against only five algorithms (including K-means++), but omits recent, more sophisticated clustering methods that would provide a stronger baseline. The authors claim their AMP algorithm "outperforms" existing methods, but the improvements are not substantial enough to justify the additional complexity of AMP over well-established approaches.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees**: While Proposition 1 claims optimality under certain conditions, the paper doesn't provide evidence that these conditions are typically satisfied in practice. The convergence analysis is limited to a single proposition without detailed discussion of convergence rates or conditions under which convergence occurs.

5. **Missing Critical Analysis**: The paper doesn't address when the AMP approach might fail or under what conditions it would be preferable to existing methods. For example, how does the algorithm perform when the true rank is unknown? How does it handle non-Gaussian noise or non-spherical clusters?

6. **Overstated Claims**: The paper claims "the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering achieves the highest accuracy among the five algorithms," but the accuracy improvements are small and not statistically significant. The paper also claims the AMP algorithm "has a low computational cost" but doesn't compare this to the computational cost of variational Bayes methods they're claiming to outperform.

**Overall Assessment**
This paper presents a technically sound extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with applications to clustering. However, the claimed advantages over established methods are not sufficiently supported by rigorous theoretical analysis or comprehensive experimental validation. The improvements demonstrated are marginal and do not justify the additional complexity of the AMP approach compared to well-established alternatives like variational Bayes or K-means++. 

The paper would benefit from:
1. More rigorous theoretical justification for why AMP would outperform existing methods
2. Comprehensive statistical analysis of the experimental results
3. Comparison with more recent and sophisticated clustering algorithms
4. Discussion of practical limitations and failure modes

While the work is technically correct, it lacks the significant novelty and substantial improvements required to justify acceptance at NIPS. The paper presents a marginal improvement over existing methods without providing convincing evidence that this improvement is meaningful for real-world applications.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The paper does not meet NIPS's standards for significance and originality. The contributions are incremental, the evidence supporting the claims is insufficient, and the potential impact on the field appears limited. The authors should significantly strengthen their theoretical justification and experimental validation before resubmitting.
