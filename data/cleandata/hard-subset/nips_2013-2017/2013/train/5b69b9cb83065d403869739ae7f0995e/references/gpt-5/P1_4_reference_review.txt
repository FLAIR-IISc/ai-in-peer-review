Summary
This paper develops an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that flexibly incorporates prior structure on the factors U and V. The authors introduce a one-parameter family of posteriors p̂(U,V|A;β) proportional to p̂(U,V|A)β, unifying marginalization (β=1) and MAP (β→∞). They derive a bilinear AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) that handles general row-factorized priors p̂U(U)=∏i p̂u(ui), p̂V(V)=∏j p̂v(vj), and not only provides approximate posterior means but also marginal MAP and MAP estimates via the functions f and G computed from local “denoising” distributions. A fixed-point optimality proposition shows that, under mild PSD conditions, a converged AMP fixed point is a global minimizer in each block when the other is fixed, and AMP fixed points are also ICM fixed points.

As a key application, the paper formulates K-means clustering as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction (with one-hot constraints on V) and specializes AMP to this setting (Algorithm 3). The resulting method differs from Lloyd’s algorithm primarily through Onsager-like corrections in the assignment step: a leave-one-out bias correction and a cluster-size balancing term. Experiments on synthetic data (m=800, N=1600) show that the AMP K-means (AMP-KM) achieves lower K-means loss than Lloyd and K-means++, while the AMP maximum-accuracy variant (AMP-MA, β=1 with discrete prior on {e1,…,er}) achieves the highest clustering accuracy and fast convergence. On the ORL faces dataset (r̂=40), AMP-KM outperforms K-means++ in most trials in both loss and accuracy.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AMP for compressed sensing (Donoho et al., Rangan) and on bilinear/low-rank AMP: IterFac for rank-1 (Rangan & Fletcher) and the general-rank AMP in Matsushita & Tanaka (2012), which focused on posterior means. Compared to BiG-AMP/bilinear GAMP (Parker et al., Krzakala et al.), the authors emphasize a different asymptotic regime (fixed rank r as m,N→∞) and allow intra-row dependence in priors. The K-means connection is related to VB matrix factorization and ICM (Lim & Teh; Raiko et al.), but here the exact one-hot constraint is enforced in the AMP framework, yielding new assignment corrections reminiscent of Bethe/Onsager adjustments rather than standard EM/Lloyd updates.

Quality
Strengths:
- The derivation is principled and consistent with Bethe/AMP methodology; the Onsager terms are clearly identified as the difference from VB/ICM, with negligible extra cost.
- The β-trick unifies posterior mean, marginal MAP, and MAP within one algorithmic framework.
- Proposition 1 gives a useful optimality property at convergence.
- The K-means specialization is elegant, intuitive (bias correction and size balancing), and empirically effective.

Weaknesses:
- State evolution, a central strength of AMP, is omitted; no theoretical performance predictions or convergence guarantees are provided for the proposed algorithm beyond Proposition 1.
- Practical convergence issues (oscillations) are acknowledged but not systematically addressed (e.g., damping, step-size control, stopping criteria).
- Empirical evaluation is limited: only one real dataset; no comparison to bilinear GAMP/BiG-AMP implementations; no timing/wall-clock results; no study of robustness to rank mis-specification, prior mis-specification, or noise mismatch; no evaluation beyond clustering to demonstrate generality (e.g., sparse/nonnegative priors).
- For clustering, the method sometimes collapses to a single cluster; mitigation strategies (initialization, priors on cluster sizes, damping, annealing β) are not explored.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written and organized. The algorithmic updates are clear, and the role of f, G, and the Onsager terms is well explained.
- Important derivations and the proof are deferred to the supplement; a brief sketch of the derivation and examples of f/G for common priors (Gaussian, Laplace, nonnegative) would help reproducibility.
- The connection of the “last terms” in (9a, 9c) to Onsager corrections could be stated explicitly to orient readers familiar with AMP.
- Minor points: reference formatting is uneven; please ensure proper citations and completeness.

Originality
- Extending general-rank bilinear AMP from posterior-mean estimation to marginal MAP and MAP via the β-parameterization is a meaningful and nontrivial contribution.
- The K-means specialization that strictly enforces one-hot assignments within low-rank AMP and yields leave-one-out/size-correction terms appears novel relative to Lloyd/ICM and NMF-based clustering.
- While related bilinear AMP works exist (BiG-AMP, dictionary learning AMP), the paper’s assumptions and specialization differ; however, a deeper empirical/theoretical comparison would strengthen the originality claim.

Significance
- If robust and scalable, the proposed AMP framework could be a useful alternative to VB/ICM for structured low-rank problems, with better accuracy due to Bethe corrections.
- The K-means improvements are practically relevant; even modest gains over strong baselines (with careful initialization) matter for high-dimensional clustering.
- The current evidence is promising but limited; broader experiments and/or state evolution analysis would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, principled AMP extension to MAP/marginal MAP with clear algorithm and insights.
- Novel and effective K-means specialization that improves over Lloyd/K-means++.
- Low per-iteration complexity and scalability.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing state evolution or convergence analysis; limited empirical scope and baselines.
- Practical stability issues (oscillations, cluster collapse) not fully addressed.
- Sparse evaluation of the “general structured prior” claim beyond clustering.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
1) Please provide state evolution equations and at least empirical validation, or discuss when they are expected to hold in the fixed-r regime.
2) Compare empirically with BiG-AMP/bilinear GAMP on synthetic and real data, and report runtime.
3) Provide guidelines for damping/annealing and handling empty/small clusters; consider priors on cluster proportions to prevent collapse.
4) Add experiments with mis-specified r and mis-specified priors, and with other priors (e.g., nonnegative or sparse factors) to demonstrate generality.
5) Clarify f/G for common priors; include pseudo-code and discuss computational costs (e.g., r×r inverses).
6) Discuss the τ estimation’s effect; is the method scale-invariant? How sensitive are results to τ updates?
7) Relate the assignment correction to leave-one-out risk or information criteria, if possible.

Overall recommendation: Borderline accept. The paper offers a principled and potentially impactful AMP extension with a compelling clustering application, but would benefit from stronger theory (state evolution) and broader empirical validation.
