

# Peer Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that can handle both marginalization problems (for posterior mean estimation) and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors extend previous AMP work for low-rank matrices [9] to handle MAP estimation, which was previously not possible. The paper then demonstrates how this algorithm can be applied to K-means clustering, providing a novel approach that directly handles the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster. The authors compare their approach against standard methods like Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes matrix factorization, showing improved performance on both synthetic and real data.

The paper builds on the AMP framework originally developed for compressed sensing [6,7], extending it to the matrix factorization setting. It differs from previous work [8,9] by handling both marginalization and MAP problems within a unified framework, and by providing a direct application to clustering that properly enforces the one-cluster-per-datam constraint.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed AMP algorithm. The derivation of the algorithm from the posterior distribution is clear, and the authors establish important properties, including a convergence guarantee (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm correspond to local minima of the objective function. The connection to variational Bayes methods is well-explained, highlighting the advantages of AMP in capturing dependencies between variables.

2. **Novel Application to Clustering**: The application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering is particularly compelling. The paper correctly identifies that previous approaches to clustering via low-rank matrix reconstruction [10,11] did not properly handle the constraint that each datum must belong to exactly one cluster. The AMP approach for clustering (Algorithm 3) elegantly addresses this constraint while outperforming standard methods.

3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation**: The numerical experiments are thorough and well-designed. The synthetic data experiments compare the proposed method against five different algorithms across various problem sizes, using appropriate metrics (K-means loss, accuracy, convergence speed). The real data experiments on the ORL face database provide convincing evidence of the method's practical utility.

4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-written with clear notation and logical flow. The pseudocode for the algorithms is particularly helpful for understanding the proposed method. The connection between the matrix reconstruction problem and clustering is well-explained, making the contribution accessible to the NIPS audience.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Convergence Analysis**: While Proposition 1 provides some convergence guarantees, the paper does not provide a complete convergence analysis for the AMP algorithm. It would be beneficial to include a more thorough discussion of when the algorithm converges and under what conditions, especially since AMP algorithms often have strong theoretical guarantees in the asymptotic limit.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper mentions that the parameter τ needs to be estimated in practice, but the specific method for doing so (m⁻²N⁻¹∥A - Uᵗ(Vᵗ)⊤∥²F) could benefit from more justification. It would be helpful to understand why this particular estimate is appropriate and whether it affects the algorithm's performance.

3. **Comparison with Recent AMP Work**: The paper references recent AMP work for dictionary learning and blind calibration [13] and for matrix reconstruction with generalized observation models [14], but does not provide a detailed comparison of how their approach differs from these. A more thorough discussion of the relationship to these recent works would strengthen the paper.

4. **Computational Complexity Justification**: While the paper claims the computational cost is O(mN), it would be useful to provide more concrete evidence of this claim through runtime measurements, especially when compared to the variational Bayes method.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of matrix factorization and clustering. The extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP problems for low-rank matrix reconstruction is novel and well-motivated. The application to clustering, particularly the proper handling of the one-cluster-per-datam constraint, demonstrates significant practical value. The theoretical foundations are solid, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is clear.

The weaknesses identified are important but not critical to the paper's contribution. The limited convergence analysis and implementation details are common in AMP papers and could be addressed in future work. The paper's core contribution is significant and well-supported.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It addresses a meaningful problem with a novel approach that advances the state of the art in both matrix factorization and clustering. The results are compelling and the method has clear potential for broader application in machine learning.

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pro Acceptance:**
- Novel extension of AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP problems for low-rank matrix reconstruction
- First method that properly handles the one-cluster-per-datam constraint in clustering via low-rank matrix reconstruction
- Strong theoretical foundation with convergence guarantees
- Comprehensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real data
- Demonstrated superior performance over standard baselines

**Con Acceptance:**
- Limited convergence analysis beyond Proposition 1
- Some implementation details (e.g., parameter τ estimation) could be better justified
- Comparison with very recent AMP work could be more detailed
- Computational complexity claim could be better supported with runtime measurements

Overall, the paper's strengths significantly outweigh its weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS' focus on theoretical and practical advances in machine learning.
