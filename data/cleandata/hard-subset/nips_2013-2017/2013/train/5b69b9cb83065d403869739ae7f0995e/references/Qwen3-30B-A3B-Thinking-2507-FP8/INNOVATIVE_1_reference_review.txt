

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper introduces a novel AMP-based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that extends previous work to handle MAP estimation, and applies this framework to develop a new clustering algorithm. The core novelty lies in the unified AMP framework that treats both marginalization and MAP problems through a single parameter β, and the application of this framework to clustering where the constraint of each datum being assigned to exactly one cluster is directly handled within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

The authors make a clear distinction from prior work (e.g., [8, 9]) by extending AMP to handle MAP estimation, which previous AMP algorithms for low-rank matrix reconstruction could not do. This is a significant theoretical advancement that enables a broader range of applications. The paper's second major contribution is the novel application of this AMP framework to K-means clustering, which the authors convincingly argue is the first method to directly handle the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. Previous approaches either used low-rank matrix reconstruction as a proxy for clustering [10, 11] or used the variational Bayes approach [4, 5], but none directly handled the exact cluster assignment constraint as the proposed AMP algorithm does.

The paper demonstrates strong experimental results, showing the proposed AMP-KM algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ in terms of normalized K-means loss, and the AMP-MA algorithm achieves higher accuracy than all competitors in clustering tasks. The convergence properties are also promising, with the AMP algorithms converging faster than the variational Bayes approach.

This work is particularly valuable because it bridges two important areas (low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering) through a novel algorithmic framework. The AMP approach has proven successful in compressed sensing and related problems [6-7], but its application to clustering in this specific way represents a significant conceptual advance. The authors' insight that clustering can be viewed as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with specific structural constraints is both elegant and powerful.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of formal convergence proof for the proposed AMP algorithm (the authors simply assume convergence). However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this to be a minor issue. The paper provides sufficient theoretical justification through the state evolution discussion (even if not fully detailed) and the convergence properties are demonstrated empirically. The paper also acknowledges this limitation by stating "we assume convergence of Algorithm 2, although the convergence is not guaranteed in general."

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with precise mathematical formulations and helpful algorithm descriptions. The experimental section is thorough, using both synthetic and real-world data (ORL Database of Faces) to validate the approach. The comparison with relevant baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, variational Bayes) is appropriate and demonstrates the advantages of the proposed method.

What makes this paper stand out is its bold conceptual leap: taking an established technique (AMP) from compressed sensing and extending it to a completely different domain (clustering) with a theoretically sound framework that solves a long-standing constraint issue. This represents a significant conceptual contribution rather than just an incremental improvement on existing methods.

The paper's contribution is clearly distinct from previous work. While [10, 11] used low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering, they didn't handle the cluster assignment constraint directly within the framework. [13, 14] proposed AMP for related problems but didn't address the specific application to clustering with the exact constraint that each datum belongs to exactly one cluster. The paper's theoretical framework for handling MAP estimation is also a clear extension beyond [9].

In terms of significance, this work provides both a new algorithmic framework and a new perspective on clustering. The AMP-based clustering algorithm demonstrates clear performance advantages over existing methods, and the theoretical insights about the relationship between AMP and variational Bayes methods (the paper shows how AMP can be viewed as a generalization of the ICM algorithm) are valuable for the broader community.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper to be a strong candidate for acceptance. The conceptual advance in applying AMP to clustering with a direct handling of the cluster assignment constraint is significant, and the experimental results support the theoretical claims. While the paper could benefit from a more detailed convergence analysis, the novelty of the approach outweighs this minor limitation.

Overall, this paper makes a compelling contribution that advances the state of the art in both low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The proposed framework is elegant, the theoretical insights are valuable, and the experimental results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the NIPS community and should be accepted.
