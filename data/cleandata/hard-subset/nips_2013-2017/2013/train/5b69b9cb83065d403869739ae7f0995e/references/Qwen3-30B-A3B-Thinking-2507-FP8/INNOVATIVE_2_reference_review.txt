

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering. The authors extend previous AMP approaches to handle general-rank matrices (rather than just rank-one cases) and demonstrate how this framework can directly address clustering problems as a special case where each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster.

The paper's main novelty lies in its unified AMP framework for both marginalization and MAP estimation in low-rank matrix reconstruction. While previous work (like [8, 9]) has explored AMP for rank-one or specific matrix reconstruction problems, this paper generalizes the approach to handle arbitrary rank r, flexible prior distributions for both factor matrices U and V, and both marginalization and MAP estimation within the same framework. The connection to clustering is particularly novel: the authors show how K-means clustering can be viewed as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with specific constraints, and they develop an AMP-based clustering algorithm that outperforms Lloyd's K-means in high-dimensional settings.

The paper makes a strong case for the advantages of AMP over variational Bayes approaches. The authors correctly note that AMP can capture dependencies between variables (unlike variational Bayes, which assumes independence), and they provide a theoretical justification for this through the Bethe approximation. They also offer a convergence guarantee (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of their AMP algorithm correspond to fixed points of the ICM algorithm used in variational Bayes, with the added benefit that AMP may avoid getting stuck in poor local minima.

The experimental results are compelling. On artificial data, the AMP algorithm for K-means clustering achieves the lowest K-means loss, and the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering achieves the highest accuracy. On the ORL face database, the AMP algorithm outperforms K-means++ in 48/50 trials in terms of K-means loss and 47/50 trials in terms of accuracy, with comparable or better convergence speed.

**Strengths:**
- **Novel framework**: The unified AMP approach for general-rank matrix reconstruction is genuinely new and extends the AMP methodology beyond its previous applications.
- **Significant application**: The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is insightful, and the resulting AMP-based clustering algorithm offers a meaningful advance over existing methods.
- **Strong theoretical grounding**: The paper provides clear motivation for why AMP should outperform variational Bayes, supported by a convergence guarantee.
- **Comprehensive evaluation**: The experiments on both artificial and real data demonstrate the practical benefits of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**
- The comparison to "bilinear generalized approximate message passing" [14] is somewhat superficial. The authors claim their approach differs by fixing rank r rather than r/m in the limit, but don't fully elaborate on how this affects practical performance.
- The paper doesn't thoroughly address how their approach differs from [13] on blind calibration and dictionary learning.
- The computational complexity analysis could be more detailed, particularly for the case when r̂ (the assumed rank) is large.
- The paper doesn't explore how the method would perform on non-Gaussian noise or more complex clustering structures.

**Why this paper is strong for NIPS:**
This paper excels in originality and significance. It introduces a novel algorithmic framework that extends AMP to a new domain (general-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible priors), and then demonstrates its value through a significant application (clustering) that outperforms existing methods. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the literature (previous AMP approaches were limited to rank-one or specific cases) and filled it with a bold, well-motivated approach.

While the paper has minor weaknesses in the comparison to related work, these don't undermine the core novelty and significance of the contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field that advances the state of the art in both low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper presents a novel approach with significant potential impact, and the empirical results strongly support the claims. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and represents a substantial advance over existing methods in the problem domain.
