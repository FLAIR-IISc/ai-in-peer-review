## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary:**

This paper presents an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work to handle MAP estimation beyond posterior mean estimation. A key contribution is applying this algorithm to K-means clustering, formulating it as a specific case of low-rank reconstruction with a unique constraint on cluster assignments. The authors demonstrate, through numerical experiments, that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means, particularly in high-dimensional data. They also provide theoretical justification for the algorithm's optimality under certain conditions.

**Relationship to Prior Work:**

The paper builds upon the growing body of work leveraging AMP for various statistical inference problems, citing seminal papers in compressed sensing and Bayesian inference. It differentiates itself from existing AMP-based low-rank reconstruction algorithms ([9]) by enabling MAP estimation. The application to clustering connects to a longer history of utilizing low-rank methods for clustering ([10, 11]), but distinguishes itself by directly enforcing the single-cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank framework. The work also positions itself relative to variational Bayes approaches, highlighting potential advantages of AMP’s ability to model dependencies between factors.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear derivation of the AMP algorithm and establishes a theoretical basis for its performance through Proposition 1, demonstrating optimality under specific conditions. The connection to belief propagation and Bethe approximation provides valuable context.
* **Generality & Flexibility:**  The Bayesian formulation and use of flexible prior distributions allows the algorithm to accommodate various structural properties of the factors U and V.
* **Novel Clustering Algorithm:** Applying AMP to K-means clustering is a novel contribution, and the numerical results demonstrate superior performance compared to the standard Lloyd’s algorithm in certain scenarios. The insight that the single assignment constraint can be directly incorporated within the low-rank formulation is a key strength.
* **Clear Presentation:** The algorithm is presented clearly and systematically, and the mathematical notation is well-defined.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The numerical experiments, covering both synthetic and real-world data, provide strong evidence for the algorithm’s effectiveness. The comparison against established methods (K-means, K-means++) strengthens these findings.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited State Evolution Discussion:** While the introduction briefly mentions the state evolution analysis, it's not elaborated upon, leaving a potential avenue for deeper theoretical understanding unexplored. The paper acknowledges this limitation.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The role and optimal setting of the parameter 'τ' (noise variance) are not fully investigated. The empirical section describes estimating it but lacks a thorough discussion of its impact on performance.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** The paper states the computational cost is O(mN), which is linear. However, the cost of calculating f and G for β < ∞, involving r̂-dimensional numerical integration, is glossed over.  A more detailed analysis of the practical computational cost, especially relating to ‘r̂’, would be valuable.
* **Assumptions for Proposition 1:** The conditions required for Proposition 1 to hold (positive semi-definiteness of certain matrices) are not extensively discussed, potentially limiting the broad applicability of this theoretical guarantee.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The theoretical grounding, combined with the novel application to K-means and promising empirical results, make it a significant piece of work. While some aspects (state evolution, parameter sensitivity, computational cost) could benefit from further elaboration, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh the weaknesses.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The extension of AMP to MAP estimation and its application to clustering with a hard assignment constraint are novel.
* **Significance:** The demonstrated performance improvements in clustering, particularly in high-dimensional settings, are significant.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the algorithm and the optimality guarantee provide a strong theoretical foundation.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Limited Discussion of Practical Considerations:** The paper lacks in-depth discussion of the practical implications of parameters like τ and the computational cost of the algorithm for large values of r̂.
* **Conditions for Optimality:** The conditions required for Proposition 1 to hold might be restrictive in some applications.

**Final Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**  I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically by providing a more detailed discussion of parameter selection, computational complexity, and the limitations of Proposition 1. Expanding on the state evolution analysis would also be beneficial.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:** Very Good – The paper is technically sound and claims are well-supported.
* **Clarity:** Good – The paper is generally clearly written and organized.
* **Originality:** Good – The work presents novel contributions in both algorithm development and application.
* **Significance:** Good – The results are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in matrix factorization, clustering, and related fields.
