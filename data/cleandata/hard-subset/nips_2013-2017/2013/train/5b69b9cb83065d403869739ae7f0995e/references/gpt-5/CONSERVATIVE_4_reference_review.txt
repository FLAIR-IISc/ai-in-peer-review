Summary
The paper proposes an AMP-based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible priors on the factors. A “temperature” parameter β unifies posterior-mean inference (β=1) and MAP (β→∞). The authors further specialize the MAP version to derive an AMP-style K-means clustering method that enforces one-hot constraints on cluster assignments and introduces assignment “corrections” reminiscent of leave-one-out adjustments. They present a fixed-point optimality proposition (under PSD assumptions) and experiments on synthetic Gaussian-mixture data and the ORL faces dataset, comparing against Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and a variational Bayes factorization baseline.

Relation to prior work
AMP/GAMP-style methods for bilinear/matrix factorization are well studied (IterFac; BiG-AMP; AMP for dictionary learning and blind calibration). The paper cites some of these but does not position the technical contribution sharply. The use of β to pass from posterior means to MAP is a well-known device. Specializing the bilinear AMP updates to the K-means constraint is interesting, but similar “correction” terms have appeared in other AMP/leave-one-out approximations. A thorough comparison to BiG-AMP and related bilinear AMP algorithms is missing.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for flexible priors in low-rank reconstruction, and a consistent Bayesian formulation.
- Unification of marginalization and MAP via a single β-parameter family is conceptually neat.
- The K-means specialization yields a simple algorithmic form with intuitive assignment corrections (bias against the current assignment; preference for smaller clusters).
- Empirical results show improvements over Lloyd’s K-means/K-means++ on the presented tasks; AMP-MA converges quickly and attains high accuracy on synthetic data.

Weaknesses
Quality/technical soundness:
- The core derivation and state evolution are deferred to the Supplement; no theoretical validation is provided in the paper. Claims such as O(1) iterations asymptotically, correctness of Onsager terms, and the scaling choice (Gaussian noise variance mτ) are not substantiated for this bilinear setting.
- Convergence is not guaranteed; oscillations occur; stopping criteria are ad hoc (Vt=Vt−1 or Vt=Vt−2). No damping or stabilization strategy is discussed.
- The fixed-point Proposition 1 is weak: under PSD assumptions it shows each block is a global minimum when the other is fixed—this largely follows from convexity in each block (given convex −log priors) and does not address global optimality of the joint nonconvex objective.
- For finite β, f and G require r̂-dimensional integrations; the “low computational cost” claim critically depends on priors with closed-form denoisers. There is little discussion of practical priors beyond the discrete one-hot case.
- Algorithm 3 contains apparent scaling inconsistencies (e.g., Λt_u in (17a) seems to miss a 1/(mτ) factor compared to earlier equations). Such discrepancies are critical in AMP and must be clarified.
- The τ estimation used in clustering is heuristic and lacks theoretical justification; its impact on performance/stability is not analyzed.

Empirical validation:
- Baselines are limited. For clustering, missing comparisons include EM for Gaussian mixtures (with full/spherical covariances), spectral clustering, spherical K-means, minibatch K-means, and recent scalable K-means refinements. For matrix factorization, there is no comparison to BiG-AMP or related bilinear AMP algorithms.
- The AMP-MA experiments use the “true priors” on synthetic data, which advantages the method and does not test robustness to misspecification. There is no study of rank mismatch (r̂≠r), unknown τ, or prior mismatch—central issues in practice.
- ORL results require K-means++ seeding to avoid collapse; in two trials the algorithm degenerates to a single cluster. There is no analysis of sensitivity to initialization/damping or remedies (e.g., temperature annealing, balancing terms).
- No wall-clock time comparisons, ablations (e.g., removing Onsager terms to replicate VB/ICM), or scalability experiments beyond a single synthetic size.

Clarity/reproducibility:
- The paper is generally well organized, but key derivations are missing. Important implementation details (damping, numerical integration/minimization for f/G, preprocessing of ORL, parameter settings) are not specified. Reproducibility is limited; no code is provided.
- Several notational/scaling issues (noted above) and reliance on improper priors without conditions for posterior propriety are concerning.

Originality
- Incremental. AMP for bilinear/matrix factorization and clustering connections have been explored. The β-trick for MAP and the one-hot constraint within an AMP update are the main novel elements here, but they are relatively modest relative to existing BiG-AMP and dictionary-learning AMP frameworks. The paper would benefit from a direct technical comparison (derivation and experiments) to BiG-AMP and from clearer articulation of what is genuinely new.

Significance
- Potentially useful if made robust and theoretically grounded, especially the K-means specialization. However, given the limited theory, convergence issues, narrow baselines, and modest gains (with occasional failures) on ORL, the current evidence does not convincingly demonstrate state-of-the-art impact.

Questions for the authors
1. Provide the full derivation and state evolution for both β=1 and β→∞ (or at least a summary with empirical validation of SE predictions).
2. Clarify the scaling in Algorithm 3; resolve inconsistencies in Λu and Λv (and their impact).
3. How do you stabilize convergence (damping, step sizes)? Can you eliminate the observed assignment oscillations without ad hoc stopping?
4. Compare directly against BiG-AMP (and variants) on both reconstruction and clustering (including ablations showing benefits of your specific Onsager terms).
5. Evaluate robustness to rank/prior/τ misspecification; include unknown number of clusters selection.
6. Add baselines: EM-GMM, spectral/spherical K-means, minibatch K-means, modern K-means variants; report runtime.
7. Provide implementation details and code for reproducibility.

Pros
- Clear problem statement; elegant unification via β.
- Intuitive K-means specialization with interpretable corrections.
- Some empirical gains over Lloyd/K-means++.

Cons
- Missing theory (derivation/SE), weak guarantees, convergence issues.
- Limited and potentially biased empirical validation; important baselines missing.
- Apparent formula inconsistencies; incomplete implementation details.
- Novelty relative to established bilinear AMP methods is unclear.

Recommendation
Reject. While the direction is promising and the K-means specialization is interesting, the paper lacks the theoretical rigor and comprehensive empirical validation needed to support its claims, especially given strong existing work on bilinear AMP. Strengthening the derivations, fixing inconsistencies, adding robust convergence strategies, and expanding the experimental comparisons would substantially improve the work.
