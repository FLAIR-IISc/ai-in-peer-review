## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary:**

This paper presents an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work to handle MAP estimation beyond posterior mean estimation.  A significant contribution is the application of this algorithm to K-means clustering, formulating it as a special case of low-rank reconstruction and presenting a novel clustering algorithm that outperforms Lloyd’s K-means in high-dimensional settings, according to the authors’ experiments. The core idea leverages the flexibility of the Bayesian framework and the efficiency of AMP, building on recent advances in the field.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. It acknowledges the Bayesian approach's flexibility and computational challenges, referencing variational Bayes and Monte Carlo methods. The connection to compressed sensing and prior AMP work (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9]) is well established. The authors rightly point out how their work differs from recent AMP algorithms for related problems [13, 14] by focusing on a different scaling limit and independence assumptions. The link to existing low-rank clustering techniques [10, 11] is also clearly stated.  

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Extension of AMP:** Extending the AMP algorithm to handle MAP estimation represents a genuine contribution, addressing a limitation of previous work [9].
* **Clustering Application:**  Formulating K-means as a low-rank reconstruction problem and applying AMP provides a fresh perspective and a potentially more effective solution, particularly in high dimensions, as demonstrated by their experiments.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors provide a theoretical foundation for their algorithm, including a proposition regarding the optimality of the fixed point, and connection to ICM algorithm. This is crucial for understanding the behavior and limitations of the method.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:**  The paper includes experiments on both artificial and real datasets, demonstrating the performance of the proposed algorithm against established methods (Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA, and AMP-MA). The results show clear advantages in certain scenarios.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, with a clear problem formulation and algorithm description.

**Weaknesses:**

* **State Evolution Omission:** While acknowledging the importance of state evolution for predicting performance, the authors deliberately omit its presentation due to space constraints.  This is a significant drawback, as state evolution is a key feature of AMP algorithms and provides valuable insight into their convergence and performance.
* **Convergence Guarantee:** The authors admit that convergence isn't guaranteed, and the provided stopping criteria might not be optimal. A more rigorous analysis of convergence would strengthen the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The impact of different prior distributions (p̂u and p̂v) on performance is not thoroughly investigated. A sensitivity analysis regarding the choice of these priors would be beneficial.
* **Limited Comparison:**  The comparison to VBMF-MA seems somewhat limited. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between the proposed AMP-based approach and variational methods would be valuable.
* **Implementation Details:** The implementation details and code availability are not provided, making it difficult to reproduce the results and assess the practical usability of the algorithm.



**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound, with a solid theoretical foundation and well-supported experimental results. The extension of the AMP algorithm to MAP estimation is a genuine contribution, and the application to clustering is insightful.  However, the lack of state evolution analysis and the convergence concerns slightly detract from the overall quality.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the algorithm is described in a step-by-step manner. However, some readers may find the derivation of the AMP algorithm challenging without a strong background in message passing.

**Originality:** The work is original in its application of AMP to both MAP estimation in low-rank matrix reconstruction and its formulation of K-means clustering as a specific instance of the more general problem. This combination of ideas, combined with the theoretical results, sets it apart from existing work.

**Significance:** The results are significant, particularly the demonstration of improved performance in high-dimensional clustering scenarios. The proposed algorithm has the potential to be a useful tool for researchers and practitioners working in these areas. The connection to K-means could open up new avenues for research in clustering and related fields.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
* The work extends the existing literature on AMP algorithms and provides a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The omission of state evolution analysis weakens the theoretical justification of the algorithm.
* Convergence is not guaranteed, and the stopping criteria may not be optimal.
* Lack of code availability hinders reproducibility.



**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1. **Address the convergence concern:** Provide a more thorough discussion of the convergence properties of the algorithm, perhaps exploring alternative stopping criteria.
2. **Consider adding a brief discussion of state evolution:** Even a qualitative description of how state evolution applies to their algorithm would significantly strengthen the paper.
3. **Provide more details on the implementation:** Include details on the software used and computational resources needed for reproducing the results.  Consider making the code publicly available.
4. **Expand the comparison to VBMF-MA:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach compared to variational methods.
