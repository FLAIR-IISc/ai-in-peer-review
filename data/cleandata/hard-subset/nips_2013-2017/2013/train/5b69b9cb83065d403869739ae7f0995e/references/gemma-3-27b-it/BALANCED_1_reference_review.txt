This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means type clustering. The authors extend existing AMP algorithms to handle MAP estimation and demonstrate its effectiveness, particularly for high-dimensional data. The core idea is to formulate clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific constraints, and leverage the flexibility of the Bayesian approach and AMP for efficient solution.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing the need for flexible and efficient algorithms for low-rank matrix reconstruction, especially when incorporating prior knowledge about the factors. The connection to compressed sensing and the utilization of AMP are sensible, given its successes in related fields. The formulation of clustering as a low-rank reconstruction problem isn’t novel, but the authors claim a first-of-its-kind AMP-based algorithm directly enforcing the single-cluster-assignment constraint. The paper clearly outlines the problem setup, the proposed algorithm, and presents numerical experiments to validate its performance. 

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the AMP algorithm appears logically sound, building upon previous work in the field. The extension to MAP estimation is a worthwhile contribution. The explanation of the marginalization and MAP problems within the Bayesian framework is clear.
* **Novelty:** While low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering isn’t new, the application of AMP with the specific constraints described – particularly the direct enforcement of single cluster assignment – represents a novel approach.
* **Potential Significance:**  The results suggest that the proposed algorithm can outperform standard K-means, especially in high-dimensional settings.  This is a potentially significant improvement, offering a more efficient and accurate clustering solution. The analysis and propositions around convergence are also valuable.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the equations are presented clearly. The authors provide sufficient detail for someone familiar with AMP and Bayesian methods to understand the approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on both artificial and real datasets provide convincing evidence of the algorithm’s performance. The comparison with established methods like K-means++ and VBMF is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the authors mention the state evolution analysis, it's not included in the paper due to space constraints. This limits the reader's ability to fully assess the theoretical guarantees and limitations of the algorithm. The conditions under which the convergence proposition holds could be clarified further.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The experiments use a fixed noise variance parameter (τ).  The paper doesn’t discuss how sensitive the algorithm’s performance is to the choice of τ, or provide guidance on how to select an appropriate value in practice.
* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** The paper mentions the computational cost being O(mN) per iteration, but a more detailed comparison of computational complexity with other algorithms (K-means, VBMF) would be helpful. Specifically, how does the cost of calculating f and G scale with *r̂*?
* **Comparison to Recent AMP Work:** The "recent" work cited ([13, 14]) is from 2013.  The field of AMP has moved quickly since then.  A more thorough review of related AMP literature is warranted, specifically concerning applications to matrix factorization and clustering.
* **Real Data Experiment:** While the ORL face database experiment is a good starting point, exploring performance on larger, more complex real-world datasets would strengthen the claims of practical significance.
* **Initialization Dependence:** The need for careful initialization for the real data experiment (using K-means++ initialization) suggests a potential sensitivity of the algorithm to initial conditions, which is not discussed in detail.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Novel application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with a specific clustering constraint.
* Strong empirical results demonstrating improved performance compared to K-means, particularly in high dimensions.
* Clear presentation and logical derivation of the algorithm.
* Potential to significantly impact clustering performance in certain applications.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* Lack of detailed theoretical analysis and convergence guarantees.
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and computational complexity.
* A somewhat dated literature review regarding recent advances in AMP.
* Dependence on K-means++ initialization for real-world data.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper presenting a novel and promising approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. While some weaknesses exist, particularly regarding theoretical analysis and a more comprehensive literature review, the strong empirical results and potential for significant impact make it a valuable contribution. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**. The authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of parameter sensitivity (specifically, τ).
2.  Provide a more detailed computational complexity analysis and comparison to alternative methods.
3.  Update the literature review to include more recent work in AMP, especially those relevant to matrix factorization and clustering.
4.  Discuss the impact of initialization on the algorithm's performance and explore potential strategies for robust initialization.
5.  Consider including a brief discussion of the state evolution analysis, even if it's limited to a high-level overview and a reference to a longer, supplementary document.
