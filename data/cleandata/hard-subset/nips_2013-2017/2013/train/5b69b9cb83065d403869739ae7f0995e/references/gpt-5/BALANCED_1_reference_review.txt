Summary and relation to prior work
The paper develops an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible priors on the factors U,V. A one-parameter “temperature” version of the posterior p̂(U,V|A;β) interpolates between marginal inference (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞). From the Bethe approximation of belief propagation, the authors derive iterative updates (with Onsager-like corrections) that yield both posterior mean/marginal-MAP approximations and a MAP algorithm. A proposition states that, at fixed points (under mild PSD conditions), the algorithm achieves blockwise global optimality with respect to U or V, even though the overall objective is nonconvex.

As a second contribution, they identify K-means clustering as a special case of low-rank reconstruction with a one-hot prior on rows of V and derive a specialized AMP algorithm (AMP-KM). The resulting assignment step contains correction terms that account for current assignments and cluster sizes; this can be interpreted as a bias correction relative to Lloyd’s algorithm. They also propose an AMP-based “maximum accuracy” clustering (AMP-MA) for β=1 with discrete priors on V.

Relation to prior work is given: IterFac for rank-one, a previous AMP algorithm for general rank focusing on posterior means, and more recent bilinear/biG-AMP-like works (dictionary learning, generalized observation models). The authors differ by fixing rank r as m,N→∞ and by allowing dependence across entries in each row of U or V. On clustering, the link between low-rank factorization and clustering is known (e.g., NMF-based clustering), but the hard one-hot constraint within an AMP framework appears novel.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The derivation outline is plausible and consistent with AMP/Bethe methodology; the updates include Onsager terms that vanish in variational-Bayes/ICM limits, which is well motivated. However, the state evolution and its proof—central to AMP papers—are omitted (promised for the supplement). Convergence is not guaranteed; oscillations and cluster collapse are reported, and no damping/mean-removal safeguards are discussed. Proposition 1 (blockwise global minimality at fixed points) is useful but limited, and relies on PSD conditions that are asserted to hold “in almost all cases” without precise assumptions.
- Empirical evaluation: Synthetic experiments (m=800, N=1600) show AMP-KM achieves lower K-means loss than Lloyd/K-means++ and that AMP-MA attains higher accuracy than VBMF-MA. On ORL faces (N=400, r̂=40), AMP-KM improves both loss and accuracy in most runs, with small overhead in iterations, but sometimes collapses to a single cluster. Baselines are limited (no EM for GMMs, spectral/spherical K-means, Hartigan’s method, or biG-AMP-type algorithms), and experiments are relatively small-scale for O(mN) methods. The “maximum accuracy” setting uses the true priors, advantaging AMP-MA relative to generic methods and does not probe prior misspecification.
- Correctness/clarity: The K-means specialization is insightful, and the bias-correction interpretation is compelling. Some equations appear to have minor scaling typos (e.g., Λu in Eq. (17a)), but the intent is clear. The role and estimation of τ are heuristic yet reasonable.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear, with a good high-level motivation, explicit algorithms, and a helpful discussion of how the Onsager terms differentiate AMP from VB/ICM. Important derivations and proofs are deferred; if accepted, the supplement should include complete derivations, the state evolution equations, and precise conditions for Proposition 1. Notation is dense but standard for AMP papers.

Originality
Moderate. Extending bilinear AMP to unify posterior mean/MAP via a temperature parameter and to handle discrete, one-hot priors for clustering is a meaningful step beyond prior general-rank AMP focused solely on posterior means. The K-means specialization that yields a practical, bias-corrected assignment rule seems novel. However, the general idea of AMP for matrix factorization and applications to related bilinear models (biG-AMP) is established.

Significance
Potentially useful. If robust and scalable, AMP-KM could be a drop-in replacement for Lloyd’s K-means with better solutions at similar cost, especially in high dimensions. The broader low-rank reconstruction framework with general priors could benefit applications where variational methods underperform. The current experimental scope and lack of theoretical performance characterization limit the immediate impact.

Pros
- Clear, principled AMP derivation for low-rank factorization that unifies MMSE/MAP with a single framework.
- Insightful K-means specialization with interpretable bias-correction terms.
- Empirical improvements over Lloyd/K-means++ on synthetic and ORL data, with modest iteration counts.
- Proposition relating fixed points to blockwise global minima provides some optimality reassurance.

Cons
- Missing state evolution and rigorous convergence/stability analysis; practical oscillations and failures observed.
- Limited baselines (no EM-GMM with spherical/diag covariance, spectral/spherical K-means, Hartigan’s method, or biG-AMP implementations).
- Small-scale experiments; no wall-clock comparisons; sensitivity to initialization and τ estimation not systematically analyzed.
- AMP-MA advantages partly hinge on access to true priors; robustness to prior misspecification not tested.
- No ablation to isolate the value of the Onsager corrections versus ICM/VB.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include full state evolution for both β=1 and β→∞ (or an annealed β schedule), with conditions for validity and a brief empirical verification.
- Add damping/adaptive damping and mean-removal techniques known to stabilize AMP; report their effect on oscillations and cluster collapse.
- Expand baselines: EM for GMMs (spherical/diagonal), spectral/spherical K-means, Hartigan’s K-means, and a public biG-AMP implementation where applicable. Include wall-clock times.
- Provide ablations: with/without Onsager terms; effect of τ estimation; robustness to unbalanced clusters and prior misspecification; mis-specified rank r̂≠r.
- Scale up experiments (e.g., m,N≥10^4 where feasible) to demonstrate O(mN) practicality and iteration counts.
- Clarify minor notational/scaling issues in Alg. 3; release code for reproducibility.

Decision
Weak reject. The paper contains appealing ideas and promising empirical signals, particularly for K-means. However, for NeurIPS, the absence of state evolution/rigorous analysis, limited baselines, and modest experimental scope currently weaken the case. With the suggested additions, this work could become a strong contribution.
