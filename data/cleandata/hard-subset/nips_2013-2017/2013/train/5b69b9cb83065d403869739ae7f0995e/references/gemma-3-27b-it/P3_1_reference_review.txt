## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary:**

This paper presents an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work by [9] to allow for MAP estimation alongside posterior mean estimation.  A key contribution lies in demonstrating how this framework naturally encompasses K-means clustering, leading to a novel clustering algorithm that, according to the authors’ experiments, outperforms Lloyd’s K-means in high-dimensional settings. The paper positions its work within the broader context of AMP algorithms for related problems like dictionary learning and highlights differences in its approach regarding rank scaling and independence assumptions.

**Related Work & Context:**

The authors correctly situate their work within the established landscape of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. They appropriately acknowledge the prevalence of Bayesian approaches and the need for approximate inference techniques due to computational complexity.  The references to variational Bayes methods [3-5], compressed sensing [6], and existing AMP algorithms [8,9,13,14] are relevant and demonstrate awareness of the field.  The comparison to variational Bayes is particularly insightful, as the authors attempt to leverage the benefits of AMP’s more sophisticated message passing framework.  The connection to K-means clustering is well-established [10, 11], however, the framing of directly incorporating the “exactly one cluster” constraint is a novel aspect.

**Strengths:**

* **Generality and Flexibility:** The proposed AMP algorithm offers a unified framework for both marginalization and MAP estimation, providing flexibility in addressing various aspects of the low-rank reconstruction problem.
* **Theoretical Foundation:**  The derivation of the algorithm based on approximate message passing provides a solid theoretical underpinning, with allusions to state evolution analysis (though not fully presented).
* **Novel Clustering Algorithm:**  The application of the algorithm to K-means clustering, explicitly enforcing the single-cluster assignment constraint, is a significant contribution. The analysis connecting MAP estimation to optimal clustering accuracy is well motivated.
* **Experimental Results:**  The numerical experiments demonstrate the potential of the proposed algorithm, specifically showing improved performance over Lloyd's K-means in high dimensions and exceeding accuracy with the AMP-MA version.  The use of both synthetic and real (ORL faces) datasets is commendable.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The equations are clearly presented and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited State Evolution Discussion:** While the authors mention state evolution as a key benefit of AMP, the lack of its presentation significantly limits the reviewer’s ability to assess the theoretical guarantees and limitations of the algorithm. This makes it difficult to judge the scalability and performance characteristics for extremely large datasets.
* **Computational Complexity Analysis:** Although the authors state the computational cost is O(mN), a more detailed breakdown of the complexity of individual steps (especially the calculation of f and G functions) would be valuable.  The practical impact of the r̂-dimensional numerical integration (for β < ∞) is not fully explored.
* **Sensitivity to Parameters:** The performance of AMP algorithms can be sensitive to parameter settings (e.g., the noise variance τ).  The paper doesn't provide sufficient guidance on how to choose these parameters in practice, or explore the robustness of the algorithm to variations in these parameters.
* **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Clustering:** The comparison is primarily to Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++. While a good starting point, a comparison to more sophisticated clustering algorithms (e.g., spectral clustering, DBSCAN) would strengthen the claims of improved performance.
* **Proposition 1's Conditions:**  The condition that  ∑m i=1 S ∞ i and ∑N j=1 T ∞ j are positive semidefinite for Proposition 1 to hold is stated but not demonstrably guaranteed for all cases.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically sound extension of AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction.
* The application to K-means clustering is a significant and potentially impactful contribution.
* The experimental results demonstrate promising performance gains over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core ideas.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of a detailed discussion of state evolution analysis hinders a full assessment of the algorithm’s theoretical properties.
* The computational complexity analysis could be more thorough.
* The sensitivity analysis regarding parameter choices is missing.
* Comparison to a wider range of state-of-the-art clustering algorithms is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a **strong contribution** to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The proposed algorithm is theoretically well-motivated, offers a flexible framework, and shows promising results. While there are weaknesses concerning the lack of detailed theoretical analysis (state evolution) and the limited comparative analysis, the strengths outweigh these concerns.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions.  

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Expand the discussion of state evolution:**  At least provide a high-level overview of the state evolution equations for the proposed algorithm.
* **Provide a more detailed computational complexity analysis:**  Break down the complexity of key steps.
* **Include a discussion of parameter sensitivity:**  Investigate the impact of different parameter settings on performance.
* **Compare the proposed algorithm to a broader range of clustering algorithms.**
* **Provide more justification or proof for the positive semidefiniteness of  ∑m i=1 S ∞ i and ∑N j=1 T ∞ j in Proposition 1.**
