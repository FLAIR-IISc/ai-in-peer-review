

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work for low-rank matrix reconstruction to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation. The authors then apply their algorithm to K-means clustering, claiming it's the first to directly handle the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

**Strengths:**
- The paper presents a theoretically grounded extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction, with a clear connection to existing work in compressed sensing and Bayesian inference.
- The proposed AMP algorithm for K-means clustering shows promising empirical results, outperforming Lloyd's K-means in both artificial and real data experiments.
- The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the relationship between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, providing a novel perspective on an established problem.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technical inconsistencies:** The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine the theoretical foundation. In equation (1), the noise distribution is defined as N(0, mτ), but the text states "N(a, σ²) denotes the Gaussian distribution with mean a and variance σ²," creating a notation conflict. The variance parameter should be consistent with standard notation.

2. **Contradictory assumptions:** The paper states "our formulation does not assume statistical independence among the components of each row of U0 and V0," yet the prior distributions are defined as p̂U(U0) = ∏i p̂u(u0,i) and p̂V(V0) = ∏j p̂v(v0,j), which explicitly assumes independence across rows. This contradiction needs to be resolved.

3. **Inadequate justification:** The authors introduce a parameter β to unify marginalization and MAP problems but provide no clear justification for this parameterization. The claim that "the distribution p̂(U, V |A;β) concentrates on the maxima of p̂(U, V |A) in the limit β → ∞" requires more precise mathematical justification.

4. **Ambiguous experimental details:** The paper states "We ran Lloyd's K-means algorithm and the K-means++ until no change was observed" but doesn't specify what constitutes "no change" (assignment, cluster centers, or loss function). This lack of precision makes it difficult to assess the experimental methodology.

5. **Inconsistent notation:** The paper uses "r̂" for the assumed rank and "r" for the true rank, but the distinction between these is not consistently maintained throughout the paper. For instance, in equation (6), the problem is formulated with "r̂" but the text later refers to "r" without clarification.

6. **Missing details:** In Algorithm 3, the authors state "f∞(b,Λ; p̂v) is piecewise constant with respect to b" but provide no justification or reference for this claim. This is a critical technical detail that requires explanation.

7. **Reference formatting issue:** Reference [20] contains a typo in the title: "Parameterisation" should be "Parameterization" to match standard English spelling.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents a theoretically interesting extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction and shows promising empirical results for clustering, the technical inconsistencies and lack of precision in key aspects significantly undermine the paper's scientific contribution. The contradictions in the assumptions about independence, the inconsistent notation, and the lack of justification for critical technical claims are particularly concerning.

The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to resolve these inconsistencies, provide clearer technical justifications, and ensure precise notation throughout. Without these corrections, the paper's theoretical foundation appears fragile, and the experimental results cannot be fully evaluated.

**Recommendation: Reject**

The paper's contribution is interesting but the technical flaws are too numerous and significant to justify acceptance. The inconsistencies in assumptions and notation, combined with the lack of sufficient justification for key claims, prevent the paper from meeting NIPS's standards for technical rigor. While the empirical results are promising, the theoretical foundation requires substantial strengthening before the paper can be considered for publication.
