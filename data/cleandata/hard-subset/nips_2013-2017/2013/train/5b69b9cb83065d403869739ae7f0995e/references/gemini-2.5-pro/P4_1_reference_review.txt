### Summary

This paper presents an algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. The main contributions are twofold. First, the authors extend a previous AMP algorithm for this problem [9], which was limited to posterior mean estimation. The proposed framework unifies the calculation of marginal posterior distributions (for marginal MAP or posterior mean estimation) and joint MAP estimation through a single inverse temperature parameter `β`. Second, this general algorithm is applied to the problem of K-means clustering. By formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one of the factor matrices consists of one-hot vectors representing cluster assignments, the authors derive a novel iterative algorithm for K-means. This new algorithm, termed AMP-KM, is an alternative to standard methods like Lloyd's algorithm [12]. The work relates to a significant body of literature on AMP, originating in compressed sensing [6], as well as work on Bayesian matrix factorization using methods like MCMC [3] and Variational Bayes (VB) [4, 5]. The key difference from VB/ICM methods is the inclusion of "Onsager reaction terms" in the AMP updates, which are argued to provide a more accurate approximation.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The proposed algorithm (Algorithm 2) is derived from the belief propagation framework, which is a standard and well-founded approach in statistical physics and machine learning. The introduction of the `β` parameter to interpolate between marginalization (`β=1`) and MAP estimation (`β→∞`) is an elegant way to unify these two problems within a single algorithmic framework.

The theoretical analysis, though brief, provides a valuable insight. Proposition 1 establishes that any fixed point of the proposed MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm. This suggests that the AMP algorithm explores the solution space in a potentially more effective way while converging to solutions that are at least as good as those found by ICM from a local optimality perspective.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and supports the paper's claims. On synthetic data, the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) demonstrate superior performance over established baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++ [16], and a VB-based method, particularly as the number of clusters increases. The results on the ORL face database confirm these findings on a real-world dataset, showing that AMP-KM consistently finds solutions with lower K-means loss and higher clustering accuracy than K-means++.

There are, however, some limitations. The convergence of the AMP algorithm is assumed rather than proven, which is a common issue with AMP but deserves mention. The authors note observing oscillations in practice and employ a heuristic stopping criterion. The method for setting the noise variance parameter `τ` in the K-means application is also heuristic (estimated from the residual at each iteration), and a more detailed analysis of its impact would strengthen the work.

### Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors first introduce the general problem of low-rank matrix reconstruction, then lucidly explain how K-means clustering can be cast as a special case. The proposed AMP algorithm is presented, and the roles of its various components are explained. A key strength is the direct comparison of the resulting AMP-KM update rule (Eq. 18b) with that of Lloyd's algorithm (Eq. 7b). This comparison provides an intuitive explanation for the AMP algorithm's improved performance, highlighting the terms that act as a bias correction and encourage exploration.

One area for improvement is the derivation of the main algorithm. The full derivation is deferred to supplementary material, making it difficult for a reader to fully assess the underlying approximations and assumptions from the main text alone. While this is common due to space constraints, a brief summary of the key approximation steps in the main paper would be beneficial.

### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions. While AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction has been proposed before [8, 9], this work extends the framework to handle general priors and MAP estimation, significantly increasing its flexibility and applicability.

The application of this specific AMP framework to K-means clustering is novel. While formulating clustering as matrix factorization is not new [10, 11], the authors claim this is the first method to directly incorporate the hard constraint of assigning each data point to exactly one cluster within a low-rank AMP reconstruction framework. The resulting Algorithm 3 is not a simple re-derivation of existing methods but a new algorithm with a distinct update rule.

The paper acknowledges recent related work on AMP for similar problems [13, 14] but notes that a detailed comparison has not been performed. A more explicit discussion of the differences in model assumptions (e.g., fixed rank `r` in this work vs. asymptotically proportional rank `r/m` in others) and scope would better situate this contribution within the rapidly developing AMP literature.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant. The proposed general framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction is a valuable addition to the suite of tools for this fundamental problem, offering a potentially more powerful alternative to standard VB methods.

The primary significance lies in the derived clustering algorithm. K-means is a cornerstone of unsupervised learning, and the proposed AMP-KM algorithm demonstrably outperforms widely used methods like Lloyd's algorithm and K-means++ on high-dimensional data. Given its competitive computational cost per iteration (O(mN), same as Lloyd's), AMP-KM could become a practical and preferred method for practitioners. The improved performance suggests that the underlying principles of AMP can effectively overcome some of the local minima issues that plague traditional gradient-based clustering algorithms. This work successfully bridges the gap between theoretical concepts from statistical physics (AMP) and a practical, widely used machine learning problem (K-means clustering), advancing the state of the art in a demonstrable way.

### Arguments for acceptance:
1.  Provides a novel and well-motivated AMP-based algorithm for K-means clustering.
2.  The proposed algorithm demonstrates superior empirical performance against strong baselines (K-means++, VBMF) on both synthetic and real-world data.
3.  The general framework for Bayesian low-rank reconstruction is a flexible and valuable extension of prior AMP-based work.
4.  The paper is clearly written and provides an intuitive explanation for the new algorithm's behavior.

### Arguments against acceptance:
1.  The convergence of the algorithm is not theoretically guaranteed, and relies on heuristic stopping criteria in experiments.
2.  The full derivation of the algorithm is omitted from the main text, hindering full scrutiny of the technical details.
3.  The comparison to very recent related AMP literature [13, 14] is superficial.
