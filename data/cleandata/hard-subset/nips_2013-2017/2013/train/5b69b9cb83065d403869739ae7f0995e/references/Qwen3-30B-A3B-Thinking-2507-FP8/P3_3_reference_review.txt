

# Peer Review: Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing

## Summary
This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that extends previous work to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation problems. The authors demonstrate how their framework can be applied to K-means clustering, presenting a novel algorithm that directly addresses the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank reconstruction framework. The paper builds on existing AMP work in compressed sensing and extends it to matrix reconstruction, with promising experimental results showing improved performance over Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes methods.

## Strengths

The paper makes several valuable contributions. First, the unified framework using parameter β to handle both marginalization and MAP problems is elegant and well-motivated. The theoretical analysis (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of their AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm is particularly strong, providing a meaningful connection to established methods. The experimental results are well-designed, using both synthetic and real-world data (ORL faces), and the comparison with multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA, AMP-MA) is comprehensive.

The paper correctly identifies a key limitation in previous work - that AMP algorithms for low-rank reconstruction [9] could only handle posterior mean estimation. By extending this to MAP estimation, they fill an important gap. The application to clustering is also well-justified, with the authors correctly noting that while low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering has been explored before [10,11], their approach is the first to directly handle the cluster assignment constraint within the low-rank framework.

## Weaknesses

The primary weakness is the incomplete theoretical analysis. AMP algorithms are valued at NIPS for their strong theoretical guarantees, particularly state evolution analysis. The authors explicitly state they omit this due to space constraints, but this is a significant omission for a NIPS submission. State evolution would have provided crucial insights into the algorithm's performance and convergence properties. Without it, the theoretical foundation is weaker than expected for a top-tier conference.

The novelty claim regarding clustering needs refinement. While the paper correctly states their algorithm handles the cluster assignment constraint "directly" in the low-rank framework, this is somewhat misleading. Lloyd's K-means algorithm already handles this constraint, and the authors' AMP-KM algorithm is, in essence, a variant of Lloyd's algorithm with different assignment rules (as shown in Equations 18a-18b). The paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between their approach and existing K-means variants.

The computational complexity analysis is also insufficient. The paper states the algorithm has "low computational cost" (O(mN) per iteration), but this is identical to Lloyd's algorithm. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the AMP approach would be faster in practice or when it would provide meaningful speed advantages. The modest improvements in accuracy (0.635 vs. 0.690 on ORL) don't seem to justify the additional algorithmic complexity for many practitioners.

The paper also doesn't sufficiently differentiate itself from recent related work [13,14] on AMP for matrix reconstruction. The authors mention they differ in "fixing the rank r rather than the ratio r/m" and not assuming statistical independence, but don't provide a thorough comparison of the algorithmic differences and their implications.

## Recommendation

**Accept (with minor revisions)**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction with clear applications to clustering. The theoretical foundation is strong where it exists, and the experimental results demonstrate meaningful improvements over baselines, particularly for high-dimensional data. However, the omission of state evolution analysis is a significant weakness for a NIPS submission, and the novelty claims need refinement.

I recommend the authors:

1. Add a concise state evolution analysis to the paper (or at least a brief discussion of how it would work) to strengthen the theoretical foundation
2. Clarify the relationship between their AMP-KM algorithm and existing K-means variants, particularly Lloyd's algorithm
3. Provide a more detailed comparison with recent AMP-based matrix reconstruction work [13,14]
4. Better explain the practical computational advantages of their approach beyond just the asymptotic complexity

With these revisions, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the field. The proposed AMP framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction is well-motivated, and the extension to clustering provides a valuable new perspective on an established problem. The paper makes a meaningful contribution that advances the state of the art in both matrix reconstruction and clustering algorithms.
