## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a well-motivated and technically sound extension of Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithms to Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and its application to K-means clustering. The work is a solid contribution, extending existing AMP frameworks and demonstrating competitive performance, especially in high-dimensional settings. However, the presentation suffers from density and a lack of clear articulation of the novelty relative to the rapidly evolving field of AMP. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary of Main Ideas:**

The paper tackles the problem of reconstructing a low-rank matrix from noisy observations, leveraging a Bayesian framework.  It extends existing AMP algorithms (building on prior work like IterFac and [9]) to handle general rank reconstruction and, crucially, to allow for MAP estimation *in addition* to posterior mean estimation.  A key contribution is demonstrating that K-means clustering can be framed as a specific instance of this low-rank reconstruction problem, leading to a novel AMP-based clustering algorithm.  The authors argue this algorithm outperforms Lloyd’s K-means in high-dimensional scenarios.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper accurately positions itself within the landscape of low-rank matrix reconstruction, referencing key techniques like Bayesian methods, Monte Carlo sampling, and variational Bayes. It correctly points out the computational challenges of Bayesian inference and highlights the advantages of AMP in terms of speed and theoretical guarantees.  The connection to existing AMP work (Donoho et al., Rangan, Matsushita et al.) is clearly established.  However, the discussion of related work could be strengthened.  The recent surge in AMP-based methods for related problems (dictionary learning, blind calibration) is acknowledged but not deeply integrated into the narrative.  Specifically, a more nuanced discussion of how this work *differs* from these concurrent efforts beyond just the rank/dimension scaling and independence assumptions would be beneficial.  

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The derivation of the AMP algorithm is presented rigorously (with details delegated to the supplementary material). The extension to MAP estimation is a valuable contribution, broadening the applicability of the method.
* **Theoretical Motivation:** The paper connects the algorithm to the broader theoretical framework of approximate message passing and Bayesian inference, providing a strong foundation. The mention of state evolution (though not detailed) suggests a path for further theoretical analysis.
* **Novel Application to Clustering:** Framing K-means as a low-rank reconstruction problem and applying AMP offers a potentially advantageous alternative to traditional Lloyd’s algorithm, particularly in high dimensions.
* **Empirical Results:** The numerical experiments demonstrate promising performance, particularly the superiority over Lloyd's K-means in the artificial data experiments and the positive results on the ORL faces dataset.
* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The Bayesian setup and the definition of the problem setting are clear and well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity & Density:** The paper is densely written and could benefit from more intuitive explanations. The numerous equations, while technically correct, can be overwhelming.  A more gradual introduction of the concepts and a stronger emphasis on the intuition behind the derivations would improve readability.
* **Novelty - Incremental, Not Transformative:** While the extensions to AMP are valuable, the overall contribution feels somewhat incremental.  The core ideas build on established principles, and the primary novelty lies in the specific application and adaptation of existing techniques. This isn't necessarily a negative, but the paper would benefit from a more honest assessment of its novelty.
* **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, while well-executed, feel somewhat limited in scope.  A more comprehensive evaluation on a wider range of datasets (particularly larger and more complex real-world datasets) would strengthen the claims.  A comparison to more recent state-of-the-art clustering algorithms beyond Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ would be valuable.
* **State Evolution - Missing Detail:** The paper mentions state evolution as a key benefit of AMP but doesn’t present any results from it. While the authors cite space limitations, even a brief discussion of the expected performance gains based on state evolution would enhance the paper.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:**  The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the algorithm’s performance to parameter choices (e.g., τ). Investigating this would be useful for practical application.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a technically sound and well-motivated extension of AMP to a relevant problem.
* The application to K-means clustering is novel and shows promising results.
* The theoretical foundation is strong, and the potential for further analysis (via state evolution) is evident.
* The paper addresses an important challenge (efficient low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper's contributions are somewhat incremental, lacking a truly breakthrough novelty.
* The presentation is dense and could be significantly improved for clarity.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Improve Clarity:** Re-write key sections with a focus on intuition and explanation, reducing the density of equations.
* **Expand Related Work:**  Provide a more detailed comparison of this work to recent advances in AMP-based methods for similar problems.
* **Enhance Empirical Evaluation:** Evaluate the algorithm on larger and more diverse datasets.  Compare to state-of-the-art clustering algorithms.
* **Discuss Parameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the impact of parameter choices on algorithm performance.
* **Consider Including State Evolution Results:**  If possible, include a brief discussion of expected performance based on state evolution.




**Confidence:** 7/10 – I believe this paper is a solid contribution, but the concerns regarding clarity and incremental novelty prevent me from giving it a higher confidence score.
