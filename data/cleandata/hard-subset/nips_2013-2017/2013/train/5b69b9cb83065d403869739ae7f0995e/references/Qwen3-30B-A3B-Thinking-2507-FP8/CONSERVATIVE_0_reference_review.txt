

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible prior distributions, and applies it to K-means clustering. The authors extend previous AMP work for rank-one matrices to general rank cases, and claim to provide the first algorithm that handles cluster assignment constraints directly within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

## Summary and Relation to Prior Work

The paper builds on existing AMP work for compressed sensing [6,7] and extends it to low-rank matrix reconstruction. It cites [9] as having proposed an AMP algorithm for general-rank cases but limited to posterior mean estimation, while the authors claim their extension handles MAP estimation as well. The clustering application builds on prior work [10,11] that used low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering, but the authors claim their approach is novel in directly handling the exact cluster assignment constraint.

## Strengths

1. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a thorough explanation of the problem setting and proposed algorithm.
2. The computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration) is reasonable and comparable to existing methods.
3. The numerical experiments on both artificial and real data provide some evidence of performance improvements over baselines.
4. The theoretical connection to variational Bayes methods is well-explained, showing how the proposed algorithm relates to existing approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated novelty claims**: The paper claims to be "the first" to handle cluster assignment constraints directly within low-rank matrix reconstruction, but previous work [10,11] has already addressed this. The distinction the authors make (not assuming statistical independence) is minor and not convincingly shown to provide significant benefits.

2. **Insufficient experimental validation**: The performance improvements over baselines are modest (e.g., 0.012 reduction in K-means loss in real data experiments). The paper lacks thorough analysis of when the proposed method outperforms established approaches, and the improvements don't appear substantial enough to justify the novelty claims.

3. **Theoretical gaps**: The paper mentions they could present state evolution analysis but omit it due to space constraints. State evolution is crucial for AMP algorithms to provide theoretical guarantees, and its absence significantly weakens the theoretical foundation.

4. **Lack of convergence guarantees**: While Proposition 1 provides some optimality guarantees for fixed points, the paper doesn't establish global convergence properties or provide evidence of the algorithm's behavior in different problem regimes.

5. **Limited comparison with relevant work**: The paper doesn't sufficiently compare with other AMP-based matrix reconstruction approaches [13,14] that have similar objectives, making it difficult to assess the relative novelty and advantages.

## Assessment of Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound in its presentation, but the claims are not sufficiently supported by either theoretical analysis or comprehensive experimental results. The omission of state evolution analysis is a significant shortcoming for a paper making theoretical claims.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with good explanations of the proposed algorithm. The connection to existing work is reasonably clear.

**Originality**: The novelty is limited. The extension to general-rank and MAP estimation is incremental, and the clustering application doesn't demonstrate sufficiently novel insights beyond existing approaches.

**Significance**: The improvements over baselines are modest and not convincingly demonstrated to be substantial enough to warrant adoption in practice. The paper doesn't provide a compelling argument for why this approach should replace established clustering methods.

## Pro and Con Arguments

**Pro for acceptance**: The paper presents a well-motivated extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with a clear connection to existing work. The algorithm is theoretically grounded and has reasonable computational complexity.

**Con for acceptance**: The claimed advantages are not sufficiently demonstrated through rigorous theoretical analysis or comprehensive experimental validation. The performance improvements over baselines are marginal, and the novelty claims are overstated relative to prior work.

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an incremental extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with clustering applications. While the approach is theoretically sound and the algorithm is well-presented, the paper falls short of demonstrating sufficient novelty and significance to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The modest performance improvements over established baselines, coupled with the lack of rigorous theoretical justification (particularly the omitted state evolution analysis), make it difficult to justify the novelty claims. The paper would benefit from more thorough experimental validation across a wider range of problem settings and a more nuanced comparison with existing AMP-based approaches for matrix reconstruction.

Given the skeptical nature of the reviewer and the preference for established methods, this paper does not meet the high standards required for acceptance at NIPS. The claimed advances are not sufficiently substantiated by the evidence provided.
