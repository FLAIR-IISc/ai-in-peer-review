

# Review: Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work in the AMP framework to handle general-rank cases and flexible structural priors. The authors then apply their algorithm to K-means clustering, claiming to be the first to directly handle the constraint that each data point belongs to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it extends the AMP framework to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction, generalizing previous work that could only handle posterior mean estimation. Second, it applies this framework to clustering, where they derive a novel AMP-based K-means algorithm that avoids the limitations of standard approaches. The authors present theoretical justification for their approach, noting that AMP can better capture dependencies between variables compared to variational Bayes methods, and demonstrate promising empirical results on both synthetic and real datasets.

The paper's key strengths lie in its novelty and potential practical impact. The AMP framework for low-rank matrix reconstruction represents a significant conceptual advance beyond previous variational Bayes approaches, offering a more principled way to handle dependencies between matrix factors. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear theoretical justification for why their AMP-based clustering approach handles the "exactly one cluster" constraint more directly than previous methods. The numerical experiments demonstrate that the AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard K-means and variational Bayes methods in terms of both accuracy and convergence speed, particularly for high-dimensional data.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a good balance between theoretical development and empirical validation. The algorithmic description is precise and easy to follow, and the authors provide sufficient detail to reproduce their results. The computational complexity analysis shows the AMP approach has a linear cost in problem size, which is a significant practical advantage.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that prevent it from being a strong NIPS submission. The most significant is the lack of sufficient justification for the novelty claim. The authors cite previous work on using low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering [10, 11] but don't clearly explain how their approach differs from these methods in handling the "exactly one cluster" constraint. If these previous methods already handled this constraint, the contribution would be less novel. The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with these methods.

Additionally, the theoretical analysis is somewhat superficial. The authors mention they don't discuss state evolution due to space constraints, but this is a key theoretical advantage of AMP algorithms that would strengthen their claim of superiority. The paper also doesn't provide a thorough comparison with other AMP-based matrix reconstruction methods [13, 14], which would better establish the novelty of their approach.

The numerical experiments are promising but somewhat limited. The synthetic data experiments only test a narrow range of problem sizes and dimensions, and the real data experiment on the ORL face database is small-scale (40 clusters, 400 images). The paper claims their approach is particularly effective for high-dimensional data, but doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support this claim across a broader range of high-dimensional problems.

While the paper has a novel algorithmic idea and promising empirical results, the theoretical justification is insufficient and the novelty claim needs stronger support. The work builds on previous AMP research [6, 7, 8, 9] but doesn't clearly distinguish itself from these works in a way that would make it a significant contribution to NIPS.

In terms of the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: Good technical foundation, but theoretical analysis is limited
- Clarity: Excellent writing and organization
- Originality: Moderate novelty (builds on previous AMP work), but the claim of novelty for the clustering application needs stronger justification
- Significance: Potentially significant for high-dimensional clustering, but limited empirical validation

Given NIPS's emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, this paper has some interesting ideas but falls short in providing sufficient evidence of a significant conceptual advance. The work represents a useful extension of existing AMP methods, but it's not sufficiently novel to warrant acceptance at NIPS. The claims about handling the "exactly one cluster" constraint need to be better substantiated against prior work, and the theoretical analysis should be more thorough.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with previous work on low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering, stronger theoretical justification for the AMP approach, and more extensive empirical validation across a broader range of high-dimensional problems. Without these improvements, the paper presents an incremental advancement rather than a significant new direction.

I recommend rejection. While the ideas are interesting and the implementation seems sound, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from previous work to justify acceptance at NIPS. The authors have made a useful contribution to the AMP literature, but it's not novel enough to meet NIPS's high standards.
