## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary:**

This paper presents an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work to handle MAP estimation in the general rank case. A key contribution is applying this AMP framework to K-means clustering, proposing a novel algorithm that directly enforces the single-cluster assignment constraint. The authors demonstrate empirically that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means, particularly in high-dimensional settings. The work builds on the recent surge of interest in AMP for various statistical problems, leveraging its efficiency and theoretical guarantees.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of low-rank matrix reconstruction, referencing key methods like Bayesian approaches, Monte Carlo sampling, and variational Bayes. It acknowledges the success of AMP in compressed sensing and its adaptation to low-rank problems (IterFac, Rangan et al.). The authors clearly differentiate their work by extending previous AMP algorithms to support MAP estimation and by formulating clustering as a constrained low-rank reconstruction problem. The comparison to variational Bayes is apt, highlighting the potential advantages of AMP in capturing dependencies between factors. The paper acknowledges related work in dictionary learning and generalized observation models, but emphasizes its unique focus on fixed rank and factor independence assumptions.  The references are generally comprehensive and relevant.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Application of AMP:** The application of AMP to both general MAP estimation for low-rank reconstruction and specifically to K-means clustering is a significant contribution.  The adaptation of AMP to directly handle the hard clustering constraint is particularly clever.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a theoretical underpinning for the algorithm, drawing connections to belief propagation and the Bethe approximation. Proposition 1, guaranteeing optimality under certain conditions, is a valuable theoretical result.
* **Efficiency:** The authors highlight the linear computational complexity of the AMP algorithm, which is a major advantage for large-scale problems.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments on both artificial and real-world data demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithms. The consistent outperformance of the AMP-KM algorithm compared to standard K-means on high-dimensional data is compelling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The equations are clearly presented, and the authors provide sufficient detail to understand the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

* **State Evolution Absence:** While mentioning the benefits of state evolution analysis, the paper doesn’t present any actual results from it. Including state evolution-based performance predictions would significantly strengthen the theoretical analysis and provide a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The practical implementation relies on tuning parameters like τ.  The paper does not thoroughly discuss parameter selection strategies or sensitivity to parameter values. A more in-depth analysis of the parameter’s impact would be beneficial.
* **Convergence Guarantee:** While Proposition 1 provides some theoretical assurance, a formal proof of convergence for the general AMP algorithm is lacking.  The authors acknowledge this limitation, but addressing it would strengthen the work.
* **Limited Comparison:** The comparison to other state-of-the-art clustering algorithms is somewhat limited.  Including comparisons to more recent and sophisticated clustering methods (e.g., spectral clustering, DBSCAN) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
* **Clarity on Prior Selection:** The paper emphasizes the flexibility of choosing priors, but doesn’t offer much guidance on *how* to select appropriate priors for specific applications.  A discussion of prior selection strategies would be valuable.
* **Implementation Details:** The paper lacks information regarding the implementation, making reproducibility difficult.

**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
* The empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains over existing methods, particularly in high dimensions.
* The work is well-written and clearly presented.
* The topic is relevant to the NIPS community.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The absence of state evolution analysis limits the depth of the theoretical contribution.
* The lack of a formal convergence proof is a concern.
* The limited comparison to other clustering algorithms makes it difficult to assess the algorithm’s overall performance.
* The paper lacks implementation details, hindering reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant potential. The novel application of AMP to clustering, combined with the theoretical justification and empirical results, makes it a valuable contribution. While the weaknesses mentioned above are present, they do not fundamentally detract from the quality of the work. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Include a discussion of state evolution analysis** and present some results.
2.  **Elaborate on parameter selection strategies** for the algorithm.
3.  **Provide more detailed implementation information** to facilitate reproducibility.
4.  **Expand the comparison to include a broader range of state-of-the-art clustering algorithms.**
5.  **Discuss prior selection strategies.**



I believe that addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and solidify its position as a valuable contribution to the NIPS community.
