### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents an algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. The primary goal is to reconstruct a low-rank matrix `A0 = U0V0^T` from noisy observations, allowing for flexible prior distributions on the factors `U0` and `V0` to incorporate structural knowledge like sparsity or non-negativity.

The paper makes two main contributions. First, it extends a previous AMP algorithm for this problem [9] to handle not only posterior mean estimation (the marginalization problem) but also maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is elegantly achieved by introducing an inverse temperature parameter `β` into the posterior distribution, which unifies both inference tasks: `β=1` for marginalization and `β→∞` for MAP estimation. The derived algorithm (Algorithm 2) is general, computationally efficient (linear in the number of matrix elements per iteration), and includes additional "reaction" terms not present in related Variational Bayes (VB) or Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithms, which can lead to better performance.

The second contribution is a novel and efficient K-means clustering algorithm derived by applying the general AMP framework. The authors formulate K-means as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one factor matrix (`V`) consists of one-hot vectors representing cluster assignments. The resulting AMP-K-means algorithm (Algorithm 3) has an intuitive update rule that, unlike Lloyd's algorithm, includes terms that correct for the influence of a data point on its own cluster center and favor assignments to smaller clusters. Numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms outperform standard baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and VB-based methods in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Effective Clustering Algorithm:** The application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering is a significant strength. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3) is not just a straightforward application of a known technique but yields a novel update rule with an intuitive interpretation (Eq. 18b). The experimental results convincingly show that this new algorithm finds better local minima than strong baselines like K-means and K-means++, leading to lower K-means loss and higher accuracy. This is a valuable practical contribution.

2.  **General and Flexible Framework:** The paper presents a general AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that can accommodate a wide variety of priors on the factor matrices. The unification of marginalization and MAP problems via the `β` parameter is elegant and increases the utility of the framework. This provides a powerful tool for a broad class of problems beyond clustering.

3.  **Solid Theoretical Grounding:** The algorithm is well-grounded in the theory of message passing on graphical models. The authors clearly connect their approach to the Bethe approximation and highlight its relationship to VB methods. Proposition 1 is a particularly nice result, showing that any fixed point of the proposed MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of the ICM algorithm. This provides a theoretical justification for why the AMP algorithm might avoid some of the poor local minima that plague ICM/Lloyd's algorithm.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the proposed algorithm is presented clearly, and the connections to previous work are well-articulated. This makes the paper's contributions accessible and understandable.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Convergence Properties:** The paper assumes convergence of the proposed algorithm, which is a common practice in the AMP literature but remains a practical concern. The authors mention observing oscillations in the experiments, which required a modified stopping condition (`V^t = V^{t-2}`). A brief discussion on the convergence behavior of AMP in this setting, and perhaps mentioning standard techniques like damping to mitigate oscillations, would strengthen the paper and provide valuable guidance for practitioners.

2.  **Estimation of Noise Variance `τ`:** In the K-means application, the parameter `τ` is not part of the original problem formulation but is inherited from the general Bayesian model. The authors propose a reasonable heuristic to estimate it at each iteration. However, the sensitivity of the algorithm's performance to this estimation scheme is not explored. It would be beneficial to add a short discussion or an ablation study on how this choice affects the final clustering result.

3.  **Computational Time Analysis:** The paper reports the number of iterations but not the wall-clock time. While the asymptotic complexity per iteration is the same as Lloyd's algorithm (`O(mNr̂)`), the constant factors involved in the AMP updates might be larger. A comparison of the actual runtime against the baselines would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practical efficiency. For instance, in the ORL faces experiment, AMP-KM took slightly more iterations on average than K-means++. Does this translate to a longer runtime?

4.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experimental evaluation is solid but could be extended to further strengthen the paper's claims. The claim that the algorithm is particularly effective for high-dimensional data is compelling but supported by only one real-world dataset. Evaluating the performance on additional high-dimensional datasets or analyzing how the performance gap between AMP-KM and Lloyd's algorithm scales with the data dimension `m` would make the results more conclusive.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and high-performing clustering algorithm that demonstrably outperforms standard methods.
*   Provides a general and flexible AMP framework for a fundamental problem in machine learning (Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction).
*   The work is technically sound, with good theoretical motivation and supporting analysis (Proposition 1).
*   The paper is clear, well-written, and positions its contributions effectively within the existing literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The discussion on practical aspects like convergence and parameter (`τ`) sensitivity could be more detailed.
*   The experimental validation, while positive, could be broadened to include more datasets and a runtime analysis.

### Conclusion

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes strong methodological and practical contributions. The derivation of a new, superior K-means algorithm from the principled AMP framework is a significant achievement. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the scope of discussion and experimentation rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology. The paper is well-suited for NeurIPS, and its ideas are likely to be built upon by other researchers in both the message-passing and clustering communities. I recommend acceptance.
