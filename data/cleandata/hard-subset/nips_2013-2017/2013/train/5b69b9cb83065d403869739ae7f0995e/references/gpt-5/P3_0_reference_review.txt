Summary and contributions
The paper develops an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction under flexible priors on the factors. The authors introduce a “temperature” parameter β, so that β=1 corresponds to marginal (MMSE) inference and β→∞ yields a MAP algorithm. The resulting iterations (Alg. 2) use generic denoisers f and their Jacobians/covariances G built from the assumed priors p̂u, p̂v; removing the Onsager-like correction terms recovers variational-Bayes/ICM updates. A fixed-point optimality proposition shows that at convergence, each block (U or V) is globally optimal given the other, under mild PSD conditions.

As a second contribution, the authors instantiate the framework for clustering by adopting a one-hot prior on V and a flat prior on U. This yields an AMP variant of K-means (Alg. 3) with two interpretable “Onsager” corrections in the assignment step: a leave-one-out debiasing term for the currently assigned cluster and a preference toward small clusters. They also propose a “maximum accuracy” clustering (β=1) that approximates marginal MAP assignments.

Empirically, the AMP-KMeans variant reduces K-means loss and increases accuracy over standard Lloyd and K-means++ on synthetic data; β=1 AMP achieves the highest accuracy among compared methods. On the ORL faces dataset, AMP-KMeans improves K-means++ in most trials (though it collapses to a single cluster in 2/50 runs).

Relation to prior work
The algorithm builds on AMP for linear inverse problems [Donoho et al.; Rangan] and bilinear/matrix factorization AMP variants (e.g., IterFac for rank-1; Matsushita & Tanaka; BiG-AMP/Parker et al.; Krzakala et al.). The paper’s β-tempering unifies MMSE and MAP within a single AMP derivation; the denoiser-centric view is broadly consistent with the generalized/bilinear AMP literature, where both MMSE and MAP denoisers are commonly considered. The authors emphasize two differences: (i) fixing rank r (rather than aspect ratio) in the large-system limit used for derivation, and (ii) allowing within-row dependencies in p̂u, p̂v (priors factorize over rows but not over components within a row). The clustering formulation as AMP on a one-hot prior is natural and seems novel in directly enforcing the “exactly one cluster” constraint within an AMP bilinear factorization.

Quality and technical soundness
- Algorithmic derivation: The update structure and Onsager terms are consistent with bilinear AMP. The β-tempering and transition from f,G to f∞,G∞ for MAP is standard. However, the full derivations and any state evolution (SE) analysis are deferred; no convergence guarantees are provided, and the algorithm can oscillate (authors stop when Vt repeats).
- Proposition 1: The block-wise global optimality at fixed points is a mild guarantee (also satisfied by ICM at its fixed points). It does not ensure descent or convergence to good minima; still, it clarifies that AMP fixed points are not worse (in count) than ICM fixed points.
- Clustering specialization: The assignment score correction in (18b) is intuitive (a leave-one-out debiasing and a balancing term) and follows from the AMP Onsager terms. The practical estimation of τ by the residual is heuristic; its stability/consistency is not analyzed.
- Complexity: O(mN) per iteration is attractive; but for general priors the r̂-dimensional integrals/minimizations in f and G can be expensive unless closed-form denoisers exist (as in the one-hot prior).

Empirical evaluation
- Synthetic: Only one noise level (τ=0.1), balanced clusters, and m=800,N=1600. AMP-KM reduces K-means loss relative to Lloyd/K-means++. β=1 AMP achieves higher accuracy than VBMF. It would be important to probe sensitivity to τ, cluster imbalance, misspecified r̂, and higher ranks where the r̂-dimensional denoisers become nontrivial.
- Real: ORL faces are high-dimensional; K-means on raw pixels is a weak baseline. No PCA/whitening reported, and AMP-KM sometimes collapsed. No timing comparisons, damping/stepsize details, or robustness analysis. Comparisons omit EM for spherical GMMs, spectral clustering, spherical K-means, or recent convex relaxations.

Originality and significance
- Originality: The β-tempered AMP in the bilinear setting and the direct one-hot prior instantiation for clustering are interesting. However, the claim that prior AMP for matrix factorization could “only treat posterior means” seems too strong: the denoiser framework in generalized/bilinear AMP typically allows both MMSE and MAP choices. The novelty then hinges on the explicit derivation and the K-means specialization with clear assignment corrections.
- Significance: If robust, the AMP-KMeans variant is appealing: same per-iteration cost as K-means, better solutions, and a principled leave-one-out correction. The broader AMP framework for matrix factorization with structured priors could be useful. The lack of SE, limited empirical breadth, and occasional failure modes reduce immediate impact.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with good intuition for the Onsager corrections in clustering. Important derivations, SE, and convergence strategies are in the supplement or omitted. Some notation could be tightened (e.g., specify when S^t_i collapses to a common S^t in Alg. 3; articulate τ estimation and damping).

Pros
- Clear AMP formulation unifying MMSE and MAP via β.
- Intuitive and efficient AMP-based K-means with principled assignment corrections.
- Demonstrated improvements over Lloyd/K-means++ on synthetic and a real dataset.
- Flexibility to incorporate within-row dependent priors.

Cons
- Limited novelty versus existing bilinear/GAMP literature; MAP denoisers are not new.
- No state evolution, limited theory; convergence is not guaranteed and oscillations observed.
- Experiments are narrow; missing stronger clustering baselines and robustness analyses.
- Practical guidance (damping, initialization, τ estimation) and runtime comparisons are sparse.

Questions for the authors
1) Please clarify the novelty versus BiG-AMP (Parker et al.) and whether your β→∞ specialization differs from MAP-BiG-AMP beyond the fixed-r scaling and within-row dependencies.
2) Do you employ damping or adaptive steps to prevent oscillations? How sensitive are results to τ estimation and initialization?
3) Can you provide SE predictions (even heuristic) and compare to empirical MSE/accuracy?
4) How does AMP-KM perform with imbalanced clusters, misspecified r̂, or varying τ?
5) Runtime comparisons to K-means/K-means++ and EM for spherical GMM?

Minor comments
- Cite and discuss spherical K-means and EM for equal-variance GMMs as baselines.
- Consider PCA/whitening for ORL; report accuracy with standard preprocessing.
- Provide code to encourage adoption.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is promising and contains a neat AMP perspective on K-means with appealing intuition and some empirical gains. However, the novelty relative to established bilinear AMP work is not fully established, theoretical support is limited (no SE), and the experimental evaluation is too narrow to substantiate robustness and impact. Strengthening comparisons, adding SE/robustness analysis, and clarifying novelty could move this to accept.

Reviewer confidence: high.
