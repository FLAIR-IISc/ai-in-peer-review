

# Peer Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation problems. The authors derive a unified AMP framework that can be specialized to handle different estimation tasks, including marginal posterior means and MAP estimates. The second contribution applies this framework to K-means clustering, presenting a novel algorithm that directly handles the constraint that each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach through extensive numerical experiments on artificial and real datasets, showing improvements over existing methods like Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes matrix factorization.

The work builds upon prior AMP literature in compressed sensing [6,7,8,9] and connects to previous low-rank matrix reconstruction approaches [3-5,10-11]. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work by handling both marginalization and MAP problems in a unified framework and by directly addressing the cluster assignment constraint in clustering.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with a well-constructed theoretical foundation. The derivation of the AMP algorithm is rigorous, and the authors provide a convergence analysis (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm, with the additional benefit of potentially avoiding poor local minima. The numerical experiments are comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines on both synthetic and real-world data (ORL Face Database), with appropriate performance metrics (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, and convergence speed).

The paper acknowledges limitations, particularly noting that convergence is not guaranteed in general and discussing the assumptions required for their convergence analysis. The experimental design is strong, with 500 problem instances for synthetic data and 50 trials for real data to ensure statistical significance.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem setting, theoretical development, and algorithmic presentation are clear and logically structured. The mathematical notation is consistent, well-defined, and appropriate for the topic. The pseudocode for the proposed algorithms (Algorithms 2 and 3) is particularly clear and would enable reproducibility by other researchers.

The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-explained, making the application of the AMP framework to clustering intuitive and compelling. The authors effectively use equations and figures (Figures 1 and 2) to illustrate key results.

## Originality

The paper makes two clear original contributions. First, it extends previous AMP-based low-rank matrix reconstruction methods [8,9] to handle both marginalization and MAP problems in a unified framework, which is a significant improvement over prior work that could only handle posterior mean estimation. Second, the application to clustering is presented as novel, with the authors correctly noting that while low-rank matrix reconstruction has been used for clustering before [10,11], their approach is the first to directly handle the constraint that each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster within this framework.

The paper thoroughly contrasts their approach with related work, making it clear how their contributions differ from previous efforts. The authors cite relevant prior work appropriately and demonstrate a deep understanding of the field.

## Significance

The proposed AMP algorithm has significant practical value due to its low computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration) and demonstrated performance improvements over existing methods. The paper shows that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means in both accuracy and convergence speed, particularly for higher-dimensional data where traditional methods struggle.

The work advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering by providing a theoretically grounded, computationally efficient method that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation. The authors' demonstration of improved performance on real-world data (ORL Face Database) further strengthens the practical significance of their work.

## Strengths

1. Clear theoretical foundation with rigorous derivation of the AMP algorithm
2. Unified framework for both marginalization and MAP problems
3. Novel application to clustering that properly handles the one-cluster-per-data constraint
4. Comprehensive experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real data
5. Strong theoretical analysis showing connections to ICM algorithm
6. Well-written with clear pseudocode and organization

## Weaknesses

1. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the performance gap between AMP and Lloyd's K-means increases with higher r (number of clusters), which is a key finding in Figure 1.
2. The claim that their clustering method is the first to directly handle the one-cluster-per-data constraint could be strengthened by more thorough comparison with [10,11].
3. The paper mentions that AMP for maximum accuracy clustering converges faster than variational Bayes, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this observation.
4. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the experimental results (e.g., t-tests for the differences in performance between methods).
5. The explanation for why τ appears in the algorithm despite not being part of the K-means problem is somewhat superficial.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- The paper presents a technically sound extension of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with both marginalization and MAP capabilities
- The clustering application is novel and well-motivated
- The theoretical analysis and experimental results provide strong evidence of the method's advantages
- The algorithm has low computational complexity (O(mN)) and demonstrates superior performance over existing methods
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- The explanation for why the performance gap increases with higher r needs more detail
- The comparison with [10,11] could be more thorough
- The statistical significance of the experimental results isn't rigorously established
- The explanation for the parameter τ could be more complete

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes clear theoretical and practical contributions to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The proposed AMP algorithm is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated. The paper's clarity, thoroughness, and significance make it a valuable contribution to NIPS.

The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and could be addressed in the camera-ready version. The paper meets all four criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it's technically sound, clearly written, original, and significant.

I recommend acceptance with confidence. The paper advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, with a theoretically grounded algorithm that demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods.
