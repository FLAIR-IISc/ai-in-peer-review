

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering. The authors claim their AMP-based approach extends previous work by handling both marginalization and MAP estimation problems, and they present a novel clustering algorithm that directly addresses the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with precise algorithm descriptions. However, as a skeptical reviewer, I find significant shortcomings in the theoretical foundation, experimental validation, and claims of novelty.

The theoretical contribution is weak. The paper cites [9] as a previous AMP algorithm for general-rank case but fails to adequately explain how their approach differs or improves upon it. Proposition 1, which claims optimality of fixed points, is presented without proof and lacks discussion of the restrictive conditions under which it holds. The assertion that the AMP algorithm is "asymptotically exact" for large-scale problems is unsupported by rigorous analysis.

The clustering application is presented as novel, but the claim that their algorithm "directly deals with the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster" is misleading. Standard K-means algorithms enforce this constraint as well, and the paper doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from existing low-rank matrix factorization approaches to clustering (citing [10, 11] without thorough comparison).

The experimental evaluation shows modest improvements over baselines but lacks statistical significance testing. Figure 1 demonstrates small differences in K-means loss and accuracy (e.g., 0.002-0.005 in K-means loss), but without error bars or statistical validation, it's impossible to determine if these differences are meaningful. The paper also fails to provide runtime comparisons, which is critical for assessing practical utility given that Lloyd's K-means is already extremely efficient.

I'm particularly concerned about the paper's claim that "the AMP algorithm for the K-means clustering achieves the smallest K-means loss among the five algorithms." The improvement is marginal, and the paper doesn't convincingly argue why this small improvement justifies the added complexity of the AMP approach over the widely used and highly optimized Lloyd's algorithm.

The paper also neglects to address limitations of their approach. For instance, they don't discuss performance under non-Gaussian noise or when the low-rank assumption is violated. The claim that "the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering achieves the highest accuracy" lacks analysis of when this advantage might disappear.

The paper's contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. While AMP has been successfully applied to various problems, the authors don't convincingly demonstrate substantial benefits over established methods for clustering. The experiments are limited to artificial data and the ORL face database without testing on more challenging or diverse datasets.

In summary, while the paper presents a technically sound algorithm, it lacks sufficient evidence of significant improvement over established methods. The claims of novelty and performance advantages are not adequately supported by the experimental results or theoretical analysis. The paper would be strengthened by more rigorous comparison with relevant prior work, statistical validation of results, and clearer explanation of the practical benefits.

Given my skepticism toward unproven approaches without extensive validation, I recommend rejection. The authors would need to provide substantially stronger evidence of significant improvements over established baselines to justify the novelty claim.
