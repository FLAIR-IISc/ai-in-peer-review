

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending the AMP framework from vector estimation to matrix factorization problems. The authors then apply this algorithm to clustering by demonstrating that clustering can be formulated as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional constraints. The paper makes two key contributions: (1) an AMP algorithm that handles both marginalization (posterior mean estimation) and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction, and (2) a novel clustering algorithm based on this AMP approach.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their AMP algorithm, building upon established AMP theory from compressed sensing [6, 7, 8, 9]. The derivation of the algorithm is well-motivated and properly contextualized within existing work. The proof of Proposition 1 (showing that fixed points of the AMP algorithm for MAP problems are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm) is particularly valuable, as it provides theoretical justification for the algorithm's behavior. The numerical experiments are comprehensive, including both synthetic data (500 problem instances across varying values of r) and a real-world dataset (ORL Faces), with careful evaluation of multiple performance metrics (K-means loss, accuracy, and convergence speed). The results consistently show that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms traditional methods (Lloyd's K-means, variational Bayes) in accuracy and convergence speed. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the fact that their AMP algorithm for K-means clustering requires more iterations (8.8 vs 6.6 for K-means++) on the real dataset, which should be reconciled with the theoretical O(1) iteration complexity claim.

## Clarity
The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. It follows a logical structure that guides the reader from problem definition to algorithm derivation to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-explained, and the algorithms are presented in a clear, step-by-step manner that makes them reproducible. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is particularly well-explained, with Algorithm 3 (AMP for K-means clustering) providing a concrete implementation that is easy to follow. The paper also effectively differentiates their work from related approaches [9, 13, 14] by highlighting key differences in formulation and assumptions, which enhances clarity.

## Originality
The paper makes a significant original contribution by extending AMP to handle low-rank matrix reconstruction, which represents a non-trivial extension beyond previous work on AMP for vector estimation. While the idea of using low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering is not new [10, 11], the authors' AMP-based approach is novel in that it directly handles the constraint that each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. This is a clear distinction from previous approaches that either use iterative methods without this constraint or handle it through additional heuristics. The paper also provides a unified framework that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation through a single parameter β, which is a valuable theoretical contribution. The authors properly contextualize their work against recent related papers [9, 13, 14], demonstrating a clear understanding of the field.

## Significance
The significance of this work is substantial. Efficient and accurate clustering of high-dimensional data is a fundamental problem in machine learning, and the proposed AMP-based approach offers clear advantages over existing methods. The empirical results demonstrate that the AMP algorithm achieves lower K-means loss and higher accuracy than both Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes methods, while also converging faster in the maximum accuracy clustering setting. The theoretical connection between AMP and clustering provides a unifying perspective that could inspire future work in both areas. The ability to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation within the same framework adds valuable flexibility for different application scenarios, making this work particularly significant for practitioners.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Clear theoretical foundation with a well-justified extension of AMP to matrix factorization
- Comprehensive numerical experiments demonstrating clear performance advantages
- Novel application of AMP to clustering with direct handling of cluster assignment constraints
- Well-written and well-structured paper with clear presentation of algorithms
- Proper contextualization against related work, highlighting key differences

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper could better explain why AMP would outperform ICM for clustering, beyond the empirical results (the theoretical justification for AMP's superiority is somewhat thin)
- The claimed O(1) iteration complexity seems to contradict the experimental results (8.8 vs 6.6 iterations), which needs clarification
- The scalability to larger datasets (beyond m=800, N=1600) is not demonstrated

## Overall Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results are compelling, and the paper is exceptionally well-written. The main weakness is the lack of strong theoretical justification for why AMP would outperform ICM for the specific clustering problem, though the empirical results do demonstrate superior performance. The claimed O(1) iteration complexity should be reconciled with the experimental results.

The paper addresses an important problem with a novel approach that advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way. Given the quality of the work and its significance to the machine learning community, I strongly recommend acceptance for NIPS. The paper meets all four criteria (Quality, Clarity, Originality, Significance) at a high level, making it an excellent candidate for publication.
