This paper introduces an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and extends it to the K-means clustering problem. The core idea is to leverage the flexibility of the Bayesian approach while mitigating computational costs through AMP, building upon prior work in compressed sensing and existing AMP algorithms for low-rank matrices. The authors extend previous AMP algorithms to handle MAP estimation and demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness in clustering, particularly in high-dimensional settings, outperforming Lloyd’s K-means algorithm.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in matrix reconstruction and clustering – balancing Bayesian flexibility with computational feasibility. The application of AMP to this problem is a natural progression given its successes in related fields. The extension to K-means clustering as a special case of low-rank reconstruction is a clever and potentially impactful contribution. The authors clearly articulate the connections to existing work, including variational Bayes methods and previous AMP-based approaches, differentiating their contributions through the ability to perform MAP estimation and a different scaling limit. The theoretical grounding, albeit sketched out due to space limitations, hints at a deeper understanding of the algorithm’s behavior.  The numerical experiments, using both synthetic and real data, provide valuable empirical evidence supporting the claims.

However, several weaknesses need to be addressed. While the paper mentions the state evolution analysis, its omission is a significant drawback. A discussion of the algorithm’s theoretical performance guarantees, even briefly, would strengthen the contribution substantially. The justification for the scaling used in the noise variance (factor of *m*) is not entirely clear and could benefit from further elaboration. The connection to existing work on dictionary learning and blind calibration [13, 14] is mentioned, but the differences are presented as rather superficial (different scaling, independence assumptions). A more detailed comparative analysis, perhaps with a small experiment, would be beneficial. The algorithmic presentation (Algorithm 2) is dense and lacks intuitive explanations for each step. Providing a more step-by-step breakdown with interpretations of each equation would greatly improve clarity.  The parameter τ in Algorithm 3 seems somewhat ad-hoc, arising from the derivation. A more principled justification for its estimation would be helpful. The explanation surrounding the convergence criterion for Algorithm 3 (oscillations in V) is a bit hand-wavy and could be clarified. Finally, the statement that the proposed algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means "when data are high-dimensional" is vague; defining what constitutes "high-dimensional" in the context of the experiments would be useful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The extension of AMP to MAP estimation in low-rank matrix reconstruction is a novel contribution. The application to K-means clustering, framing it within the low-rank reconstruction paradigm and addressing the single-cluster assignment constraint directly, is a unique approach.
*   **Significance:** The potential for improved performance in high-dimensional clustering tasks is significant, particularly given the limitations of traditional K-means.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The derivation builds upon established principles of AMP and Bayesian inference. The numerical results demonstrate the algorithm’s effectiveness.
*   **Clarity:** While dense, the paper's core concepts are understandable, and the connections to relevant literature are well-established.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Detail:** The omission of the state evolution analysis limits the theoretical understanding of the algorithm's performance.
*   **Algorithmic Clarity:** The presentation of Algorithm 2 is dense and requires significant effort to follow.
*   **Limited Comparative Analysis:** The comparison to related work, particularly [13, 14], feels superficial.
*   **Ad-hoc Parameter Estimation:** The justification for estimating τ in Algorithm 3 lacks a strong theoretical basis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**.  While the ideas are promising and the contributions are novel, the lack of theoretical depth and clarity in the algorithmic presentation prevent it from meeting the standard for NIPS acceptance in its current form.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly including the state evolution analysis, providing a more detailed explanation of the algorithm, and strengthening the comparative analysis – would significantly improve the paper’s quality and increase its chances of acceptance.  The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the field, and with further refinement, this work could become a valuable contribution to the literature.
