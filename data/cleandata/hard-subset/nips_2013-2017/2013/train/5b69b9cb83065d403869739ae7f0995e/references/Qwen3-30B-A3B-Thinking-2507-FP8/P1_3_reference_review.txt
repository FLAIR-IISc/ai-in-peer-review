

# Peer Review: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

## Summary

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous work on AMP for matrix factorization. The authors develop a unified framework that can handle both marginalization problems (posterior mean estimation) and MAP problems (maximum a posteriori estimation) within the same algorithmic structure. The key innovation is applying this AMP framework to K-means clustering, where they demonstrate that their method directly handles the constraint that each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. The authors provide theoretical analysis, including a convergence result (Proposition 1), and extensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world datasets, showing that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard K-means and variational Bayes methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

The paper builds on the growing literature of AMP algorithms for sparse signal recovery (Donoho et al., 2009) and extends it to matrix reconstruction (Rangan & Fletcher, 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka, 2012). It distinguishes itself from prior work by handling both marginalization and MAP problems within the same framework, and by providing a principled approach to clustering that directly addresses the constraint of single-cluster assignments.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound approach with solid theoretical foundations. The derivation of the AMP algorithm for the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem is well-justified and builds logically on previous AMP work. The authors provide a clear convergence analysis (Proposition 1) showing that fixed points of their AMP algorithm are also fixed points of the ICM algorithm, with the additional benefit of potentially avoiding bad local minima. The computational complexity (O(mN) per iteration) is well-motivated and appropriate for large-scale problems.

**Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The synthetic data experiments demonstrate consistent performance improvements over multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA) across different cluster numbers. The real-world experiments on the ORL face dataset provide compelling evidence of the algorithm's practical utility, with the AMP algorithm outperforming K-means++ in 48/50 trials. The authors report multiple metrics (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, convergence speed) and provide standard deviations across 500 synthetic instances, which enhances the reliability of their claims.

**Novelty and Significance:** The paper makes a significant contribution by extending AMP to handle both marginalization and MAP problems in low-rank matrix reconstruction, which previous AMP algorithms for matrix factorization could not do. The application to clustering is particularly novel, as the authors claim it's the first method to directly handle the "exactly one cluster" constraint within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. This represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches that either used low-rank reconstruction as a preprocessing step for clustering (Xu et al., 2003) or handled the constraint implicitly.

## Weaknesses

**Lack of Intuition for Performance Gains:** While the paper demonstrates that their AMP algorithm outperforms baselines, it doesn't provide sufficient theoretical or intuitive explanation for *why* it achieves better performance. The connection between the AMP framework and the improved clustering results is not deeply explored. For example, the paper states that "the AMP algorithm for maximum accuracy clustering achieves the highest accuracy" but doesn't explain why the AMP framework leads to better cluster assignments.

**Limited Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper compares against Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ but doesn't include more recent clustering algorithms that have been proposed in the literature. For instance, the paper could have compared against methods like the k-means++ initialization variant (Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007) or other advanced clustering approaches that handle high-dimensional data more effectively.

**Parameter Sensitivity:** The parameter τ appears in the algorithm but isn't well-motivated in the context of the K-means problem. The authors propose estimating it as m⁻²N⁻¹∥A - Uᵗ(Vᵗ)⊤∥²F, but the paper doesn't explore how sensitive the algorithm is to this estimation or whether different estimation strategies would affect performance.

**Clarity of Algorithm 3:** The rewritten form of the AMP algorithm for K-means (Algorithm 3) contains a complex term 2m(nᵗₗ)⁻¹I(vᵗⱼ = eₗ) that isn't sufficiently explained. While the paper mentions this corrects for bias, the intuition for this specific correction isn't clear to readers unfamiliar with the AMP framework.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of low-rank matrix factorization and clustering. The theoretical development is sound, the experiments are comprehensive, and the application to clustering represents a novel and significant advancement. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from previous work and demonstrates clear empirical advantages over existing methods.

The main weakness is the lack of sufficient explanation for *why* the AMP approach outperforms other methods, which would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution. Additionally, the comparison with more recent clustering algorithms would provide a more complete picture of the method's relative strengths.

## Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The work represents a significant technical advance that is well-supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical results. The paper is clearly written, makes a distinct contribution to the field, and demonstrates clear significance through its improved performance on clustering tasks, particularly in high-dimensional settings. While there are some minor weaknesses in explaining the performance gains, these do not detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work.

The paper's contribution is sufficiently distinct from previous NIPS papers on AMP (e.g., Donoho et al., 2009; Rangan & Fletcher, 2012) and makes a clear advancement in applying AMP to a new class of problems (clustering with exact assignment constraints). The results have clear potential for impact in machine learning practice, particularly for high-dimensional clustering tasks.

**Final Score: 8 (Strong Accept)**
