This paper presents a flexible and efficient algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The authors' main contributions are twofold. First, they extend a previous AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix recovery to handle general estimation tasks, including Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation, by introducing an inverse temperature parameter. This provides a unified framework for both posterior marginalization and MAP problems. Second, they apply this framework to the problem of K-means clustering by formulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one of the factor matrices is constrained to have one-hot encoded rows. This novel application leads to an iterative clustering algorithm that is similar in spirit to Lloyd's algorithm but includes additional corrective terms. This work builds upon the well-established AMP literature, which originated in compressed sensing (Donoho et al., 2009) and has been extended to various matrix factorization problems (Rangan et al., 2012; Parker et al., 2013). The idea of formulating clustering as a matrix factorization problem is also not new (Ding et al., 2010), but the paper's contribution lies in deriving a principled, message-passing-based algorithm that directly incorporates the discrete cluster assignment constraints. The authors provide empirical evidence on synthetic and real datasets showing that their proposed algorithms outperform standard baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a variational Bayes approach.

### Strengths

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically solid. The proposed AMP algorithm (Algorithm 2) is a sensible extension of existing work. The theoretical analysis, particularly Proposition 1, provides valuable insight by connecting the fixed points of the proposed MAP algorithm to those of the widely used Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm. This result suggests that the proposed algorithm may be less prone to getting stuck in poor local minima, a claim which is supported by the experimental results. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and metrics, and demonstrate a clear performance advantage for the proposed methods on the tested problems.

*   **Originality and Significance:** The primary novelty lies in the elegant application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering. This provides a fresh and principled perspective on a classic, fundamental problem in machine learning. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3, Eq. 18b) is non-trivial and its update rule has a compelling intuitive interpretation: it corrects for the bias of a cluster center towards the data points currently assigned to it and favors assignments to smaller clusters. This could be a significant contribution if the observed performance gains hold more broadly, potentially offering a powerful new tool for practitioners. The general algorithm for Bayesian matrix reconstruction is also a valuable contribution, providing a flexible and efficient inference tool with potential applications beyond clustering.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The problem is well-motivated, and the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is explained very well. The authors do a good job of interpreting the terms in their final clustering algorithm, making the differences from Lloyd's algorithm transparent and intuitive.

### Weaknesses

*   **Clarity of Derivation:** The derivation of the main AMP algorithm is entirely deferred to the supplementary material. While this is understandable due to page limits, it makes it difficult for the reader to fully appreciate the technical details and assumptions underlying the algorithm from the main paper alone. A brief sketch of the derivation or key approximation steps would have improved the self-containedness of the paper.

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** As is common with AMP-style algorithms, convergence is not guaranteed and is assumed in the analysis. The authors note observing oscillations in experiments, which highlights the practical relevance of this issue. Furthermore, one of the key theoretical advantages of AMP—the ability to precisely characterize its asymptotic performance via state evolution—is mentioned only in passing. Including even a brief discussion of the state evolution equations and the insights they provide would have significantly strengthened the paper's theoretical contribution.

*   **Heuristics and Parameter Sensitivity:** The proposed method for estimating the noise variance parameter τ in the K-means application (Section 4.3) appears heuristic. Since this parameter plays a crucial role in the update equations, the algorithm's sensitivity to its estimation should be discussed. It is unclear if this adaptive estimation preserves the theoretical properties that motivate the use of AMP.

*   **Scope of Experiments:** While the experiments are positive, they are somewhat limited. The synthetic experiments are conducted for a single combination of problem dimensions and noise level. The real-data evaluation uses only one dataset (ORL faces). To make a more compelling case for the general superiority of the proposed algorithm, an evaluation across a wider range of datasets with varying characteristics (e.g., dimensionality, number of clusters, noise levels, cluster separation) would be beneficial.

### Arguments for Acceptance

1.  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated application of a powerful theoretical tool (AMP) to a fundamental problem (K-means clustering).
2.  The resulting algorithm is both computationally efficient and empirically superior to strong and widely used baselines (K-means++, Variational Bayes) in the presented experiments.
3.  The theoretical connection established between the fixed points of the proposed algorithm and ICM is an interesting result that helps explain its strong performance.
4.  The general framework is flexible and could inspire further work on applying AMP to other problems involving structured matrix factorization.

### Arguments against Acceptance

1.  The core technical derivation is omitted from the main paper, making a full assessment difficult.
2.  The experimental validation, while promising, is not extensive enough to fully establish the algorithm's robustness and advantages across a wide range of conditions.
3.  The discussion of theoretical properties is somewhat incomplete, lacking a guarantee of convergence and a deeper exploration of the state evolution analysis.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

*   I would strongly encourage the authors to include a brief, high-level sketch of the algorithm's derivation in the main text to improve its accessibility.
*   To strengthen the experimental section, please consider adding results on more datasets. It would also be valuable to include an ablation study or sensitivity analysis regarding the estimation of the parameter τ in the clustering algorithm.
*   The claim that the AMP algorithm has "not more fixed points than the ICM algorithm" should be stated more precisely. Proposition 1 shows that AMP fixed points are a subset of ICM fixed points. A brief discussion on whether there exist ICM fixed points that are *not* accessible to the AMP algorithm would be very insightful.
*   Even if space constraints prevent a full analysis, mentioning the form of the state evolution equations and briefly stating the performance they predict (e.g., final MSE or clustering accuracy) would substantially increase the paper's impact by highlighting the analytical power of the AMP framework.
*   Regarding the failure cases on the ORL dataset, it would be informative to add a brief comment on the nature of the failure (e.g., did the algorithm diverge, or converge to a trivial solution where all points are in one cluster?).

Overall, this is a high-quality paper with interesting ideas and promising results. Despite the weaknesses mentioned, the strengths in originality and potential significance are compelling. The application of AMP to K-means is clever and yields an algorithm that appears to be a genuine improvement over standard methods.
