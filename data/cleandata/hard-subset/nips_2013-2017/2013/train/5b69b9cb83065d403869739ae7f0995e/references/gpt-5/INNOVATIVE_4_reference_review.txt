Summary
The paper develops an AMP-based algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that (i) generalizes prior AMP formulations to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation via a temperature parameter β, and (ii) specializes this framework to clustering by imposing a one-hot prior on V, yielding a novel AMP variant for K-means. The general algorithm recovers posterior means when β=1 and approximates MAP in the β→∞ limit, with Onsager corrections leading to O(mN) per-iteration complexity. A fixed-point optimality proposition shows that, upon convergence, each block update is a global minimizer of CMAP holding the other block fixed, and that AMP has no more fixed points than ICM. For clustering, the derived assignment rule introduces a principled leave-one-out bias correction and cluster-size effect absent from Lloyd’s algorithm. Experiments on synthetic data and the ORL faces dataset show improved K-means loss and accuracy over Lloyd/K-means++, and competitive speed; an AMP variant for maximum-accuracy clustering also outperforms a VBMF baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on AMP/GAMP/IterFac for sparse/bilinear estimation and prior AMP for matrix factorization; it differs by introducing a β-continuation that unifies Bayesian marginalization and MAP, by fixing rank r (vs. ratios) in the asymptotics, and by allowing non-factorized per-row priors. The clustering specialization is related to low-rank views of K-means and NMF-based clustering but is, to my knowledge, the first AMP that directly enforces the one-hot assignment constraint and yields a concrete leave-one-out and size-correction term in the assignment step. The relation to variational Bayes/ICM is clarified: dropping Onsager terms recovers those methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a clean β-parameterization that connects AMP approximations for posterior means to MAP estimation and enables non-Gaussian/discrete priors, including exact one-hot constraints.
- New perspective on K-means: an AMP-based assignment rule with an interpretable leave-one-out correction and size regularization; practically relevant and elegant.
- Efficiency: O(mN) per-iteration with lightweight extra Onsager terms; clear path to applying a wide class of priors via f and G.
- Solid empirical signal: consistent improvements over Lloyd/K-means++ on high-dimensional data; AMP-MA beats VBMF-MA; fast convergence.
- Theoretical touchpoint: fixed-point optimality proposition and a clear link to VB/ICM.

Weaknesses
- Missing theory: state evolution and convergence conditions are deferred; without at least a summary/empirical validation of SE, claims about asymptotic exactness remain qualitative.
- Limited baselines: for clustering, comparisons are only to Lloyd/K-means++; no GMM-EM (spherical/diagonal/full), spectral clustering, or spherical K-means, which are standard for high-dimensional data.
- Sensitivity/robustness: AMP-KM occasionally collapses to a single cluster; oscillations are handled by ad hoc stopping. More diagnostics and mitigation (e.g., damping/β-annealing) would help.
- Parameter handling: τ is estimated ad hoc; impact on performance and stability is not quantified. The effect of rank mis-specification (r̂≠r), although discussed, is not evaluated.
- Practicality of f and G: for general priors β<∞ requires r̂-dimensional integration; for β→∞ requires solving r̂-dim optimization and computing Hessians or inverses—some guidance on efficient implementations is needed.
- Real-data evaluation is narrow (one dataset, modest protocol); reporting of wall-clock times and ablations (with/without Onsager terms) would strengthen claims.

Questions for authors
1) Can you provide a brief state-evolution summary (even empirical SE curves) for the proposed AMP, and comment on conditions under which the Onsager terms ensure stability?
2) Did you try damping or β-annealing (e.g., start at β≈1 and increase) to mitigate oscillations/collapse? Does that improve robustness?
3) How sensitive are results to the τ estimator? Could you use a leave-one-out variance estimator consistent with your assignment correction?
4) How does AMP-KM compare to spherical K-means, GMM-EM with spherical/diagonal covariance, or spectral clustering on the same synthetic and ORL setups?
5) What happens under rank mis-specification (r̂<r, r̂>r)? Any graceful degradation or overfitting?
6) For discrete priors, can you exploit structure to compute f and G in O(r̂) and avoid integration/Hessians? Some implementation tips would help reproducibility.

Minor comments
- Please clarify the precise assumptions under which G∞=(Λ−H)−1 holds and when ∑iS∞i, ∑jT∞j are guaranteed PSD.
- Notational clarity: dimensions of Λu, Λv; scaling choices (mτ) and their empirical impact; define all variables at first appearance.
- Report standard deviations and wall-clock times; include an ablation without Onsager terms to illustrate their contribution.
- A small discussion of connections/differences to BiG-AMP would be useful (beyond the brief note).

Overall assessment
This paper proposes a bold and genuinely novel bridge between AMP for matrix factorization and clustering with hard one-hot assignments. Despite missing theoretical details and limited baselines, the conceptual contribution (β-unified AMP with exact one-hot priors) and the practical gains on high-dimensional clustering are compelling. With additional comparisons and minor clarifications, this work could influence both AMP-based factorization and scalable clustering.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning). Novelty and promising direction outweigh the current empirical/theory gaps.
