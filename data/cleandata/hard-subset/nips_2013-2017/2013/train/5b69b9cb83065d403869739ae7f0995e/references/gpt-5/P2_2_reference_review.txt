Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with a “temperature” parameter β that unifies posterior-marginal (β=1) and MAP (β→∞) inference. Compared to prior AMP formulations for bilinear/low-rank problems (e.g., IterFac for rank-1; Matsushita & Tanaka 2012 for general rank), the main technical extension is support for general priors via functions f and G and an explicit MAP limit, enabling both posterior mean and MAP-type estimators. As a second contribution, the paper instantiates this framework to clustering, showing that K-means arises as a special case (one-hot V), and deriving an AMP variant for K-means that modifies the assignment step with Onsager-like corrections to account for current assignments and cluster sizes. A mild optimality property (Proposition 1) shows any fixed point is block-wise globally optimal given the other block, under PSD conditions.

Empirically, on synthetic Gaussian mixture data (m=800, N=1600), the AMP K-means variant achieves lower K-means loss than Lloyd and K-means++, and the AMP maximum accuracy variant (with the true priors) achieves higher assignment accuracy than VBMF. On the ORL faces, the AMP K-means variant typically improves both K-means loss and accuracy over K-means++.

The work is related to variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF), which assumes a factorized posterior and yields ICM/Lloyd’s algorithm in the MAP limit, and to bilinear/generalized AMP (e.g., Rangan 2011; IterFac 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka 2012). The paper distinguishes itself by allowing MAP estimation and by explicitly addressing the one-hot constraint for clustering within the AMP framework.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The AMP updates are reasonable and consistent with AMP derivations; the β-parametrization neatly interpolates between MMSE and MAP. However, the derivation is deferred to the supplement and there is no state evolution analysis or empirical validation of state evolution predictions. Convergence is assumed and not guaranteed; oscillations are observed and handled heuristically. Proposition 1 provides a mild fixed-point property but does not address convergence, stability, or quality of local minima.
- Experimental support: The synthetic study shows clear gains in the metrics each method optimizes (AMP-KM vs Lloyd/K++ on K-means loss; AMP-MA vs VBMF-MA on accuracy). Real-data results on ORL are encouraging but somewhat limited (only one dataset, occasional collapse to a single cluster). Missing are: wall-clock time comparisons, ablations isolating the effect of the Onsager terms, robustness to mis-specified priors and rank, and broader comparisons (e.g., EM for GMMs with tied covariance, spectral clustering, BiG-AMP).
- Practicality: Per-iteration O(mN) is standard; the extra terms are O(m+N). For β<∞, f and G may require r̂-dimensional integration unless closed forms exist; the paper would benefit from concrete worked-out priors (e.g., Gaussian, Laplace, nonnegativity) and computational details for G∞ via Hessians. The τ estimation heuristic for K-means is ad hoc and its impact is not analyzed.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, with clear problem setup and algorithms. The link from the general AMP to the K-means specialization is elegant and insightful, and the interpretation of the correction terms is helpful. That said:
- Many crucial details are in the supplement (derivation, proof, conditions for PSD of summed covariances), which makes it hard to fully assess correctness.
- Some notation and scaling choices (e.g., noise variance mτ) could use brief intuition earlier.
- References have formatting issues and a few are incomplete.
- Reproducibility would be improved by providing explicit f/G for common priors and pseudocode with damping/stepsize suggestions.

Originality
Moderate to high. Extending bilinear AMP to a β-parametrized family that includes MAP, and showing how to enforce exact 1-of-K assignment to produce an AMP variant of K-means with principled corrections, are novel and interesting. The clustering instantiation, with explicit bias and cluster-size corrections derived from AMP, seems new relative to prior NMF-based clustering and Lloyd/ICM. Prior bilinear AMP works did not, to my knowledge, present the MAP specialization and one-hot discrete prior in this way. The paper appropriately cites relevant AMP and VBMF literature, though a more thorough comparison to closely related bilinear GAMP/BiG-AMP (which can handle MMSE/MAP with general priors) would strengthen claims of novelty.

Significance
Potentially useful. If robust, the proposed AMP-KM could be a drop-in alternative to Lloyd’s K-means in high dimensions, with clear empirical benefits. The general β-AMP framework for low-rank reconstruction could be impactful for practitioners seeking flexible priors and either MMSE or MAP solutions. However, the current scope is limited: no state evolution, limited empirical breadth, and no demonstrations beyond clustering of how the general framework helps with other structured priors (e.g., nonnegativity, sparsity). This tempers the immediate impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled AMP derivation leading to a practically relevant K-means variant with intuitive corrections.
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines (Lloyd, K-means++) on synthetic and a real dataset.
- Unifying β-parameter view bridging posterior means and MAP for bilinear problems.
- Low per-iteration complexity; insights into how Onsager terms correct biases.

Cons for acceptance
- No state evolution analysis or convergence guarantees; oscillations observed and handled ad hoc.
- Derivation and proofs are deferred; limited theoretical depth beyond Proposition 1.
- Experimental breadth is narrow; missing comparisons (e.g., EM-GMM, spectral clustering, BiG-AMP), runtime, robustness, and ablations.
- Practical guidance for general priors (f/G computation) is sparse; τ estimation heuristic lacks analysis.
- Occasional catastrophic failures (single-cluster collapse) on ORL.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide at least empirical state evolution validation and convergence diagnostics; add damping/stepsize or adaptive damping to mitigate oscillations.
- Include ablations isolating the effect of the Onsager terms in assignments; measure wall-clock time.
- Compare against EM for Gaussian mixtures (equal/diagonal covariance) and spectral clustering; discuss relations to BiG-AMP and clarify differences.
- Demonstrate the generality by instantiating at least one additional prior (e.g., nonnegative or sparse factors) with worked-out f/G.
- Analyze sensitivity to τ estimation, rank mismatch, and initialization; report robustness across datasets.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The ideas are promising and the clustering specialization is elegant with noticeable empirical gains, but the submission lacks theoretical backing (state evolution/convergence), broader and fairer empirical evaluation, and concrete demonstrations beyond clustering to substantiate the general low-rank AMP framework. Strengthening these aspects would make this a solid NeurIPS paper.
