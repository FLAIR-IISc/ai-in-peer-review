This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. While the authors correctly identify a desire for flexible reconstruction methods and position AMP as a potentially efficient approach, the paper suffers from a lack of compelling novelty and insufficient validation against established baselines, raising significant concerns regarding its overall contribution.

The core idea – extending existing AMP algorithms for low-rank reconstruction to handle MAP estimation and applying it to clustering – feels incremental. The authors acknowledge prior work on AMP in related areas ([8, 9, 13, 14]), but the described extension for MAP estimation appears to be a natural consequence of the Bayesian framework and isn’t presented as a particularly innovative algorithmic step.  Similarly, framing clustering as a low-rank reconstruction problem isn’t new ([10, 11]), and the proposed constraint of assigning each datum to exactly one cluster seems a fairly straightforward adaptation. 

My primary concern lies with the empirical evaluation. The artificial data experiments, while showing some performance advantages, lack thorough comparison to strong, well-optimized baselines. Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ are included, but more sophisticated methods for low-rank matrix factorization and clustering, such as those based on alternating least squares or stochastic gradient descent, are missing. The claim of outperforming K-means++ in nearly all trials in the face dataset experiment is interesting but requires more scrutiny; a clear justification for the choice of parameters and a more detailed analysis of the results are needed. Simply stating the number of trials where the proposed method "outperformed" is insufficient.  The justification for estimating τ during the algorithm’s execution feels ad-hoc, and its impact on performance should be investigated.

The theoretical presentation, while outlining the AMP algorithm and its connection to variational Bayes, is dense and could benefit from greater clarity. The proposition regarding optimality of the fixed point, while potentially interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the practical performance claims. The discussion of state evolution is omitted due to space constraints, a missed opportunity to provide crucial theoretical support for the algorithm’s efficiency and performance bounds. The assertion that the algorithm is "almost symmetric" requires careful justification; symmetry properties are often important in AMP algorithms.

Several statements lack sufficient supporting evidence. For example, the claim that the AMP algorithm offers improved performance compared to variational Bayes due to its ability to reflect dependencies between U and V is intuitive but needs more rigorous justification—either theoretical or empirical.  The statement that the AMP algorithm converges "fast" is subjective without quantitative comparison to other methods.

**Strengths:**

*   The paper identifies a relevant problem (flexible low-rank matrix reconstruction).
*   The AMP framework offers potential for computational efficiency.
*   The application to K-means clustering is a reasonable direction.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited novelty of the proposed approach.
*   Insufficiently rigorous empirical validation against strong baselines.
*   Dense and occasionally unclear theoretical presentation.
*   Lack of state evolution analysis.
*   Ad-hoc parameter estimation (τ).
*   Claims of improvement lack strong supporting evidence.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   If the authors can provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating a significant and consistent advantage.
*   If they can clarify the theoretical underpinnings and provide a more compelling justification for the claimed benefits of AMP over other techniques.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   The current lack of novelty and insufficient empirical validation.
*   The paper feels like an incremental extension of existing work without a substantial demonstration of its practical impact.
*   The theoretical presentation needs improvement for broader accessibility.




**Overall Assessment:** Reject. The paper presents a reasonable engineering effort but lacks the novelty, rigor, and compelling empirical evidence necessary for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to significantly strengthen the experimental validation and provide a more compelling theoretical justification for their approach.
