This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The core idea is extending existing AMP algorithms to handle MAP estimation and leveraging this framework for clustering by formulating it as a special case of low-rank reconstruction. While the authors correctly identify AMP as a promising tool due to its efficiency and theoretical guarantees, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant contribution to the field, particularly given the established landscape of existing methods.

The paper’s strengths lie in its formalization of the problem and extension of existing AMP algorithms. The attempt to unify marginalization and MAP estimation under a single framework via a β parameter is a reasonable approach. The connection to K-means clustering is also noted, though not entirely novel. However, the presentation suffers from a lack of critical comparison with established techniques and a reliance on numerical experiments that, while presented, are not compelling enough to justify the proposed method's complexity.

My primary concern is the lack of robust comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods. While Lloyd's K-means is used as a baseline, it is a rather weak one given the availability of more sophisticated algorithms like K-means++ (which *is* included but not sufficiently analyzed) and various accelerated variants.  The claim of outperforming Lloyd’s K-means, while potentially true, is insufficient justification for the added complexity of the AMP-based approach.  The variational Bayes method is mentioned, but a thorough empirical comparison is missing. The authors acknowledge similarities to recent work in AMP for dictionary learning and blind calibration, yet a detailed comparative analysis remains “to be made,” a significant omission.  The theoretical advantages, alluded to through the mention of state evolution (not presented), aren’t concretely demonstrated in practice.

The description of the algorithm itself is dense and lacks sufficient intuitive explanation. The introduction of parameters like τ, seemingly motivated by scaling considerations, feels ad-hoc without clearer justification of its impact and optimal setting.  The connection between the theoretical properties mentioned (positive semidefiniteness of covariance matrices) and practical performance isn’t convincingly established.  The explanation of how the proposed AMP algorithm addresses limitations of the variational Bayes method – namely, the independence assumption – is similarly hand-wavy.

The numerical experiments, while comprehensive in the sense of considering different values of *r*, lack critical analysis. The gains observed on the artificial datasets aren’t substantial enough to warrant the algorithmic complexity. The improvement on the ORL dataset is marginal, and the frequent occurrence of empty clusters suggests instability or sensitivity to initialization. The comparison relies heavily on average performance; reporting the variance and distribution of results across trials is crucial to assess robustness. Furthermore, the parameter tuning details for both the proposed method and the baselines are insufficient for reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a formal extension of AMP algorithms to MAP estimation.
*   The application of AMP to clustering is a reasonable exploration.
*   The authors identify potential theoretical advantages of AMP over variational Bayes.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of compelling comparison to state-of-the-art methods.
*   Insufficiently detailed and justified algorithm description.
*   Numerical results are not sufficiently convincing to justify the complexity.
*   Theoretical claims are not backed up by experimental validation.
*   Limited discussion of practical limitations and sensitivity analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**.  While the ideas presented are not entirely without merit, the lack of a rigorous comparison to established methods and the insufficiently compelling empirical results do not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS.  The paper feels more like a preliminary investigation than a fully developed contribution. Significant revisions, including a thorough comparative evaluation and a clearer presentation of the algorithm's benefits, would be required for reconsideration.  The authors need to demonstrate a tangible advantage over existing techniques to justify the added complexity of their approach.
