### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The key contributions are twofold. First, it extends a previous AMP algorithm for this problem to handle not just posterior mean estimation but also Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is achieved elegantly by introducing an inverse temperature parameter `β` that unifies the treatment of the marginalization problem (`β=1`) and the MAP problem (`β → ∞`). Second, and more significantly, the paper applies this framework to K-means type clustering. By formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one factor matrix consists of one-hot encoded cluster assignments, the authors derive a new iterative clustering algorithm. This new algorithm, termed AMP-KM, is shown to be a principled modification of the classic Lloyd's algorithm, incorporating additional terms that correct for biases in cluster center estimation. The authors provide theoretical justification for their AMP approach, arguing its superiority over variational methods and analyzing its fixed points. Finally, they present compelling numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world data (ORL faces database) that demonstrate their proposed clustering algorithms (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) outperform standard baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a variational Bayes approach, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

### Review

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The derivation of the AMP algorithm appears sound, building upon established work in the field. The introduction of the `β` parameter to unify marginal and MAP estimation is a standard but effective technique that is well-executed here.

The theoretical analysis, while brief, provides valuable insight. Proposition 1, which establishes that any fixed point of the proposed AMP algorithm for the MAP problem is also a fixed point of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm (of which Lloyd's algorithm is an instance), is a strong result. It correctly implies that the AMP algorithm has no more fixed points than ICM and may therefore be less prone to getting trapped in poor local minima. This provides a formal justification for the improved performance seen in experiments. The reformulation of the update step in Eq. (16) as a regularized minimization is also very insightful.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. The comparison against multiple relevant baselines on synthetic data across a range of problem sizes (number of clusters `r`) clearly demonstrates the proposed method's superiority in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy. The application to the ORL face database confirms these findings on a real-world task. The authors are honest about practical implementation details, such as the termination condition for AMP-KM to handle oscillations and the heuristic for estimating the noise variance `τ`.

One minor weakness is the reliance on assumed convergence. Like many AMP algorithms, convergence is not guaranteed in general. The paper acknowledges this by stating, "We assume in the rest of this section the convergence of Algorithm 2," but a brief discussion of the conditions under which convergence is expected or the typical behavior when it fails would strengthen the work.

#### Clarity

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The problem is well-motivated, and the connection between the general low-rank reconstruction framework and the specific application of K-means clustering is laid out exceptionally well in Section 2. The previous work is adequately summarized, positioning the proposed contribution effectively.

The highlight in terms of clarity is the derivation and explanation of the AMP algorithm for K-means clustering (Algorithm 3 and Eq. 18). The authors do an excellent job of reformulating the abstract AMP update equations into an intuitive form that can be directly compared to Lloyd's algorithm. The explanation that the new terms correct for the bias of a cluster center towards its current members and favor smaller clusters is insightful and easy to grasp. This makes the "magic" of the AMP derivation tangible and understandable.

A point for improvement would be to provide more intuition or detail on the derivation of Algorithm 2 in the main text. The paper defers the entire derivation to the supplementary material, stating it is "similar to that described in [9]." While understandable due to space constraints, this makes the core technical contribution difficult to fully assess without the supplement and slightly reduces the self-contained nature of the paper.

#### Originality

The paper demonstrates solid originality. While AMP for low-rank matrix recovery is not entirely new (as acknowledged by citing [8, 9]), this work makes several novel contributions:
1.  The explicit generalization to handle MAP estimation via the `β` parameter, moving beyond the posterior-mean focus of [9].
2.  The application of this AMP framework to K-means clustering is the primary and most original contribution. While casting clustering as matrix factorization is known [10, 11], deriving a new, high-performance clustering algorithm from the principled AMP framework is a novel and significant step. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3 / Eq. 18) is not an ad-hoc modification of K-means but emerges directly from the AMP derivation.
3.  The theoretical analysis of the fixed points (Proposition 1) provides a novel justification for the algorithm's effectiveness compared to standard methods.

The authors briefly mention other recent AMP papers for matrix problems ([13, 14]) but could have elaborated slightly more on the distinctions to better situate their work. However, the core idea of tackling the discrete constraints of clustering within this specific AMP framework appears to be unique.

#### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant. K-means clustering is one of the most fundamental and widely used algorithms in machine learning. Any principled improvement that offers better performance with comparable computational cost is of great interest to both researchers and practitioners.

The proposed AMP-KM algorithm represents such an improvement. It provides a new state-of-the-art deterministic clustering method that directly optimizes the K-means objective. The experimental results, showing substantial gains over Lloyd's algorithm and the popular K-means++ initialization scheme, are impressive. Given its simple interpretation as a "bias-corrected" version of Lloyd's algorithm and its linear-time complexity per iteration, the algorithm has the potential for wide adoption.

Furthermore, the work successfully bridges the gap between the theoretical domain of approximate message passing (often associated with statistical physics and coding theory) and the practical, mainstream problem of data clustering. This serves as an excellent case study for the power of AMP-style algorithms and is likely to inspire further research into applying these techniques to other core problems in machine learning.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   **Novelty and Impact:** Proposes a novel and highly effective clustering algorithm derived from first principles (AMP), advancing the state-of-the-art for the fundamental K-means problem.
*   **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The algorithm's superiority is convincingly demonstrated through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Good Theoretical Backing:** The connection to the Bethe approximation and the fixed-point analysis provide solid theoretical motivation for why the algorithm works well.
*   **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, especially in its intuitive explanation of how the proposed algorithm improves upon the classic Lloyd's algorithm.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   **Derivation in Supplement:** The core derivation of the main algorithm is entirely in the supplementary material, making the main paper less self-contained.
*   **Limited Comparison to Concurrent AMP Work:** The discussion contrasting the method with other very recent AMP-based matrix factorization papers [13, 14] is minimal.

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, well-motivated, and empirically powerful algorithm for K-means clustering. The strengths, particularly the significance of improving a cornerstone algorithm like K-means and the clarity of the presentation, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I recommend this paper for acceptance.
