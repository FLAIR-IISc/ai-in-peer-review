

# Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending existing AMP methods to handle not only posterior mean estimation but also maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors then apply this algorithm to K-means clustering, claiming their approach is the first to directly incorporate the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework.

The paper's main novelty lies in extending AMP from the sparse vector reconstruction context (where it has been successfully applied) to the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem. The authors build upon prior work by [9], which developed an AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix reconstruction but was limited to posterior mean estimation. By introducing a one-parameter extension to the posterior distribution (Equation 8), they unify the treatment of marginalization and MAP problems, allowing their AMP algorithm to handle both. This is a significant conceptual advance over existing methods.

For clustering, the paper demonstrates that the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem includes clustering as a special case (Section 2.2), and the authors develop a novel AMP-based clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3) that directly addresses the assignment constraint. While low-rank matrix reconstruction has been previously applied to clustering [10, 11], the authors claim their method is the first to properly handle the constraint that each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster. Their numerical experiments on both synthetic and real data (Figures 1 and 2) show that their AMP-based clustering outperforms Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes approaches in terms of both clustering loss and accuracy, particularly for higher-dimensional data.

The paper's strengths are clear: it presents a novel algorithmic framework that bridges AMP methods with low-rank matrix reconstruction, and it demonstrates a meaningful application to clustering with empirical validation. The theoretical foundation is well-explained, and the algorithm is shown to have computational complexity comparable to existing methods (O(mN) per iteration). The paper also provides a useful comparison to variational Bayes approaches and demonstrates that the AMP algorithm avoids getting stuck in local minima more effectively than the ICM algorithm (Proposition 1).

Minor weaknesses include the somewhat limited theoretical analysis of the AMP algorithm's convergence properties (the paper assumes convergence without proving it), and the numerical experiments could have included more comprehensive comparisons with recent clustering methods. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, these are minor concerns.

The significance of this work is substantial. It provides a new algorithmic approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction that is flexible enough to handle various estimation problems, and it demonstrates a novel application to clustering that outperforms standard methods. The AMP framework's potential to handle complex structural constraints in matrix reconstruction is particularly valuable, as the authors note that "the Bayesian approach achieves such flexibility by allowing us to select prior distributions of U0 and V0 reflecting a priori knowledge on the structural properties."

This paper represents a significant step forward in applying AMP methods to matrix factorization problems. While the core AMP idea is not entirely new, the specific extension to handle MAP estimation for low-rank matrices and the subsequent application to clustering constitute a meaningful advance. The paper clearly articulates how their work differs from related approaches [13, 14], particularly in the scaling assumptions and handling of dependencies between matrix factors.

The authors have provided a compelling case for the novelty and significance of their contribution. They have not simply extended existing methods incrementally but have introduced a new framework that enables more flexible and potentially more effective low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The numerical results support their claims, and the proposed algorithm has practical advantages in terms of convergence speed and performance.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold approaches, I find this paper's contribution to be significant. While it might not be the most technically perfect paper (with some minor theoretical gaps), the core idea of extending AMP to handle MAP estimation for low-rank matrices is novel and has clear potential for broader application in matrix factorization problems. The application to clustering is particularly compelling as it directly addresses a constraint that has been challenging in previous low-rank approaches.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a substantial contribution to the field by introducing a novel algorithmic framework that bridges AMP with low-rank matrix reconstruction, and it demonstrates the practical value of this approach through a compelling application to clustering. The work is sufficiently novel, significant, and well-supported by experiments to warrant publication at NIPS.
