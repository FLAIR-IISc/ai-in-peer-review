### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents an approximate message passing algorithm for probabilistic matrix factorization. The authors first frame matrix factorization as a generalization of the clustering problem. Based on this connection, they derive a message passing algorithm for the general matrix factorization case and then specialize it for clustering. The performance of the resulting clustering algorithm is evaluated empirically against k-means++.

**Quality and Technical Correctness**

My primary concern with this paper is its lack of mathematical rigor. The derivations, both in the main text and the appendix, are often too brief and omit crucial, non-trivial steps. This makes it very difficult for a reviewer to verify the technical correctness of the results. The following points highlight specific areas where greater detail and justification are needed:

1.  **Asymptotic Analysis:** The asymptotic analysis appears to rely on unstated assumptions. For example, the derivation of Equation (19) seems to implicitly assume that m and n grow at the same rate. This assumption should be stated explicitly and justified. The overall analysis in this section is quite terse, and a more detailed derivation would significantly strengthen the paper's mathematical foundation.

2.  **Approximations:** Several approximations are made without sufficient rationale. For instance, on line 167, the expectation of A_il^2 is approximated by mτ_w^2. Since A is the observed data and mτ_w^2 represents the variance of the likelihood, this approximation requires justification. Please explain the reasoning behind this step.

3.  **Convergence to MAP:** The paper suggests a connection to the MAP problem as β → ∞. However, the convergence of the algorithm relies on minimizing KL divergence. It is not generally true that the limit of the minimum KL divergence equals the minimum of the limit of KL divergence (i.e., lim_β min KL ≠ min lim_β KL). Could the authors please elaborate on why their algorithm is expected to converge to the MAP solution in this limit?

4.  **Omitted Proof Steps:** Several proofs are highly condensed, making them difficult to follow. For example, the proof of Proposition 1 is very brief, and the derivation of Equation (17) is not clear. Similarly, in the appendix (line 139), it is not obvious why the second term of Equation (11) is considered O(1/√m). A more detailed, step-by-step derivation for these key results is essential.

5.  **Missing Parameters:** The parameter β appears to be missing from the problem formulation in Section 2, yet it is used in the appendix. This should be clarified.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The main contributions and the overall approach are easy to follow, despite the issues with the underlying mathematical detail.

**Originality**

The originality of the proposed algorithm is questionable. The method appears to be a direct application of Expectation Propagation (EP) to the matrix factorization problem. This has been explored previously (e.g., Stern et al., "Matchbox: a large-scale bayesian recommender system," WWW 2009). The authors should clearly position their work with respect to this and other related prior art, highlighting what novel contributions their method offers. Section 1 of the appendix, in particular, seems to describe standard EP update procedures. If this is not the case, the authors should explain the key differences.

On a constructive note, there may be an interesting connection between the proposed clustering algorithm and the "clutter problem" described in Minka's original EP paper. If the likelihood in the clutter problem were replaced with a uniform mixture of Gaussians, it might yield a similar formulation. The authors may wish to comment on this potential link.

**Significance**

The significance of the paper is limited by two main factors. First, due to the sketchy proofs, it is hard to evaluate the technical correctness and theoretical contribution. Second, while the application to clustering is a key focus, the empirical evaluation is insufficient. Comparing the proposed method solely against k-means++ does not adequately establish its practical utility, given the extensive body of work on clustering algorithms. A more comprehensive experimental comparison against a wider range of modern clustering methods is needed to demonstrate the algorithm's value.

In summary, while the paper addresses an interesting problem, its contributions are undermined by a lack of mathematical rigor, questionable originality, and a weak empirical evaluation.
