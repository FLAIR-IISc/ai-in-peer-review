This paper addresses the problem of reconstructing a low-rank matrix from noisy observations. The authors formulate this problem within a Bayesian framework, which offers the flexibility to incorporate additional structural properties of the matrix factors, such as sparsity, through the choice of prior distributions. The primary contribution is the development of an efficient algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP) to perform Bayesian inference. This algorithm extends previous AMP-based methods for low-rank recovery by unifying the calculation of posterior marginals and Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) estimates. As a second major contribution, the authors demonstrate a compelling application of their method to K-means type clustering. They reformulate the clustering task as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem where one factor matrix is constrained to represent cluster assignments. This approach connects their work to a well-established line of research linking matrix factorization and clustering [10, 11], but provides a novel algorithmic solution derived directly from the AMP framework.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The proposed AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix reconstruction, which handles both marginalization and MAP estimation within a single parameterized framework (via the `β` parameter), is a solid contribution. It builds upon and generalizes existing AMP literature [8, 9] in a useful way. The connection to the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm and variational methods is well-articulated, highlighting that the AMP approach includes additional "Onsager" correction terms that can intuitively lead to better performance by avoiding poor local minima.
*   **Application to Clustering:** The application to clustering is a significant strength. By reformulating the K-means problem, the authors derive a new clustering algorithm (Algorithm 3) that is distinct from the classic Lloyd's algorithm. The derived update rule (18b) has an intuitive interpretation, correcting for the bias a data point exerts on its own cluster center and favoring smaller clusters. This provides a principled, message-passing-based alternative to heuristic K-means variants.
*   **Experimental Quality:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. On synthetic data, the proposed AMP-KM and AMP-MA algorithms consistently outperform strong baselines like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a variational Bayes approach, in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy. The experiments on the ORL face database demonstrate that these performance gains translate to a real-world problem, even when using the sophisticated initialization from K-means++. The fast convergence reported for the AMP methods is also a notable practical advantage.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The problem setting is formally defined, the connection between matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-explained, and the proposed algorithm is presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness is the limited theoretical analysis of the proposed algorithm's convergence. While Proposition 1 provides a useful characterization of the algorithm's fixed points for the MAP case, it does not guarantee that the algorithm will converge. The authors note that a state evolution analysis is possible but omit it due to space constraints; its inclusion, perhaps in an appendix, would significantly strengthen the paper's theoretical foundation and provide insight into the conditions under which the algorithm is expected to perform well.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** While the paper cites several related AMP works [13, 14], the discussion of the differences is quite brief. A more detailed comparison of the technical assumptions (e.g., fixed rank `r` vs. fixed ratio `r/m`) and the implications of these differences would help to better contextualize the paper's contribution within the rapidly developing field of AMP-based methods.
*   **Clustering Context:** While the empirical comparison to K-means and K-means++ is strong, the authors could briefly acknowledge the broader literature on provable algorithms for K-means from the theoretical computer science community. This would provide a more complete picture and frame the contribution as a new, empirically powerful heuristic rather than implicitly comparing it against the entire field of clustering.

**Summary of arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated AMP algorithm for a fundamental problem. The application to clustering is clever and leads to a new algorithm that empirically outperforms widely-used methods. The experimental results are strong and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The work lacks a formal convergence guarantee, which is a notable theoretical limitation.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a novel algorithm with compelling empirical results on an important problem. The weaknesses regarding theoretical guarantees are common in this line of work, and they are outweighed by the strengths of the algorithmic contribution and the quality of the experimental validation.
