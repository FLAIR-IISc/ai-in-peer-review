This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix factorization, and subsequently applies this algorithm to the clustering problem by reformulating it as a specific instance of matrix reconstruction. The authors present numerical experiments demonstrating competitive performance compared to k-means++ for clustering.

**Quality:** The mathematical derivations presented are somewhat terse, omitting several non-trivial steps even within the appendix. Specific concerns arise regarding the justification of certain approximations and the rigor of the asymptotic analysis. 
1) In the appendix (line 139), the claim that the second term is O(1/√m) lacks sufficient explanation, and the role of β appears to be inconsistently applied throughout Section 2.
2) The asymptotic analysis, particularly the derivation of (19), appears to rely on the assumption that m and N grow at comparable rates. The brevity of this analysis raises concerns regarding its overall mathematical rigor.
3) The justification for approximating the expectation of A²<sub>il</sub> with mτ<sub>w</sub>² (line 167) is unclear, as this utilizes the variance of the likelihood function rather than directly estimating the expectation from observed data A.
4) The convergence of the AMP algorithm as β approaches infinity to approximate the MAP problem is not fully justified. While AMP minimizes KL divergence, the interchange of limits and minimization may not hold. A more detailed explanation of this convergence behavior is needed.
5) The proof of Proposition 1 is concise and could benefit from further elaboration. The derivation of equation (17) requires additional explanation.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and presents its central ideas in a generally accessible manner. The flow of the argument is logical and easy to follow.

**Originality:** The proposed algorithm largely focuses on estimating the first and second moments of the marginal posterior distribution. This suggests a strong similarity to Expectation Propagation (EP), which has been previously applied to matrix factorization, as demonstrated in Stern et al. (http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf). Section 1 of the appendix closely resembles standard EP update procedures. A clearer delineation of the novelty of this work relative to existing EP approaches would strengthen the originality claim. Furthermore, a potential connection exists between the authors' clustering algorithm and the original clutter problem addressed in the EP literature (http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf), particularly when considering a uniform mixture of Gaussian distributions as the likelihood.

**Significance:** While the paper demonstrates promising results for clustering, the comparison is limited to k-means++. Given the extensive body of work in clustering algorithms, a broader evaluation against state-of-the-art methods is needed to fully establish the practical significance of the proposed approach. The limited detail in the proofs, even in the appendix, makes it difficult to fully assess the technical correctness of the work and its potential impact. The possibility that this work represents an application of expectation-propagation to matrix factorization, a previously explored direction (Stern et al.), further diminishes the perceived significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a well-structured approach to low-rank matrix reconstruction using AMP.
*   The proposed algorithm demonstrates competitive performance on clustering tasks.
*   The application of AMP to clustering provides a novel perspective on this classic problem.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The mathematical derivations lack sufficient detail and rigor.
*   The originality of the approach is questionable, given its similarity to existing EP methods.
*   The significance of the work is limited by the narrow scope of the experimental evaluation.
*   The convergence guarantees are not fully justified.
