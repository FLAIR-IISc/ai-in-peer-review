Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, leveraging Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing within a Bayesian model. As detailed in the Supplementary Material, the algorithm incorporates two approximations based on the assumption of a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The method is evaluated in a unique manner by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing performance against Lloyd’s K-means. It is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), currently the only other message-passing approach for reconstruction found in the literature.

**Cons**

Several concerns raise doubts regarding acceptance. Evaluating the algorithm on a non-standard problem – clustering represented as matrix factorization – makes it difficult to extrapolate experimental results to more traditional matrix reconstruction scenarios. For example, the VBMF comparisons cite Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen (2007), both of which utilize the Netflix dataset for evaluation. Ideally, this paper would employ the same dataset for a more direct comparison. While BP generally surpasses Variational methods in accuracy, assessing this specific BP variant with a novel criterion casts doubt on its true competitiveness.  If using Netflix or a similar dataset is infeasible, the authors should clearly articulate the reasons why. 

The algorithm itself represents a largely direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed using Variational Message Passing, and while novel, it doesn’t represent a significant paradigm shift.  The most compelling aspect lies in the approximations introduced in the limits  $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the input matrix dimensions. However, the validity of these approximations, currently only presented in supplementary material, remains untested and could benefit from further clarification. 

A significant issue lies in the presentation of prior work. The initial reading led me to believe the paper was proposing the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution, based on the abstract and Section 2.2. This impression proved incorrect upon consultation with other reviewers, revealing the connection is well-established. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this existing knowledge; if the connection is widely known, it should state so, otherwise, appropriate citations are necessary.

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the common terminology for applying BP to continuous distributions, and the relationship between EP and the presented algorithm should be discussed.  Relatedly, the core algorithm is fundamentally a Belief Propagation application, but this is not stated until Section 4.1.  This key detail should be included in the abstract.

**Pros**

Despite these concerns, the paper is interesting and presents a new algorithm with potential utility. The mathematical framework is generally accessible, and while I did not independently verify the derivations, I believe it would be feasible to do so.  While the novel evaluation criterion warrants scrutiny, the K-means problem appears sufficiently general to allow for a reasonable algorithmic comparison, demonstrating a clear advantage for the proposed method in the experiments.

**Clarity**

The initial presentation is relatively easy to follow, with a clear introduction and summary of prior work.  However, relegating the algorithmic derivation to the Supplemental Material is a drawback; an outline in the main text would be preferable. The experiments section is clear, and while some plots are difficult to interpret in grayscale, they remain readable. 

I struggled to fully understand the algorithm's description and could not independently verify the derivation’s correctness. The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect. Even superficially, it’s difficult to parse. For instance, the factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ preceding each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out for improved readability, and the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.

There’s an excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For example, $\tauw$ is used without a corresponding $\tau$; simply replacing $\tauw$ with $\tau$ would suffice.  Similarly, Section 4 lacks p, q, or r, relying solely on \hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r} – a statement at the section's beginning such as “We will omit the hats for brevity” would be helpful.  The 't' superscripts in the messages appear unnecessary; replacing "=" with "\gets" would eliminate the need for them. I find it unlikely the algorithm was originally developed using such cumbersome notation. Revisiting the initial notation for simplicity is recommended.

The exposition could be improved with further explanation: clarify that equation 5 represents the negative logarithm of equation 2, and that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. Algorithm 2 exhibits near row-column symmetry; this should be pointed out, and the algorithm could be halved by stating, “then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v.” The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definition; stating “f\beta is the mean of q\beta” would be clearer than providing only the equation. At the end of Section 4.2, the statement “G\beta(b, \Lambda; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)” isn’t immediately obvious or apparent from the initial definition of G. 

None of the messages include \beta subscripts, despite their dependence on f and G, which do. However, f and G only depend on q_\beta, not directly on \beta. Eliminating these subscripts entirely, even from q, is suggested, as \beta is simply a global variable.  Before equations (15) and (16), a statement like “in the limit \beta \to \infty, f and G take the following form:” would be beneficial. 

On a deeper level, questions remain regarding the algorithm’s nuances.  What is the significance of the m factor in the additive noise variance? Does it have implications for matrix factorization applications, or the algorithm's derivation?  Is it the sole factor preventing row-column symmetry? The authors likely have answers to these questions but do not address them.

**Other Questions:**

Is the use of a tilde to denote column vectors standard? I initially found this confusing. Why is N capitalized while m is lowercase? In section 5, a citation after VBMF-MA indicating the primary implementation reference would be helpful, given the multiple variational matrix factorization citations earlier in the paper.

**Supplementary Material:**

For equation 5, representing the terms involving $z$ as a product of exponentials would clarify its probabilistic interpretation.  Regarding section 2, the step from equation 14 to 15 regarding the big-O of $m$ terms was unclear; further elaboration would be helpful. In the top of page 2, the statement “A technique used in ... is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters” lacks context without prior knowledge of the referenced works. Clarifying the novelty of the algorithm’s derivation, both in the text and the main paper, is crucial. 

The English is generally good, but occasional awkward phrasing exists, particularly regarding article usage. This can be distracting. For example, the title could be improved to “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” A list of specific suggestions will be provided separately.

The diagram in Figure 1 of the Supplementary Material is helpful, and including more such diagrams would enhance the paper. 

In conclusion, the mathematics is interesting and the algorithm warrants publication as a first attempt to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, significant improvements in clarity and scholarly rigor are needed before acceptance. The current presentation places an unacceptable burden on readers and could reflect poorly on the conference.
