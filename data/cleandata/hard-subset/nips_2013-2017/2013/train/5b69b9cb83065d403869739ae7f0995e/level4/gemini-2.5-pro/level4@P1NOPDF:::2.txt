This paper presents a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the problem. The algorithm, detailed in the supplementary material, incorporates two simplifying approximations based on the assumption of a large number of rows and columns. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel setting by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing its performance against Lloyd's K-means algorithm. It is also compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), another message-passing based reconstruction algorithm.

While the paper presents an interesting new algorithm, it has several significant weaknesses in its evaluation, scholarship, and presentation that prevent me from recommending its acceptance in its current form.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Evaluation on a Non-Standard Task:** By evaluating a new matrix reconstruction algorithm on a non-standard problem (clustering), it is difficult to extrapolate from the experimental results to predict how the algorithm would perform on more conventional benchmarks. For instance, the two VBMF papers cited by the authors (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen, 2007) both use the Netflix dataset to evaluate their algorithms. It would have been more informative if the present paper had used the same or a similar dataset to allow for a direct comparison. Although BP is often more accurate than variational methods, evaluating this new BP variant on a novel task casts doubt on its competitiveness for standard matrix reconstruction problems. If there is a compelling reason why standard datasets could not be used, the authors should explain this in the paper.

2.  **Scholarship and Positioning:** There are serious issues regarding the citation and framing of prior work.
    *   The paper's abstract and Section 2.2 give the misleading impression that applying matrix factorization to clustering is a novel contribution. This connection is, in fact, well-known. This omission is a significant scholarly lapse. The authors must make it clear to the reader that this is an established technique, either by stating it is common knowledge or by citing relevant prior work.
    *   The algorithm is most clearly understood as an application of Belief Propagation, yet this is not mentioned until Section 4.1. This key information should be stated upfront in the abstract and introduction.
    *   The paper should cite Expectation Propagation (EP), which is the standard name for applying BP to continuous distributions. The relationship between the proposed algorithm and EP should be explained.

**Critique of the Algorithm and its Presentation**

1.  **Novelty and Derivation:** The algorithm appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. The most interesting contribution seems to be the approximations introduced in the large matrix limit ($m, N \to \infty$). However, these core approximations are described only in the supplementary material, their validity is never directly tested, and their novelty with respect to previous work is not clarified. The derivation is a central part of the paper's contribution and should at least be outlined in the main text.

2.  **Clarity and Mathematical Notation:** The mathematical presentation is the weakest part of the paper and is a significant barrier to understanding.
    *   The notation is unnecessarily cumbersome, with an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts that obscure the core ideas. For example, why is there a $\tau_w$ but no $\tau$? Why not replace $\hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r}$ with p, q, r in Section 4 and state that the hats are dropped for brevity? The 't' superscripts in the message updates appear redundant and could be replaced with an assignment arrow (`\gets`). The authors should consider reverting to a simpler notation, which was likely used during the initial derivation.
    *   The exposition could be significantly improved. Key relationships are not explained. For example, the authors should state that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, or that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. Algorithm 2 is nearly row-column symmetric, a fact that should be highlighted to simplify its presentation.
    *   The meaning of functions is not clearly explained upon their introduction. For instance, the definitions in (10) would be clearer if accompanied by prose explanations (e.g., "$f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$"). Similarly, the fact that "$G_\beta$ is a scaled covariance matrix of $q_\beta$" is mentioned too late and should accompany its definition.

3.  **Unanswered Questions:** Several aspects of the model and algorithm are left unexplained. What is the significance of the factor `m` in the additive noise variance? Does it have a practical role in matrix factorization applications or a theoretical one in the derivation of the approximations? It appears to be the only element that breaks the row-column symmetry of the algorithm; is this correct? The authors likely know the answers but have not shared these insights with the reader.

**Minor Points for Improvement**

*   In Section 5, please add a specific citation for the VBMF-MA implementation to clarify which of the previously mentioned VBMF papers guided the implementation.
*   In the supplementary material, the derivation from equation 14 to 15, which involves dropping terms of order O(1/m), was difficult to follow and could be explained more clearly.
*   Also in the supplement, the text mentions that a technique from prior work is to "give an approximate representation of these messages in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters." It is unclear how the approximation used here relates to or differs from what has been published before. The novelty of the derivation should be clarified.

**Conclusion**

The paper introduces a new and potentially useful algorithm, filling a gap by being one of the first to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. The core mathematical ideas seem interesting. However, the paper in its current form is not ready for publication. The lack of proper scholarly context, the questionable evaluation framework, and the profoundly unclear presentation of the algorithm and its derivation place an unacceptable burden on the reader. The paper requires a major revision to address these issues of clarity and scholarship before it can be considered for acceptance.
