Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates simplifying approximations assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The method is evaluated in an unconventional way, framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing performance to Lloyd’s K-means algorithm. It is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), appearing to be the only prior work employing message-passing for reconstruction.

**Cons**

A key concern is the evaluation of the algorithm on a non-standard problem – clustering formulated as matrix factorization. This makes extrapolating experimental results to more typical matrix reconstruction scenarios difficult. For example, the cited VBMF references (Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin & Karhunen, 2007) both utilize the Netflix dataset for evaluation. Ideally, this paper would employ the same dataset. While BP often surpasses Variational methods in accuracy, evaluating this specific BP variant with a novel criterion raises questions about its true competitiveness. If Netflix or a comparable dataset is infeasible, the authors should clearly explain why.

The algorithm itself represents a relatively direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed using Variational Message Passing. While novel, it isn’t a particularly groundbreaking advance. The most intriguing aspect lies in the approximations introduced in the limits $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the input matrix dimensions. However, the validity of these approximations – described only in the Supplementary Material – isn’t directly tested, and they could benefit from a clearer explanation.

A significant issue concerns the citation of prior work. The paper initially presented the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution, as suggested by the abstract and Section 2.2. This impression was misleading, as other reviewers informed me this connection is well-established. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this prior knowledge, either through citation or a statement confirming its widespread recognition.

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it’s the standard term for applying BP to continuous distributions. The relationship between EP and the current algorithm should be explained. Relatedly, while the core algorithm is best understood as Belief Propagation, this is not mentioned until Section 4.1 and should be highlighted in the abstract.

**Pros**

The paper is interesting and presents a new, potentially valuable algorithm. The mathematics is largely accessible, and while I haven't verified it independently, it seems plausible. Although the new evaluation criterion warrants scrutiny, the K-means problem might be sufficiently general to provide a valid comparison, and the experiments do demonstrate a benefit for the proposed algorithm.

**Clarity**

The initial presentation is reasonably clear, with a well-written introduction and summary of existing work. Moving the algorithm’s derivation to the Supplementary Material is a drawback; outlining it in the main paper would be preferable. The experiments section is clear, although some plots are difficult to interpret in grayscale. 

However, understanding the algorithm’s description proved challenging, hindering a complete verification of the derivation. The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect. Even superficially, it’s difficult to follow. For instance, the repeated factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e add unnecessary complexity and could be factored out for readability.  The last two terms in 9b and 9e could also be combined.

The notation is overly burdened with hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. The presence of $\tauw$ without a corresponding $\tau$ is confusing; simply replacing $\tauw$ with $\tau$ would be clearer. Section 4, the most important section, uses only $\hat{p}$, $\hat{q}$, and $\hat{r}$.  A statement at the beginning of the section like, “We will drop the hats for brevity” would improve readability. The ‘t’ superscripts in the messages seem unnecessary. Replacing "=" with "\gets" would allow their removal.  The original derivation likely didn't involve such cumbersome notation, and a return to simpler notation is recommended.

The exposition could be significantly improved. Explaining that equation 5 represents the negative logarithm of equation 2, or that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$, would be helpful. Algorithm 2 exhibits row-column symmetry; pointing this out, and shortening the presentation by stating “then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v,” would enhance clarity. 

The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be defined near their introduction. Stating “f\beta is the mean of q\beta” is clearer than presenting an equation without explanation. Similarly, clarifying that “G\beta(b, \Lambda; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)” at its definition, rather than only at the end of Section 4.2, would improve understanding.

Currently, none of the messages have $\beta$ subscripts, despite their dependence on f and G, which do. Since f and G only depend on q_\beta, eliminating these subscripts entirely—even from q—is advisable.  $\beta$ functions as a global variable, which is acceptable. Before equations (15) and (16), a statement like “in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, f and G take the following form:” would be appropriate. 

On a more fundamental level, further clarification of the algorithm’s elements would be beneficial. What is the significance of the 'm' factor in the additive noise variance? Does it have broader implications for matrix factorization applications or the derivation's approximation? Does it contribute to the asymmetry between rows and columns? The authors likely have answers to these questions, but they are not addressed in the paper.

**Other questions:**

Is the use of a tilde to denote column vectors a standard convention? The capitalization of 'N' versus the lowercase 'm' is also unclear. In Section 5, a citation after “VBMF-MA” is needed to identify the primary reference guiding its implementation. The two earlier citations for variational matrix factorization leave ambiguity. 

**Supplementary Material:**

In the Supplementary Material, rewriting equation 5 as a product of exponentials would clarify its connection to the probability density function. The step from equation 14 to 15 in Section 2 is unclear, particularly regarding the big-O notation of m terms. The statement at the top of page 2—"A technique used in… is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters"—lacks context without prior knowledge of the referenced papers. Clarifying the novelty of the algorithm's derivation is essential, both in the Supplementary Material and the main paper.

The English is generally good, but occasional issues with articles can be distracting. A more descriptive title, such as "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm," would be more informative. (See specific suggestions for minor edits on pages 1-7). The diagram in Figure 1 of the Supplementary Material is helpful, and including more such diagrams would enhance the paper.

**Overall:**

The mathematics appears interesting, and the algorithm merits publication as a first attempt to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper requires significant revisions to address clarity and scholarly rigor. In its current state, it places an unacceptable burden on readers and would reflect poorly on the conference.
