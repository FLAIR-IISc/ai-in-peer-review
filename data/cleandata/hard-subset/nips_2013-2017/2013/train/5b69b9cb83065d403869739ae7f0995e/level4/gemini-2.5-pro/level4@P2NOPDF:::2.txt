### **Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"**

#### **Summary**
This paper introduces a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction derived from applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model. The algorithm relies on two simplifying approximations, valid for large matrices, which are detailed in the supplementary material. The authors evaluate their method in a novel setting by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem, comparing their algorithm's performance against Lloyd's K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).

#### **Strengths**
The paper is interesting to read and presents a novel algorithm with clear potential utility. Applying BP to this problem is a valuable contribution to the field. The mathematical framework, while complex, appears sound and well-motivated. The novel evaluation against K-means is clever and effectively demonstrates a clear benefit for the proposed algorithm in the conducted experiments.

#### **Weaknesses**
Despite its strengths, the paper has several significant weaknesses in its current form related to evaluation, scholarship, and clarity.

**1. Evaluation and Benchmarking**
A primary concern is the non-standard evaluation framework. By testing a new reconstruction algorithm on a clustering task, it becomes difficult to contextualize its performance against state-of-the-art methods on conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, the cited VBMF papers by Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko et al. (2007) both use the Netflix dataset for benchmarking. The absence of results on such a standard dataset makes it challenging to assess the proposed algorithm's actual competitiveness. While BP is often more accurate than variational methods, this unconventional evaluation strategy casts doubt on its practical advantages. The authors should either include experiments on a standard dataset or provide a compelling justification for its omission.

**2. Novelty and Contribution**
The core algorithm appears to be a relatively direct application of BP to a model previously addressed by Variational Message Passing. While this is a new application, it is more incremental than groundbreaking. The most scientifically interesting aspect of the work lies in the approximations derived in the limit of large matrix dimensions ($m, N \to \infty$). However, these approximations are described only in the supplementary material, their validity is never directly tested, and their explanation could be significantly improved.

**3. Scholarship and Citations**
There are serious issues regarding the citation of prior work. The abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that applying matrix factorization to clustering is a novel contribution of this paper. This connection is, in fact, well-known in the literature. This omission is misleading and fails to properly contextualize the work. If the connection is considered common knowledge, it should be stated as such; otherwise, relevant prior work must be cited.

Furthermore, the paper should cite and discuss Expectation Propagation (EP), which is the standard term for applying BP to continuous distributions. The relationship between the proposed algorithm and EP should be clarified. Finally, the fact that the algorithm is an application of Belief Propagation is a central point that should be mentioned in the abstract, yet it is not revealed until Section 4.1.

**4. Clarity and Presentation**
The clarity of the presentation, particularly regarding the algorithm's derivation and description, is a major drawback.

*   **Derivation:** The full derivation is relegated to the supplementary material. At a minimum, a high-level outline of the derivation should be included in the main paper to make it self-contained.

*   **Mathematical Notation:** The mathematical presentation is difficult to follow. The notation is overly cumbersome, with an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts that hinder readability.
    *   For example, factors like $1 / m \tau_w$ could be factored out of equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e.
    *   Terms could be combined in 9b and 9e.
    *   The use of $\tau_w$ without a corresponding $\tau$ is confusing; simply using $\tau$ would be clearer.
    *   In Section 4, parameters like $p, q, r$ only appear with hats ($\hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r}$). The hats could be dropped for brevity within the section.
    *   The time superscripts ('t') in the message updates appear unnecessary. Using an assignment operator (`\gets`) instead of an equality sign would allow these to be removed, simplifying the equations.

*   **Exposition:** The exposition could be greatly improved by explaining the relationships between equations and the meaning of terms.
    *   For instance, the authors should state that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2.
    *   Algorithm 2 is nearly symmetric with respect to rows and columns, a property that should be highlighted to aid understanding and potentially shorten the description.
    *   The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be explained in prose (e.g., "f_β is the mean of q_β") alongside their definitions. The explanation of $G_\beta$ as a scaled covariance matrix comes too late.
    *   The propagation of the $\beta$ subscript is inconsistent (present on f and G but not on the messages that depend on them), suggesting it could be treated as a global parameter to simplify notation.

*   **Unanswered Questions:** The paper fails to discuss the significance of certain modeling choices. For example, what is the role of the factor `m` in the additive noise variance? Is it this factor that breaks the row-column symmetry of the algorithm? Discussing these points would provide deeper insight.

#### **Detailed Comments and Questions**
*   Is the use of a tilde for column vectors standard notation? This was initially confusing.
*   Is there a reason `N` is capitalized while `m` is lowercase?
*   Section 5 should include a specific citation for the VBMF-MA implementation used for comparison.
*   In the supplementary material, the derivation step from equation 14 to 15 involving big-O notation was difficult to follow and could benefit from more explanation.
*   The novelty of the message approximation technique mentioned on page 2 of the supplement is unclear. The authors should clarify how their approach relates to or differs from the cited prior work.
*   Figure 1 in the supplement is very helpful. Including more such diagrams in the main paper would enhance clarity.

#### **Minor Language Suggestions**
The English is generally good, but some minor grammatical errors, primarily related to articles, could be corrected to improve readability.
*   p. 1: "Since properties" -> "Since the properties"
*   p. 3: "Previous works" -> "Previous work"
*   p. 4: "particularized" -> "specialized"
*   p. 6: "stuck to bad local minima" -> "stuck in bad local minima"
*   p. 7: "This is contrastive to that" -> "This is in contrast to"

#### **Conclusion**
The algorithm presented in this paper is an interesting and potentially important contribution as one of the first applications of BP to matrix factorization. However, the paper in its current form suffers from significant issues in its evaluation, citation practices, and clarity of presentation. These shortcomings place an undue burden on the reader and prevent a clear assessment of the work's contributions. While the core ideas are valuable, the paper requires substantial revision before it can be considered for publication.
