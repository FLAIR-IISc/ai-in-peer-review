Summary
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for matrix factorization and then specializes it to clustering. The authors argue that matrix factorization generalizes clustering, derive a message passing scheme in that setting, and empirically compare the resulting clustering method against k-means++.

Strengths
- The problem is timely and important, and the connection drawn between matrix factorization and clustering is interesting.
- The paper is generally well structured and the main idea is easy to follow.
- The empirical section is a good first step toward assessing practical value.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality and rigor of derivations
Overall, the derivations are too terse and omit key nontrivial steps (even in the appendix), making it hard to assess correctness. Several points need clarification or correction:

1) Appendix line 139: order-of-magnitude claim for (11).
- You state that the second term of (11) is O(1/sqrt(m)). Please justify this scaling. As written, it is unclear what concentration or independence assumptions are being used, and whether constants depend on n or other dimensions.
- In addition, β appears to be omitted in Section 2 (and possibly here). Please clarify where β enters the updates and whether any rescaling absorbs it.

2) Asymptotic regime for state evolution and derivation of (19).
- Parts of the analysis (including the derivation of (19)) seem to implicitly assume m and n grow at the same rate. Please make the asymptotic regime explicit (e.g., m,n→∞ with m/n→δ) and state where the results rely on that scaling.
- The asymptotic arguments are terse; providing a clear statement of assumptions (distributional, independence, bounded moments, etc.) and a roadmap to the limit (law of large numbers, central limit theorem, Onsager correction) would greatly improve rigor.

3) Appendix line 167: expectation of A_il^2.
- You assert E[A_il^2] = m τ_w^2. Since A is observed data, and τ_w^2 is the variance of the likelihood noise, this identity is not immediate. If the generative model is A = UV^T + W with W_ij ~ N(0, τ_w^2), then E[A_il^2] should include both signal and noise contributions, and its scaling with m depends on index conventions. Please derive this approximation carefully and state the assumptions under which it holds.

4) β → ∞ limit and connection to MAP.
- How does the message passing algorithm at finite β converge to the MAP estimator as β→∞? The algorithm appears to minimize a Bethe (or related) free energy rather than a KL divergence directly, and exchanging limits with minimization is not always valid. Please state conditions under which lim_{β→∞} argmin F_β = argmin F_∞ holds, or provide a counterexample/intuition if not.

5) Proposition 1 and equation (17).
- The proof is too brief. Please provide the full derivation of (17), including any approximations, scaling limits, and use of independence or Gaussianity. If this relies on standard AMP arguments (Onsager term derivation, state evolution), cite them explicitly and adapt the steps to your model.

Clarity and presentation
- The main narrative is clear, but key notation and assumptions need to be stated up front: dimensions (m,n,k), priors, likelihood, the role of β, and the precise factor graph.
- Provide a concise algorithm box or pseudocode, with per-iteration computational and memory complexity, as well as recommended damping and stopping criteria.
- Clearly distinguish between exact expectations under the model and approximations justified by large-system limits.

Originality and relation to prior work
- The algorithm appears closely related to Expectation Propagation (EP): it estimates first and second moments of marginal posteriors and Section 1 of the appendix resembles standard EP updates. EP has been applied to matrix factorization previously (e.g., Stern et al., WWW’09). Please clarify the novelty relative to:
  - EP-based matrix factorization (e.g., the above) and other Gaussian/loopy message passing approaches.
  - AMP/GAMP-style algorithms for generalized linear models and matrix factorization, including the role of Onsager corrections and state evolution. If your method departs from EP in a principled AMP way, make those differences explicit and explain the benefits (accuracy, convergence guarantees, analyzability).
- There may be a connection to the EP “clutter” model (Minka, UAI’01) if the likelihood is a uniform mixture of Gaussians. It would be helpful to discuss whether the proposed clustering specialization coincides with, generalizes, or differs from this setting, and what that implies for performance and analysis.

Significance and empirical evaluation
- Focusing on clustering is reasonable, but comparing only against k-means++ is insufficient given the breadth of clustering literature. To establish practical value, please include stronger baselines, such as:
  - Gaussian Mixture Models with EM (with and without k-means++ initialization).
  - Spectral clustering variants.
  - Hierarchical clustering (e.g., Ward linkage) and DP-means/Bayesian GMMs where appropriate.
  - If feasible, an EP-based matrix factorization baseline and/or related AMP/GAMP methods.
- Report standard metrics (e.g., accuracy, NMI, ARI), runtime, and memory, and assess sensitivity to:
  - Aspect ratio m/n and scale (to test the asymptotic assumptions).
  - Noise level τ_w^2 and model misspecification.
  - Number of clusters and initialization.
  - Algorithmic hyperparameters (β, damping).
- Consider including ablations (e.g., with and without Onsager terms or different approximations) to substantiate the contribution of each component.

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand the appendix to include the missing derivation steps, especially for (11), (17), and (19), with clear assumptions and scaling statements.
- Make the β dependence explicit throughout and rigorously address the β→∞ (MAP) connection.
- Provide an algorithm summary with complexity and practical guidance (initialization, damping, stopping).
- Strengthen the related work section to position the contribution relative to EP-based matrix factorization and AMP/GAMP literature, and articulate the unique aspects of your approach.
- Broaden and deepen the empirical comparison as outlined above; add reproducibility details (datasets, preprocessing, hyperparameters, code).

Overall evaluation
The paper tackles a promising idea and is clearly written at a high level, but the current version lacks sufficient mathematical detail to assess correctness, and the novelty relative to established EP/AMP approaches is unclear. The empirical evaluation is too narrow to establish significance. With more rigorous derivations, a clearer positioning with respect to EP and AMP, and a broader experimental study, the work could have meaningful impact. As it stands, I cannot confidently recommend acceptance.
