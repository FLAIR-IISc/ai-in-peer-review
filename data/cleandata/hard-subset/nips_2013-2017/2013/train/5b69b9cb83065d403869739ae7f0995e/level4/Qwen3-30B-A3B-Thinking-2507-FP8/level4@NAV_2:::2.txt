

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering" 

This paper introduces a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, detailed in the supplementary material, incorporates two simplifying approximations that assume a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The paper evaluates this algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing it against Lloyd's K-means algorithm, as well as Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

Cons

Several concerns regarding acceptance merit consideration. The evaluation on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization) makes it difficult to extrapolate the experimental results to conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, two references cited for VBMF (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen, 2007) both use the Netflix dataset for evaluation, which would have provided a more direct comparison. The absence of such a standard dataset creates uncertainty about the algorithm's actual competitiveness. If the Netflix dataset cannot be used, the authors should explain why in the paper.

The algorithm appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While novel, it does not represent a groundbreaking contribution. The most interesting aspect is the approximations introduced in the limit of large matrix dimensions, but their validity—only described in supplementary material—is never directly tested, and the explanations could be clearer.

The paper contains serious citation issues. The abstract and Section 2.2 present the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution, yet this connection is well-established in the literature. The paper should clarify this to avoid misleading readers. If the connection is too well-known to cite specific references, it should state this explicitly. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the standard term for applying BP to continuous distributions, and the relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm should be explained. The fact that the algorithm is an application of Belief Propagation is not mentioned until Section 4.1, though it should be included in the abstract.

Pros

The paper is engaging and presents a potentially useful algorithm. The mathematical content is understandable, and while I did not replicate it by hand, the derivations appear feasible. Although I'm skeptical about new evaluation criteria, the K-means problem seems sufficiently general to provide a fair comparison, and the experiments demonstrate clear benefits for the new algorithm.

Clarity

The early sections are generally clear, with a well-structured introduction and summary of prior work. However, the derivation of the algorithm appearing only in the supplementary material is a significant drawback; the main paper should outline the key steps. The experiments section is clear, though some figures are difficult to interpret in black and white.

The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect. The notation is unnecessarily complex, with factors of $1/m\tau$ appearing in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e that could be factored out to improve readability. The last two terms in 9b and 9e could also be combined. The excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts is confusing; for instance, the paper uses $\tau_w$ without $\tau$, and in Section 4 uses $\hat{p}$, $\hat{q}$, $\hat{r}$ instead of simpler notation. The $t$ superscripts in the algorithm are unnecessary; replacing "=" with "$\gets$" would eliminate the need for them.

The exposition could be improved by explicitly stating that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2 and that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. The algorithm's near row-column symmetry should be highlighted, which would make it easier to understand. The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definition (e.g., "f is the mean of q" rather than just giving the equation).

The supplementary material has similar issues. The step from equation 14 to 15 in Section 2 regarding the big-O of $m$ terms is unclear. The explanation of the approximation technique used in the supplementary material should clarify how it relates to prior work.

Other questions

Why does the additive noise variance include a factor of $m$? This seems to break the row-column symmetry of the algorithm, yet the paper does not discuss its significance or role in applications. The use of a tilde to denote column vectors is confusing, and the inconsistent capitalization of $N$ versus $m$ is distracting.

In Section 5, a citation after VBMF-MA should indicate the primary reference for implementing this competing algorithm. The paper cites two variational matrix factorization papers earlier, but it's unclear which is intended.

The English is generally good, but there are minor language issues that could be improved: "Since properties" should be "Since the properties," "according to problems" should be "according to the problem," "has flexibility" should be "has enough flexibility," and "motivate use of" should be "motivate the use of." Similar suggestions apply to other sections.

The supplementary material also has language issues, such as "of these message" (should be "messages") and "a few number" (should be "a few").

The diagram in Figure 1 of the supplementary material is helpful, and additional diagrams would enhance the paper.

The mathematics is interesting and the algorithm should be published as the first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper requires significant revisions to improve clarity and scholarship before it can be accepted. The current lack of clarity and insufficient scholarly attention to prior work would place an unacceptable burden on readers and reflect poorly on the conference.
