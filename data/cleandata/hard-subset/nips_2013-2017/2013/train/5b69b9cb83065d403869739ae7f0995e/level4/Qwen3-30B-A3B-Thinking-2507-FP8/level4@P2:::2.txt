

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper introduces an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work from compressed sensing to matrix reconstruction. The authors formulate the problem as a Bayesian inference task, incorporating structural properties beyond low-rankness (e.g., sparsity) through flexible prior distributions. The key contribution is an AMP-based algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation, which the authors apply to clustering by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints. The paper demonstrates the algorithm's superiority over Lloyd's K-means and variational Bayes methods on clustering tasks.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a technically sound contribution by extending AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction, addressing a gap in the literature (prior AMP work was limited to rank-one cases or posterior mean estimation). The derivation, while relegated to supplementary material, appears mathematically rigorous.
2. The experimental evaluation on clustering is well-designed, with both synthetic and real-world data showing clear advantages over competing methods (K-means++, VBMF, and Lloyd's algorithm) in terms of accuracy and convergence speed.
3. The connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction is conceptually interesting, and the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm offers a novel perspective on an established problem.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's originality claim regarding the clustering-matrix reconstruction connection is problematic. The authors present this as a novel contribution, but the connection has been established in prior work (e.g., Xu et al., 2003; Ding et al., 2010), which should have been properly acknowledged. This undermines the paper's novelty claim.
2. The experimental evaluation is limited to clustering, not general matrix reconstruction problems. Without evaluation on standard matrix reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix dataset), it's difficult to assess the algorithm's broader applicability. The authors should either expand their evaluation or clarify why standard benchmarks are inappropriate.
3. The paper suffers from significant clarity issues. The notation is unnecessarily complex (excessive hats, tildes, and subscripts), and key derivations appear only in supplementary material. The mathematical expressions (e.g., equations 9a-9d) are difficult to parse due to inconsistent scaling factors (1/mτ) and redundant terms. The authors should simplify notation (e.g., eliminate unnecessary "t" superscripts and standardize τ notation).
4. The paper fails to explain key design choices. For instance, the role of the factor "m" in the noise variance is unclear and breaks row-column symmetry, but the authors don't discuss its implications. Similarly, the paper doesn't clarify why AMP is used instead of established methods like variational Bayes, despite acknowledging EP (Expectation Propagation) as a related technique.

Quality: The technical approach is sound, but the limited experimental scope reduces confidence in the algorithm's general utility. The paper would benefit from more thorough theoretical analysis of the AMP approximations, which are only described in supplementary material.

Clarity: The paper would be significantly improved by simplifying notation, moving key derivations to the main text, and clarifying mathematical expressions. The authors should explain why equations 5 and 8 are equivalent to the negative log-likelihood and the original posterior raised to power β, respectively.

Originality: The AMP extension to general-rank matrix reconstruction is novel, but the clustering application is not. The authors should acknowledge prior work on matrix reconstruction for clustering and focus on the AMP-specific contributions.

Significance: The algorithm shows promise for clustering applications, but the narrow evaluation scope limits its perceived significance. The paper would be stronger with broader experimental validation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The AMP extension to low-rank matrix reconstruction is technically sound and fills an important gap.
- The clustering application demonstrates clear practical advantages over existing methods.
- The paper introduces a novel algorithm with potential for broader application in matrix reconstruction.

Arguments against acceptance:
- The paper misrepresents the clustering-matrix reconstruction connection as novel when it's well-established.
- Experimental validation is limited to clustering, not general matrix reconstruction.
- Clarity issues make the paper difficult to follow without significant effort.
- Key theoretical approximations lack direct validation and are only described in supplementary material.

The paper has merit but requires substantial revisions to address clarity issues, properly contextualize prior work, and expand experimental evaluation. While the algorithm itself appears promising, the current presentation creates unnecessary barriers to understanding and undermines the paper's novelty claims. I recommend rejection in its current form, but the authors should be encouraged to revise with improved clarity and broader experimental validation.
