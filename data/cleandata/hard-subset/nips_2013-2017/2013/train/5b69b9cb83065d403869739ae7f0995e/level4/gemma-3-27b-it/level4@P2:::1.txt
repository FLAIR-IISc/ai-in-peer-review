This paper introduces an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for reconstructing low-rank matrices from noisy observations, particularly when additional structural properties are known about the matrix factors. The authors formulate the problem within a Bayesian framework to leverage these properties and demonstrate the application of their algorithm to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a low-rank reconstruction problem.

The core contribution lies in extending existing AMP algorithms for matrix reconstruction to handle general rank cases and enable MAP estimation, a capability absent in prior work [9]. Furthermore, the application of this AMP-based approach to K-means clustering, directly enforcing the constraint of exclusive cluster assignment within the low-rank framework, represents a novel direction. The authors showcase empirically that their algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means algorithm, especially in high-dimensional data settings.

The proposed approach is technically sound and the connection to existing literature on AMP [6, 7, 8] is clearly established. The novelty of applying AMP to this specific low-rank matrix reconstruction setting, and its subsequent application to clustering with the stated constraint, is a strength. The experimental evaluation is thorough, comparing performance not only in terms of reconstruction error (Frobenius norm) but also clustering accuracy, and including comparisons to established algorithms like K-means and K-means++ [16]. The presentation of results across both artificial and real datasets (the ORL face database [20]) strengthens the empirical validation.

However, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of theoretical convergence guarantees. While the authors acknowledge the lack of such guarantees (a common challenge in K-means-related algorithms), referencing relevant work in theoretical computer science offering provable approximation algorithms for the K-means objective function could provide valuable context. The derivation of the algorithm, while presented, is dense, and a clearer explanation of the key steps and assumptions would be helpful. A comparison with variational Bayes approaches [4, 5] beyond merely mentioning their existence would further enhance the work. Specifically, a more detailed discussion of the potential benefits of AMP over variational methods, given its ability to capture dependencies between matrix factors, could solidify the contribution. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural priors.
*   Demonstrated improvement over standard K-means, particularly in high dimensions.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Clear connection to existing literature on AMP and matrix factorization.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis regarding convergence guarantees.
*   Derivation of the algorithm could benefit from increased clarity.
*   A more in-depth comparison with variational Bayes methods is warranted.



Overall, this is a solid paper with promising results. While the theoretical aspects could be strengthened, the novel application of AMP, coupled with the compelling empirical evaluation, suggests that this work represents a valuable contribution to the field.
