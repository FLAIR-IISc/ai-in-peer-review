This paper proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction derived from applying Belief Propagation (BP) to a Bayesian model of the problem. As detailed in the supplementary material, the algorithm relies on two simplifying approximations that are valid in the limit of large matrix dimensions. The algorithm is evaluated unconventionally by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing its performance against Lloyd's K-means algorithm. It is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), a notable prior message-passing approach.

### Cons

Several significant issues argue against the paper's acceptance.

**1. Experimental Evaluation and Competitiveness:** The choice of a non-standard evaluation task—clustering formulated as matrix factorization—makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance on conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For example, the VBMF papers cited by the authors (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen, 2007) both benchmark their methods on the Netflix dataset. The authors should have used this or a similar dataset to allow for direct comparison with prior work. If such a dataset cannot be used, a compelling reason for its omission must be provided. While BP is often more accurate than variational methods, evaluating this new variant on a novel criterion obscures its true competitiveness against established baselines.

**2. Novelty and Contribution:** The core algorithm is a relatively straightforward application of Belief Propagation to a problem previously addressed by Variational Message Passing. While novel, this contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking. The most significant contribution may be the approximations introduced for large matrices ($m \to \infty, N \to \infty$). However, these are relegated to the supplementary material, their validity is not empirically tested, and their explanation could be clearer.

**3. Citation of Prior Work:** There are serious deficiencies in the paper's citation of prior work. The abstract and Section 2.2 misleadingly suggest that applying matrix factorization to clustering is an original contribution of this paper. This connection is, in fact, well-established. If this link is considered common knowledge, the paper should state so; otherwise, relevant prior work must be cited. Furthermore, the paper fails to cite Expectation Propagation (EP), which is the standard term for applying BP to continuous distributions, and should discuss the relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm. Relatedly, the fact that the algorithm is an application of Belief Propagation is a central point that should be stated in the abstract, not delayed until Section 4.1.

### Pros

Despite these criticisms, the paper presents a new and potentially useful algorithm. The manuscript is engaging, and the mathematical derivations appear sound and replicable in principle. While the novelty of the evaluation criterion raises concerns, the K-means task is general enough to provide a potentially fair comparison and effectively demonstrates the proposed algorithm's benefits in the presented experiments.

### Clarity

The paper's clarity is mixed. The introduction and literature review are easy to follow. However, relegating the entire algorithm derivation to the supplement is a significant drawback; at least an outline should be included in the main text. The experiments are clearly described, and the plots are readable despite rendering poorly in black and white.

The mathematical presentation of the algorithm itself is the weakest aspect of the paper and is particularly difficult to parse.
*   **Notation:** The notation is unnecessarily cumbersome. Factors like $1 / m \tau_w$ in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out. The last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined. The profusion of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts hinders readability. For instance, $\tau_w$ could be simplified to $\tau$. In Section 4, where variables like p, q, and r appear only with hats, the hats could be dropped for brevity after an initial note. The 't' superscripts in the message updates are redundant; replacing "=" with an assignment arrow "$\gets$" would allow for their removal. The authors should consider reverting to a simpler notation, which they likely used during the initial derivation.
*   **Exposition:** The exposition could be improved by explicitly stating connections and interpretations. For example, the authors should explain that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, and that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. The near row-column symmetry of Algorithm 2 should be noted, which would allow for a more concise presentation.
*   **Explanation:** The meaning of functions, such as those in equation 10, should be explained near their definitions (e.g., "f$_\beta$ is the mean of q$_\beta$"). The interpretation of G$_\beta$ as a scaled covariance matrix is mentioned too late and should appear with its definition. The use of $\beta$ subscripts is also inconsistent; they appear on f and G but not on the messages that depend on them. It would be clearer to treat $\beta$ as a global parameter and state the limiting forms of f and G as $\beta \to \infty$.

There are also deeper conceptual points that need clarification. What is the significance of the factor of $m$ in the additive noise variance? What role does it play in the problem formulation and the derivation's approximations? It appears to be the only element breaking the algorithm's row-column symmetry, which merits discussion.

### Other Questions and Suggestions

*   Is the use of a tilde to denote column vectors standard in this field? This was initially unclear.
*   Why is the matrix dimension $N$ capitalized while $m$ is lowercase?
*   In Section 5, please add a specific citation for the VBMF-MA implementation to clarify which of the two VBMF references was used.
*   In the supplementary material, the derivation from equation 14 to 15 involving big-O notation was difficult to follow and could be explained more thoroughly.
*   The novelty of the approximation technique described at the top of page 2 of the supplement should be clarified with respect to the cited prior work, both in the supplement and in the main paper.
*   The diagram in Figure 1 of the supplement is very helpful. Adding more diagrams to the main paper would enhance clarity.
*   While the paper is generally well-written, it contains numerous minor grammatical errors, primarily concerning the use of articles ("a", "the"). Fixing these would improve readability (e.g., "Since properties" -> "Since the properties," "Previous works" -> "Previous work").

### Conclusion

In summary, this paper introduces a potentially valuable algorithm that warrants publication. However, the current manuscript is not ready for acceptance at NIPS. Significant revisions are required to address major issues in experimental evaluation, positioning with respect to prior work, and the clarity of the mathematical presentation. As it stands, the paper places an undue burden on the reader and does not meet the high standards of this conference.
