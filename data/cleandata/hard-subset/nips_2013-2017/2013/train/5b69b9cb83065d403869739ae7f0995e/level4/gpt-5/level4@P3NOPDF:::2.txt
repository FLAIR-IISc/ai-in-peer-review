Review of “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering”

Summary
The paper proposes a new belief-propagation (BP)–based message-passing algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction under a Bayesian model. Two large-system approximations (limits in the number of rows m and columns N) are used to make the algorithm tractable; these are presented in the Supplementary Material. The method is evaluated via a non-standard but creative protocol that casts clustering (K-means) as a matrix reconstruction problem, and is compared against Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).

Strengths
- The paper addresses an important problem and introduces a potentially useful BP-based algorithm with clear empirical gains on the proposed clustering-as-reconstruction benchmark.
- The writing is generally engaging; the introduction and related work overview are accessible.
- The experimental section is understandable and, despite some plotting issues in grayscale, the figures are readable.
- The supplementary Figure 1 is helpful; more such schematics could further aid comprehension.

Weaknesses and main concerns
- Non-standard evaluation and unclear competitiveness: The primary evaluation frames clustering as matrix factorization, which makes it difficult to extrapolate to standard reconstruction tasks. Prior VBMF works (e.g., Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin & Karhunen, 2007) used the Netflix dataset and compared to established baselines. Ideally, this paper would evaluate on Netflix or a comparable dataset. If this is infeasible, the paper should clearly justify why and provide an alternative, broadly accepted benchmark. While BP often outperforms variational methods, a novel evaluation criterion leaves the algorithm’s true competitiveness uncertain.
- Incremental novelty relative to prior message passing: The main algorithm reads as a fairly direct BP instantiation for a model previously tackled with variational message passing. The most interesting elements are the large-system approximations (m → ∞ and N → ∞), but these approximations are not empirically validated and are described only in the supplement. Their derivation and applicability should be outlined in the main text and, if possible, stress-tested experimentally (e.g., ablation or finite-size sensitivity).
- Scholarship and attribution: 
  - The link between matrix factorization and clustering appears presented as if new; this connection is well known. Please make this explicit and cite representative prior work, or state that it is widely known if no single definitive source exists.
  - Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited and the relationship between EP and the proposed BP variant clarified, especially given the continuous latent variables. 
  - The abstract should explicitly state that the method is a BP-based algorithm; this is only made clear in Section 4.1.
- Clarity and mathematical presentation: The derivation is confined to the Supplementary Material; a high-level derivation and intuition should appear in the main text. Several notational and expository choices impede readability:
  - Equations (9a), (9b), (9d), (9e): factors of 1/(m τ_w) could be factored out to improve readability; the last two terms in (9b) and (9e) can be combined.
  - Notation overload (hats, tildes, subscripts, superscripts): for example, there is τ_w but no τ; consider renaming τ_w to τ if no ambiguity arises. In Section 4, p, q, r appear only with hats; consider dropping hats throughout that section after a brief note. The superscript t for iterations seems unnecessary; replacing “=” with “←” in update equations would let you omit all superscripts.
  - Expository bridges: state that Eq. (5) is the negative log of Eq. (2), and that Eq. (8) is Eq. (2) raised to the power β. Algorithm 2 is nearly row–column symmetric; point this out and consider presenting only one set of updates with a “swap u and v” instruction to halve redundancy.
  - Definition and meaning of functions in Eq. (10): define f_β as the mean of q_β and G_β as its (scaled) covariance at the point of introduction, with a brief plain-language interpretation. The later remark that “G_β(b, Λ; p) is a scaled covariance of q_β(u; b, Λ, p)” should be anticipated at definition time.
  - β subscripts: messages lack β subscripts although they depend on f_β and G_β; since β only enters through q_β, consider treating β as a global parameter and dropping β subscripts from f, G, and messages. Before Eqs. (15)–(16), explicitly state the β → ∞ limiting forms.
- Conceptual clarity: Please explain the role and significance of the factor m in the additive noise variance. Does it arise from the approximations, does it influence row–column asymmetry, and how does it relate to typical matrix factorization applications?

Additional comments and questions
- Notation conventions: clarify early if tildes denote column vectors; unify capitalization (why N uppercase but m lowercase?).
- Competing method details: In Section 5, add an explicit citation after VBMF-MA indicating the primary reference used for the implementation (Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007; or both), and specify any deviations from those references.
- Supplementary material:
  - Eq. (5): using a product of exponentials for the two z-related terms would make it clear which is the pdf.
  - Section 2, Eqs. (14) → (15): please detail the big-O(m) steps; this is a sticking point.
  - Page 2 top: “these message” → “these messages”; “a few number” → “a few.” More importantly, clarify precisely how your approximation relates to prior “approximate representation of messages” techniques and what is novel in your derivation; reflect this in the main paper as well.

Minor language and style suggestions (non-exhaustive)
- Title: consider “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message-passing algorithm.”
- Page 1: “Since properties” → “Since the properties”; “according to problems” → “according to the problem”; “has flexibility” → “has enough flexibility” (or “is flexible enough”); “motivate use of” → “motivate the use of.”
- Page 2: “We present results” → “We present the results.”
- Page 3: “We regard that” → “We see that”; consider italicizing “maximum accuracy clustering”; “Previous works” → “Previous work”; “ICM algorithm; it” (use colon or comma, not semicolon with capital I).
- Page 4: “particularized” → “specialized.”
- Page 5: “plausible properties” → “discernible properties.”
- Page 6: “stuck to bad local minima” → “stuck in bad local minima.”
- Page 7: “This is contrastive to that” → “This is in contrast to the fact that.”

Overall assessment and recommendation
This is a promising contribution that brings BP to low-rank matrix reconstruction and demonstrates encouraging results on a creative clustering-as-reconstruction evaluation. However, the current submission has significant issues in evaluation design, validation of key approximations, attribution, and mathematical clarity. These collectively place too much burden on the reader and obscure the contribution’s scope and novelty.

Recommendation: Reject for now. I encourage resubmission after (i) adding standard reconstruction benchmarks or strong justification for alternatives; (ii) validating or at least stress-testing the large-system approximations; (iii) strengthening citations (clustering–factorization link, EP, VBMF implementation details) and mentioning BP in the abstract; and (iv) streamlining notation and exposition as outlined above.
