This paper presents an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to the clustering problem. The authors formulate the reconstruction as a Bayesian problem and leverage AMP, extending existing work to handle MAP estimation and demonstrate its application to clustering, outperforming Lloyd’s K-means algorithm in experiments.

**Quality:** The paper’s technical details require significant clarification and justification. While the core idea is promising, the mathematical derivations, even those relegated to the appendix, lack rigor and omit crucial steps. Specifically: 1) The claim in line 139 of the Appendix regarding the scaling of the second term in equation (11) – specifically the O(1/√m) dependence – is unsubstantiated and appears to be missing the β parameter introduced earlier. 2) The asymptotic analysis relies on the assumption that *m* and *N* grow at the same rate, a condition that should be explicitly stated and its implications discussed. The brevity of the analysis raises concerns about its overall validity. 3) The justification for approximating the expectation of A²<sub>*il*</sub> with *mτ<sub>w</sub>²* (line 167) is unclear, as it equates an expectation of observed data with a variance parameter from the likelihood model. A more thorough explanation is needed. 4) The convergence of the AMP algorithm as β approaches infinity to approximate the MAP problem is not convincingly demonstrated. The argument relies on the interchange of limits, which isn’t generally valid for the KL divergence. 5) The proof of Proposition 1 is condensed and lacks sufficient detail. The derivation of equation (17) requires further elaboration.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and the central motivation is easily understood. The organization flows logically, presenting the problem, proposed solution, and experimental results in a clear sequence.

**Originality:** The work's novelty is questionable. The proposed algorithm largely focuses on estimating the first and second moments of the marginal posterior. This strongly suggests a close relationship to Expectation Propagation (EP), a technique already applied to matrix factorization, as demonstrated in Stern et al. (2009) [http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf]. Section 1 of the Appendix closely resembles standard EP update procedures. Furthermore, the clustering application appears to be connected to the original clutter problem formulation of EP (Minka, 1998 [http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf]), especially when considering a uniform mixture of Gaussian distributions as the likelihood. The authors should explicitly address this connection and clearly delineate the contributions of their work beyond a direct application of EP.

**Significance:** The paper primarily emphasizes the application to clustering. Given the extensive existing literature in clustering, comparing performance solely to K-means++ may not adequately establish the practical significance of the proposed algorithm. The lack of detailed proofs and the potential connection to existing EP methods also hinder a comprehensive assessment of the work’s impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The AMP algorithm demonstrates improved performance over K-means++ in the presented experiments, suggesting potential benefits for high-dimensional clustering.
*   The formulation of the problem within a Bayesian framework offers flexibility in incorporating prior knowledge.
*   The extension of existing AMP algorithms to handle MAP estimation is a potentially valuable contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The technical details lack rigor and are insufficiently justified, raising concerns about the correctness of the derivations.
*   The work appears to be a direct application of Expectation Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization, with limited novelty.
*   The evaluation is limited to a single clustering algorithm (K-means++) and may not fully demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed approach.
*   The mathematical derivations, even in the appendix, are too terse and difficult to follow without significant effort from the reviewer.



**Overall Recommendation:** I lean towards **reject** due to the concerns regarding technical rigor and originality. The authors need to significantly strengthen the mathematical justification for their approach, clearly position their work within the context of existing EP methods, and provide a more comprehensive evaluation to demonstrate practical significance. A substantial revision addressing these points would be necessary for reconsideration.
