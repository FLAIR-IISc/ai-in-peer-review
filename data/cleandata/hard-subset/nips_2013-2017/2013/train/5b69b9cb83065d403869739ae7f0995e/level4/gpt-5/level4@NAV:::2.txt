Review – Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This work proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction derived by applying Belief Propagation (BP) message passing to a Bayesian formulation of the problem. As detailed in the Supplementary Material, the method introduces two simplifying approximations that assume, respectively, a large number of rows and a large number of columns in the data matrix. The evaluation is unconventional: it benchmarks against Lloyd’s K-means by casting clustering as a matrix reconstruction task, and it also compares with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only prior message-passing approach to this reconstruction problem.

Cons
There are several reasons to be cautious about acceptance. Because the new method is assessed on a non-standard task (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), it is hard to infer how it would fare on more typical matrix reconstruction benchmarks. For example, the two cited VBMF references—Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen (2007)—evaluate on the Netflix dataset against earlier methods. Ideally, this paper would have used the same dataset. While BP often outperforms Variational methods, introducing a new evaluation criterion leaves uncertainty about the method’s real competitiveness. If Netflix or a similar dataset is unsuitable, the paper should explicitly explain why.

The algorithm largely reads as a direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed via Variational Message Passing. It is new, but not particularly disruptive. The most compelling aspect is the approximations in the limits N→∞ and m→∞ for an m×N matrix. However, these approximations (described only in the supplement) are not directly validated, and their presentation could be clearer.

There are significant issues with positioning relative to prior work. On first pass, the paper seemed to present the use of matrix factorization for clustering as an original contribution; the abstract and Section 2.2 suggest as much. Other reviews indicate this connection is well-established. The paper should make this explicit. If the idea is sufficiently well-known to lack a canonical citation, state that; otherwise, provide appropriate references.

Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, since that is the common name for BP-style methods on continuous distributions, and the relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm should be clarified. Relatedly, the main method is most naturally framed as BP, yet this is not stated until Section 4.1; it should be mentioned in the abstract.

Pros
The paper is engaging and introduces a new algorithm that could be useful. The mathematical development is followable and seems replicable, even if not re-derived by hand here. Although a new evaluation metric invites skepticism, K-means may be general enough to enable a fair comparison, and the experiments show an advantage for the proposed method.

Clarity
The early exposition is relatively clear. The introduction and the prior work summary are well-written. However, relegating the derivation to the Supplementary Material is a weakness; an outline should appear in the main text. The experiments are presented clearly, and although some plots do not reproduce well in grayscale, they remain legible.

I found the algorithmic presentation difficult to parse, and I could not verify derivational correctness. The mathematics is the weakest component. Even superficially, some expressions are hard to read. For example, why do factors of 1/(m τ_w) precede every term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e? These could be factored out for readability. Also, the last two terms in 9b and in 9e, respectively, could be combined.

The notation feels overloaded with hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. There is a τ_w but no τ—why not just use τ? The critical Section 4 has no p, q, or r, only their hat versions; consider stating at the start of the section, “We drop hats for brevity.” The superscript t for iteration indices seems unnecessary: if the left-hand side uses “t+1,” simply replace “=” with “←” and omit all t’s. It is hard to imagine the derivation was originally conducted with such heavy notation; consider reverting to an earlier, simpler scheme.

The exposition can be tightened. For instance, explain that equation 5 is the negative log of equation 2, or that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power β. Algorithm 2 appears nearly row/column symmetric—point this out, and consider halving its length by saying “duplicate with u and v swapped.”

Define the functions in equation 10 near where they appear. It is clearer to say “f_β is the mean of q_β” than to present an equation without interpretation; if you include the equation, say what it means. At the end of Section 4.2 you note “G_β(b, Λ; p) is a scaled covariance of q_β(u; b, Λ, p),” but this is not evident initially and is not mentioned at G’s definition.

Messages carry no β subscripts, despite depending on f and G, which do. Since f and G do not depend on β directly but via q_β, it is unclear why β subscripts propagate only to f and G. I suggest removing β subscripts entirely, even from q. Treat β as a global parameter and, prior to (15) and (16), simply note “in the limit β→∞, f and G take the following forms.”

On a deeper point, please clarify the role of the factor m in the additive noise variance. Does it matter in practical matrix factorization applications? Does it affect the approximation used in the derivation? It also appears to be the sole source of row/column asymmetry—if so, say so. These seem known to the authors but are not discussed.

Other questions:
- Is a tilde a standard notation for column vectors? This was not immediately clear.
- Why is N uppercase and m lowercase?
- In Section 5, please add a citation after VBMF-MA to identify the primary reference guiding that implementation. Two VBMF citations appear earlier; it is unclear which one (or both) is used.

Supplementary Material:
- For equation 5, writing the two z-related terms as a product of exponentials would make it clearer that one is a pdf.
- In Section 2, the transition from equation 14 to 15 involving O(m) terms was a sticking point; more detail would help.
- At the top of page 2: “A technique used in … is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without consulting those references, it is hard to see how your approximation matches prior art. Please clarify what is new in your derivation—both here and in the main paper.

Language and style:
The English is strong overall, but occasional article usage is off and can distract. The title might be better as “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” Additional specific suggestions:
p. 1
- “Since properties” -> “Since the properties”
- “according to problems” -> “according to the problem”
- “has flexibility” -> “has enough flexibility”
- “motivate use of” -> “motivate the use of”
p. 2
- “We present results” -> “We present the results”
p. 3
- “We regard that” -> “We see that”
- “maximum accuracy clustering” could be italicized
- “Previous works” -> “Previous work”
- “ICM algorithm; It” -> “ICM algorithm: it”
p. 4
- “particularized” -> “specialized”
p. 5
- “plausible properties” -> “discernable properties”?
p. 6
- “stuck to bad local minima” -> “stuck in bad local minima”?
p. 7
- “This is contrastive to that” -> “This is in contrast to the fact that”
Supplementary Material:
p. 2
- “of these message” -> “of these messages”
- “a few number” -> “a few”

I found the diagram in Supplementary Figure 1 very helpful; adding more diagrams would further improve clarity.

In sum, the mathematics is promising and the algorithm merits publication somewhere—if this is indeed the first application of belief propagation to matrix factorization, it fills an important niche. However, the paper requires substantial revision. As it stands, the issues with clarity and scholarship impose too much burden on readers, which would reflect poorly on a conference that accepts it in its current form.
