This paper introduces an algorithm for the reconstruction of matrices from noisy data. The method focuses on the specific setting of low-rank matrices with additional assumptions on their constituent factors and employs an Approximate Message Passing framework to accelerate traditionally computationally intensive Bayesian methods. The authors also draw a connection between matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, which serves as a compelling application domain for their proposed algorithm.

To my knowledge, the use of an Approximate Message Passing approach for matrix reconstruction is a novel and interesting contribution. While the relationship between low-rank matrix factorization and K-means is well-established (for instance, PCA's role as a factor-two approximation for K-means), the authors leverage this link effectively to design a strong experimental evaluation. Their algorithm is benchmarked against k-means and k-means++, and the empirical results suggest it surpasses these classical methods in both speed and efficiency. A commendable feature of the evaluation is its comprehensive nature, assessing performance based on both the Frobenius norm residual and the quality of the final clusters.

The primary limitation of the work is the absence of strong theoretical guarantees regarding the algorithm's convergence. This is not entirely unexpected, as many algorithms for K-means share this characteristic of having limited formal properties. To strengthen the paper, the authors could cite additional literature from Theoretical Computer Science on provably accurate algorithms for the K-means objective function. In summary, this is a solid paper presenting an Approximate Message Passing algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and k-means. It features a promising experimental evaluation, though the theoretical results are somewhat underdeveloped.
