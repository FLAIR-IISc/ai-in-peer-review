The authors propose an algorithm for reconstructing low-rank matrices from noisy observations. Specifically, they focus on a setting with additional assumptions on the matrix factors and employ an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) approach to accelerate the classical, and often computationally expensive, Bayesian method. The paper further draws a connection between matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, providing a compelling application domain for the proposed algorithm.

To the best of my knowledge, the application of Approximate Message Passing to this matrix reconstruction problem is novel and constitutes a significant contribution. While the connection between low-rank matrix factorization and K-means is well-established (e.g., the use of PCA to obtain a factor-two approximation for K-means), this connection provides a strong basis for the paper's experimental evaluation. The authors effectively leverage it to compare their method against standard baselines like k-means and k-means++. The empirical results are promising, suggesting that the proposed approach is not only faster but also more efficient than traditional k-means and k-means++. A particular strength of the evaluation is the use of multiple metrics, comparing performance based on both the Frobenius norm residual and the quality of the resulting clusterings.

The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm, particularly regarding its convergence. This limitation is, however, common in the K-means literature, where many algorithms similarly lack strong convergence guarantees. To better position their work, the authors should consider citing and discussing relevant literature from Theoretical Computer Science that offers algorithms with provable performance guarantees for the K-means objective.

In summary, this is a solid paper that introduces a novel Approximate Message Passing algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with a compelling application to k-means. The work is supported by a promising experimental evaluation, though it is somewhat limited by a lack of theoretical results.
