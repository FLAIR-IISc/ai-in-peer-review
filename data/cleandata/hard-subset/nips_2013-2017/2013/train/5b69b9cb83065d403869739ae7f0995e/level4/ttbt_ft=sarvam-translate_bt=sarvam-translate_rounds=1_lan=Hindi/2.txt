Review of "Low-Rank Matrix Reconstruction and Clustering"

This paper contributes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the application of confidence propagation (BP) message-passing. The algorithm described in the "Supplementary Material" involves two simplification approximations, assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel way against Lloyd's K-means algorithm, clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem. It is also compared to Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be a previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

**Disadvantages**

There are some arguments against accepting this paper. Because a new algorithm is being evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), it is not easy to predict how the algorithm will perform on more traditional matrix reconstruction problems. For example, two references are cited for VBMF, which are Lynn and Teh (2007); and Raico, Illin and Karhunen (2007). Both of these papers use the Netflix dataset to evaluate their algorithms against their predecessors. It would be ideal if the current paper used the same dataset. Although BP is generally more accurate than Variational, evaluating the current BP version using a new criterion raises doubts about its actual competitiveness. Even if Netflix or similar datasets cannot be used, the authors should state why in the paper.

The algorithm itself seems to be a more or less direct application of BP to a problem that was previously addressed with Variational message passing. Although new, it is not entirely unprecedented. The most interesting part for me is the approximations presented in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ are the dimensions of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, which are only described in the Supplementary Material, is never directly tested, and I think they could be explained a little more clearly.

**Some Serious Problems Regarding Citations of Previous Work**

There are some serious problems regarding the citation of previous work. When I first read the paper, I thought it was presenting a fundamental contribution to the application of matrix factorization in clustering. The summary and the text of Section 2.2 give this impression. I felt betrayed when I learned from other reviewers that the connection is well known. I see no good reason why the paper would not make it clear to the reader. If it is too well known to cite a particular reference, one should simply say that it is well known. Otherwise, cite the previous work.

Also, EP should be cited, as it is commonly called the application of BP to continuous squares of distribution by people, and the paper's relationship to EP should be explained. Relatively, the main algorithm is most clearly understood as an application of confidence propagation, but this fact is not mentioned until Section 4.1. It should be mentioned in the summary.

**Pros**

The paper was interesting to read, and presents a new and potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics of the paper was possible to follow, and although I did not repeat it by hand, I had the impression that it would be possible to do so. Although I think it is reasonable to doubt the new evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may be sufficiently general to make a proper comparison with the algorithm, and certainly shows an advantage for the new algorithm in experiments.

**Clarity**

I found the initial presentation quite easy to follow. The introduction was clear, as was the summary of previous work. The fact that the derivation of the algorithm appears only in the Supplementary Material is a drawback. I wish the derivation could have been outlined in the main paper. The experiment section was clear, and although some plots appear poorly in black and white, they were still readable.

I had difficulty understanding the details of the algorithm, and I could not verify the correctness of the derivation. Mathematics was the weakest part of this presentation. Even at a very superficial level, it was difficult to parse. For example, I do not understand why the factors 1 / m \tau_w appear before each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d and 9e. These can be removed from the factors, so that the expressions are easier to read. Also, the last two terms in 9b and 9e can be added respectively.

It seems there is an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts and superscripts. For example, there is a \tauw but no \tau, why not just replace \tauw with \tau? Also, there is no p, q or r in the most important Section 4, but only \hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r} - why not give readers a break and say at the beginning of the section "We will drop the hats here for brevity"? And the 't' superscript in the messages seems completely unnecessary. When the left hand side is "t + 1" you just need to change "=" to "\gets" and then you can drop all the t's. I find it hard to imagine that the algorithm was originally achieved by the authors using such cumbersome notation. I would suggest you go back to the original notation and see if it is simpler.

**Improvements can be made to the presentation:** Why not explain that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2? Or that equation 8 is simply the power \beta of equation 2? The algorithm 2 seems to be almost row-column symmetric, why not indicate this? And even cut it in half, saying, "Then copy and paste these equations, switch U and V"?

**10: Meaning of the functions should be explained near their definition.** It would be clearer to say "f\beta is the mean of q\beta", rather than giving a single equation; but if you decide to give a single equation, why not explain what it means? At the end of Section 4.2, it says that "g\beta(b, \lambda; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q\beta(u; b, \lambda, p)", but this was not clear at first, and it was not mentioned in the definition of g either.

**No Subscripts in Any Messages:** There are no \beta subscripts in any messages, even if they depend on f and G in which \beta subscripts are. But f and G do not depend directly on \beta, only on q_\beta. So it is not clear why these subscripts are promoted only up to f and G and not further. I would suggest they be completely eliminated, even from q. The parameter \beta is simply a global variable, which is perfectly fine. Before (15) and (16), it would be necessary to say that "in the limit \beta \to \infty, f and G take the following form:".

**In-Depth:** There were other things about the algorithm that I would like to understand better. What is the importance of the m factor in additive noise variance? Does it play a role in the applications of matrix factorization? Does it play a role in the approximations used to derive the algorithm? It seems that this is the only thing that is failing to make the algorithm row-column symmetric, is that true? I think the authors know the answers to these questions, but do not comment on them.

**Other Questions:**

Is it standard to use tildes to represent column vectors? I did not understand this at first.

Just curious - why is N written in capital letters, but M is small?

In Section 5, I would like a citation after VBMF-MA to point out the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competitive algorithm. The first paper appears to have two references for variance matrix factorization, and it is not clear which one is intended (or if it is both).

**In the Supplementary Material:** For equation 5, I think it would be clearer to write a product of exponents for two terms that include z, so that it becomes clearer that there is a pdf. For Section 2, I had a problem with the large-O in the step from equation 14 to 15, which had m terms. I am not sure I just need to think more, but that's where I got stuck.

Also in the Supplementary Material, it is stated at the top of page 2 that "... a technique used in these messages is to give an approximate representation of these messages in terms of some number of real-valued parameters". Without reading these references, I am not sure how the approximation below the text given below is related to the previously published text. It would be good to clarify the novelty of the derivation of the algorithm in the text, and even in the main paper.

English is very good, and the meaning always comes out, but there are some places where it seems as if it was written by someone who is not entirely skilled in the use of articles. This may be disturbing to some readers. Even in the title - I think it should be something like "Low-Rank Matrix Reconstruction and Clustering using an Approximate Message Passing Algorithm". For other places, I would make a list of suggestions that could be useful to the authors:

There are also some language problems in the Supplementary Material, for example:

Page 2

I liked the diagram in Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material, which was very helpful to me. If it is possible to create more diagrams, it will be added to the paper.

The mathematics seems interesting, and the algorithm should be published somewhere - as the first attempt to implement confidence propagation in matrix factorization, it plays a certain important role. But more work needs to be done before the paper is accepted. There is a significant lack of clarity and scholarship in the paper that puts an unacceptable burden on the readers, which will be poorly reflected on a conference that accepted it in its current state.
