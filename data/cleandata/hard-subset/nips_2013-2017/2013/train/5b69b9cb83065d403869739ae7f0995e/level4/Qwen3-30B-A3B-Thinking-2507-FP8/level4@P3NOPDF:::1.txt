

The paper introduces a novel Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction under noisy observations, leveraging structured factor assumptions to accelerate the computationally intensive Bayesian approach. A key contribution is the theoretical and empirical linkage between low-rank matrix factorization and K-means clustering—a connection previously established in approximation algorithms (e.g., PCA yielding a factor-2 approximation for K-means). The authors exploit this link to frame their AMP-based method as a scalable alternative to classical K-means and k-means++, demonstrating significant computational advantages in empirical evaluations. Their experimental design is particularly strong: they assess both reconstruction accuracy via Frobenius norm error and clustering quality through assignment metrics, providing a comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art baselines.

The AMP approach for low-rank matrix reconstruction represents a meaningful technical contribution, as it addresses a gap in efficient Bayesian inference for structured matrices. The K-means application is well-motivated and effectively showcases the algorithm’s practical utility, with results indicating faster convergence and superior scalability over k-means and k-means++. This empirical rigor is a major strength, especially given the increasing demand for scalable clustering solutions in high-dimensional settings.

However, the theoretical foundation requires strengthening. While the authors acknowledge the absence of strong convergence guarantees for their AMP-based method, this limitation is not adequately contextualized. Many existing K-means algorithms (e.g., Lloyd’s method) similarly lack rigorous convergence proofs under general conditions. To bolster the theoretical narrative, the authors should explicitly cite recent advances in *theoretical computer science* that provide provably accurate K-means algorithms (e.g., work by Kumar et al. on (1+ε)-approximation guarantees, or recent polynomial-time algorithms for constant approximation factors). Such citations would clarify the trade-offs between their efficient AMP approach and theoretically grounded alternatives, positioning their work more precisely within the literature. Without these references, the claim that "theoretical guarantees are weak" risks appearing dismissive of the broader field’s progress.

The paper’s clarity and structure are generally sound, though minor improvements would enhance readability. For instance, the description of the factor assumptions could be more precise—specifying whether the low-rank structure imposes constraints on the factor matrices (e.g., orthogonality, sparsity) would strengthen the problem formulation. Additionally, the experimental section would benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity to contextualize the reported speedups. While the empirical results are compelling, the absence of runtime comparisons in terms of absolute time (beyond relative speed) slightly limits the practical impact.

Overall, this is a promising contribution with clear practical value. The AMP framework offers a compelling acceleration path for matrix reconstruction and K-means, validated through thorough experiments. The theoretical gap is notable but not fatal for a NIPS submission, especially given the paper’s strong empirical foundation and the well-documented challenges in proving convergence for non-convex optimization problems like K-means. With minor revisions to address the theoretical context and clarify the factor assumptions, the paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**  
*Strengths*: Novel AMP-based approach for low-rank reconstruction; insightful K-means connection; rigorous experimental evaluation with dual metrics (reconstruction error and clustering quality).  
*Weaknesses*: Limited theoretical guarantees; insufficient citation of provable K-means algorithms in TCS literature.  
*Actionable Suggestion*: Explicitly cite recent TCS work on provably accurate K-means solvers (e.g., Kumar et al., 2004; Awasthi et al., 2015) to contextualize the theoretical trade-offs.
