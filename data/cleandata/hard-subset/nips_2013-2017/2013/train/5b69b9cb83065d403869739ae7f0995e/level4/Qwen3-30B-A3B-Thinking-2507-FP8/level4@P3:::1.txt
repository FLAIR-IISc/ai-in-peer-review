

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to K-means clustering. The authors formulate the problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing for flexible incorporation of structural properties (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity) of matrix factors beyond simple low-rankness. They derive an AMP-based algorithm that efficiently approximates Bayesian inference for matrix reconstruction, extending prior work on AMP for sparse vector recovery to the matrix factorization setting. The key innovation lies in their unified treatment of both marginalization and MAP estimation through a single algorithmic framework parameterized by β, which generalizes previous AMP approaches for rank-one matrices [8, 9]. The authors further demonstrate how their method can be applied to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with additional structural constraints, addressing a limitation of prior approaches that did not properly handle the requirement that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster.

The paper builds upon recent NIPS work on AMP algorithms, particularly [6, 7, 8, 9], while making significant contributions to the matrix factorization domain. The authors correctly position their work relative to related literature, noting that while [10, 11] have previously connected low-rank matrix factorization with clustering, their approach is the first to directly handle cluster assignment constraints within the low-rank framework. This connection is well-motivated and provides a natural application for their algorithm.

Regarding quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach with clear theoretical foundations. The AMP algorithm is derived from well-established principles, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of its properties, including computational efficiency (O(mN) per iteration) and connections to variational Bayes methods. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, variational Bayes matrix factorization) on both synthetic and real datasets (ORL Face Database). The results demonstrate consistent improvements in clustering accuracy and convergence speed, with the AMP algorithm for K-means achieving lower K-means loss than competitors for r ≥ 5. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the algorithm's behavior, including its relationship to the ICM algorithm and the convergence properties of its fixed points (Proposition 1).

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-structured sections and clear algorithm descriptions. The derivation of the AMP algorithm is presented logically, and the connection between matrix reconstruction and clustering is well-explained. The authors use a consistent notation throughout, and the supplementary material appears to provide sufficient technical details. The only minor issue is that the description of the algorithm's initialization could be slightly clearer.

The originality of the work is strong. While AMP algorithms have been applied to related problems in compressed sensing [6, 7] and rank-one matrix reconstruction [8, 9], this paper makes the first systematic application to general low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible structural priors. The connection to K-means clustering is novel in that it properly handles the assignment constraint, distinguishing it from previous approaches [10, 11]. The authors have carefully positioned their work against recent NIPS literature and demonstrate clear differences from related work, particularly in the treatment of the rank parameter and the relaxation of independence assumptions between matrix factors.

The significance of the work is substantial. The proposed algorithm provides a new, efficient approach to a fundamental problem with broad applications in machine learning and data analysis. The experimental results on both synthetic and real data demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods, particularly in high-dimensional settings where K-means typically struggles. The authors have identified a meaningful application domain (clustering) where their method outperforms established baselines, and they provide a clear explanation for why their approach works better (accounting for cluster size and previous assignments in the assignment step).

The main weakness of the paper is the limited theoretical analysis of convergence properties for the general case. While Proposition 1 provides some guarantees for the MAP problem, the paper does not establish global convergence or provide bounds on the number of iterations required for the general AMP algorithm. This is understandable given the complexity of the problem, but the authors could strengthen their theoretical foundation by citing relevant work on convergence of message passing algorithms for non-convex problems [18] or discussing how their algorithm relates to the state evolution framework for AMP [6, 18]. The paper acknowledges this limitation but could better contextualize it within the broader literature on clustering algorithms, which often have similarly weak theoretical guarantees.

The paper also has a minor issue in its presentation of the K-means connection: the authors claim their approach "directly deals with the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster," but the mathematical formulation (Equation 6) does not explicitly enforce this constraint in the optimization. The constraint is handled implicitly through the discrete prior on V, but this could be made clearer.

Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field. The AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction is technically sound, the application to clustering is well-motivated and well-executed, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its advantages. The paper meets all four evaluation criteria well, with particular strengths in originality and significance. The minor theoretical limitations and presentation issues do not detract substantially from the paper's overall contribution.

Pro: Novel AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible structural priors; Clear connection to K-means clustering with proper handling of assignment constraints; Comprehensive experimental evaluation showing consistent improvements over baselines; Strong theoretical foundation with clear algorithmic derivation.

Con: Limited theoretical analysis of convergence properties beyond Proposition 1; Minor clarity issue in presentation of K-means constraint; Some minor formatting inconsistencies in the mathematical notation.

This paper represents a significant contribution to the field of matrix factorization and clustering algorithms. It offers a practical, efficient, and theoretically grounded approach that outperforms existing methods in both synthetic and real-world experiments. The work is well-suited for NIPS and would be valuable to the community. I recommend acceptance.
