

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"  

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. As detailed in the Supplementary Material, the algorithm employs two simplifying approximations derived under the assumptions of large row and column counts in the input matrix. The work evaluates the algorithm in a novel manner by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing it against Lloyd’s K-means algorithm. It also contrasts with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the only prior message-passing reconstruction algorithm.  

**Weaknesses**  
The evaluation on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization) complicates generalization to conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. For instance, both VBMF references (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen, 2007) use the Netflix dataset for evaluation—this standard should be adopted here. Without it, the novelty of the evaluation criterion undermines claims of competitiveness, even if BP typically outperforms Variational methods. The authors must justify the omission of a standard dataset.  

The algorithm itself is a straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed via Variational Message Passing. While novel, it lacks groundbreaking impact. The most compelling aspect—the asymptotic approximations for $N \to \infty$ and $m \to \infty$ ($m \times N$ input matrix dimensions)—is described only in the Supplementary Material without direct validation or clearer explanation.  

Critical citation issues undermine scholarly rigor. The abstract and Section 2.2 misleadingly present the matrix factorization–clustering connection as original, despite it being well-established (as noted by other reviewers). The paper must explicitly acknowledge this prior work or state its ubiquity. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the standard term for BP applied to continuous distributions, and the relationship to the proposed algorithm must be clarified. The core algorithm is introduced as a BP application only in Section 4.1, but this should be stated in the abstract.  

**Strengths**  
The paper presents an interesting, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally followable, and the K-means clustering experiments demonstrate a clear benefit for the proposed method, suggesting the evaluation criterion is sufficiently general.  

**Clarity and Presentation**  
The early sections (introduction, prior work summary) are clear. However, the algorithm derivation’s exclusion from the main paper (confined to Supplementary Material) is a significant drawback. The experiments section is readable, though some figures lack clarity in black-and-white.  

The mathematical exposition is the paper’s weakest element. Key issues include:  
- Unexplained factors (e.g., $1 / m \tau_w$ in Equations 9a, 9b, 9d, 9e) that could be factored out.  
- Redundant notation: excess hats ($\hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r}$), tildes, and subscripts (e.g., $\tau_w$ vs. $\tau$; unnecessary $t$ superscripts in messages). The authors should simplify notation (e.g., drop hats in Section 4 for brevity, use $\gets$ instead of $t$-indexed equations).  
- Unclear relationships (e.g., Equation 5 as the negative log of Equation 2, Equation 8 as Equation 2 raised to $\beta$; row-column symmetry in Algorithm 2).  
- Ambiguous definitions (e.g., functions in Equation 10 lack explanatory context; $G_\beta$’s description in Section 4.2 is unclear).  
- Unjustified $\beta$ subscripts (e.g., in messages), which propagate inconsistently from $f_\beta$ and $G_\beta$. The parameter $\beta$ should be treated as a global variable without subscripts.  

Deeper conceptual gaps persist:  
- The role of the $m$ factor in additive noise variance is unexplained. Does it affect symmetry (breaking row-column symmetry?) or practical applications?  
- Standard notation questions: Why use $\tilde{}$ for column vectors? Why capitalize $N$ but not $m$?  
- Section 5 requires a citation for VBMF-MA’s implementation reference (the two existing citations for variational matrix factorization are ambiguous).  

**Language and Minor Issues**  
The English is generally strong, but minor grammatical issues distract:  
- p. 1: "Since properties" → "Since the properties"; "according to problems" → "according to the problem"; "has flexibility" → "has sufficient flexibility"; "motivate use of" → "motivate the use of."  
- p. 2: "We present results" → "We present the results."  
- p. 3: "We regard that" → "We see that"; "maximum accuracy clustering" should be italicized; "Previous works" → "Previous work"; "ICM algorithm; It" → "ICM algorithm: it."  
- p. 4: "particularized" → "specialized."  
- p. 5: "plausible properties" → "discernible properties."  
- p. 6: "stuck to bad local minima" → "stuck in bad local minima."  
- p. 7: "This is contrastive to that" → "This is in contrast to the fact that."  
- Supplementary Material: "a few number" → "a few"; "of these message" → "of these messages."  

The Supplementary Material’s Figure 1 is helpful; additional diagrams would further improve clarity.  

**Conclusion**  
The mathematical approach is intriguing, and the paper fills a niche as the first BP application to matrix factorization. However, the work suffers from insufficient clarity, unresolved notation, citation gaps, and inadequate experimental justification. The current presentation imposes an unacceptable burden on readers and would reflect poorly on NIPS. The paper requires significant revisions to address the above points before acceptance.
