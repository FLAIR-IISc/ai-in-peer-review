This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations, based on the Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The authors formulate the problem within a Bayesian setting, which provides the flexibility to incorporate various structural priors on the matrix factors, such as sparsity or non-negativity. The derived AMP algorithm offers a unified approach to solve both the marginalization problem (for posterior mean estimation) and the Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) problem. As a key application, the paper demonstrates how K-means clustering can be reformulated as a specific instance of this low-rank matrix reconstruction problem. The authors then apply their AMP algorithm to this clustering task, resulting in a new algorithm that is empirically compared against standard methods.

The paper's main strengths lie in its originality, the quality of its experimental evaluation, and its potential significance.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Originality and Quality:** The core contribution is the development of a flexible AMP algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. While building on prior work in AMP for compressed sensing and rank-one matrix recovery, this work extends the framework to the general-rank case in a way that handles a broad class of priors and addresses both marginalization and MAP estimation within a single formalism (via the inverse temperature parameter β). The connection drawn between the proposed algorithm and Variational Bayes (VB) or Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) is insightful, clearly highlighting the additional "memory" terms that distinguish the AMP approach. Proposition 1, which characterizes the algorithm's fixed points, provides a useful piece of theoretical analysis, suggesting that the AMP algorithm may be less prone to getting stuck in poor local minima compared to ICM.

2.  **Significance and Experimental Validation:** The application to K-means clustering is a significant contribution. The resulting AMP-KM algorithm is not a simple re-packaging of Lloyd's algorithm; as shown in equation (18b), it includes novel correction terms that account for a cluster's current size and prevent a data point from being overly influenced by its own contribution to a cluster center. The empirical results are compelling. On both synthetic and real-world data (the ORL face database), the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) consistently outperform established baselines like Lloyd's K-means and K-means++. They achieve both lower K-means loss and higher clustering accuracy, demonstrating a tangible improvement over the state of the art in practice. The fast convergence reported for the AMP-MA algorithm is also a notable practical advantage.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem is well-defined, and the derivation of the algorithm, while relegated to supplementary material, is presented in a clear and understandable final form. The connection between the general matrix reconstruction problem and the specific case of clustering is explained effectively.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of theoretical guarantees for the convergence of the proposed algorithm. The authors assume convergence in their analysis of the algorithm's fixed points, but no proof of convergence is provided. This is a common issue for such iterative methods, but it remains a limitation. The authors themselves observe oscillatory behavior in experiments, requiring a modified stopping criterion, which underscores the absence of guaranteed convergence.

2.  **Context within Clustering Literature:** While the connection to low-rank factorization is well-motivated, the paper could be strengthened by positioning its K-means algorithm within the broader context of clustering literature, particularly work from the theoretical computer science community on provably accurate algorithms for the K-means objective (e.g., those offering approximation guarantees). While the proposed algorithm is heuristic, discussing this related body of work would provide a more complete picture for the reader.

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and well-motivated AMP-based algorithm for a fundamental problem. The application to clustering is clever and leads to a new algorithm that demonstrably outperforms widely-used standard methods in rigorous experiments. The work is technically sound and presents a significant and practical contribution.

*   **Con-acceptance:** The theoretical analysis is somewhat limited, lacking guarantees on the algorithm's convergence.

Overall, this is a solid paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The strengths—a novel and flexible algorithm backed by strong empirical evidence of its superiority over standard techniques—clearly outweigh the weakness of limited theoretical guarantees. The proposed method is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on matrix factorization, clustering, and related signal processing problems.
