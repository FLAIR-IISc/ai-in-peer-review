This paper introduces a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, derived by applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the problem. The algorithm, detailed in the supplementary material, employs two simplifying approximations based on the assumption of a large number of rows and columns. A key aspect of the paper is its novel evaluation methodology, which frames clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem to compare the proposed algorithm against Lloyd's K-means. The algorithm is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), another message-passing approach.

While the paper presents an interesting new algorithm, it has several significant weaknesses in its current form that prevent a recommendation for acceptance. The primary concerns relate to the evaluation methodology, positioning of prior work, and clarity of the technical presentation.

### Major Weaknesses

1.  **Unconventional and Insufficiently Justified Evaluation:** The decision to evaluate a new matrix reconstruction algorithm on a non-standard task (clustering formulated as matrix reconstruction) makes it very difficult to assess its performance in a broader context. Leading methods in this area, including the VBMF algorithm cited (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007), are typically evaluated on benchmark datasets like the Netflix dataset. This allows for direct comparison with a large body of existing work. By using a novel evaluation criterion, the paper creates ambiguity about the algorithm's competitiveness on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. While BP-based methods are often more accurate than their variational counterparts, this cannot be confirmed without a more standard comparison. The authors should either evaluate their method on a standard dataset or provide a compelling justification for why the clustering task is a sufficient and informative benchmark for general matrix factorization.

2.  **Insufficient Citation and Positioning of Prior Work:** The paper fails to properly contextualize its contributions, which is a serious scholarly issue. The abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that applying matrix factorization to clustering is a novel contribution of this work. However, this connection is well-established in the literature. This omission misleads the reader about the paper's novelty. If the connection is considered common knowledge, it should be stated as such; otherwise, relevant prior work must be cited. Furthermore, the paper omits any mention of Expectation Propagation (EP), which is the standard term for applying BP to models with continuous variables. The relationship between the proposed algorithm and EP should be explicitly discussed. Finally, the fact that the algorithm is an application of Belief Propagation is a central detail that should be mentioned in the abstract, not delayed until Section 4.1.

3.  **Limited Technical Novelty and Unverified Approximations:** The core algorithm appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with variational methods. While this is a valid contribution, it is not particularly groundbreaking. The most interesting technical aspect is the introduction of approximations in the large-system limit (N, m → ∞). However, these approximations are only described in the supplementary material, their validity is never directly tested, and their explanation could be significantly improved. For a key contribution, this part of the work is underdeveloped and under-scrutinized.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Algorithm:** The paper successfully presents a new and potentially useful algorithm. As one of the first efforts to apply BP to this class of matrix factorization problems, it represents a valuable contribution to the field.

2.  **Promising Experimental Results:** Within the chosen experimental framework, the algorithm demonstrates a clear benefit, outperforming K-means and VBMF on the clustering task. This suggests that the approach has merit and warrants further investigation.

3.  **Readable Foundation:** The introduction and motivation are well-written, and the core mathematical framework seems followable in principle, even if the detailed derivation has clarity issues.

### Detailed Comments for Improvement

**Clarity and Presentation:**
*   The derivation of the algorithm is a critical part of the paper and should be at least outlined in the main text, rather than being relegated entirely to the supplementary material.
*   The mathematical exposition needs significant improvement. The description of the algorithm in Section 4 is difficult to parse.
*   The connections between equations should be made more explicit. For example, explain that Equation 5 is the negative logarithm of Equation 2, and that Equation 8 is Equation 2 raised to the power of β.
*   Point out important properties of the algorithm, such as the near row-column symmetry in Algorithm 2. This could even be used to shorten its description.

**Mathematical Notation:**
The notation is overly cumbersome and hinders readability. Consider the following simplifications:
*   In Equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e, the common factor of `1/mτ_w` could be factored out. The last two terms in 9b and 9e could also be combined.
*   The profusion of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts is confusing. For instance, if there is no `τ`, why use `τ_w`? Why not just `τ`? In Section 4, if `p`, `q`, and `r` are never used, simply use them instead of `p̂`, `q̂`, `r̂` and state that the hats are being dropped for brevity.
*   The time superscripts (`t`, `t+1`) in the message updates seem unnecessary. Replacing "=" with an assignment operator "←" would make the iterative nature clear and allow for the removal of these superscripts.

**Explanation of Technical Details:**
*   The meaning of functions should be explained in prose alongside their definitions. For example, state that `f_β` is the mean of `q_β`. The explanation for `G_β` as a scaled covariance matrix comes too late.
*   The role and significance of certain modeling choices are unclear. For instance, what is the importance of the factor `m` in the additive noise variance? Does it play a role in the approximations? It seems to be the only element breaking the row-column symmetry of the algorithm, which is an interesting point worth discussing.

**Specific Questions and Suggestions:**
*   **Citations:** Please provide a specific citation for the VBMF-MA implementation used for comparison in Section 5.
*   **Supplementary Material:** The step from Equation 14 to 15 concerning the O(m) terms was difficult to follow and requires more explanation. Additionally, please clarify the novelty of the message approximation technique in relation to the cited prior work.
*   **Language:** While the English is generally good, there are numerous instances of missing articles ("the," "a") that can be distracting. A thorough proofreading is recommended. For example: "Since properties" → "Since the properties"; "motivate use of" → "motivate the use of."

### Conclusion

The core idea of applying belief propagation to matrix factorization is interesting, and the resulting algorithm shows promise. However, the paper is not yet ready for publication. The significant issues regarding the positioning of prior work, the non-standard evaluation, and the overall lack of clarity in the technical presentation place an unacceptable burden on the reader. The paper requires substantial revision to address these points before it can be considered for acceptance.
