Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, described in the Supplementary Material, incorporates two simplifying approximations, assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

There are several arguments against accepting the paper in its current form. The experimental results are difficult to interpret, as the algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization). It is challenging to predict how the algorithm would perform on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, previous papers on VBMF, such as Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen (2007), use the Netflix dataset to evaluate their algorithm against predecessors. It would be ideal if the present paper had used the same dataset or explained why this was not possible.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. Although new, it is not groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect of the algorithm is the approximations introduced in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ is the dimension of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, described only in the supplementary material, is not directly tested, and their explanation could be clearer.

There are serious issues regarding the citation of prior work. The paper implies that the application of matrix factorization to clustering is an original contribution, which is not the case. The connection is well-known, and the paper should make this clear to the reader. If it is too well-known to cite any particular reference, then the paper should state that it is well-known. Otherwise, relevant prior work should be cited. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is commonly referred to as the application of BP to continuous classes of distributions, and its relationship to the paper's algorithm should be explained.

The paper has some positive aspects. It presents a new and potentially useful algorithm, and the mathematics is possible to follow. Although the evaluation criteria may be unconventional, the K-means problem may provide a fair comparison of the algorithms, and the experiments show a benefit for the new algorithm.

However, the paper's clarity is a significant issue. The early presentation is easy to follow, but the derivation of the algorithm, which only appears in the Supplemental Material, is a drawback. The experiments section is clear, but the description of the algorithm is difficult to understand, and the mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation. The notation is cumbersome, with an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. Simplifying the notation and providing clearer explanations would improve the paper's clarity.

There are also some deeper issues with the algorithm that require further explanation. The significance of the $m$ factor in the additive noise variance is unclear, and its role in applications of matrix factorization and the approximation used to derive the algorithm should be discussed.

Other minor issues include the use of a tilde to denote column vectors, which may not be standard, and the inconsistent capitalization of $N$ and $m$. A citation is needed after VBMF-MA to indicate the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competing algorithm.

In the Supplementary Material, equation 5 could be rewritten to make it clearer that one term is the pdf. The step from equation 14 to 15 regarding big-O of $m$ terms is unclear and requires further explanation. The novelty of the algorithm's derivation should be clarified in the text itself and in the main paper.

The English is good, but there are some places where it reads like it was written by someone not entirely skilled in the use of articles. Some minor suggestions for improvement include:

* "Since properties" -> "Since the properties"
* "according to problems" -> "according to the problem"
* "has flexibility" -> "has enough flexibility"
* "motivate use of" -> "motivate the use of"
* "We present results" -> "We present the results"
* "We regard that" -> "We see that"
* "maximum accuracy clustering" could be italicized
* "Previous works" -> "Previous work"
* "ICM algorithm; It" -> "ICM algorithm: it"
* "particularized" -> "specialized"
* "plausible properties" -> "discernable properties"
* "stuck to bad local minima" -> "stuck in bad local minima"
* "This is contrastive to that" -> "This is in contrast to the fact that"

The Supplementary Material also has some language issues, such as "of these message" -> "of these messages" and "a few number" -> "a few".

In conclusion, while the paper presents a new algorithm, it requires significant revisions to address the issues mentioned above. The paper needs to improve its clarity, scholarship, and notation to reduce the burden on readers and meet the standards of the conference.
