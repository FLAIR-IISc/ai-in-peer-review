This paper addresses the problem of low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations within a Bayesian framework. The authors propose a novel algorithm based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP), derived from belief propagation. A key feature of the proposed framework is its flexibility, allowing for the incorporation of various structural priors on the matrix factors (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity). The paper presents a unified algorithm that can handle both marginal posterior estimation (for computing posterior means) and Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation by introducing an inverse temperature parameter, β. As a primary application, the authors reformulate the K-means clustering problem as a specific instance of low-rank matrix reconstruction and adapt their AMP algorithm accordingly. Numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world data show that the proposed clustering algorithm outperforms standard Lloyd's K-means and the K-means++ algorithm in terms of both clustering accuracy and the final K-means loss.

**Strengths**
*   The application of the AMP framework to K-means clustering is novel and provides an interesting new perspective on the problem. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3) includes terms that intuitively correct for biases in cluster center estimation, which is a compelling feature.
*   The unified treatment of marginalization and MAP estimation through the β parameter is elegant.
*   The experimental results are promising. The proposed AMP-based clustering methods consistently outperform strong baselines (K-means, K-means++) on the presented tasks, suggesting the practical potential of this approach, particularly for high-dimensional data.
*   Proposition 1, which establishes that any fixed point of the AMP algorithm for the MAP problem is also a fixed point of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm, is a nice theoretical result that provides insight into the algorithm's behavior.

**Weaknesses and Questions**

**1. Clarity of Mathematical Derivations**
The primary weakness of this paper lies in the lack of detail in its mathematical derivations, which makes it difficult to fully assess the technical correctness of the work. Many non-trivial steps are omitted, both in the main text and the supplementary material.
*   The asymptotic analysis used to derive the algorithm is presented very tersely. For instance, the derivation seems to rely on an implicit assumption that the matrix dimensions *m* and *N* grow in the same order, which should be stated and justified more formally.
*   The claim that the algorithm for finite β converges to the MAP solution as β → ∞ requires more rigorous justification. It is not generally true that the limit of a minimum is the minimum of the limit (i.e., lim_{β→∞} min KL ≠ min lim_{β→∞} KL), and this interchange of operators needs to be validated in this context.
*   The proof of Proposition 1 is too condensed. Specifically, the derivation of the reformulation presented in Equation (16), which is central to the proof, is not provided.
*   Several other specific steps in the derivations are unclear. For example, in the supplementary material (line 139), the justification for the claim that a particular term in Eq. (11) is O(1/√m) is missing. Similarly (line 167), an approximation is made regarding the expectation of A_{ij}^2, which is not obvious and requires clarification.

**2. Originality in Relation to Expectation Propagation (EP)**
The connection between AMP and Expectation Propagation (EP) is not discussed, which raises questions about the novelty of the proposed algorithm for the general matrix factorization problem.
*   The proposed algorithm focuses on estimating the first and second moments of the marginal posteriors. In this respect, it is very similar in spirit and form to EP. The authors should clarify whether their algorithm is an instance of EP applied to this problem.
*   EP has previously been applied to matrix factorization for collaborative filtering by Stern et al. ("Matchbox: Large Scale Bayesian Recommendations," WWW 2009). The update equations derived in this paper appear to be standard EP updates and bear a strong resemblance to those in prior work. A detailed comparison and discussion of the novelty of the present work in light of these previous results are necessary.
*   As a suggestion, it would also be interesting to relate the proposed clustering method to the "clutter problem" described in Minka's original EP paper ("Expectation Propagation for Approximate Bayesian Inference," UAI 2001).

**3. Scope of Experimental Evaluation**
While the application to clustering is presented as a major contribution, the experimental validation is somewhat narrow.
*   The clustering literature is vast, and many powerful algorithms exist beyond K-means. To convincingly demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed method, the empirical comparison should be expanded to include other modern, state-of-the-art clustering techniques. A comparison limited to K-means and K-means++ is insufficient to establish superiority in the general clustering setting.

**Overall Assessment**
This paper introduces an interesting AMP-based algorithm for low-rank matrix factorization and demonstrates its potential in the context of clustering. The idea of using AMP to escape local minima that affect standard K-means is compelling, and the initial results are promising. However, the paper in its current form suffers from significant weaknesses. The mathematical derivations are too sketchy to be properly verified, the novelty with respect to established methods like Expectation Propagation is unclear, and the experimental evaluation for the clustering application is too limited. I believe the paper is not ready for publication without major revisions to address these critical issues.
