This paper presents a new Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction. Derived from the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm with approximations suited for large matrices, this method extends prior AMP work to the general-rank case and allows for estimations beyond the posterior mean, such as MAP estimation. A key contribution is the application of this algorithm to clustering, which the authors reformulate as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints on one of the factor matrices. The proposed algorithm is evaluated against Lloyd's K-means and a Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF) approach on this clustering task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The paper introduces a novel and potentially useful algorithm. Applying BP to this problem is a valuable contribution, extending the line of work on message-passing for matrix factorization beyond variational methods. The connection to clustering provides an interesting application domain.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The proposed AMP-based clustering algorithms demonstrate superior performance over established methods like K-means, K-means++, and VBMF on both synthetic and real-world datasets, achieving lower K-means loss and higher clustering accuracy.
*   **Soundness:** The mathematical framework appears solid. Although the derivation was not replicated in full, the steps seem plausible and the overall approach is well-grounded in statistical physics and information theory literature.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Evaluation Scope:** By focusing exclusively on clustering, the paper makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance on more conventional low-rank matrix reconstruction tasks. Standard benchmarks like the Netflix dataset, used by cited VBMF papers, are absent. This omission makes it hard to gauge the algorithm's competitiveness in the broader matrix factorization literature. The authors should either include experiments on a standard benchmark or provide a strong justification for why clustering serves as a sufficient proxy.
*   **Positioning and Originality:** The paper could be perceived as an incremental advancement, applying a known technique (BP) to a problem where variational methods were already used. Furthermore, the framing of the contributions is somewhat misleading. The formulation of clustering as matrix factorization is presented as a novel aspect of the work, but this is a well-known connection that should be credited to prior art.
*   **Inadequate Citation:** The paper fails to cite relevant work. Specifically, the term Expectation Propagation (EP) is standard for BP-like algorithms on continuous distributions, and its relationship to the proposed method should be discussed. Additionally, the algorithm's foundation in Belief Propagation is a critical detail that should be stated upfront in the abstract and introduction, rather than being delayed until Section 4.

**Clarity:**

The paper's clarity is a significant concern and requires substantial revision.
*   **Derivation and Algorithm Presentation:** The complete derivation is relegated to supplementary material, leaving the main text without a clear intuition for how the algorithm was developed. The presentation of Algorithm 2 is dense and difficult to parse due to cumbersome notation. Simple typographic changes, like factoring out common terms in Equation 9 and simplifying the excessive use of hats, tildes, and iteration superscripts, would greatly improve readability.
*   **Lack of Exposition:** The text often fails to connect the mathematical formulations with their intuitive meanings. For example, it should be explicitly stated that Equation 5 is the negative logarithm of the posterior in Equation 2, and that Equation 8 raises the posterior to the power of β. The functional roles of `f` and `G` (as the mean and scaled covariance of a distribution) should be explained at their introduction in Equation 10, not much later. Highlighting the algorithm's near row-column symmetry would also aid comprehension.
*   **Unanswered Technical Questions:** The paper leaves key technical questions unanswered. The significance of the factor `m` in the noise variance is never explained. This appears to be a crucial element related to the large-system limit derivation and the breaking of perfect symmetry, and its role should be clarified.
*   **Language and Style:** While the English is generally understandable, there are frequent errors in article usage ("a" vs. "the") and awkward phrasings that detract from the paper's overall quality and can be distracting to the reader.

In summary, the paper introduces an interesting algorithm with strong empirical performance on a specific application. However, it suffers from significant weaknesses in clarity, scholarly positioning, and the scope of its evaluation. The paper would need major revisions to address these issues before it could be considered for acceptance.
