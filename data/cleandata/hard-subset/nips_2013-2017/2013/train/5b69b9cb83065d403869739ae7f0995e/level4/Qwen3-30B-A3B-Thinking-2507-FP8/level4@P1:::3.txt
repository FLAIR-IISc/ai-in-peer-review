

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, with a novel application to clustering problems. The authors demonstrate that clustering can be reformulated as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with additional structural constraints, and they derive an AMP-based algorithm that handles both marginalization and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The paper shows that their AMP algorithm for clustering outperforms standard K-means methods in numerical experiments.

Quality: The technical contributions are interesting but the mathematical foundations are not sufficiently developed. The paper contains several serious gaps in the theoretical analysis that make it difficult to assess the validity of the proposed approach. Specifically:
1) In Appendix line 139, the claim that the second term of (11) is O(1/√m) appears to omit the β parameter discussed in Section 2, creating confusion about the scaling.
2) Several asymptotic analyses (including the derivation of equation 19) assume m and N grow in the same order, but the paper does not justify this assumption or discuss its implications for finite-size problems.
3) The justification for claiming mτw² as the expectation of Aᵢⱼ² in line 167 is insufficient—this is merely stated as the variance of the likelihood without proper derivation.
4) The connection between the AMP algorithm for finite β and the MAP problem is unclear. The AMP algorithm minimizes KL divergence, but limβ→∞ min KL ≠ min limβ→∞ KL in general, so the convergence claim requires stronger justification.
5) Proposition 1's proof is too brief; the derivation of equation (17) is missing and crucial for understanding the algorithm's optimality properties.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the relationship between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The main contributions and experimental results are easy to follow, making it straightforward to grasp the paper's core ideas.

Originality: The paper's main technical contribution appears to be an application of Expectation-Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization rather than a fundamentally new algorithm. Section 1 of the Appendix closely follows standard EP update procedures, and the paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from existing work. Stern et al. (2009) already applied EP to matrix factorization in "Expectation-Propagation for the Generative Aspect Model," which should be properly acknowledged. There is also an interesting potential connection to the clutter problem in the original EP paper (Minka, 2001), though the authors do not explore this.

Significance: The paper's significance is limited by its narrow experimental comparison. The authors primarily compare their clustering algorithm to K-means++ without benchmarking against other state-of-the-art clustering methods (e.g., spectral clustering, hierarchical clustering, or recent deep learning approaches). Given the vast literature on clustering, a more comprehensive comparison would be necessary to establish practical value. Additionally, the sketchy mathematical foundation makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed algorithm offers meaningful theoretical or empirical advantages over existing methods.

Pros for acceptance: The paper identifies an interesting connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, and the AMP-based approach has potential computational advantages. The experimental results on artificial and real data sets are promising, showing better performance than K-means++ in many cases.

Cons for acceptance: The theoretical foundation is too weak to support the claims, the originality is questionable due to the EP connection, and the experimental comparison is insufficient for a conference paper of NIPS's caliber. The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical treatment and a more comprehensive comparison to existing clustering methods.
