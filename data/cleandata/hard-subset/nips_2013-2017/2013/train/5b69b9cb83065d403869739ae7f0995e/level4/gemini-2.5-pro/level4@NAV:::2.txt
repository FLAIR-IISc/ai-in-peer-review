### Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, derived from applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian formulation of the problem. As detailed in the supplementary material, the algorithm leverages two simplifying approximations, which assume a large number of rows and columns in the matrix. The authors introduce an unconventional evaluation framework, comparing their method to Lloyd's K-means by casting clustering as a matrix reconstruction task. The algorithm is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), noted as the primary existing message-passing approach for this problem.

#### Cons

Several weaknesses argue against acceptance in its current form.

The evaluation on a non-standard task (clustering framed as matrix factorization) makes it difficult to extrapolate the algorithm's performance to more conventional matrix reconstruction applications. For instance, the cited VBMF papers (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007) benchmark on the Netflix dataset. Adopting this standard dataset would have provided a more direct and convincing comparison. The absence of such a benchmark, or a justification for its omission, casts doubt on the algorithm's true competitiveness, even considering the theoretical advantages of BP over variational methods.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively direct application of BP to a problem previously tackled with Variational Message Passing. While novel in this context, the contribution feels more incremental than groundbreaking. The most compelling aspect of the work—the large-dimension approximations detailed in the supplementary material—is underdeveloped. The validity of these approximations is not experimentally verified, and their explanation could be significantly improved.

The treatment of prior work is a significant concern. The abstract and Section 2.2 misleadingly suggest that framing clustering as a matrix factorization problem is a novel contribution of this paper. This connection is, in fact, well-established. The failure to acknowledge this, either by citing relevant literature or stating it is common knowledge, undermines the paper's scholarly integrity.

Furthermore, the paper should cite Expectation Propagation (EP), the common term for applying BP to continuous distributions, and discuss its relationship to the proposed method. The core algorithm is fundamentally an application of Belief Propagation, a critical fact that should be stated upfront in the abstract rather than being deferred until Section 4.1.

#### Pros

The paper is engaging and introduces a novel algorithm with clear potential utility. The mathematical development is generally tractable, and I am confident that the derivations could be reproduced. While the unconventional evaluation criterion invites skepticism, the K-means comparison seems reasonably general and effectively demonstrates the proposed algorithm's advantages in the conducted experiments.

#### Clarity

The initial sections, including the introduction and summary of previous work, are well-written and easy to follow. The experiments section is also clear, and the figures are readable even in black and white.

However, the paper's clarity falters significantly in its core technical sections. A major weakness is that the full derivation is relegated to supplementary material; a high-level outline in the main paper would be beneficial. The description of the algorithm is difficult to follow, and the mathematical presentation is the paper's weakest element.

The notation is often cumbersome and hard to parse. For example:
*   Common factors in Equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be extracted to improve readability, and the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.
*   The notation is overwrought with hats, tildes, and subscripts. For instance, `τ_w` could simply be `τ`. The hats on `p, q, r` in Section 4 seem superfluous and could be dropped with a clarifying statement at the beginning of the section. The 't' superscripts for iterations are redundant; replacing '=' with an assignment operator '←' would allow them to be omitted. A simpler, more intuitive notational system should be adopted.

The exposition could also be improved. The authors should explicitly state key relationships between equations (e.g., that Eq. 5 is the negative logarithm of Eq. 2, and Eq. 8 is Eq. 2 raised to the power of β). The near row-column symmetry of Algorithm 2 is an important feature that should be highlighted to simplify its presentation. The meaning of the functions introduced in Equation 10 should be explained in prose alongside their definitions. For instance, the interpretation of `G_β` as a scaled covariance matrix is mentioned too late.

The use of `β` subscripts on `f` and `G` but not on the messages seems inconsistent and could be eliminated by treating `β` as a global parameter, with a statement like "in the limit β → ∞..." preceding Equations 15 and 16.

Several deeper points require clarification. What is the theoretical or practical significance of the `m` factor in the additive noise variance? How does it affect the large-scale approximations or the algorithm's row-column symmetry? The authors likely have insights on these points that would greatly benefit the reader.

#### Other Questions

*   Is the use of a tilde to denote column vectors a standard convention? It was initially confusing.
*   Is there a reason for capitalizing `N` but not `m` for the matrix dimensions?
*   In Section 5, the VBMF-MA implementation requires a specific citation to clarify which of the two previously mentioned references guided its development.
*   In the supplementary material, Equation 5 would be clearer if the terms involving `z` were written as a product of exponentials to highlight the PDF. The step from Equation 14 to 15 regarding O(m) terms was difficult to follow.
*   Also in the supplement, the text "A technique used in ... is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters" is vague. The novelty of the paper's approximation relative to these prior works should be clarified in the main text.
*   The English is generally very good, but minor grammatical issues, particularly with the use of articles, are present. These can be distracting. A list of suggestions:
    *   p. 1: "Since properties" -> "Since the properties"; "according to problems" -> "according to the problem"; "has flexibility" -> "has enough flexibility"; "motivate use of" -> "motivate the use of"
    *   p. 2: "We present results" -> "We present the results"
    *   p. 3: "We regard that" -> "We see that"; consider italicizing "maximum accuracy clustering"; "Previous works" -> "Previous work"; "ICM algorithm; It" -> "ICM algorithm: it"
    *   p. 4: "particularized" -> "specialized"
    *   p. 5: "plausible properties" -> "discernible properties"
    *   p. 6: "stuck to bad local minima" -> "stuck in bad local minima"
    *   p. 7: "This is contrastive to that" -> "This is in contrast to the fact that"
    *   Suppl. p. 2: "of these message" -> "of these messages"; "a few number" -> "a few"
*   The factor graph in Figure 1 of the supplementary material was very helpful. More such diagrams in the main paper would enhance clarity.

In summary, the paper introduces a mathematically interesting algorithm that warrants publication. As a novel application of belief propagation to matrix factorization, it represents an important contribution. However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for acceptance. The significant issues with clarity, notation, and scholarly practice (particularly citation of prior work) place an undue burden on the reader and would reflect poorly on the conference. The paper requires substantial revision before it can be reconsidered.
