The authors present an algorithm for matrix reconstruction under noisy observations, focusing on low-rank matrices with additional assumptions on the factors. They employ an Approximate Message Passing approach to speed up the classical, computationally expensive, Bayesian approach. Notably, the authors also connect matrix reconstruction with K-means clustering, providing an interesting application domain for the proposed algorithm.

To the best of my knowledge, the application of Approximate Message Passing to matrix reconstruction is novel and interesting. While the connections between low-rank matrix factorizations and K-means are fairly well-established (e.g., PCA provides a factor two approximation algorithm for K-means), this allows the authors to provide a comprehensive experimental evaluation of their algorithm and compare it to k-means and k-means++. The empirical results suggest that their approach is faster and more efficient than classical k-means and k-means++, which is an encouraging finding. A notable strength of the experimental evaluation is the comparison of both the Frobenius norm residual and the actual clusterings.

The main weakness of the paper lies in the limited theoretical guarantees provided for the proposed algorithm, particularly with regards to convergence. While this is not uncommon in algorithms for K-means, which often have weak theoretical properties, the authors could strengthen their paper by citing more theoretical computer science literature that provides provably accurate algorithms for the K-means objective function. This would help contextualize their results and provide a clearer understanding of the algorithm's limitations.

Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to Approximate Message Passing algorithms for low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means, with promising experimental results. However, the theoretical results are somewhat weak, and the authors could benefit from further exploration of the algorithm's convergence properties.
