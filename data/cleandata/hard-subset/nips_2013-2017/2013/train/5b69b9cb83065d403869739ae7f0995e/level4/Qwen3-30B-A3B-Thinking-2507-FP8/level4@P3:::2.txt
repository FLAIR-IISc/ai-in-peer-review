

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending the AMP framework from sparse vector recovery to matrix factorization problems. The authors formulate the reconstruction problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing flexible incorporation of structural properties (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity) through prior distributions. The key contribution is an AMP-based algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation for low-rank matrix reconstruction, which the authors then apply to clustering by reformulating it as a matrix reconstruction problem with an additional structural constraint. The paper demonstrates that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means on both synthetic and real-world data.

The work builds on recent advances in AMP algorithms for compressed sensing [6,7] and extends the IterFac algorithm [8] to the general-rank case [9]. While the connection between matrix factorization and clustering has been previously explored [10,11], the authors claim their approach is the first to directly incorporate the constraint that each datum must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the matrix reconstruction framework, which represents a meaningful contribution.

Strengths:
1. Quality: The algorithm is theoretically grounded, with a clear derivation of the AMP algorithm for matrix reconstruction. The numerical experiments are comprehensive, comparing against multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, VBMF-MA, AMP-MA) on both synthetic and real data. The results consistently show the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms competitors in terms of both clustering loss and accuracy.

2. Originality: The paper successfully applies AMP to a new domain (matrix reconstruction), which is distinct from prior AMP work focused on sparse vector recovery. The reformulation of clustering as matrix reconstruction with structural constraints represents a novel application of the AMP framework.

3. Significance: The paper demonstrates that the AMP-based clustering approach provides a principled alternative to traditional methods, with clear advantages in high-dimensional settings. The algorithm's efficiency (linear computational cost) and theoretical guarantees make it a valuable contribution to the field.

4. Clarity: The introduction and problem formulation are well-structured and accessible. The experimental results are clearly presented with appropriate statistical measures.

Weaknesses:
1. Quality: The paper's main theoretical contribution (the AMP algorithm) relies on approximations in the limit of large matrix dimensions, but the validity of these approximations is not sufficiently justified. The authors should provide more concrete evidence of the approximation quality, perhaps through additional experiments or a more detailed theoretical analysis.

2. Originality: The paper's claim that the connection between clustering and matrix factorization is novel is problematic. This connection has been well-established in prior work [10,11], and the paper fails to adequately acknowledge this. The authors should revise the abstract and introduction to clarify this relationship and properly cite prior work.

3. Clarity: The paper's notation is unnecessarily complex, with excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts that hinder readability. For example, the notation τw appears without explanation while τ is absent, and the algorithm's iteration indices (t, t+1) could be simplified. The derivation of the algorithm (presented only in supplementary material) should be summarized in the main paper with clearer explanations of key steps.

4. Clarity: The paper does not adequately explain why the noise variance uses a factor of m (mτ instead of τ), which breaks the row-column symmetry of the algorithm. This detail is important for understanding the algorithm's behavior and should be clarified.

5. Significance: While the clustering results are promising, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate how the algorithm would perform on standard matrix reconstruction tasks (e.g., recommendation systems), which would strengthen the claim of broader applicability.

The paper is well-written overall, with only minor grammatical issues (e.g., "the properties" instead of "properties" in the abstract). However, the excessive notation and lack of clarity regarding the well-known matrix factorization-clustering connection undermine the paper's impact.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound algorithm with promising empirical results. While the theoretical contribution is not revolutionary, the application to clustering represents a meaningful extension of AMP to a new domain. The paper would be significantly strengthened by clarifying the relationship to prior work on matrix factorization-clustering connections, simplifying the notation, and providing better justification for the large-matrix approximations. With these revisions, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.

Recommendation: Weak Accept (after major revisions)
