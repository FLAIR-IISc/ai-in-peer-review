Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"
This paper proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, derived by applying Belief Propagation (BP) message passing to a Bayesian model of the problem. As detailed in the Supplementary Material, the method includes two simplifying approximations that assume a large number of rows and a large number of columns in the input matrix. The evaluation is unconventional: the method is compared to Lloyd’s K-means by formulating clustering as matrix reconstruction, and it is also compared to Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only prior message-passing approach for this task.

Cons
Several issues argue against acceptance. Because the new algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard task (clustering cast as matrix factorization), it is difficult to extrapolate the results to more conventional matrix reconstruction settings. For example, the two cited VBMF references—Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen (2007)—evaluate on the Netflix dataset and compare to predecessors; ideally, the present paper would use the same dataset. Although BP is usually more accurate than variational methods, assessing this BP variant under a new criterion casts doubt on its actual competitiveness. If Netflix or a similar dataset cannot be used, the paper should explicitly explain why.

The algorithm itself seems to be a fairly direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While new, it is not especially groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect is the approximations introduced in the limits N → ∞ and m → ∞, where the input matrix is m × N. However, these approximations—described only in the Supplementary Material—are neither directly validated nor clearly explained; they should be clarified and tested.

There are also serious citation issues. On first reading, the paper appears to present matrix factorization for clustering as an original contribution; the abstract and Section 2.2 give that impression. I later learned from other reviewers that this connection is well known. The paper should make this explicit. If the result is too well known to cite a specific source, say so; otherwise, provide appropriate references.

EP should also be cited, as that is the common term for applying BP-like ideas to continuous distributions, and the relationship between EP and the paper’s algorithm should be explained. Relatedly, the main algorithm is best understood as an instance of Belief Propagation, yet this is not stated until Section 4.1; it should be mentioned in the abstract.

Pros
The paper is engaging and introduces a potentially useful algorithm. The mathematical development is generally followable; although I did not verify it line-by-line, it seems feasible to do so. While new evaluation criteria warrant skepticism, casting K-means as a testbed may be broad enough to offer a fair comparison, and the experiments show clear benefits for the proposed method.

Clarity
The early presentation is reasonably clear. The introduction and related work summary are well written. However, relegating the derivation to the Supplementary Material is a drawback; a concise outline of the derivation should appear in the main paper. The experiments are clearly described, and although some plots reproduce poorly in black and white, they remain readable.

I found the algorithm description hard to follow and could not confirm the derivation’s correctness. The mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation and is difficult to parse even superficially. For example, I do not see why factors of 1/(m τ_w) precede each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e. These could be factored out to improve readability. Also, the last two terms in 9b and 9e, respectively, could be combined.

There is an overuse of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, there is a τ_w but no τ; why not just write τ instead of τ_w? Also, the crucial Section 4 contains no p, q, or r—only hat{p}, hat{q}, hat{r}. Consider telling readers at the start of the section that you will drop the hats for brevity. The superscript t’s on messages also seem unnecessary: when the left-hand side has “t+1,” you can replace “=” with “←” and omit all t’s. It is hard to imagine the algorithm was originally derived in such cumbersome notation; consider reverting to the simpler notation you first used.

The exposition could be tightened. For instance, explain that equation 5 is the negative log of equation 2, and that equation 8 is simply equation 2 raised to the power β. Algorithm 2 is nearly row–column symmetric; point this out and even halve its length by stating that the equations can be duplicated with u and v swapped.

The functions in equation 10 should be defined where they appear. It would be clearer to say “f_β is the mean of q_β” rather than giving an equation without interpretation; if you do include an equation, also state its meaning. At the end of Section 4.2, you write, “G_β(b, Λ; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q_β(u; b, Λ, p),” but this is not obvious initially and is not mentioned at G’s definition.

None of the messages carry β subscripts, even though they depend on f and G, which do. But f and G do not depend directly on β, only through q_β. It is unclear why β-subscripts propagate only to f and G and not further. I suggest removing them entirely, even from q. Treat β as a global parameter. Before (15) and (16), simply say, “in the limit β → ∞, f and G take the following form.”

At a deeper level, I would like clarification on the algorithm’s modeling choices. What is the role of the m factor in the additive noise variance? Does it matter in practical matrix factorization applications? Does it affect the approximation used to derive the algorithm? It seems to be the only source of row–column asymmetry—if so, please confirm. I suspect the authors know the answers but have not commented on them.

Other questions:
- Is it standard to use a tilde to denote column vectors? This was not immediately clear.
- Why is N uppercase but m lowercase?
- In Section 5, please add a citation after VBMF-MA indicating the primary reference that guided your implementation. Two variational matrix factorization references appear earlier; it is unclear which (if either) you followed.

Supplementary Material:
- For equation 5, writing the two terms involving z as a product of exponentials would make it clearer which one is the pdf.
- In Section 2, the step from equation 14 to 15 involving big-O in m was hard to follow; this is where I got stuck and would appreciate clarification.

Also in the Supplementary Material, at the top of page 2: “A technique used in … is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without consulting those references, it is unclear how your approximation relates to prior work. Please clarify the novelty of your derivation there and in the main paper.

Language and style
The English is generally strong and the meaning clear, but there are occasional issues with articles and phrasing that can distract some readers. Even the title could be improved—for example: “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” Additional specific suggestions:
- p. 1
  - “Since properties” → “Since the properties”
  - “according to problems” → “according to the problem”
  - “has flexibility” → “has enough flexibility”
  - “motivate use of” → “motivate the use of”
- p. 2
  - “We present results” → “We present the results”
- p. 3
  - “We regard that” → “We see that”
  - Consider italicizing “maximum accuracy clustering.”
  - “Previous works” → “Previous work”
  - “ICM algorithm; It” → “ICM algorithm: it”
- p. 4
  - “particularized” → “specialized”
- p. 5
  - “plausible properties” → perhaps “discernible properties”
- p. 6
  - “stuck to bad local minima” → “stuck in bad local minima”
- p. 7
  - “This is contrastive to that” → “This is in contrast to the fact that”

Supplementary Material language issues:
- p. 2
  - “of these message” → “of these messages”
  - “a few number” → “a few”

I found Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material very helpful; adding more diagrams of this kind would strengthen the paper.

Overall assessment
The mathematics appears interesting, and the algorithm deserves publication somewhere. As the first attempt to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization, it fills an important niche. However, the paper needs substantial revision before it is ready for acceptance. Its current shortcomings in clarity and scholarship impose too great a burden on readers and would reflect poorly on a conference that accepted it in its present form.
