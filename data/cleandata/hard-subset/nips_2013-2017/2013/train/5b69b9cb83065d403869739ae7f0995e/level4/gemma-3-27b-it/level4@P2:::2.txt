Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of approximate message passing (AMP), derived from belief propagation (BP), to a Bayesian model. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, leverages simplifying approximations based on the large-scale limits of the input matrix dimensions. A key contribution is evaluating the algorithm by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing its performance to Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).

**Cons**

A primary concern is the evaluation context. Assessing the algorithm on clustering, presented as a non-standard application of matrix factorization, makes it difficult to extrapolate results to more conventional matrix reconstruction scenarios. The paper cites Lin & Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin & Karhunen (2007) for VBMF, both employing the Netflix dataset; mirroring this evaluation would strengthen the comparison. While AMP often surpasses variational methods, evaluating this specific BP variant using a new criterion raises doubts regarding its true competitiveness. If using Netflix or a similar dataset is unfeasible, a clear explanation is needed.

The algorithm itself represents an incremental extension of existing work, applying BP to a problem previously addressed with variational message passing. While novel, it lacks a truly groundbreaking element. The most interesting aspect lies in the approximations made in the large-dimensional limit ($m, N \to \infty$), detailed in the supplementary material. However, the validity of these approximations remains untested, and a clearer explanation is warranted.

A significant issue is the presentation of prior work. The paper initially implies an original contribution by applying matrix factorization to clustering (in the abstract and Section 2.2), creating a misleading impression. This should be addressed by acknowledging this connection as a well-known concept, with appropriate citations. The relevance of Expectation Propagation (EP), a closely related technique, should be discussed, and the relationship to the proposed algorithm explained. Crucially, the algorithm’s foundation in Belief Propagation isn't mentioned until Section 4.1; it should be highlighted in the abstract.

**Pros**

Despite these criticisms, the paper is an interesting read and proposes a potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is largely accessible, and while full replication wasn't undertaken, the approach appears sound. The K-means problem offers a potentially valid benchmark for comparing algorithms, and the experimental results demonstrate a benefit for the proposed approach.

**Clarity**

The initial presentation is reasonably clear. The introduction and background information are well-structured. However, relegating the algorithm's derivation to the Supplementary Material is a drawback. Outlining the key steps in the main paper would improve accessibility. The experiments section is clear, though plot readability in black and white is somewhat limited.

The algorithm description is challenging to follow, hindering verification of the derivation. The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect. Excessive notation (hats, tildes, subscripts, superscripts) complicates parsing. For instance, the inconsistent use of $\tau$ vs. $\tau_w$ is confusing. Simplifying the notation – perhaps dropping the ‘t’ superscripts or adopting a consistent notation for vector columns – would significantly improve readability. Explicitly stating relationships (e.g., equation 5 as the negative logarithm of equation 2, equation 8 as equation 2 raised to the power of β) would be beneficial. Highlighting the row-column symmetry of Algorithm 2 could shorten its presentation. The meaning of functions defined in section 4.1 should be explained immediately after their introduction (e.g., stating that $f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$). The lack of $\beta$ subscripts in the messages, despite their dependence on $f$ and $G$ which do have them, is inconsistent. Consider omitting them entirely, with a clarifying statement before equations (15) and (16).

Deeper questions remain unanswered: What is the significance of the *m* factor in the noise variance? Does it relate to matrix factorization applications or the algorithm’s derivation? Addressing these points would enhance understanding.

Minor points: The convention of using tildes for column vectors should be clarified. Consistency in capitalization (N vs. m) would be appreciated. A citation following “VBMF-MA” in Section 5 would clarify the implementation details of the comparison algorithm.

**Supplementary Material Feedback:**

Equation 5 could benefit from explicitly showing the product of exponentials. Clarification is needed regarding the step from equation 14 to 15 concerning the big-O notation. The novelty of the algorithm’s derivation should be more clearly articulated. Issues with article usage exist throughout, though they don’t impede understanding. A diagram similar to Figure 1 could be beneficial elsewhere.

**Overall Assessment**

The algorithm shows promise, and the work contributes a new perspective to matrix factorization. However, significant improvements are needed regarding clarity, presentation of related work, and justification of the evaluation methodology. The paper, in its current state, places an unacceptable burden on reviewers and is unlikely to meet the high standards of NIPS. While the underlying research merits publication elsewhere, substantial revisions are necessary for acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Reject (with strong encouragement for resubmission after addressing the noted concerns).
