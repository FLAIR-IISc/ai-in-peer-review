This paper introduces an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior work to handle maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation and applying it to the K-means clustering problem. The authors formulate the reconstruction problem in a Bayesian framework, leveraging the flexibility of prior distributions to incorporate structural properties beyond low-rankness. They demonstrate through numerical experiments that their AMP-based approach outperforms Lloyd’s K-means algorithm, particularly in high-dimensional settings.

The core idea of utilizing AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction is novel and potentially significant. While low-rank matrix factorization and its connection to K-means clustering are established areas (e.g., [10, 11]), this work offers a computationally efficient alternative to traditional Bayesian methods like Markov Chain Monte Carlo or variational Bayes. The extension of existing AMP algorithms [8, 9] to enable MAP estimation is a valuable contribution. The experimental results are convincing, showcasing improved performance compared to K-means [12] and K-means++ [16] in terms of both reconstruction error and clustering accuracy. The authors also appropriately highlight differences from related recent works in AMP [13, 14], namely the scaling of rank and the independence assumptions within factors.

However, the paper lacks rigorous theoretical analysis regarding the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm. While the authors acknowledge this and note the difficulty of proving convergence for non-convex problems, strengthening this aspect would significantly bolster the contribution. Specifically, the discussion of Proposition 1 feels somewhat isolated, and a more thorough exploration of conditions under which the fixed point is guaranteed to be a global minimum would be beneficial. Furthermore, the parameter τ, introduced for scaling, appears empirically chosen and its influence on performance is not extensively investigated. A more detailed justification or sensitivity analysis concerning τ would be valuable. The writing, while generally clear, occasionally suffers from dense mathematical notation without sufficient explanation. For example, the explanation of equations (9a)-(9d) could benefit from more intuitive interpretations of the variables. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The application of AMP to Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with MAP estimation is a novel approach.
*   **Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm offers a computationally efficient alternative to existing Bayesian methods.
*   **Empirical Performance:**  Numerical results demonstrate superior performance compared to K-means and K-means++ in high-dimensional settings.
*   **Connections to Clustering:** The successful application to K-means clustering highlights the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Guarantees:** Limited theoretical analysis regarding convergence and optimality.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Insufficient investigation of the influence of the scaling parameter τ.
*   **Dense Notation:** Occasionally dense mathematical notation hinders readability.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially impactful algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction.
*   The empirical results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
*   The connection to K-means clustering provides a practical application and validation.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of strong theoretical guarantees weakens the overall contribution.
*   The discussion surrounding the scaling parameter τ could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction. While the lack of extensive theoretical analysis is a concern, the novelty, efficiency, and strong empirical performance of the proposed algorithm make it worthy of acceptance. I recommend accepting this paper, with the encouragement that the authors address the theoretical limitations in future work.
