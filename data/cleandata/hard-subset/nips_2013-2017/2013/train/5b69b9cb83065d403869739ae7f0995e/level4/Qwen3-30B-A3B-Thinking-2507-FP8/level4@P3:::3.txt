

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending previous AMP work in compressed sensing to matrix factorization. The authors demonstrate that their framework can handle both marginalization and MAP estimation, and they apply it to clustering by reformulating K-means as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints. The paper builds on recent AMP developments for matrix reconstruction [9] and extends them to handle various estimation objectives.

The main contributions are two-fold: (1) an AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that generalizes previous work to handle both marginalization and MAP estimation, and (2) an application of this algorithm to clustering that directly addresses the constraint that each data point must belong to exactly one cluster. The authors show that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ in numerical experiments on both synthetic and real datasets.

Quality: The paper presents a theoretically motivated approach that builds on the AMP framework, which has established connections to compressed sensing and Bayesian inference. The algorithmic development is largely sound, and the numerical experiments demonstrate clear performance improvements over baseline methods. However, the mathematical derivations lack sufficient rigor. Key issues include:
- The asymptotic analysis (Section 4.1) assumes m and N grow at the same rate without justification, making it difficult to assess the validity of the approximation for finite problem sizes.
- The appendix contains several non-trivial steps that are omitted or inadequately explained (e.g., the O(1/√m) term in Appendix, line 139; the derivation of Equation 19; the justification for mτw² as the expectation of Aᵢⱼ²).
- The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief to verify its validity, particularly the derivation of Equation (17).

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to algorithm development to applications. The main ideas are accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian inference and AMP. The authors effectively explain the connection between matrix factorization and clustering, making the application to clustering intuitive. The algorithm descriptions are precise, though the technical details could be expanded for better reproducibility.

Originality: The paper makes a valuable contribution by extending AMP to handle the full range of Bayesian estimation problems for low-rank matrix reconstruction (not just marginalization) and by applying it to clustering in a way that directly respects the one-cluster-per-data-point constraint. However, the core algorithm appears to be an application of Expectation Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization. The algorithmic updates in Section 4.1 closely resemble standard EP updates (as detailed in Stern et al. [1]), and the connection to EP's clutter problem (Minka [2]) is worth noting. While the paper does not explicitly acknowledge this relationship, it doesn't invalidate the contribution—rather, it suggests the authors could strengthen their position by discussing how their AMP approach relates to EP and why it might be preferable for their specific problem.

Significance: The paper makes a significant contribution to the clustering literature by providing a new algorithm that outperforms established baselines in high-dimensional settings. The performance gains on both synthetic and real data (ORL Face Database) are compelling and suggest practical utility. However, the paper's significance would be strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons to other state-of-the-art clustering methods beyond K-means++ (e.g., spectral clustering, hierarchical clustering, or other Bayesian clustering approaches). The claim that the algorithm "outperforms Lloyd's K-means" is somewhat limited, as Lloyd's algorithm is not the most sophisticated baseline in modern clustering.

Strengths: The paper successfully bridges Bayesian matrix factorization with clustering, demonstrating the flexibility of the AMP framework. The numerical results are thorough and convincing, showing consistent improvements over baselines. The algorithm's low computational cost (O(mN) per iteration) and convergence properties are well-justified.

Weaknesses: The mathematical rigor is insufficient for a NIPS paper, particularly in the asymptotic analysis and the proof of Proposition 1. The connection to EP is underdeveloped, which weakens the novelty claim. The significance would be enhanced by broader experimental comparisons.

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound algorithm with promising empirical results that advances the application of AMP to matrix factorization and clustering. While the mathematical foundations require strengthening, the algorithmic contribution is valuable and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the mathematical gaps in the revision.

Pro: Well-motivated extension of AMP to matrix factorization; clear application to clustering; strong empirical results on both synthetic and real data; low computational complexity.

Con: Insufficient mathematical rigor in derivations; underdeveloped novelty claims regarding EP connection; limited comparison to state-of-the-art clustering methods.
