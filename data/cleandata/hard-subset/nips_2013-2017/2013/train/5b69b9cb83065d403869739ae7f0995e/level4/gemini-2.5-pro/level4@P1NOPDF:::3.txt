This paper proposes an approximate message passing algorithm for matrix factorization. The authors first frame matrix factorization as a generalization of the clustering problem and derive their algorithm. This general algorithm is then specialized for clustering, and the authors conduct experiments comparing its performance to the k-means++ algorithm.

**Quality**

The mathematical derivations throughout the paper, including in the appendix, are overly brief and omit many non-trivial steps. This makes it difficult to verify the correctness of the results. Specifically:

1.  In the Appendix (line 139), the authors state that the second term of Equation (11) is O(1/√m). The justification for this is unclear. Additionally, the parameter β appears to be missing from the discussion in Section 2.

2.  Some of the analysis, including the derivation of Equation (19), appears to assume that m and n grow at the same rate. Overall, the asymptotic analysis in this section is terse, raising concerns about its mathematical rigor.

3.  On line 167, the authors approximate the expectation of A_{il}^2 with mτ_w^2. However, A is observed data, while mτ_w^2 is the variance of the likelihood. The rationale for this approximation needs to be clarified and justified.

4.  It is unclear how the proposed message passing algorithm, derived for a finite β, converges to the MAP solution. While the algorithm minimizes the KL divergence, the authors do not justify swapping the limit and the minimization operator (i.e., that lim_{β→∞} min KL = min lim_{β→∞} KL), which does not hold in general.

5.  The proof of Proposition 1 is too brief to follow. In particular, the derivation of Equation (17) is not explained.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-structured, and its central claims are easy to follow.

**Originality**

The novelty of the proposed method is a major concern. Since the algorithm focuses on estimating the first and second moments of the marginal posterior, it appears to be a direct application of Expectation Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization. This problem has already been addressed by Stern et al. (2009) in "Matchbox: Large Scale Bayesian Recommendations" [http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf]. Furthermore, the update procedures described in Appendix Section 1 seem to be standard EP updates. The authors should clearly differentiate their contribution from this existing work.

On a related note, there may be an interesting connection between the authors' proposed clustering algorithm and the "clutter" problem in the original EP paper by Minka (2001) ["Expectation Propagation for approximate Bayesian inference", http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf]. Specifically, a connection might be drawn if the likelihood in the clutter problem is replaced with a uniform mixture of Gaussian distributions. This is intended as a constructive suggestion for exploring related literature.

**Significance**

The paper's significance is limited by several factors. The authors focus primarily on the application to clustering, but given the vast literature on this topic, a comparison solely against k-means++ is insufficient to demonstrate the algorithm's practical utility. More importantly, the technical contributions are difficult to evaluate. The proofs are very sketchy, which makes it hard to assess their correctness. Finally, as noted above, the algorithm appears to be a straightforward application of Expectation Propagation to matrix factorization, a problem that has been addressed in prior work.
