### Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

**Summary**

This paper presents a novel algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The algorithm is derived from the Belief Propagation (BP) algorithm applied to a probabilistic model of the problem, using approximations valid in the large-system limit (i.e., as the matrix dimensions `m, N -> infinity`). The proposed framework is flexible, accommodating various prior distributions to incorporate structural properties like sparsity, and is unified to handle both posterior marginalization (for mean estimation) and Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation through a single inverse temperature parameter, `β`.

As a primary contribution, the paper applies this algorithm to the K-means clustering problem by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction task with discrete constraints on one of the factor matrices. The authors conduct numerical experiments on both synthetic and real-world data (the ORL face database), showing that their AMP-based clustering algorithms outperform standard methods like Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and a Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF) approach in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Algorithm:** The paper introduces a novel and potentially powerful application of the AMP framework to the general problem of low-rank matrix reconstruction. While AMP has been used for the rank-one case, this work extends it to the general-rank setting and provides a unified method for both marginal and MAP inference, which is a significant contribution.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results for the clustering application are compelling. The proposed AMP-KM and AMP-MA algorithms demonstrate a clear and consistent advantage over established baselines, including Lloyd's K-means and VBMF, on both synthetic and real data. The ability to find better local minima for the K-means objective is particularly noteworthy.

3.  **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides valuable theoretical insight. Proposition 1, which shows that a fixed point of the AMP algorithm for the MAP problem is also a fixed point of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm, is an interesting result. It suggests that the algorithm's dynamics may help it avoid some of the poor local optima that plague standard ICM/alternating minimization approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper's clarity is a major concern and significantly hinders its accessibility and reproducibility.
    *   **Derivation in Supplementary Material:** The core derivation of the algorithm is entirely relegated to the supplementary material. The main paper would be much stronger if it included at least a high-level overview or sketch of the derivation to provide intuition for the update equations in Algorithm 2.
    *   **Algorithm Description:** The presentation of Algorithm 2 is dense and difficult to parse. The notation is cumbersome, with an abundance of hats, tildes, and superscripts. Simple typographic improvements, such as factoring out the common `1/(mτ)` term in Eq. (9a) and (9c) or combining the last two terms, would greatly improve readability.
    *   **Lack of Exposition:** The paper frequently misses opportunities to explain the intuition behind the mathematics. For example, it should be explicitly stated that Eq. (5) is the negative logarithm of Eq. (2), that Eq. (8) is a power of the posterior, and the interpretation of `f` and `G` as the mean and scaled covariance of `q̂` should accompany their definition in Eq. (10), not be delayed until the end of Section 4.2.

2.  **Scholarly Context and Citations:**
    *   **Clustering as Matrix Factorization:** The paper presents the formulation of clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem as if it were a novel contribution. This connection is well-established in the literature (e.g., in papers [10] and [11] already cited by the authors). This lack of proper attribution is a significant oversight and should be corrected to properly contextualize the work.
    *   **Relation to BP/EP:** The connection to Belief Propagation is not mentioned until Section 4.1. This fundamental aspect of the method should be stated upfront in the abstract and introduction. Furthermore, given that the algorithm applies BP to a model with continuous variables, its relationship to Expectation Propagation (EP) should be discussed and relevant work cited.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:** While the clustering results are strong, the decision to evaluate the algorithm *only* on clustering makes it difficult to assess its general utility for low-rank matrix reconstruction. Prior work on VBMF (e.g., [4], [5]) often uses standard collaborative filtering benchmarks like the Netflix dataset. Without a comparison on such a task, it is hard to gauge how the proposed AMP algorithm performs against the state-of-the-art in the broader matrix factorization field. The authors should either include such an experiment or justify why clustering is a sufficient and representative benchmark.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**
*   The paper introduces a novel and non-trivial extension of the powerful AMP framework to general low-rank matrix reconstruction.
*   The empirical results on the chosen application (clustering) are strong and demonstrate a clear performance benefit over standard methods.
*   The work contains interesting theoretical connections between the proposed algorithm and classic methods like ICM.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The paper suffers from significant clarity issues, particularly in the presentation of the algorithm and the relegation of its derivation to supplementary material.
*   There are notable oversights in scholarship, especially in failing to properly credit prior work on the connection between clustering and matrix factorization.
*   The evaluation is narrow, making it difficult to assess the algorithm's general applicability and competitiveness in the wider field of matrix reconstruction.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Notation:** Please consider simplifying the notation. For example, using `t` and `t-1` as subscripts instead of superscripts, or using an assignment operator (`<-`) to imply iteration, could reduce clutter. Why are there so many `p̂` and `q̂` variables but no `p` or `q` in Section 4? Simply stating that the assumed priors are being used would allow for dropping the hats.
*   **Intuition:** Please provide more intuition for the algorithm's components. What is the role of the factor `m` in the noise variance? How does it relate to the large-system limit approximations? The "correction" terms that differentiate Algorithm 3 from Lloyd's K-means are fascinating; the intuition provided is good but could be expanded upon.
*   **Clarity of Derivation:** In the supplementary material, the step from Eq. (14) to (15) involving the `O(1/sqrt(m))` terms was difficult to follow. More explanation here would be beneficial.
*   **VBMF Citation:** In Section 5, please clarify which specific VBMF algorithm was implemented for the comparison (e.g., from [4] or [5]).
*   **Minor Grammar:** While the English is generally good, a pass for article usage ("the", "a") would improve flow. For example, "Since the properties...", "motivate the use of...".

In its current form, the paper's weaknesses in clarity and scholarship place an undue burden on the reader. While the core ideas are promising, the paper requires substantial revision before it can be accepted.
