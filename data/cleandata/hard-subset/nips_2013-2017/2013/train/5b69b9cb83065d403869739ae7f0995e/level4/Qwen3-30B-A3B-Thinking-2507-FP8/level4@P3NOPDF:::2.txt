

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"  

This paper introduces a novel belief propagation (BP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, framed as a Bayesian inference problem. The approach formulates clustering as a matrix reconstruction task, enabling comparison against Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF). While the algorithm represents an application of BP to a problem previously addressed via Variational Message Passing, the paper’s novelty lies in its asymptotic approximations (as $N, m \to \infty$) and the unconventional problem encoding.  

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Unjustified Evaluation Framework**: The paper evaluates performance by reducing clustering to matrix reconstruction—a non-standard task. This obscures generalizability to conventional matrix reconstruction problems (e.g., Netflix dataset, used in prior VBMF work [Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007]). Without benchmarking on standard datasets or explaining why such datasets are unsuitable, the competitiveness of the BP variant remains questionable. The K-means comparison, while novel, does not sufficiently validate the method for core matrix reconstruction tasks.  

2. **Misrepresentation of Novelty**: The abstract and Section 2.2 imply that applying matrix factorization to clustering is a new contribution. However, this connection is well-established in prior work (e.g., [X, Y]). The paper should explicitly state this to avoid misleading readers, rather than omitting citations. Similarly, the relationship to Expectation Propagation (EP)—a standard term for BP in continuous distributions—must be clarified, as the algorithm is essentially an EP application.  

3. **Clarity and Mathematical Rigor**: The derivation is relegated to supplementary material, hindering reproducibility. Key approximations (e.g., asymptotic limits) are described only in the supplement without empirical validation. The mathematical exposition is overly complex: redundant symbols (e.g., $\tau_w$ vs. $\tau$, excessive hats/tildes), unclear scaling factors (e.g., $1/m\tau_w$ in Eq. 9a–9e), and unexplained notational choices (e.g., $t$-superscripts in messages) impede comprehension. The paper should simplify notation (e.g., drop $t$-subscripts by using $\gets$ instead of $=$) and clarify relationships (e.g., explain why Eq. 5 is the negative log of Eq. 2).  

**Minor Issues**  
- **Citations**: The VBMF-MA implementation lacks a primary reference in Section 5. The two cited VBMF papers earlier are ambiguous; one should be specified.  
- **Language**: Minor grammatical issues (e.g., "has flexibility" → "has sufficient flexibility"; "a few number" → "a few") distract readers. The title should specify the method: "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing."  
- **Supplementary Material**: The derivation from Eq. 14 to 15 (big-O handling) is unclear. The novelty of the message approximation should be explicitly contrasted with prior work.  
- **Notational Consistency**: Why are $N$ (capitalized) and $m$ (lowercase) used for matrix dimensions? Why are $\beta$ subscripts omitted for messages despite dependence on $\beta$-parametrized terms?  

**Strengths**  
The algorithm is well-motivated for its specific problem (clustering-as-reconstruction), and the K-means comparison demonstrates clear advantages. The supplementary diagram (Fig. 1) is helpful, and the mathematics is generally followable with effort. The English is otherwise clear, though minor phrasing issues persist.  

**Recommendation**  
The paper addresses a niche application of BP to matrix factorization but suffers from critical flaws in evaluation, clarity, and scholarly rigor. The evaluation framework undermines claims of competitiveness, the novelty is overstated, and the mathematical exposition is unnecessarily opaque. While the algorithm has potential, these issues impose an unacceptable burden on readers.  

**Required Revisions**  
1. **Clarify and justify the evaluation framework**: Either benchmark on standard datasets (e.g., Netflix) or explicitly justify why they are unsuitable.  
2. **Correct the novelty claim**: Acknowledge the established link between matrix factorization and clustering, citing key works.  
3. **Simplify and clarify mathematics**:  
   - Move key derivations to the main paper.  
   - Eliminate redundant notation (e.g., $\beta$ subscripts for messages, $t$-superscripts).  
   - Explain scaling factors (e.g., $m$ in noise variance) and their impact on symmetry.  
4. **Fix language and citations**: Address all minor grammatical issues and specify VBMF-MA’s reference.  

Without these revisions, the paper fails to establish its contribution or validity for the intended audience. It is currently unsuitable for publication in its present form. The core idea merits attention, but the current presentation lacks the rigor expected at NIPS.  

**Decision: Reject**  
*Reason*: Major flaws in evaluation design, scholarly presentation, and mathematical clarity prevent the paper from meeting NIPS standards. The required revisions would substantially alter the manuscript, making a revised submission worthwhile only if fully addressed.
