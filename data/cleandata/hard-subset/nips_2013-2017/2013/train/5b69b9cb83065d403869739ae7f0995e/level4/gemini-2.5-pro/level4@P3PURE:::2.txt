### Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

**Summary**

This paper introduces a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction by applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the problem. The derivation, detailed in the supplementary material, relies on two simplifying approximations made in the limit of a large number of rows and columns. The algorithm's performance is evaluated in a novel setting by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing it against Lloyd's K-means algorithm. It is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), a previous message-passing approach.

**Strengths**

*   The paper presents a novel and potentially useful algorithm, marking one of the first applications of belief propagation to this problem.
*   The connection made between matrix reconstruction and clustering provides an interesting new testbed for evaluation, and the experiments convincingly demonstrate a benefit for the proposed algorithm in this context.
*   The paper is generally well-written, and the mathematical development, though dense, appears sound and replicable in principle.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Evaluation on a Non-Standard Task:** By evaluating a new matrix reconstruction algorithm primarily on a clustering task, it is difficult to assess its competitiveness on more conventional problems like collaborative filtering. Key VBMF papers, cited by the authors (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007), use standard datasets like the Netflix dataset for evaluation. The absence of such a comparison makes it hard to situate this work's performance relative to the state of the art. While BP is often more accurate than variational methods, this novel evaluation criterion introduces uncertainty. If standard datasets cannot be used, the authors should provide a justification.
*   **Incremental Novelty:** The core algorithm is a direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While this is a new contribution, it is not a groundbreaking theoretical advance. The most interesting aspect is the set of approximations derived for large matrices, but their validity is not directly tested, and their explanation is confined to the supplementary material.
*   **Insufficient Citation and Positioning:** The paper fails to properly credit prior work. The connection between matrix factorization and clustering is presented as if it were a novel contribution of this paper, which is misleading as this connection is well-known. If it is too established to cite a single source, it should be described as such; otherwise, relevant prior work must be cited. Furthermore, the application of BP to continuous distributions is commonly known as Expectation Propagation (EP), which should be cited and its relationship to the proposed algorithm explained. Finally, the fact that the algorithm is based on Belief Propagation—its core identity—is not mentioned until Section 4.1 and should be stated upfront in the abstract.

**Clarity and Presentation**

The paper's clarity is mixed. While the introduction is clear, the technical presentation of the algorithm is difficult to follow, hindering a full assessment of its correctness. The relegation of the entire derivation to the supplementary material is a significant drawback; a high-level outline should be included in the main paper.

The following detailed points should be addressed to improve the paper's clarity:

**1. Mathematical Notation and Exposition:**
The notation is overly cumbersome and significantly impedes readability.
*   **Redundant Notation:** In equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e, the common factor of $1 / m \tau_w$ could be factored out. The last two terms in 9b and 9e could also be combined.
*   **Simplification of Variables:** There is an excess of hats, tildes, and subscripts. For instance, since there is no $\tau$, why not replace $\tau_w$ with $\tau$? In Section 4, all variables p, q, r appear with hats; these could be dropped for brevity within the section. The 't' superscripts in the message updates appear unnecessary; replacing "=" with a left-arrow "←" for updates would allow the time indices to be dropped, simplifying the equations considerably.
*   **Lack of Intuition:** The exposition would benefit from more explanatory text. For example, the authors should state that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, or that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. The near row-column symmetry of Algorithm 2 should be highlighted, which could also simplify its presentation.
*   **Undefined Terms:** The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be explained in prose near their definition (e.g., "$f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$"). Similarly, the fact that "$G_\beta$ is a scaled covariance matrix" is mentioned only at the end of Section 4.2 and should appear with its definition.
*   **Inconsistent Subscripts:** The messages lack the $\beta$ subscript, even though they depend on $f$ and $G$, which do. This seems inconsistent. It may be clearer to treat $\beta$ as a global parameter and remove the subscripts from $q$, $f$, and $G$, instead stating before equations (15) and (16) that "in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, $f$ and $G$ take the following form...".

**2. Unanswered Questions:**
The paper would be stronger if it provided more insight into its own modeling choices.
*   What is the significance of the factor `m` in the additive noise variance? Does this play a role in standard matrix factorization applications or in the approximations used here? It appears to be the only element breaking the row-column symmetry of the algorithm, which is worth discussing.
*   Is the use of a tilde to denote column vectors standard? This was initially confusing.
*   Why is the matrix dimension `N` capitalized but `m` is lowercase?

**3. Minor Issues and Corrections:**
*   A specific citation for the VBMF-MA implementation used in Section 5 is needed.
*   In the supplementary material, the derivation from equation 14 to 15, specifically concerning the big-O terms, was difficult to follow and could be clarified.
*   Also in the supplement (page 2), the sentence "A technique used in ... is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters" is vague. The novelty of the paper's specific approximation relative to these prior techniques should be made explicit.
*   The supplementary material's Figure 1 is very helpful. Additional diagrams in the main paper could improve understanding.
*   There are several minor grammatical errors, primarily related to the use of articles ("a", "the"). While the meaning is clear, correction would improve readability. A list of suggestions is provided below.

**Language Suggestions:**
*   p. 1: "Since properties" -> "Since the properties"
*   p. 1: "according to problems" -> "according to the problem"
*   p. 1: "has flexibility" -> "has enough flexibility"
*   p. 1: "motivate use of" -> "motivate the use of"
*   p. 2: "We present results" -> "We present the results"
*   p. 3: "We regard that" -> "We see that" or "We observe that"
*   p. 3: "Previous works" -> "Previous work"
*   p. 3: "ICM algorithm; It" -> "ICM algorithm: it"
*   p. 4: "particularized" -> "specialized"
*   p. 6: "stuck to bad local minima" -> "stuck in bad local minima"
*   p. 7: "This is contrastive to that" -> "This is in contrast to the fact that"
*   Suppl. p. 2: "of these message" -> "of these messages"
*   Suppl. p. 2: "a few number" -> "a few"

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting algorithm and represents a solid effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, significant revisions are required before it can be accepted. The lack of comparison on standard benchmarks, inadequate citation of prior work, and poor clarity in the technical presentation place an unacceptable burden on the reader and misrepresent the work's context within the field. If these issues are addressed, the paper could be a valuable contribution.
