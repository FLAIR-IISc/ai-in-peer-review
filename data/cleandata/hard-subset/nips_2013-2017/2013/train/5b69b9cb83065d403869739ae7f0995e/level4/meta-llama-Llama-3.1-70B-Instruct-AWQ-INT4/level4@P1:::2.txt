Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper presents a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel manner against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem. It is also compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which seems to be the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

The paper has both strengths and weaknesses. On the positive side, the paper is interesting to read, and the mathematics is possible to follow. The algorithm is new and potentially useful, and the experiments show a benefit for the new algorithm in comparison to existing methods. The paper also contributes to the understanding of the relationship between BP and Variational Bayes.

However, there are several arguments against accepting the paper. One major concern is that the algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), making it difficult to interpolate the experimental results to predict how the algorithm would perform on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, previous papers on VBMF use the Netflix dataset to evaluate their algorithm against predecessors, and it would be ideal if the present paper had used the same dataset. Even if Netflix or a similar dataset can't be used, the authors should explain in the paper why this is the case.

Another concern is that the algorithm itself appears to be a more or less straightforward application of BP to a problem that had been previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. Although new, it is not exactly groundbreaking. The most interesting part of the algorithm, to me, is the approximations introduced in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ is the dimension of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, which are only described in supplementary material, is never directly tested, and I think they could be explained more clearly.

There are also some serious problems regarding the citation of prior work. When I first read the paper, I thought that it was introducing the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution. The text of the abstract and Section 2.2 give this impression. I felt betrayed when I learned from other reviewers that the connection is well-known. I don't see a good reason why the paper would not make this clear to the reader. If it is too well-known to cite any particular reference, then one should just say that it is well-known. Otherwise, cite prior work.

The paper could also benefit from improved clarity and exposition. The early presentation is fairly easy to follow, but the derivation of the algorithm only appears in the supplementary material, which is a drawback. I wish that the derivation could be outlined in the main paper. The experiments section is clear, but the mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation. Even at a very superficial level, it was difficult to parse. For instance, I don't understand why factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ appear before each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e. These could be factored out to make the expressions easier to read.

Additionally, there are some minor issues with notation and language. There seem to be an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, there is a $\tau_w$ but no $\tau$; why not just replace $\tau_w$ by $\tau$? Also, the most important Section 4 contains no p, q, or r, but only \hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r} - why not give readers a break and say at the beginning of the section "We'll be dropping the hats here for brevity"? The 't' superscripts in the messages seem to be wholly unnecessary. When the left-hand side has a "t+1" you just need to change "=" to "\gets" and then you can drop all of the t's.

In the supplementary material, for equation 5, I think it would be clearer to write a product of exponentials for the two terms involving $z$, to make it more obvious that one is the pdf. For section 2, I had trouble with the step from equation 14 to 15, regarding big-O of $m$ terms. I am not sure if I just need to think harder, but this is where I got stuck.

The English is very good, and the meaning always gets across, but there are some places where it reads like it was written by someone not entirely skilled in the use of articles. This can be distracting for some readers. Even in the title - I think it should be something like "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm". For other places, I will make a list of suggestions which may be of use to the authors.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a new and potentially useful algorithm, it needs more work before it can be accepted. The paper is sufficiently lacking in clarity and scholarship as to put an unacceptable burden on readers, which would reflect poorly on a conference that accepted it in its present state.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem.
* The algorithm is evaluated in a novel manner against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem.
* The paper contributes to the understanding of the relationship between BP and Variational Bayes.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), making it difficult to interpolate the experimental results to predict how the algorithm would perform on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems.
* The algorithm itself appears to be a more or less straightforward application of BP to a problem that had been previously addressed with Variational Message Passing.
* The paper lacks clarity and scholarship, putting an unacceptable burden on readers.
* There are some serious problems regarding the citation of prior work.
* The paper could benefit from improved clarity and exposition.

Recommendation:

* The authors should address the concerns mentioned above, particularly regarding the evaluation of the algorithm on a non-standard problem and the lack of clarity and scholarship.
* The authors should also consider using a more standard dataset, such as the Netflix dataset, to evaluate their algorithm against predecessors.
* The paper could benefit from improved clarity and exposition, particularly in the derivation of the algorithm and the mathematics.
* The authors should also consider providing more explanations and justifications for the approximations introduced in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$.
