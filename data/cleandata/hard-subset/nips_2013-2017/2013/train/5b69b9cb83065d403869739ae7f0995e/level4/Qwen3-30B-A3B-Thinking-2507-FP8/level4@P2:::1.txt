

The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending prior AMP work [8,9] to handle both marginal posterior estimation and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation. The authors derive a unified AMP framework that incorporates structural priors (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity) on the factor matrices and demonstrate its application to K-means clustering by reformulating clustering as a constrained low-rank reconstruction problem. The key novelty lies in adapting AMP to the general-rank case for MAP estimation—a gap in prior work [9]—and providing a novel algorithm for K-means that enforces exact cluster assignments within the reconstruction framework. The authors compare their AMP-KM and AMP-MA (maximum accuracy) algorithms against Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF) on synthetic and real data (ORL faces), showing superior accuracy and faster convergence.

**Strengths**  
*Quality*: The technical approach is sound and well-motivated. The AMP derivation for the marginalization and MAP problems (Algorithm 2) is rigorous, with clear connections to belief propagation and variational Bayes. The authors address computational bottlenecks (e.g., linear cost O(mN) per iteration) and provide a convergence analysis via Proposition 1, showing AMP fixed points coincide with ICM fixed points under mild conditions. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive: 500 synthetic trials with varying cluster counts and 50 real-data runs on ORL faces, measuring normalized K-means loss, accuracy (via Hungarian matching), and convergence speed. Results consistently show AMP-KM outperforms Lloyd’s K-means (especially for r ≥ 5) and AMP-MA achieves higher accuracy than VBMF-MA, with faster convergence.  

*Clarity*: The paper is exceptionally well-organized. The problem setting (Section 2) clearly distinguishes marginalization vs. MAP problems, and the clustering reformulation (Section 2.2) logically bridges matrix reconstruction to K-means. Algorithm descriptions (Algorithms 2–3) are precise, with intuitive explanations of key terms (e.g., the bias-correction term in Equation 18b). Supplementary material (referenced but not included) likely contains the state evolution proofs, which aligns with NIPS’ emphasis on theoretical depth for scalable methods.  

*Originality*: The AMP extension for MAP estimation is novel (prior AMP work [9] handled only posterior means). While low-rank matrix reconstruction for clustering is not new [10,11], the authors’ constraint-handling approach—directly enforcing exact cluster assignments within the reconstruction framework—is a significant advance over prior methods [10,11]. This is clearly differentiated from related work (Section 1), including recent AMP variants for dictionary learning [13] and generalized matrix models [14].  

*Significance*: The method addresses a practical gap: K-means’ sensitivity to initialization and local minima. AMP-KM’s consistent outperformance (e.g., 0.400 vs. 0.412 mean K-means loss on ORL faces) and faster convergence (8.8 vs. 6.6 iterations on average) demonstrate tangible utility for high-dimensional data. The algorithm’s flexibility for diverse structural priors (sparsity, non-negativity) broadens applicability beyond clustering, aligning with NIPS’ focus on scalable, adaptable methods.  

**Weaknesses**  
The primary limitation is the lack of theoretical guarantees beyond Proposition 1. While the paper shows AMP fixed points are ICM fixed points (avoiding worse local minima), it does not establish convergence to global optima or characterize the landscape of CMAP(U,V) for non-convex cases. This is a fair critique given K-means’ inherent NP-hardness, but the authors could strengthen the argument by citing recent theoretical advances for K-means (e.g., [16] on k-means++ guarantees or [19] on approximation algorithms), even if not directly applicable to their AMP framework. The discussion of state evolution (Section 4.1) is promising but omitted due to space constraints; including even a high-level statement would bolster theoretical credibility.  

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel AMP extension for MAP estimation; strong empirical results showing superiority over K-means variants; clear theoretical connections to existing work; practical relevance to high-dimensional clustering.  
*Con*: Limited theoretical analysis beyond fixed-point properties; no comparison to other AMP-based matrix reconstruction methods (e.g., [14]); the bias-correction term in Equation 18b requires intuitive justification beyond "correction."  

**Recommendation**  
This is a strong, promising contribution that advances the state of the art in scalable matrix factorization and clustering. The theoretical foundation is solid, the empirical evaluation is thorough, and the algorithm’s efficiency and accuracy gains are compelling. While the theoretical guarantees could be expanded, the work’s practical significance and novelty justify acceptance. The authors should briefly address the convergence discussion in the final version to preempt reviewer concerns. This paper would be a valuable addition to NIPS 2013.
