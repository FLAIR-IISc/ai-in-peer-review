Review of “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering”

This paper proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, described in the Supplementary Material, introduces two simplifying approximations corresponding to the limits of large numbers of rows and columns, respectively. The method is evaluated in a novel way by casting clustering as matrix reconstruction and comparing against Lloyd’s K-means, and is also compared with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only prior message-passing approach.

Cons
- The evaluation is conducted on a non-standard formulation (clustering as matrix factorization), making it hard to extrapolate performance to conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. Two cited VBMF references—Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen (2007)—use the Netflix dataset to compare against prior work. Ideally, this paper should evaluate on the same dataset. Although BP often outperforms variational approaches, introducing a new evaluation criterion creates uncertainty about competitiveness. If Netflix (or a similar dataset) cannot be used, the paper should explain why.
- The algorithm reads as a fairly direct application of BP to a problem previously treated with Variational Message Passing. It is new but not groundbreaking. The most interesting aspects are the approximations introduced in the limits N→∞ and m→∞, where the matrix has dimensions m × N. However, the validity of these approximations—described only in the supplement—is neither directly tested nor explained with sufficient clarity.
- Prior work is not cited adequately. The abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that formulating clustering as matrix factorization is novel. This connection is well known. If no single reference is canonical, the paper should say so explicitly; otherwise, cite prior work.
- Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the common framework for BP-like methods on continuous distributions. The relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm should be clarified. Relatedly, the algorithm is most naturally described as BP, yet this is not stated until Section 4.1. It should be noted in the abstract.

Pros
- The paper is engaging and presents a potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally followable, and while I did not re-derive it, it seems feasible to do so.
- Although I am cautious about new evaluation criteria, the K-means setting may be broad enough to yield a fair comparison and, in the experiments, favors the proposed method.

Clarity
- The introduction and related work are clear. However, relegating the derivation to the supplement is a drawback; an outline of the derivation should appear in the main text. The experiments are clearly described, and the plots, though not ideal in black and white, remain readable.
- The algorithmic description is hard to parse, and I could not verify the derivation. The mathematics is the weakest part. For example, the repeated factors 1/(m τ_w) in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out to improve readability, and the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.
- There is an overuse of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For example, there is a τ_w but no τ; why not simply write τ? Section 4 uses only ĥp, ĥq, ĥr; consider dropping hats entirely for readability. The superscript t on messages seems unnecessary; when the left-hand side has “t+1,” replacing “=” by “←” would allow all t’s to be dropped. I doubt the derivation originally required such cumbersome notation; please consider simplifying.
- Exposition could be improved: explain that equation 5 is the negative log of equation 2, and that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power β. Algorithm 2 is nearly row–column symmetric; note this symmetry and consider halving its length by stating “copy these equations with u and v swapped.”
- Define the functions in equation 10 when they first appear. It would be clearer to say “f_β is the mean of q_β” rather than only giving a formula; if you provide formulas, also state their meaning. At the end of Section 4.2, you note that G_β(b, Λ; p) is a scaled covariance of q_β(u; b, Λ, p), but this should be stated at the definition of G as well.
- None of the messages carry β subscripts, even though they depend on f and G, which do. Since f and G depend on β only through q_β, it is unclear why the β subscript applies only to f and G. I suggest removing β subscripts entirely, even from q. Treat β as a global parameter. Before (15) and (16), simply state: “In the limit β → ∞, f and G take the following forms.”
- Deeper questions remain. What is the role of the factor m in the additive noise variance? Is this standard in matrix factorization models? Does it affect the large-system approximations? It appears to be the only source of row–column asymmetry; is that correct? Please discuss.

Other questions
- Is it standard in your notation to use a tilde for column vectors? This was not obvious.
- Why is N uppercase but m lowercase?
- In Section 5, please add a citation after “VBMF-MA” indicating the primary reference for your implementation. Two VBMF citations appear earlier; it is unclear which one you follow (or if both).

Supplementary Material
- For equation 5, consider writing the two terms involving z as a product of exponentials to make it obvious that one is a pdf.
- In Section 2, the step from equation 14 to 15 about O(m) terms was hard to follow; this is where I got stuck.
- At the top of page 2: “a technique … is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Please clarify how the approximation you use relates to prior work and what is novel here; also reflect this in the main paper.

Language and style
- The English is generally good, but occasional issues with articles and phrasing can distract. A more descriptive title would help, e.g., “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.”
- Specific suggestions:
  - p.1: “Since properties” → “Since the properties”; “according to problems” → “according to the problem”; “has flexibility” → “has enough flexibility”; “motivate use of” → “motivate the use of.”
  - p.2: “We present results” → “We present the results.”
  - p.3: “We regard that” → “We see that”; consider italicizing “maximum accuracy clustering”; “Previous works” → “Previous work”; “ICM algorithm; It” → “ICM algorithm: it.”
  - p.4: “particularized” → “specialized.”
  - p.5: “plausible properties” → “discernible properties”?
  - p.6: “stuck to bad local minima” → “stuck in bad local minima”?
  - p.7: “This is contrastive to that” → “This is in contrast to the fact that.”
  - Supplement p.2: “of these message” → “of these messages”; “a few number” → “a few.”
- Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material is very helpful; more diagrams of this kind would strengthen the paper.

Overall assessment
The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm merits publication somewhere; as (to my knowledge) the first attempt to apply BP to matrix factorization, it fills an important niche. However, the paper requires substantially improved clarity, stronger scholarship on prior work and positioning, and more standard empirical evaluation. In its current form, it places an undue burden on readers, which would reflect poorly on a venue that accepted it as is.
