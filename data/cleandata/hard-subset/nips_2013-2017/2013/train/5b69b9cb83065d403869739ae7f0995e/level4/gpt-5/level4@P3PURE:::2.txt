Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

Summary
The paper proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. As described in the Supplementary Material, the algorithm incorporates two simplifying approximations, assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The method is evaluated in a novel way by casting clustering as a matrix reconstruction task and comparing against Lloyd’s K-means algorithm. It is also compared with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only prior message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

Cons
- Evaluation on a non-standard problem makes it difficult to extrapolate performance to conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. Prior VBMF works (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen, 2007) benchmark on the Netflix dataset and compare against predecessors. It would be ideal for this paper to use the same dataset. Although BP is often more accurate than Variational methods, introducing a new evaluation criterion leaves uncertainty about true competitiveness. If Netflix or a similar dataset cannot be used, the paper should explain why.
- The algorithm reads as a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. It is new but not particularly groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect is the approximations introduced in the limits N → ∞ and m → ∞ (for an m × N matrix), but their validity—described only in the Supplementary Material—is not directly tested and should be explained more clearly.
- There are serious issues with citation and positioning. On first reading, the abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that casting clustering as matrix factorization is an original contribution. I felt betrayed when I learned from other reviewers that this connection is well known. The paper should make this clear. If it is too well known to cite a single source, say so; otherwise cite prior work.
- Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, since “EP” is the standard name for BP-style approximations on continuous distributions. The relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm should be explained. Relatedly, the main algorithm is most naturally understood as BP, but this is not stated until Section 4.1; it should be mentioned in the abstract.

Pros
- The paper is interesting and presents a new, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally followable, and although I did not verify every step by hand, it seems feasible to do so.
- While one should be cautious about new evaluation criteria, the K-means formulation may be sufficiently general to yield a fair comparison, and the experiments do show an advantage for the new method.

Clarity and presentation
- The introduction and summary of prior work are clear. However, placing the core derivation only in the Supplementary Material is a drawback; an outline of the derivation should appear in the main paper. The experiments section is clear; some plots are hard to read in black and white but remain interpretable.

Mathematical presentation and notation
- I found the algorithm description difficult to follow and could not fully check the derivation. The mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation. Even superficially, some expressions are unnecessarily hard to parse. For example, factors of 1/(m τ_w) appear before each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e; these could be factored out to improve readability. Also, the last two terms in 9b and 9e, respectively, could be combined.
- There is an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For example, there is a τ_w but no τ; why not simply write τ? Section 4, which is central, uses only ĥp, ĥq, ĥr; consider stating at the start of the section that “we drop the hats for brevity.” The superscript t on messages seems unnecessary; when the left-hand side has “t+1,” using “←” instead of “=” would allow all superscript t’s to be dropped. I find it hard to imagine that the derivation originally used such cumbersome notation; please consider simplifying by returning to earlier, cleaner notation.
- Exposition could be improved by explicitly noting simple relationships. For example, explain that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, and that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power β. Algorithm 2 appears nearly row–column symmetric; point this out, and consider halving its length by stating “then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v.”
- The meanings of the functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definitions. It would be clearer to say “f_β is the mean of q_β” rather than only giving a formula; if you do provide an equation, also state what it represents. At the end of Section 4.2, you write “G_β(b, Λ; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q_β(u; b, Λ, p),” but this was not obvious earlier and was not mentioned when G was defined.
- None of the messages carry β subscripts, even though they depend on f and G, which do carry β subscripts. Since f and G do not depend directly on β but only on q_β, it is unclear why β subscripts propagate only that far. I suggest eliminating β subscripts altogether, even from q, treating β as a global parameter. Before equations (15) and (16), simply write “in the limit β → ∞, f and G take the following form.”

Deeper algorithmic questions
- What is the significance of the factor m in the additive noise variance? Does it play a role in practical applications of matrix factorization or in the approximations used to derive the algorithm? It seems to be the only element that breaks row–column symmetry—if so, please confirm and discuss. It appears that the authors know the answers but do not comment on them.

Other questions and requests
- Is it standard to use a tilde to denote column vectors? This was not immediately clear.
- Why is N capitalized but m lowercase?
- In Section 5, please add a citation immediately after “VBMF-MA” indicating the primary reference guiding your implementation. Earlier, two references are cited for variational matrix factorization; it is not clear which one (or whether both) is intended for VBMF-MA.

Comments on the Supplementary Material
- For equation 5, it would be clearer to write the two terms involving z as a product of exponentials, making it obvious that one is a pdf.
- In Section 2, the step from equation 14 to 15 involving big-O in m was hard to follow; this is where I got stuck.
- At the top of page 2: “a technique used in … is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without consulting those references, it is not clear how your approximation relates to previous work. Please clarify the novelty of your derivation in the Supplementary Material and, ideally, in the main paper as well.
- I liked Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material; if possible, add more diagrams of this kind.

Language and style
The English is generally good and the meaning is clear, but there are several places where article usage and phrasing could be improved, which may distract some readers. Even the title could be clearer—for example: “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” Additional suggestions:
- Page 1: “Since properties” → “Since the properties”; “according to problems” → “according to the problem”; “has flexibility” → “has enough flexibility”; “motivate use of” → “motivate the use of.”
- Page 2: “We present results” → “We present the results.”
- Page 3: “We regard that” → “We see that”; consider italicizing “maximum accuracy clustering”; “Previous works” → “Previous work”; “ICM algorithm; It” → “ICM algorithm: it.”
- Page 4: “particularized” → “specialized.”
- Page 5: “plausible properties” → perhaps “discernible properties.”
- Page 6: “stuck to bad local minima” → “stuck in bad local minima.”
- Page 7: “This is contrastive to that” → “This is in contrast to the fact that.”
- Supplementary Material, page 2: “of these message” → “of these messages”; “a few number” → “a few.”

Recommendation
The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm deserves eventual publication—if this is indeed the first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization, it fills an important niche. However, in its current form the paper needs substantial work. The lack of clarity in the core derivation and the shortcomings in scholarship (positioning, citations, and evaluation choices) place an undue burden on readers and would reflect poorly on a venue that accepted it as is. I therefore recommend rejection at this time, with encouragement to revise along the lines above.
