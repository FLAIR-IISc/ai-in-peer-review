This paper studies Bayesian reconstruction of low-rank matrices from noisy observations and proposes an efficient approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm derived from belief propagation. The method accommodates additional structure on the factors (e.g., sparsity, nonnegativity) via flexible priors and introduces a temperature parameter β that unifies marginal posterior inference (β = 1) and MAP estimation (β → ∞). The authors further show that K-means–type clustering fits into their framework by imposing a one-hot constraint on factor V, yielding an AMP-based clustering algorithm that directly enforces single-cluster assignment per data point. Empirically, their methods outperform Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ in both reconstruction loss and clustering accuracy on synthetic and ORL face data.

Relation to prior work: AMP has been successful in compressed sensing and related bilinear problems; the rank-1 IterFac algorithm and an AMP algorithm for general rank have appeared, but the latter targets posterior means only. This paper extends AMP to handle MAP and marginal MAP via the β-parameterized posterior, and applies AMP to clustering with an explicit one-hot constraint—an appealing and, to my knowledge, novel instantiation in the low-rank reconstruction framework. Connections between low-rank factorization and K-means are well-known (e.g., the PCA-based constant-factor approximation for K-means), but the presented AMP formulation enables a direct, efficient alternative to Lloyd’s algorithm.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: A clean AMP derivation for general-rank matrix reconstruction that covers both posterior mean/marginal MAP and MAP estimation through a single parameterized family. The algorithm retains O(mN) per-iteration complexity and includes Onsager-like corrections absent in variational Bayes, with a fixed-point optimality property relative to ICM.
- Practical impact: The AMP-based K-means variant (AMP-KM) directly encodes one-hot assignments and empirically improves the K-means objective over Lloyd/K-means++, especially in high dimensions. The AMP-based maximum-accuracy variant (AMP-MA) also achieves superior clustering accuracy in synthetic experiments, with competitive or better convergence.
- Experimental evaluation: Comparisons include both reconstruction loss (Frobenius norm) and clustering accuracy, on synthetic data and a standard face dataset. Reporting both metrics is helpful, and the results consistently favor AMP variants.

Weaknesses
- Theory: The paper offers limited theoretical guarantees. Convergence of the AMP iterations is assumed but not established, and state-evolution analysis—central to understanding AMP performance—is deferred. While this is common in K-means–related work, it remains a gap here.
- Related theory in clustering: The manuscript would benefit from citing more results from theoretical computer science on provable algorithms/approximations for the K-means objective, to better contextualize the empirical improvements (e.g., works on approximation guarantees and seeding).

Quality
- The algorithmic derivation appears careful and consistent with the AMP literature, including discussion of the Bethe approximation and the role of Onsager terms. The fixed-point proposition for the MAP case provides a useful optimality interpretation relative to ICM. Experimental protocols are reasonable, with clear stopping criteria and multiple baselines. The main limitation is the absence of state-evolution predictions or formal convergence guarantees.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized, with a clear problem setup, precise notation, and explicit algorithms. The β-parameterization is a neat device that unifies inference tasks. Given space constraints, some technical details are relegated to the supplement; pointing readers to specific lemmas/theorems there (especially for the fixed-point result) would further aid clarity.

Originality
- Extending general-rank AMP to handle MAP via the β-trick and deploying it to K-means with a strict one-hot prior are, to my knowledge, novel and interesting. The formulation contrasts with variational Bayes factorizations by incorporating dependency structure through the Bethe approximation.

Significance
- Efficient, scalable factorization with structured priors is broadly relevant. The demonstrated gains in both K-means loss and clustering accuracy, together with low per-iteration cost, suggest the approach could be of practical interest. Stronger theoretical support (e.g., partial convergence conditions or state evolution) would further increase impact.

Pros for acceptance
- Novel AMP formulation covering both marginal and MAP inference for low-rank reconstruction.
- Principled and efficient AMP-based alternative to Lloyd’s K-means with consistent empirical gains.
- Clear algorithms and meaningful evaluation on synthetic and real data.
- Useful connection and comparison to variational Bayes/ICM, including a fixed-point optimality property.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited theoretical guarantees; convergence and state evolution not established within the paper.
- Missing citations to TCS literature on provable K-means algorithms to situate contributions more fully.

Overall assessment
A solid contribution that advances AMP for low-rank matrix reconstruction and delivers a practical AMP-based clustering method that improves on standard K-means baselines. The empirical results are promising; the main weakness is theoretical. I recommend acceptance if the authors can strengthen the discussion of guarantees (even partial) and add citations to relevant K-means theory.
