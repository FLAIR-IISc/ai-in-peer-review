This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for matrix factorization, motivated by a connection to clustering. The authors demonstrate how the derived AMP algorithm can be specialized for clustering tasks and provide experimental comparisons with k-means++. While the paper presents a clear overall approach, significant concerns regarding the mathematical rigor and novelty of the contributions hinder a positive assessment.

**Quality:** The mathematical derivations throughout the paper, and particularly in the appendix, are insufficiently detailed. Many non-trivial steps are omitted, making it difficult to fully assess the correctness of the proposed method. Specifically:

1.  In Appendix, line 139, the claim that the second term of equation (11) is O(1/√m) lacks justification. The role of β, introduced in Section 2, is unclear in this context.
2.  The asymptotic analysis in Section 3 appears to assume that *m* and *n* grow at similar rates. This assumption is not explicitly stated and may limit the generality of the results. The analysis is terse overall, raising concerns about its mathematical robustness.
3.  Line 167 states that *mτ<sub>w</sub><sup>2</sup>* is the expectation of *A<sub>il</sub><sup>2</sup>*, where *A* represents the observed data and *mτ<sub>w</sub><sup>2</sup>* is solely the variance of the likelihood. This approximation requires further justification, as the observed data inherently contains information beyond just the variance.
4.  The convergence behavior of the AMP algorithm when β is finite, and its relation to the maximum a posteriori (MAP) problem, is not adequately addressed. While AMP minimizes KL divergence, the interchange of limit and minimization operations (*lim<sub>β</sub> min KL = min lim<sub>β</sub> KL*) is not guaranteed and requires further investigation.
5.  The proof of Proposition 1 is too concise to allow for a thorough evaluation. The derivation of equation (17) is particularly unclear and requires further elaboration.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and the core idea – connecting matrix factorization to clustering through AMP – is readily understandable.

**Originality:** A central concern is whether the proposed algorithm is a novel application or merely a re-implementation of existing Expectation Propagation (EP) techniques. The authors primarily focus on estimating the first and second moments of the marginal posterior, a characteristic feature of EP. The work by Stern et al. (http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf) already demonstrates the application of EP to matrix factorization, and Appendix Section 1 resembles standard EP update procedures. The authors should clarify the distinct contributions of their work compared to established EP methods.

Furthermore, an intriguing connection appears to exist between the authors’ clustering algorithm and the original ‘clutter’ problem addressed in the foundational EP paper by Minka (http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf), particularly if the likelihood of the clutter problem is replaced with a uniform mixture of Gaussian distributions. Exploring this connection would provide valuable context and potentially highlight the novelty of their approach. (This is offered as a suggestion for further investigation, not a criticism of originality).

**Significance:** While the authors highlight the application to clustering, the comparison is limited to k-means++. Given the extensive body of literature on clustering algorithms, a more comprehensive evaluation against state-of-the-art methods is necessary to demonstrate the practical significance of the proposed approach. The lack of detailed proofs, even in the appendix, further complicates the assessment of the method’s technical merits. The potential overlap with existing EP work (Stern et al.) also casts doubt on the overall impact of the contribution.



**Overall Recommendation:**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but significant concerns regarding the mathematical rigor, clarity of derivations, and potential lack of novelty prevent a favorable recommendation in its current form. The authors should address the specific questions raised above, provide more detailed proofs, and clearly articulate the contributions of their work relative to existing EP methods. A more comprehensive experimental evaluation, particularly within the context of established clustering algorithms, is also recommended. I recommend rejection.
