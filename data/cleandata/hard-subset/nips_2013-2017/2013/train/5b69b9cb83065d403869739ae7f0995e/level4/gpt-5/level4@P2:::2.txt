Review of “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing”

Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) approach for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with flexible priors on the factors, enabling additional structure such as sparsity or one-hot constraints. A temperature-like parameter beta unifies marginal inference (beta=1) and MAP estimation (beta→infinity); the algorithm specializes to both. A key application is clustering: by enforcing one-hot rows in V, the method yields an AMP-based alternative to K-means and a maximum-accuracy clustering variant. Experiments on synthetic data and the ORL Faces dataset show improvements over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ and, for marginal inference, comparisons against variational Bayesian matrix factorization (VBMF).

The work builds on AMP for compressed sensing and prior bilinear AMP variants: IterFac for rank-one estimation, an AMP method for general rank that targets posterior means, and recent bilinear/generalized AMP results (dictionary learning, blind calibration). Compared to VBMF (Lim & Teh 2007; Raiko et al. 2007), the contribution is a BP/Bethe-based algorithm with Onsager-like corrections and a beta-parameterized pathway to MAP. The connection between matrix factorization and clustering is established in prior work (e.g., NMF-based clustering), but here the one-hot constraint is handled directly within the AMP framework.

Strengths
- Technical contribution: a clear AMP formulation for low-rank factorization that covers both posterior means and MAP via the beta parameter; an explicit K-means specialization with principled corrections to assignments.
- Practical impact: competitive empirical performance on clustering, especially in high dimensions and on the ORL dataset; fast convergence.
- Scope: flexible priors allow incorporating structural properties beyond low rank; the algorithm is O(mN) per iteration and nearly symmetric in U and V.
- Clarity in parts: the introduction, literature overview, and experiments are generally easy to follow.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Evaluation scope: the main empirical results focus on clustering framed as matrix factorization. This makes it hard to gauge performance on standard matrix completion/reconstruction benchmarks used in prior VBMF work (e.g., Netflix or comparable datasets). If such datasets are infeasible here, the paper should explain why and provide alternative, widely recognized baselines or tasks to demonstrate competitiveness.
- Novelty positioning: the paper initially reads as if the factorization–clustering linkage were novel. The abstract and Section 2.2 should be explicit that this connection is well known and should cite representative prior art. Likewise, expectation propagation (EP) should be cited and the relationship to the presented AMP variant discussed, since EP is a common lens for continuous BP approximations.
- Validation of approximations: the large-system approximations used to derive the algorithm (m,N→infinity with fixed rank) are not empirically probed; a brief outline of state evolution or targeted sanity checks would strengthen confidence. Some technical steps appear only in the supplement and deserve at least a concise outline in the main text.
- Presentation/notation: the algorithmic description is difficult to parse. Heavy use of hats, tildes, and iteration superscripts, and repeated 1/(m tau) factors obscure the structure. The near symmetry between the U and V updates is not highlighted, and some terms could be factored or combined to improve readability.

Quality
The proposed algorithm appears technically sound and well motivated by the Bethe/AMP framework. The MAP specialization and the K-means variant are natural and practically relevant. However, the lack of tests on standard reconstruction tasks and limited discussion of the asymptotic approximations leave open questions about general applicability and robustness.

Clarity
The intro and experiments are clear. The derivation being relegated to the supplement is a drawback. The algorithm section would benefit from:
- Explaining early that equation 5 is the negative log of equation 2 and that equation 8 is proportional to equation 2 raised to beta.
- Factoring common constants (e.g., 1/(m tau)) in equations 9a–9e, and combining redundant terms where possible.
- Explicitly stating that f is the mean of q and G is its beta-scaled covariance at the point of definition.
- Treating beta as a global parameter rather than decorating only f and G with beta subscripts; before the MAP limit, note how f and G change as beta→infinity.
- Briefly motivating the m factor in the noise variance, its role in scaling/state evolution, and its effect on row–column symmetry.

Originality
The AMP formulation for general rank with a unified beta path to MAP, and the direct handling of one-hot constraints within the AMP framework, are incremental but useful contributions. The clustering angle is novel within this AMP formulation, though the underlying factorization–clustering connection is not.

Significance
The results suggest practical gains for clustering, especially in high dimensions. Demonstrating performance on standard matrix reconstruction/completion datasets would materially increase the paper’s impact and help the community adopt the method beyond clustering.

Specific suggestions
- Cite prior work on clustering via matrix factorization (e.g., Xu et al. 2003; Ding et al. 2010) directly in the abstract/Section 2.2, and cite EP with a brief comparison to the proposed AMP.
- Mention belief propagation explicitly in the abstract.
- Clarify which VBMF implementation the comparison follows (Lim & Teh 2007 vs. Raiko et al. 2007), and add a citation near VBMF-MA in Section 5.
- Simplify notation: reduce hats where possible, avoid unnecessary iteration superscripts by using assignment arrows, and define at first use why N is capitalized and m is lowercase. If tildes denote column vectors, state this.
- In the supplement, clarify the step from equations 14 to 15 (big-O in m), rewrite equation 5 to separate exponentials for clarity, and explain how the message approximations relate to prior techniques.
- Language edits (examples): “the properties” (p.1), “according to the problem,” “has enough flexibility,” “motivate the use of,” “We present the results” (p.2), “We see that,” “Previous work,” “ICM algorithm: it,” “specialized,” “stuck in bad local minima,” “in contrast to the fact that.” The title could be “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.”

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros: interesting AMP formulation; practical clustering gains; efficient algorithm; clear motivation.
Cons: evaluation not aligned with standard factorization benchmarks; incomplete citation of known connections and EP; derivation and approximations insufficiently validated in the main text; heavy notation and presentation issues.

Recommendation
Promising contribution, but I recommend rejection in its current form. With stronger empirical validation on conventional reconstruction tasks, clearer positioning and citations, and improved exposition, the paper would be a solid candidate for acceptance.
