Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, leveraging Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing within a Bayesian framework. As detailed in the supplementary material, the algorithm incorporates two simplifying approximations, assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and compared to Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), a previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

Cons
There are several concerns regarding the paper's acceptance. Firstly, the algorithm's evaluation on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization) makes it challenging to extrapolate the experimental results to conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, the authors could have used the Netflix dataset, as in references Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen (2007), to evaluate their algorithm against predecessors. The use of BP, typically more accurate than Variational methods, raises doubts about its competitiveness when evaluated using a new criterion. The authors should explain why they did not use a standard dataset.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. Although new, it is not groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect is the introduction of approximations in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the input matrix dimensions. However, the validity of these approximations, described only in the supplementary material, is not directly tested, and their explanation could be clearer.

There are significant issues regarding the citation of prior work. The paper implies that the application of matrix factorization to clustering is an original contribution, which is not the case. The authors should acknowledge the connection as well-known or cite relevant references. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is commonly associated with applying BP to continuous classes of distributions, and its relationship to the paper's algorithm should be explained.

Pros
The paper presents a new and potentially useful algorithm, and the mathematics is generally followable. Although the evaluation criteria may raise suspicions, the K-means problem provides a fair comparison of the algorithms, demonstrating the benefits of the new algorithm in the experiments.

Clarity
The early presentation is clear, with a well-written introduction and summary of earlier work. However, the derivation of the algorithm, only provided in the supplementary material, is a drawback. The experiments section is clear, but the mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation. The notation is cumbersome, with an excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. The exposition could be improved by explaining the significance of certain equations and providing clearer definitions of functions.

Other issues include the use of a tilde to denote column vectors, which may not be standard, and the inconsistent capitalization of variables (N vs. m). In the supplementary material, the step from equation 14 to 15 is unclear, and the novelty of the algorithm's derivation should be clarified. The English is good, but there are some language issues, particularly with the use of articles.

Recommendations for improvement include:

* Using a standard dataset for evaluation
* Clarifying the citation of prior work
* Improving the notation and exposition
* Providing clearer definitions and explanations
* Addressing language issues
* Creating additional diagrams to aid understanding

The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm fills an important role as the first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper requires significant work before it can be accepted, primarily due to clarity and scholarship concerns that would reflect poorly on a conference that accepted it in its current state.
