This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for matrix factorization, shows that matrix factorization generalizes clustering, then specializes the algorithm to clustering. Experiments compare the proposed method to k-means++.

Quality: The mathematical development is too sketchy and omits many nontrivial steps, even in the appendix.

1) Appendix line 139: Why is the second term of (11) O(1/sqrt(m))? Also, the dependence on beta appears to be dropped in Section 2; please clarify where beta enters and how scaling arguments depend on it.

2) Several derivations (e.g., leading to (19)) seem to assume m and n grow at the same rate. If so, this assumption should be stated explicitly, and the implications for rectangular regimes (m/n -> 0 or infinity) should be discussed. Overall, the asymptotic analysis in this section is terse, and I am concerned about its rigor.

3) Line 167: Why is m * tau_w^2 used as E[A_{il}^2]? A is observed data; m * tau_w^2 is the variance of the likelihood term, not the second moment of A_{il}. Please justify this approximation, define the underlying generative model explicitly, and clarify whether mean contributions are neglected.

4) Convergence to MAP: How does the message passing algorithm for finite beta recover the MAP solution as beta -> infinity? The algorithm minimizes a KL divergence, but interchanging limit and minimization (lim_beta min KL_beta = min lim_beta KL_beta) is not generally valid. Please provide a rigorous argument or conditions under which the limit yields the MAP fixed points.

5) Proposition 1: The proof is too brief. In particular, how is (17) derived? Please include the missing steps or a self-contained sketch that makes the argument verifiable.

Clarity: The paper is well structured and the main contribution is easy to follow. However, readability suffers where key steps are skipped and notation (e.g., beta) is not consistently tracked.

Originality: The algorithm appears to estimate first and second moments of marginal posteriors, which suggests it may be an instance of Expectation Propagation (EP) applied to matrix factorization. EP has been used for matrix factorization before; see Stern et al., “Matchbox: Large Scale Online Bayesian Recommendations,” WWW 2009 (http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf). Section 1 of the Appendix reads like standard EP updates; if there are substantive deviations, please highlight and justify them.

There also seems to be a connection between the proposed clustering specialization and the “clutter” problem in Minka’s original EP paper (http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf), especially if the clutter likelihood is replaced by a uniform mixture of Gaussians. I offer this as a potentially interesting link rather than a challenge to originality.

Significance: The empirical evaluation focuses on clustering but compares only to k-means++, which is insufficient given the breadth of the clustering literature. Stronger baselines (e.g., Gaussian mixture models with EM, spectral clustering, agglomerative methods, DP-means, modern initialization variants) would better establish practical utility. Because many proofs are sketchy, it is difficult to assess technical correctness. Finally, if the method is essentially EP for matrix factorization, prior work (e.g., Stern et al.) may limit the novelty unless clear methodological differences and advantages are demonstrated.
