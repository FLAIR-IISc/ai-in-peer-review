Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction under additive Gaussian noise with flexible priors on the factors. Building on belief propagation, the authors derive an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm that handles general priors and introduces a “temperature” parameter β to unify posterior mean/marginal MAP estimation (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞). They then show that K-means–type clustering can be cast as a special case of the reconstruction problem, with a one-hot constraint on V, and derive an AMP variant for clustering. Empirically, they report improved K-means loss (and, for the marginalization-based variant, improved accuracy) over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ on synthetic data, and competitive performance on the ORL faces dataset.

This work builds on prior AMP developments in compressed sensing (Donoho et al., Bayati & Montanari) and their bilinear extensions, including IterFac (rank-one case), the rank-r AMP of Matsushita & Tanaka (2012), and concurrent bilinear generalized AMP (biG-AMP; Parker et al., 2013) and dictionary-learning AMP (Krzakala et al., 2013). The paper positions its novelty as enabling MAP (not just posterior means) via the β trick and emphasizing the clustering application, where the one-hot constraint is treated directly.

Strengths:
- Clearly motivated formulation that accommodates structural priors beyond low rank, and a unified view of marginalization and MAP via the β-parameterized posterior.
- A practical AMP algorithm with per-iteration O(mN) cost and Onsager corrections that distinguish it from variational Bayes/ICM.
- Interesting application to clustering that embeds the one-hot assignment constraint into a bilinear AMP framework and yields an interpretable correction to standard K-means updates.
- Empirical results on synthetic data are favorable, with improved K-means loss and accuracy over K-means/K-means++ and a VB baseline; convergence is fast in practice.

Weaknesses and detailed comments (Quality and clarity):
The technical development is terse, with several nontrivial steps relegated to the supplement or omitted. Specific points that require clarification and/or stronger justification:

1) Appendix, around line 139: Why is the second term of (11) O(1/√m)? Please make the scaling explicit and ensure β’s role is consistently handled (β seems to be omitted in parts of Section 2). Clarify the asymptotic regime and normalization choices that yield this order.

2) Assumption on aspect ratio: Several derivations (e.g., around (19)) appear to rely on m and N growing to infinity at comparable rates. Please state the precise large-system limit (e.g., m,N→∞ with N/m→α, fixed rank r) and what breaks if the aspect ratio is extreme. Some steps read as heuristic; a more careful statement of conditions would help assess rigor.

3) Approximation E[Ail^2] ≈ mτw^2 (Appendix, line ~167): A is observed, not its expectation under the likelihood; replacing Ail^2 by its variance term mτ requires justification. If this is a state-evolution-motivated substitution, please say so and explain when it is accurate. In practice, you propose estimating τ via residuals; connect this empirical step to the theoretical approximation.

4) β→∞ and MAP: The algorithm for finite β is motivated via Bethe free energy minimization/KL projections, but it is not clear that taking β→∞ commutes with the fixed-point characterization. Could you provide conditions under which limβ→∞ of the algorithm’s fixed points coincide with MAP stationary points, or at least a heuristic justification (e.g., Laplace arguments for q̂, stability of Onsager terms) and empirical evidence? As noted, in general limβ min KL ≠ min limβ KL.

5) Proposition 1: The proof sketch is too brief. In particular, please derive equation (17) in detail and justify the positivity assumptions on ∑i S∞i and ∑j T∞j. Since G is a scaled covariance for β<∞, positivity is clear there; for MAP, explain the limiting argument G→G∞ and provide precise conditions under which Λ−H is positive semidefinite.

6) Convergence and damping: AMP can diverge without damping or step-size control, especially off-model. Please discuss practical convergence strategies (damping, denoiser smoothing) and whether they were needed in experiments. Also, clarify the observed oscillations in AMP-KM assignments and the chosen stopping rule.

7) Notation and completeness: A few definitions (e.g., precise domain of f and G, dimensions of Λu,Λv in the clustering specialization) could be made more explicit. Since state evolution is not presented, a brief summary of expected performance regimes would help the reader.

Originality:
The derivation is in the AMP tradition rather than classical variational methods. However, there appears to be a close connection to expectation propagation (EP). The update structure in Appendix Section 1 resembles EP moment-matching with Gaussian site approximations; see, for example, the EP matrix factorization of Stern, Herbrich, and Graepel (WWW 2009). Please clarify the relationship between your AMP (with Onsager corrections) and EP: what is identical, what differs (e.g., the origin and role of the Onsager terms), and when the two coincide. It would also be useful to relate your clustering specialization to the “clutter problem” example in Minka (UAI 2001): if the likelihood is a uniform mixture of Gaussians with one-hot latent variables, is the proposed AMP-KM equivalent to a particular EP limit?

Novelty relative to bilinear AMP variants (IterFac, Matsushita & Tanaka 2012; Parker et al. 2013; Krzakala et al. 2013) should also be sharpened. You emphasize MAP estimation via β as a key contribution; please make precise which previous bilinear AMP works cannot handle MAP, and why your extension is nontrivial.

Significance:
The clustering application is compelling, and the empirical gains on synthetic data are promising. On real data, you compare AMP-KM to K-means++ and show modest improvements on ORL. To strengthen practical impact, consider:
- Comparisons to broader clustering baselines (GMM/EM with diagonal/full covariances, spectral clustering, NMF-based clustering).
- Sensitivity to mis-specified priors and to rank/cluster-number mismatch.
- Runtime comparisons and scalability plots.
- Additional datasets beyond ORL.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid and timely contribution within the AMP literature; unifies marginalization and MAP via β.
- Principled treatment of one-hot constraints for clustering; interpretable corrections to K-means assignments.
- Empirically competitive with strong baselines on synthetic data; simple O(mN) iterations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Technical derivations are sketchy; key steps (scaling, β→∞ limit, Proposition 1) need clearer justification.
- Limited empirical scope on real data and baselines.
- Relationship to EP-based matrix factorization and to concurrent bilinear AMP work is insufficiently clarified, leaving novelty somewhat ambiguous.

Recommendation and suggestions:
I am borderline leaning toward rejection in the current form due to the gaps in analysis and limited empirical breadth, but I see potential. To improve the paper:
- Expand the supplement to fully derive the key equations (including (17)) and carefully state the asymptotic regime and assumptions.
- Provide a dedicated section comparing AMP vs EP (and VB), highlighting the role of Onsager terms and when the methods differ in fixed points and performance.
- Justify the β→∞ MAP limit more convincingly and discuss convergence safeguards.
- Broaden the empirical evaluation and report runtimes.

Overall, this is an interesting application of AMP to structured low-rank reconstruction and clustering; with stronger theoretical exposition and broader experiments, it could make a solid contribution.
