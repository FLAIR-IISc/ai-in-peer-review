Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering" 

This paper contributes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates simplifying approximations derived under assumptions of large matrix dimensions. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel manner by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing performance against Lloyd's K-means algorithm, as well as Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF). 

Cons 

There are arguments against acceptance. Evaluating the algorithm on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization) makes it difficult to extrapolate experimental results to conventional matrix reconstruction scenarios. For instance, references [1, 2] (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen, 2007) cite the Netflix dataset for evaluating VBMF. It would be ideal if the present paper used the same dataset. While BP is typically more accurate than Variational methods, evaluating this BP variant using a novel criterion introduces doubt regarding its true competitiveness. If using Netflix or similar datasets is unfeasible, the authors should explain why. 

The algorithm is a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. Although novel, it isn’t groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect lies in the approximations derived in the limit $m, N \to \infty$, described in the supplementary material. However, the validity of these approximations isn’t directly tested, and their explanation could be clearer. 

A significant issue is the citation of prior work. The paper initially gives the impression of introducing matrix factorization for clustering as an original contribution in the abstract and Section 2.2, which is misleading given this connection is well-established. The paper should clearly acknowledge existing work, either by citing relevant references or explicitly stating the concept’s prior known status. 

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it's the standard term for applying BP to continuous classes of distributions, and the relationship between EP and the paper’s algorithm should be explained. Relatedly, while the algorithm is most naturally understood as an application of Belief Propagation, this fact isn’t mentioned until Section 4.1 and should be included in the abstract. 

Pros 

The paper is interesting and presents a potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is comprehensible, and, while not manually verified, appears sound. Despite reservations about the evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may be sufficiently general for a fair comparison, and the experiments demonstrate a benefit for the new algorithm. 

Clarity 

The early presentation is relatively clear, with a well-written introduction and summary of previous work. The derivation of the algorithm being relegated to the Supplementary Material is a drawback; outlining it in the main paper would improve accessibility. The experiments section is clear, though some plots are difficult to interpret in grayscale. 

I found the algorithmic description difficult to follow and was unable to verify the derivation. The mathematics is the weakest aspect of the presentation. Even superficially, it’s difficult to parse. For example, the factors of $1 / m\tau$ in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out for improved readability, and the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined. 

There’s an overuse of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, $\tau_w$ is used without a corresponding $\tau$; replacing $\tau_w$ with $\tau$ would suffice. Dropping the hats entirely throughout Section 4 would enhance clarity – a statement like “We’ll be dropping the hats for brevity” at the section’s start would explain this choice. The ‘t’ superscripts in the messages appear unnecessary; using “\gets” instead of “=” would allow their removal. I doubt the algorithm was originally derived using such cumbersome notation. Revisiting the initial notation might reveal a simpler representation. 

The exposition could be improved: explicitly stating that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, and that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$, would be beneficial. Algorithm 2's near row-column symmetry should be noted, and the algorithm’s presentation could be halved by suggesting copying and pasting equations while switching ‘u’ and ‘v’. 

The meaning of functions in Section 10 should be explained near their definition. Saying “$f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$” would be clearer than providing an equation, though including an equation alongside that explanation would be ideal. The scaled covariance matrix aspect of $G_\beta(b, \Lambda; p)$ isn’t immediately obvious, and wasn’t mentioned at its definition. 

The parameter $\beta$ is global, but its subscript appears inconsistently in messages. Eliminating these subscripts, even from $q$, would simplify the notation. Before equations 15 and 16, stating “in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, f and G take the following form:” would be helpful. 

Deeper questions remain regarding the algorithm. What is the significance of the m factor in the noise variance? Does it relate to applications of matrix factorization or the approximation used for the derivation? Does it solely disrupt row-column symmetry? The authors should address these points. 

Other questions: 
Is a tilde conventionally used to denote column vectors?
Why is N capitalized but m lowercase? 
Section 5 would benefit from a citation alongside VBMF-MA to indicate the primary reference for the competing algorithm’s implementation. The two existing citations for variational matrix factorization are insufficient to determine which is intended. 

Supplementary Material: 
For equation 5, using product notation for the exponential terms would clarify the pdf representation. In Section 2, the step from equation 14 to 15 regarding the big-O of m terms was unclear. 
The statement “a few number” should be “a few.”
I appreciated the diagram in Figure 1 of the Supplementary Material. More diagrams would enhance the paper. 

Overall 

The mathematics is interesting and the algorithm warrants publication. However, the paper requires substantial revision to address issues of clarity and scholarly rigor, lessening the burden on readers and ensuring a high standard for a prestigious conference. The English is generally good, though occasional awkward phrasing distracts. A list of specific suggestions is included throughout the review.
