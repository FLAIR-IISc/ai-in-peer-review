

The authors propose an algorithm for reconstructing low-rank matrices under noisy observations, leveraging additional structural assumptions on the factor matrices. Their approach employs Approximate Message Passing (AMP) to accelerate classical Bayesian methods, which are typically computationally prohibitive. A notable contribution is the connection drawn between matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, offering a meaningful application context for their framework.  

To the best of my knowledge, the AMP-based methodology for this matrix reconstruction problem is novel and well-motivated. While the relationship between low-rank factorizations and K-means is well-established (e.g., PCA yields a factor-2 approximation for K-means), this link enables the authors to conduct a thorough experimental evaluation. They benchmark their algorithm against K-means and K-means++, demonstrating superior speed and efficiency in empirical results. Crucially, their evaluation assesses both the Frobenius norm residual and the quality of the resulting clusterings—a comprehensive approach that strengthens their claims.  

The primary limitation lies in the limited theoretical guarantees regarding convergence. While this gap is common among K-means algorithms, the authors could bolster their work by citing recent theoretical computer science literature on provably accurate K-means solvers (e.g., works by Kumar et al. or Ostrovsky et al.). A more extensive discussion of existing theoretical foundations would better contextualize their algorithm’s practical performance.  

Overall, this is a valuable contribution: the AMP framework provides a computationally efficient path for low-rank matrix reconstruction, and the K-means connection offers a compelling empirical validation. The experimental results are promising, particularly the efficiency gains over standard clustering methods. However, the theoretical foundation requires further development to match the strength of the empirical findings. The paper presents a solid foundation for future work, but the current theoretical shortcomings prevent it from being fully compelling.
