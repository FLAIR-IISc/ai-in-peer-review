Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction under flexible structural priors on the factors. The authors introduce a one-parameter family of posteriors p̂(U,V|A; β) that interpolates between standard Bayesian inference (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞), derive an AMP algorithm for this family, and claim it extends previous AMP methods that handle only posterior means to also handle MAP and marginal-MAP estimators. They then specialize the framework to clustering by encoding the one-hot assignment constraint in the prior on V, yielding an AMP variant of K-means that adjusts assignments using Onsager-like corrections depending on current cluster sizes and self-bias. Experiments compare the proposed algorithms to Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and a variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF) approach on synthetic data and the ORL faces dataset, showing improved K-means objective values and, for maximum-accuracy clustering, higher assignment accuracy and fast convergence.

This work connects to: AMP for compressed sensing and bilinear models (e.g., IterFac for rank-one, bilinear GAMP), variational Bayes approaches to matrix factorization, and prior uses of low-rank/NMF for clustering. The single-assignment constraint is enforced directly here, which differs from standard relaxations in NMF-based clustering.

Strengths:
- Unifies posterior-mean and MAP-style objectives via the β-parameterized posterior and provides a coherent AMP algorithm applicable across priors, including discrete ones for clustering.
- The clustering specialization is elegant and gives a principled correction to Lloyd’s steps that accounts for bias and cluster sizes.
- Empirical results show promising improvements over K-means/K-means++ on synthetic data and ORL, with reasonable iteration counts.

Quality (technical correctness):
While the overall approach is interesting, several derivations are too terse, even in the appendix, making it hard to verify correctness.

1) Appendix, line 139: Why is the second term of Eq. (11) O(1/√m)? Please show the scaling explicitly, including how β enters. Relatedly, β seems to be omitted in Section 2 at a few points—please reconcile the notation and scaling consistently.

2) Several steps (e.g., derivation of Eq. (19)) appear to assume m and n grow at the same rate. Please state this assumption clearly at the outset and justify any limits used. As written, the asymptotic arguments are terse; more details are needed for mathematical rigor.

3) Appendix, line 167: Why is m τ_w^2 the expectation of A_{iℓ}^2? A is observed and has signal plus noise; m τ_w^2 reflects the noise variance term in the likelihood, not the total variance of A. If you are replacing empirical second moments with noise variance, please justify the approximation and state the conditions (e.g., large m, concentration around the noise level after subtracting the signal estimate).

4) Convergence to MAP as β→∞: The message passing algorithm is motivated by minimizing a Bethe/KL objective, but in general lim_{β→∞} min KL ≠ min lim_{β→∞} KL. Please provide a careful argument (or conditions) under which the β-continuation yields the MAP fixed points of the limiting problem, and clarify what is guaranteed about the limit points (e.g., correspondence to stationary points of CMAP, not necessarily global optima).

5) Proposition 1: The proof sketch is too brief. In particular, how is Eq. (17) derived? Please expand the derivation leading to the surrogate minimization with the quadratic “negative metric” penalty and precisely state the assumptions under which Σ_i S_i and Σ_j T_j are positive semidefinite in the β→∞ limit (e.g., via G∞ and the Hessian identity).

Additional technical points:
- State evolution: You state it can be provided but omit it due to space. Given the centrality of AMP, even a concise state evolution statement with references and a discussion of when it applies (random A? independence assumptions on U,V?) would strengthen the paper; otherwise more empirical validation of predicted scaling and iteration counts is advisable.
- Complexity: Per-iteration cost is O(mN), but f and G require r̂-dimensional integrations (β<∞) or optimizations (β→∞). Please discuss practical scalability with r̂, and any use of damping/line-search that may be needed for stability.
- Estimation of τ in clustering: You propose τ̂_t = m^{-2} N^{-1} ||A − U^t (V^t)^⊤||_F^2. Please justify this estimator within your scaling and show robustness to mis-specification.

Clarity:
The paper is well-structured and the main idea is easy to follow. However, clarity would improve by:
- Ensuring β is introduced consistently and not omitted where it affects scaling.
- Making the m,N→∞ assumptions explicit early and used consistently.
- Expanding the appendix derivations to include the nontrivial steps cited above and defining all symbols at first use (e.g., τ_w).
- Explicitly stating when priors may be improper and the conditions for posterior propriety.

Originality:
The algorithm appears closely related to Expectation Propagation (EP) applied to matrix factorization, since you propagate (approximate) first and second moments of marginal posteriors and use Bethe-style corrections. EP has been used for matrix factorization (e.g., Stern et al., “Matchbox” recommender system; see http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf). Section 1 of your appendix looks like standard EP updates. Please clarify precisely how your AMP updates differ from EP (e.g., Onsager terms derived from dense-graph limits vs. moment matching alone), and from recent bilinear AMP methods (IterFac, and bilinear GAMP by Parker et al., arXiv:1310.2632). For clustering, there also seems to be a connection to Minka’s clutter problem in EP (if the likelihood is a uniform mixture of Gaussians); clarifying similarities and differences would help position the contribution.

Significance:
The clustering application is interesting, but the empirical scope is limited. Comparing only to Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ (plus VBMF-MA) may not suffice given the large clustering literature. Consider adding baselines such as EM for Gaussian mixtures (with tied/spherical covariance), spectral clustering, spherical K-means, and NMF-based clustering. Also:
- Report sensitivity to initialization and to mis-specified r̂ and priors (you note r̂ may differ from r).
- Provide runtimes, not just iteration counts.
- Discuss the two ORL runs where AMP collapsed to a single cluster, and whether damping or alternative τ estimation prevents this.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean unification of posterior mean, marginal-MAP, and MAP within an AMP framework.
- Direct enforcement of single-assignment constraints for clustering with principled corrections.
- Promising empirical improvements over K-means/K-means++ with few iterations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Key derivations are sketchy; several steps and assumptions are not justified.
- Novelty relative to EP and existing bilinear AMP needs clearer differentiation.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow; stronger baselines and robustness analyses are needed.
