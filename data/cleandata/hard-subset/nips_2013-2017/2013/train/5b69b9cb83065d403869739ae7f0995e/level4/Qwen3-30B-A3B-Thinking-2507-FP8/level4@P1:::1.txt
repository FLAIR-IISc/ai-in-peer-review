

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural constraints on the factors, and applies it to clustering via reformulation as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem. The authors formulate the problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing them to incorporate various structural properties (e.g., sparsity, non-negativity) of the factor matrices through appropriate prior distributions. The AMP approach provides a computationally efficient alternative to existing methods like variational Bayes, which typically require more expensive optimization procedures. The paper demonstrates that this approach can be applied to K-means clustering by reformulating the clustering objective as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with specific structural constraints on the factor matrices. The authors claim that their AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard Lloyd's K-means and K-means++ in both synthetic and real-world experiments.

The paper makes two primary contributions: (1) an AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation problems in a unified framework, and (2) an application of this algorithm to clustering, where they claim to be the first to directly handle the constraint that each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster within the low-rank matrix reconstruction framework. The authors compare their approach to existing methods, including variational Bayes matrix factorization and Lloyd's K-means, and demonstrate through extensive experiments that their method achieves better clustering accuracy and lower reconstruction error while converging faster than competing approaches.

In terms of quality, the paper presents a technically sound approach that builds on previous work in AMP algorithms for compressed sensing and low-rank matrix factorization. The authors provide a clear derivation of their AMP algorithm, including the necessary theoretical foundation and convergence analysis. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, using both synthetic data (with controlled parameters) and the ORL face database to validate their approach. The results consistently show that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard K-means methods in terms of both reconstruction error (Frobenius norm) and clustering accuracy.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, with well-organized sections that logically progress from problem formulation to algorithm derivation and experimental validation. The authors effectively use equations to convey the mathematical details of their approach, though some technical sections (particularly in the supplementary material) could benefit from additional intuition to help readers grasp the key insights more easily. The paper adequately references previous work, including the seminal AMP papers by Donoho et al. and the connection to low-rank matrix factorization for clustering.

The originality of the paper is somewhat limited by the fact that the connection between low-rank matrix factorization and clustering is well-established (e.g., PCA-based approaches to K-means). However, the paper's novel contribution lies in the AMP-based implementation of this connection, particularly in handling the exact cluster assignment constraint directly within the low-rank framework. The authors properly acknowledge related work, including the use of matrix factorization for clustering in [10, 11], and clearly articulate how their approach differs from existing methods.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through the experimental results, which show clear advantages of the proposed method over standard clustering approaches. The AMP algorithm provides a computationally efficient alternative to traditional clustering algorithms, with faster convergence rates in practice. The ability to incorporate structural priors (like sparsity) through the Bayesian framework represents a useful extension to existing clustering methods. The paper addresses an important problem in unsupervised learning with practical applications in high-dimensional data analysis.

Strengths:
- Clear technical contribution of extending AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation with both synthetic and real data
- Demonstrated advantages over standard K-means methods in terms of accuracy and convergence speed
- Well-organized presentation of the mathematical framework
- Appropriate comparison to relevant prior work

Weaknesses:
- The theoretical guarantees for convergence of the AMP algorithm are limited, which is a notable gap given the paper's claims about efficiency and performance
- The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, while useful, is not entirely novel (though the AMP implementation is)
- The paper would benefit from more discussion of when the AMP approach might fail or be less effective compared to other methods
- Some aspects of the algorithm derivation (particularly in the supplementary material) could be made more accessible to a broader audience

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering. The AMP-based approach provides a computationally efficient method that outperforms existing techniques in the experimental evaluation. While the theoretical guarantees could be strengthened, the practical advantages demonstrated through extensive experiments make this a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
