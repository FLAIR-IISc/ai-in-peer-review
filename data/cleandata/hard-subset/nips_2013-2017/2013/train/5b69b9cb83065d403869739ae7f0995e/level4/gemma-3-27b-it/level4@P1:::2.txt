Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering" 

This paper contributes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates simplifying approximations based on the large-scale limits of the input matrix dimensions. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel manner against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem, and is also compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only previously published message-passing reconstruction algorithm. 

**Cons**

There are arguments against acceptance. Evaluating the new algorithm on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization) makes it difficult to extrapolate the experimental results to predict performance on conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. For instance, the citations for VBMF, Lin and Teh (2007); and Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen (2007), both utilize the Netflix dataset for evaluation. Ideally, the present paper would employ the same dataset for a fairer comparison. While BP is generally more accurate than Variational methods, evaluating this BP variant using a novel criterion raises doubts about its actual competitiveness. If using Netflix or a similar dataset is infeasible, the authors should clearly explain why.

The algorithm itself appears as a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While novel, it isn’t groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect lies in the approximations introduced in the large-dimensional limits ($N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$), but the validity of these approximations – described only in the supplementary material – is never directly tested and could be explained more clearly.

There are issues regarding the citation of prior work. Initially, the abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that the application of matrix factorization to clustering is an original contribution. This was misleading, as the connection is well-established. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this existing work, either by citing relevant references or stating that the connection is well-known. 

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the commonly used term for applying BP to continuous classes of distributions, and the relationship between EP and the paper’s algorithm should be discussed. Relatedly, the algorithm is fundamentally a Belief Propagation method, but this isn't mentioned until Section 4.1. It should be stated in the abstract.

**Pros**

The paper is interesting and presents a new, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is comprehensible, and while I did not replicate it by hand, it seemed feasible to do so. Despite reservations about the new evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may be sufficiently general to provide a valid comparison, and the experiments demonstrate a benefit for the proposed algorithm.

**Clarity**

The early presentation is relatively easy to follow. The introduction and summary of previous work are clear. However, relegating the derivation of the algorithm to the Supplementary Material is a drawback; outlining it in the main paper would be preferable. The experiments section is clear, although some plots appear poorly in black and white, they remain readable.

I found the description of the algorithm challenging and was unable to verify the derivation. The mathematics is the weakest aspect of the presentation. Even superficially, it’s difficult to parse. For example, the factors of $1 / m\tau$ in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out for better readability, and the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.

There is an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, $\tau_w$ appears without a corresponding $\tau$; replacing $\tau_w$ with $\tau$ would simplify the notation. Furthermore, Section 4 could benefit from stating, “We will be dropping the hats for brevity.” The 't' superscripts on the messages seem unnecessary. Using “$\gets$” instead of “=” would allow for their removal. I suspect the algorithm was originally developed using simpler notation, and revisiting that initial formulation is recommended.

The exposition could be improved. Explicitly stating that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, and equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$, would enhance understanding. Algorithm 2’s approximate row-column symmetry should be highlighted, and the algorithm's description could be halved by noting the symmetry and stating, "then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v."

The meaning of functions defined in equation (10) should be explained near their definition. Saying “$f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$” would be clearer than presenting an equation; if an equation is used, its meaning should be explained. At the end of Section 4.2, stating that $G_\beta(b, \Lambda; p)$ is a scaled covariance matrix of $q_\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)$ is helpful, but this wasn't apparent at its initial definition.

The parameter $\beta$ is global and doesn't require subscripts on the messages. Before equations (15) and (16), a statement like “in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, f and G take the following form:” would be helpful. 

On a deeper level, questions remain about the algorithm. What is the significance of the m factor in the additive noise variance? Does it relate to applications of matrix factorization or the approximation used in the algorithm’s derivation? Does it cause the asymmetry in the algorithm? The authors should address these questions.

Other points:

* Is a tilde conventionally used to denote column vectors?
* Why is N capitalized while m is lowercase?
* In Section 5, a citation after VBMF-MA indicating the primary reference for its implementation is needed. The two earlier citations for variational matrix factorization are unclear.

**Supplementary Material**

* In equation 5, using a product of exponentials would clarify the probability density function.
* In Section 2, the step from equation 14 to 15 regarding the big-O notation of $m$ terms was difficult to follow.
* The phrase "a few number" should be "a few".
* A diagram like the one in Figure 1 is helpful; more diagrams would be beneficial.

**Overall**

The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm merits publication as the first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper requires substantial revision to improve clarity and scholarship before acceptance. Its current state places an unacceptable burden on readers, which would reflect poorly on the conference.
