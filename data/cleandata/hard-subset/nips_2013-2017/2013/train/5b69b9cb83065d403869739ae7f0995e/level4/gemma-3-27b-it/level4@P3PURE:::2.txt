Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper contributes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, as described in the Supplementary Material, incorporates two simplifying approximations, based on assuming a large number of rows and columns, respectively, in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel manner against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem. It is also compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

**Cons**

There are several arguments against accepting the paper in its current form. Evaluating a new algorithm on a non-standard problem – clustering encoded as matrix factorization – makes it difficult to extrapolate the experimental results and predict its performance on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, the cited references for VBMF, Lin and Teh (2007); and Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen (2007), both use the Netflix dataset for evaluation. It would be ideal if the present paper used the same dataset. While BP is generally more accurate than Variational methods, evaluating this specific BP variant using a novel criterion casts doubt on its true competitiveness. Even if Netflix or a similar dataset is unusable, the authors should clearly explain why.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While novel, it is not particularly groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect of the work, to me, lies in the approximations introduced in the limits $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ are the dimensions of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, detailed only in the supplementary material, is never directly tested and could benefit from clearer explanation.

Significant concerns exist regarding the citation of prior work. Initially, the paper led me to believe it was introducing matrix factorization for clustering as an original contribution, an impression created by the abstract and Section 2.2. I felt misled upon learning from other reviewers that this connection is well-known. There is no justification for failing to acknowledge this prior work; if it is widely known, it should be stated as such. Otherwise, appropriate references should be included.

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as that is the commonly used term for applying BP to continuous classes of distributions, and the relationship between EP and the paper’s algorithm should be discussed. Relatedly, the core algorithm is most accurately described as an application of Belief Propagation, but this is not mentioned until Section 4.1. This should be stated in the abstract.

**Pros**

The paper is interesting and presents a new, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally accessible, and while I did not replicate the derivations by hand, I believe it would be possible to do so. Despite justified skepticism regarding the novel evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may be sufficiently general to provide a fair comparison, and the experiments do demonstrate a benefit for the proposed algorithm.

**Clarity**

The initial presentation is reasonably easy to follow, with a clear introduction and summary of prior work. However, relegating the algorithm’s derivation to the Supplemental Material is a significant drawback. Outlining the derivation in the main paper would be highly beneficial. The experiments section is clear, and while some plots appear poorly in black and white, they remain readable.

I found the algorithm’s description difficult to understand and was unable to verify the correctness of the derivation. The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect of the paper. Even superficially, it is challenging to parse. For example, the factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ preceding each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out to improve readability. Additionally, the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.

There is an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, $\tauw$ is used without a corresponding $\tau$; simply replacing $\tauw$ with $\tau$ would suffice. Moreover, Section 4, the most important part of the paper, contains only $\hat{p}, \hat{q},$ and $\hat{r}$ – why not state at the beginning of the section, “We will be dropping the hats here for brevity”? The ‘t’ superscripts in the messages appear wholly unnecessary. Changing “=” to “\gets” on the left-hand side eliminates the need for all the ‘t’s. It is difficult to imagine the authors originally derived the algorithm using such cumbersome notation. Revisiting the initial notation and simplifying it is strongly recommended.

The exposition could be improved. Explaining that equation 5 represents the negative logarithm of equation 2, or that equation 8 is simply equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$, would enhance understanding. Algorithm 2 exhibits near row-column symmetry; pointing this out and reducing its length by stating, "then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v," would be beneficial. The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definition. Saying “$f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$” would be clearer than providing only the equation. Similarly, at the end of Section 4.2, stating “$G_\beta(b, \Lambda; p)$ is a scaled covariance matrix of $q_\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)$” would be more helpful, as this relationship was not immediately obvious or mentioned at the definition of G. 

None of the messages contain $\beta$ subscripts, despite their dependence on f and G, which do. However, f and G do not directly depend on $\beta$, only on $q_\beta$. Eliminating the subscripts entirely, even from q, would be advisable. The parameter $\beta$ is simply a global variable, which is perfectly acceptable. Before equations (15) and (16), a statement like “in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, f and G take the following form:” would be useful.

On a deeper level, several aspects of the algorithm require further clarification. What is the significance of the m factor in the additive noise variance? Does it play a role in applications of matrix factorization? Does it relate to the approximations used to derive the algorithm? It appears to be the only element preventing row-column symmetry; is this true? The authors likely know the answers to these questions, but do not discuss them.

**Other Questions:**

Is using a tilde to denote column vectors standard practice? I initially did not understand this.
Why is N capitalized, while m is lowercase?
In Section 5, a citation following VBMF-MA would be helpful to identify the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competing algorithm. The two existing citations for variational matrix factorization are unclear in their intended relevance (or if both are intended).
In the Supplementary Material, equation 5 would benefit from a product of exponentials to more clearly represent the probability density function. I encountered difficulty with the step from equation 14 to 15 in Section 2 regarding the big-O notation of m terms. I may need to consider it further, but this is where I became stuck.
Additionally, in the Supplementary Material, the text on page 2 states, “A technique used in ... is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without referencing these publications, it is unclear how the approximation described below relates to existing work. Clarifying the novelty of the algorithm’s derivation within the text, and even in the main paper, would be beneficial.

The English is generally good, and the meaning is always clear, but there are instances where the use of articles feels unnatural. This can be distracting for some readers. Even the title could be improved – something like "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm" would be more descriptive. I will provide a list of specific suggestions to the authors separately. 

The Supplementary Material also contains some language issues, such as “of these message” instead of “of these messages” and “a few number” instead of “a few”. I found the diagram in Figure 1 of the Supplementary Material very helpful; including more diagrams would enhance the paper.

The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm warrants publication as a first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization, filling an important role. However, the paper requires substantial revision before acceptance. The lack of clarity and scholarship places an unacceptable burden on readers, which would reflect negatively on the conference.
