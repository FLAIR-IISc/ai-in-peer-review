This paper presents an algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The problem is formulated within a Bayesian framework, which allows for the flexible incorporation of structural priors on the factor matrices, such as sparsity or non-negativity. The paper's primary contributions are twofold: first, it extends a previous AMP algorithm for general-rank matrix factorization to perform Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation, in addition to posterior mean estimation. Second, it applies this algorithm to the problem of K-means clustering by reformulating it as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction where one of the factor matrices is constrained to represent one-hot encoded cluster assignments. Numerical experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets suggest that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms standard methods like Lloyd's K-means and K-means++.

**Quality and Clarity**

The paper is well-structured and the main ideas are presented clearly. The connection drawn between low-rank matrix factorization and clustering is insightful, and the overall narrative is easy to follow.

However, the paper's main weakness is its lack of technical rigor. Many of the mathematical derivations and proofs are overly brief and omit crucial steps, making it difficult for a reviewer to verify the technical correctness of the claims. The derivation of the AMP algorithm relies on an asymptotic analysis (in the limit of large matrix dimensions `m` and `N`) that is not formally presented or justified. The proofs, such as that of Proposition 1, are sketched rather than detailed. This lack of detail undermines confidence in the theoretical foundations of the proposed algorithm.

**Originality**

The application of an AMP-style algorithm to the K-means clustering problem appears to be a novel contribution. However, the originality of the general matrix factorization algorithm itself is a significant concern. The derived update equations are highly similar to those obtained from Expectation Propagation (EP), a well-established method for approximate Bayesian inference. The authors do not discuss or clarify the relationship between their method and EP. In particular, the paper misses a key citation to Stern et al. (2009), who applied a closely related variational message passing algorithm to matrix factorization. A thorough discussion comparing the proposed method to EP-based approaches is necessary to establish the novelty of this work.

**Significance**

The paper addresses an important problem, and the application to clustering shows promising results. The ability of the proposed algorithm to find better solutions to the non-convex K-means objective is noteworthy and could be impactful. However, the potential significance of this work is currently limited by the aforementioned concerns about technical rigor and originality. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while promising, is not comprehensive enough to make a strong case for the algorithm's practical utility. The comparison is limited to variants of the K-means algorithm, while a stronger case would require comparison against a wider range of modern clustering methods.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper presents a novel and clever reformulation of the K-means clustering problem as an instance of low-rank matrix factorization with specific structural constraints.
*   The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating that the proposed AMP algorithm can achieve a lower K-means loss and higher clustering accuracy than standard K-means and K-means++.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly communicates its core ideas.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Lack of Rigor:** The mathematical derivations are sketchy and lack the necessary detail to allow for proper verification. Key assumptions and theoretical steps are not adequately justified.
*   **Unclear Originality:** The relationship between the proposed algorithm and Expectation Propagation (EP) is not addressed. This raises serious questions about the novelty of the core algorithm, especially in light of uncited prior work like Stern et al. (2009).
*   **Limited Experimental Comparison:** The evaluation is confined to K-means variants, which is insufficient to demonstrate the algorithm's competitiveness against a broader set of state-of-the-art clustering techniques.

**Detailed Questions and Suggestions**

1.  **Relation to Expectation Propagation (EP):** Could the authors please clarify the precise relationship between their AMP algorithm and EP for this model? A comparison to the variational message passing algorithm in Stern et al., "Matchmaking: A Variational Algorithm for Fusing User Similarity Models" (WWW 2009), which is closely related to EP, would be essential for contextualizing this work.

2.  **Rigor of Derivations:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more rigorous and detailed derivation of the AMP updates. Please explicitly state the assumptions made in the asymptotic analysis (e.g., regarding the scaling of `m` and `N`) and provide justification for why certain terms are considered negligible.

3.  **Justification for the MAP Limit:** The paper claims the MAP estimation algorithm is derived by taking the limit β → ∞. This step, which involves interchanging a limit and an optimization, is not always valid. Could the authors provide a formal justification for this procedure?

4.  **Proof of Proposition 1:** The provided proof is too concise. A more detailed proof, including the derivation of the reformulation presented in Equation (16), is needed to make the argument convincing.

5.  **Noise Parameter Estimation:** In the clustering application, the noise parameter `τ` is estimated adaptively. Please provide a clearer theoretical or empirical justification for the specific estimator used.

6.  **Broader Experimental Comparison:** To better demonstrate the significance of the proposed clustering method, I strongly recommend comparing it against a more diverse set of strong baselines, such as Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and spectral clustering.
