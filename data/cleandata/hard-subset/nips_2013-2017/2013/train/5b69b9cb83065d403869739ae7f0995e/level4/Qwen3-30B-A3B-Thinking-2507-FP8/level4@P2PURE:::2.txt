

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"  
This paper proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, based on applying belief propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. As detailed in the Supplementary Material, the algorithm employs two simplifying approximations derived under the assumptions of large row and column counts in the input matrix. The approach evaluates clustering by reformulating it as a matrix reconstruction problem and compares performance against Lloyd’s K-means algorithm. It also contrasts with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the only prior message-passing reconstruction algorithm.  

Cons  
Several concerns warrant rejection. The algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), making it difficult to generalize results to conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. For instance, both cited VBMF references (Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007) use the Netflix dataset for evaluation, yet this paper does not. It would be preferable if the authors had adopted the same dataset; if this is infeasible, they should explicitly justify why. While BP typically outperforms variational methods, evaluating the proposed BP variant using a novel criterion raises doubts about its competitiveness.  

The algorithm itself appears to be a straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed via Variational Message Passing. Though novel, it is not groundbreaking. The most compelling aspect is the asymptotic approximations as $N \to \infty$ and $m \to \infty$ (where $m \times N$ is the input matrix size), but their validity—only described in the Supplementary Material—is never empirically tested, and the explanation lacks clarity.  

The paper misrepresents the novelty of matrix factorization for clustering. The abstract and Section 2.2 suggest this connection is original, yet it is well-established in prior work (as noted by other reviewers). The authors should clarify this upfront: if the connection is too standard to cite, state it explicitly; otherwise, provide appropriate references. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the standard term for applying BP to continuous distributions, and the relationship to the proposed algorithm must be explained. The core algorithm is framed as a BP application, but this is first mentioned in Section 4.1; it should be stated in the abstract.  

Pros  
The paper presents an interesting, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally followable, and while I did not replicate it, the derivations appear reproducible. Despite skepticism about the novel evaluation criterion, the K-means comparison is sufficiently general to fairly assess the algorithm, and experiments demonstrate its benefits.  

Clarity  
The early sections are clear, with a well-structured introduction and literature summary. However, deriving the algorithm solely in the Supplementary Material is a drawback; a high-level outline in the main paper would improve accessibility. The experiments section is clear, though some black-and-white plots are hard to read.  

The mathematical exposition is the weakest aspect. Key issues include:  
- Unexplained factors (e.g., $1/(m \tau_w)$ in Equations 9a, 9b, 9d, 9e) that could be factored out for readability.  
- Unnecessary complexity in notation: excess hats ($\hat{p}$, $\hat{q}$, $\hat{r}$), tildes, subscripts, and superscripts (e.g., $\tau_w$ vs. $\tau$; $t$-superscripts in messages). The $t$-superscripts could be eliminated by using $\gets$ instead of $=$.  
- Lack of explanatory notes (e.g., why Equation 5 is the negative log of Equation 2; why Algorithm 2 is nearly row-column symmetric but not explicitly noted).  
- Ambiguous notation: functions in Equation 10 lack clear explanations (e.g., "f$\beta$ is the mean of q$\beta$"); the definition of $G\beta$ in Section 4.2 is unclear without prior context.  
- Redundant $\beta$ subscripts (e.g., in messages), despite $\beta$ being a global parameter. Eliminating these would simplify exposition (e.g., stating "in the limit $\beta \to \infty, f$ and $G$ take the following form").  

Deeper conceptual gaps include:  
- The significance of the $m$ factor in additive noise variance (does it affect row-column symmetry or practical applications? The paper does not address this).  
- The use of a tilde for column vectors (non-standard and confusing).  
- Inconsistent capitalization ($N$ vs. $m$).  
- Unclear citation for VBMF-MA in Section 5 (two prior references for variational matrix factorization exist).  

Supplementary Material Notes:  
- Equation 5 would be clearer as a product of exponentials to highlight the PDF structure.  
- The step from Equation 14 to 15 (regarding $\mathcal{O}(m)$ terms) is opaque.  
- The description of the message approximation lacks clarity on its novelty relative to prior work.  

Language Improvements:  
- p. 1: "Since properties" → "Since the properties"; "according to problems" → "according to the problem"; "has flexibility" → "has sufficient flexibility"; "motivate use of" → "motivate the use of".  
- p. 2: "We present results" → "We present the results".  
- p. 3: "We regard that" → "We see that"; "maximum accuracy clustering" (italicize); "Previous works" → "Previous work"; "ICM algorithm; It" → "ICM algorithm: it".  
- p. 4: "particularized" → "specialized".  
- p. 5: "plausible properties" → "discernible properties".  
- p. 6: "stuck to bad local minima" → "stuck in bad local minima".  
- p. 7: "This is contrastive to that" → "This is in contrast to the fact that".  
- Supplementary Material: "of these message" → "of these messages"; "a few number" → "a few".  

The English is otherwise strong, but minor article usage issues distract readers. The title should be revised (e.g., "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using approximate message passing"). Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material is helpful; additional diagrams would further aid understanding.  

While the mathematical approach is interesting and the algorithm fills a niche as the first BP application to matrix factorization, the paper’s lack of clarity and scholarly rigor imposes an unacceptable burden on readers. It requires significant revision before acceptance.
