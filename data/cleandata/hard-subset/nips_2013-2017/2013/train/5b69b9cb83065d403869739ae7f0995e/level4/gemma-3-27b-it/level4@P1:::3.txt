This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for matrix factorization. The authors demonstrate that the matrix factorization problem can be viewed as a generalization of the clustering problem, and then specialize the derived AMP algorithm to clustering. Finally, they present experiments comparing the proposed algorithm to the k-means++ algorithm.

**Quality:** The mathematical derivations in this paper are somewhat sketchy and omit several non-trivial steps, even in the appendix. 

1) In line 139 of the Appendix, the explanation of why the second term of (11) is O(1/√m) is unclear. The parameter β appears to be missing from the analysis in Section 2.
2) Some of the analysis, including the derivation of (19), appears to assume that *m* and *N* grow at the same rate. The asymptotic analysis in this section feels terse, and I am concerned about its mathematical rigor.
3) In line 167, the justification for equating E[A<sub>il</sub><sup>2</sup>] with *mτ<sub>w</sub><sup>2</sup>* is unclear. *A* is observed data, while *mτ<sub>w</sub><sup>2</sup>* represents the variance of the likelihood; the rationale for this approximation needs to be clarified.
4) It is not immediately clear how the message passing algorithm, derived for finite β, converges to the MAP problem. While message passing algorithms minimize KL divergence, it is not generally true that lim<sub>β→∞</sub> min KL = min lim<sub>β→∞</sub> KL.
5) The proof of Proposition 1 is brief. How is equation (17) derived?

**Clarity:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, making the main points clear.

**Originality:** Given that the authors primarily focus on estimating the first and second moments of the marginal posterior, is the algorithm simply an application of Expectation Propagation (EP) to matrix factorization? EP has already been used in this context, as demonstrated in [http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf](http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf). Section 1 of the Appendix resembles standard EP update procedures; please clarify if this is not the case. 

Additionally, there may be an interesting connection between the authors’ clustering algorithm and the clutter problem addressed in the original EP paper [http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf](http://research.microsoft.com/en-us/um/people/minka/papers/ep/minka-ep-uai.pdf), if the likelihood of the clutter problem were modified to a uniform mixture of Gaussian distributions. To clarify, I am suggesting a potential connection rather than questioning the originality of the work.

**Significance:** The authors primarily focus on the application to clustering. However, given the extensive literature in this field, comparison to only k-means++ may not be sufficient to demonstrate the practical utility of the algorithm. The proofs in the paper, even in the appendix, are sketchy, making it difficult to evaluate the technical correctness. Furthermore, I suspect that this work may be an application of expectation propagation to a matrix factorization problem, which has already been explored by Stern et al. [http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf](http://research.microsoft.com/pubs/79460/www09.pdf).

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering.
*   The algorithm shows promising performance in the experiments, outperforming k-means++ in some cases.
*   The authors identify a connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering, offering a unified framework.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The mathematical derivations lack rigor and omit important details.
*   The originality is questionable, as the approach appears closely related to existing Expectation Propagation techniques.
*   The significance is not fully established, as the comparison is limited to k-means++.
*   The brevity of the proofs hinders a thorough evaluation of the technical correctness.
