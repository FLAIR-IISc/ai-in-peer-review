This paper presents an algorithm for matrix reconstruction in noise-dominated observation scenarios. This particular framework is based on some additional assumptions about low-rank matrices and their causes. It uses an approximate message passing method to accelerate classical, computationally expensive, and Bayesian methods. The authors also link matrix reconstruction to K-means clustering, which is an interesting application area for the proposed algorithm.

To my knowledge, the approximate message passing method for matrix reconstruction is new and interesting. The relationship between low-rank matrix factorization and K-means is quite well known (e.g., PCA provides a factor-to-approximation algorithm for K-means). However, it allows the authors to provide a nice experimental evaluation of their algorithm and compare it with k-means and k-means++. Interestingly, according to their empirical data, their method seems faster and more effective than classical k-means and k-means++. The authors also compare it with Frobenius norm residuals and actual clustering, which is a nice feature of their experimental evaluation.

The main weakness of this paper is that the proposed algorithm comes with very little theoretical guarantee in terms of convergence. This is expected, as many other algorithms for K-means also have weak properties. The authors could have cited more theoretical computer science papers that provide provably accurate algorithms for the objective function of K-means. It is a strong paper presenting an approximate message passing algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means. The experimental evaluation is promising, but the theoretical results are somewhat weak.
