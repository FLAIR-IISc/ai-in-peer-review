Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering" 
This paper presents a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, as detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates two simplifying approximations, derived assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix, respectively. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel way against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem. It is also compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only prior message-passing reconstruction algorithm. 
Cons 
Several arguments weigh against acceptance. Because the algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem—clustering encoded as matrix factorization—it is difficult to extrapolate the experimental results to predict its performance on more conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. For example, the references for VBMF (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen, 2007) both utilize the Netflix dataset to evaluate their algorithm against prior work. Ideally, the present paper would employ the same dataset. While BP is typically more accurate than Variational methods, evaluating this specific BP variant using a novel criterion raises doubts about its true competitiveness. Even if the Netflix dataset or a similar one is impractical, the authors should explain why in the paper. 
The algorithm itself is a fairly direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed using Variational Message Passing. While novel, it is not a groundbreaking advance. The most interesting aspect, in my view, lies in the approximations introduced in the limits $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the dimensions of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations—described only in the supplementary material—is never directly tested, and could benefit from further clarification. 
There are notable issues with the citation of prior work. Initially, the paper led me to believe it was introducing the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution, an impression given by the abstract and Section 2.2. I was surprised to learn from other reviewers that this connection is already well-established. There is no apparent justification for omitting this information from the paper. If the connection is widely known, the authors should state this explicitly; otherwise, they should cite relevant prior work. 
Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as that is the standard terminology for applying BP to continuous classes of distributions, and the relationship between EP and the presented algorithm should be explained. Relatedly, the core algorithm is most naturally understood as an application of Belief Propagation, but this is not stated until Section 4.1. It should be mentioned in the abstract. 
Pros 
The paper is interesting and presents a new, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematical content is accessible, and while I did not verify it manually, I believe it could be done. Despite reasonable skepticism regarding the new evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may be sufficiently general to provide a fair comparison of algorithms, and the experiments do demonstrate a benefit for the proposed method. 
Clarity 
The early presentation is generally easy to follow. The introduction and summary of previous work are clear. The placement of the algorithm’s derivation in the Supplemental Material is a drawback; I would prefer to see an outline in the main paper. The experiments section is clear, although some plots appear poorly in black and white, they remain readable. 
I found the description of the algorithm difficult to follow and was unable to verify the correctness of the derivation. The mathematics is the weakest aspect of the presentation. Even superficially, it is difficult to parse. For instance, the factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ preceding each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out to improve readability. Moreover, the last two terms in 9b and 9e, respectively, could be combined. 
There appears to be an overuse of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For example, there is a $\tauw$ but no $\tau$; simply replacing $\tauw$ with $\tau$ would suffice. Furthermore, Section 4, the most important section, contains no p, q, or r, only \hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r} – the authors could state at the beginning of the section, “We will be dropping the hats here for brevity.” Finally, the 't' superscripts in the messages seem unnecessary; changing "=" to "\gets" would eliminate the need for them. I find it hard to believe the algorithm was originally derived using such cumbersome notation. I suggest revisiting the initial notation used to see if a simpler form exists. 
The exposition could be improved by explaining that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, or that equation 8 is equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. Algorithm 2 is nearly row-column symmetrical; this could be pointed out and the algorithm made half as long by stating, “then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v.” The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be clarified near their definitions. It would be clearer to state "f\beta is the mean of q\beta" instead of providing an equation; if an equation is given, its meaning should also be stated. At the end of Section 4.2, the statement "G\beta(b, \Lambda; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)" is not immediately obvious and wasn't even mentioned in the definition of G. 
None of the messages have \beta subscripts, even though they depend on f and G which do. However, f and G do not directly depend on \beta, only on q_\beta. Thus, it is unclear why these subscripts are propagated only as far as f and G. I suggest eliminating them entirely, even from q. The parameter \beta is simply a global variable, which is acceptable. Before equations (15) and (16), it would be necessary to state, “in the limit \beta \to \infty, f and G take the following form:”. 
On a deeper level, I have questions about the algorithm that remain unanswered. What is the significance of the m factor in the additive noise variance? Does it have a role in applications of matrix factorization? Does it factor into the approximation used to derive the algorithm? It seems to be the only aspect preventing the algorithm from being row-column symmetrical; is this correct? I believe the authors know the answers but do not comment on them. 
Other questions: 
Is it standard to use a tilde to denote column vectors? I did not initially understand this. 
Why is N capitalized, but m lowercase? 
In section 5, a citation after VBMF-MA is needed to indicate the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competing algorithm. Two citations for variational matrix factorization appear earlier in the paper, and it is unclear which is intended (or if both are relevant). 
In the supplementary material: For equation 5, writing a product of exponentials for the two terms involving $z$ would make it clearer that one represents a pdf. In section 2, I had trouble with the step from equation 14 to 15 regarding the big-O of $m$ terms. I am not sure if further thought is needed, but this is where I became stuck. 
Also in the supplementary material, at the top of page 2, it states, “A technique used in ... is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without reading these references, it’s unclear how exactly the approximation described below this text relates to prior publications. Clarifying the novelty of the algorithm’s derivation in the text, and even in the main paper, would be beneficial. 
The English is very good, and the meaning is always conveyed, but there are instances where the writing appears to be by someone not fully proficient in article usage. This can be distracting for some readers. Even the title could be improved—for instance, “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” Specific suggestions are provided below: 
p. 1 
“Since properties” -> “Since the properties” 
“according to problems” -> “according to the problem” 
“has flexibility” -> “has enough flexibility” 
“motivate use of” -> “motivate the use of” 
p. 2 
“We present results” -> “We present the results” 
p. 3 
“We regard that” -> “We see that” 
“maximum accuracy clustering” could be italicized 
“Previous works” -> “Previous work” 
“ICM algorithm; It” -> “ICM algorithm: it” 
p. 4 
“particularized” -> “specialized” 
p. 5 
“plausible properties” -> “discernable properties” 
p. 6 
“stuck to bad local minima” -> “stuck in bad local minima” 
p. 7 
“This is contrastive to that” -> “This is in contrast to the fact that” 
The Supplementary Material also contains some language issues, for instance: 
p. 2 
“of these message” -> “of these messages” 
“a few number” -> “a few” 
I found the diagram in Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material very helpful. Including more diagrams would enhance the paper. 
The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm merits publication somewhere as the first attempt to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization, filling an important niche. However, the paper requires further work before acceptance. The lack of clarity and scholarship places an unacceptable burden on readers, which would reflect negatively on the conference.
