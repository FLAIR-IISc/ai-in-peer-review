Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, which applies Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates two simplifying approximations based on the assumption of a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and compared to Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

Cons
There are several concerns regarding the paper's acceptability. The algorithm's performance is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), making it challenging to generalize the experimental results to more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, the Netflix dataset, commonly used in previous works (e.g., Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen, 2007), would have been a more suitable choice for evaluation. The authors should explain why this dataset was not used. Furthermore, the introduction of a new evaluation criterion raises concerns about the algorithm's competitiveness, as BP is typically more accurate than Variational methods.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. Although novel, it is not particularly groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect of the algorithm is the approximations introduced in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the dimensions of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, only described in the supplementary material, is not directly tested, and their explanation could be improved.

There are serious issues regarding the citation of prior work. The paper gives the impression that it is introducing the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution, which is not the case. The connection is well-known, and the authors should have made this clear to the reader. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is commonly referred to as the application of BP to continuous classes of distributions. The relationship between EP and the paper's algorithm should be explained.

Pros
The paper presents a new and potentially useful algorithm, and the mathematics are generally followable. Although the evaluation criterion is unconventional, the K-means problem may provide a fair comparison of the algorithms, and the experiments demonstrate a benefit for the new algorithm.

Clarity
The early presentation is clear, but the derivation of the algorithm, only provided in the Supplemental Material, is a drawback. The experiments section is well-written, but the mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation. The notation is cumbersome, with an excess of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, the use of $\tauw$ instead of $\tau$ is unnecessary, and the 't' superscripts in the messages could be dropped.

The exposition could be improved by explaining the significance of certain equations and providing more context. For example, it would be helpful to explain that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2 or that equation 8 is simply equation 2 to the power of $\beta$. The meaning of functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definition.

Other issues include the lack of clarity regarding the significance of the $m$ factor in the additive noise variance and its role in applications of matrix factorization. The authors should comment on the relationship between the algorithm's approximations and the $m$ factor.

Other questions and suggestions
Is it standard to use a tilde to denote column vectors? Why is $N$ capitalized, but $m$ lowercase? A citation is needed after VBMF-MA to indicate the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competing algorithm.

In the supplementary material, equation 5 could be rewritten to make it clearer that one term is the pdf. The step from equation 14 to 15 in section 2 is unclear, and the novelty of the algorithm's derivation should be clarified in the text.

The English is generally good, but there are some places where it reads like it was written by someone not entirely skilled in the use of articles. Some suggestions for improvement are provided.

In conclusion, while the paper presents an interesting algorithm, it needs more work before it can be accepted. The paper is lacking in clarity and scholarship, which would put an unacceptable burden on readers and reflect poorly on a conference that accepted it in its present state.
