Review- Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper introduces a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, leveraging Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing within a Bayesian model. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, employs simplifying approximations based on the large-dimension assumption (large number of rows and columns) of the input matrix.  The algorithm’s performance is evaluated by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem, offering a unique experimental approach. It is also benchmarked against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), representing the primary existing message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

**Cons**

Several concerns hinder acceptance. Evaluating a new algorithm on a non-standard problem—clustering encoded as matrix factorization—makes it difficult to extrapolate the experimental results to more conventional matrix reconstruction scenarios. For example, the VBMF comparisons cite Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen (2007), both of which utilize the Netflix dataset for evaluation. Ideally, the current work would employ the same dataset for a more direct comparison. While BP generally surpasses Variational methods in accuracy, assessing this specific BP variant with a novel criterion raises questions about its true competitiveness.  If utilizing Netflix or a similar dataset is infeasible, the authors should clearly articulate the reasons within the paper.

The algorithm itself represents a relatively direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed using Variational Message Passing. While novel, it doesn’t constitute a groundbreaking advancement. The most intriguing aspect lies in the approximations introduced in the limits $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the input matrix dimensions. However, the validity of these approximations, presented solely in the Supplementary Material, remains untested and could benefit from further clarification.

Significant issues exist regarding the citation of prior work. The initial presentation of the paper, particularly in the abstract and Section 2.2, implies an original contribution in applying matrix factorization to clustering. This impression was misleading, as other reviewers clarified the connection is well-established. The paper should explicitly acknowledge this existing knowledge, either through citation or a statement confirming its widespread recognition.

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the standard terminology for applying BP to continuous distributions, and the relationship between EP and the presented algorithm should be discussed.  Relatedly, the core algorithm is fundamentally a Belief Propagation application, but this is not mentioned until Section 4.1; it should be stated in the abstract.

**Pros**

The paper is engaging and presents a new, potentially valuable algorithm. The mathematical framework is comprehensible, and while I haven’t verified it manually, it appears amenable to replication. Despite reservations regarding the new evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may offer a sufficiently general comparison, and the experiments demonstrate a benefit for the proposed algorithm.

**Clarity**

The initial presentation is relatively clear, with a well-structured introduction and summary of prior work. The algorithm’s derivation being relegated to the Supplementary Material is a drawback; outlining it in the main paper would be beneficial. The experiments section is clear, and the plots, despite some legibility issues in black and white, remain readable.

However, understanding the algorithm’s description proved challenging, hindering verification of the derivation’s correctness. The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect, even at a superficial level. For instance, the factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ preceding each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out for improved readability, and the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.

The notation is excessively complex, with an abundance of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. The presence of $\tauw$ without a corresponding $\tau$ is perplexing; replacing $\tauw$ with $\tau$ would simplify the notation.  Similarly, Section 4, the most crucial section, exclusively uses $\hat{p}$, $\hat{q}$, and $\hat{r}$ – a statement at the section’s beginning indicating the removal of hats for brevity would be helpful. The ‘t’ superscripts in the messages appear unnecessary; replacing "=" with "\gets" would eliminate the need for them. It’s difficult to believe the algorithm was originally developed using such cumbersome notation. Revisiting the initial notation for simplicity is recommended.

The exposition could be improved by explicitly stating the relationship between equation 5 and the negative logarithm of equation 2, and between equation 8 and equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. Algorithm 2 exhibits near row-column symmetry; highlighting this and potentially halving its length by suggesting a copy-and-paste approach for switching variables would enhance clarity.

The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be defined near their introduction. Expressing “f\beta is the mean of q\beta” would be clearer than presenting the equation alone.  At the end of Section 4.2, the statement that “G\beta(b, \Lambda; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)” wasn’t immediately obvious and wasn’t mentioned during G’s definition.

None of the messages include $\beta$ subscripts, despite their dependence on f and G, which do. Since f and G only depend on $q_\beta$, propagating the subscript further seems unnecessary. Eliminating them entirely, even from q, is suggested, as $\beta$ functions as a global variable. A statement before equations (15) and (16) like “in the limit \beta \to \infty, f and G take the following form:” would be helpful.

On a deeper level, the significance of the m factor in the additive noise variance warrants further discussion. Does it have implications for matrix factorization applications or the algorithm’s derivation? It appears to be the sole factor preventing row-column symmetry; confirming this would be valuable. The authors likely have answers to these questions but do not address them.

Other questions:

Is using a tilde to denote column vectors standard practice? I initially found it confusing.
Why is N capitalized while m is lowercase?
Section 5 would benefit from a citation following VBMF-MA to indicate the primary reference guiding its implementation. The two earlier citations for variational matrix factorization create ambiguity.

In the Supplementary Material: For equation 5, representing the terms involving $z$ as a product of exponentials would clarify their probability density function nature. Regarding the step from equation 14 to 15 in Section 2, the big-O notation of the m terms requires further scrutiny; I found it difficult to follow.

The statement in the Supplementary Material, at the top of page 2, regarding “a technique used in … is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters” lacks context without prior knowledge of the referenced works. Clarifying the novelty of the algorithm’s derivation, both in the text and the main paper, would be beneficial.

The English is generally good, but occasional awkward phrasing, particularly regarding article usage, can be distracting. The title could be improved, for example, to “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” Specific suggestions for improvement are provided as a separate list.

The Supplementary Material also contains minor language issues, such as “of these message” and “a few number.” The diagram in Figure 1 of the Supplementary Material is helpful, and including more diagrams would enhance the paper.

In conclusion, the mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm deserves publication as a first attempt to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper requires substantial revisions to address the clarity and scholarship concerns before it can be accepted. The current state places an unacceptable burden on readers and could reflect poorly on the conference.
