The authors present an algorithm for matrix reconstruction from noisy observations. They focus on low-rank matrices and leverage Approximate Message Passing (AMP) to accelerate the computationally demanding Bayesian approach, particularly when additional structural assumptions are made on the matrix factors. A notable application of this work is connecting matrix reconstruction to K-means clustering, providing a compelling experimental evaluation of the proposed algorithms.

To the best of my knowledge, the application of AMP to matrix reconstruction is a novel and interesting contribution. While the connection between low-rank matrix factorization and K-means is established – for example, PCA offers a factor-two approximation for K-means – this work allows for a strong experimental validation of the proposed algorithms, with comparisons to both Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++. The empirical results suggest that the proposed approach can be faster and more efficient than these classical algorithms. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation, considering both the Frobenius norm residual and clustering accuracy, which is a valuable aspect of their experiments.

A potential weakness of the paper lies in the limited theoretical guarantees regarding convergence. This is a common challenge in algorithms for K-means and related problems. However, the authors could strengthen the paper by citing relevant work in theoretical computer science that offers provably accurate algorithms for the K-means objective function, or by discussing the conditions under which their algorithm is likely to converge. 

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a promising AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering. The experimental evaluation is convincing, and the theoretical limitations are acknowledged.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction.
*   Strong empirical performance compared to standard K-means algorithms.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation, considering both reconstruction error and clustering accuracy.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation of the algorithm and experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited theoretical analysis of convergence properties.
*   Potential for the algorithm to get stuck in local minima (though this is common for K-means-like algorithms).
