- Paper proposes a Bayesian AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that incorporates structural priors and is derived from belief propagation.  
- The method can compute posterior means (marginalization) and approximate MAP estimates, and casts K-means clustering as a constrained low-rank reconstruction instance.  
- Empirical results show the AMP-based clustering (AMP-KM) outperforms Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ on tested synthetic and real datasets, especially in high dimensions.  
- Novel application: applying AMP to K-means-style clustering with an explicit one-cluster-per-datum constraint is interesting and notable.  
- Framework flexibility: allowing different priors p̂u and p̂v makes the approach adaptable to many structured low-rank problems.  
- Promising empirical performance: experiments indicate improved K-means loss and clustering accuracy versus standard baselines.  
- Presentation: paper is well-structured and clearly written with logical motivation and algorithm presentation.  
- Derivations are overly condensed; many non-trivial steps are omitted, reducing verifiability and technical rigor.  
- Asymptotic analysis is terse and appears to assume m and N grow at the same rate without explicit statement or justification.  
- Specific unexplained steps: claim that a term is O(1/√m) (appx. line 139) and derivation of equation (19) lack explanation.  
- Unjustified approximation: replacing m⁻² Σ Aᵢⱼ² Sᵗᵢ with τm⁻¹ Σ Sᵗᵢ (line 167) needs formal rationale linking observed moments to noise variance.  
- MAP claim for β → ∞ is insufficiently justified because interchanging limits and optimization (lim and min) is nontrivial and potentially invalid.  
- Proofs are too brief; Proposition 1 and equation (16) require more detailed derivations to be convincing.  
- Relation to prior work is unclear: connections to Expectation Propagation and prior matrix factorization work (e.g., Matchbox) are not discussed, raising questions about novelty.  
- Experimental evaluation is narrow: comparisons are limited to K-means/K-means++, so broader benchmarking against modern clustering methods is needed to establish practical significance.
