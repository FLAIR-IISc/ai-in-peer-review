

This paper introduces an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction under noisy observations, leveraging specific structural assumptions on the factor matrices to accelerate the computationally intensive Bayesian approach. The work establishes a connection between matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering—a well-established link (e.g., PCA provides a 2-approximation for K-means)—which enables a compelling empirical evaluation against standard clustering baselines. The proposed method demonstrates significant computational advantages over k-means and k-means++, with empirical results showing faster convergence and competitive clustering quality. Notably, the authors evaluate both the Frobenius norm residual (a standard reconstruction metric) and the actual cluster assignments, providing a comprehensive assessment of performance.  

The AMP framework for matrix reconstruction represents a novel contribution to the literature, and the experimental design effectively highlights its practical utility. The speed and efficiency gains over traditional clustering methods are particularly compelling, suggesting potential applications in large-scale data analysis where computational cost is prohibitive. The connection to K-means also offers a meaningful bridge between matrix factorization and clustering theory, enriching the paper’s theoretical context.  

However, the theoretical foundation of the proposed algorithm remains underdeveloped. While convergence guarantees for AMP-based methods in similar settings are often limited, the paper lacks even a discussion of existing theoretical results or a clear justification for the absence of rigorous analysis. The authors should explicitly address this gap by citing recent theoretical advances in provably accurate K-means algorithms from Theoretical Computer Science (e.g., works on approximation schemes or convergence bounds for clustering objectives). Such references would better position the paper within the broader landscape of algorithmic guarantees and strengthen its contribution.  

The experimental section is generally strong but could benefit from additional scalability tests on larger datasets or comparisons with more recent clustering baselines (e.g., spectral methods or deep learning-based approaches). While the current results are promising, a more extensive evaluation would further validate the method’s practical relevance.  

Overall, this is a promising contribution that successfully merges matrix reconstruction with clustering through an efficient AMP-based approach. The empirical results are well-executed and demonstrate clear advantages over standard methods. However, the limited theoretical discussion represents a significant weakness that detracts from the paper’s overall impact. Addressing this gap—by incorporating relevant theoretical references or adding convergence analysis—would substantially improve the work. The paper is suitable for acceptance with minor revisions, particularly to strengthen the theoretical context and discussion.
