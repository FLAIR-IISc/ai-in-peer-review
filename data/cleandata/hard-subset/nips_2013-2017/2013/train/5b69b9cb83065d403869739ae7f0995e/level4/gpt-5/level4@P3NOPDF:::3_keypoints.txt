- Paper proposes an AMP algorithm for matrix factorization and a clustering specialization, comparing empirically to k-means++.

- Strength: timely problem, interesting link between matrix factorization and clustering.

- Strength: clear high-level narrative and well-structured presentation.

- Strength: initial empirical section is a useful first step.

- Major weakness: derivations are too terse and omit nontrivial steps, making correctness hard to assess.

- Missing justification for order-of-magnitude claim in Appendix line 139 (second term of (11) scaling and role of β).

- Asymptotic regime for state evolution and (19) is not specified; need m,n→∞ scaling, assumptions, and roadmap for limits.

- Appendix line 167: unjustified claim E[A_il^2] = m τ_w^2; signal/noise contributions and indexing must be derived.

- Unclear theoretical link between finite-β message passing and MAP as β→∞; need conditions for exchanging limits/minimization.

- Proposition 1 / equation (17) proof is too brief; full derivation and AMP standard-argument references required.

- Clarity issues: key notation and assumptions (dimensions, priors, likelihood, β, factor graph) should be stated up front and exact vs. asymptotic expectations distinguished.

- Presentation suggestion: provide algorithm pseudocode with per-iteration complexity, memory, damping, and stopping criteria.

- Originality concerns: relation to EP, EP-based matrix factorization, and AMP/GAMP literature is not sufficiently clarified; novelty over existing methods must be made explicit.

- Empirical evaluation is too narrow (only k-means++ baseline); needs stronger baselines (GMM/EM, spectral, hierarchical, DP-means, EP/AMP methods), metrics (NMI, ARI), runtime/memory, and sensitivity analyses (m/n, noise, k, hyperparameters).

- Suggestions: expand appendix derivations, make β dependence explicit, provide algorithm summary, strengthen related work positioning, and broaden experiments with reproducibility details.

- Overall: promising idea but insufficient mathematical detail, unclear novelty, and narrow experiments—cannot recommend acceptance in current form.
