Review of “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering”

Summary
The paper proposes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction derived by applying Belief Propagation (BP) to a Bayesian model. The method relies on two simplifying approximations motivated by the large-row and large-column limits of the input matrix. The evaluation is unusual: clustering is cast as a matrix reconstruction task, enabling comparison with Lloyd’s K-means and with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the closest prior message-passing approach.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation choice and comparability: Because the new algorithm is tested on a non-standard proxy task (clustering-as-factorization), it is hard to infer performance on conventional matrix reconstruction problems. Prior VBMF work (Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin & Karhunen, 2007) uses the Netflix dataset; ideally, this paper would do the same. If Netflix or a similar benchmark cannot be used, the paper should clearly explain why. While BP often outperforms variational approximations, evaluating a new BP variant on a new metric leaves its competitiveness uncertain.
- Incremental novelty: The core algorithm reads as a fairly direct BP counterpart to earlier Variational Message Passing approaches. The most novel elements are the approximations taken in the limits N→∞ and m→∞ (for an m×N matrix), but these are only described in the supplement, not validated experimentally, and need clearer exposition.
- Scholarship and positioning: The abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that the clustering–matrix factorization connection is introduced here. That link is well known; the paper should either say so explicitly or cite appropriate references. Expectation Propagation (EP) should also be cited, as it is the common name for BP-like approximations on continuous distributions; the relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm should be clarified. Finally, that the method is an instance of BP should be stated up front, ideally in the abstract, not deferred to Section 4.1.

Strengths
- The problem is interesting and the algorithm could be practically useful.
- The narrative is generally readable; while I did not re-derive the equations, they felt reproducible.
- Although non-standard, the K-means benchmark may be broad enough to be informative and, in the reported experiments, highlights advantages of the proposed method.

Clarity and presentation
- Structure: The introduction and related work are clear. However, relegating the derivation entirely to the supplement weakens the paper; an outline of the key steps should appear in the main text. The experiments are clearly described; some plots are hard to read in black-and-white but still interpretable.
- Readability of equations: Equations 9a, 9b, 9d, 9e repeatedly include factors 1/(m tau_w) before each term; these could be factored out to improve readability. The last two terms in 9b and in 9e could also be combined.
- Notation overload: There are too many hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts, which obscures the main ideas.
  - There is tau_w but no tau; consider renaming tau_w to tau if no ambiguity arises.
  - Section 4 uses only hatted variables (p̂, q̂, r̂). Consider dropping hats there (“we omit hats for brevity”).
  - Superscripts t for iterations seem unnecessary; when the left-hand side is at t+1, use an assignment arrow and omit explicit time superscripts elsewhere.
  - Overall, please prefer the simplest notation consistent with correctness.
- Explanatory links: 
  - State explicitly that Eq. 5 is the negative log of Eq. 2.
  - Note that Eq. 8 is Eq. 2 raised to the power beta.
  - Algorithm 2 is almost row–column symmetric; point this out and consider halving its length by presenting one half and instructing the reader to swap u and v.
- Definitions and interpretation:
  - For the functions in Eq. 10, define their meaning near their introduction. For instance, say “f_beta is the mean of q_beta” (and similarly for G_beta as a scaled covariance), and only then provide formulas.
  - The beta subscripts are inconsistently propagated: messages lack beta while depending on f_beta and G_beta. Since f and G depend on beta only via q_beta, consider dropping the beta subscript entirely (even from q), treating beta as a global parameter. Before Eqs. 15–16, say “in the limit beta→∞, f and G take the following form.”
- Conceptual questions:
  - What role does the factor m play in the additive noise variance? Is it standard in matrix factorization models? Is it required by the approximation? Is this the only source of row–column asymmetry?
  - Clarify whether using a tilde to denote column vectors is standard in your notation.
  - Why is N uppercase while m is lowercase?

Related work and implementation details
- Please add a citation next to VBMF-MA in Section 5 pointing to the primary reference guiding your implementation, since two VBMF citations appear earlier and it is unclear which was followed.
- EP should be cited, and its relation to your BP-based approximation explained.

Supplementary material
- In Eq. 5, writing the two z-related terms as a product of exponentials would make it obvious which one is a pdf.
- In Section 2, the step from Eq. 14 to Eq. 15 (big-O in m) was unclear; please expand this argument.
- Page 2: “a few number of real-valued parameters” should be “a few real-valued parameters.” Also, explain more precisely how your message approximations relate to prior techniques, and clarify what is novel in your derivation (in both supplement and main text).
- Figure 1 in the supplement is very helpful; adding more diagrams would improve accessibility.

Language and title suggestions
The English is generally good but occasionally awkward, especially with articles. Consider the title “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.”
Selected edits:
- p.1: “Since properties” → “Since the properties”; “according to problems” → “according to the problem”; “has flexibility” → “has enough flexibility”; “motivate use of” → “motivate the use of.”
- p.2: “We present results” → “We present the results.”
- p.3: “We regard that” → “We see that”; consider italicizing “maximum accuracy clustering”; “Previous works” → “Previous work”; “ICM algorithm; It” → “ICM algorithm: it.”
- p.4: “particularized” → “specialized.”
- p.5: “plausible properties” → “discernible properties.”
- p.6: “stuck to bad local minima” → “stuck in bad local minima.”
- p.7: “This is contrastive to that” → “This is in contrast to the fact that.”
- Supplement p.2: “of these message” → “of these messages”; “a few number” → “a few.”

Conclusion
The paper tackles an appealing problem and presents a new algorithm that is likely to be useful. However, the current version needs stronger positioning relative to known connections and EP, clearer justification and testing of the large-dimension approximations, a more accessible derivation (at least sketched in the main text), cleaner notation, and better-aligned evaluation. As it stands, the clarity and scholarship issues place too much burden on the reader. With these revisions, the work would be substantially stronger and more suitable for publication.
