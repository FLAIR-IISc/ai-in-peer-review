This paper proposes an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) algorithm for reconstructing low-rank matrices from noisy observations, under additional structure on the latent factors. The goal is to retain much of the modeling flexibility of a Bayesian formulation while achieving substantial computational speedups. The authors further leverage the well-known connection between low-rank factorizations and k-means clustering to evaluate their method both as a reconstruction tool and as a clustering algorithm.

Novelty and significance: To the best of my knowledge, applying AMP to this specific matrix reconstruction setting is novel and interesting. While the link between low-rank factorization and k-means is established (e.g., PCA providing a factor-2 approximation for k-means under certain formulations), using this connection to benchmark the proposed AMP method against k-means and k-means++ is reasonable and broadens the paper’s appeal.

Empirical evaluation: The experimental section is a strength. The authors report both Frobenius norm residuals and clustering outcomes, which is a thoughtful choice because it evaluates the method on the reconstruction objective and on the induced discrete structure. The reported speed and accuracy improvements over classical k-means and k-means++ are compelling, and the comparisons appear fair. These results suggest that the proposed AMP framework could be a practical alternative when one wants both interpretability via factors and competitive clustering performance.

Main limitation: The most significant weakness is the lack of theoretical guarantees, particularly regarding convergence and performance. Although this is not uncommon—many practical k-means variants also lack strong guarantees—AMP-based methods often admit some form of state-evolution analysis under simplifying assumptions. Even partial results or a careful discussion of when and why convergence might be expected would considerably strengthen the paper.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Theoretical context and guarantees:
  - Provide at least a discussion of convergence behavior (e.g., damping, step-size choices, fixed-point conditions, and failure modes). If full state evolution is not tractable, clarify what assumptions would be needed and what partial guarantees might hold.
  - Situate the work within the TCS literature on provable k-means algorithms. At minimum, consider citing representative lines of work, such as k-means++ seeding (Arthur and Vassilvitskii), analyses under separation assumptions (e.g., Ostrovsky et al.), spectral or PCA-based guarantees (e.g., connections highlighted by Ding and He), and more recent results on stable instances or semi-definite relaxations. This will help readers understand where your approach fits relative to algorithms with worst-case or assumption-driven guarantees.
- Modeling assumptions and identifiability:
  - Clearly state the structural assumptions on the factors (e.g., sparsity, nonnegativity, priors) and discuss identifiability or equivalence classes of solutions. Explain when these assumptions are critical for performance.
  - Clarify the noise model used in both the derivation and experiments, and whether the algorithm is robust to model misspecification.
- Algorithmic clarity and complexity:
  - Provide per-iteration time and memory complexity, and contrast it with the classical Bayesian approach you are accelerating and with baseline clustering methods. Include stopping criteria and typical iteration counts.
  - Discuss scalability: how performance and runtime scale with matrix dimensions, rank, and number of clusters. If applicable, note opportunities for parallelization or streaming updates.
- Connection to clustering:
  - Make the mapping from the low-rank factors to hard cluster assignments explicit. If a rounding or post-processing step is used, describe it and its computational cost. Clarify whether the method is optimizing a surrogate for the k-means objective and how this affects the observed clustering quality.
  - Consider reporting multiple clustering metrics (e.g., ARI/NMI/purity) to complement the Frobenius residual, and show consistency across metrics.
- Experimental breadth and robustness:
  - Expand sensitivity analyses: vary noise levels, true rank vs. specified rank, number of clusters, and initialization strategies. Report robustness to misspecification.
  - Where possible, include additional baselines beyond k-means and k-means++, such as PCA followed by k-means, spectral clustering, NMF-based clustering, mini-batch k-means, and EM for Gaussian mixtures (especially if your factor priors have a probabilistic clustering interpretation).
  - Provide ablations to quantify the contribution of key components (e.g., specific priors, damping).
  - If experiments mix synthetic and real data, clarify the data generation, ground-truth availability, and the diversity of real datasets.
- Reproducibility:
  - Include details on hyperparameter selection and tuning protocol for all methods. If feasible, release code and scripts, and report random seeds and hardware to substantiate the claimed speedups.

Overall assessment: This is a solid and promising paper. The application of AMP to structured low-rank matrix reconstruction appears novel and practically effective, and the empirical results—evaluated on both reconstruction error and clustering quality—are encouraging. The primary area for improvement is theoretical grounding: even partial convergence analysis or a sharper positioning relative to the k-means theory literature would significantly enhance the contribution. With strengthened theory, clarified assumptions, and a broader empirical study, this work would be a strong addition to the literature on scalable Bayesian-inspired methods for matrix factorization and clustering.
