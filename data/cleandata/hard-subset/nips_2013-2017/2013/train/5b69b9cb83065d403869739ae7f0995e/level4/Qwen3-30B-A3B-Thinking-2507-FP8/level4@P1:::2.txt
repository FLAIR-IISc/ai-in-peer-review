

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering via approximate message passing"

This paper introduces an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending the AMP framework to the matrix factorization setting. The authors formulate the problem in a Bayesian framework that allows incorporating structural properties of the factors (beyond low-rankness), such as sparsity, and apply the resulting algorithm to clustering by reformulating the problem as a low-rank matrix reconstruction task with additional structural constraints. The work builds on Rangan et al. (2012) for the rank-one case and Matsushita and Tanaka (2012) for the general-rank case, extending their AMP approach to handle various estimation problems (marginalization and MAP) and structural priors.

Strengths:
1. The paper presents a technically sound application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural priors, which is a meaningful contribution to the literature. While AMP has been used for sparse vector recovery and rank-one matrix reconstruction, its application to general low-rank matrix reconstruction with various structural priors is novel.
2. The authors provide a clear comparison between their AMP approach and existing methods, particularly variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF) and Lloyd's K-means. Their experimental results demonstrate that the proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms Lloyd's K-means in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy, especially for high-dimensional data.
3. The mathematical derivation, while complex, is well-structured and logically progresses from the Bayesian formulation to the AMP algorithm. The connection between the AMP algorithm and the variational Bayes approach is well-explained.
4. The paper is generally well-written with clear technical explanations and appropriate mathematical notation (once the notation is understood).

Weaknesses:
1. The connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction is not novel (as established in prior work by Xu et al., 2003 and Ding et al., 2010), and the paper does not adequately acknowledge this in the main text. The abstract and Section 2.2 initially suggest this is an original contribution, which is misleading. The authors should clarify that this connection is well-established and focus on the novelty of their AMP-based implementation.
2. The derivation of the algorithm is relegated to the supplementary material, which is problematic for a conference paper. The main paper should provide at least an outline of the derivation, especially given the importance of the AMP technique.
3. The experimental evaluation relies on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization) rather than conventional matrix reconstruction tasks. While the authors justify this by the novelty of their approach, they do not sufficiently explain why they cannot evaluate on standard datasets like Netflix (which was used in prior VBMF work by Lim and Teh, 2007). The lack of comparison on standard matrix reconstruction problems weakens the claim of broader applicability.
4. The notation is unnecessarily complicated, with excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For example, the notation $\hat{p}$, $\hat{q}$, $\hat{r}$ appears without explanation, and the $t$ superscripts in Algorithm 2 could be eliminated by using assignment operators instead. The paper would be significantly more readable with simpler notation.
5. The role of the factor $m$ in the noise variance (which makes the algorithm asymmetric in rows and columns) is not clearly explained, despite its importance for the algorithm's behavior.

Recommendation:
The paper presents a valid technical contribution to the AMP framework for matrix factorization. However, the lack of clarity in the main text, the non-standard evaluation approach, and the insufficient acknowledgment of prior work on the clustering-matrix factorization connection are significant weaknesses. The paper would be much stronger if it clarified the novelty of its approach to the clustering problem, provided a more accessible derivation of the algorithm in the main text, and evaluated on standard matrix reconstruction problems.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel application of AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural priors
- Clear experimental results showing performance improvements over existing methods in clustering
- Well-structured mathematical presentation (despite some notational issues)

Arguments against acceptance:
- The clustering-matrix factorization connection is well-established in prior work
- Algorithm derivation is only in supplementary material, making the paper less accessible
- Experimental evaluation on a non-standard problem limits the perceived significance
- Unnecessarily complex notation hinders readability

Final recommendation: Weak acceptance. The paper presents a technically sound contribution but requires significant improvements to its presentation and justification before it can be accepted for publication at NIPS.
