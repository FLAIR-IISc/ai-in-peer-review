Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering" 

This paper contributes a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on an application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, as detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates two simplifying approximations based on the assumption of a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated in a novel manner against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem. It is also compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

**Cons**

There are arguments against accepting the paper. Since a new algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem – clustering encoded as matrix factorization – it is not straightforward to extrapolate the experimental results to predict its performance on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, two references are cited for VBMF (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen, 2007), and both of these papers evaluate their algorithms using the Netflix dataset. It would be ideal if the present paper had used the same dataset. Although BP is generally more accurate than Variational methods, evaluating this specific BP variant using a novel criterion raises doubts about its true competitiveness. Even if the Netflix dataset or a similar one is not feasible, the authors should explain why this is the case. 

The algorithm itself appears to be a fairly direct application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While novel, it isn't fundamentally groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect, in my opinion, lies in the approximations introduced in the limits $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ represents the dimensions of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, detailed only in the supplementary material, is never directly tested, and I believe they could be explained more clearly.

There are serious issues regarding the citation of prior work. Initially, I understood the paper to introduce the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution. The abstract and Section 2.2 create this impression. I felt misled when I learned from other reviewers that this connection is well-established. There is no clear reason why the paper doesn't acknowledge this existing knowledge. If it is widely known, the authors should state it explicitly; otherwise, they should cite relevant prior work. 

Furthermore, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as that is the common term for applying BP to continuous classes of distributions. The relationship between EP and the paper’s algorithm should be explained. Relatedly, the core algorithm is most readily understood as an application of Belief Propagation, but this fact is not mentioned until Section 4.1. It should be stated in the abstract.

**Pros**

The paper is interesting and presents a new, potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is comprehensible, and while I haven’t replicated it by hand, I believe it would be possible to do so. Despite reservations about the new evaluation criteria, the K-means problem may be sufficiently general to allow for a fair comparison of the algorithms, and the experiments do demonstrate a benefit for the new algorithm.

**Clarity**

The early presentation is relatively easy to follow. The introduction is clear, as is the summary of existing work. The derivation of the algorithm being relegated to the Supplementary Material is a drawback; I would prefer to see an outline of the derivation in the main paper. The experiments section is clear, and although some plots appear poorly in black and white, they remain readable.

I found the description of the algorithm itself difficult to understand, preventing me from verifying the correctness of the derivation. The mathematical presentation is the weakest aspect of the paper. Even on a superficial level, it is difficult to parse. For example, I don’t understand why factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ appear before each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e. These could be factored out to improve readability. Additionally, the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.

There seems to be an excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For instance, there is a $\tau_w$ but no $\tau$; simply replacing $\tau_w$ with $\tau$ would suffice. Also, Section 4, the most important section, contains no p, q, or r – only $\hat{p}$, $\hat{q}$, and $\hat{r}$. The authors should state at the beginning of the section: “We will be dropping the hats here for brevity.” The ‘t’ superscripts in the messages seem wholly unnecessary. Changing "=" to "\gets" on the left-hand side would eliminate the need for all the ‘t’s. I find it hard to believe that the authors originally derived the algorithm using such cumbersome notation. I suggest revisiting the initial notation used and looking for simpler alternatives.

The exposition could be improved. For example, the authors could explain that equation 5 is the negative logarithm of equation 2, or that equation 8 is simply equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$. Algorithm 2 appears almost row-column symmetrical; the authors should point this out and even reduce its length by stating, “then copy and paste these equations, switching u and v.”

The meaning of the functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definition. It would be clearer to state “$f_\beta$ is the mean of $q_\beta$” rather than simply providing the equation. If an equation is given, its meaning should also be stated. At the end of Section 4.2, it states “$G_\beta(b, \Lambda; p)$ is a scaled covariance matrix of $q_\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)$,” but this wasn’t obvious initially and wasn’t even mentioned when $G$ was defined.

None of the messages have $\beta$ subscripts, despite their dependence on $f$ and $G$, which do have $\beta$ subscripts. However, $f$ and $G$ depend only on $q_\beta$, not directly on $\beta$. I suggest eliminating the subscripts entirely, even from $q$. The parameter $\beta$ is simply a global variable, which is perfectly acceptable. Before equations (15) and (16), the authors should state something like: “in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, $f$ and $G$ take the following form:”.

On a deeper level, I have several questions about the algorithm. What is the significance of the $m$ factor in the additive noise variance? Does it play a role in applications of matrix factorization? Does it contribute to the approximation used to derive the algorithm? Is it the only factor preventing the algorithm from being row-column symmetrical? I believe the authors have answers to these questions but haven’t commented on them.

**Other Questions:**

Is it standard to use a tilde to denote column vectors? I didn't understand this initially. Just curious – why is $N$ capitalized, but $m$ lowercase? In Section 5, I would like a citation after VBMF-MA to indicate the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competing algorithm. Two citations for variational matrix factorization appear earlier in the paper, and it’s unclear which is intended (or if it is both).

In the supplementary material: For equation 5, writing a product of exponentials for the two terms involving $z$ would make it more obvious that they represent probability density functions. In Section 2, I struggled with the step from equation 14 to 15 regarding the big-O notation of $m$ terms. I may need to consider it further, but this is where I got stuck. 

Also in the supplementary material, at the top of page 2, it states: “A technique used in … is to give an approximate representation of these messages in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without reading these references, I’m unsure how exactly the approximation described below this text relates to prior publications. Clarifying the novelty of the algorithm’s derivation in the text itself, and even in the main paper, would be beneficial.

The English is generally good, and the meaning is always conveyed, but there are instances where it reads as if written by someone not fully proficient in the use of articles. This can be distracting for some readers. Even the title could be improved – perhaps “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm.” I have compiled a list of additional suggestions for the authors:

p. 1
“Since properties” -> “Since the properties”
“according to problems” -> “according to the problem”
“has flexibility” -> “has enough flexibility”
“motivate use of” -> “motivate the use of”

p. 2
“We present results” -> “We present the results”

p. 3
“We regard that” -> “We see that”
“maximum accuracy clustering” could be italicized
“Previous works” -> “Previous work”
“ICM algorithm; It” -> “ICM algorithm: it”

p. 4
“particularized” -> “specialized”

p. 5
“plausible properties” -> “discernible properties”

p. 6
“stuck to bad local minima” -> “stuck in bad local minima”

p. 7
“This is contrastive to that” -> “This is in contrast to the fact that”

The Supplementary Material also contains some language issues:

p. 2
“of these message” -> “of these messages”
“a few number” -> “a few”

I found the diagram in Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material very helpful. Creating more diagrams would enhance the paper.

The mathematics is interesting, and the algorithm deserves publication, even as a first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization, as it fills an important niche. However, the paper needs further work before it can be accepted. The current lack of clarity and scholarship places an unacceptable burden on readers and would reflect poorly on the conference.
