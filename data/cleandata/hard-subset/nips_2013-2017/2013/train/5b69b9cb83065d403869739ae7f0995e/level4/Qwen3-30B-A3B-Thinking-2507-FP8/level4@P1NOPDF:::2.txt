

Review of "Low-rank Matrix Reconstruction and Clustering via Approximate Message Passing"  

This paper introduces a new algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Belief Propagation (BP) applied to a Bayesian model. The method, detailed in the supplementary material, employs two approximations assuming large row and column counts in the input matrix. It evaluates performance by framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem, comparing against Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF)—the only prior BP-based reconstruction algorithm.  

**Concerns**  
The experimental setup poses significant challenges. Evaluating the algorithm on clustering (encoded as matrix factorization) makes it hard to generalize results to standard matrix reconstruction tasks. Both VBMF papers (Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007) use the Netflix dataset for benchmarking; replicating this would strengthen competitiveness claims. The paper’s novel evaluation metric, while interesting, creates ambiguity about the algorithm’s true performance relative to established methods. The authors should explicitly justify the dataset choice or adopt Netflix if feasible.  

The algorithm itself appears to be a straightforward adaptation of BP to a problem previously addressed via Variational Message Passing. While novel, it lacks groundbreaking impact. The most compelling aspect—the asymptotic approximations as $N, m \to \infty$ (where $m \times N$ are the matrix dimensions)—is only described in the supplementary material and lacks validation. These approximations require clearer explanation and justification.  

Critical scholarship issues undermine the paper. The abstract and Section 2.2 imply that matrix factorization for clustering is a novel contribution, yet this connection is well-established. The authors must clarify this upfront—either by citing key prior work or stating it as a known approach. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is the standard term for BP applied to continuous distributions, and the relationship to the proposed algorithm must be explained. The core BP application is first mentioned in Section 4.1, but should be highlighted in the abstract.  

**Clarity and Presentation**  
The mathematics is the paper’s weakest element. Key notational choices hinder comprehension:  
- Unnecessary symbols (e.g., $\tau_w$ instead of $\tau$; excessive hats, tildes, and superscripts like $t$ in messages).  
- Ambiguous terms (e.g., factors of $1/m\tau_w$ in equations 9a–9e, which could be factored out; uncombined terms in 9b and 9e).  
- Section 4’s heavy notation (e.g., $\hat{p}, \hat{q}, \hat{r}$) could be simplified by stating upfront: "We omit hats for brevity."  
- Explanatory gaps: Why is equation 5 the negative log of equation 2? Why is equation 8 a power of equation 2? Algorithm 2’s row-column symmetry could be noted to reduce redundancy.  

The paper also omits critical context:  
- The role of $m$ in additive noise variance (breaking row-column symmetry) is unexplained.  
- The use of $\tilde{z}$ for column vectors is unclear.  
- Why $N$ is capitalized but $m$ is not.  
- Section 5 lacks a citation for VBMF-MA’s primary reference.  

Minor language issues persist despite generally strong English: "a few number" → "a few," "stuck to bad local minima" → "stuck in bad local minima," "particularized" → "specialized." The title should specify the method: "Low-rank Matrix Reconstruction and Clustering via Approximate Message Passing."  

**Strengths**  
The paper presents a potentially useful algorithm that is mathematically coherent (though notation obscures this). The clustering-as-reconstruction framing offers a reasonable benchmark, and the new algorithm shows clear advantages in these experiments. The introduction and early sections are clear, and Figure 1 in the supplementary material is helpful.  

**Conclusion**  
This work fills a niche as the first BP-based matrix factorization algorithm, but the current submission is insufficient for publication. Major gaps in clarity, scholarship, and justification—particularly regarding the non-standard evaluation, unvalidated approximations, and misleading novelty claims—place an unacceptable burden on readers. Addressing the notation issues, clarifying the asymptotic approximations, and properly contextualizing the contributions would significantly strengthen the paper. As written, it lacks the rigor expected at NIPS.
