Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper introduces an algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that applies belief-propagation-style message passing within a Bayesian framework. The method, with derivation deferred to the Supplementary Material, relies on two large-system approximations (assuming both the number of rows and columns are large). The empirical evaluation takes a nonstandard route by recasting clustering as a low-rank reconstruction task, enabling a comparison with Lloyd’s K-means. The approach is also contrasted with Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), which appears to be the closest prior message-passing-style reconstruction baseline.

Cons
There are several reasons for caution. Because the evaluation focuses on clustering encoded as matrix factorization, it is difficult to infer performance on standard matrix reconstruction tasks. For example, the two VBMF references cited (Lim and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen, 2007) use the Netflix dataset for benchmarking against earlier methods; ideally, this paper would include that dataset or a comparable one. While BP-type methods often outperform variational approaches, assessing this BP variant under a new evaluation criterion leaves open questions about its real competitiveness. If Netflix or similar data are impractical here, the paper should explicitly state why.

Methodologically, the core contribution seems a relatively direct application of BP to a problem previously treated via variational message passing. Although novel, it does not feel groundbreaking. The most intriguing aspect is the pair of approximations taken in the limits N→∞ and m→∞, but their validity is neither directly tested nor clearly explained in the main text (and appears only in the supplement).

The treatment of related work needs tightening. On first reading, the abstract and Section 2.2 give the impression that connecting clustering to matrix factorization is a new contribution, whereas this connection is already established. The paper should be explicit about this (either stating it is well known if no single canonical citation exists, or providing appropriate references). In addition, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, since EP is often the umbrella under which BP-like approximations for continuous distributions are discussed, and the relationship between EP and the proposed algorithm should be clarified. Finally, the fact that the main algorithm arises from belief propagation is only stated in Section 4.1; it should be mentioned in the abstract.

Pros
The paper is engaging and presents a potentially useful new algorithm. The mathematical development is readable and, although I did not re-derive it fully, it seems tractable to check. While new evaluation protocols warrant scrutiny, framing the benchmark via the K-means objective is arguably general enough to be informative, and the experiments do show an advantage for the proposed method.

Clarity
I found the early sections clear: the introduction and summary of prior work are well presented. However, relegating the derivation to the Supplementary Material is a weakness; a concise outline in the main paper would help. The experiments are clearly described, and even though some plots are not optimal in grayscale, they remain interpretable.

The algorithm presentation is the least accessible part, and I could not fully verify the derivation. Even superficially, some expressions are hard to parse. For instance, factors of 1/(m τ_w) appear repeatedly in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e; factoring these out would aid readability. Likewise, the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be consolidated.

Notation feels heavier than necessary (many hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts). For example, there is a τ_w but no τ; if possible, consider replacing τ_w with τ. In Section 4, the most critical one, only hatted quantities appear; a brief note at the start saying “we drop hats for brevity” would help. The superscript t for iterations could be removed by using assignment arrows (←) where t+1 appears, eliminating most ts on the right-hand side.

Exposition can be streamlined. It would help to state explicitly that equation (5) is the negative log of (2), and that equation (8) is simply (2) raised to the power β. Algorithm 2 is nearly symmetric in rows and columns; pointing this out and indicating how to transpose the u- and v-updates would both highlight structure and cut repetition.

The functions in (10) should be interpreted at the point of definition: for instance, “fβ is the mean of qβ, and Gβ is its β-scaled covariance,” alongside the formulas. As written, the observation near the end of Section 4.2 that Gβ is a scaled covariance is not immediate from the earlier definitions. Also, the β-dependence is inconsistent across symbols: messages do not carry β, yet they depend on f and G, which do. Since f and G only depend on β through qβ, I suggest treating β as a global parameter without proliferating β-subscripts; just note before (15) and (16) that as β→∞, f and G take the stated forms.

At a deeper level, I would appreciate more explanation of the factor m in the additive noise variance. What role does it play in applications of matrix factorization? How does it enter the approximations used to derive the algorithm? It appears to be the main source of row–column asymmetry; is that correct? These points seem known to the authors but are not discussed.

Other questions:
- Is using a tilde to denote column vectors standard? This was not immediately clear.
- Why is N uppercase while m is lowercase?
- In Section 5, please cite the primary reference that guided the VBMF-MA implementation; two VBMF citations appear earlier, and it is unclear which (or whether both) were followed.

In the supplementary material: For equation (5), writing the two z-related terms as a product of exponentials would make it more obvious which one is the pdf. In Section 2, the transition from (14) to (15) involving O(m) terms was hard to follow; more detail would help. Also, at the top of page 2, the text says “A technique used in ... is to give an approximate representation of these message in terms of a few number of real-valued parameters.” Without consulting those references, the relationship between the present approximation and prior work is opaque; please clarify what is new about the derivation in both the supplement and the main paper.

Language and style: The English is strong overall, but occasional article usage and phrasing may distract readers. Even the title could be improved (e.g., “Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering using an approximate message passing algorithm”). Specific suggestions include:
- p. 1
  - “Since properties” -> “Since the properties”
  - “according to problems” -> “according to the problem”
  - “has flexibility” -> “has enough flexibility”
  - “motivate use of” -> “motivate the use of”
- p. 2
  - “We present results” -> “We present the results”
- p. 3
  - “We regard that” -> “We see that”
  - consider italicizing “maximum accuracy clustering”
  - “Previous works” -> “Previous work”
  - “ICM algorithm; It” -> “ICM algorithm: it”
- p. 4
  - “particularized” -> “specialized”
- p. 5
  - “plausible properties” -> perhaps “notable properties”
- p. 6
  - “stuck to bad local minima” -> “stuck in bad local minima”
- p. 7
  - “This is contrastive to that” -> “This is in contrast to the fact that”
Supplement:
- p. 2
  - “of these message” -> “of these messages”
  - “a few number” -> “a few”

I appreciated the diagram in Figure 1 of the Supplement; adding similar diagrams to the main paper would further improve clarity.

In sum, the mathematics is promising and the algorithm merits publication somewhere; as an early application of belief propagation to matrix factorization, it fills a meaningful gap. However, in its current form the paper needs substantial improvements in clarity and scholarship. As it stands, it places too much burden on the reader, which would reflect poorly on a conference if accepted without revision.
