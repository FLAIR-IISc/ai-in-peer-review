This paper presents an algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations, developed within a Bayesian framework using Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The authors make two primary contributions. First, they derive a general-rank AMP algorithm that extends previous work by enabling not only posterior mean estimation but also Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) estimation. This is accomplished by introducing an inverse temperature parameter, `β`, which unifies the treatment of marginalization and MAP problems. Second, they demonstrate a novel and effective application of this framework to the problem of K-means clustering. By reformulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction task where one factor matrix is constrained to contain one-hot encoded cluster assignments, they derive a new iterative algorithm for K-means.

The proposed approach is situated within the context of existing methods for matrix factorization. The authors correctly position their work relative to computationally expensive Monte Carlo methods and deterministic approaches like Variational Bayes (VB). They argue that AMP, derived from the Bethe approximation of belief propagation, can capture dependencies between the factor matrices more effectively than the mean-field approximation underlying VB. The connection between low-rank matrix factorization and clustering has been explored previously (e.g., by Ding et al., 2010), but the direct derivation of a clustering algorithm that explicitly handles the one-hot assignment constraint within the AMP framework appears to be a novel contribution.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The core technical contribution—a flexible AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation—is a valuable extension of prior work in this domain. The application to K-means clustering is particularly insightful. The resulting update rule (Equation 18b) provides a fresh perspective on the classic Lloyd's algorithm, introducing intuitive correction terms that account for a data point's influence on its own cluster center and penalize assignments to already large clusters. This is a strong and interesting result that could inspire further work.

2.  **Technical Quality and Experimental Support:** The claims of the paper are well-supported by a solid experimental evaluation. The authors conduct experiments on both synthetic and real-world data (ORL Face Database), comparing their methods against appropriate and strong baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization). The results are compelling, showing that the proposed AMP-based algorithms consistently outperform their counterparts in terms of both the K-means loss function and clustering accuracy. The fast convergence reported for the AMP methods is also a significant practical advantage.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The problem setting is laid out precisely, and the connection between the general matrix reconstruction problem and the specific clustering application is well-explained. The algorithms are presented clearly, and the high-level intuition behind the AMP approach and its advantages over VB is effectively communicated.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Guarantees:** The primary weakness of the paper is the lack of a formal proof of convergence for the proposed algorithm. This is a common limitation for AMP-style algorithms and many clustering heuristics. While Proposition 1 offers a valuable insight—that any fixed point of the AMP-MAP algorithm is also a fixed point of the Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm—it does not guarantee that the algorithm will converge. The authors acknowledge this and note empirically that they sometimes observe oscillations, requiring a modified stopping criterion.

2.  **Context of Related Work:** While the paper references other recent AMP algorithms for related problems [13, 14], the comparison is brief. A more detailed discussion of the technical differences, particularly regarding the assumptions on the scaling of the rank `r` versus the matrix dimensions `m` and `N`, would help readers better understand the specific contributions and limitations of this work in the broader context of AMP literature. Additionally, given the focus on K-means, acknowledging the extensive literature on provably good approximation algorithms for K-means from the theoretical computer science community would provide a more complete picture, clarifying that the proposed method is a powerful heuristic rather than an approximation algorithm with worst-case guarantees.

### **Summary of Arguments**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated AMP-based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction that unifies marginalization and MAP estimation.
*   The application to K-means clustering is insightful, leading to a new algorithm that empirically outperforms standard methods like Lloyd's and K-means++.
*   The experimental results are thorough, convincing, and demonstrate the practical effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed approach on both synthetic and real data.
*   The paper is clearly written and the contributions are well-articulated.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The algorithm lacks theoretical convergence guarantees, which is a significant theoretical limitation, even if common for this class of problems.
*   The positioning with respect to closely related contemporary AMP literature could be more detailed.

### **Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a solid contribution to both the literature on approximate inference and the practical toolkit for clustering. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the K-means algorithm derived from the AMP framework and its impressive empirical performance, substantially outweigh the weakness of lacking a convergence proof. The work is likely to be of significant interest to researchers in machine learning, signal processing, and related fields. I recommend acceptance.
