This paper studies low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations in a Bayesian framework that can encode additional structure on the factors (e.g., sparsity, nonnegativity). The authors develop an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm derived from belief propagation to perform efficient approximate inference. They further extend prior AMP formulations to handle not only posterior means but also MAP estimation via a “temperature” parameter β, unifying marginalization and MAP within a single procedure. A second contribution is an application to clustering: by casting K-means as a constrained low-rank factorization with one-hot assignments, they derive an AMP-based clustering algorithm that directly enforces the “exactly one cluster per point” constraint. Experiments on synthetic data and the ORL face dataset compare both reconstruction error (Frobenius norm) and clustering accuracy, showing improvements over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ in high dimensions, and competitive performance against variational Bayes matrix factorization baselines.

Relation to prior work: AMP has a strong track record in compressed sensing, and rank-one matrix settings (e.g., IterFac). General-rank AMP variants exist but typically target posterior means; enabling MAP estimation and explicitly handling one-hot assignment constraints for clustering appear to be novel. While connections between low-rank factorization and K-means are well known (e.g., through PCA/NMF-based formulations), the paper leverages this link effectively for a practical algorithm and clear empirical comparisons.

Strengths:
- A clean AMP formulation for low-rank reconstruction that flexibly incorporates structural priors and unifies marginalization and MAP via β.
- Low per-iteration cost O(mN) and an AMP form that closely relates to (and improves upon) variational-Bayes/ICM-style updates.
- A principled derivation of an AMP variant for K-means that includes intuitive correction terms (addressing bias toward currently assigned clusters and favoring smaller clusters).
- Thorough empirical evaluation on synthetic and real data, reporting both objective loss and clustering accuracy; consistent gains over K-means/K-means++ and favorable comparisons to VBMF.

Weaknesses:
- Limited theoretical guarantees. Convergence is assumed, and the paper does not provide state evolution, conditions for convergence, or recovery guarantees. Given the AMP literature, some analysis (even partial or asymptotic) would strengthen the contribution.
- Practical details such as estimation of τ, handling oscillations, and sensitivity to initialization merit deeper discussion. The reported oscillations and occasional collapse to a single cluster on ORL suggest the need for stabilization heuristics or damping guidance.
- The clustering comparisons focus primarily on K-means variants and VBMF. Broader baselines (e.g., spectral clustering or recent provable algorithms) and citations from the theoretical computer science literature on K-means guarantees would better contextualize the contribution.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a brief state-evolution analysis or at least a discussion of when the AMP fixed points are expected to be accurate and stable; clarify conditions under which the extra Onsager-like terms are positive semidefinite.
- Include citations to provable K-means algorithms and spectral methods, and discuss how the proposed AMP approach compares in settings where guarantees exist.
- Provide ablation results contrasting AMP with/without the additional correction terms to quantify their impact, and report robustness to initialization and τ estimation.

Overall assessment: A solid and timely contribution that extends AMP to low-rank matrix reconstruction with MAP estimation and delivers an effective AMP-based clustering algorithm. The empirical results are promising and the methodology is well-motivated. Theoretical guarantees are the main weakness, but the paper is likely to be of interest to the NIPS community.
