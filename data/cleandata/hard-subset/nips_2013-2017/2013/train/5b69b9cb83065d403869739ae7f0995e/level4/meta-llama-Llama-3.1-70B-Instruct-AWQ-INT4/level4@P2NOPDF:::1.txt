The authors propose an algorithm for matrix reconstruction under noisy observations, focusing on low-rank matrices with specific assumptions on the factors. They utilize an Approximate Message Passing approach to expedite the computationally expensive classical Bayesian method. Notably, the authors establish a connection between matrix reconstruction and K-means clustering, providing an interesting application domain for their algorithm.

To the best of my knowledge, applying Approximate Message Passing to matrix reconstruction is a novel and intriguing concept. Although the relationship between low-rank matrix factorizations and K-means is well-established (e.g., PCA offers a factor two approximation algorithm for K-means), this connection enables the authors to conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation of their algorithm and compare it to K-means and K-means++. The empirical results suggest that their approach is faster and more efficient than classical K-means and K-means++. A notable aspect of their experimental evaluation is the comparison of both the Frobenius norm residual and the actual clusterings.

The primary limitation of the paper lies in the scarcity of theoretical guarantees for the proposed algorithm's convergence. This is not unexpected, as many K-means algorithms also exhibit weak theoretical properties. To strengthen their work, the authors may consider citing relevant papers from Theoretical Computer Science that provide provably accurate algorithms for the K-means objective function.

Overall, the paper presents a solid contribution to Approximate Message Passing algorithms for low-rank matrix reconstruction and K-means, with promising experimental results, albeit somewhat limited theoretical guarantees.
