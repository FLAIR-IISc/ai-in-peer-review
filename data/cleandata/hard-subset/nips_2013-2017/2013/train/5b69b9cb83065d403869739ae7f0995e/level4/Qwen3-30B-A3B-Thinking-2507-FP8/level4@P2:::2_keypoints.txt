- Paper introduces an AMP algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, extending AMP beyond rank-one/posterior-mean cases.
- Authors formulate matrix reconstruction as Bayesian inference with flexible priors to encode structure beyond low rank (e.g., sparsity).
- Proposed AMP algorithm handles both marginalization and MAP estimation and is applied to clustering via a low-rank reformulation.
- Experimental results (synthetic and real data) show the AMP-based clustering outperforms K-means++, VBMF, and Lloyd’s algorithm in accuracy and convergence speed.
- Technical derivation appears mathematically rigorous, though much of it is in supplementary material.
- The clustering↔matrix-reconstruction connection is presented as novel but was previously established (e.g., Xu et al. 2003; Ding et al. 2010), undermining the originality claim.
- Evaluation is limited to clustering; no experiments on standard matrix-reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., Netflix) to demonstrate broader applicability.
- Paper has significant clarity issues: overly complex notation (excessive hats/tildes/subscripts), inconsistent scaling factors, and key derivations relegated to the supplement.
- Important design choices are unexplained (e.g., the role of factor "m" in noise variance and its symmetry implications).
- Authors do not justify why AMP is preferred over established alternatives like variational Bayes or EP.
- Theoretical approximations underlying AMP lack direct validation and are only briefly described in supplementary material.
- Originality: AMP extension to general-rank matrix reconstruction is novel; applying it to clustering is not a novel contribution.
- Significance: The algorithm shows promise for clustering, but narrow experimental scope limits perceived impact.
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a technical gap by extending AMP, demonstrates practical clustering advantages, and introduces a potentially broadly useful algorithm.
- Arguments against acceptance: misstates prior work on clustering-matrix links, limited experimental validation, clarity and presentation problems, and insufficient theoretical validation.
- Recommendation: reject in current form but encourage revision addressing clarity, prior-work acknowledgement, broader experiments, and more thorough theoretical analysis.
