Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, described in the "Supplementary Material", incorporates two simplifying approximations, assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The algorithm is evaluated against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and compared against Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the only previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

Cons
There are several arguments against accepting the paper in its current form. Since the algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), it is challenging to extrapolate the experimental results to predict its performance on more conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, two references are cited for VBMF, Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen (2007), which use the Netflix dataset to evaluate their algorithm against predecessors. Ideally, the present paper should have used the same dataset for comparison. Although BP is generally more accurate than Variational methods, evaluating the present BP variant using a new criterion raises doubts about its actual competitiveness. If the Netflix or a similar dataset cannot be used, the authors should explain this in the paper.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. While new, it is not groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect of the algorithm is the approximations introduced in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ are the dimensions of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, described only in the supplementary material, is not directly tested, and their explanation could be clearer.

There are serious issues regarding the citation of prior work. The paper gives the impression that it introduces the application of matrix factorization to clustering as an original contribution, particularly in the abstract and Section 2.2. However, this connection is well-known, and the paper should make this clear to the reader. If it is too well-known to cite any particular reference, the authors should state this explicitly. Otherwise, they should cite prior work. Additionally, Expectation Propagation (EP) should be cited, as it is commonly referred to when applying BP to continuous classes of distributions, and its relationship to the paper's algorithm should be explained.

Pros
The paper is interesting to read and presents a new and potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally followable, and although the derivation is not replicated by hand, it seems possible to do so. While the new evaluation criteria may raise suspicions, the K-means problem may be general enough to provide a fair comparison of the algorithms, and the experiments demonstrate a benefit for the new algorithm.

Clarity
The early presentation is fairly easy to follow, with a clear introduction and summary of earlier work. However, the derivation of the algorithm only appearing in the Supplemental Material is a drawback. It would be beneficial to outline the derivation in the main paper. The experiments section is clear, and although some plots are difficult to read in black and white, they are still understandable.

The description of the algorithm is challenging to understand, and the correctness of the derivation is difficult to verify. The mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation, even at a superficial level. For instance, the factors of $1 / m \tau_w$ before each term in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be factored out to make the expressions easier to read. Additionally, the last two terms in 9b and 9e could be combined.

There is an excess of notation, including hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. For example, $\tauw$ could be replaced with $\tau$. In Section 4, the use of hats could be simplified by stating that they will be dropped for brevity. The 't' superscripts in the messages seem unnecessary and could be removed by changing "=" to "\gets".

The exposition could be improved by explaining the relationships between equations, such as equation 5 being the negative logarithm of equation 2, or equation 8 being equation 2 to the power of $\beta$. Algorithm 2 is almost row-column symmetrical, and this could be pointed out to make it clearer. The meaning of functions in equation 10 should be explained near their definition.

The messages do not have $\beta$ subscripts, despite depending on $f$ and $G$ which have $\beta$ subscripts. However, $f$ and $G$ do not depend directly on $\beta$, only on $q_\beta$. It is not clear why these subscripts are propagated only as far as $f$ and $G$ and not further. Eliminating them entirely, even from $q$, might simplify the notation.

Other questions and suggestions
Is it standard to use a tilde to denote column vectors? The use of capital "N" and lowercase "m" for matrix dimensions seems inconsistent. In Section 5, a citation is needed after VBMF-MA to indicate the primary reference guiding the implementation of this competing algorithm.

In the supplementary material, equation 5 could be rewritten as a product of exponentials to make it clearer that one term is the pdf. The step from equation 14 to 15 in Section 2 is not entirely clear, and it would be helpful to clarify the novelty of the algorithm's derivation in the text itself and the main paper.

The English is generally good, but there are some places where it reads like it was written by a non-native speaker. This can be distracting for some readers. Here is a list of suggested corrections:

p. 1 
"Since properties" -> "Since the properties"
"according to problems" -> "according to the problem"
"has flexibility" -> "has enough flexibility"
"motivate use of" -> "motivate the use of"

p. 2 
"We present results" -> "We present the results"

p. 3 
"We regard that" -> "We see that"
"maximum accuracy clustering" could be italicized
"Previous works" -> "Previous work"
"ICM algorithm; It" -> "ICM algorithm: it"

p. 4 
"particularized" -> "specialized"

p. 5 
"plausible properties" -> "discernable properties"

p. 6 
"stuck to bad local minima" -> "stuck in bad local minima"

p. 7 
"This is contrastive to that" -> "This is in contrast to the fact that"

The Supplementary Material also has some language issues, such as:

p. 2 
"of these message" -> "of these messages"
"a few number" -> "a few"

The diagram in Figure 1 in the Supplementary Material is very helpful, and adding more diagrams could improve the paper.

The mathematics seems interesting, and the algorithm should be published somewhere, as it fills an important role as the first effort to apply Belief Propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper needs more work before it can be accepted, as it lacks clarity and scholarship, which would reflect poorly on a conference that accepted it in its present state.
