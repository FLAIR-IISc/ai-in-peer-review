

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"  
This paper proposes a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction using Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing within a Bayesian framework. The method, detailed in the Supplementary Material, employs two approximations assuming large row and column counts in the input matrix. The evaluation innovatively compares the algorithm against Lloyd’s K-means by framing clustering as matrix reconstruction, alongside Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the sole prior message-passing reconstruction approach.  

Cons  
The non-standard problem formulation (clustering as matrix factorization) complicates generalization to conventional reconstruction tasks. The authors cite Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko et al. (2007) for VBMF but omit using the Netflix dataset—standard in prior VBMF evaluations—without justification. This undermines competitiveness claims, as BP typically outperforms Variational methods. The algorithm constitutes a straightforward BP application to a problem previously addressed via Variational Message Passing, lacking groundbreaking novelty. While the asymptotic approximations ($N \to \infty$, $m \to \infty$) are intriguing, their validity remains untested and poorly explained in the Supplementary Material. Critical citation issues include misrepresenting matrix-factorization-to-clustering as novel (Sections 2.2, abstract), when the connection is well-established; prior work must be acknowledged. EP (Expectation Propagation) should be cited, and the BP foundation should be stated in the abstract, not deferred to Section 4.1.  

Pros  
The paper presents a novel, potentially useful algorithm with accessible mathematics. The K-means comparison demonstrates clear benefits for the new method, offering a fair evaluation despite the non-standard criterion.  

Clarity  
Early sections are well-structured, but the algorithm derivation’s absence from the main paper is a significant drawback. The mathematics is the weakest aspect: cumbersome notation (excessive hats, tildes, subscripts; e.g., $\tau_w$ vs. $\tau$, redundant $t$ superscripts), unclear equation motivations (e.g., Equation 5 as the negative log of Equation 2), and unresolved symbol consistency (e.g., $\beta$ subscripts in $f_\beta$, $G_\beta$ but not in messages) impede comprehension. Key explanations are missing (e.g., why $1/(m\tau_w)$ factors appear in Equations 9a–9e; meaning of $G_\beta$). The row-column symmetry of Algorithm 2 is unnoted, and Section 4 lacks brevity (e.g., unnecessary hats). Deeper questions remain unaddressed: the role of $m$ in noise variance, its impact on asymmetry, and the significance of $\beta \to \infty$.  

Other Issues  
- English requires polishing (e.g., "a few number" → "a few," "stuck to bad local minima" → "stuck in bad local minima").  
- Section 5 needs a primary citation for VBMF-MA implementation.  
- Supplementary Material has notation gaps (e.g., "of these message" → "messages," unclear relation to prior work on message approximations).  
- Figure 1 is helpful; more diagrams would aid clarity.  

The mathematics is promising, and the algorithm fills a niche as the first BP application to matrix factorization. However, the paper’s clarity deficits and scholarly shortcomings impose an unjustified burden on readers, warranting revision before acceptance.
