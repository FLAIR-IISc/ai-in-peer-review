

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction, which the authors subsequently apply to clustering problems. They demonstrate that clustering can be reformulated as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with additional structural constraints, and derive an AMP algorithm that handles both the low-rank property and the clustering constraint. The work extends previous AMP approaches for compressed sensing [6,7] and the rank-one case [8] to the general-rank matrix reconstruction problem, with the key innovation being the application to clustering where each data point must be assigned to exactly one cluster.

Quality: The paper presents a theoretically grounded approach that appears well-motivated. However, several mathematical aspects are inadequately justified, making it difficult to fully assess the technical correctness. Specifically:

1. The asymptotic analysis in Section 2.1 assumes m and N grow in the same order, but the paper doesn't clearly justify this assumption or discuss its implications for practical applications.

2. In the appendix (line 139), the claim that the second term of (11) is O(1/√m) appears to omit the β parameter, which is a significant oversight in the theoretical development.

3. The connection between the finite-β AMP algorithm and the MAP problem needs more rigorous justification. The paper claims that "taking the limit β → ∞ yields an algorithm for the MAP problem," but fails to establish why limβ→∞ min KL = min limβ→∞ KL.

4. The proof of Proposition 1 is insufficiently detailed. The derivation of equation (17) is not explained, making it difficult to verify the convergence claims.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and clearly written overall. The main ideas are presented logically, with a good flow from problem formulation to algorithm derivation to experimental validation. The presentation of the algorithm and its application to clustering is particularly accessible. The experimental section is well-designed, with appropriate performance metrics and clear results.

Originality: The paper's main contribution lies in extending AMP to general-rank matrix reconstruction and applying it to clustering with the specific constraint that each data point must belong to exactly one cluster. However, the authors should more clearly distinguish their approach from existing work on applying expectation propagation (EP) to matrix factorization [Stern et al., 2009]. The algorithm appears to implement EP updates for matrix factorization, and the authors should acknowledge this connection and clarify how their approach differs from EP-based methods. Additionally, the connection to the "clutter problem" in EP literature [Minka, 2001] seems relevant, though the authors don't explore it.

Significance: The proposed AMP algorithm shows promising empirical results in clustering experiments, particularly on high-dimensional data where it outperforms K-means++. However, the significance would be stronger with more comprehensive experimental validation. The paper compares only to K-means++ and doesn't address how the method scales to larger datasets or how it compares to other clustering approaches. The theoretical results on the algorithm's convergence and performance are also limited, making it difficult to assess the broader impact of the work.

Arguments for acceptance:
- The paper addresses an important problem (low-rank matrix reconstruction with structural constraints)
- The proposed AMP algorithm is theoretically grounded and shows good empirical performance
- The connection between matrix reconstruction and clustering is insightful
- The paper is well-written and clearly structured

Arguments against acceptance:
- The mathematical derivations are too sketchy to be fully verified
- The connection to existing EP-based methods is not sufficiently acknowledged or distinguished
- The experimental validation is limited to comparisons with K-means++
- The theoretical claims about the algorithm's properties need stronger justification

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach with potential value for practitioners working on matrix factorization and clustering. While the core idea is promising, the paper would benefit from more rigorous mathematical treatment and clearer differentiation from existing methods. The technical concerns regarding the mathematical derivations are significant enough that I recommend revision to address these issues before acceptance.
