This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on Approximate Message Passing (AMP). The method is derived from a Belief Propagation (BP) approach to a Bayesian formulation of the problem, allowing for the incorporation of structural priors on the matrix factors. The algorithm is evaluated on a K-means clustering task, which is framed as a specific instance of low-rank matrix reconstruction. In experiments, the proposed method is shown to outperform Lloyd’s K-means algorithm and a Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF) approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Potential Utility:** The application of the AMP framework to general low-rank matrix reconstruction is a valuable contribution. It extends previous work that was limited to rank-one cases or posterior mean estimation. The resulting algorithm is interesting, appears mathematically sound, and has the potential to be a useful tool for this class of problems.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant performance improvement in the chosen application domain. On both synthetic and real-world clustering tasks, the proposed AMP-based algorithms (AMP-KM and AMP-MA) outperform well-established baselines like Lloyd’s K-means, K-means++, and VBMF, in terms of both K-means loss and clustering accuracy.

3.  **Unified Framework:** The parameterization using β to unify the marginalization problem (for posterior means) and the MAP problem is elegant. This provides a single algorithmic framework for tackling different inference goals.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of the Algorithm and its Derivation:** The core contribution, Algorithm 2, is presented in a way that is difficult to parse and build intuition for.
    *   The complete derivation is relegated to the supplementary material. A high-level outline of the key approximations (based on the large m, N limit) should be included in the main paper to make the work more self-contained.
    *   The mathematical notation is unnecessarily complex, which hinders readability. For example, in Eqs. (9a) and (9c), the `1/mτ` factor could be pulled outside the main expression. The update steps for U and V are nearly symmetric, a fact that could be highlighted to simplify the presentation. The numerous superscripts, subscripts, and hats could also be streamlined.
    *   The paper lacks conceptual explanations for key components. For instance, the functions `f` and `G` in Eq. (10) are only later identified as a scaled mean and covariance. Explaining this intuition at their introduction would greatly improve understanding.

2.  **Positioning and Scholarship:** The paper fails to adequately connect its contributions to the existing literature, which raises concerns about scholarship.
    *   The idea of formulating clustering as a low-rank matrix factorization problem is presented as if it were a novel contribution of this paper. This connection is, in fact, well-established in the literature. This should be explicitly stated, and prior work should be cited accordingly.
    *   The algorithm is derived from Belief Propagation on a model with continuous variables. The relationship to Expectation Propagation (EP), a standard and closely related framework for approximate inference in such models, is not discussed.
    *   The method is fundamentally a BP-based algorithm, yet this is not mentioned until Section 4.1. This information is crucial for context and should appear in the abstract and introduction.

3.  **Scope of Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to clustering. While the results are strong, this non-standard benchmark makes it difficult to assess the algorithm's performance on more conventional matrix reconstruction tasks, such as collaborative filtering. Prior work cited in the paper (e.g., [4, 5]) uses standard datasets like the Netflix prize data. Without a comparison on such a benchmark, it is hard to gauge the significance of the proposed method for the broader matrix reconstruction community. The authors should either include such experiments or justify why the clustering task is a sufficient and representative evaluation.

### **Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   **Notation Simplification:** To improve clarity, consider replacing the `t` and `t+1` superscripts with an assignment operator (`←`). Also, could the many variables with hats (e.g., `p̂`, `q̂`, `r̂`) be simplified? Perhaps a statement at the beginning of Section 4 could clarify that all distributions are assumed/approximate, allowing the hats to be dropped for readability.
*   **Role of `m` in Noise Variance:** The noise variance is defined as `mτ`. This factor appears to break the row-column symmetry of the problem. What is the theoretical motivation for this scaling? Is it essential for the derivation of the AMP algorithm, or does it play a practical role?
*   **Specific Citation:** In Section 5, please clarify which VBMF method was implemented for the VBMF-MA baseline, as both [4] and [5] are cited earlier for variational approaches.
*   **Supplementary Material:** The derivation step from Eq. (14) to (15) in the supplement, related to the order of magnitude of terms, was difficult to follow. Further clarification would be helpful. The paper should also be more explicit about what aspects of the derivation are novel versus what is adapted from prior AMP literature.
*   **Minor Language Suggestions:** The paper is well-written, but some minor grammatical edits would improve its polish. For example: "Since the properties" (p.1), "motivate the use of" (p.1), "stuck in bad local minima" (p.6), and "This is in contrast to" (p.7).

### **Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting and potentially powerful AMP algorithm for low-rank matrix factorization. The empirical results on clustering are impressive. However, the paper in its current form suffers from significant issues in clarity, positioning with respect to prior work, and the scope of its evaluation. The algorithm's presentation is difficult to follow, and the failure to acknowledge well-known concepts (like the matrix factorization/clustering link) is a serious flaw. While the core idea is promising, the paper requires substantial revision before it can be accepted.
