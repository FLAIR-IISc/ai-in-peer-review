This paper introduces a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction from noisy observations, leveraging an Approximate Message Passing (AMP) framework. The proposed method is designed as a computationally efficient alternative to traditional, and often expensive, Bayesian approaches. A key contribution is the application of this matrix reconstruction algorithm to the problem of K-means clustering, providing a new perspective on this well-studied task.

The primary strength of this work is the novel application of Approximate Message Passing to the matrix reconstruction problem. This appears to be a new and promising direction. The paper is supported by a strong experimental evaluation, where the authors effectively use K-means clustering as a practical testbed for their algorithm. The comparisons against standard baselines like K-means and K-means++ are thorough, and the empirical results are particularly noteworthy, suggesting that the proposed AMP-based algorithm is not only more accurate but also significantly faster than these established methods. The evaluation is robust, considering both the Frobenius norm for reconstruction error and the quality of the resulting clusterings, which strengthens the authors' claims.

The main weakness of the paper lies in its limited theoretical analysis. The proposed algorithm lacks formal guarantees regarding its convergence or the quality of the solution. While the authors' empirical results are compelling, the absence of theoretical support is a notable limitation. To be fair, providing such guarantees for non-convex problems like K-means is notoriously difficult, and many widely-used algorithms share this limitation.

To strengthen the paper and better position it within the existing literature, the authors should consider discussing and citing relevant work from the Theoretical Computer Science community on provably accurate algorithms for the K-means objective. This would provide valuable context and highlight the trade-offs between their efficient, heuristic approach and algorithms with formal performance bounds.

Overall, this is a solid contribution that introduces a novel and efficient AMP-based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with a compelling application to K-means clustering. The strengths lie in the novelty of the approach and a promising set of experimental results that demonstrate clear performance benefits over standard methods. While the theoretical foundation could be improved, the paper presents an interesting new direction and is likely to be of interest to the community.
