Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the application of Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model of the reconstruction problem. The algorithm, detailed in the "Supplementary Material", incorporates two simplifying approximations, assuming a large number of rows and columns in the input matrix. The authors evaluate the algorithm against Lloyd's K-means algorithm by formulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and compare it to Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), a previous message-passing reconstruction algorithm.

Cons

There are several concerns regarding the paper. Firstly, the algorithm is evaluated on a non-standard problem (clustering encoded as matrix factorization), making it challenging to generalize the experimental results to conventional matrix reconstruction problems. For instance, previous papers on VBMF (Lin and Teh, 2007; Raiko, Ilin and Karhunen, 2007) used the Netflix dataset for evaluation, which would be an ideal benchmark for comparison. The authors should explain why this dataset was not used.

The algorithm itself appears to be a relatively straightforward application of BP to a problem previously addressed with Variational Message Passing. Although new, it is not groundbreaking. The most interesting aspect of the algorithm is the approximations introduced in the limit $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$, where $m \times N$ is the dimension of the input matrix. However, the validity of these approximations, described only in the supplementary material, is not directly tested.

The paper has serious issues regarding the citation of prior work. The connection between matrix factorization and clustering is not clearly attributed, giving the impression that it is an original contribution. The authors should either cite prior work or acknowledge that this connection is well-known. Additionally, the paper should cite Expectation Propagation (EP) and explain its relationship to the proposed algorithm.

Pros

The paper is interesting to read and presents a new and potentially useful algorithm. The mathematics is generally followable, although some derivations are only presented in the supplementary material. The evaluation of the algorithm against Lloyd's K-means and VBMF is novel and shows a benefit for the new algorithm in the experiments.

Clarity

The early presentation is clear, with a well-written introduction and summary of earlier work. However, the derivation of the algorithm only appearing in the supplementary material is a drawback. The experiments section is clear, but some plots are difficult to read in black and white.

The description of the algorithm is difficult to understand, and the mathematics is the weakest part of the presentation. The notation is cumbersome, with excessive use of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts. The authors should consider simplifying the notation to improve readability.

Other issues include:

* The significance of the $m$ factor in the additive noise variance is not explained.
* The use of a tilde to denote column vectors is not standard.
* The capitalization of $N$ but not $m$ is inconsistent.
* A citation is needed for the primary reference guiding the implementation of VBMF-MA.
* The supplementary material could be improved with more diagrams and clearer explanations of the algorithm's derivation and novelty.

Language

The English is generally good, but there are some places where it reads like it was written by someone not entirely skilled in the use of articles. This can be distracting for some readers. Suggestions for improvement are provided.

Conclusion

The mathematics seems interesting, and the algorithm should be published somewhere, filling an important role as the first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization. However, the paper needs more work before it can be accepted. The paper is sufficiently lacking in clarity and scholarship as to put an unacceptable burden on readers, which would reflect poorly on a conference that accepted it in its present state.
