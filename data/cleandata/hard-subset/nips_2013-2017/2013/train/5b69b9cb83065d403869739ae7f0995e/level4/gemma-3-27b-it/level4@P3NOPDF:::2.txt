## Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"

This paper presents a novel algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction based on applying Belief Propagation (BP) message-passing to a Bayesian model. The algorithm, detailed in the Supplementary Material, incorporates approximations based on the large-dimensional limits of the input matrix ($N$ rows and $m$ columns). The authors evaluate the algorithm in a unique manner, framing clustering as a matrix reconstruction problem and comparing it against Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF), the latter being the primary prior work using message passing for reconstruction.

**Cons**

A primary concern is the evaluation methodology. Assessing the algorithm on clustering, formulated as matrix factorization, makes it difficult to extrapolate the results to more conventional matrix reconstruction scenarios. Existing VBMF literature, such as Lin and Teh (2007) and Raiko, Ilin, and Karhunen (2007), uses the Netflix dataset for evaluation. While the authors may have reasons for not using such a dataset (which should be explicitly stated), using the same benchmark would strengthen the comparative analysis. Although BP is generally more accurate than Variational Bayes methods, evaluating this specific BP variant using a non-standard criterion raises questions about its true competitiveness.

The algorithm itself represents a relatively direct application of BP to a problem previously tackled with Variational Message Passing and, while novel, isn’t a particularly groundbreaking advancement. The most intriguing aspect lies in the approximations introduced in the limits $N\to\infty$ and $m\to\infty$. However, the validity of these approximations, currently relegated to the Supplementary Material, remains untested and requires more thorough explanation in the main paper. 

A significant issue pertains to the presentation of prior work. The paper initially implies the novelty of applying matrix factorization to clustering (in the abstract and Section 2.2). This creates a misleading impression, rectified only by information from other reviewers. The authors should explicitly acknowledge this established connection, either by citing relevant prior work or stating that it is a well-known approach. 

Furthermore, the connection to Expectation Propagation (EP) – typically understood as applying BP to continuous distributions – is missing. The algorithm’s relationship to EP should be explicitly discussed, and the fact that the core algorithm is a Belief Propagation application should be mentioned earlier, ideally in the abstract, rather than being delayed until Section 4.1.

**Pros**

The paper presents a new and potentially valuable algorithm that is interesting to read. The mathematical formulation is largely comprehensible, and I believe replication is feasible, though not attempted. Despite reservations regarding the evaluation criteria, the K-means framing provides a reasonable benchmark, and the experimental results demonstrate a benefit for the proposed algorithm. 

**Clarity**

The initial presentation is clear, with a well-written introduction and summary of related work. However, relegating the algorithm’s derivation to the Supplementary Material is a significant drawback. An outline in the main paper would greatly improve understanding. The experiments section is clear, though some plots suffer from limited readability in black and white.

I struggled to follow the algorithm’s detailed description and verify the derivation’s correctness. The mathematical notation is the weakest aspect of the presentation. The expressions in equations 9a, 9b, 9d, and 9e could be simplified by factoring out common terms (e.g., $1 / m \tau_w$). Combining terms (e.g., in 9b and 9e) would also enhance readability.

The abundance of hats, tildes, subscripts, and superscripts makes the notation unnecessarily cumbersome. For instance, the use of $\tauw$ without a corresponding $\tau$ is confusing. Simplifying notation would be beneficial. Consider dropping the hats in Section 4, stating explicitly “We’ll be dropping the hats here for brevity.” The ‘t’ superscripts in the messages appear superfluous; replacing "=" with "\gets" could allow their removal. I suspect the authors did not originally derive the algorithm using such complex notation, and revisiting initial formulations for simplicity is recommended.

Exposition could be improved by explaining relationships between equations (e.g., equation 5 as the negative logarithm of equation 2, equation 8 as equation 2 raised to the power of $\beta$). Algorithm 2 exhibits row-column symmetry, which should be noted and exploited to reduce its length. Explaining the meaning of functions in equation 10 near their definition, instead of just providing the equation, would aid understanding. Clarify statements such as “G\beta(b, \Lambda; p) is a scaled covariance matrix of q\beta(u; b, \Lambda, p)” by mentioning this relationship at the definition of G.

The missing $\beta$ subscripts in the messages, despite their dependence on functions (f and G) with $\beta$ subscripts, is also confusing. Consider eliminating the subscripts entirely, treating $\beta$ as a global variable. Before equations 15 and 16, state, “in the limit $\beta \to \infty$, f and G take the following form:”.

On a deeper level, questions remain regarding the algorithm's design. What is the significance of the $m$ factor in the additive noise variance? Does it have broader implications for matrix factorization applications? Does it influence the approximations used in the algorithm’s derivation? Is this factor the primary reason for the algorithm’s lack of row-column symmetry? The authors should address these questions.

Minor points: Is a tilde the standard notation for column vectors? Why are *N* capitalized while *m* is lowercase? Section 5 requires a citation after “VBMF-MA” to identify the reference guiding its implementation. 

**Supplementary Material**

In the Supplementary Material, equation 5 would be clearer if written as a product of exponentials. The step from equation 14 to 15 in Section 2 is unclear regarding the big-O notation of *m* terms. The novelty of the approximation described after “A technique used in…” requires clarification, both in the Supplementary Material and, ideally, the main paper.

The English is generally good, but occasional awkward phrasing distracts from the overall clarity. (e.g., use of articles).

**Overall**

The mathematics is interesting, and publishing this first effort to apply belief propagation to matrix factorization has value. However, the paper requires significant revisions to address the clarity and scholarship concerns. The current version places an unacceptable burden on readers and would reflect poorly on the conference if accepted in its present state. I recommend rejection with encouragement to resubmit after substantial revisions.
