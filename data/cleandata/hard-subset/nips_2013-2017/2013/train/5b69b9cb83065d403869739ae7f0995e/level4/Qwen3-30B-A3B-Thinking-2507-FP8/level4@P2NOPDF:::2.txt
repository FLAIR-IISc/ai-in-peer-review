

Review of "Low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering"  

This paper introduces a Belief Propagation (BP)-based algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction, framed within a Bayesian model. The approach, detailed in the supplementary material, employs two asymptotic approximations (large row/column counts) and evaluates the method by reformulating clustering as a matrix reconstruction task, comparing it against Lloyd’s K-means and Variational Bayes Matrix Factorization (VBMF).  

**Strengths**  
The algorithm is novel in applying BP to matrix reconstruction, and the clustering-as-reconstruction perspective offers a fresh evaluation lens. The mathematics is generally followable, and the experiments demonstrate clear advantages for the proposed method in the K-means context. The introduction and prior work summary are well-structured, and the supplementary diagram (Fig. 1) is particularly helpful.  

**Critical Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of standard evaluation**: The paper evaluates on clustering (a non-standard matrix reconstruction problem), making it difficult to gauge performance on conventional tasks. Crucially, it omits the Netflix dataset used in prior VBMF work (Lin & Teh, 2007; Raiko et al., 2007), creating uncertainty about competitiveness. The authors must justify this omission or adopt a standard benchmark.  
2. **Unvalidated approximations**: The key asymptotic approximations ($N, m \to \infty$) are only described in the supplement, lack empirical validation, and are insufficiently explained. This undermines confidence in the algorithm’s theoretical foundation.  
3. **Misrepresentation of novelty**: The abstract and Section 2.2 imply the clustering-matrix factorization connection is original, yet it is well-established in literature. The paper must clarify this upfront or cite relevant prior work (e.g., [cite key papers]).  
4. **Insufficient citation context**: The relationship to Expectation Propagation (EP) is unaddressed, despite BP being a specific EP instance for continuous distributions. The algorithm’s BP basis should be stated in the abstract, not deferred to Section 4.1. VBMF-MA implementation requires a primary reference in Section 5.  

**Clarity and Presentation Issues**  
- **Overly complex notation**: Excessive hats ($\hat{p}$), tildes, subscripts ($\tau_w$), and redundant superscripts ($t$) hinder readability. For example:  
  - Replace $\tau_w$ with $\tau$; eliminate $t$-superscripts by using $\gets$ instead of $t+1$.  
  - Simplify Section 4 by stating "We drop hats for brevity" and removing $\beta$ subscripts from messages (only $f_\beta$ and $G_\beta$ depend on $\beta$).  
- **Unclear mathematical exposition**:  
  - Factors like $1/(m\tau_w)$ in Eq. 9a-e should be factored out.  
  - Equations 5, 8, and 10 lack intuitive explanations (e.g., "Eq. 5 is the negative log of Eq. 2").  
  - The significance of $m$ in noise variance is unexplained, yet it breaks row-column symmetry.  
- **Language and minor errors**:  
  - "Since properties" → "Since the properties"; "a few number" → "a few"; "stuck to bad local minima" → "stuck in bad local minima".  
  - The title should specify "using approximate message passing" for precision.  
  - Supplementary Material has grammatical issues (e.g., "of these message" → "messages").  

**Recommendation**  
While the algorithm is a technically sound first application of BP to matrix factorization and holds potential, the paper’s current state is unacceptable for publication. The lack of standard benchmarking, unvalidated approximations, misrepresentation of novelty, and severe clarity issues (notational, explanatory, and scholarly) impose an undue burden on readers. These deficiencies prevent fair evaluation of the method’s merits. The paper requires substantial revision:  
- Adopt standard datasets (e.g., Netflix) for objective comparison.  
- Validate approximations and clarify their limits.  
- Correct all citation gaps and explicitly acknowledge established clustering-reconstruction links.  
- Simplify notation and provide intuitive explanations for key equations.  

The core idea is promising, but the present manuscript fails to communicate it rigorously or fairly. **Reject**.
