Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction under general priors on the factors U and V. A temperature parameter β is introduced to unify posterior mean/marginal MAP estimation (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞). The algorithm reduces to a Bethe-approximate inference procedure that preserves some dependencies between U and V, unlike mean-field VB factorizations. The authors also specialize the method to clustering by casting K-means as a rank-r factorization with one-hot constraints on V, yielding an AMP-based K-means variant with an Onsager-like correction to the assignment step. A proposition shows that, at fixed points with positive semidefinite aggregate covariances, each block is a global minimizer of the objective holding the other fixed, relating AMP fixed points to ICM/K-means fixed points. Experiments on synthetic data and the ORL faces dataset indicate improved K-means loss and accuracy compared to Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ and improved accuracy and speed compared to a VB baseline for maximum-accuracy clustering.

The work builds on AMP/GAMP literature [Donoho et al. 2009; Rangan 2011; Bayati & Montanari 2011], the rank-one bilinear AMP (IterFac) [Rangan & Fletcher 2012], and an earlier AMP for general-rank matrix reconstruction [Matsushita & Tanaka 2012], which handled posterior means. The clustering connection relates to established views of K-means as low-rank matrix factorization with indicator matrices and to NMF-based clustering (e.g., Ding et al., Xu et al.). The paper also cites contemporaneous bilinear GAMP work [Parker et al.] and AMP for dictionary learning [Krzakala et al.], and clarifies differences (fixed r vs. r/m limit, non-factorized priors within a row).

Strengths:
- A clean, general AMP formulation that accommodates non-factorized (within-row) priors on U and V and unifies β=1 and β→∞ cases. The β→∞ specialization and the G∞ identity are practically useful for MAP.
- Clear derivation of an AMP-based K-means algorithm with an intuitive leave-one-out/cluster-size correction in the assignment step. The connection to VB/ICM via removal of Onsager terms is insightful.
- Proposition relating AMP fixed points to coordinate-wise global minima is a nice theoretical property and strengthens the optimization interpretation.
- Empirical results show consistent improvements over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ on synthetic data (in K-means loss) and ORL (in both loss and accuracy), and AMP-MA outperforms VBMF in accuracy and speed on the synthetic setup.
- Computational complexity is linear in mN per iteration; per-iteration overhead of Onsager terms is negligible.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Convergence is assumed; practical stability issues are acknowledged (oscillating assignments), and a heuristic stopping rule is used. Damping/stepsize control are not discussed, and no systematic study of convergence robustness is provided.
- State evolution—one of AMP’s main advantages—is not presented, even empirically. No comparison of SE predictions vs. measurements or phase-transition analyses are shown.
- The experimental scope is narrow: one synthetic regime (m=800, N=1600, τ=0.1) with r̂=r and true priors for AMP-MA; no sensitivity to rank mis-specification, noise mismatch, or prior mismatch is examined. Real-data experiments compare only to K-means++ and report occasional catastrophic collapse (single cluster).
- Fairness/details of baselines: on synthetic data, Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ are strong baselines for K-means loss, but for maximum-accuracy clustering, EM for Gaussian mixtures (with spherical/diagonal covariances) and spectral clustering would be more informative. On ORL, AMP-KM uses K-means++ seeding (good), but results with other common baselines (spectral clustering, NMF variants) are missing.
- Practicality of the general β=1 case: f and G require r̂-dimensional integrals unless priors are conjugate or discrete. The paper’s experiments use distributions that admit simple forms, but guidance for general priors is limited.
- The novelty claim for “first to directly enforce one-hot assignment in a low-rank reconstruction framework” is slightly overstated; the K-means formulation with indicator matrices is classical, and its matrix-factorization view is well known. The novelty is the AMP-based solver and its correction terms.

Quality:
- The technical development is sound at a high level, consistent with prior AMP derivations. The optimization interpretation (Proposition 1) is valuable. However, the lack of SE analysis/evidence and limited convergence discussion reduce confidence in reliability across regimes. Experimental methodology is mostly clear, but additional baselines and ablations would strengthen claims.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well organized, with clear notation and a compact algorithmic presentation. The connection to VB/ICM is explained well. Some derivation details are deferred to the supplement; brief intuition for the Onsager corrections in the clustering specialization is appreciated. Minor clarity gaps: practical computation of f/G for general priors, and conditions under which G∞=(Λ−H)^{-1} holds.

Originality:
- Extending general-rank bilinear AMP to handle β→∞ MAP estimation and non-factorized within-row priors is a meaningful contribution. The K-means specialization with an explicit correction is novel to my knowledge within the AMP literature.

Significance:
- If robust, the AMP-KM variant is a practically relevant drop-in replacement for Lloyd’s K-means with better minima at similar cost. The general AMP framework for low-rank reconstruction under flexible priors could be impactful, especially if accompanied by SE and broader experiments. Current empirical scope limits the demonstrated significance.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide a state evolution for at least simple priors (Gaussian-Gaussian, discrete-Gaussian) and compare to experiments. Even partial/semi-empirical SE would elevate the paper.
- Add damping/adaptive stepsize and convergence diagnostics; report sensitivity to damping and initialization; quantify the frequency of oscillations/collapses and remedies.
- Expand experiments: vary (m,N,τ), test mis-specified r̂, mis-specified priors, and robustness to heavy-tailed noise. Include runtime comparisons and ablations showing the gain from Onsager terms vs. VB/ICM.
- For clustering, compare to EM for GMMs (spherical/diagonal), spectral clustering, and NMF-based clustering; include more datasets. Report how often AMP collapses to one cluster and strategies to prevent it (e.g., balanced priors on V, temperature annealing in β).
- Clarify computational strategies for f/G with generic priors; list priors with closed forms and discuss numerical quadrature or proximal approximations. Provide code/pseudocode in the supplement.
- Calibrate and justify the τ estimator and discuss its effect; consider an EM-style update for τ.
- Cite classical equivalences between K-means and matrix factorization/indicator matrices (e.g., Ding & He, “K-means clustering via matrix factorization”), and discuss relation to SDP relaxations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid AMP contribution with a clean unifying formulation (β), non-factorized priors, and useful optimization interpretation.
- Practical and seemingly effective AMP-based K-means with consistent improvements on reported tasks.
- Clear connection to and improvement over VB/ICM approximations.

Cons for acceptance:
- Missing SE analysis and limited convergence/stability study.
- Experimental scope limited; important baselines and robustness tests absent.
- Occasional pathological behavior (single-cluster collapse) not fully addressed.

Overall, this is a promising submission with novel components and encouraging results, but it would benefit from stronger theoretical characterization (SE/convergence) and broader, more rigorous empirical validation.
