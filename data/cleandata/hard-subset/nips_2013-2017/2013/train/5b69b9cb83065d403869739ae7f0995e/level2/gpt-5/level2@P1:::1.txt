Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper addresses Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with structured factors via an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm derived from belief propagation, and shows how a special case yields an efficient clustering method that directly enforces one-of-K assignments. The authors introduce a one-parameter family of posteriors p̂(U,V|A; β) that unifies marginal inference (β=1) and MAP estimation (β→∞), derive an AMP algorithm that outputs approximate marginals, posterior means, or MAP solutions depending on β, and give a specialized AMP procedure for K-means-type clustering with a debiasing/cluster-size correction in the assignment step. They compare to variational Bayes matrix factorization (VBMF), Lloyd’s K-means, and K-means++ on synthetic data and the ORL faces dataset.

The work builds on AMP for linear models (Donoho et al. 2009; Rangan 2011) and bilinear/low-rank settings (IterFac, Rangan & Fletcher 2012; Matsushita & Tanaka 2012). It is contemporaneous with other bilinear AMP formulations (e.g., Krzakala et al. 2013; Parker et al. 2013). The key new aspects are: (i) extending the general-rank low-rank AMP beyond posterior means to include MAP via β→∞, (ii) an explicit formulation that allows non-independent priors across components within a row, and (iii) a derivation and practical algorithm for hard-assignment clustering within the low-rank AMP framework, including a principled leave-one-out-like correction in the assignment update.

Strengths:
- Technical contribution: A clear and useful extension of low-rank AMP to handle MAP estimation and general priors via the β parameter, with interpretable update equations and connections to Bethe free energy approximations. The link to ICM/VBMF emerges naturally by removing Onsager terms.
- Practical algorithm for clustering: The AMP-KM algorithm performs the same centroid update as Lloyd’s but modifies assignments with bias and cluster-size corrections that are well-motivated. This is a simple drop-in alternative.
- Empirical results: On synthetic data, AMP-KM consistently achieves lower K-means objective than Lloyd’s/K-means++, and the β=1 version (AMP-MA) achieves higher clustering accuracy than VBMF-MA. On ORL, AMP-KM outperforms K-means++ in most trials.
- Complexity: Per-iteration cost O(mN) with small r̂-dependent constants; inversion is only r̂×r̂.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited empirical scope for the general low-rank reconstruction problem: Although the paper is positioned as a general method for low-rank Bayesian inference with structured priors (e.g., sparsity, nonnegativity), experiments focus almost exclusively on clustering. No reconstruction benchmarks (e.g., NMSE vs SNR, robustness to prior mismatch) or comparisons to MCMC/VB on standard matrix factorization datasets are shown.
- Comparison set for clustering is narrow: The baselines are Lloyd’s, K-means++, and VBMF for accuracy. Missing comparisons: EM for Gaussian mixtures (with tied spherical covariance, which matches the generative model), spectral clustering (Ng, Jordan, Weiss 2002) for high-dimensional data, and modern K-means variants with many restarts or mini-batch variants. For ORL, methods often used include PCA+k-means or GMM; those would strengthen claims.
- Convergence and stability: AMP often requires damping/tuning in bilinear problems. The paper assumes convergence and observes oscillations (handled by an ad-hoc stopping rule for AMP-KM) but does not discuss damping or convergence diagnostics. Theoretical guarantees are deferred; the state evolution is mentioned but not provided (even in supplement per main text), and the conditions under which Proposition 1 applies (positive semidefiniteness of sums of S/T) are not fully characterized.
- Sensitivity to τ, rank, and initialization: τ is estimated online; sensitivity analyses are not reported. Synthetic experiments set r̂=r; behavior under model-order mismatch is not explored (critical for clustering). For ORL, AMP-KM needed K-means++ seeding to avoid collapse, suggesting sensitivity to initialization.
- Novelty vs. contemporaneous AMP work: The paper cites two concurrent preprints on bilinear AMP (Krzakala et al. 2013; Parker et al. 2013) but the differences (fixed rank vs. rank ratios, intra-row dependence) and implications are only briefly discussed. A more careful empirical/theoretical comparison would clarify novelty.

Quality:
- The derivation appears consistent with AMP principles, with clear notation for f/G and their β→∞ limits, and a useful interpretation of Onsager terms. Proposition 1 gives a reassuring block-wise optimality at fixed points. However, the lack of state evolution derivations, convergence discussion (e.g., necessity of damping, step-size), and ablation of Onsager terms leave open questions about robustness beyond the presented datasets.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized. The mapping from the general AMP algorithm to the clustering specialization is clear, and the assignment correction is nicely interpreted. Suggestions:
  - Move the key parts of the derivation and state evolution to the supplement (currently stated as omitted due to space), including precise assumptions (scaling, randomness of A/U/V) and proofs for Proposition 1.
  - Clarify the computational cost of evaluating f and G for non-discrete priors and discuss practical approximations (e.g., separable priors, proximal steps for f∞).
  - Explicitly present the damping strategy, if used, and recommended hyperparameters for stability.

Originality:
- Moderate. Extending low-rank AMP to MAP via β and providing a clustering specialization with hard assignments seems new and useful. The broader low-rank AMP landscape is active in 2012–2013; a tighter contrast to BiG-AMP and dictionary-learning AMP would help.

Significance:
- The clustering variant is appealing in practice and appears to improve over widely-used baselines. The general AMP framework could be impactful if validated on a broader set of low-rank tasks and priors. As is, the significance is more in the clustering contribution than in general low-rank reconstruction.

Questions for the authors:
1. Can you provide state evolution (in supplement) and empirical validation (e.g., MSE vs. SE predictions) for both β=1 and β→∞?
2. Do you use damping or step-size control? If so, specify; if not, how often do oscillations occur and how sensitive is performance to the stopping rule?
3. How sensitive are AMP-KM and AMP-MA to τ estimation? Would using a robust residual estimate or EM-like variance update improve stability?
4. How do the methods perform under r̂≠r (over/under-specified K)? Please include synthetic results.
5. Can you compare to GMM-EM (spherical) and spectral clustering on your synthetic and ORL experiments?
6. For the general framework, please include at least one experiment with structured priors (e.g., sparsity or nonnegativity) to demonstrate flexibility.

Minor comments:
- In (17a), Λtu appears as mτ(Vt)⊤Vt; confirm scaling consistency with earlier equations.
- Please report wall-clock time, not only iteration counts, since AMP per iteration is heavier than Lloyd’s.
- Consider relating the 2m/ntl term in (18b) to leave-one-out corrections known in cross-validation or bias-corrected k-means.

Overall assessment:
A solid and thoughtfully presented AMP formulation with a practically useful clustering specialization and promising empirical results. The paper would be strengthened by (i) including state evolution and convergence details, (ii) broadening empirical validation beyond clustering and adding stronger baselines, and (iii) clarifying novelty against contemporaneous bilinear AMP works. I lean toward acceptance if these issues can be addressed in the final version.
