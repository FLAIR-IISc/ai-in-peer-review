Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction with structured priors on factors U and V, and derives an efficient approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm that unifies posterior marginalization and MAP estimation via an inverse-temperature parameter β. For β=1, the scheme approximates marginal posteriors and posterior means; letting β→∞ yields a MAP algorithm with tractable factor-wise updates. A fixed-point optimality proposition ensures that, at convergence, each block update reaches a global minimum with the other block fixed. The paper further reformulates K-means clustering as a special case of low-rank matrix reconstruction with a discrete one-hot prior on V and derives an AMP-based K-means algorithm (AMP-KM) with Onsager-like corrections to the assignment step. Experiments on synthetic data and the ORL faces dataset show improvements over Lloyd’s K-means and K-means++ in K-means loss and accuracy, and over a variational Bayes matrix factorization baseline in accuracy for maximum-accuracy clustering.

The work is well-situated within AMP for compressed sensing [6], GAMP [7], rank-one matrix factorization (IterFac) [8], and an earlier AMP for general-rank matrix factorization limited to posterior means [9]. It is contemporaneous with AMP for bilinear models/dictionary learning [13,14]. Compared to variational Bayes matrix factorization [4], the approach captures dependencies between U and V via the Bethe approximation and introduces Onsager corrections with negligible extra cost.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The algorithm is derived from belief propagation with explicit updates and uses a β-parameterized posterior to interpolate between marginalization and MAP. Proposition 1 provides a meaningful fixed-point optimality guarantee. The state evolution analysis is deferred; convergence is not guaranteed and oscillations are reported for the clustering variant, but the derivation and connections to known AMP results are coherent.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments (m=800, N=1600) compare fairly across multiple baselines with clear metrics. On ORL, AMP-KM shows consistent improvement over K-means++, though two runs collapsed to a single cluster, indicating sensitivity. Using true priors in maximum-accuracy experiments favors Bayesian methods; robustness to prior mismatch is not explored.
- Reproducibility: Key update equations and stopping criteria are provided, but proofs and state evolution details are in the supplement. Practical guidance on damping, initialization, and τ-estimation is brief.

Clarity
The presentation is generally clear with consistent notation and a helpful derivation of special-case algorithms (Algorithm 3). The K-means interpretation and the role of correction terms (cluster-size bias and leave-one-out effect) are well explained. Some implementation details (damping, β-scheduling, computational cost of f/G for general priors) could be expanded.

Originality
Two contributions appear novel and well-motivated:
- Extending AMP for general-rank matrix factorization to handle MAP estimation (β→∞) and marginalization within one algorithmic template, beyond [9] which focused on posterior means.
- An AMP-based K-means algorithm that enforces exact one-hot assignments and introduces principled Onsager corrections to the assignment step. Prior clustering via matrix factorization (e.g., NMF-based) did not directly impose the one-hot constraint within an AMP framework.

Significance
If robust, the approach could influence scalable Bayesian matrix factorization by offering a unified, efficient algorithm for posterior quantities and MAP, and could provide a practical drop-in improvement over Lloyd’s K-means in high dimensions. The empirical scope is limited (one real dataset, fixed-rank setting, true priors in synthetic experiments). A stronger comparison to contemporaneous bilinear AMP (BiG-AMP) [14] would sharpen the contribution and its applicability across observation models and priors.

Strengths
- Clear derivation of a general AMP scheme with a β “temperature” unifying marginalization and MAP.
- Principled correction terms that connect to Bethe free energy/Onsager intuition and demonstrably improve K-means optimization.
- Fixed-point optimality proposition that clarifies the relation to ICM and the potential for avoiding poor local minima.
- Competitive to superior empirical performance with low per-iteration cost.

Weaknesses
- Convergence behavior is not characterized; oscillations in AMP-KM are handled by heuristic termination. No damping/stepsize guidance or state evolution summary is provided.
- Empirical evaluation is narrow: one synthetic setting (with true priors) and one real dataset; no tests under rank mismatch, prior mismatch, missing data, or non-Gaussian noise.
- Baselines omit strong probabilistic clustering methods (e.g., GMM-EM) and contemporaneous bilinear AMP (BiG-AMP) [14], which targets related models.
- τ estimation for clustering is heuristic; sensitivity and stability are not analyzed.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Add comparisons to BiG-AMP [14] on the reconstruction task (posterior means and, if feasible, MAP via β or proximal variants), and to GMM-EM for clustering.
- Provide damping/stepsize and initialization guidelines; report convergence behavior (oscillation rate, effect of damping) and ablations showing the impact of the Onsager terms versus ICM.
- Include at least one robustness study: prior mismatch, rank misspecification (r̂ ≠ r), and sensitivity to τ estimation for AMP-KM.

Nice-to-have:
- Summarize the state evolution equations and their empirical fidelity on synthetic data.
- Explore additional datasets (e.g., other image/ text clustering benchmarks) and observation models (e.g., missing entries).
- Clarify computational cost for general priors (closed-form vs. numerical integration/optimization) and provide implementation tips or code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean extension of AMP to unify marginalization and MAP in bilinear matrix factorization.
- Novel, practically useful AMP-K-means variant with consistent empirical gains and sound intuition.
- Clear relation to variational Bayes and ICM, with a fixed-point optimality result.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited empirical breadth and missing key baselines (BiG-AMP, GMM-EM); robustness not established.
- Convergence/oscillation issues not systematically addressed; lack of state evolution overview.
- Reliance on true priors in synthetic experiments may overstate practical performance.
