

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction that handles structural properties (sparsity, non-negativity, etc.) of factor matrices. The authors extend previous AMP work to handle both marginalization and MAP problems in a unified framework through a parameter β, and apply this to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with structural constraints. The key claims are: (1) the proposed AMP algorithm provides a flexible and efficient approach for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction; (2) the AMP-based clustering algorithm outperforms existing methods in both artificial and real-world data.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with thorough theoretical analysis, including a proposition proving the optimality of the AMP algorithm's fixed points. The numerical experiments are well-designed, comparing the proposed method against multiple baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, variational Bayes matrix factorization) across artificial and real datasets (ORL Face Database). Results consistently show the AMP-based clustering achieves lower K-means loss and higher accuracy than competitors, with faster convergence than variational Bayes methods.

The paper excels in originality by extending AMP to matrix reconstruction with structural constraints and directly addressing the one-cluster-per-data-point constraint in clustering—something previous low-rank approaches didn't handle. The authors demonstrate strong field knowledge through comprehensive literature review and appropriate comparisons to related work.

Strengths: The unified treatment of marginalization and MAP problems via parameter β is elegant; the algorithm's low computational cost (O(mN) per iteration) and theoretical guarantees are significant contributions. The practical demonstration on real-world clustering tasks is convincing.

Weaknesses: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss limitations in terms of scalability to extremely large matrices (e.g., m, N > 10^6), which is increasingly important in modern applications. The parameter τ for the clustering algorithm is estimated heuristically, and the paper doesn't explore sensitivity to this choice. While the theoretical analysis is strong, the paper could better explain why the AMP approach outperforms variational Bayes in practice.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The significance is high as the method offers a theoretically grounded, efficient alternative to existing clustering approaches that consistently outperforms benchmarks. The work makes a meaningful contribution to both matrix reconstruction and clustering literature.

Overall, this is a strong paper that advances the state of the art with a novel, well-supported algorithm. The authors should address scalability limitations in future work, but the current contribution is significant and well-executed. I recommend acceptance.
