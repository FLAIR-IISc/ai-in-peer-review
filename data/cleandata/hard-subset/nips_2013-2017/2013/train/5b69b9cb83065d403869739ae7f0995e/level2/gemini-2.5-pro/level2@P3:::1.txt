This paper presents an algorithm for Bayesian low-rank matrix reconstruction based on the approximate message passing (AMP) framework. The work extends previous AMP approaches for this problem by introducing a unified formulation, controlled by an inverse temperature parameter β, that can handle both posterior marginalization (for β=1) and maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation (in the limit β→∞). The primary contribution is the application of this framework to K-means type clustering. By reformulating clustering as a low-rank matrix reconstruction problem with a specific discrete prior on one of the factor matrices, the paper derives a novel and efficient clustering algorithm. This approach is positioned as an alternative to variational Bayes methods and standard iterative algorithms like Lloyd’s K-means, building upon the theoretical foundations of AMP developed in compressed sensing and related fields.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The application of the AMP framework to derive a new K-means clustering algorithm is a significant and novel contribution. The resulting algorithm (Algorithm 3, Eq. 18b) is distinct from the classic Lloyd's algorithm. The additional terms in the assignment step have an intuitive interpretation as a form of bias correction that accounts for a data point's influence on its current cluster center, as well as the relative size of the cluster. This is a compelling idea that demonstrably leads to improved performance.

2.  **Quality:** The technical development appears sound. Proposition 1, which relates the fixed points of the proposed AMP algorithm for the MAP problem to those of the standard Iterated Conditional Modes (ICM) algorithm, provides valuable theoretical justification. It suggests that the AMP algorithm may be less susceptible to poor local minima, a claim that is well-supported by the experimental results. The experiments themselves are well-designed, comparing the proposed methods against appropriate and strong baselines (Lloyd's K-means, K-means++, Variational Bayes) on both synthetic and real-world data.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. The problem setting is established precisely in Section 2, and the connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is explained well. The proposed algorithm is presented clearly, and the comparison to Lloyd's algorithm in Section 4.3 effectively highlights the key differences.

4.  **Empirical Results:** The experimental validation is a strong point of the submission. On synthetic data, the proposed AMP-KM and AMP-MA algorithms consistently outperform their respective baselines (K-means++/Lloyd's and VBMF-MA) in terms of K-means loss and clustering accuracy, especially as the number of clusters increases. The results on the ORL face database further demonstrate the practical utility and superior performance of the AMP-based approach over the widely used K-means++ algorithm.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Derivation and Theoretical Analysis:** The full derivation of the AMP algorithm is relegated to the supplementary material. While this is understandable due to space constraints, the paper would benefit from a more intuitive, high-level sketch of the derivation in the main text. Furthermore, the paper mentions that the performance of the algorithm can be predicted using state evolution, a powerful analytical tool for AMP. The omission of this analysis is a missed opportunity. Including a brief discussion of the state evolution predictions and showing their correspondence with the empirical results would significantly strengthen the theoretical grounding of the work.

2.  **Contextualization with Related Work:** The paper briefly mentions concurrent work on AMP for dictionary learning and bilinear problems [13, 14] but states that a detailed comparison remains to be made. A more detailed discussion of the technical differences—for instance, the implications of fixing the rank `r` versus fixing the ratio `r/m` in the large system limit—would help to better position this contribution within the rapidly evolving literature on message-passing algorithms.

3.  **Algorithmic Details:** The proposed method for estimating the noise parameter τ in the K-means application (Section 4.3) is an important practical detail. This adaptive scheme appears reasonable, but a brief discussion of its justification, its impact on convergence, and its sensitivity would be valuable. It is not immediately clear if this ad-hoc update fits within the theoretical assumptions of the AMP derivation.

4.  **Evaluation of Failure Cases:** In the real-data experiment, the AMP algorithm failed to produce a meaningful clustering in 2 out of 50 trials. While the success rate is high, the evaluation would be more complete with a brief discussion of potential failure modes. Does the algorithm diverge, or converge to a trivial solution under certain initializations? Providing insight into this would be helpful for practitioners.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **Pro-acceptance:** The paper introduces a novel and highly effective clustering algorithm derived from the principled AMP framework. The connection between low-rank matrix reconstruction and clustering is exploited in a new way, leading to an algorithm that empirically outperforms strong and widely used baselines. The work is technically solid, clearly presented, and the results are significant for the clustering community.
*   **Con-acceptance:** The theoretical analysis could be deepened by including a discussion of the state evolution analysis, which is a hallmark of AMP-related papers. The derivation is not self-contained in the main text, and some practical details of the algorithm could be better justified.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly the novelty of the proposed clustering algorithm and its strong empirical performance, substantially outweigh the weaknesses. The suggestions for improvement would enhance the paper but are not essential for its acceptance. This work represents a solid contribution to the field.
