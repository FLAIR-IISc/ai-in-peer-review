

The paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction and applies it to clustering problems. The work builds upon existing AMP techniques for compressed sensing and extends them to the matrix factorization setting. The authors formulate the low-rank matrix reconstruction problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing for flexible incorporation of structural properties like sparsity and non-negativity. They derive an AMP algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation problems, which they then apply to clustering by reformulating it as a constrained low-rank matrix reconstruction problem.

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their AMP algorithm, including a proof of Proposition 1 which establishes the algorithm's convergence properties. The derivation of the AMP algorithm for the general-rank case extends previous work (such as [8, 9]) by handling both marginalization and MAP problems in a unified framework. The numerical experiments on both synthetic and real data demonstrate consistent performance improvements over baselines, particularly for high-dimensional data. The comparison with Lloyd's K-means algorithm and variational Bayes matrix factorization provides a reasonable benchmark for the proposed method.

The clarity of the paper is generally good. The problem formulation is well-motivated and the connections between matrix reconstruction and clustering are clearly explained. The algorithm descriptions are presented with sufficient mathematical detail, though the supplementary material references might make the paper harder to follow for readers without access to those materials. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implementation considerations, particularly regarding the selection of the parameter τ in the clustering algorithm.

The originality of the work is moderate. While the AMP approach to matrix reconstruction is novel in the specific context of handling both marginalization and MAP problems together, the connection between clustering and low-rank matrix reconstruction has been explored previously (e.g., [10, 11]). The key novelty lies in the AMP-based implementation that directly handles the cluster assignment constraint. However, the paper could better clarify the distinction between this work and the related approaches in [13, 14] by providing a more detailed comparison of their algorithmic structures and theoretical guarantees.

The significance of the work is demonstrated through the empirical results. The proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm achieves better clustering accuracy and faster convergence than the standard Lloyd's K-means algorithm on both synthetic and real datasets, particularly for high-dimensional data. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the practical implementation of clustering algorithms, as the AMP approach appears to be more robust to initialization than traditional methods.

The paper could be strengthened in several ways. First, the authors should provide a more thorough comparison with related work on clustering via matrix factorization, particularly regarding how their approach handles the cluster assignment constraint differently from prior methods. Second, while the numerical results are promising, a statistical significance test for the performance differences would strengthen the claims. Third, the paper should discuss the limitations of the approach, such as when the low-rank assumption might not hold or when the data does not exhibit the assumed structural properties. Finally, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity in terms of big-O notation would help readers understand the scalability of the proposed method.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of matrix factorization and clustering. The AMP-based approach provides a theoretically grounded method for low-rank matrix reconstruction with practical benefits for clustering applications. While the novelty is somewhat incremental, the empirical results and theoretical justification provide a solid foundation for the proposed method. The work would be improved with a more detailed comparison to related approaches and a clearer discussion of limitations. The paper meets the quality standards expected at NIPS, with strong theoretical motivation, clear presentation of the algorithm, and compelling experimental results that demonstrate practical advantages over existing methods.
