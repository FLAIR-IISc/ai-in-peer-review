

This paper proposes an approximate message passing (AMP) algorithm for low-rank matrix reconstruction with applications to clustering. The authors formulate the problem in a Bayesian framework, allowing them to incorporate structural properties of the factor matrices (e.g., sparsity) beyond low-rankness. The key contribution is an AMP-based algorithm that handles both marginalization and MAP estimation problems for low-rank matrix reconstruction, which they then apply to K-means clustering by reformulating it as a constrained low-rank matrix reconstruction problem.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, it extends previous AMP algorithms (e.g., [9]) to handle general-rank matrix reconstruction with a unified framework that accommodates both marginalization and MAP estimation. The authors provide a theoretical justification for their approach, including Proposition 1 which establishes a connection between the AMP fixed points and the ICM algorithm. Second, they successfully apply the algorithm to clustering, demonstrating that their AMP-based clustering method (Algorithm 3) outperforms Lloyd's K-means algorithm in numerical experiments. The experimental evaluation is thorough, including both artificial data with varying cluster numbers and the real-world ORL Face Database, with multiple performance metrics (normalized K-means loss, accuracy, convergence speed).

The paper is well-written, with clear mathematical formulations and precise algorithm descriptions. The authors effectively contrast their approach with related work, particularly noting how their method differs from previous AMP-based matrix reconstruction algorithms by fixing the rank r rather than the ratio r/m in the asymptotic limit. The computational complexity analysis (O(mN) per iteration) is appropriate, and the discussion of the relationship between AMP and variational Bayes matrix factorization is insightful.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the connection to prior clustering work is insufficiently developed. While the authors cite [10, 11] as having applied low-rank matrix reconstruction to clustering, they don't adequately explain how their specific approach to handling the constraint that each datum should be assigned to exactly one cluster differs from these prior works. This weakens the claim of novelty regarding the clustering application.

Second, the theoretical justification for the AMP algorithm is limited. While Proposition 1 provides a useful optimality guarantee for the MAP problem, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the convergence properties of the proposed algorithm. The authors note that convergence is not guaranteed in general, but don't provide sufficient evidence to convince readers that the algorithm converges well in practice, especially for the clustering application.

Third, the experimental results lack statistical significance testing. The paper reports averages and standard deviations over 500 instances, but doesn't provide statistical tests to confirm that the observed performance differences (e.g., AMP-KM outperforming K-means++) are statistically significant.

The significance of the work is somewhat limited by the narrow scope of the experimental validation. While the ORL Face Database results are promising, the paper doesn't discuss potential limitations of the approach in more challenging real-world scenarios or provide guidance on when the AMP-based method would be preferable to existing clustering algorithms.

The paper is well-positioned in the context of recent NIPS work on AMP algorithms (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 13, 14]), but could better position itself against other matrix factorization and clustering approaches that have appeared at NIPS. The authors cite relevant prior work, but could more explicitly contrast their method with other Bayesian approaches to matrix factorization and clustering.

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with a well-motivated algorithmic contribution that advances the state of the art in low-rank matrix reconstruction and its application to clustering. The proposed AMP-based clustering algorithm demonstrates promising empirical performance. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with related clustering work, stronger theoretical justification for the algorithm's convergence, and statistical validation of the experimental results.

Strengths: Novel AMP extension for low-rank matrix reconstruction, thorough experimental validation, clear theoretical connection to existing AMP work, well-structured presentation.

Weaknesses: Insufficient contrast with prior clustering work, limited theoretical convergence analysis, no statistical significance testing for results, limited discussion of practical limitations.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field, and with the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for NIPS.
