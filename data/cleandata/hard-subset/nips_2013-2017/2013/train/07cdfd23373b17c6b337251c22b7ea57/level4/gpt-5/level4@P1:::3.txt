Summary
This paper presents a scalable approach to latent space inference for large networks by representing graphs as bags of triangular motifs and introducing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) with O(K) triangle-generating parameters. The authors develop a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm that uses mini-batches of triangles and natural gradients to estimate mixed-membership role vectors and triangle parameters. Compared to edge-based latent variable models such as MMSB [Airoldi et al., 2008] and its scalable variant a-MMSB [Gopalan et al., 2013], PTM avoids O(N2) edge processing and O(K2) or O(K3) parameterizations by exploiting higher-order (triadic) structure and parameter sharing. Compared to the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) [Ho et al., 2011], it replaces the O(K3) triangle tensor with a grouped parameterization B0, Bxx, Bxxx that scales linearly in K. Experiments on synthetic and real networks (up to >1M nodes and ~100 roles) show substantial speedups (orders of magnitude) with competitive or improved performance on latent role recovery and link prediction. The algorithm converges in a few passes and runs efficiently on a single multi-core machine.

Strengths
- Methodological contribution: A clear and effective O(K) parameter sharing scheme for triangle-generating distributions that maintains modeling expressiveness while enabling O(NK) global updates.
- Scalable inference: A well-implemented structured SVI procedure with practical engineering choices (mini-batches of triangles, mixture-of-deltas local approximation) that reduces local updates from O(K3) to O(K).
- Empirical validation: Strong runtime and convergence results; competitive or better accuracy than MMSB and MMTM on synthetic role recovery and link prediction; convincing large-scale demonstrations (e.g., 1M-node YouTube network).
- Clarity of exposition: The model and inference are described clearly, with intuitive motivation from network triadic structure and ERGM literature.

Weaknesses and requested clarifications
- p1: “Parsimonious” is central to the paper but not defined. Please briefly explain what “parsimonious modeling” means here (e.g., lower-dimensional parameterization via role-configuration equivalence classes, with shared parameters B0, Bxx, Bxxx that reduce O(K3) to O(K)), and why this yields better statistical efficiency and scalability.
- p1: Your discussion of a-MMSB suggests that downsampling zeros is “still not fast enough” and implies a potential accuracy issue. In Gopalan et al., downsampling zeros is reported not to compromise accuracy while improving speed. Please clarify this point to avoid a misleading impression and separate accuracy from computational cost.
- p4: The LDA analogy is not appropriate for defending non-generativity at the graph level. LDA’s generative process produces valid documents, whereas your model can generate collections of triangles that do not correspond to any graph (due to missing constraints on overlapping motifs). I agree this is acceptable pragmatically, but the LDA comparison should be removed or reframed.
- p5: Please cite Hoffman et al. (JMLR, “Stochastic Variational Inference”) alongside Amari and Sato. With local (per-triangle) latent variables, Sato’s online VB conditions are not sufficient; the theory supporting your structured SVI with local variables is developed in Hoffman et al.
- p6: The procedure for generating power-law networks in latent space is underspecified. Please describe the generative process in the main text (or provide a precise pointer to the Appendix), including how degree heterogeneity arises from the latent roles.
- p6: Since you position a-MMSB as the scalable MMSB variant, you should compare against it directly, not only MMSB batch. This is especially important for large datasets where scalability is the main claim.
- Empirical fairness: The synthetic-data initialization gives each method the same seeds, which is good, but the MCMC baselines are single-threaded. If possible, clarify whether multi-threaded implementations would materially change conclusions for runtime.
- Link prediction metric: For your triangle-based ranking, please provide more details (e.g., exact marginalization over k and roles, handling of subsampling) to ensure reproducibility.

Quality
- The model and inference are technically sound, with careful derivations and practical approximations (structured mean-field; mixture-of-deltas) justified empirically. The O(K) parameterization is well motivated, and the SVI setup follows standard practice. Please add the missing citations and clarifications noted above.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable. Suggestions:
  - Define “parsimonious” precisely (p1).
  - Reframe the LDA analogy (p4).
  - Expand on the power-law generator (p6).
  - Small: p2 “mixture-membership” → “mixed-membership”; p8 consider removing the final sentence—the penultimate sentence is a stronger ending.

Originality
- The parameter-sharing design for triangle-based latent roles and the O(K) SVI algorithm constitute a meaningful and novel combination of ideas relative to MMTM and a-MMSB. The mixture-of-deltas approximation for local updates is a practical contribution.

Significance
- Strong potential impact for large-scale network analysis: enabling latent role inference with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine in hours, with competitive accuracy. This is likely to be useful to both researchers and practitioners.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Significant scalability advance with clear empirical evidence.
- Solid modeling idea that reduces parameter count without sacrificing performance.
- Clean, practical SVI algorithm with fast convergence.
- Results on substantial real datasets.

Cons:
- Missing comparison to a-MMSB, the most relevant scalable baseline.
- Some theoretical framing and citations (SVI with local variables) need tightening.
- Power-law generator details are vague.
- LDA analogy is not appropriate as stated.

Small comments
- p2: “mixture-membership” → “mixed-membership.”
- p8: Consider removing the last sentence; the previous sentence provides a stronger conclusion.
- Bibliography: Cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference” (Hoffman et al.).

Recommendation
Accept, contingent on addressing the requested clarifications, correcting citations, and adding a comparison to a-MMSB. The paper offers a valuable and well-executed contribution to scalable latent space inference for networks.
