This paper develops a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the triangle model of networks. It introduces a well-motivated parameter-sharing subclass that reduces complexity in a clever way, and employs stochastic variational inference (SVI) to handle very large graphs. The paper is clearly written and technically competent. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the comments below.

Major comments
- p1: Clarify “parsimonious modeling.” This term is central to your contribution but not standard in this context. Please define exactly what is being shared, how the parameter count scales relative to the full model, and why this particular sharing induces the desired inductive bias. A short sentence quantifying the reduction (e.g., from O(K^3) to O(K^2) parameters) would help.
- p1: Your introduction suggests that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy. My reading of Gopalan et al. indicates that, under their scheme, sub-sampling non-edges can retain accuracy (and yield unbiased/consistent gradient estimates) when paired with appropriate weighting. Please revise to reflect the conditions under which down-sampling is accurate, or cite evidence to the contrary.
- p4: The defense by analogy to LDA is not persuasive. Sampling from LDA’s generative process yields a legitimate document, albeit stylized. In contrast, sampling from your triangle model can produce objects that are not valid graphs. This does not detract from the model’s practical value, but the LDA analogy does not address the generative mismatch. Consider reframing this section to acknowledge the approximation and emphasize empirical benefits, theoretical properties you can guarantee, or constraints that enforce graph validity.
- p5: Please cite Hoffman, Blei, Wang, and Paisley (SVI) alongside Amari and Sato. In settings with local latent variables, Sato’s result alone does not cover the required stochastic natural gradient updates; the needed theory, including step-size conditions and unbiasedness requirements, is in Hoffman et al. It would also help to state the step-size schedule and assumptions ensuring convergence.
- p6: Elaborate on how you generated “power-law” networks in the latent space. Which priors or mechanisms induce heavy-tailed degree distributions (e.g., degree-correction, sparse priors, hyperparameter settings)? Provide enough detail to ensure reproducibility and to clarify whether the power law arises from the generative model or from post hoc thresholding choices.
- p6: Since you cite Gopalan et al. as a way to speed up MMSB, please compare against their algorithm in addition to (or instead of) batch MMSB. A fair comparison should include runtime, memory, and accuracy (e.g., held-out edge prediction or link ranking) across matched budgets.

Minor comments
- p2: “mixture-membership” → “mixed-membership.”
- p8: Consider removing the final sentence; the penultimate sentence is a stronger ending.
- Bibliography: Cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference” (Hoffman, Blei, Wang, and Paisley, 2013).

Additional suggestions for clarity and reproducibility
- Provide explicit per-iteration time and memory complexity for both the full and parameter-sharing variants, including dependence on K, number of edges, and subsampling size.
- State the subsampling scheme for non-edges and edges, and how you construct unbiased stochastic gradients (including any importance weights).
- Briefly discuss initialization, identifiability, and sensitivity to K and hyperparameters; show that results are robust across sensible settings.
- If the model can produce non-graphs under certain settings, note practical constraints (e.g., symmetry, sparsity controls) that mitigate this in practice.

Overall assessment
The work is timely and addresses a core scalability challenge in higher-order network modeling. The parameter-sharing subclass is appealing, and the SVI-based approach appears sound and well implemented. With the clarifications above—especially on the LDA analogy, the role of down-sampling, the appropriate methodological citations, and experimental comparisons to stochastic MMSB variants—the paper will be both clearer and stronger. I recommend acceptance.
