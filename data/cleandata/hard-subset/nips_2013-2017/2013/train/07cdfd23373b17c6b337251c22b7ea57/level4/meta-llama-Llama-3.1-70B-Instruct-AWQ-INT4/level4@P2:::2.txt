This paper proposes a novel approach to scalable latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a probabilistic latent space model that defines the generative process for a bag of triangular motifs. By leveraging a succinct representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm, the PTM is able to analyze real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours.

The PTM is related to previous work on latent space models, such as the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB) and the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM). However, the PTM improves upon these models by reducing the dimension of the triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) to O(K), allowing for more efficient inference algorithms. The authors also develop a fast stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for performing variational inference, which has a time complexity of O(NK).

The strengths of the paper include the novel modeling contribution, which combines the ideas of aMMSB and MMTM to create a more scalable implementation. The authors also provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule, which is a key component of the stochastic variational inference algorithm. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the PTM in recovering latent space roles and performing link prediction on synthetic and real networks.

However, there are some ongoing concerns and areas for improvement. The presentation of the material could be improved, particularly in the explanation of the PTM and its relationship to ERGMs. The authors could also provide more discussion on the interpretability of the inferred latent space and what insights can be gained from the similarities and differences among the graphs. Additionally, the difference between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clear, and the authors could provide more explanation on why subsampling the triangles for each node provides an adequate approximation.

The experimental results also raise some questions. For example, the robustness of the latent space recovery results to the threshold of 0.125 is not clear, and the authors could provide more discussion on this point. Additionally, the performance of the method when fewer edges are observed is not evaluated, and the authors could consider adding more experiments to address this concern.

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to scalable latent space inference of large-scale networks. While there are some areas for improvement, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the PTM in recovering latent space roles and performing link prediction on synthetic and real networks.
