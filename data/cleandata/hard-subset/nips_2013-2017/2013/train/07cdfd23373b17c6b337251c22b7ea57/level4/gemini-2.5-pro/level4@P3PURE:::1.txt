This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to further enhance its scalability. Experiments on synthetic data suggest that the parameter reduction may lead to stronger statistical power, while results on real-world datasets show that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

The PTM is an interesting specialization of the MMTM. However, the practical advantage of achieving scalability in K (the number of roles) is not clearly demonstrated. A critical question to evaluate the method's value is: how does the ability to learn an MMTM-style model with a large K help in practice? Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may not be as restrictive as it is for single-membership models. The experiments on real networks would have been much more compelling if they had shown the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with a large K could be shown to outperform MMTM limited to a small K, it would be a powerful demonstration of PTM's utility. Unfortunately, such experiments were not performed. Furthermore, the paper does not adequately describe how K was chosen for either PTM or its competitor, MMSB, in the real-data experiments; this lack of detail on model selection could potentially bias the results.

The authors are also reticent about the impact of the simplifying assumptions their model makes. While it is clear that constraining the parameter space improves scalability, a discussion of the model's failure modes is needed. For instance, if the true parameter matrix B (using the authors' notation) contains O(K^3) distinct values, as is possible in MMSB and MMTM, one would expect PTM to perform poorly. In this regard, the description of the true B matrix used in the synthetic experiments is insufficient. The authors should have experimented with a variety of B matrices, including some that favor the PTM's assumptions and others that violate them. It is more beneficial for the reader to understand the conditions under which PTM succeeds or fails than to only see cases where it succeeds.

Additionally, while the paper introduces several interesting and effective techniques to make PTM scalable, it does a poor job of describing and analyzing their impact. Several key questions are left unanswered:
1.  What is the impact of the threshold δ? How does the choice of a larger or smaller δ affect the trade-off between scalability and accuracy in the experiments?
2.  The authors claim that a downsampling approach like a-MMSB compromises accuracy. Why is PTM immune to this problem? MMTM and PTM already ignore information by modeling only closed triplets (i.e., ignoring triplets with only one or two edges). Why is it acceptable to ignore this information in PTM, but not to ignore non-edges in MMSB?
3.  What is the impact of the O(K) approximation used for the local update (page 5)? What happens if 2K, 4K, or 100K triples are sampled instead? Does the quality of this approximation degrade as K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the discussion of the potential weaknesses of the model and algorithm is lacking.

**Clarity:**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:**

While the modeling aspect is somewhat incremental, the approach of achieving scalability by constraining model parameters is an interesting and original direction, especially as much recent work has focused on generalizing existing models rather than scaling them.

**Significance:**

If the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to large networks, as few such models are truly scalable.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The title seems overly broad. The paper proposes a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model (PTM), whereas the title might suggest a more general contribution.
*   I have a more fundamental concern about whether PTM is a valid probabilistic model. The authors argue that the model's "bag-of-triples" assumption is similar in spirit to the bag-of-words assumption in topic modeling. I am not convinced by this analogy. When LDA generates a bag-of-words, there is always a corresponding document that could have produced it, ensuring a proper likelihood function. In contrast, a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM or PTM will, with very high probability, not correspond to any valid network graph. This implies that MMTM/PTM are not truly generative models for networks, as the vast majority of their posterior probability mass is placed on modeling objects that are not graphs. It seems potentially dangerous to abandon the basic principles of likelihood in a "probabilistic" method. What does probability mean within the PTM framework? I would be curious to hear the authors' perspective on this issue.
