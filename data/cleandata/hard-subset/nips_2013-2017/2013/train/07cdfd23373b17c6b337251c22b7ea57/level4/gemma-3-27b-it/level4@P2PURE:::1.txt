This paper introduces the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) from O(K^3) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, incorporating further approximations to improve scalability. Results on synthetic data suggest that this parameter reduction can increase statistical power, and experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.

Quality:
PTM represents an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of improved scalability with respect to K (the number of possible roles) remains unclear. To properly evaluate this method, it's crucial to understand “how does enabling MMTM with large K help?” Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, the challenges associated with small K may be less significant than in single-membership models. For network experiments, a compelling analysis would demonstrate the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K; observing PTM with large K outperform MMTM with small K would strongly support its advantages. This type of experiment was not conducted. Furthermore, the criteria for selecting K values in the real data experiments for both PTM and MMSB were not clearly articulated, potentially introducing bias into the results.

The authors are somewhat reserved regarding the implications of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space demonstrably improves scalability, it’s important to understand *when* this model might perform poorly. If the true parameter matrix B (following the authors’ notation) in MMSB and MMTM contains a substantial number of distinct values – potentially O(K^3) – PTM’s performance is likely to suffer. The lack of detail surrounding the choice of the true B in the synthetic experiments is problematic; experimentation with various B matrices, some favorable to PTM and others unfavorable, would be highly informative.  It’s more valuable to understand both the strengths and weaknesses of PTM than to solely demonstrate its successes.

The paper also introduces several techniques to enhance PTM’s scalability. However, the description and analysis of the impact of these approximations are insufficient. 1) What is the effect of the threshold δ? Do larger or smaller values of δ yield better results on both real-world and synthetic data?  What is the trade-off between reducing δ to improve scalability and preserving accuracy? 2) The authors claim a-MMSB fails due to accuracy loss from downsampling; however, it’s unclear why PTM avoids this issue. Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets lacking a single edge – why can’t MMSB do the same? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation on the local update step (page 5)?  How would sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples affect the approximation quality, especially as K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced the experimental setup provides a fair comparison to competitors, and the discussion of the algorithm/model’s potential limitations is inadequate.

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

Originality: The modeling approach is somewhat incremental, but achieving scalability through parameter constraints is an interesting and original direction, particularly given the recent focus on generalizing existing models in this area.

Significance:  If the model’s simplifying assumptions and inference algorithm prove practical, it could significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large networks, as scalability remains a major challenge for many such models.

Additional Comments: The title is overly broad. The authors propose a scalable *algorithm* for a specific latent space model – PTM – rather than a broadly applicable scalable approach. 

Furthermore, I question whether PTM is truly a probabilistic method. The authors’ analogy to the bag-of-words assumption in Section 3 is unconvincing. LDA generates bags-of-words from underlying documents with a well-defined, albeit potentially simplistic, likelihood function. In contrast, MMTM and PTM generate bags-of-triples that, with high probability, do not correspond to any valid network. Consequently, MMTM/PTM do not model networks generatively, and the majority of posterior probability mass is allocated to non-networks. Abandoning fundamental principles of likelihood in “probabilistic” methods raises concerns (what does probability even *mean* in PTM?). I am interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives on this point. Ultimately, the authors propose modeling assumptions and inference techniques to improve MMTM scalability, but the side effects of these choices are not sufficiently explored.
