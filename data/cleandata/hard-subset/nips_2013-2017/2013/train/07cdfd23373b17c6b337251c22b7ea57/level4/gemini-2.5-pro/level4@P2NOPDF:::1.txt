### **Summary**

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a specialization of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) designed for improved scalability. The core contribution is constraining the model's parameter space from O(K³) to O(K), where K is the number of latent roles. To accompany this more compact model, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm and incorporate several further approximations to enhance computational efficiency. The experimental results on synthetic data suggest this parameter reduction can improve statistical power, while results on real-world networks demonstrate that PTM is competitive with existing methods in terms of predictive accuracy.

### **Review**

**Quality and Significance**

This paper addresses the important problem of scalability in latent space models for network data. The approach of constraining the parameter space to achieve this goal is an interesting and valuable direction. The proposed model and its associated inference algorithm have the potential for significant practical impact, provided the underlying assumptions are validated. However, the current evaluation is insufficient to fully support the claims, and the paper lacks a critical analysis of the model's limitations and the trade-offs introduced by the various approximations.

My major concerns are detailed below:

**1. Insufficient Motivation and Validation for Scaling with K:**
The primary motivation for PTM is scalability in K, the number of roles. However, the practical benefit of learning a model with a very large K is not clearly established. In mixed-membership models, unlike in single-membership models, a smaller K can still provide substantial expressive power. The most convincing experiment to demonstrate PTM's utility would be to show that a PTM with a large, computationally feasible K can outperform a standard MMTM that is restricted to a small K due to computational constraints. This critical experiment is absent from the paper.

Furthermore, the methodology for choosing K in the real-world experiments is not described for either PTM or its competitor, MMSB. This is a crucial omission, as the model selection process could significantly bias the comparison and obscure the true performance differences.

**2. Lack of Analysis on Model Limitations:**
The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the scenarios where PTM is likely to fail. Any model simplification introduces trade-offs, and it is essential to characterize them. Specifically, if the true generative process of a network involves an interaction matrix *B* with O(K³) distinct parameters, PTM's O(K) assumption would be incorrect, and the model would presumably perform poorly.

The synthetic experiments miss an opportunity to explore this. The paper does not clearly describe the structure of the true *B* matrix used for data generation. A more rigorous evaluation would test the model on various synthetic datasets, including some generated from a full-rank *B* matrix that is unfavorable to PTM's assumptions. This would help readers understand not just when PTM succeeds, but also when it is likely to fail.

**3. Inadequate Analysis of Algorithmic Approximations:**
The authors introduce several clever and effective tricks to make inference scalable, but their impact and sensitivity are not analyzed. This leaves several important open questions:
*   **Threshold δ:** The paper introduces a threshold δ for subsampling triplets. What is the sensitivity of the model's performance (both accuracy and runtime) to the choice of δ? An ablation study is needed to understand this trade-off.
*   **Data Subsampling Comparison:** The authors claim that downsampling non-edges in a-MMSB compromises accuracy. However, MMTM and PTM inherently discard information by considering only triplets with at least two edges. The paper should provide a clearer justification for why its form of data reduction is acceptable while others are not.
*   **Local Update Approximation:** The paper proposes an O(K) approximation for a local update step. How does the quality of this approximation depend on K? For instance, what is the performance impact of sampling 2K, 4K, or more triplets for this step, and does the approximation's quality degrade as K grows?

**4. Fundamental Questions about the Probabilistic Formulation:**
There is a potential conceptual issue with framing MMTM and PTM as true probabilistic models for networks. The paper's analogy to the "bag-of-words" assumption in LDA is questionable. While any bag of words generated by LDA corresponds to a valid (though perhaps nonsensical) document, a "bag-of-triples" generated by MMTM/PTM will, with very high probability, not correspond to any valid graph due to transitivity constraints (e.g., the triples (i,j,k), (j,k,l), and (i,k,l) might be present, but not (i,j,l)). This means the model is not a true generative model for the space of graphs, as most of its probability mass lies on objects that are not graphs. This departure from a well-defined likelihood over the target data structure is concerning and warrants further discussion.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-organized and very clearly written. The motivations and methods are explained in an easy-to-follow manner.

**Originality**
While the model is an incremental modification of MMTM, the core idea of achieving scalability via parameter constraint is an original and timely contribution. This contrasts with much recent work in this area that has focused on developing more complex models rather than more efficient ones.

### **Conclusion**

The paper presents an interesting model and a promising direction for scalable network analysis. However, the experimental evaluation is not comprehensive enough to fully support the claims, and the paper avoids a critical discussion of the model's limitations and the impact of its various approximations. While the ideas are valuable, the work in its current form is not ready for publication. I would be willing to reconsider my recommendation if the authors can address the major concerns outlined above, particularly through more rigorous experimental validation.

**Additional Comment**
The title, "Scalable Probabilistic Latent Space Models for Network Data," seems overly broad. The paper proposes a scalable algorithm for a specific type of latent space model (PTM) rather than a general framework.
