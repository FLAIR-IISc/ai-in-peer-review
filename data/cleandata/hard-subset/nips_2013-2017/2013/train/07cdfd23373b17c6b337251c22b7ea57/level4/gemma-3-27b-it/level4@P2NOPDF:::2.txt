This paper introduces a novel technique for learning latent structure within social network data by modeling triangle count statistics – effectively treating these counts as a bag-of-words representation. Building on the idea of a mixed-membership stochastic blockmodel (aMMSB), the authors propose a model that focuses on triangle configurations to identify a set of *K* latent roles, thereby reducing the parameter space and enhancing scalability. They leverage recent advancements in stochastic variational inference (SVI), deriving both global and local update rules for their model, with a particularly insightful approximation offered for the local updates.

The core contribution lies in the integration of aMMSB principles with a mixed-membership triangle model (MMTM), although the presentation of this combination could benefit from further clarity and conciseness. While the learning algorithm largely applies established SVI techniques, the proposed local update approximation is both intuitive and promising.

However, several aspects of the work warrant further consideration. The authors claim their “Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs.” This analogy feels imprecise; ERGMs are capable of modeling a much broader range of network statistics than solely triangles, and the notion of a "latent space counterpart" appears broadly applicable to many latent network models, diminishing its specificity here. 

A key question remains regarding the interpretability of the inferred latent space. Do the discovered roles offer meaningful insights into the underlying similarities and differences between the analyzed graphs? The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how these roles can be characterized and understood in practical terms.

Several technical details require clarification. The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² is currently unclear. The rationale for subsampling triangles (using delta) to approximate the full dataset is not fully explained – justification for the adequacy of this approximation is needed. Given the model’s goal of recovering a set of non-zero roles, exploring a more explicitly binary representation of these roles could be beneficial. Furthermore, the sensitivity of the latent space recovery to the threshold value of 0.125 (which appears somewhat arbitrary) needs to be assessed. 

Regarding experiments, the paper should specify which latent space model was used to generate the power-law networks for comparison. There's also a discrepancy concerning the computational complexity of PTM-CGS. The authors previously emphasize the model’s O(K) parameter count, yet state a K³ complexity for PTM-CGS – a clarification is needed.

A potential limitation concerns performance on sparse networks. The experiments only withhold 10% of edges, likely to avoid significant bias in the adjacent triangle distribution resulting from extensive edge removal during preprocessing. It’s important to address how the method performs with fewer observed edges. Finally, the result showing PTM outperforming MMSB on a synthetic network *generated* by MMSB (Figure 1, left four panels) is counterintuitive and deserves further investigation.

In summary, this work presents a promising approach to scalable network modeling using SVI on triangle counts, effectively implementing a restricted parameter mixed-membership blockmodel. The use of triangle configurations to define latent roles is a novel contribution. Addressing the raised concerns regarding clarity, justification of approximations, interpretability, and robustness will significantly strengthen the paper.
