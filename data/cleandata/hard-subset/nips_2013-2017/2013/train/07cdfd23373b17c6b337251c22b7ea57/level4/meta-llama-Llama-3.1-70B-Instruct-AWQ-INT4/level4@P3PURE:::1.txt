This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation tricks to make it further scalable. The experimental results show that the reduction of the number of variables may lead to stronger statistical power on synthetic datasets, and that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy on real-world datasets.

Quality:
While PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, it is questionable whether achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) has practical advantages. To empirically evaluate the value of such a method, it is crucial to answer "how does it help if we can learn MMTM with large K?" Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may not be as troublesome as it is in single-membership models. For real network experiments, it would be more informative to see the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K, it would demonstrate the power of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the description of how K's were chosen in real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB is lacking, and the method of model selection could have biased the experimental results.

The authors are also reticent about the impact of the simplifying assumptions they make. While it is clear that constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it would be beneficial to discuss when such a model would fail. If there are indeed O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly. In this regard, it is unsatisfactory that the choice of true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described. The authors should have experimented with various B's, some of which favor PTM and others that do not. It would be more beneficial for readers to know when PTM would succeed and when it would fail, rather than only knowing when it would succeed.

On the other hand, the authors introduce several approximation tricks to make PTM scale, which I find to be very interesting and effective. However, the paper does a poor job in describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. Specifically, the following questions remain unanswered: 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold δ? On real-world and synthetic data experiments, does it help to choose larger or smaller δ? How much can we lower δ to improve scalability while not sacrificing much accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work since downsampling compromises accuracy; why would PTM be free of such problems? MMTM and PTM are actually ignoring triplets with only one edge. Why can't we ignore non-edges in MMSB while we can in PTM? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates (in page 5)? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K of triples? Would the quality of the approximation suffer when K gets larger?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm/model is poor.

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

Originality: In terms of modeling, it is a bit incremental, but achieving scalability via constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction to pursue, as recently in this topic, a lot of work has been focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance: Provided the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm hold in practice, it would have a good impact on the practice of latent-space models on networks, as not many of them actually scale to large data.

Additional Comments: Personally, I think the title is too broad. There are other scalable approaches to other latent space models, and the authors propose a scalable algorithm to a latent space model, PTM.

I am also not convinced that PTM is a truly probabilistic method. The authors argue that this is in the same spirit as the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not convinced. When LDA generates a bag-of-words, there always exists a document corresponding to it, so it has a proper likelihood function, no matter how unrealistically simple it might be. On the other hand, with very high probability, there does not exist any network corresponding to a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM. Therefore, MMTM/PTM is not really a generative model for networks, and the majority of the posterior probability mass is placed on modeling non-networks. It might be dangerous to abandon basic principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" methods (what does probability mean in PTM?). I am curious what the authors and other reviewers think. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks that would make inference on MMTM scalable. However, the side effects of such decisions are poorly investigated.
