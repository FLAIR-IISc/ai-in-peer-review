This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a method designed to accelerate inference in Mixed-Membership Triangular Models (MMTM) by reducing the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K). The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm, further enhanced with approximations, to improve scalability. Results on synthetic data suggest a potential for increased statistical power with this parameter reduction, and experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate competitive accuracy compared to existing approaches.

**Quality:**

While PTM presents an interesting specialization of MMTM, the practical benefit of improved scalability with respect to K (the number of roles) remains unclear. A crucial evaluation would demonstrate the value of applying this method when learning MMTM with large K – specifically, showing scenarios where PTM with a large K outperforms MMTM with a smaller K. This was not explored in the presented experiments. Furthermore, the method of selecting K for both PTM and its competitor, MMSB, in the real-world network experiments lacks sufficient detail, raising concerns about potential bias in the results. 

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the implications of the simplifying assumptions made. While parameter space constraints demonstrably improve scalability, it is important to understand the conditions under which the model might perform poorly. For example, if the parameter matrix B (in the authors’ notation) contains a substantial number of distinct values, PTM’s performance could suffer. The synthetic data experiments should have included a range of B matrices, some favoring PTM and others challenging its assumptions, to provide a more complete picture of its strengths and weaknesses. Understanding *when* PTM succeeds and fails is more valuable than solely demonstrating its successes.

The numerous approximation tricks employed to enhance PTM’s scalability are interesting, but the paper provides insufficient analysis of their impact. Specifically: 1) The effect of the threshold δ is unclear. How does varying δ influence performance on both real and synthetic data? What is the trade-off between scalability and accuracy as δ is lowered? 2) The claim that downsampling compromises accuracy in a-MMSB, while PTM avoids this issue, requires further justification. Both MMTM and PTM disregard triplets with only one edge; why can’t a similar approach be applied to MMSB? 3) The impact of the O(K) approximation in the local update step (page 5) needs further investigation. How does performance change when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does approximation quality degrade as K increases?

In summary, I am not fully convinced that the experimental setup provides a fair comparison to competitors, and the discussion of potential model limitations is inadequate.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Originality:** The modeling approach is somewhat incremental, but the strategy of achieving scalability through parameter constraints is an original and promising direction, particularly given the recent trend towards generalizing existing models in this field.

**Significance:** If the underlying assumptions of the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, PTM has the potential to significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large-scale networks, a domain where scalability remains a major challenge.

**Additional Comments:** The title may be overly broad. The paper presents a scalable *algorithm* for a specific latent space model (PTM), rather than a broadly applicable scalable approach. 

I also question whether PTM can truly be considered a probabilistic method. The authors’ analogy to the bag-of-words assumption in Section 3 is unconvincing. LDA generates bags-of-words from underlying documents with a defined likelihood function, however unrealistic. In contrast, MMTM and PTM generate bags-of-triples that rarely correspond to actual networks, placing the majority of posterior probability mass on non-existent network structures. Abandoning fundamental principles of likelihood in “probabilistic” methods raises concerns about the interpretation of probabilities within PTM. I am interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives on this point. The paper proposes valuable modeling assumptions and inference techniques for scaling MMTM, but the consequences of these choices require more thorough investigation.
