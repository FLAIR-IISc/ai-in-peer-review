This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data by modeling triangle count statistics – analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text. The central modeling advance builds upon aMMSB: instead of parameterizing all between-"community" interactions, the authors leverage triangle configurations to identify a set of K roles, thereby reducing the number of parameters and improving scalability. They apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving both global and local update rules for the proposed model.

The core contribution is a straightforward combination of aMMSB and MMTM, although the presentation requires somewhat tedious bookkeeping and could be improved for clarity. While most of the learning algorithm is a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors offer an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule. 

Given the prevalence of directed networks, a discussion of potential extensions to these methods would be valuable. 

Several points require further clarification:

* The statement that “our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs” is questionable. First, ERGMs can incorporate a wider range of statistics than just triangles. Second, the claim of a “latent space counterpart” feels overly broad, as many latent space network models could arguably be described as such.
* Are the inferred latent spaces interpretable? If so, what insights do they provide regarding the similarities and differences between the graphs analyzed?
* The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is unclear.
* The justification for approximating triangle counts by subsampling for each node (using delta) is not adequately explained.
* Given the goal of recovering a “set of non-zero roles,” a more binary notion of “role” within the model might be beneficial.
* How sensitive are the latent space recovery results to the threshold of 0.125, which appears somewhat arbitrary?
* What latent space model was used to generate the power-law networks in the experiments?
* The reported O(K^3) complexity of PTM CGS seems inconsistent with the claim of O(K) parameters.
* A potential concern is performance when the observed graph contains fewer edges. The experiments only withhold 10% of edges, likely to avoid significant bias in the adjacent triangle distribution caused by more extensive removal during preprocessing.
* The result that PTM outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated *by* MMSB (Figure 1, left four panels) is counterintuitive. 

In summary, this work employs stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel (with a restricted parameter set) based on triangle counts in a graph. The modeling choices and approximations presented allow for a more scalable O(NK) algorithm.
