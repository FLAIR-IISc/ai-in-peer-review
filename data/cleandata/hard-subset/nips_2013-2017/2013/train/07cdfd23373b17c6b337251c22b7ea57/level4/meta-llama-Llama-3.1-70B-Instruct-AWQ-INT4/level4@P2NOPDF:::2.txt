This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data by fitting a model to triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, similar to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance is motivated by the Affiliation Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB), where instead of parameterizing all between-community interactions, the authors consider the configurations of the triangles to identify a set of K roles to parameterize. This approach results in fewer parameters in the model and a more scalable implementation. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving a global update and local updates for the proposed model.

The novel modeling contribution is a combination of aMMSB with the Mixed Membership Matrix Triad Model (MMTM), although the presentation of this material could be improved due to the tedious bookkeeping required. While most of the learning algorithm is a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule.

However, there are several ongoing concerns. The authors' statement that their Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is "essentially a latent space counterpart to Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs)" seems curious, as ERGMs can include more varied statistics than just triangles, and it is unclear how PTM is a "latent space counterpart" unless all latent space network models are considered as such.

Additionally, it is unclear whether the inferred latent space is interpretable and what insights can be gained about the similarities and differences among the graphs. The difference between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clear, and it is not explained why subsampling the triangles for each node (using delta) provides an adequate approximation. Furthermore, if the goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles," perhaps a more binary notion of "role" should be incorporated into the model.

The robustness of the latent space recovery results to the threshold of 0.125 is also a concern, as this value seems somewhat arbitrary. The authors should clarify which latent space model was used for the experiments, as various models can produce power-law networks. It is also unclear why the PTM with collapsed Gibbs sampling (CGS) has K^3 complexity, as the authors argue that there are O(K) parameters rather than O(K^3).

Another concern is the performance of the method when fewer edges are observed, as the experiments only hold out 10% of the edges. This may be because the distribution of adjacent triangles will be biased if too many edges are removed prior to the preprocessing step. The results also show that the method beats the Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB) on a synthetic network generated via MMSB, which is counterintuitive.

The authors' use of stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel (with a restricted set of parameters) on the triangle counts in a graph is a valuable contribution. Their modeling choices and approximations allow for a more scalable O(NK) algorithm. A detailed discussion of how these methods might extend to directed networks would be welcome, given their prevalence.
