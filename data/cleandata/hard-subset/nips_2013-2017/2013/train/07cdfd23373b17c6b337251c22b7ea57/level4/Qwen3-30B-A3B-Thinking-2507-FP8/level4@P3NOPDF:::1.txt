

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) from O(K³) to O(K) for faster inference, supported by a stochastic variational inference algorithm and additional scalability tricks. Experiments on synthetic data suggest reduced parameters enhance statistical power, while real-world results position PTM competitively against existing methods.  

**Quality.** While PTM presents an interesting specialization of MMTM, its practical advantage remains unclear. The paper claims scalability in K (number of roles) as a key contribution, yet fails to demonstrate *why* this matters. For mixed-membership models like MMTM, small K is less problematic than in single-membership settings (e.g., SBM), so the primary question should be: *Does PTM with large K outperform MMTM with small K?* Crucially, the authors omit this critical comparison. Real-world experiments also lack transparency on how K was selected for PTM and its competitor MMSB, risking biased conclusions due to unreported model selection practices.  

Furthermore, the paper inadequately addresses model limitations. PTM’s simplifying assumptions—particularly the constraint on parameter matrix B—could severely degrade performance if B contains O(K³) distinct entries. Yet the synthetic experiments use a single, unspecified B, ignoring cases where PTM would fail. A balanced evaluation should include both "favorable" and "unfavorable" B matrices to clarify PTM’s scope. Without this, readers learn only when PTM succeeds, not when it fails.  

The paper also poorly justifies key approximation techniques:  
1. **Threshold δ**: How does δ impact scalability vs. accuracy? Can it be minimized without significant accuracy loss?  
2. **Downsampling**: The authors dismiss a-MMSB due to accuracy loss from downsampling, but PTM similarly ignores triplets with only one edge. Why is this acceptable for PTM but not MMSB?  
3. **Local update approximation**: What is the effect of sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does approximation quality degrade as K grows?  
These gaps prevent readers from assessing the trade-offs of PTM’s efficiency gains.  

**Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making technical contributions accessible.  

**Originality.** The approach of constraining parameters to achieve scalability is novel in a field dominated by model generalization. However, the modeling contribution itself is incremental.  

**Significance.** If PTM’s assumptions hold in practice, it could significantly advance scalable latent-space network modeling. Yet the lack of failure analysis and questionable experimental design undermines this potential.  

**Additional Comments.** The title ("Parsimonious Triangular Model") is misleading; PTM is a scalable *algorithm* for a specific model, not a broad scalable method for latent-space models. More critically, PTM’s probabilistic foundation is unsound. The authors compare it to LDA’s "bag-of-words" assumption, but this analogy fails: LDA generates valid documents (each bag corresponds to a real document), while PTM’s "bag-of-triples" almost surely describes a *non-network* (as most triple combinations do not form a valid adjacency matrix). Consequently, PTM’s posterior mass concentrates on non-networks, violating core probabilistic principles. This is not merely a simplification—it is a fundamental misalignment with generative modeling. The paper’s claim that PTM is "probabilistic" is therefore misleading and potentially dangerous for practitioners.  

**Conclusion.** PTM offers a technically interesting path to scalability but is undermined by three critical flaws: (1) the absence of a fairness test comparing PTM (large K) vs. MMTM (small K); (2) insufficient analysis of model failure modes; and (3) a flawed probabilistic interpretation. Without addressing these, the paper’s contribution is overstated. While the writing is strong, the experimental and theoretical shortcomings prevent this from being a compelling advance for NIPS. I recommend rejection pending substantial revisions addressing the model’s limitations and probabilistic validity.
