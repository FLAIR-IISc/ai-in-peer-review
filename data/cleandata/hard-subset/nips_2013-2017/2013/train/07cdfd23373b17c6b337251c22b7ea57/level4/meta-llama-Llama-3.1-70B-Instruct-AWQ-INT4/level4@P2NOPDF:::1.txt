This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a specialization of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) that reduces the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation tricks to enhance scalability. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve stronger statistical power and competitive accuracy with existing methods.

Quality:
While PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, its practical advantages in achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) are questionable. To empirically evaluate the value of this method, it is crucial to investigate how it can help learn MMTM with large K. Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may not be as problematic as it is in single-membership models. A more informative experiment would be to compare the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K, demonstrating whether PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the method of model selection and the choice of K in real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB were not clearly described, which could have biased the experimental results.

The authors are also reticent about the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it is essential to discuss when the model may fail. If there are indeed O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly. The choice of true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described, and the authors should have experimented with various B's that favor and disfavor PTM. It would be more beneficial for readers to understand when PTM succeeds and fails, rather than only knowing when it succeeds.

On the other hand, the authors introduce several tricks to make PTM scalable, which are interesting and effective. However, the paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. Specifically, the impact of choosing the threshold δ is unclear, and it is unknown whether choosing larger or smaller δ helps on real-world and synthetic data experiments. Additionally, the authors claim that a-MMSB does not work due to downsampling compromising accuracy, but it is unclear why PTM is free from this problem. The paper also ignores the impact of O(K) approximation to local updates and does not discuss what happens when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples.

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm/model is poor.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized.

Originality: While the modeling is somewhat incremental, achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction to pursue, as recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance: If the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the practice of latent-space models on networks, as few of these models actually scale to large data.

Additional Comments: The title is too broad, as there are other scalable approaches to latent space models, and the authors propose a scalable algorithm for a specific model, PTM. Furthermore, I am not convinced that PTM is a truly probabilistic method. The authors argue that their approach is similar in spirit to the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not convinced. In contrast to LDA, which generates bags-of-words with corresponding documents, MMTM and PTM generate bags-of-triples that may not correspond to actual networks. This raises concerns about abandoning basic principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" methods and the meaning of probability in PTM. I am curious to hear the authors' and other reviewers' thoughts on this matter.
