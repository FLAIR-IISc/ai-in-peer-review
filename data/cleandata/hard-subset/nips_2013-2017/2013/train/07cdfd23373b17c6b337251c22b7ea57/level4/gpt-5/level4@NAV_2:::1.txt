This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameterization of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K), enabling faster inference. The authors derive a stochastic variational inference procedure for PTM and add further approximations to improve scalability. On synthetic data, the results suggest that reducing the parameter count can yield better statistical efficiency, and on real-world networks the method achieves competitive or better accuracy than prior approaches at substantially lower runtime.

Quality:
PTM is an appealing specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of scaling in K (number of roles) is not fully substantiated. To motivate why scalability in K matters, we need evidence that “running with larger K” improves downstream performance in ways that smaller K cannot, especially since mixed-membership models (MMSB/MMTM) often mitigate small-K limitations by allowing overlap. A more compelling evaluation would present performance for both MMTM and PTM as a function of K; demonstrating that PTM at large K outperforms MMTM at small K would directly support the paper’s thesis. As it stands, I could not find a clear description of how K was selected on the real datasets for PTM (and MMSB); the model selection strategy could have biased outcomes.

The paper also under-discusses the implications of its simplifying assumptions. Constraining the parameter space yields scalability, but when does this harm fidelity? If, in truth, the triangle-generating tensor B in MMSB/MMTM contains many distinct entries (i.e., closer to O(K^3)), one would expect PTM to underfit. In this light, the synthetic setup lacks transparency: the true B used in simulations is not clearly specified, and there are no scenarios where the ground-truth B favors or disfavors PTM. A systematic study delineating when PTM succeeds versus fails would be more informative than only showcasing settings in which it works well.

The paper proposes several practical approximations whose effects are not analyzed in sufficient depth:
1) δ-thresholding for triangle subsampling: What is the empirical trade-off between δ, accuracy, and runtime on both synthetic and real data? How far can δ be reduced before accuracy meaningfully degrades?
2) Claims about a-MMSB and downsampling: If downsampling non-edges harms accuracy in MMSB, why is PTM immune to analogous information loss (PTM/MMTM exclude single-edge triples)? If ignoring non-edges is problematic for MMSB, why is ignoring certain triplets acceptable for PTM?
3) The O(K) local-update approximation (page 5): How does performance vary if the number of sampled triples is 2K, 4K, or 100K? Does approximation quality deteriorate as K grows?

Overall, I remain unconvinced that the experimental setup is fully fair to competing methods, and the discussion of potential failure modes is limited.

Clarity:
The manuscript is very clearly written and well organized.

Originality:
Modeling-wise, the contribution is somewhat incremental, but using parameter sharing to attain scalability is a timely and interesting direction, particularly as much recent work has emphasized model generality over computational tractability.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference hold broadly in practice, this work could meaningfully impact the application of latent-space models to large networks, where few probabilistic methods currently scale.

Additional Comments:
- The title seems too broad; many scalable approaches exist for other latent-space models, while this paper presents a scalable approach for a specific latent-space model (PTM).
- I am not fully persuaded that PTM is a probabilistic model of networks in the usual sense. The paper argues an analogy to the bag-of-words assumption, but in LDA a sampled bag of words always corresponds to some document, yielding a proper likelihood even if simplistic. In contrast, with high probability there is no actual network corresponding to a sampled bag-of-triples under MMTM/PTM; the posterior mass is therefore over “non-networks,” so the model is not generative for networks. It is risky to relax basic likelihood principles in a “probabilistic” framework—what exactly is the probability measure over in PTM? I would welcome the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives here. The modeling choices and inference heuristics do make MMTM-like inference scalable, but the side effects are insufficiently explored.
