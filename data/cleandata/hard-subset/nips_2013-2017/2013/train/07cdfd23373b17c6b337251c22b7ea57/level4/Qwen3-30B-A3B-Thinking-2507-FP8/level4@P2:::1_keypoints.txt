- Proposal: PTM reduces parameterization from O(K^3) to O(K) and introduces a stochastic variational inference algorithm with approximation techniques for faster inference.  
- Empirical claim: Achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups over state-of-the-art while maintaining competitive accuracy on synthetic and real networks.  
- Strength: Addresses three scalability principles—succinct representation (triangles), parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference—with a well-motivated O(K) parameterization.  
- Major experimental limitation: Fails to demonstrate PTM’s advantage when K is large; missing critical comparison of PTM (large K) vs MMTM (small K).  
- Experimental bias concern: Real-world experiments do not clarify how K was chosen for PTM and MMSB, risking biased comparisons.  
- Missing failure-mode analysis: Does not investigate cases where true B has O(K^3) distinct values, a scenario where PTM would likely fail.  
- Synthetic experiment weakness: Synthetic B was chosen to favor PTM; paper lacks tests on adversarial or neutral B settings.  
- Parameter sensitivity gaps: Insufficient evaluation of triangle subsampling threshold δ and its accuracy vs scalability trade-offs.  
- Approximation analysis gap: O(K) local update approximation (Section 4.3) lacks study of how approximation quality changes with sampled triple counts (e.g., 2K, 4K, 100K) as K grows.  
- Clarity: Paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and reproducible.  
- Originality: Novel focus on parameter-space reduction for scalability rather than model generalization.  
- Significance: If assumptions hold, PTM could enable latent-space modeling on million-node networks, addressing an important scalability gap.  
- Title and scope concern: Title is overly broad given the paper focuses on a single model (PTM), not general scalable probabilistic inference.  
- Probabilistic foundation concern: Questionable probabilistic interpretation—PTM’s bags-of-triangles formulation assigns most mass to non-networks and deviates from standard probabilistic modeling assumptions.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Impressive scalability (1M nodes in hours on one machine), competitive accuracy, and excellent writing.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Lack of critical experimental comparison (large-K PTM vs small-K MMTM), unexamined limitations/failure modes, and unclear probabilistic justification.  
- Overall: Valuable and practically significant approach but requires stronger, more thorough experimental validation and clearer discussion of probabilistic interpretation to fully support superiority claims.
