- Introduces PTM reducing MMTM parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) with stochastic variational inference and scaling tricks.  
- Practical benefit of PTM’s K-scalability is questionable without experiments showing large-K PTM outperforming small-K MMTM.  
- Real-data experiments lack clarification on how K was chosen, risking biased comparisons with competitors.  
- Paper fails to characterize failure modes: no synthetic tests where B has Ω(K^3) distinct values to show when PTM should break.  
- Need to know when PTM succeeds or fails, not just success cases.  
- Description of approximation techniques is superficial and leaves key questions unanswered.  
- Unclear how threshold δ trades off scalability vs. accuracy, whether there’s an optimal δ, and how it scales with K.  
- Unexplained why PTM avoids the accuracy loss from downsampling in MMSB despite similar triplet discarding.  
- Missing analysis of the O(K) approximation’s impact on local updates and performance as more triples are sampled (e.g., 2K, 100K).  
- Paper is well-written and logically structured (clarity positive).  
- Constraining parameters for scalability is a novel direction in the field (originality positive).  
- Significance is conditional: PTM could be impactful if its assumptions hold, but unaddressed limitations weaken its value.  
- Title is overly broad and misrepresents the contribution as a general approach rather than a specific scalable algorithm.  
- PTM lacks a proper generative foundation/likelihood; generating “bags of triples” rarely yields valid networks and undermines probabilistic interpretation.  
- Experimental design is unfair and incomplete; technical contributions are modest and empirical claims about scalability need stronger support—recommend rejection.
