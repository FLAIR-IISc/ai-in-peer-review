This paper proposes a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. The authors introduce a powerful and elegant subclass of the model that utilizes parameter sharing to manage complexity. To enable inference on very large networks, they effectively apply stochastic variational inference. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the proposed method is thoroughly executed. Overall, this is a strong contribution that I believe is suitable for acceptance.

Below are several points, ranging from major to minor, that could further strengthen the manuscript.

### Major Comments

1.  **Justification via Analogy to LDA (p4):** I am sympathetic to the pragmatic benefits of the triangle model, but the defense of its generative properties by appealing to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) seems inappropriate. If one follows the generative process of LDA, the output will always be a valid, albeit potentially nonsensical, document. In contrast, the generative process for the triangle model can produce objects that are not valid networks. While this does not diminish the model's practical utility, the justification by means of the LDA analogy is unconvincing and should be revised.

2.  **Experimental Comparison to Gopalan et al. (p6):** The paper cites Gopalan et al. as a method for speeding up inference for the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB). However, the experiments compare the proposed method only against a batch version of MMSB. To properly evaluate the scalability and performance of the proposed algorithm, a direct comparison with the accelerated approach of Gopalan et al. is essential, either in addition to or instead of the batch MMSB baseline.

3.  **Citation for Stochastic Variational Inference (p5):** The paper cites Amari and Sato in the discussion of stochastic variational inference. However, Sato's algorithm does not directly apply to scenarios with local latent variables, which are present in this model. The theoretical framework developed in Hoffman et al. ("Stochastic Variational Inference") is the more appropriate foundation for this setting. The authors should cite Hoffman et al. here, as their theory is necessary for the application described.

### Questions and Suggestions for Clarification

1.  **Definition of "Parsimonious" Modeling (p1):** The term "parsimonious modeling" is central to the paper's motivation but is not a standard expression in the context of probabilistic models. It would be helpful to unpack this term in a sentence or two to ensure the reader fully grasps the concept as intended by the authors.

2.  **Characterization of Gopalan et al. (p1):** The introduction contains a sentence that seems to imply that down-sampling the zero-valued entries in Gopalan et al. compromises accuracy. My reading of their work is that the subsampling scheme is carefully constructed to provide an unbiased estimate of the gradient, and thus does not compromise accuracy in expectation. This characterization may be misleading and should be clarified.

3.  **Generation of Power Law Networks (p6):** The experimental section mentions the generation of power-law networks in the latent space, but the paper is vague on the details of this process. Please provide a more explicit description of how these networks were generated.

### Minor Comments

*   **p2:** The term "mixture-membership" should be corrected to "mixed-membership".
*   **p8:** The second-to-last sentence of the conclusion provides a strong summary of the paper's contributions. I suggest removing the final sentence, as ending on the penultimate one would make for a more powerful conclusion.
*   **Bibliography:** Please update the citation for "Stochastic Variational Inference" to the JMLR version of the paper.
