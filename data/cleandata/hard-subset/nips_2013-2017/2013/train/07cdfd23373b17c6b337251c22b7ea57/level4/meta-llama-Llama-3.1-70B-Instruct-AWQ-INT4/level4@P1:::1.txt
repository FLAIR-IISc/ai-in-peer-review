This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors develop a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. They also introduce a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and additional approximation tricks to make it further scalable.

The main idea of the paper is to represent networks succinctly as a bag of triangular motifs, which requires much less computational cost compared to edge-based models. The authors argue that this representation, combined with a parsimonious statistical model and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm, enables the analysis of real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' arguments. The experimental results on synthetic and real networks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in terms of accuracy and scalability. However, there are some concerns regarding the experimental setup and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm and model.

One concern is that the experimental setup may not be fair to competitors. For example, the authors do not provide a clear description of how the number of roles (K) was chosen in the real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB. The method of model selection could have biased the experimental results. Additionally, the authors do not provide experiments that compare the performance of PTM and MMTM as a function of K, which would be interesting to see.

Another concern is that the authors are a bit reticent on the impact of the simplifying assumptions they make. While it is clear that constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it would be nice to hear about when such a model would fail. For instance, if there are indeed O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly. The authors should have experimented with various B's, some of which favor PTM and others that are against the model, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of PTM.

The authors introduce several tricks to make PTM scale, including the choice of the threshold δ, the O(K) approximation to local update, and the use of a stochastic variational inference algorithm. However, the paper does a poor job in describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. For example, what is the impact of choosing the threshold δ? How much can we lower δ to improve scalability while not sacrificing much of accuracy? What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to local update, and how does it affect the quality of the approximation when K gets larger?

Overall, while the paper proposes an interesting and original direction to pursue in achieving scalability via constraining parameters, the experimental setup and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm and model are not satisfactory. The authors should address these concerns to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of PTM.

Additional Comments:

* The title is too broad, as there are other scalable approaches to other latent space models. The authors propose a scalable algorithm to a specific latent space model, PTM.
* The authors argue that PTM is a probabilistic method, but it is not clear if this is the case. The majority of the posterior probability mass is placed on modeling non-networks, which may be problematic. The authors should provide more discussion on this point.
