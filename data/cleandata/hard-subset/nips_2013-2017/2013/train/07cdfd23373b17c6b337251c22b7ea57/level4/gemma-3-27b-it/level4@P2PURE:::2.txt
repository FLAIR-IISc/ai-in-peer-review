This paper introduces a technique for learning latent structure within social network data. The approach fits a model to triangle count statistics—obtained via a preprocessing step analogous to a bag-of-words representation in text analysis—and is motivated by the aMMSB model. Rather than parameterizing all interactions between “communities,” the authors leverage triangle configurations to identify and parameterize a set of K roles, resulting in a reduced parameter space and improved scalability. They employ recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving both global and local update rules for the proposed model.

The primary modeling contribution lies in a relatively straightforward combination of aMMSB with the MMTM, although the presentation requires some tedious detail and could benefit from clarification. While the learning algorithm largely applies standard stochastic variational inference, the authors present an intuitive and compelling approximation for the local update rule.

Considering the prevalence of directed networks, a discussion of potential extensions to accommodate such structures would be valuable. 

Several points require further attention: 
- The claim that "our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs" is questionable. First, ERGMs can incorporate a broader range of statistics than solely triangles, and second, the notion of a “latent space counterpart” feels overly broad given that many latent space network models could be described as such.
- The interpretability of the inferred latent space is unclear. If interpretable, what insights do these latent representations provide regarding similarities and differences between the graphs being analyzed?
- The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained.
- The justification for approximating by subsampling triangles for each node (using delta) is not adequately provided. 
- If the goal is to identify a set of active roles, incorporating a more binary representation of “role” within the model might be beneficial. 
- The robustness of the latent space recovery results to the chosen threshold of 0.125 (which appears somewhat arbitrary) should be investigated.
- Various latent space models can generate power-law networks. Clarification is needed regarding which specific model was used for the experiments.
- The reported K^3 complexity of PTM CGS is puzzling, given the authors’ assertion of O(K) parameters.
- The performance of this method with sparse networks—networks containing fewer edges—is a concern. The experiments only withhold 10% of edges, likely to avoid biased triangle distributions resulting from more aggressive edge removal during preprocessing.
- The observation that the method outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated using MMSB (Figure 1, left four panels) is counterintuitive.

In summary, this work leverages stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel (with a restricted parameter set) based on triangle counts in a graph. The resulting modeling choices and approximations enable a more scalable O(NK) algorithm.
