1. PTM reduces parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) but the practical benefit of handling larger K is unclear.  
2. Experiments should show PTM and MMTM performance as a function of K to justify scaling to larger K.  
3. The paper lacks detail on how K was selected for PTM and MMSB, risking biased comparisons.  
4. The manuscript does not thoroughly discuss model limitations or when PTM’s parameter constraints cause performance degradation.  
5. Synthetic experiments do not explore a broad range of B matrices, especially those unfavorable to PTM (many distinct B values).  
6. The multiple approximations introduced are under-analyzed regarding their impact on accuracy and scalability.  
7. Sensitivity to the threshold δ is not evaluated—its effect on accuracy and runtime is unknown.  
8. The claim that a-MMSB’s downsampling harms accuracy needs clearer distinction from PTM, since both discard single-edge triplets.  
9. The effect of the O(K) approximation in local updates is not quantified; how different sample sizes affect approximation quality as K grows is untested.  
10. Overall experimental evaluation is insufficiently robust to support the paper’s claims.  
11. The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized.  
12. The approach is somewhat incremental but the idea of achieving scalability via parameter constraints is original and promising.  
13. The impact could be significant if the simplifying assumptions hold, given scalability challenges in large-network latent models.  
14. The title may be overly broad relative to the paper’s specific focus on PTM.  
15. There are conceptual concerns about whether PTM is a proper probabilistic model—the bag-of-triples analogy to LDA is unconvincing and the likelihood interpretation is unclear.
