This paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces the parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) from O(K³) to O(K), enabling faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to further improve scalability. Results on synthetic data suggest that reducing the number of variables can enhance statistical power, and experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with existing approaches in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of achieving scalability with respect to K (the number of possible roles) is unclear. To adequately evaluate this method, we need to understand “how does it help if we can learn MMTM with large K?” Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may not be as problematic as it is in single-membership models. For real network experiments, it would be more insightful to observe the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K: if PTM with a large K consistently outperforms MMTM with a small K, this would clearly demonstrate the value of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the rationale for choosing specific values of K in the real data experiments for both PTM and the competitor MMSB is not well-explained, and the model selection process could have introduced bias.

The authors are also somewhat reserved regarding the implications of the simplifying assumptions they make. While constraining the parameter space undoubtedly achieves scalability, it would be valuable to understand under what conditions the model might perform poorly. If the true parameter matrix B (following the authors’ notation) in MMSB and MMTM contains a substantial number of distinct values – on the order of O(K³) – PTM is likely to struggle. In this context, the lack of detailed description regarding the choice of the true B in the synthetic data experiments is concerning; experimenting with various B matrices, including those favorable and unfavorable to PTM, would be beneficial. It would be more informative to understand both the strengths and weaknesses of PTM, rather than solely focusing on scenarios where it succeeds.

The paper introduces several techniques to improve PTM’s scalability, which I find interesting and potentially effective. However, the description and analysis of the impact of these approximations are inadequate. Specifically: 1) What is the effect of the threshold δ? Do larger or smaller values of δ yield better results on real-world and synthetic datasets? How much can δ be reduced to enhance scalability without significantly compromising accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB is ineffective because downsampling compromises accuracy; why is PTM immune to this issue? Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge. Why can’t we similarly ignore non-edges in MMSB? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation on the local update step (page 5)? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does the quality of the approximation degrade as K increases? 

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup provides a fair comparison to competitors, and the discussion of the algorithm/model’s potential limitations is insufficient.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:** In terms of modeling, the contribution is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability through parameter constraints is an interesting and original direction, particularly given the current trend towards generalizing existing models.

**Significance:** If the simplifying assumptions made in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to large-scale networks, as many existing models struggle with scalability.

**Additional Comments:** I believe the title is overly broad. Many approaches exist to improve the scalability of latent space models; this paper presents a scalable algorithm *for* a specific latent space model—PTM.

I also question whether PTM can truly be considered a probabilistic method. The authors argue in the last paragraph of Section 3 that it is analogous to the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not persuaded. When LDA generates a bag-of-words, it corresponds to an underlying document with a defined likelihood, however unrealistic the simplification. Conversely, with high probability, there is no corresponding network for a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM or PTM. Consequently, MMTM/PTM are not strictly generative models for networks, and the majority of the posterior probability mass is allocated to modeling non-networks. Abandoning the fundamental principles of likelihood in “probabilistic” methods is concerning (what does probability even *mean* in PTM?). I am eager to hear the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks that facilitate scalable inference on MMTM, but the potential drawbacks of these decisions are not thoroughly investigated.
