This paper presents a scalable method for probabilistic latent space inference in large-scale networks. The approach is built on three key ideas: representing the network as a "bag of triangles," developing a parsimonious statistical model (the Parsimonious Triangular Model, or PTM) that significantly reduces the number of parameters by sharing them across role configurations, and designing a fast stochastic variational inference algorithm. The PTM is a clever subclass of the mixed-membership triangular model that reduces the parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K), a crucial factor for scalability. The authors derive a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for this model that has a time complexity of O(NK). The combination of these elements allows the authors to analyze networks with over a million nodes and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine. The experimental results show that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than existing approaches while achieving competitive or improved accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Evaluation**

Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to scalable network analysis. The ideas are novel, the technical work is sound, and the results are impressive. The paper is well-written and clearly organized. Its primary significance lies in providing a practical and effective tool for exploring latent structures in truly large networks, a task that has been out of reach for many previous probabilistic models. I recommend acceptance.

My main suggestions for improvement are focused on strengthening the experimental comparison and clarifying a few key points in the text.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper successfully combines three principles for scalability (succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference) into a cohesive and powerful framework. The development of the PTM, which reduces parameter complexity from cubic to linear in the number of roles (K), is a significant and clever contribution that enables scaling to a large number of latent roles.
*   **Quality:** The technical development of the stochastic variational inference algorithm is sound. The use of a structured mean-field approximation and an O(K) approximation for the local updates are well-motivated and effective. The empirical evaluation is extensive, demonstrating impressive speedups on both synthetic and large real-world networks.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally very well-written and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of motivating their approach and situating it within the relevant literature.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
1.  **Experimental Comparison:** The experiments compare the proposed method against the batch variational inference algorithm for MMSB. However, the introduction also cites Gopalan et al.'s a-MMSB as a more scalable version of MMSB. A comparison with a-MMSB would be much more compelling and relevant, as it represents a more recent state-of-the-art for scalable inference in this model class. Comparing against the older batch algorithm, which is known to be slow, makes the proposed method's speedup seem larger but is less informative about its performance relative to current scalable methods.
2.  **Clarity on "Parsimonious Modeling":** The term 'parsimonious modeling' is central to the paper's contribution, but it may not be a standard expression in this context. While the idea of reducing the parameter space is clear and compelling, it would be helpful to explicitly define what is meant by this principle in the introduction to ensure it is immediately clear to all readers.
3.  **Justification of the Triangle Model:** The paper correctly notes that the generative process does not guarantee a valid network structure. While this is acceptable from a pragmatic standpoint since the goal is latent space inference, any comparison to models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to justify this is potentially misleading. The generative process for LDA always produces a valid (if not meaningful) document, whereas the PTM's process can produce an object that is not a graph at all. While this does not diminish the practical utility of the model, the paper should be careful with how it frames this limitation.
4.  **Missing Citation for SVI:** The development of the stochastic variational inference algorithm should cite Hoffman et al. (JMLR, 2013) alongside Amari (1998) and Sato (2001). The theory for stochastic variational inference with both local and global parameters, which is the setting in this paper, is developed in Hoffman et al., whereas Sato's algorithm does not directly apply to models with local variables.
5.  **Reproducibility of Synthetic Data:** The experimental setup mentions a model that generates power-law networks from a latent space. However, the paper is vague on the details of this generative process. Please provide a more complete description, either in the main text or the appendix, to improve the reproducibility of the experiments.
6.  **Discussion of a-MMSB in Introduction:** The discussion of Gopalan et al. (a-MMSB) could be slightly rephrased. The current text might be read as implying that down-sampling zero elements is a weakness of the method. My understanding of that work is that down-sampling is key to its scalability and does not necessarily compromise accuracy. It would be better to state simply that, despite this innovation, the method's scalability remains limited for the network sizes considered here, without any potentially negative connotation about the down-sampling technique.

**Minor Comments:**
*   p. 2: Check for any instances of "mixture-membership" and ensure they are "mixed-membership".
*   p. 8: The final paragraph is strong. Consider removing the very last sentence ("These results are orders of magnitude faster..."). The second-to-last sentence ("Our implementation allows network analysis... with competitive or improved accuracy...") provides a more powerful conclusion.
*   Bibliography: Please update the reference for "Stochastic Variational Inference" to the JMLR version by Hoffman et al. (2013).
