The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. They devise a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior with very large networks. The paper is well-written and well-executed. I think it is acceptable.

p1: The authors mention "parsimonious" modeling, which may not be a commonly used term in the context of probabilistic models. It would be helpful to provide a brief explanation of this term, as it plays a crucial role in the paper.

p1: Upon reviewing Gopalan et al., it appears that down-sampling zeros does not compromise accuracy. The sentence in the introduction may be misleading and should be revised to reflect this.

p4: While the triangle model may be pragmatically acceptable, the comparison to LDA is not entirely fair. Running the generative process of LDA yields a document, albeit a potentially nonsensical one, whereas running the triangle model can produce objects that are not networks. Although this does not detract from the model's practical value, the defense via LDA is not entirely appropriate.

p5: The authors should cite Hoffman et al., including Amari and Sato, as their scenario involves "local variables" (in Hoffman's terminology), rendering Sato's algorithm inapplicable. Hoffman's work provides the necessary theoretical foundation.

p6: The paper lacks clarity regarding the generation of power-law networks in the latent space. Further details on this process would be beneficial.

p6: The authors cite Gopalan et al. as a means of accelerating MMSB. A comparison to their algorithm, in addition to or instead of MMSB batch, would be a valuable addition.

Small comments:

p2: "mixture-membership" should be replaced with "mixed-membership".

p8: I suggest removing the last sentence, as the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger conclusion.

In the bibliography, the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference" should be cited.

The paper is well-written, and the authors have made a significant contribution to scalable approximate inference algorithms for network models. With some revisions to address the mentioned points, the paper can be further improved.
