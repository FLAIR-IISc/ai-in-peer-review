This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data, which is a welcome contribution to the field of network analysis. The model is fit to triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance is motivated by the a-MMSB model, where instead of parameterizing all between-"community" interactions, they consider the configurations of the triangles to identify a set of K roles to parameterize. This results in fewer parameters in the model and, in turn, a more scalable implementation.

The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving a global update and local updates for the proposed model. While the novel modeling contribution is a straightforward combination of the a-MMSB with the MMTM, the presentation of this material could be improved for clarity and concision. The bookkeeping required to derive the model is indeed tedious, but the authors could provide more intuition and explanation for the key steps.

The learning algorithm is largely a direct application of stochastic variational inference, but the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule. This approximation, which replaces the full parameterization with a mixture-of-deltas variational distribution, is a key contribution of the paper. It allows for a faster local update and is shown to work well in practice.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on a range of synthetic and real-world networks, including large-scale social networks. The results show that their method achieves competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction, while being orders of magnitude faster than previous work.

However, there are some ongoing concerns and areas for improvement. Firstly, the authors could provide a more detailed discussion of how their methods might extend to directed networks, which are common in many real-world applications. Additionally, it is not clear how the inferred latent space is interpretable, and what insights can be gained from the similarities and differences among the graphs.

Furthermore, the authors could provide more explanation and justification for some of the modeling choices, such as the use of subsampling triangles for each node (using delta) and the choice of threshold for determining non-zero roles. It is also unclear why the PTM Gibbs sampler has K^3 complexity, despite the authors arguing that there are O(K) parameters.

Finally, the authors could provide more discussion on the robustness of their results to the threshold used for determining non-zero roles and the impact of observing fewer edges on the performance of their method. Overall, this is a solid contribution to the field of network analysis, and with some further clarification and justification, it has the potential to make a significant impact.
