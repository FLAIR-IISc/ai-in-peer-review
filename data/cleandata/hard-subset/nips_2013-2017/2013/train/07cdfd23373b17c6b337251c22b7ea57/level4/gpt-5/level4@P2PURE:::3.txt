The authors present a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the “triangle model” of networks. They introduce a strong subclass that shares parameters in a clever way and use stochastic variational inference to estimate the posterior on very large networks. The paper is well written and carefully executed. I think it is acceptable.

p1: What do you mean by “parsimonious” modeling? This is not, to my knowledge, a standard term in the context of probabilistic models (though I may be mistaken). Please unpack the term in a sentence, since it is central to the paper.

p1: Based on my reading of Gopalan et al., down-sampling zeros does not compromise accuracy. Your sentence in the introduction is therefore misleading.

p4: I’m sympathetic to the triangle model from a pragmatic standpoint, but invoking LDA as a defense is not appropriate. Running LDA’s generative process produces an object that is a document (even if a silly one), whereas running your model can yield objects that are not networks. This doesn’t bother me—your model is useful and practical—but the LDA-based justification is not a fair comparison.

p5: You should cite Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato. In your setting, which involves “local variables” (in Hoffman’s terminology), Sato’s algorithm does not apply; the necessary theory is in Hoffman.

p6: How did you generate power-law networks in the latent space? The paper is vague on this point.

p6: Since you cite Gopalan et al. as a way to speed up MMSB, you should compare to their algorithm, in addition to (or instead of) MMSB batch.

Small comments:
- p2: mixture-membership -> mixed-membership
- p8: Consider removing the last sentence; the second-to-last is a stronger way to end the paper.
- Bibliography: Please cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference.”
