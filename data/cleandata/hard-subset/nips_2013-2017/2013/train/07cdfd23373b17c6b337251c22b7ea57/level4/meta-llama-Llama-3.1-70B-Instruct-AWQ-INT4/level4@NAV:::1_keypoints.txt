- Paper proposes PTM, a constrained version of MMTM that reduces parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) for faster inference.  
- Authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm and additional approximations to improve scalability.  
- Experiments claim stronger statistical power and competitive accuracy on synthetic and real datasets.  
- Practical scalability advantages are unclear without comparisons to learning full MMTM with large K.  
- Need experiments comparing PTM and MMTM as a function of K to show whether PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K.  
- Choice of K for real-data experiments is not clearly described and may bias results.  
- Impact of simplifying constraints is insufficiently analyzed; PTM may fail if true B has O(K^3) distinct values.  
- Synthetic experiments lack clarity about the true B used and should test a variety of B to show strengths and weaknesses.  
- Several approximation techniques are interesting but poorly analyzed; effects of threshold δ, downsampling, and ignoring non-edges are unexplored.  
- Unclear impact and approximation quality of the O(K) local-update approximation and sampling different numbers of triples.  
- Experimental setup may be unfair to competitors and the paper inadequately discusses potential model/algorithm weaknesses.  
- Paper is well-written and clearly organized.  
- Modeling approach is somewhat incremental but the scalability-via-constraints direction is interesting and original.  
- If assumptions hold in practice, the work could significantly impact scalable latent-space modeling for large networks.  
- Title may be too broad; PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific model rather than a general scalable latent-space approach.  
- Doubts about whether PTM is truly probabilistic; the bag-of-triples analogy is unconvincing and raises concerns about likelihood validity and side effects of modeling/inference choices.
