This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation tricks to make it further scalable. Experimental results on synthetic datasets demonstrate that the reduction of the number of variables may lead to stronger statistical power, and on real-world datasets, the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

Quality:
While PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, the practical advantage of achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) is questionable. To empirically evaluate the value of this method, it is crucial to investigate the benefits of learning MMTM with large K. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may not be as problematic as it is in single-membership models. A more compelling experiment would be to compare the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K, demonstrating whether PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted.

Furthermore, the authors do not provide a clear description of how K was chosen in the real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB. The method of model selection could have biased the experimental results. The authors are also reticent about the impact of the simplifying assumptions they make. While constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it would be beneficial to discuss when the model is likely to fail. If there are indeed O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly. The choice of the true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described, and the authors should have experimented with various B's, some of which favor PTM and others that do not.

The paper introduces several tricks to make PTM scalable, but it does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. Specifically, the impact of choosing the threshold δ is unclear. Does choosing a larger or smaller δ help in real-world and synthetic data experiments? How much can δ be lowered to improve scalability without sacrificing accuracy? Additionally, the authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy, but it is unclear why PTM would be exempt from this issue. Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge, so it is unclear why non-edges cannot be ignored in MMSB while they can in PTM. Finally, the impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates is unclear, and it would be beneficial to investigate how the quality of the approximation suffers when K gets larger.

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm and model is poor.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow.

Originality:
While the modeling is somewhat incremental, achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction to pursue, as recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the practice of latent-space models on networks, as many of these models do not scale to large data.

Additional Comments:
The title of the paper is too broad, as there are other scalable approaches to latent space models. The authors propose a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model, PTM.

I am also concerned that PTM may not be a truly probabilistic method. The authors argue that their approach is similar in spirit to the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not convinced. In LDA, there is always a document corresponding to a bag-of-words, which allows for a proper likelihood function. In contrast, it is unlikely that a network exists that corresponds to a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM or PTM. Therefore, MMTM and PTM may not be generative models for networks, and the majority of the posterior probability mass may be placed on modeling non-networks. This could be problematic, as it may lead to a misunderstanding of what probability means in PTM. I would like to hear the authors' and other reviewers' thoughts on this matter.
