This paper proposes the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to further improve scalability. On synthetic data, they show that reducing the number of variables can increase statistical power, and on real-world datasets, they report that PTM is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

Quality:
- PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical advantage of achieving scalability specifically in K (the number of roles) is not clearly established. To evaluate the benefit of such scalability, it is crucial to answer: what is gained by learning MMTM with large K? Because MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may be less problematic than in single-membership models. For the real-network experiments, it would be especially informative to report performance for both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K, that would directly demonstrate the value of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments are missing.
- I also could not find a clear description of how K was chosen in the real-data experiments for either PTM or the MMSB competitor. The model-selection procedure could bias the results; this needs to be detailed and, ideally, controlled for.
- The paper is reticent about the impact of its simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space improves scalability, it is important to articulate when the model is likely to fail. If there truly are O(K^3) distinct values in the B matrix (in the authors’ notation) of MMSB/MMTM, PTM should perform poorly, correct? In this light, the synthetic experiments are under-specified: the choice of the true B is not clearly described. The authors should have tested a variety of B matrices, some favorable to PTM and others adversarial, to help readers understand when PTM succeeds and when it fails, rather than only when it succeeds.
- The paper introduces several scalable inference tricks that are interesting and appear effective, but their impact is not adequately described or analyzed:
  1) Threshold δ: What is the effect of the threshold δ on performance and runtime? On both real and synthetic data, does a larger or smaller δ help? How low can δ be set to improve scalability without materially harming accuracy?
  2) Downsampling vs. ignoring edges: The authors claim that a-MMSB degrades because downsampling compromises accuracy. Why would PTM avoid this problem? MMTM and PTM effectively ignore triplets with only one edge. Why is it acceptable to ignore non-edges in PTM/MMTM but not in MMSB?
  3) O(K) local-update approximation (page 5): What is the empirical effect of this approximation? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does the approximation quality deteriorate as K grows?
- In sum, I am not convinced the experimental setup is fair to competitors, and the discussion of the algorithm’s/model’s potential weaknesses is insufficient.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and well organized.

Originality:
- The modeling contribution is somewhat incremental, but constraining parameters to achieve scalability is a worthwhile and original direction, especially given that recent work in this area has often focused on generalizing existing models rather than simplifying them for scale.

Significance:
- If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, the work could have meaningful impact on the use of latent-space models for networks, where scalability remains a major challenge.

Additional comments:
- The title feels too broad. There are other scalable approaches to latent-space models; this paper proposes a scalable algorithm for a specific model (PTM).
- I am not convinced PTM is truly probabilistic in the sense claimed. The last paragraph of Section 3 argues an analogy to the bag-of-words assumption, but when LDA generates a bag of words, there always exists a corresponding document, so the model has a legitimate likelihood, however simplified. In contrast, with high probability there is no actual network corresponding to a bag of triples generated by MMTM/PTM. Thus MMTM/PTM is not a generative model for networks, and most posterior mass may be placed on “non-networks.” It is risky to depart from basic likelihood principles in a method presented as probabilistic (what exactly does probability mean in PTM?). I am curious how the authors and other reviewers view this point. The proposed modeling assumptions and inference tricks could make MMTM scalable, but the side effects of these decisions are insufficiently investigated.
