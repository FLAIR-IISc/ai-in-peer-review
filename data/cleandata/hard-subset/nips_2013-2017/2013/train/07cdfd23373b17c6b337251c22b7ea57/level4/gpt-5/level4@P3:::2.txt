Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a scalable probabilistic latent-space model for large networks built on a bag-of-triangles representation. Instead of modeling edges, the method represents a network as a multiset of open and closed triangular motifs and learns mixed-membership role vectors for nodes. The key modeling idea is to reduce the O(K^3) triangle-configuration parameter tensor used by MMTM to O(K) parameters by grouping triangle configurations according to whether all three roles are the same, two are the same (with a “majority” role), or all are distinct. This shares parameters across many configurations, akin to the a-MMSB idea of tying inter-role interactions. Inference is performed via stochastic variational inference (SVI) with natural gradients, using a structured mean-field posterior over per-triangle role triplets and a practical O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation for local updates. Empirically, the method scales to 1M nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine and attains competitive or improved performance on latent role recovery and link prediction relative to MMSB, a-MMSB, and MMTM.

Relative to previous work: MMSB and a-MMSB model edges with O(K^2) or tied parameters; MMTM models triangles but incurs O(K^3) complexity; ERGM uses triangle and other higher-order statistics but is not a latent-space model; SVI provides the algorithmic framework. This paper combines the triangular representation with a-MMSB-style parameter tying and SVI, plus a new local-update approximation, yielding practical O(NK) inference.

Strengths:
- Scalability: The O(K) parameterization and SVI lead to O(NK) global updates and O(K) local updates (with the proposed approximation), enabling analysis of million-node networks with K ≈ 100.
- Sound algorithmic design: The structured mean-field choice for per-triangle role triplets is appropriate; the use of unbiased mini-batch gradients and natural gradients follows established theory.
- Practical local-update approximation: The mixture-of-deltas approach reduces O(K^3) local cost to O(K) and appears empirically effective; the re-sampling of off-diagonal role triples mitigates bias.
- Empirical performance: On both synthetic and real networks, the method is orders of magnitude faster than strong baselines, with competitive or better accuracy on latent role recovery and link prediction. Convergence in 2–5 passes is compelling.
- Memory efficiency: Discarding local variational parameters between iterations is a sensible engineering choice for large graphs.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
Quality and clarity
- The claim that PTM is a “latent space counterpart to ERGMs” is confusing. ERGMs include diverse sufficient statistics beyond triangles and are generative over edges. PTM is a latent-variable model over observed triangle motifs and is not generative for networks. Please refine this statement to avoid overreach or clarify the precise sense in which PTM relates to ERGM ideas (e.g., using higher-order motifs as sufficient statistics).
- Notation: The distinction between Δ^1 and Δ^2 and how these simplices instantiate B0, Bxx, and Bxxx is not clearly explained. A concise description early in Section 3 (e.g., Δ^1 denotes a 2-category simplex, Δ^2 a 3-category simplex) and a small example mapping from role triplets to motif probabilities would improve readability.
- Complexity vs. parameters: The text asserts O(K) parameters but reports O(K^3) per-iteration cost for PTM collapsed Gibbs sampling. Please clarify: the O(K^3) comes from enumerating all role triplets in blocked updates despite parameter tying, whereas variational inference avoids this cost. Stating this explicitly will preempt confusion.

Modeling choices and empirical validation
- Triangle subsampling (δ): Please justify more fully why δ-subsampling preserves the relevant motif distribution for inference, and discuss any bias introduced for high-degree nodes. A brief derivation or a pointer to precise results in [8], plus a sensitivity analysis over δ, would strengthen the case.
- Directed networks: Many real networks are directed. A discussion of how PTM extends to directed motifs (increasing motif types and potential parameter tying schemes) would broaden impact.
- Interpretability: What does a role mean under PTM, and are roles consistent across datasets? Example roles and qualitative analysis (e.g., nodes with high membership in a role and their induced subgraphs) would be helpful.
- Binary vs. continuous roles: Since evaluation thresholds θ̂ at 0.125 to recover “non-zero roles,” consider reporting sensitivity to this threshold, or using a calibration method (e.g., selecting thresholds by maximizing F1 on validation) to demonstrate robustness.
- Synthetic data details: The “power-law” latent-space generator is not specified; please provide a clear description or cite the exact model used. Also, clarify initialization: seeding 2% of nodes is helpful for comparability, but please discuss how the method performs with more realistic, unsupervised initializations (you propose one; consider including results).
- Held-out sparsity: Link prediction holds out 10% of edges. Since triangle extraction depends on observed edges, larger holdout fractions may distort motif counts. Please discuss how performance changes as the holdout rate increases, or justify the 10% choice.
- Beating MMSB on MMSB-generated graphs: The observation that PTM outperforms MMSB on MMSB-simulated data is counterintuitive. Possible explanations (e.g., regularization from parameter tying, stronger optimization behavior, or mismatch between MMSB batch variational inference and the data regime) should be discussed, ideally with an ablation comparing full vs. tied parameters.

Originality:
The parameter-sharing scheme that reduces K^3 to O(K) adapts ideas from a-MMSB to the triangular setting, and the triangular representation comes from MMTM. The new contribution lies in the synthesis and in the O(K) mixture-of-deltas local update within a structured SVI framework. This is an incremental but meaningful advance that unlocks substantial scalability.

Significance:
Scaling latent-space inference to million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine is impactful. The approach should be useful for community detection, exploration, and link prediction at scale. The demonstrated speedups over a-MMSB and MMTM are compelling.

Clarity:
Overall the paper is readable, but certain sections (role-configuration equivalence classes, Δ notation, and the relationship between parameter counts and per-iteration complexity) would benefit from tighter exposition and small illustrative examples. Including the exact form of the natural gradients and key update equations in the main text (with full derivations in the appendix) would aid reproducibility.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong scalability with principled SVI and careful engineering.
- Competitive or improved accuracy on both synthetic and real data.
- Simple, elegant parameter tying that reduces complexity from K^3 to O(K).
- Practical local-update approximation with convincing empirical behavior.

Cons against acceptance:
- Some claims (ERGM counterpart) are imprecise; parts of the exposition are hard to follow.
- Limited discussion of directed networks and interpretability.
- Insufficient sensitivity analyses (δ-subsampling, role-threshold choice, holdout rate).
- Counterintuitive superiority over MMSB on MMSB data not fully explained.

Overall assessment:
A solid, well-motivated, and practically important contribution. With improved clarity around modeling claims and additional robustness analyses, this work will be a valuable addition to the literature on scalable latent-space network models. I lean toward acceptance.
