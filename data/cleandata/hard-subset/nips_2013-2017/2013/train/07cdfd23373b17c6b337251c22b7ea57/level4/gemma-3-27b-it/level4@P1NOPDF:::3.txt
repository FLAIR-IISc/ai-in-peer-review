The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the “triangle model” of networks. They introduce a powerful subclass of the model that cleverly shares parameters, and utilize stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior in very large networks. The paper is well-written and well-executed. I find it acceptable.

p1: What is meant by “parsimonious” modeling? This isn't a standard term in the context of probabilistic models, though I may be mistaken. I suggest briefly defining the term, as it is central to the paper.

p1: My reading of Gopalan et al. suggests that down-sampling zeros does not compromise accuracy. The statement in your introduction regarding this is therefore misleading.

p4: While I understand the pragmatic appeal of the triangle model, I don’t think the comparison to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is valid. Running the generative process of LDA will always produce a valid document, even if it’s nonsensical, whereas your model can generate outputs that are not valid networks. This doesn’t diminish the model’s value—it remains a good and practical approach—but the defense using LDA is inappropriate.

p5: You should cite Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato. In your scenario, which involves “local variables” (as described by Hoffman), Sato’s algorithm does not apply. The relevant theoretical foundation can be found in Hoffman’s work.

p6: How were power-law networks generated in the latent space? The paper is vague on this point and would benefit from clarification.

p6: You previously cite Gopalan et al. as a method for speeding up MMSB. I suggest comparing your algorithm to theirs, in addition to, or instead of, MMSB batch.

Small comments:

p2: Change “mixture-membership” to “mixed-membership.”

p8: I recommend removing the final sentence. The second-to-last sentence provides a stronger conclusion.

In the bibliography, please cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference.”
