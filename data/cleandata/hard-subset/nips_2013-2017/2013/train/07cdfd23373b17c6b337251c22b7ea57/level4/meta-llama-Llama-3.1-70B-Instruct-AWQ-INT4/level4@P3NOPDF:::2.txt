This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data by leveraging triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance is motivated by the Affiliation Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB), where instead of parameterizing all between-community interactions, the authors consider the configurations of the triangles to identify a set of K roles to parameterize. This results in fewer parameters in the model and, in turn, a more scalable implementation. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving a global update and local updates for the proposed model.

The novel modeling contribution is a straightforward combination of the aMMSB with the Mixed Membership Triangle Model (MMTM), although it requires somewhat tedious bookkeeping. The presentation of this material could be improved for better clarity. However, the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule, which is a notable contribution.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to tackle large-scale networks efficiently. The authors' modeling choices and approximations allow for a more scalable O(NK) algorithm, making it a promising approach for analyzing large social networks.

However, there are some concerns and areas for improvement. Firstly, the authors' claim that their Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs) is unclear. ERGMs can include much more varied statistics than just triangles, and it is not evident how the PTM is a "latent space counterpart" unless all latent space network models are considered as such.

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the inferred latent space is interpretable. If so, what insights do we gain about the similarities and differences among these graphs? Additionally, the difference between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained, and it is not evident why subsampling the triangles for each node (using delta) provides an adequate approximation.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion on how the methods might extend to directed networks, given their prevalence. Moreover, if the goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles," perhaps there should be more of a binary notion of "role" in the model.

Some additional questions and concerns arise from the experimental setup. The choice of the threshold value of 0.125 seems arbitrary, and it is unclear how robust the latent space recovery results are to this threshold. Additionally, it is not specified which latent space model was used for the experiments, as various models can produce power-law networks.

The authors claim that the PTM has a complexity of O(K^3) for the collapsed Gibbs sampler (CGS), but this seems inconsistent with their earlier argument that there are O(K) parameters rather than O(K^3). Furthermore, it is concerning how the method will perform when fewer edges are observed, as the experiments only hold out 10% of the edges. This might be due to the biased distribution of adjacent triangles if too many edges are removed prior to the preprocessing step.

Finally, it is counterintuitive that the method beats the aMMSB on a synthetic network generated via aMMSB (Figure 1, left 4 panels). This result warrants further explanation or investigation.

In conclusion, this work uses stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel (with a restricted set of parameters) on the triangle counts in a graph. While the authors' modeling choices and approximations allow for a more scalable algorithm, there are several concerns and areas for improvement that need to be addressed to strengthen the paper.
