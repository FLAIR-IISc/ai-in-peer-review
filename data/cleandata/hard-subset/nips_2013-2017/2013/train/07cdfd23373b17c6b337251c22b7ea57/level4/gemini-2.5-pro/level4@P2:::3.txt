This paper presents a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks. The core contribution is a three-part strategy: 1) representing networks succinctly as a "bag of triangular motifs," 2) proposing a "Parsimonious Triangular Model" (PTM) that reduces the parameter space complexity from O(K³) in the prior Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) by cleverly sharing parameters, and 3) developing a fast stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with an O(K) approximate local update for inference. The authors demonstrate that their method can analyze million-node networks with hundreds of latent roles in a matter of hours on a single machine, claiming orders-of-magnitude speedups over previous methods like the batch MMSB and MMTM Gibbs sampler, while achieving competitive or improved accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

### Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The primary strength of this paper is its impressive scalability. The combination of the triangle representation, a parsimonious model, and an efficient SVI algorithm addresses a critical bottleneck in network analysis. The ability to infer hundreds of roles on a million-node network in a few hours on a single machine is a significant practical achievement that advances the state of the art.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper makes several novel technical contributions. The PTM model's parameter-sharing scheme is an elegant way to reduce model complexity without sacrificing expressive power, analogous to similar successful ideas in other domains. Furthermore, the development of an O(K) approximation to the local variational update is a clever and necessary step to make the SVI algorithm truly scalable in the number of roles, K. The work is technically sound and the proposed algorithm is well-motivated.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors effectively motivate their design choices by outlining three key principles for large-scale inference and demonstrating how their approach embodies all three, in contrast to prior work.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Insufficient Experimental Comparison:** The paper's most significant weakness is its choice of experimental baselines. The authors cite Gopalan et al. (a-MMSB) as a state-of-the-art scalable method that improves upon the standard batch MMSB algorithm. However, they only compare their method against the much slower batch MMSB and the MMTM Gibbs sampler. To convincingly support the claim of being "orders of magnitude faster than recent work on scalable latent space network modeling," a direct and thorough comparison with a-MMSB is essential. Without it, the claimed performance improvements are not fully substantiated against the most relevant competitor.

2.  **Lack of Clarity on Synthetic Data Generation:** The experimental section is vague on how the synthetic power-law networks were generated from the latent space model. This lack of detail hinders the reproducibility of the experiments. The paper would be strengthened by a more complete description of this data generation process, either in the main text or the appendix.

3.  **Mischaracterization of Prior Work:** The introduction contains a potentially misleading statement about Gopalan et al.'s a-MMSB. The paper states that a-MMSB's down-sampling of zero-valued entries in the adjacency matrix mitigates the O(N²) dependency but "is still not fast enough." While the latter may be true, the phrasing might imply that down-sampling compromises accuracy. My understanding is that the stochastic optimization based on sub-sampling edges (and non-edges) is an unbiased procedure that does not inherently degrade the quality of the final solution. The authors should clarify this point to more accurately represent the prior work.

4.  **Weak Justification for Non-Generative Model:** In Section 3, the authors correctly note that their model is not a generative model for networks per se, as its process can produce a set of triangles that is inconsistent with any single graph. The paper defends this by analogy to topic models like LDA, suggesting that a generated document from LDA might also be "silly." This analogy is not entirely fitting; a document generated by LDA is still a valid document (a collection of words), whereas the output of the PTM's generative process may not be a valid graph. While the pragmatic choice to focus on inference is perfectly acceptable and effective, the justification could be more direct rather than relying on a weak analogy.

5.  **Missing Citation:** The description of stochastic natural gradient ascent should cite Hoffman et al. ("Stochastic Variational Inference," JMLR 2013). The framework presented by Hoffman et al. is the direct theoretical foundation for SVI in models with both global and local variational parameters, which is precisely the setting in this paper.

### Minor Comments

*   **Terminology:** The term "parsimonious modeling" is central to the paper but is not a standard expression in this context. A brief explanatory phrase upon its first use in the introduction (e.g., "...parsimonious statistical modeling, which reduces model complexity by sharing parameters...") would be helpful.
*   **Typo:** p2: "mixture-membership" should be corrected to "mixed-membership".
*   **Bibliography:** The bibliography should be updated to cite the JMLR version of Hoffman et al.'s "Stochastic Variational Inference."
*   **Conclusion:** The final sentence of the paper feels slightly redundant. The second-to-last sentence provides a stronger and more impactful conclusion.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper introduces a novel and highly scalable framework for probabilistic latent space analysis of massive networks. The contributions in modeling (PTM) and inference (scalable SVI) are significant and well-executed. The empirical results demonstrate a remarkable improvement in scalability. The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation, which omits a comparison to a key state-of-the-art scalable baseline.

Despite this weakness, the technical contributions and the impressive performance gains are substantial enough to warrant publication. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address the concerns raised, particularly by adding a comparison to a-MMSB and clarifying the experimental details for reproducibility.
