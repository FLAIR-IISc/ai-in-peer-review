This paper introduces the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference procedure for PTM and add approximation techniques to further improve scalability. On synthetic data, they show that reducing the number of variables can yield stronger statistical power, and on real-world datasets the method is competitive with existing approaches in accuracy.

Quality:
PTM is an appealing specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of scaling well in K (the number of roles) is unclear. To judge the value of such scalability, we need to answer: how does learning MMTM with large K help in practice? Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, small K may not be as problematic as in single-membership settings. For real-network experiments, it would be especially informative to evaluate both MMTM and PTM as a function of K; if PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K, that would substantiate PTM’s advantage. Unfortunately, these experiments are missing. I also could not find a clear description of how K was selected in the real-data experiments for both PTM and the MMSB competitor; the model-selection procedure may have biased the results.

The paper also downplays the consequences of its simplifying assumptions. Constraining the parameter space clearly improves scalability, but when does the model fail? If the parameter matrix B (in the authors’ notation) in MMSB/MMTM truly has O(K^3) distinct entries, PTM should perform poorly, correct? In this light, it is unsatisfactory that the true B used in the synthetic experiments is not clearly specified; the authors should test a range of B’s, some favorable to PTM and some unfavorable. It would be more informative to identify when PTM succeeds and when it fails, rather than only when it succeeds.

The paper proposes several tricks to scale PTM; while these seem interesting and effective, their description and analysis are insufficient. 1) What is the effect of the threshold δ? On both real and synthetic datasets, does a larger or smaller δ help? How low can δ be set to improve scalability without materially harming accuracy? 2) The authors argue that a-MMSB fails because downsampling degrades accuracy; why is PTM immune to this issue? MMTM and PTM both ignore triplets with only one edge. Why is it acceptable to ignore non-edges in PTM but not in MMSB? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to the local update (page 5)? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does the approximation quality worsen as K increases?

In sum, I am not convinced the experimental setup is fair to competing methods, and the discussion of potential weaknesses of the algorithm/model is lacking.

Clarity: The paper is very clearly written and well organized.

Originality: From a modeling perspective it is somewhat incremental, but achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction, especially since much recent work has focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance: If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a meaningful impact on the use of latent-space models for networks, as many do not scale to large datasets.

Additional Comments: The title feels too broad. There are other scalable approaches for other latent-space models, and the authors present a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model—PTM. I am also unsure whether PTM is genuinely probabilistic. The authors claim in the last paragraph of Section 3 that it is in the spirit of the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not convinced. In LDA, any bag of words corresponds to some document, so there is a proper likelihood, however simplified. In contrast, with high probability there is no network corresponding to a bag of triples generated by MMTM/PTM. Hence MMTM/PTM is not truly a generative model for networks, and most posterior mass is over non-networks. It may be risky to abandon basic likelihood principles in a “probabilistic” method (what does probability mean in PTM?). I am interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ views. The paper proposes modeling assumptions and inference tricks that make MMTM scalable, but the side effects of these choices are not well examined.
