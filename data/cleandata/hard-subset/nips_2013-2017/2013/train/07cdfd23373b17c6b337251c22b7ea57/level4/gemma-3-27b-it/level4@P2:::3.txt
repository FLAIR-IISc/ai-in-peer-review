This paper introduces a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large networks. The authors propose a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) combined with a stochastic variational inference algorithm, enabling analysis of networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles – a scale inaccessible to many existing methods. The work is well-written and the experimental results demonstrate significant speedups over state-of-the-art techniques, with competitive or improved accuracy. I recommend acceptance.

The core idea is to represent networks as a “bag of triangular motifs,” a succinct representation that captures higher-order structural statistics. The PTM builds upon this representation and achieves scalability through parameter sharing, reducing the model's complexity from O(K<sup>3</sup>) to O(K), where K is the number of latent roles.  The stochastic variational inference algorithm further contributes to scalability, achieving a time complexity of O(NK). 

Several aspects of this work are particularly strong. The parameter sharing strategy within the PTM is clever and effectively addresses the scalability issues of previous models like the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM). The use of stochastic variational inference is well-justified and the reported speedups are impressive, particularly when compared to the a-MMSB algorithm by Gopalan et al. and the MMTM Gibbs sampler by Ho et al. The thorough experimental evaluation, including synthetic and real-world datasets, strengthens the claims made by the authors. 

However, a few points could be clarified. On page 1, the term “parsimonious” modeling is used without sufficient explanation. While understood in a general sense, explicitly defining it within the context of probabilistic models would improve clarity. Additionally, the statement regarding Gopalan et al.’s down-sampling technique on page 1 is somewhat misleading. The authors suggest down-sampling compromises accuracy, whereas Gopalan et al. demonstrate that it does not. 

Regarding the model’s generative process (page 4), the comparison to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is not entirely convincing.  While LDA generates valid, albeit potentially trivial, documents, the PTM can generate outputs that do not correspond to valid networks.  Acknowledging this limitation, even while defending the pragmatic utility of the model, would strengthen the argument.

On page 5, the discussion of stochastic variational inference should cite Hoffman et al. (JMLR version) alongside Amari and Sato. The authors’ scenario involves “local variables” (in Hoffman’s terminology), rendering Sato’s algorithm inapplicable; the relevant theoretical foundation is found in Hoffman et al. Furthermore, the generation of power-law networks in the latent space (page 6) is not described with sufficient detail. Expanding on this aspect would enhance reproducibility. Finally, the comparison should include Gopalan et al.’s algorithm directly, in addition to or instead of the MMSB batch method.

Minor comments: On page 2, “mixture-membership” should be “mixed-membership.” On page 8, the final sentence could be removed; the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger conclusion. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant scalability improvements over existing methods.
*   Clever parameter sharing strategy in the PTM.
*   Thorough experimental evaluation demonstrating competitive accuracy.
*   Addresses a critical challenge in network analysis – scaling to large networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor clarity issues regarding the definition of "parsimonious" modeling.
*   Potentially misleading statement about Gopalan et al.’s down-sampling technique.
*   Weak analogy to LDA.
*   Insufficient detail regarding power-law network generation.
