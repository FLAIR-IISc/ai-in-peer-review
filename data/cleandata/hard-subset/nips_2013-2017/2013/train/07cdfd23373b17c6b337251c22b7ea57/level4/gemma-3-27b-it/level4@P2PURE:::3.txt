The authors present a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. They introduce a powerful and cleverly parameterized subclass of this model, and leverage stochastic variational inference to estimate the posterior distribution in very large networks. The paper is well-written and technically sound; I recommend it for acceptance.

p1: The term “parsimonious” modeling is used without definition. While I may be mistaken, this isn’t a standard expression within the context of probabilistic models. I suggest clarifying this term in a single sentence, as it is central to the paper’s contribution. 
p1: My reading of Gopalan et al. suggests that down-sampling zeros does not negatively impact accuracy. The statement in your introduction regarding this is therefore misleading.
p4: While I understand the pragmatic appeal of the triangle model, the comparison to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) feels inappropriate. Running the generative process of LDA will always produce a valid, albeit potentially nonsensical, “document.” In contrast, your model can generate outputs that are not valid networks. This does not detract from the model's usefulness – it remains a good and practical approach – but the defense based on LDA is not convincing.
p5: Please cite Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato. Specifically, in your scenario involving “local variables” (as defined by Hoffman), Sato’s algorithm is inapplicable. The relevant theoretical foundation for this point is found in Hoffman et al.
p6: The method for generating power-law networks in the latent space is unclear. The paper would benefit from more detail regarding this process.
p6: You cite Gopalan et al. as a method for accelerating MMSB. A comparison to their algorithm, either in addition to or instead of MMSB batch, would strengthen the evaluation.

Small comments:
p2: “mixture-membership” should be “mixed-membership”.
p8: I recommend removing the final sentence. The penultimate sentence provides a stronger conclusion.

Please also cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference” in the bibliography.
