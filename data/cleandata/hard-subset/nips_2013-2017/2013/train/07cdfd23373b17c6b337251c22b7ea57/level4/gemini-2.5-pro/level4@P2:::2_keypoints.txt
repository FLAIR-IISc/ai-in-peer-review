- Proposes Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) combining bag-of-triangles representation, parameter sharing, and efficient inference for scalable latent structure learning.  
- Reduces parameter count from O(K³) to O(K) via parameter-sharing and triangle-based representation.  
- Introduces an SVI algorithm with a "mixture-of-deltas" local-update approximation that cuts per-triangle complexity from O(K³) to O(K).  
- Achieves overall inference time complexity O(NK), enabling analysis of million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine.  
- Demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups over a-MMSB and MMTM on real networks (e.g., Brightkite, Stanford).  
- Shows strong empirical performance on synthetic latent recovery and competitive/superior link prediction on real social networks.  

- Notation clarity issues (e.g., undefined simplices ∆¹ vs ∆²) that may confuse readers.  
- Justification for δ-subsampling of triangles is deferred to prior work; needs a brief self-contained intuition.  
- Claim that PTM is a "latent space counterpart" to ERGMs is overstated and potentially misleading.  

- Missing details on the generative model for power-law network experiments, hindering reproducibility.  
- Latent recovery evaluation uses an arbitrary 0.125 threshold on mixed-membership vectors without sensitivity analysis.  
- Counterintuitive result of PTM outperforming MMSB on MMSB-generated data lacks discussion (possible overfitting explanation needed).  

- Apparent contradiction in complexity: PTM claimed O(K) parameters but CGS complexity reported as O(K³) needs clarification.  
- Robustness to higher edge sparsity (holding out >10% edges) is not evaluated despite reliance on triangle statistics.  
- No qualitative interpretation of inferred latent roles on real networks to demonstrate practical utility.  
- No discussion of extending the triangle-based approach to directed graphs, limiting applicability.  

- Overall contribution: a highly scalable triangle-based mixed-membership model with O(NK) inference and major speedups, but needs clearer exposition, more experimental rigor, and deeper analysis.  

- Arguments for acceptance: massive scalability and speedups, practical O(NK) complexity, novel effective local-update approximation, strong empirical results.  
- Arguments against acceptance: clarity and notation problems, missing experimental details and robustness checks, and unresolved contradictory complexity claims.
