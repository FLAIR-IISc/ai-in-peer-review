

The authors propose a scalable approach to latent space inference for large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) and stochastic variational inference. Their key insight is to represent networks as a bag of triangular motifs (instead of adjacency matrices), develop a parameter-sharing scheme that reduces the model's parameter complexity from O(K³) to O(K), and apply stochastic variational inference to achieve O(NK) time complexity. This enables analysis of networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in hours, significantly outperforming existing methods.

The paper makes a strong technical contribution by addressing a genuine scalability challenge in network analysis. The parameter-sharing scheme in the PTM is elegant and effectively reduces model dimensionality without sacrificing accuracy. The experimental results demonstrate clear scalability advantages, with the proposed method running orders of magnitude faster than competing approaches while maintaining or improving accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to scalable network analysis.

However, I have a few suggestions for improvement:
1. The term "parsimonious" is not commonly used in probabilistic modeling contexts, and the authors should clarify its meaning in the introduction.
2. The claim that "downsampling zero elements in the matrix" (referring to a-MMSB) "mitigates" the adjacency matrix issue is misleading. Gopalan et al. (2013) demonstrate that downsampling zeros does not compromise accuracy, so the paper should more precisely describe the limitations of existing methods.
3. The authors' defense of their model by comparing it to LDA is not appropriate. While LDA generates valid documents (albeit "silly" ones), the PTM can generate triangle configurations that do not correspond to any valid network. This is a pragmatic choice, but the LDA comparison does not strengthen their argument.
4. The paper should cite Hoffman et al. (2013) for the stochastic variational inference framework, as Sato's algorithm does not apply to the local variables scenario described.
5. The paper is vague about how power-law networks were generated in Section 5.1. A brief description would improve clarity.
6. The authors should compare their method directly to Gopalan et al.'s a-MMSB algorithm rather than only to the original MMSB batch method.

Minor corrections:
- "mixture-membership" should be "mixed-membership" (p. 2)
- Consider removing the last sentence of the paper (p. 8), as the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger conclusion.
- In the bibliography, cite the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference" (Hoffman et al., 2013).

With these minor revisions, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are convincing. The authors have made a meaningful contribution to scalable network analysis that should be of interest to the NIPS community.
