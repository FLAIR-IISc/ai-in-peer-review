This paper presents a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks. The core contributions are threefold: (1) it builds upon the "bag-of-triangles" representation of networks, which is more succinct than an adjacency matrix for many network types; (2) it proposes a new model, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which dramatically reduces the parameter complexity from O(K³) in the related Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) by sharing parameters for different role configurations; and (3) it develops a scalable stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm for this model with a time complexity of O(NK), enabling analysis of million-node networks. The authors situate their work in relation to previous mixed-membership models like MMSB (Airoldi et al., 2008) and its scalable variants (Gopalan et al., 2013), as well as the MMTM (Ho et al., 2012) from which the triangle representation is drawn. The experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in speed over existing methods, with comparable or better performance on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its impressive scalability. The ability to infer a 100-role latent space on a million-node network in a few hours on a single machine is a significant step forward. The empirical results, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over MMTM and a-MMSB on benchmark datasets like Brightkite and Stanford, convincingly demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method. This work effectively addresses a major bottleneck in probabilistic network modeling.

2.  **Originality and Technical Quality:** The paper's main technical novelty lies in the design of the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). The parameter sharing scheme, which groups role triplet configurations based on the number of distinct roles, is a clever and effective way to control model complexity while retaining expressive power. This parsimony is key to the scalability of the inference algorithm. Furthermore, the development of the SVI algorithm is well-executed, incorporating a structured mean-field approximation to capture important dependencies and an O(K) approximation for the local updates, both of which are critical for achieving the final O(NK) complexity.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors motivate their approach through three clear principles (succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, scalable inference) and methodically explain how their work embodies each. The experimental setup is well-described and the results are presented clearly, strongly supporting the paper's claims.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Experimental Comparison:** While the paper mentions Gopalan et al. (a-MMSB) as a state-of-the-art method in the introduction, the quantitative experimental comparisons are primarily against the batch MMSB variational algorithm and the MMTM Gibbs sampler. The batch MMSB is not a fair baseline for scalability, and while the speedup over MMTM is impressive, a direct comparison against the a-MMSB algorithm on the synthetic data tasks (latent space recovery) would have been more compelling. Given that a-MMSB is a direct competitor in scalable latent space inference for networks, its absence from the main quantitative comparisons in Section 5.2 is a notable omission.

2.  **Model Justification:** The paper notes that the generative process does not guarantee the produced set of triangles corresponds to a valid network. The authors defend this by analogy to topic models like LDA, where a document generated from the model may not be coherent. I find this defense unconvincing. A generated document from LDA is still structurally a "document" (a collection of words). In contrast, the PTM can generate objects that are fundamentally not networks (e.g., due to inconsistent edge values in overlapping triangles). While this does not detract from the model's utility for posterior inference on existing networks—which is the paper's stated goal—a more direct justification of this modeling choice, perhaps by emphasizing it as a composite likelihood or a pseudo-likelihood approach, would be more rigorous than the LDA analogy.

3.  **Clarity on Technical Details:**
    *   The paper introduces "parsimonious statistical modeling" as a key principle. While the meaning becomes clear through the description of the PTM, this is not a standard term in probabilistic modeling. A brief, explicit definition in the introduction would be helpful for the reader.
    *   The description of how power-law networks were generated from the latent space model is vague. Providing more detail here is important for reproducibility.
    *   In the introduction, the sentence describing a-MMSB's down-sampling of zeros could be read as implying it compromises accuracy. My reading of Gopalan et al. is that this is a key part of the stochastic optimization and does not necessarily harm the final solution. This point could be rephrased for greater precision.

4.  **Missing Citations:** The paper develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm with local and global variables. The theoretical foundation for this type of algorithm is laid out in Hoffman et al. ("Stochastic Variational Inference," JMLR 2013). This work should be cited alongside Amari (1998) and Sato (2001), as Sato's algorithm does not directly apply to models with local random variables that need to be re-inferred for each mini-batch.

**Minor Comments:**

*   p2: "mixture-membership" should be "mixed-membership".
*   p8: The second-to-last sentence is a powerful conclusion. The final sentence feels slightly weaker and could be removed to end on a stronger note.
*   Bibliography: The reference for "Stochastic Variational Inference" should be to the JMLR version (Hoffman et al., 2013), which is the definitive publication.

**Summary:**

This is a well-executed and valuable paper that makes a strong contribution to scalable network analysis. The proposed PTM model and its associated SVI algorithm are novel, technically sound, and demonstrate impressive empirical performance. The weaknesses are relatively minor and mostly relate to experimental comparisons and framing of certain model properties. I believe this paper is a clear contribution to the field and recommend acceptance.
