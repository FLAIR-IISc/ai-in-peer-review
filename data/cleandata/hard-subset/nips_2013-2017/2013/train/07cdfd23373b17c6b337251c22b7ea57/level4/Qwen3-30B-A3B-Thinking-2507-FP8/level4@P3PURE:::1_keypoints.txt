- Proposes PTM to reduce MMTM’s O(K^3) parameters to O(K) with stochastic variational inference and scalability approximations.  
- Practical advantage of scaling in K is unclear since mixed-membership models tolerate small K; authors must show PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K.  
- Real-data experiments omit comparisons between MMTM (small K) and PTM (large K) and fail to explain how K was chosen, risking biased model selection.  
- Paper inadequately analyzes when PTM’s simplifying constraints fail; if true B has O(K^3) distinct values, PTM should perform poorly.  
- Synthetic experiments do not specify or vary the true B matrices; testing diverse B (favorable and unfavorable to PTM) is needed.  
- The introduced approximation tricks lack detailed analysis: the effect of threshold δ on scalability vs. accuracy is unexplored.  
- Authors claim a-MMSB fails from downsampling but do not explain why PTM avoids this issue despite both ignoring single-edge triplets.  
- Impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates is unexamined; performance changes when sampling different numbers of triples (2K, 4K, 100K) are not reported.  
- Overall experimental setup is unfair to competitors and does not adequately probe PTM’s limitations.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized.  
- Modeling contribution is somewhat incremental, but the scalability-via-parameter-constraints direction is original and relevant.  
- PTM could be significant for large-scale networks if its simplifying assumptions hold in practice.  
- Title is overly broad—PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific model, not a general scalable approach.  
- PTM’s probabilistic validity is questionable: the “bag-of-triples” formulation likely assigns mass to non-networks, undermining the generative interpretation and meaning of probability.  
- Side-effects of the modeling assumptions and inference tricks are insufficiently investigated.
