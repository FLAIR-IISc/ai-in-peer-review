Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a scalable approach to latent space inference for large networks by modeling networks as bags of triangular motifs and introducing the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). PTM shares parameters across classes of triangle role configurations (B0, Bxx, Bxxx), reducing the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K3) in MMTM to O(K). The authors develop a structured mean-field stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients and mini-batch subsampling of triangle motifs, yielding O(NK) global updates and O(K) approximate local updates per sampled triangle via a “mixture-of-deltas” variational family.

Experiments on synthetic data (including MMSB- and power-law–based simulators) and several large real networks show competitive or improved latent role recovery and link prediction, with runtimes orders of magnitude faster than MMSB variational inference and MMTM Gibbs sampling. The method scales to >1M nodes and hundreds of roles on a single multi-core machine.

This work builds on and connects to: mixed-membership block models (MMSB; Airoldi et al.), scalable MMSB variants with parameter tying and stochastic optimization (a-MMSB; Gopalan et al.), triangle-based modeling (MMTM; Ho et al.), ERGM statistics highlighting the importance of triangles, and SVI/natural gradients (Hoffman et al.; Amari; Sato). The main novelty is bringing triangle-motif modeling together with parameter tying for O(K) structure and a practical structured SVI algorithm that scales in both N and K.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for moving from edges to triangle motifs; good discussion of triadic closure and motif statistics.
- Elegant parameter sharing that reduces tensor size from O(K3) to O(K) while retaining useful structure.
- Well-designed structured SVI with natural gradients and mini-batching; discarding local variational parameters between iterations is a practical memory-saving detail.
- Strong empirical results demonstrating both accuracy and dramatic speedups on large networks.
- Clarity of exposition is generally high; algorithmic choices are well justified.

Weaknesses and questions
- Fairness of some comparisons: the most relevant scalable MMSB baseline (a-MMSB) is cited but not evaluated experimentally; direct comparisons would strengthen claims about scalability and accuracy.
- The analogy to LDA as a defense for not being a generative model of networks is not appropriate; the paper should state the modeling choice without that comparison.
- The “mixture-of-deltas” local variational approximation is key to achieving O(K) local complexity, but more detail on its impact (e.g., ablations vs. full K3 tensor, sensitivity to the construction and resampling of A, and effects on the bound and gradients) would be helpful.
- Synthetic data: the construction of power-law networks in latent space is underspecified; more detail is needed to reproduce results.
- A statement in the introduction about downsampling zeros in a-MMSB affecting accuracy appears misleading relative to Gopalan et al.’s findings.

Assessment by review criteria
- Quality: The model and inference are technically sound, with appropriate use of structured SVI and natural gradients. Claims are mostly supported by experiments. Some theoretical statements (e.g., unbiasedness and bound properties under the mixture-of-deltas restriction) would benefit from clearer exposition or pointers to proofs.
- Clarity: Writing is generally clear and well organized. A few terminology and citation issues (see specific comments) detract slightly.
- Originality: The combination of triangle-based representation with O(K) parameter tying and a scalable structured SVI procedure is novel and practically impactful, even if individual ingredients extend known ideas (e.g., parameter tying as in a-MMSB).
- Significance: High. The ability to recover latent roles and perform link prediction on million-node networks within hours on a single machine is valuable and likely to be used by practitioners.

Specific comments and suggestions
- p1: Define “parsimonious” modeling concretely (e.g., parameter tying that reduces triangle parameterization from O(K3) to O(K) while preserving key role interactions).
- p1: The sentence suggesting that a-MMSB’s zero downsampling hurts accuracy is misleading; Gopalan et al. report minimal loss. Please revise or qualify.
- p4: Remove or reframe the LDA analogy. Unlike LDA, the proposed process can yield sets of triangles inconsistent with any graph; that’s fine, but the analogy suggests a property the model does not have.
- p5: Please cite Hoffman et al. (JMLR 2013) on SVI alongside Amari (natural gradients) and Sato. In the presence of local variables, the convergence and update structure you use follows Hoffman et al.
- p6: Provide details on how power-law networks are generated in the latent space (priors, link functions, degree control), sufficient for reproduction.
- p6: Include experimental comparison to a-MMSB (Gopalan et al.), which is designed for scalability and is the closest probabilistic baseline.
- Algorithmic detail: Elaborate on the construction of the candidate set A in the mixture-of-deltas posterior, how often it is resampled per triangle, and any diagnostics showing the approximation does not degrade performance.
- Notation: “mixture-membership” → “mixed-membership.”
- Conclusion (p8): Consider ending with the penultimate sentence; the final sentence is weaker and can be removed.
- Bibliography: Cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference.”

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, novel, and practically useful scaling of triangle-based latent role models.
- Significant empirical speedups with competitive or improved accuracy.
- Clean, implementable algorithm likely to impact practice.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing experimental comparison to the most relevant scalable MMSB method (a-MMSB).
- Some key approximations not fully analyzed or ablated.
- Minor clarity and citation issues.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance. The paper presents a compelling, technically sound approach that substantially advances the scalability of probabilistic latent space inference for networks, with strong empirical evidence on large real data. Addressing the comparison to a-MMSB and clarifying the points above would further strengthen the paper.
