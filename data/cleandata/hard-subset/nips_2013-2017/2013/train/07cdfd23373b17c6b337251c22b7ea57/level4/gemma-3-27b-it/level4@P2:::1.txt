This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a modification of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) designed to improve scalability by reducing the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K), where K is the number of latent roles. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, incorporating several approximation techniques to further enhance scalability. Experiments on synthetic and real-world networks demonstrate competitive accuracy with existing methods, alongside significant gains in computational efficiency.

**Quality:**

The PTM presents a valuable specialization of the MMTM, addressing a critical limitation – scalability with respect to K. However, the paper’s justification for prioritizing scalability in K feels somewhat unconvincing. While scaling to many latent roles is desirable, the benefit isn’t fully articulated. Existing mixed-membership models haven’t necessarily been hampered by modest K values, leading to the question of whether enabling larger K truly unlocks new analytical capabilities. The experimental evaluation would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating a clear advantage of PTM with larger K over MMTM with smaller K – a comparison currently absent. The method of model selection (choosing K) for both PTM and the competitor MMSB isn’t clearly described, potentially introducing bias into the results.

The authors are hesitant to discuss the potential drawbacks of their simplifying assumptions. Reducing the parameter space achieves scalability, but a more thorough analysis of *when* this simplification might lead to performance degradation is needed. If the true underlying parameters exhibit the full O(K^3) complexity, PTM’s constrained representation could be suboptimal. The synthetic experiments should include scenarios with varying underlying parameter structures – some favoring PTM, others highlighting its limitations – to provide a more complete picture of its performance profile.

Several approximation techniques are introduced for scalability, but their impact remains largely undiscussed. Specifically: 1) The impact of the threshold δ in the triangle subsampling process requires investigation. How does varying δ affect the trade-off between scalability and accuracy on both real and synthetic data? 2) The authors contrast PTM with a-MMSB, citing accuracy compromises due to downsampling. However, PTM also employs downsampling (ignoring triplets with only one edge). A clear explanation of why PTM avoids the same accuracy issues as a-MMSB is necessary. 3) The O(K) approximation to the local update (Section 4) requires further scrutiny. What’s the impact of varying the sample size (2K, 4K, 100K)? Does approximation quality diminish as K increases?

Overall, the experimental setup lacks sufficient control to ensure a fair comparison with competitors, and the paper provides a limited discussion of the model’s potential weaknesses.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Originality:**

The modeling approach represents an incremental but valuable improvement to existing methods. Constraining the parameter space to achieve scalability is an original and promising direction, particularly within a field currently focused on generalizing existing models.

**Significance:**

If the simplifying assumptions underlying the model and inference algorithm prove valid in practice, PTM could significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large-scale networks. Many existing methods struggle to scale, and a practical solution would be highly valuable.

**Additional Comments:**

The title may be overly broad. The paper presents a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model (PTM), rather than a universally scalable approach to all latent space models.

The claim of “probabilistic” modeling is questionable. While analogous to the bag-of-words assumption in topic modeling, PTM doesn't correspond to a valid generative model for networks. LDA generates documents with associated likelihoods, however MMTM/PTM frequently generate configurations of triangles that do not correspond to any valid network structure. The majority of posterior probability mass may therefore be assigned to non-networks, raising concerns about the interpretation of “probability” within this framework. This point warrants further discussion. The authors focus on inference techniques that enable scalability for MMTM; however, the consequences of these choices, particularly the abandonment of a proper likelihood function, are underexplored.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial scalability issue in latent space network modeling.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance on synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Offers a novel and potentially impactful approach to parameter reduction.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Justification for prioritizing scalability in K is weak.
*   Experimental setup lacks sufficient control for a fair comparison.
*   Insufficient discussion of the model’s potential weaknesses and limitations.
*   Questionable use of the term “probabilistic” given the lack of a valid generative model.
*   Impact of approximation techniques is not thoroughly investigated.

**Overall Assessment:**

I am leaning towards **weak reject**. While the paper presents a technically sound and clearly articulated approach, the lack of a compelling justification for the primary design choice (scalability in K), the questionable experimental setup, and the limited discussion of potential weaknesses raise significant concerns. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper and justify acceptance.
