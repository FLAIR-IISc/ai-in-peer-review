This paper proposes a scalable approach to inferring latent structure in large networks by modeling counts of triangular motifs rather than edges. The network is preprocessed into a bag-of-triangles representation, analogous to a bag-of-words for text. Building on the MMTM’s triangle-based view and a-MMSB’s parameter sharing, the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) by sharing parameters across equivalence classes of role configurations. They then develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field updates and an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation for the local updates, yielding an overall O(NK) procedure. Empirically, the method scales to million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine and achieves competitive or improved performance on latent-space recovery and link prediction relative to prior probabilistic methods such as MMSB, a-MMSB, and MMTM.

Relation to prior work: The paper situates itself at the intersection of mixed-membership blockmodels (MMSB/a-MMSB), triangular motif representations (MMTM), and SVI. The main modeling idea—parameter sharing across role configurations—is directly motivated by a-MMSB’s reduction from O(K^2) to O(K) for inter-role parameters, applied here to triangle-based generative structure. The use of triangle motifs aligns with prior work showing they are informative and more compact than edge-based representations. The algorithmic framework follows standard SVI, with a noteworthy local-update approximation.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation for using triangles to reduce data size while preserving informative structure; aligns with the literature on motifs and clustering coefficients.
- A parsimonious parameterization of triangle-generation probabilities (O(K) parameters) that plausibly improves both scalability and statistical efficiency relative to O(K^3) tensors.
- A structured mean-field variational family that captures dependencies among role assignments within a triangle; the proposed O(K) mixture-of-deltas approximation for local updates is intuitive and practically useful.
- Demonstrated scalability: convergence in a few passes, wall-clock improvements of orders of magnitude over prior methods on large networks.
- Competitive or better accuracy on latent-space recovery and link prediction, including on real datasets.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The paper states that “our PTM is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, representing the process of generating triangular motifs.” This characterization is confusing: (1) ERGMs encompass a broad set of sufficient statistics beyond triangles; and (2) it is unclear in what precise sense PTM is a “latent space counterpart” as opposed to one more member of the latent-space family. Clarification and more careful phrasing would help.
- Interpretability: Are the inferred roles/latent positions interpretable? If so, what do they reveal about similarities and differences across graphs?
- Notation/clarity: The distinction between the 1-simplex and the 2-simplex parameterizations (i.e., how B0, Bxx, and Bxxx are defined) is not clearly explained.
- Subsampling: The paper relies on δ-subsampling of triangles but does not, in this submission, clearly justify why this yields an adequate approximation in general. A more explicit discussion of the bias/variance trade-off and when subsampling preserves key statistics would be valuable.
- If the evaluation goal is to recover the set of non-zero roles, a more explicitly binary or sparse prior over roles might be more appropriate. As is, recovery depends on a post hoc threshold.
- Sensitivity: How robust are the latent-space recovery results to the chosen threshold of 0.125? This threshold appears somewhat arbitrary; a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claim.
- Power-law simulator: Many latent-space models yield power-law degree distributions. Which specific model was used in the experiments?
- PTM CGS complexity: Why does the PTM collapsed blocked Gibbs sampler still have O(K^3) complexity if the model has only O(K) parameters? Please reconcile the parameter count with the sampling complexity.
- Data missingness: How does performance degrade when larger fractions of edges are unobserved? Holding out only 10% may avoid strongly biasing triangle counts, but many real settings have sparser or noisier observations.
- Synthetic results: It is counterintuitive that the proposed method outperforms MMSB on data generated by MMSB (Figure 1, left four panels). Additional discussion (e.g., effects of parameter sharing, optimization bias/variance, or improved regularization) would help reconcile this result.
- Directed networks: Given the prevalence of directed graphs, a detailed discussion of how the model and inference would extend to directed motifs would be welcome.
- Presentation: While the core idea is straightforward—a combination of a-MMSB-style sharing with MMTM’s triangle view—the exposition could be streamlined. Some of the bookkeeping obscures the main insights.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong empirical scalability with solid accuracy.
- Well-motivated parameter sharing that reduces complexity from O(K^3) to O(K).
- Practical and effective local-update approximation within SVI.
- Triangle-based representation leverages informative higher-order structure.

Cons for acceptance:
- Several clarity issues (ERGM comparison, simplex parameterizations, subsampling justification).
- Limited analysis of sensitivity (threshold choice) and robustness to missing edges.
- Some results (beating MMSB on MMSB-generated data) are surprising and need deeper explanation.
- No treatment of directed networks.

Overall assessment: This work applies SVI to a mixed-membership triangle-based model with a restricted set of parameters, yielding an O(NK) algorithm. The methodological combination is natural rather than radically novel, but the engineering and approximation choices are thoughtful and deliver substantial scalability gains with competitive accuracy. Clarifying the modeling claims and addressing the noted concerns would strengthen the paper.
