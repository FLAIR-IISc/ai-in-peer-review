

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) from O(K³) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and incorporate additional approximation techniques to further enhance scalability. Experiments on synthetic data demonstrate that reducing the number of parameters can yield stronger statistical power, while real-world dataset results indicate that PTM achieves competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.

Quality:  
PTM represents an interesting specialization of MMTM, but its practical advantage remains questionable. The key issue is whether achieving scalability in terms of K (the number of roles) provides meaningful benefits. To evaluate this, it is essential to address: "How does PTM help if MMTM can be learned with large K?" Unlike single-membership models, using small K in mixed-membership models (e.g., MMSB, MMTM) is less problematic. For real networks, the most compelling experiment would compare PTM with large K against MMTM with small K—only such a comparison could demonstrate PTM's superiority. Unfortunately, the paper omits this critical analysis. Furthermore, the paper lacks clarity on how K was selected for real data experiments involving PTM and its competitor MMSB, raising concerns about potential bias from model selection methods.  

The authors also inadequately address the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space enables scalability, it is crucial to understand when PTM fails. If the true parameter matrix B (in authors' notation) contains O(K³) distinct values—as in MMSB/MMTM—PTM should perform poorly. However, the synthetic experiments do not clarify the choice of true B, nor do they test diverse B matrices (some favorable to PTM, others not). Readers would benefit more from understanding PTM's failure conditions than solely its success conditions.  

The paper introduces several approximation tricks to enhance scalability, but provides insufficient analysis of their impact. Specifically:  
1) What is the effect of threshold δ? Does a larger or smaller δ improve performance on real/synthetic data? How low can δ be set without significant accuracy loss?  
2) The authors claim a-MMSB fails due to downsampling compromising accuracy, yet PTM avoids this issue. However, both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge—why can MMSB not similarly ignore non-edges?  
3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation for local updates (p.5)? How does performance degrade when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples, especially as K increases?  

In summary, the experimental setup is not fairly evaluated against competitors, and the analysis of the model's limitations is inadequate.  

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured.  

Originality: While the modeling approach is somewhat incremental, the focus on scalability via parameter space constraints represents an interesting and original direction, particularly given recent trends toward model generalization in this domain.  

Significance: If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, PTM could significantly impact the application of latent-space network models, as few existing methods scale effectively to large datasets.  

Additional Comments: The title is overly broad; PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model, not a general solution for scalable approaches to latent-space models. Additionally, PTM's status as a probabilistic method is questionable. The authors compare it to the bag-of-words assumption in LDA, but this analogy is flawed: LDA generates documents corresponding to bags of words, ensuring a valid likelihood. In contrast, a bag of triples generated by PTM/MMTM almost never corresponds to a real network, placing most posterior mass on non-network structures. Abandoning likelihood principles in "probabilistic" methods risks conceptual incoherence (e.g., the meaning of probability in PTM). The side effects of the proposed assumptions and approximations are not sufficiently investigated.
