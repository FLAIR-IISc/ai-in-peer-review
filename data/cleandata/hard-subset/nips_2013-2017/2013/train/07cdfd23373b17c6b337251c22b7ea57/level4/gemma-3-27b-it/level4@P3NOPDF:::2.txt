This paper introduces a novel approach to learning latent structure within social network data, focusing on triangle count statistics as a proxy for network configuration – analogous to a bag-of-words representation in text analysis. Motivated by the advantages of attribute mixed-membership stochastic blockmodels (aMMSB), the authors propose a model that reduces the parameter space by focusing on triangle configurations to identify *K* latent roles, thereby improving scalability. They leverage recent advances in stochastic variational inference (SVI) to fit the model, deriving both global and local update rules.

The core contribution lies in the combination of aMMSB principles with a mixed-membership triangle model (MMTM), although the presentation of this integration feels somewhat cumbersome and could benefit from clarification. While the majority of the learning algorithm relies on established SVI techniques, the authors introduce an intuitive and compelling approximation for the local update step. 

The paper’s focus on undirected networks is a limitation. A discussion of potential extensions to directed networks, which are prevalent in many real-world scenarios, would significantly strengthen the work.

Several points require further attention. The authors state their “Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs.” This statement is problematic for two reasons: first, Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs) can incorporate a much wider range of network statistics beyond just triangles; second, the analogy to a “latent space counterpart” feels overly broad and applies to almost any latent space network model. 

Furthermore, the interpretability of the inferred latent space is unclear. Understanding what the recovered latent roles reveal about the similarities and differences between the analyzed graphs is crucial. The distinction between Δ<sup>1</sup> and Δ<sup>2</sup> is not adequately explained, hindering comprehension. The rationale for subsampling triangles for each node (using delta) to obtain an adequate approximation also requires more justification. If the goal is to identify a set of non-zero roles, a more explicitly binary representation of roles within the model might be beneficial.

The sensitivity of the latent space recovery results to the chosen threshold of 0.125 (which appears rather arbitrary) should be investigated. The paper would benefit from specifying which latent space model was employed to generate the power-law networks used in the experiments. A clarification regarding the O(K<sup>3</sup>) complexity of PTM-CGS is needed; the authors previously emphasized the model's O(K) parameterization. 

A potential concern arises regarding the method’s performance when dealing with sparse networks. The experiments only withhold 10% of edges, likely to avoid significant bias in the distribution of adjacent triangles resulting from excessive edge removal during preprocessing.  Finally, the observation that PTM outperforms MMSB on synthetic networks *generated by* MMSB (Figure 1, left four panels) is counterintuitive and warrants further investigation.

In summary, this work presents a valuable contribution to scalable network modeling by leveraging SVI on triangle count statistics within a mixed-membership framework. The modeling choices enable an efficient O(NK) algorithm. However, addressing the outstanding questions regarding model interpretation, justification of approximations, and robustness would substantially enhance the paper’s impact.
