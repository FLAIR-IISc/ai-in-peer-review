

This paper introduces a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for learning latent structure in social networks, leveraging triangle count statistics as a feature representation analogous to bag-of-words in text. The core innovation adapts the aMMSB framework by parameterizing interactions through triangle configurations rather than all between-community pairs, reducing parameters and enabling scalability. The authors employ stochastic variational inference (SVI) with novel global and local update rules, which they present as intuitive approximations. While the modeling approach combines established components (aMMSB and MMTM), the technical execution requires clearer exposition.

The primary strength lies in the model's reduced parameterization (O(K) vs. O(K²) in standard MMSB) and efficient SVI implementation (O(NK) complexity), enabling scalability to large networks. The local update approximation is particularly well-motivated and insightful. However, several critical issues undermine the paper's contribution and clarity.

First, the claim that PTM is "essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is misleading. ERGMs encompass diverse statistics beyond triangles, and the phrasing implies a fundamental equivalence that does not hold—latent space models are a broad category, not a specific counterpart. This mischaracterization weakens the theoretical positioning.

Second, the presentation lacks sufficient detail on key technical choices. The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² is unclear, and the justification for triangle subsampling (using Δ) to approximate global triangle counts is insufficient. Why does subsampling suffice, and how does it affect statistical validity? Similarly, the model's K³ complexity for PTM CGS contradicts the stated O(K) parameter count, requiring explicit reconciliation.

Third, the paper omits crucial experimental and interpretability details. The latent space embeddings—central to the model's purpose—are not assessed for interpretability: What do inferred roles signify about network structure? How do roles differ across graphs? The arbitrary threshold (0.125) for role inference also lacks justification; robustness to this choice is untested. Additionally, the synthetic experiment (Fig. 1, left) shows PTM outperforming MMSB on MMSB-generated data, which is counterintuitive and demands stronger explanation.

Methodological limitations are unaddressed. The experiments hold out only 10% of edges, likely due to bias in triangle statistics when edges are removed—a significant constraint given real-world networks often have sparse observations. The model's performance under sparsity (e.g., 5% edge holdout) is unexplored. Furthermore, the paper does not clarify which latent space model generated the synthetic power-law networks used in experiments, nor why PTM would outperform MMSB on such data.

The discussion of directed networks is superficial. While the paper focuses on undirected graphs, a brief analysis of how PTM could extend to directed settings (e.g., via oriented triangles) would strengthen relevance to broader network analysis.

Finally, the paper's claim that PTM "recovers the set of non-zero roles" suggests a binary role assignment, yet the model appears continuous. A clearer binary role formulation would align better with the stated goal.

In summary, PTM offers a plausible scalability improvement through triangle-based parameterization and SVI, but the work is undermined by technical ambiguities, insufficient justification for key design choices, and unaddressed experimental limitations. The presentation of the model and its relationship to existing frameworks (e.g., ERGMs) requires significant revision. While the core idea has merit, the current manuscript lacks the rigor and clarity expected for NIPS. The contribution is modest, and the unresolved concerns prevent confident assessment of its novelty or impact. A revised version addressing these points would be necessary for acceptance.
