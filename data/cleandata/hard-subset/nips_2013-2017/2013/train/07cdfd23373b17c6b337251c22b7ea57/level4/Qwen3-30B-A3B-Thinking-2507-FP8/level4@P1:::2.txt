

This paper presents a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks, addressing a critical challenge in network analysis where existing probabilistic methods struggle with networks exceeding 100,000 nodes. The authors build on prior work including the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB) and its extension a-MMSB, which address scalability through reduced parameter spaces but remain limited by their reliance on adjacency matrices. They also draw from the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), which uses a bag-of-triangles representation to reduce network storage from O(N²) to O(Nδ²), but suffers from O(K³) parameter complexity that prevents scaling to large numbers of latent roles (K).

The paper's core contribution is the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces parameter complexity from O(K³) to O(K) by strategically sharing parameters across role configurations. Specifically, the authors partition the O(K³) role configuration space into three meaningful groups: (1) all three roles identical (parameterized by Bxxx), (2) two roles identical (parameterized by Bxx), and (3) all roles distinct (parameterized by B0). This elegant parameter reduction enables a stochastic variational inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity, allowing analysis of networks with over a million nodes and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine within hours—a significant improvement over prior methods.

The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves competitive or improved accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks while providing orders-of-magnitude speed improvements. Their empirical results on synthetic and real-world networks validate the method's scalability and performance, with the ability to process a 1M-node YouTube social network in just 4 hours using a single machine with 8 threads.

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a clear and important contribution to network analysis by addressing a critical scalability bottleneck. The O(NK) complexity represents a significant advancement over existing methods that scale as O(N²K²) or O(Nδ²K³).
2. The parameter sharing strategy in PTM is well-motivated and effectively reduces complexity without sacrificing model expressiveness. The technical development is sound and well-justified.
3. The experimental results are comprehensive and demonstrate the method's practical utility on both synthetic and real-world networks, with convincing comparisons to state-of-the-art approaches.
4. The paper provides a clear explanation of the technical innovations, including the structured mean-field approximation that captures higher-order dependencies between latent variables.

Weaknesses:
1. The explanation of why the PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is confusing and poorly supported. ERGMs encompass a much broader class of models that can incorporate diverse network statistics beyond triangles, and it's unclear how the PTM specifically relates to latent space models in general.
2. The threshold of 0.125 for NMI evaluation appears arbitrary and is not adequately justified. The robustness of the results to different threshold values is not addressed.
3. The Delta^1 vs Delta^2 distinction in the model parameters is not clearly explained, causing confusion about the parameterization of the model.
4. The paper does not sufficiently address how the method would perform on sparse networks or when more edges are removed (beyond the 10% holdout used in experiments), which is a critical practical consideration.
5. The claim that their method outperforms MMSB on synthetic networks generated by MMSB (Figure 1, left 4 panels) seems counterintuitive and requires more detailed explanation.

The paper's technical contributions are significant and well-executed, with clear potential for practical impact in network analysis. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the scalability of latent space models for large networks, which is a major challenge in the field. The method's ability to handle networks with millions of nodes while maintaining competitive accuracy represents a meaningful advancement.

The paper is well-written overall, though some technical explanations could be clearer. The presentation of the model development, while technically sound, would benefit from more intuitive explanations of the parameter sharing strategy and the role of the triangle equivalence classes.

Pro and Con for Acceptance:
- Pro: The paper presents a significant scalability advance in latent space inference for large networks, with a well-motivated technical approach and compelling empirical results demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speed improvements.
- Pro: The method is practical and has been validated on both synthetic and real-world networks, showing competitive or improved accuracy compared to state-of-the-art approaches.
- Con: Some technical explanations (particularly regarding parameter sharing and the ERGM relationship) are unclear, and the paper doesn't adequately address performance on sparse networks or the impact of the arbitrary threshold value.
- Con: The paper's claim about the PTM being a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is misleading and poorly supported, potentially causing confusion for readers.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses a critical challenge in network analysis. The technical approach is sound, the empirical results are convincing, and the method has clear practical value. While some aspects of the presentation could be improved, the paper's core contribution is significant and aligns well with NIPS's focus on high-quality scientific contributions. The scalability improvements represent a meaningful advancement in the field of network analysis.
