This paper introduces a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks, which models pairwise interactions between nodes. The key contributions are twofold: the development of a powerful subclass of the model that utilizes parameter sharing for greater statistical efficiency, and the application of stochastic variational inference (SVI) to enable posterior inference on very large networks. The paper is well-written, the proposed method is sound, and the technical development is clearly presented. I believe this is a solid contribution and recommend acceptance, though I have several suggestions for improvement.

**Major Comments**

1.  **Clarity on "Parsimonious" Modeling (p1):** The term "parsimonious modeling" is used in the introduction as a key motivation. While the concept of parsimony is generally understood, its specific meaning in this context is not immediately clear. I suggest the authors add a sentence to unpack this term and explain precisely how their parameter-sharing approach achieves a more parsimonious model, as this would strengthen the paper's introduction.

2.  **Characterization of Gopalan et al. (p1):** The introduction seems to mischaracterize the work of Gopalan et al. regarding the down-sampling of zero-valued entries in network data. My understanding of that work is that their method of down-sampling does not compromise accuracy. The current phrasing could be revised to more accurately reflect the findings of the cited paper.

3.  **Analogy to LDA (p4):** On page 4, the paper defends the triangle model's property of potentially generating non-network objects by drawing an analogy to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). I find this comparison unconvincing. While LDA's generative process might produce a semantically nonsensical document, the output is still structurally a "document" (i.e., a collection of words). In contrast, the generative process for the triangle model can produce objects that are not valid networks. While this may be acceptable from a pragmatic modeling standpoint, justifying it with the LDA analogy feels inappropriate. A more direct defense of the model's properties would be stronger.

4.  **Citation for Natural Gradients with SVI (p5):** Regarding the discussion of natural gradients, the citation to Amari and Sato should be accompanied by a citation to Hoffman et al. (2013, "Stochastic Variational Inference"). In scenarios with local variables, as is the case here, the theoretical justification for applying SVI relies on the developments in Hoffman et al., as Sato's original algorithm is not directly applicable.

5.  **Experimental Details on Synthetic Data (p6):** The experimental section lacks detail on how the synthetic power-law networks were generated in the latent space. To ensure the reproducibility of these experiments, it would be beneficial for the authors to clarify the specific procedure used.

6.  **Experimental Comparisons (p6):** The paper cites Gopalan et al. as a motivation for speeding up inference in mixed-membership stochastic blockmodels (MMSB). However, the experiments only compare against a standard batch version of MMSB. A more compelling and relevant comparison would be against the scalable algorithm proposed by Gopalan et al. itself, as this represents a more direct state-of-the-art competitor for scalable inference in a related class of models.

**Minor Comments**

*   **p2:** The term "mixture-membership" should be corrected to "mixed-membership."
*   **p8:** The final sentence of the paper feels weaker than the penultimate one. I suggest removing it to allow the paper to end on the stronger, more impactful statement made in the second-to-last sentence.
*   **Bibliography:** The reference for "Stochastic Variational Inference" by Hoffman et al. should cite the final JMLR version of the paper.
