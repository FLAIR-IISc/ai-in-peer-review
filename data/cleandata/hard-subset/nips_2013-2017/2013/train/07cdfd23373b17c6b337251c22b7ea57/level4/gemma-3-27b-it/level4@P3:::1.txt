This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a modification of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), to achieve scalability with respect to the number of latent roles (K). The core idea is to constrain the O(K³) parameter space of MMTM to O(K) by sharing parameters, enabling faster inference via a stochastic variational inference algorithm with several approximation techniques. Experiments on synthetic and real-world networks demonstrate competitive accuracy and significant speed improvements over existing methods.

**Quality:**

The PTM represents a worthwhile effort to address the scalability limitations of existing probabilistic latent space models for networks. The motivation – that large networks may necessitate a large number of latent roles – is sound. However, the paper’s evaluation doesn’t fully justify the benefits of achieving scalability in K. While reducing computational complexity is valuable, the core question of *why* being able to model a larger K is advantageous remains largely unanswered. The authors acknowledge MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models where small K isn’t necessarily problematic, but don't strongly demonstrate a scenario where larger K, enabled by PTM, leads to demonstrably better results. 

A crucial experiment would involve systematically varying K for both PTM and MMTM and evaluating performance.  Currently, the presentation of results lacks this crucial comparison.  The method of model selection for K in the real-world experiments isn’t clearly described, raising concerns about potential bias.  The authors should address this by detailing the criteria used to choose K for each method and dataset.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of the simplifying assumptions. While scalability is achieved through parameter sharing, a clearer articulation of *when* PTM might perform poorly compared to MMTM is needed. If the true underlying parameter structure truly necessitates a full O(K³) representation, PTM’s performance should degrade. The experiments should include scenarios designed to test this sensitivity, perhaps by generating synthetic data with parameter structures favoring or disfavoring parameter sharing. The description of the synthetic data generation process is also insufficient; detailing the structure of the 'true' B matrix would be helpful.

Several approximation techniques are introduced for scalability (δ-subsampling, the O(K) local update). The paper needs a more rigorous analysis of their impact. Specifically: 1) How does the choice of δ affect accuracy and scalability on both real and synthetic data? A sensitivity analysis would be valuable. 2) The authors dismiss a-MMSB due to downsampling affecting accuracy, but PTM ignores single-edge triplets.  The justification for why PTM avoids similar accuracy issues isn't compelling. 3) A more detailed exploration of the O(K) approximation to the local update is necessary. How does performance change with different sample sizes (2K, 4K, etc.) as K grows?

Overall, while the technical contributions are solid, the experimental validation and discussion of limitations are insufficient to fully convince the reader of PTM’s broad applicability and advantages.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The explanations are clear, and the mathematical notation is generally accessible. The structure of the paper logically progresses from motivation to model description, inference algorithm, and experimental results.

**Originality:**

The combination of parameter sharing for scalability in the context of triangular network modeling is a novel and interesting approach. While the core ideas build upon existing work (MMSB, MMTM), the PTM represents a tangible improvement in scalability and presents a reasonable direction for future research.

**Significance:**

If the simplifying assumptions of PTM hold in practice, it has the potential to significantly impact the application of latent space models to large-scale networks. The demonstrated scalability improvements are substantial and address a critical bottleneck in the field. However, the significance is contingent on demonstrating the practical benefits of modeling a larger number of latent roles and a more thorough understanding of the model’s limitations.

**Additional Comments:**

The title, "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks," is somewhat broad. A more specific title reflecting the PTM and its unique contribution would be preferable.

The authors’ claim of a “probabilistic” model warrants further scrutiny. The lack of a proper likelihood function (as discussed in the original draft review) is concerning. While the bag-of-triples representation is convenient for inference, it fundamentally deviates from the principles of likelihood-based probabilistic modeling. Acknowledging this limitation and discussing its implications would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments Pro Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant challenge in network modeling: scalability to large networks with potentially many latent roles.
*   Presents a novel and technically sound approach (PTM) for achieving this scalability.
*   Demonstrates substantial speed improvements compared to existing methods.
*   Well-written and clearly explained.

**Arguments Con Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation doesn't fully justify the benefits of scalability in K.
*   Lacks a thorough discussion of the model’s limitations and potential failure modes.
*   Insufficient analysis of the impact of approximation techniques.
*   The probabilistic framing of the model is questionable given the lack of a proper likelihood function.
*   Fairness of comparisons to competitors isn’t fully established.



**Recommendation:**

**Weak Reject**. The paper presents a technically interesting contribution but falls short of demonstrating its broad applicability and significance due to limitations in the experimental validation and discussion of limitations. Addressing the concerns outlined above – particularly a more comprehensive experimental evaluation with varying K, a thorough analysis of approximation impacts, and a more nuanced discussion of the model’s limitations – would significantly strengthen the paper.
