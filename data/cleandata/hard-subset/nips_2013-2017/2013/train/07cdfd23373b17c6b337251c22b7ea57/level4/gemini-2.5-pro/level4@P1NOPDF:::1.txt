This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to further improve its scalability. Experiments on a synthetic dataset suggest that this parameter reduction can lead to stronger statistical power, while results on real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with existing approaches in terms of accuracy.

**Quality**

While PTM is an interesting specialization of MMTM, the practical advantage of achieving good scalability in K (the number of possible roles) is questionable. To empirically evaluate the method's value, it is critical to answer the question: how does the ability to learn MMTM with a large K help in practice? For mixed-membership models like MMSB and MMTM, a small K is not necessarily as problematic as it is for single-membership models. A more compelling experiment for the real-world networks would be to show the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with a large K were shown to outperform MMTM with a small K, it would clearly demonstrate the power of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments were not performed. The paper also does not clearly describe how K was chosen in the real-data experiments for PTM and its competitor MMSB; the method of model selection could have biased the experimental results.

The authors are also reticent about the impact of their simplifying assumptions. It is clear that scalability can be achieved by constraining the parameter space, but it would be beneficial to understand the failure modes of such a model. For instance, if the true parameter matrix B (using the authors' notation) truly contains O(K^3) distinct values, one would expect PTM to perform poorly. In this regard, the lack of a clear description of the true B matrix in the synthetic experiments is a weakness. The authors should have experimented with various B matrices, including some that favor the PTM assumptions and some that violate them. It would be more informative for readers to understand the conditions under which PTM succeeds and fails, rather than only seeing cases where it succeeds.

Furthermore, while the authors introduce several interesting and effective tricks to make PTM scalable, the paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing their impact.
1.  What is the impact of the threshold δ? The paper should investigate how the choice of δ (larger vs. smaller) affects performance on both real and synthetic data. An ablation study exploring the trade-off between scalability (by lowering δ) and accuracy would be valuable.
2.  The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy. Why is PTM free of this problem? The paper notes that MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge. It is unclear why a similar approach (e.g., ignoring non-edges) is considered problematic for MMSB but acceptable for PTM.
3.  What is the impact of the O(K) approximation for the local updates (page 5)? How does performance change if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples instead? Does the quality of this approximation degrade as K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to the competing methods, and the paper provides a poor description of the potential weaknesses of the proposed model and algorithm.

**Clarity**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality**

The modeling contribution is somewhat incremental, but achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction, especially given that much recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

**Significance**

If the simplifying assumptions of the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the application of latent space models to large networks, as few existing methods are truly scalable.

**Additional Comments**

I find the title to be overly broad. Many scalable approaches exist for other latent space models; this paper proposes a scalable algorithm for a specific model, PTM.

I am also not convinced that PTM is a truly probabilistic model. The authors' analogy to the bag-of-words assumption (Section 3) is not convincing. In the case of LDA, for any bag-of-words it generates, a corresponding document can be constructed. It therefore has a proper, albeit simple, likelihood function. In contrast, a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM or PTM will, with very high probability, not correspond to any valid network. This implies that MMTM/PTM is not a true generative model for networks, and that the majority of its posterior probability mass is concentrated on configurations that are not valid networks. Abandoning the basic principles of likelihood seems problematic for a "probabilistic" method. It raises the fundamental question of what the probabilities in PTM actually represent. I would be interested to hear the authors' and other reviewers' thoughts on this matter.

The authors propose a set of modeling assumptions and inference tricks to make MMTM scalable. However, the side-effects of these choices are poorly investigated.
