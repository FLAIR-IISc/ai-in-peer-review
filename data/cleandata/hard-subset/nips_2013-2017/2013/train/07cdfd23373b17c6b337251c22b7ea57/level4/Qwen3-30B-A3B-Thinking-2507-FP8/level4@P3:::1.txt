

This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks, introducing a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that reduces the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) compared to the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM). The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity, enabling analysis of networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in hours. The work builds on previous network representation approaches, particularly the triangular motif representation introduced in Ho et al. (NIPS 2012), and extends the mixed-membership modeling framework of Airoldi et al. (NIPS 2008) and Gopalan et al. (NIPS 2013), addressing three key scalability challenges: succinct network representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with a well-justified approach. The PTM design cleverly reduces complexity through parameter sharing across role configurations, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm is efficiently implemented. The experimental results demonstrate significant speedups (orders of magnitude) over prior work while maintaining competitive accuracy. However, several aspects of the experimental evaluation require strengthening:

1. The comparison between PTM and MMTM is not sufficiently thorough. The authors should have demonstrated how PTM with large K compares to MMTM with small K, which would better showcase PTM's scalability advantage. The current experiments do not address this critical comparison.

2. The synthetic experiments lack sufficient diversity in parameter configurations. The paper should have tested with various parameter matrices B to demonstrate when PTM succeeds and when it fails, rather than only showing favorable cases. This would provide valuable insight into the model's limitations.

3. The impact of the δ subsampling parameter requires deeper analysis. The paper should investigate how varying δ affects accuracy and scalability trade-offs, particularly how low δ can be set without significant accuracy loss.

4. The claim that PTM avoids accuracy issues of a-MMSB is not adequately supported. The authors should explain why the triangle-based approach circumvents the downsampling accuracy problems that affect a-MMSB, especially since both methods ignore certain network structures.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The technical content is clearly presented, with good figures and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The authors effectively explain their novel contributions and relationship to previous work.

Originality: The core idea of reducing the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) is novel within the context of triangular motif-based latent space models. While parameter reduction has been used elsewhere (e.g., a-MMSB), the specific application to triangular motifs represents a meaningful contribution to the scalability of network analysis methods. The approach is original in its design for this specific problem.

Significance: If the PTM assumptions hold in practice, this work would have substantial practical impact by enabling latent space analysis on networks orders of magnitude larger than what is currently feasible. This addresses a real bottleneck in network analysis. However, the significance is somewhat limited by the lack of thorough investigation into when the model assumptions fail.

Strengths:
- Novel parameter reduction technique for triangular motif models
- Demonstrated scalability to million-node networks
- Competitive accuracy to prior methods
- Well-written and well-organized paper
- Clear experimental validation of scalability claims

Weaknesses:
- Incomplete experimental comparison between PTM and MMTM (not showing PTM with large K vs. MMTM with small K)
- Insufficient analysis of when the PTM assumptions fail
- Lack of detailed investigation into δ subsampling impact
- Unsubstantiated claims about PTM's accuracy advantages over a-MMSB

The paper is clearly within NIPS scope as it addresses a fundamental problem in network analysis with a novel probabilistic approach. While the paper makes a strong technical contribution, the experimental evaluation would be significantly strengthened by more thorough comparisons and analysis of model limitations. The authors should address these concerns in revision to fully demonstrate the value of their approach.

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in scalable network analysis. With minor revisions to strengthen the experimental evaluation, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
