### **Review**

#### **Summary**

This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a technique for learning latent structure in social network data. The approach first preprocesses the network to extract triangle count statistics for each node, creating a representation analogous to a bag-of-words for text documents. The core modeling contribution, inspired by the assorted Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB) and the Mixed-Membership Topic Model (MMTM), is to parameterize a small set of K "roles" based on triangle configurations rather than modeling all K² pairwise community interactions. This parsimonious parameterization reduces the model's complexity, leading to a more scalable inference algorithm. The authors derive a learning procedure based on stochastic variational inference, featuring a novel approximation for the local update rule that enhances scalability.

#### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Scalable Model:** The primary strength of this work is its novel approach to creating a parsimonious mixed-membership model. By focusing on a set of K roles defined by triangle types instead of the O(K²) between-community interaction parameters, the model achieves significant scalability. This is a well-motivated and practical contribution for analyzing large-scale network data where triadic closure is a dominant feature.

2.  **Efficient Inference:** The application of stochastic variational inference is appropriate and well-executed. In particular, the approximation for the local update rule is an intuitive and clever contribution that enables the O(NK) algorithm, making the method practical for large graphs.

3.  **Clear Problem Formulation:** The analogy between counting triangular motifs in a graph and creating a bag-of-words representation from a document is an effective framing that makes the motivation for the model clear and accessible.

#### **Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

While the core idea is promising, the paper could be significantly improved by addressing several issues related to clarity, justification, and experimental evaluation.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**
*   The model is presented as a combination of aMMSB and MMTM. While the connection is clear, the derivation involves considerable bookkeeping, and the presentation could be streamlined to improve reader comprehension.
*   The distinction between the triangle sets Δ¹ and Δ² is not sufficiently clear and requires a more explicit explanation.

**2. Model Claims and Justification:**
*   The paper claims that PTM "is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs." This statement seems overstated. Firstly, Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs) are a much broader class of models that can incorporate a wide variety of network statistics beyond triangles. Secondly, the term "latent space counterpart" is vague; arguably, most latent variable models for networks could be described this way. The authors should either provide a more rigorous justification for this connection or temper the claim.
*   A key element of the scalable inference algorithm is the subsampling of triangles for each node (controlled by δ). However, the paper lacks a clear theoretical or empirical justification for why this provides an adequate approximation.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**
*   **Interpretability:** The experiments focus on quantitative metrics like link prediction but miss an opportunity for qualitative analysis. Are the inferred latent roles interpretable? A case study demonstrating what the model reveals about the structure of a real-world network would significantly strengthen the paper's impact.
*   **Robustness:** The latent space recovery results rely on a threshold of 0.125 to determine a node's "non-zero roles." This value seems arbitrary. An analysis of the model's sensitivity to this threshold is needed to demonstrate the robustness of the findings.
*   **Missing Details:** The experiments involving synthetic power-law networks do not specify which generative model was used. Since various models can produce such networks, this detail is crucial for reproducibility and context.
*   **Performance on Sparse Data:** A major concern is how the model would perform when fewer edges are observed. The preprocessing step relies heavily on triangle counts, which are sensitive to missing edges. The experiments only hold out 10% of the edges for testing. It would be valuable to see how performance degrades as the network becomes sparser, as this is a common real-world scenario.
*   **Counterintuitive Results:** It is surprising that PTM outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated directly from the MMSB model (Figure 1). This result is counterintuitive and requires a more thorough explanation. Is this an artifact of the specific data generation process or evaluation metric, or does it reveal something deeper about the models?

**4. Technical Questions and Suggestions:**
*   **Complexity:** There appears to be a discrepancy regarding the model's complexity. The paper argues for scalability based on O(K) parameters, yet the reported complexity for the PTM Collapsed Gibbs Sampler (CGS) is O(K³). Please clarify why the CGS inference does not benefit from the model's parsimonious parameterization.
*   **Model Specification:** If a primary goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles" for each node, the authors might consider incorporating a more explicit notion of sparsity into the model, for instance through a sparsity-inducing prior on the mixed-membership vectors.
*   **Directed Networks:** Given the prevalence of directed networks in many domains, a discussion of how this triangle-based approach might be extended to directed graphs would be a welcome addition, even as future work.

#### **Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting and scalable mixed-membership blockmodel that cleverly uses triangle counts to define a parsimonious set of latent roles. The modeling choices and the approximations made during inference allow the method to scale to large networks. However, the paper would be stronger with improved clarity in its presentation, better justification for key claims and approximations, and a more thorough experimental evaluation that includes interpretability, robustness checks, and performance analysis on sparser data. Addressing the questions raised above would significantly enhance the quality and impact of this work.
