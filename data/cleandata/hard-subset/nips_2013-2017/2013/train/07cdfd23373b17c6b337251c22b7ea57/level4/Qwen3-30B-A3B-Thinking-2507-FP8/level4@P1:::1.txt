

This paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces the O(K³) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, incorporating additional approximation techniques to enhance scalability. The approach demonstrates competitive accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction while achieving orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over existing methods on large-scale networks.

Quality: The paper makes a technically sound contribution by addressing the scalability challenges of latent space inference for large networks. However, the experimental evaluation lacks crucial elements needed to fully assess the method's advantages. A key question remains unanswered: What practical benefit does scaling with respect to K (the number of latent roles) provide, when mixed-membership models like MMSB and MMTM can often work effectively with relatively small K? To properly evaluate PTM's value, it would be essential to demonstrate that PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K—a comparison that was not conducted. The paper also fails to clearly describe how K was chosen for real-world experiments, potentially introducing bias in the results.

The authors are somewhat vague about the limitations of their simplifying assumptions. While it's clear that parameter constraints enable scalability, the paper doesn't adequately address when PTM might fail. If the true parameter matrix B (in authors' notation) actually contains O(K³) distinct values—as would be the case in some realistic scenarios—PTM should perform poorly. The synthetic data experiments don't sufficiently test this, as they don't explore various B matrices that would favor or challenge the PTM assumptions. It would be far more valuable to readers to understand both when PTM succeeds and when it fails, rather than focusing solely on its successes.

The paper also poorly describes the impact of several key approximations. First, the effect of the δ subsampling threshold isn't properly analyzed. For both synthetic and real-world networks, it's unclear whether larger or smaller δ values are preferable, and how low δ can be pushed before accuracy suffers significantly. Second, the paper claims that a-MMSB's downsampling compromises accuracy, yet doesn't explain why PTM avoids this issue. Both MMTM and PTM ignore single-edge triangles, so the rationale for why this doesn't affect PTM as it does a-MMSB isn't clear. Third, the O(K) approximation to the local update (page 5) is not properly evaluated—how does performance change when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does approximation quality degrade as K increases? Without addressing these questions, it's difficult to fully assess the trade-offs involved.

In summary, I'm unconvinced the experimental setup fairly compares PTM to competitors, and the paper provides insufficient analysis of potential weaknesses in the model and algorithm.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized, with clear technical exposition.

Originality: While the modeling approach represents a modest extension of previous work, the focus on achieving scalability through parameter constraints is a fresh direction in a field that has recently emphasized generalizing existing models rather than optimizing them for scalability.

Significance: If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, this work could have significant practical impact by enabling latent space analysis on networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles—something that remains challenging for most existing methods. However, this potential impact is tempered by the limited evaluation of the model's limitations.

Additional Comments: The title is overly broad. The paper doesn't propose a general scalable approach to latent space inference but rather a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model (PTM). The paper would be strengthened by more careful consideration of the model's limitations and a more thorough comparison across varying values of K. 

The paper's claim that PTM is a "probabilistic method" raises important theoretical questions. While authors compare PTM to the bag-of-words assumption in LDA, this analogy is misleading. In LDA, every bag of words corresponds to a valid document, ensuring a proper likelihood function. In contrast, PTM generates bags of triangles that almost never correspond to actual networks—meaning the vast majority of the posterior probability mass is placed on non-network structures. This undermines the probabilistic foundation of the approach, making it questionable whether PTM should be considered a true probabilistic model. This fundamental issue deserves more careful discussion.
