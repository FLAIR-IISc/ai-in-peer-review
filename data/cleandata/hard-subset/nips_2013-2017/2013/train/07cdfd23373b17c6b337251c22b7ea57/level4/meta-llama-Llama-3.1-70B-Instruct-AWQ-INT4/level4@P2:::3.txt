The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. They develop a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior with very large networks. The paper is well written, and well executed. I think it is acceptable.

The paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The proposed method is based on a succinct representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors argue that their approach is capable of analyzing real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours, which is out of reach for many existing methods.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem of latent space inference in networks and motivate the need for a scalable approach. They also provide a clear explanation of their proposed method, including the representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, the parsimonious statistical model, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm.

The authors evaluate their method on both synthetic and real-world networks and demonstrate its ability to recover latent space roles and predict links. They also compare their method to existing approaches, including the mixed-membership stochastic blockmodel (MMSB) and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM), and show that it is more scalable and accurate.

Overall, I think the paper is well-written and well-executed, and I believe it is acceptable for publication.
