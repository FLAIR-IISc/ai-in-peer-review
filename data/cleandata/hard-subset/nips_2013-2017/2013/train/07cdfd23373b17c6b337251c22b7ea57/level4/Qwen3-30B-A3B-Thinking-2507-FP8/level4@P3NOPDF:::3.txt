

This paper presents a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks, introducing a parameter-sharing subclass that enables efficient inference on large networks via stochastic variational inference. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly written, making a valuable contribution to scalable network modeling. The approach is both novel and practical, and the experimental results demonstrate significant scalability improvements over prior methods. The paper is strongly acceptable for publication.

**Major Comments:**

1.  **Clarify "Parsimonious" Modeling (p1):** The term "parsimonious" is used without definition in the context of probabilistic models, where it is not standard terminology. Please explicitly define this concept upon first use, as it is central to the paper's contribution and the justification for the proposed parameter-sharing subclass. For example: "We define a *parsimonious* model as one that minimizes the number of unique parameters required to capture the latent structure, achieved through strategic parameter sharing."

2.  **Down-Sampling Zeros (p1):** The claim that "down-sampling the zeros does not compromise accuracy" (as per Gopalan et al.) is misleading. Gopalan et al. demonstrate that down-sampling *does not significantly harm accuracy* for their specific task (MMSB inference), but this does not universally apply to all models or inference settings. Revise the statement to accurately reflect that down-sampling is a valid *approximation technique* for the triangle model that maintains sufficient accuracy, as supported by the results presented.

3.  **LDA Analogy (p4):** The defense of the model's generative process by analogy to LDA is inappropriate. While LDA generates documents (even if "silly"), the triangle model's generative process *can produce objects that are not valid networks* (e.g., graphs violating triangle constraints). This is a pragmatic model choice, but the analogy misrepresents the generative process. Remove the LDA comparison or replace it with a more accurate justification for the model's practical utility.

4.  **Citation of Hoffman et al. (p5):** The paper states that Sato's algorithm applies to the scenario with "local variables." However, Sato's work assumes a specific conjugate structure; Hoffman et al.'s stochastic variational inference (SVI) framework is the correct theoretical foundation for the case with local variables (as defined in Hoffman's work). Cite Hoffman et al. (2013) *and* include the relevant theory from Hoffman's paper (which explicitly addresses local variables), not Sato. The reference to Amari & Sato is misplaced here.

5.  **Power Law Generation (p6):** The method for generating power-law networks in the latent space is not sufficiently described. Specify *how* the power-law degree distribution is imposed on the latent network (e.g., via a specific prior, sampling procedure, or parameter choice for the latent graph generation). This detail is crucial for reproducibility and understanding the experimental setup.

6.  **Comparison to Gopalan et al. (p6):** The paper cites Gopalan et al. as a method for speeding up MMSB. To strengthen the empirical comparison, *directly compare* the proposed algorithm against Gopalan et al.'s specific implementation (or a fair reimplementation) for the triangle model, rather than only comparing to batch MMSB. This is essential for establishing the relative advantage of the proposed approach.

**Minor Comments:**

*   p2: Correct "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership".
*   p8: Remove the final sentence; the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger concluding statement.
*   Bibliography: Replace the reference to "Stochastic Variational Inference" with the JMLR version (Hoffman et al., 2013, *JMLR*).

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper addresses a significant challenge in scalable network modeling with a well-executed method. The core technical contributions—especially the clever parameter-sharing subclass enabling SVI for the triangle model—are sound and impactful. The writing is clear and the experiments are relevant. The minor issues identified above are readily addressable and do not undermine the paper's core strengths. The work provides a valuable tool for practitioners dealing with large-scale network data and advances the state-of-the-art in scalable inference for complex network models. The paper is strongly recommended for acceptance.
