This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a modification of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) designed to improve scalability by reducing the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K). The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm with additional approximations to further enhance scalability. Results on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.

**Quality:**

The PTM appears as a reasonable specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of improved scalability with respect to *K* (the number of roles) remains unclear. A key question is whether the ability to handle larger *K* truly adds value, particularly given that mixed-membership models may not suffer from small *K* limitations in the same way as single-membership models. To assess this, experiments demonstrating the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of *K* would be highly valuable. Specifically, showing scenarios where PTM with a large *K* outperforms MMTM with a smaller *K* would strongly justify the approach. This was not done in the current work. Moreover, the method of *K* selection for PTM and the competitor, MMSB, lacks sufficient detail, potentially introducing bias into the experimental results.

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the model’s limitations. While constraining the parameter space achieves scalability, it’s crucial to understand when this simplification might lead to performance degradation. If the true parameter matrix *B* contains many distinct values (as defined in the paper), PTM's performance should suffer; however, the experiments with synthetic data do not adequately explore this. It would be more informative to present results across a range of *B* matrices, including those favorable and unfavorable to PTM, to delineate its strengths and weaknesses. Knowing *when* PTM fails is as important as knowing when it succeeds.

The numerous approximations introduced to improve scalability are interesting and potentially effective, but their impact is poorly analyzed. Specifically: 1) What is the sensitivity of the results to the threshold δ? How does its value impact accuracy and scalability on both real-world and synthetic data? 2) The authors claim a-MMSB is less effective due to downsampling compromising accuracy. What distinguishes PTM from this issue? Both MMTM and PTM discard triplets with only one edge – why is this acceptable for PTM but not for MMSB? 3) What is the effect of the O(K) approximation in the local update step (page 5)? How do different sample sizes (2K, 4K, 100K triples) affect the approximation quality as *K* increases?

In summary, I find the experimental evaluation insufficiently robust and the discussion of the model's potential shortcomings lacking.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized.

**Originality:**

The modeling approach is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability by parameter constraint is an original and promising direction, particularly within a research area currently focused on generalizing existing models.

**Significance:**

If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, this work could significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large networks, an area where scalability is often a major barrier.

**Additional Comments:**

The title may be overly broad, as it suggests a general scalable approach, while the paper focuses on a specific scalable algorithm for a particular model (PTM). 

I also question whether PTM is truly a probabilistic model. The authors draw a parallel to the bag-of-words assumption in LDA, but this analogy is not entirely convincing. LDA generates a bag-of-words from an underlying document, maintaining a proper likelihood function, however simple. In contrast, the bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM rarely corresponds to a valid network. Consequently, a significant portion of the posterior probability mass is assigned to modeling non-networks. Abandoning fundamental principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" modeling is a concern. What is the meaning of probability within the PTM framework? I encourage the authors and other reviewers to consider this point. The paper presents modeling assumptions and inference techniques that enhance MMTM’s scalability, but the consequences of these choices require further investigation.
