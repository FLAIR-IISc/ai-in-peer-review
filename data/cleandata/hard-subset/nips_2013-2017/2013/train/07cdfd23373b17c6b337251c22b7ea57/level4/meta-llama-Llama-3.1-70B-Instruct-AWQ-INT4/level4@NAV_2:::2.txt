This paper presents a novel approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The proposed method leverages a succinct representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. By doing so, the authors demonstrate the ability to analyze real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours, a setting that is out of reach for many existing methods.

The main idea of the paper is to reduce the dimensionality of the parameter space by partitioning the configuration space of latent role triplets into several groups and sharing parameters within the same group. This approach yields a more compact and lower-dimensional model, allowing for more efficient inference algorithms. The authors also develop a fast stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for performing variational inference, which has a time complexity of O(NK).

The paper relates to previous work on latent space models, such as the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB) and the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM). However, the proposed approach improves upon these models by reducing the number of parameters and achieving a more scalable implementation.

The strengths of the paper include the development of a novel modeling approach that combines the advantages of both MMSB and MMTM, the derivation of a global update and local updates for the proposed model using stochastic variational inference, and the demonstration of the method's scalability and accuracy on large real-world networks.

However, there are some concerns and areas for improvement. The presentation of the material could be improved, particularly in the explanation of the novel modeling contribution. The authors should also provide a more detailed discussion of how the methods might extend to directed networks. Additionally, the interpretability of the inferred latent space and the robustness of the results to the threshold used for role recovery are not fully addressed.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of network analysis and latent space modeling. The proposed method has the potential to be widely applicable and to advance the state of the art in this area.

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest about evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of the work.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized, but some sections could be improved for clarity. The authors provide enough information for the expert reader to reproduce the results, but some explanations could be more detailed.

Originality: The paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, and the approach is significantly different from previous contributions. The authors adequately reference related work and demonstrate how their method differs from previous approaches.

Significance: The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The method has the potential to be widely applicable and to advance the state of the art in network analysis and latent space modeling.
