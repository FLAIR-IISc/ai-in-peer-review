This paper introduces the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameterization of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K), enabling faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and propose additional approximations to improve scalability. On synthetic data, they argue that parameter reduction can improve statistical power, and on real networks they report accuracy competitive with existing methods.

Quality:
PTM is an appealing specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of scalability in K (the number of roles) is not clearly demonstrated. For mixed-membership models such as MMSB and MMTM, small K is often less problematic than in single-membership models. To justify PTM’s value, it is essential to show how performance changes with K. A compelling experiment would plot the accuracy of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K; if PTM with large K outperforms MMTM constrained to small K, the advantage would be clear. Unfortunately, such an analysis is missing. Moreover, the paper does not adequately describe how K was selected for PTM or its competitors on real data, raising concerns that model selection could have biased results.

The paper also underplays the consequences of its simplifying assumptions. Constraining the parameter space clearly improves scalability, but when does the model break down? If the true B matrix (in the authors’ notation) truly has O(K^3) distinct values—as allowed by MMSB/MMTM—one would expect PTM to perform poorly. The synthetic experiments do not clearly describe the true B used, and the study does not explore settings both favorable and unfavorable to PTM. A more informative evaluation would vary B to include cases aligned with PTM’s constraints and cases that violate them, clarifying when PTM succeeds or fails.

The scalability tricks are interesting but insufficiently analyzed. In particular:
1) Threshold δ: What is the effect of choosing different δ values? On both synthetic and real data, does a larger or smaller δ help? How far can δ be reduced to gain speed without hurting accuracy?
2) Subsampling and ignored events: The paper claims a-MMSB suffers from downsampling because it reduces accuracy. Why is PTM not similarly affected? MMTM and PTM ignore certain triplets (e.g., those with only one edge). Why is it acceptable to ignore non-edges in PTM but not in MMSB? A principled comparison is needed.
3) O(K) local update approximation (page 5): What is the trade-off as a function of the number of sampled triples? What happens with 2K, 4K, or 100K samples? Does the approximation degrade as K grows?

Overall, I am not convinced the experimental setup is fair to competitors, and the paper does not adequately discuss potential failure modes arising from its modeling and inference approximations.

Clarity:
The paper is well written and well organized. The presentation of the model and inference procedure is generally clear.

Originality:
The modeling contribution is somewhat incremental, but the idea of achieving scalability by constraining the parameter space is timely and interesting. Much recent work has emphasized generalization; emphasizing parsimony to obtain speed is a valuable and original direction.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions underlying the model and the inference algorithm hold in practice, the approach could have meaningful impact on latent-space modeling for networks, where scalability remains a major bottleneck.

Additional Comments:
- The title feels overly broad. There are many scalable approaches to other latent-space models; this paper focuses on a specific scalable model and algorithm (PTM).
- I am not convinced PTM is a fully probabilistic generative model for networks. The authors argue in Section 3 that their approach is analogous to the bag-of-words assumption. However, in LDA a generated bag-of-words always corresponds to some document and thus has a well-defined likelihood, even if the model is simplistic. In contrast, with high probability there is no network consistent with a bag-of-triples produced by MMTM/PTM. As a result, these models are not generative for networks in the usual sense, and much of the posterior mass may correspond to non-networks. This raises concerns about the interpretation of “probability” in PTM and the potential dangers of relaxing likelihood principles in methods presented as probabilistic. I would welcome further justification or discussion from the authors and reviewers.
- In summary, the paper proposes promising modeling and inference ideas that improve scalability, but the empirical validation appears incomplete, the fairness to baselines is questionable, and the side effects of the approximations are insufficiently examined. Providing performance vs. K, transparent model selection procedures, ablations on δ and sampling budgets, and stress tests on adversarial B would substantially strengthen the work.
