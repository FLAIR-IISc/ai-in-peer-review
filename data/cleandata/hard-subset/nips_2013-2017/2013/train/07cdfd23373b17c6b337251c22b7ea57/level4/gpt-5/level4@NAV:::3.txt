The authors present a scalable approximate inference method for the “triangle model” of networks. They introduce a strong subclass that ties parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to approximate the posterior on very large graphs. The paper is clearly written and well executed. I judge it to be acceptable.

p1: What do you mean by “parsimonious” modeling? This isn’t a standard term in the context of probabilistic models (I may be mistaken). Since it is central to the paper, I recommend defining or unpacking it in a sentence.

p1: Based on my reading of Gopalan et al., subsampling the zeros does not degrade accuracy. The sentence in your introduction suggests otherwise and is misleading.

p4: I’m sympathetic to the triangle model from a pragmatic standpoint, but the appeal to LDA is not appropriate. If we run LDA’s generative process, we obtain something that qualifies as a document—even if a contrived one—whereas running your model can yield objects that are not valid networks. This doesn’t bother me—it’s a useful, practical model—but the LDA-based defense is not fair.

p5: You should cite Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato. In your setting, which involves “local variables” (in Hoffman’s terminology), Sato’s algorithm does not apply. The requisite theory is in Hoffman’s work.

p6: How did you construct power-law networks in the latent space? The paper is unclear on this point.

p6: You previously cite Gopalan et al. as a way to accelerate MMSB. You should compare to their algorithm as well as (or instead of) MMSB in batch form.
