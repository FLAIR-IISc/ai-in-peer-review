This paper introduces the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K), enabling faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, along with additional approximation techniques to further improve scalability. On synthetic data, they show that reducing the number of parameters can increase statistical power, and on real-world datasets, PTM is competitive in accuracy with existing methods.

Quality:
PTM is an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical value of scalability in K (the number of roles) is unclear. To assess the benefit, we need to understand: what do we gain by learning MMTM with large K? Because MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may be less problematic than in single-membership models. A compelling experiment would show performance for both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K, that would strongly support the approach. Unfortunately, such experiments are missing. Moreover, the paper does not clearly explain how K was chosen in the real-data experiments for PTM and for the MMSB competitor; the model selection procedure could bias the results.

The paper is also reticent about the impact of its simplifying assumptions. Constraining the parameter space clearly yields scalability, but when does the model fail? If the true MMSB/MMTM parameter matrix B genuinely has O(K^3) distinct values (using the authors’ notation), PTM should perform poorly, correct? In this regard, the choice of the true B in the synthetic experiments is not clearly described. The authors should have tested a range of B matrices—some favorable to PTM’s constraints and some adversarial. It would be more informative to delineate when PTM succeeds and when it fails than to show only when it succeeds.

The paper also introduces several approximation tricks to scale PTM. While these seem interesting and effective, their impact is under-described and unanalyzed:
1) Threshold δ: What is the effect of choosing different δ values? On both synthetic and real data, do larger or smaller δ perform better? How low can δ be set to improve scalability without materially sacrificing accuracy?
2) Downsampling vs. ignoring structures: The authors claim a-MMSB fails because downsampling harms accuracy; why is PTM immune to an analogous issue? MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge. Why is it acceptable to ignore non-edges in PTM but not in MMSB? Please clarify the theoretical or empirical basis for this asymmetry.
3) O(K) local-update approximation (page 5): What is the impact of sampling O(K) triples locally? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does approximation quality degrade as K grows?

Overall, I am not convinced the experimental setup is fair to competing methods, and the paper does not adequately discuss potential weaknesses of the model or inference procedure.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized.

Originality:
The modeling contribution is somewhat incremental, but constraining parameters to achieve scalability is a timely and interesting direction, especially given recent emphasis on model generalization rather than parsimony.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, the work could have substantial impact. Many latent-space network models do not scale to large datasets, and this approach could broaden their applicability.

Additional Comments:
- The title feels too broad. There are other scalable approaches for other latent-space models; this paper proposes a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model (PTM).
- I am not convinced PTM is truly probabilistic in the generative sense claimed. The last paragraph of Section 3 draws an analogy to the bag-of-words assumption, but in LDA any bag of words corresponds to some (possibly unrealistic) document, so there is a proper likelihood. In contrast, with high probability there is no actual network corresponding to a bag of triples generated by MMTM or PTM. Thus, MMTM/PTM is not a generative model for networks in the usual sense, and much of the posterior probability mass may correspond to non-networks. It seems risky to abandon basic likelihood principles in a “probabilistic” method—what does probability mean in PTM under this construction? I would be very interested to hear the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives.
- In summary, the paper proposes modeling assumptions and inference tricks that make MMTM-like inference scalable, but the side-effects and limitations of these choices are insufficiently investigated.
