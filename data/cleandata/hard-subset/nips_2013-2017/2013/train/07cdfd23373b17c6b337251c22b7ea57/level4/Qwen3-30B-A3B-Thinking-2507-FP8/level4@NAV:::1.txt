

This paper introduces the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) from O(K^3) to O(K) for accelerated inference. The authors present a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, supplemented by additional approximation techniques to enhance scalability. Results from synthetic data demonstrate that reducing variables can yield stronger statistical power, while real-world experiments show PTM achieves competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.

Quality:  
PTM represents an intriguing specialization of MMTM, yet its practical advantage remains unclear. The focus on scalability with respect to K (number of roles) lacks justification: to assess value, it must be demonstrated how PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K. For real networks, performance as a function of K should be reported—currently absent. Furthermore, the paper fails to clarify how K was selected for real data experiments involving PTM and its competitor MMSB, risking model selection bias.  

The authors also omit critical discussion of model limitations. If the parameter matrix B in MMSB/MMTM contains O(K^3) distinct values, PTM should fail. However, synthetic experiments do not specify the true B used, nor do they test diverse B matrices (some favorable, some unfavorable to PTM). Readers would benefit more from understanding PTM's failure conditions than solely its success cases.  

Additionally, the paper inadequately describes approximation techniques:  
1) The impact of threshold δ on scalability vs. accuracy is unexplored—does smaller δ improve scalability without significant accuracy loss?  
2) The claim that a-MMSB fails due to downsampling inaccuracies is unaddressed for PTM, which similarly ignores triplets with one edge.  
3) The effect of O(K) approximation on local updates (p.5) is unclear—how do sampling sizes (2K, 4K, 100K triples) affect approximation quality as K grows?  

In summary, the experimental setup is unfair to competitors, and the model's weaknesses are poorly characterized.  

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.  

Originality: While incremental in modeling, the approach of achieving scalability via parameter constraints offers a novel direction, contrasting recent work focused on model generalization.  

Significance: If the model and inference assumptions hold in practice, PTM could significantly advance scalable latent-space network modeling, as few existing methods handle large data.  

Additional Comments: The title is overly broad; PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model, not a universal scalable approach. Crucially, PTM’s probabilistic foundation is questionable. Unlike LDA (which has a valid likelihood for generated bags-of-words), PTM generates "bags-of-triples" that rarely correspond to actual networks. Thus, PTM’s posterior mass concentrates on non-networks, undermining its claim as a generative model. The probabilistic interpretation lacks rigor, and the paper neglects investigating such side effects.
