This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which achieves faster inference by constraining the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K). The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, supplemented by approximation techniques to further enhance its scalability. Experiments on synthetic data suggest this parameter reduction can improve statistical power, while results on real-world datasets show the proposed method’s accuracy is competitive with existing approaches.

**Quality:**
While PTM is an interesting specialization of MMTM, the practical utility of its scalability with respect to K (the number of possible roles) is not clearly established. To empirically justify such a method, it is vital to answer the question: "What is the benefit of learning an MMTM with a large K?" Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K is not necessarily as limiting as it would be in single-membership models. For the real network experiments, a more compelling evaluation would have been to show the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with a large K could be shown to outperform MMTM restricted to a small K, it would powerfully demonstrate the model's value. Unfortunately, such an experiment was not conducted. Furthermore, there is no clear description of how K was chosen for either PTM or its competitor, MMSB, in the real-data experiments; this raises concerns that the model selection method may have biased the results.

The authors are also reticent about the limitations imposed by their simplifying assumptions. It is evident that constraining the parameter space enhances scalability, but it would be beneficial to understand the conditions under which such a model would fail. If the true parameter matrix B (using the authors' notation) genuinely contains O(K^3) distinct values, one would expect PTM to perform poorly. In this light, the synthetic data experiments are unsatisfactory because the construction of the true matrix B is not clearly described. The authors should have experimented with a variety of B matrices—some that favor PTM's assumptions and others that challenge them. It is more valuable for readers to understand the boundaries of a model's applicability—when it succeeds and when it fails—than to only see scenarios where it succeeds.

Additionally, while the authors introduce several interesting and effective tricks to make PTM scalable, the paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing their impact.
1) What is the effect of the threshold δ? In the experiments, does a larger or smaller δ yield better results, and what is the trade-off between scalability and accuracy as δ is varied?
2) The authors argue that a-MMSB is ineffective because downsampling harms accuracy. Why would PTM be exempt from a similar problem, given that it effectively ignores all triplets with only one edge? Why is ignoring non-edges in MMSB not viable, while a similar simplification is acceptable for PTM?
3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to the local update (page 5)? What occurs if 2K, 4K, or 100K triples are sampled instead? Does the quality of this approximation degrade as K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the discussion of the potential weaknesses of the model and algorithm is insufficient.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:**
From a modeling perspective, the work is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction, particularly as much recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

**Significance:**
Provided that the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm are valid in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to large networks, where scalability remains a major challenge.

**Additional Comments:**
I find the title to be overly broad. The authors present a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model, PTM, not a general approach applicable to all latent space models.

I am also uncertain whether PTM qualifies as a true probabilistic method. The authors' justification in the final paragraph of Section 3, comparing it to the bag-of-words assumption, is not convincing. When LDA generates a bag-of-words, there is always a corresponding document, ensuring a proper likelihood function, however simplistic. Conversely, a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM or PTM will, with extremely high probability, not correspond to any valid network graph. This means that MMTM/PTM is not a true generative model for networks, and the vast majority of its posterior probability mass is assigned to modeling non-networks. Abandoning the basic principles of likelihood in what is presented as a "probabilistic" method seems dangerous (what does probability signify in PTM?). I would be interested to hear the authors' and other reviewers' thoughts on this.

The authors propose a set of modeling assumptions and inference tricks to make MMTM scalable. The side-effects of these decisions, however, are poorly investigated.
