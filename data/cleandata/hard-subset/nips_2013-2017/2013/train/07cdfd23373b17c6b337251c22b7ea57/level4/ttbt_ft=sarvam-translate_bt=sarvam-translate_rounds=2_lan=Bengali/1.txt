This paper proposes the Parsimony Triangular Model (PTM), which limits the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Member Triangular Model (MMTM) to a faster estimate. The authors have developed a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and additional estimation techniques to make it more scalable. Synthetic datasets show that reducing the number of variables can lead to stronger statistical performance, and real-world datasets show that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM seems like an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical advantage of achieving better scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) is questionable. To evaluate the value of such a method empirically, it is crucial for us to answer the question: "If we can learn MMTM with a large K, how does it help?" Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-member models, using a small K may not be as cumbersome as a single-member model! It would be really interesting to see the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K for real network testing: if we really see that PTM with a large K surpasses MMTM with a small K, it would show the strength of PTM. Unfortunately, this type of testing has not been done. In fact, I have not received good details about how K was chosen in real data tests for both PTM and its competitor MMSB; model selection methods can influence experimental results.

The authors are somewhat silent about the impact of the simplification assumptions they made. It is clear that we can achieve better scalability by limiting the parameter space, but it would be good to hear about when such models will fail. If the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM (following the authors' notation) really has an O(K^3) independent value, then PTM will work poorly, right? In this regard, the choice of true B in the synthetic data test is not clearly described, which is disappointing; the authors should have tested with different B, some in favor of PTM and some against the model. Readers will be more beneficial to know when PTM will succeed and when it will fail, rather than just knowing when it will succeed.

On the other hand, the authors have introduced several techniques to scale PTM; although I find them very interesting and effective, this paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of such assumptions. 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold \delta? In real-world and synthetic data tests, is it helpful to choose a large or small \delta? How much \delta can we reduce without sacrificing too much accuracy so that scalability can be improved? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy; why should PTM be free from this problem? MMTM and PTM actually only ignore one edge-to-edge triplet. Why can't we ignore the non-edges in MMSB, where we can in PTM? 3) What is the impact of O(K) assumptions for local updates (on page 5)? What if we sample a 2K, 4K, or 100K triplet? Will the quality of the assumptions be compromised if K is large?

In summary, I am not sure that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm/model is weak.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and organized.

**Originality:** It is somewhat incremental in terms of modeling, but achieving scalability by limiting parameters is an interesting and original aspect, as much work has been done recently on generalizing existing models on this subject.

**Importance:** If the simplification assumptions in the model and inference algorithm can be realistically captured, it will have a good impact on the practice of latent-space models in networks, because too much of them actually do not scale to large data.

**Additional Comments:** Personally, I think the title is too broad. There are other scalable methods for other latent space models, and the authors have proposed a scalable algorithm for a latent space model - PTM.

Also, I am not sure that PTM is really a promising method. In the last paragraph of section 3, the authors argue that it is similar to word-bag assumptions, but I am not sure. When LDA creates a word-bag, there is always a document consistent with it; so it has the correct probability function, no matter how unrealistically simple it is. On the other hand, there is no network with a word-bag of a triplet created by MMTM and PTM with a very high probability consistency. Therefore, MMTM/PTM is not actually a generative model for networks, and most backward probability weights are placed on non-network modeling! Abandoning the fundamental principles of probability in the "probability" method can be dangerous (what does probability mean in PTM?)... I am curious what the authors and other reviewers think. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference techniques that will make the assumption scalable over MMTM. However, the side effects of such decisions are poorly investigated.
