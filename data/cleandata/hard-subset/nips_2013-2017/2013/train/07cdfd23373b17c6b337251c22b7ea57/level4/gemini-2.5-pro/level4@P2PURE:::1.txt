This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a specialization of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) that reduces the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, incorporating additional approximations to further enhance its scalability. Experiments on synthetic data suggest that this parameter reduction can lead to greater statistical power, while results on real-world datasets demonstrate that PTM is competitive with existing methods in terms of predictive accuracy.

**Quality:**

While PTM is an interesting specialization of MMTM, the paper's central motivation—scalability in K (the number of roles)—is not sufficiently justified. A critical missing element is an empirical demonstration of the practical benefits of learning a model with a large K. The authors do not address why large K is necessary, especially since mixed-membership models like MMSB and MMTM can often capture complex network structures even with a relatively small K, unlike single-membership models.

A compelling experiment would have been to compare the performance of PTM and MMTM as a function of K. Demonstrating that PTM with a large K can outperform an MMTM restricted to a small K (due to computational constraints) would have provided strong evidence for PTM's utility. This crucial experiment was not included. Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear description of how K was selected for PTM and its competitor, MMSB, in the real-world experiments. This omission is significant, as the model selection procedure could introduce bias into the experimental comparison.

The paper is also silent on the limitations imposed by its simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space clearly improves scalability, a discussion of the scenarios where the PTM model would fail is necessary. For instance, if the true interaction tensor B contains O(K^3) distinct values, as is possible in the full MMTM, PTM would presumably perform poorly. This concern is compounded by the lack of detail regarding the true parameter matrix B used in the synthetic experiments. To properly characterize the model, the authors should have experimented with various structures for B, including cases that are favorable to PTM's assumptions and cases that violate them. Understanding both the strengths and weaknesses of the model is more valuable to the reader than only seeing scenarios where it succeeds.

Similarly, the paper introduces several effective approximations to make the inference algorithm scalable, but it fails to adequately analyze their impact:
1.  The impact of the threshold δ is not explored. How does its value affect the trade-off between scalability and accuracy? An analysis of this sensitivity is needed.
2.  The paper argues against downsampling for methods like a-MMSB, claiming it compromises accuracy. However, PTM's approach of ignoring triplets with only one edge is itself a form of data subsampling. The justification for why this is acceptable for PTM but not for MMSB is not convincing.
3.  The impact of the O(K) approximation for the local variational updates (page 5) is not analyzed. How does the quality of this approximation change if more triples are sampled (e.g., 2K or 4K)? Does its performance degrade as K increases?

In summary, the experimental evaluation is not convincing due to a potential lack of fairness to competing methods and an insufficient analysis of the model's and algorithm's potential weaknesses.

**Clarity:**
The paper is well-organized and clearly written.

**Originality:**
While the model itself is an incremental extension, the approach of achieving scalability by constraining the parameter space is an interesting and original direction, especially as much recent work has focused on model generalization.

**Significance:**
If the model's simplifying assumptions and the inference approximations hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact, as few latent space network models are truly scalable to large datasets.

**Additional Comments:**
The title seems overly broad. The paper presents a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model (PTM), not a general approach applicable to all.

A more fundamental concern is whether PTM can be considered a true probabilistic model of networks. The authors draw an analogy to the bag-of-words assumption in topic modeling, but this comparison may be flawed. For any bag of words generated by a model like LDA, there exists a corresponding document. In contrast, a "bag-of-triples" generated by PTM or MMTM will, with very high probability, not correspond to any valid network (i.e., the collection of triples is not realizable as a graph). This implies that these are not true generative models for networks and that their posterior distributions place the majority of their probability mass on objects that are not networks. This departure from a well-defined likelihood over the object of interest (graphs) is a potentially problematic deviation from standard probabilistic modeling principles, and it raises questions about the interpretation of the model's probabilities.
