This research paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which limits the O(K) parameter space of mixed-membership triangular models (MMTM) to O(K), enabling faster estimation. The authors develop a stochastic variance estimation algorithm for PTM and additional approximation tricks to make it more scalable. It has been shown from synthetic datasets that reducing the number of variables can provide strong statistical power, and from real-world datasets, the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) is questionable. To empirically evaluate the value of such a method, it is important for us to answer, "How does it help if we can learn MMTM with a large K?" Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may not be as difficult as in single-membership models! For real network experiments, it would be really interesting to see the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K: if we can actually see that PTM performs better than MMTM with a small K, it would show the power of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. In fact, I was unable to find good details about how K was chosen for both PTM and its competitor MMSB in real data experiments; the model selection method could have biased the experimental results.

The authors are also a bit reluctant to discuss the impact of the simplified assumptions they make. It is clear that we can achieve good scalability by limiting the parameter space, but it would also be good to hear when such a model will fail. If the parameter matrices B of MMSB and MMTM (according to the authors' notation) actually have O(K^3) distinct values, then PTM should perform poorly, right? In this regard, it is unsatisfactory that the choice of true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described; the authors should have experimented with different B, some of which are in favor of PTM and some against the model. It would be more beneficial for readers to know when PTM will succeed and when it will fail, rather than just knowing when it will succeed.

On the other hand, the authors offer several tricks to scale PTM; while I find them very interesting and effective, this paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of such approximations. 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold \delta? On real-world and synthetic data experiments, is it helpful to choose a large or small \delta? How much can we reduce \delta to improve scalability without sacrificing too much accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy; why would PTM be free from such a problem? MMTM and PTM are actually only ignoring a biased triangle. Why can't we ignore non-bias in MMTB while we can in PTM? 3) What is the impact of O(K) approximation for local updates (in page 5)? What happens if we sample the triangles at 2K, 4K, or 100K? Will the quality of the approximation be affected as K gets larger?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was appropriate for competitors, and the description of the potential weakness of the algorithm/model is poor.

**Clarity:** The research paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:** It is a bit incremental in terms of modeling, but achieving scalability by limiting the parameter is an interesting and original direction to follow, as much work has recently been focused on generalizing existing models in this area.

**Importance:** If simplified assumptions in the model and estimation algorithm remain in practice, it will have a good impact on the practice of latent-place models on the network, as many of them do not actually reach large data.

**Additional Notes:** Personally, I think the title is too broad. There are other scalable approaches for other latent-place models, and the authors propose a scalable algorithm for a latent-place model – PTM.

Also, I am not sure that PTM is really a probabilistic method. The authors argue in the last paragraph of Section 3 that this is similar to the word-bag assumption, but I am not sure. When LDA generates a word-bag, there is always a corresponding document; therefore it has a proper probability function, no matter how unrealistically simple it is. On the other hand, there is no network corresponding to the triangle's bag generated by MMTM and PTM with very high probability. Therefore MMTM/PTM is not really a generative model for the network, and most of the posterior probability mass is placed on modeling the non-network! Abandoning the basic principles of probability in "probabilistic" methods can be dangerous (what does probability mean in PTM?)... I am curious what the authors and other reviewers think. The authors propose modeling assumptions and estimation tricks that will make estimation scalable over MMTM. However, the side effects of such a decision are poorly investigated.
