The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the “triangle model” of networks. They develop a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior with very large networks. The paper is well written and well executed. I believe it is acceptable.

p1: What is “parsimonious” modeling? I don't believe this is a common expression in the context of probabilistic models. (I might be wrong about that.) I suggest unpacking that term in a sentence since it is important to the paper.
p1: From my reading of Gopalan et al., down-sampling the zeros does not compromise accuracy. Your sentence in the introduction is misleading.
p4: I'm sympathetic to the triangle model being acceptable from a pragmatic perspective, but I don't think appealing to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is a fair comparison. If we run the generative process of LDA, we will obtain something that “is” a document, albeit a trivial one, even if it’s not the original. Running your model, however, can produce objects that are not networks. Again, this does not bother me – it's a good and practical model – but the defense by means of LDA is not appropriate.
p5: You should cite Hoffman et al. along with Amari and Sato. In your scenario, where there are “local variables” (in Hoffman’s language), Sato’s algorithm does not apply. The necessary theory is in Hoffman et al.
p6: How did you generate power-law networks in the latent space? The paper is vague regarding this point.
p6: Earlier you cite Gopalan et al. as a way of speeding up MMSB. You should compare to their algorithm, in addition to (or instead of) MMSB batch.

The authors present a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks. By leveraging a succinct bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm, they demonstrate the ability to analyze networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours. This represents a significant improvement over existing methods. The paper is technically sound and well-executed, achieving competitive or improved accuracy while offering substantial speedups compared to state-of-the-art probabilistic approaches.

The introduction effectively motivates the problem and highlights the limitations of existing methods in scaling to large networks. The authors clearly articulate the three principles crucial for large-scale inference – succinct network representation, parsimonious statistical modeling, and scalable inference algorithms – and position their work within this framework. 

The core contribution of the paper lies in the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). The reduction in the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K<sup>3</sup>) to O(K) through parameter sharing is a clever and impactful design choice. The development of a fast stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm further enhances the scalability of the approach.

The experimental results are compelling. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both synthetic and real-world networks, showing significant speedups compared to existing algorithms like MMSB and MMTM.  The convergence analysis and the demonstration of inference on a 1M-node YouTube social network are particularly noteworthy. 

However, a few points could be clarified. The term “parsimonious” modeling (p1) could benefit from a brief explanation, particularly for readers unfamiliar with this terminology in the context of probabilistic modeling. The statement regarding the accuracy of downsampling zeros in Gopalan et al. (p1) appears to be a misinterpretation of their work and should be revised. The analogy to LDA to justify the generative process (p4) feels weak, as the output of LDA is demonstrably a document, while the output of the triangle model isn’t necessarily a valid network.  Finally, the reference to stochastic gradient ascent should include Hoffman et al. (JMLR) alongside Amari and Sato, as the former provides the relevant theory for the described setting with “local variables”. The explanation for generating power-law networks in the latent space (p6) could also be more detailed. A comparison with Gopalan et al.’s algorithm, rather than just MMSB batch, would be beneficial.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The method demonstrably scales to networks with over a million vertices, a significant advance over existing approaches.
*   **Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm is orders of magnitude faster than state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Accuracy:** The method achieves competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a well-reasoned and technically sound approach.
*   **Novelty:** The parameter sharing strategy in the PTM and the application of stochastic natural gradient ascent are novel contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor clarifications are needed regarding the definition of "parsimonious" modeling, the interpretation of Gopalan et al.’s work, and the justification for the generative process.
*   A more detailed explanation of power-law network generation and a comparison with Gopalan et al.’s algorithm would strengthen the experimental evaluation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of network analysis. The proposed method addresses a critical challenge – scalability – and offers a compelling solution. The minor issues raised above can be readily addressed, and I recommend acceptance.
