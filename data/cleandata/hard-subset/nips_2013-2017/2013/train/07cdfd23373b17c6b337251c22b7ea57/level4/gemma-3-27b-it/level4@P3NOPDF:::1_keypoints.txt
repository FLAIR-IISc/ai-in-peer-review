- PTM constrains MMTM to reduce parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) and uses stochastic variational inference with extra approximations for scalability.  
- Practical benefit of scalability w.r.t. K is unclear; need experiments showing PTM enables much larger K and outperforms MMTM with small K.  
- Chosen K values in real-data comparisons (PTM vs. MMSB) are not justified, risking biased evaluation.  
- Authors under-discuss limitations: PTM will likely fail when the true B matrix has many distinct values (approaching O(K^3)).  
- Synthetic experiments are insufficiently diverse and appear tailored to favor PTM; need adversarial B matrices as well.  
- Approximation techniques lack analysis: sensitivity to threshold δ and the scalability–accuracy trade-off are not studied.  
- Unclear why PTM tolerates downsampling while MMSB supposedly does not; need explanation why edge-dropping strategies wouldn’t help MMSB.  
- Impact of the O(K) local-update approximation is unexplored; experiments varying sampled triples (2K, 4K, 100K) are needed to assess quality as K grows.  
- Overall experimental setup may be unfair to competing methods and doesn’t thoroughly address PTM’s weaknesses.  
- Paper is well-written and logically organized (clarity is good).  
- Modeling contribution is incremental but interesting: constraining parameters for scalability is a valuable direction.  
- If PTM’s assumptions hold in practice, it could significantly affect scalable latent-space modeling for large networks.  
- Title is too broad; it should state the method is a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model (PTM), not a universal solution.  
- Doubts about PTM’s probabilistic interpretation: the bag-of-triples analogy is unconvincing and PTM may assign posterior mass to non-networks, undermining likelihood-based meaning.  
- Proposed modeling assumptions and inference tricks improve scalability, but their practical consequences are not thoroughly investigated.
