

This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) and stochastic variational inference. The authors address three key scalability challenges in probabilistic latent space modeling: (1) succinct network representation, (2) parsimonious statistical modeling, and (3) efficient inference algorithms. They build upon the bag-of-triangles representation [8], which significantly reduces network representation from O(N²) to O(Nδ²), and develop a PTM that reduces the triangle-generating parameter dimension from O(K³) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) [8] to O(K) through parameter sharing. This enables their stochastic variational inference algorithm to achieve O(NK) time complexity, making it feasible to analyze networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in hours.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real networks. The authors show that their approach achieves competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction compared to state-of-the-art methods [5, 8], while reducing runtime by orders of magnitude. For example, on a 1M-node YouTube social network with 100 latent roles, their method converges in 4 hours using a single machine with 8 threads, whereas previous methods would require days or weeks. The theoretical analysis is sound, with proper justification for the parameter sharing scheme and the structured mean-field approximation, which outperforms naive mean-field in practice.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-organized presentation and effective use of examples to explain complex concepts. The authors clearly articulate the generative process of the PTM and the rationale behind their parameter sharing strategy. The algorithm description is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility, and the experimental setup is transparent with appropriate baselines. The figures effectively illustrate convergence behavior and performance comparisons.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by addressing all three scalability challenges simultaneously, whereas previous work [1, 5, 8] typically focuses on only one or two. The parameter sharing scheme for the triangle-generating parameters is novel and directly addresses the O(K³) bottleneck in MMTM. While the bag-of-triangles representation [8] and stochastic variational inference [10] have precedents, the specific combination and application to latent space modeling for large networks represents a significant advancement.

The significance of this work is substantial. Large-scale network analysis is increasingly important in social science, biology, and information systems, but existing methods struggle with networks beyond 100,000 nodes. The authors demonstrate that their approach enables latent space analysis on networks with millions of nodes, opening new possibilities for research and applications. Their method achieves this without sacrificing accuracy, as evidenced by their link prediction experiments on real networks where their triangle-based approach outperforms edge-based MMSB.

Several minor issues could be addressed to strengthen the paper. On page 1, the term "parsimonious" is used without sufficient explanation; while the authors define it through their modeling approach, a brief clarification would benefit readers unfamiliar with the term in this context. The claim that "down-sampling zero elements in the matrix" (referring to a-MMSB [5]) does not compromise accuracy is misleading; Gopalan et al. [5] explicitly state that their method maintains accuracy through careful down-sampling, so the phrasing should be revised to acknowledge this.

The defense of the model's limitations by comparing to LDA (page 4) is not appropriate. While the authors correctly note that their model does not generate valid networks, the LDA comparison is unhelpful since LDA's generative process produces valid documents. A more constructive approach would be to acknowledge this limitation and emphasize the model's utility for inference rather than generation.

On page 5, the authors should cite Hoffman et al. [10] (not Amari and Sato) for the stochastic variational inference framework, as the latter does not apply to the local variables in their model. Additionally, the description of how they generated power-law networks in the latent space (page 6) is too vague; a brief technical specification would strengthen the experimental setup.

The paper would benefit from a more direct comparison with Gopalan et al.'s a-MMSB [5], the most relevant scalable method. While the authors mention this method in the introduction, the experimental comparisons should include it as a primary baseline rather than relying primarily on MMSB batch variational.

The writing is generally strong, but I noticed a minor typo ("mixture-membership" should be "mixed-membership" on page 2) and the last sentence of the conclusion could be removed as it's redundant with the second-to-last sentence.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to scalable network analysis. The technical approach is sound, the experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods. The paper is well-written and makes a compelling case for the importance of their approach. While there are minor issues to address, they do not detract from the paper's overall quality or contribution. The work advances the state of the art in network modeling by enabling latent space inference on networks orders of magnitude larger than previously feasible, with competitive or improved accuracy.

I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a critical challenge in network analysis with a technically sound and practically valuable solution. The authors have clearly demonstrated the significance of their work through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation on large-scale networks. This is exactly the kind of work that advances the field and would be of interest to the NIPS community.
