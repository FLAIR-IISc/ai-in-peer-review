This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks, building upon the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) and stochastic variational inference. The authors introduce a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces the O(K^3) parameter space of MMTM to O(K) for faster inference. The PTM assumes a generative process for a bag of triangular motifs, where each vertex can take a mixture distribution over K latent roles. The authors then develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, which has a time complexity of O(NK).

Quality:
The PTM seems to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but it is questionable what the practical advantage of achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) is. The authors demonstrate that the reduction of the number of variables may lead to stronger statistical power on synthetic datasets, and that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy on real-world datasets. However, it is unclear how the PTM would perform if the true parameter matrix B of MMTM has O(K^3) distinct values. The choice of true B in synthetic data experiments is not clearly described, and the authors should have experimented with various B's that are both in favor of and against the PTM.

The authors also introduce several approximation tricks to make PTM scale, including the use of a mixture-of-deltas variational distribution and stochastic natural gradient ascent. However, the impact of these approximations is not thoroughly analyzed. For example, the effect of choosing the threshold δ on real-world and synthetic data experiments is not discussed, and it is unclear how much δ can be lowered to improve scalability without sacrificing accuracy.

Clarity:
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm, and the text is easy to follow.

Originality:
In terms of modeling, the PTM is a bit incremental, but achieving scalability via constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction to pursue. Recently, much work has focused on generalizing existing models, and the authors' approach is a welcome change.

Significance:
Provided the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm hold in practice, the PTM would have a good impact on the practice of latent-space models on networks, as not many of them actually scale to large data.

Additional Comments:
The title of the paper is too broad, as there are other scalable approaches to other latent space models. The authors propose a scalable algorithm to a specific latent space model, PTM.

The PTM is not a generative model of networks per se, as it does not model empty and single-edge motifs, and the generative process does not force overlapping triangles to have consistent edge values. The authors argue that this is similar in spirit to the bag-of-words assumption, but it is unclear what the implications of this are for the interpretation of the results. The majority of posterior probability mass is placed on modeling non-networks, which may be problematic.

Arguments For Acceptance:
* The PTM is a novel and interesting specialization of MMTM that achieves good scalability in terms of K.
* The stochastic variational inference algorithm is well-suited for large-scale networks.
* The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the PTM on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

Arguments Against Acceptance:
* The practical advantage of achieving good scalability in terms of K is unclear.
* The impact of the approximation tricks is not thoroughly analyzed.
* The PTM is not a generative model of networks per se, which may be problematic.

Recommendation:
I recommend acceptance of the paper, but with revisions to address the concerns mentioned above. The authors should provide a clearer explanation of the practical advantage of achieving good scalability in terms of K, and they should thoroughly analyze the impact of the approximation tricks. Additionally, the authors should discuss the implications of the PTM not being a generative model of networks per se.
