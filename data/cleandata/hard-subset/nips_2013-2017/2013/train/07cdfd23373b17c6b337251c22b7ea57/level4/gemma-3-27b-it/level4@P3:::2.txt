This paper proposes a scalable approach to latent space inference in large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (the Parsimonious Triangular Model or PTM), and a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The core idea is to move beyond edge-based representations (like those used in MMSB and a-MMSB) to triangle-based representations, allowing for scalability to networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of latent roles. The PTM reduces the parameter space by sharing parameters across latent roles, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm further enhances scalability via mini-batching and a structured mean-field approximation. The authors demonstrate significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art methods on both synthetic and real-world networks, with competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction.

This work builds upon existing research in probabilistic network modeling, particularly the mixed-membership stochastic blockmodel (MMSB) [Airoldi et al., 2008] and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) [Ho et al., 2013]. The contribution lies in combining the succinct triangle representation of MMTM with the parameter reduction techniques of a-MMSB, resulting in a model and algorithm that can scale to significantly larger networks than previously possible. While the core ideas—triangle counts, parameter sharing, stochastic variational inference—are not entirely novel in isolation, their integration into a cohesive and scalable framework represents a valuable contribution.

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its scalability. The authors convincingly demonstrate the ability to analyze networks with over a million vertices in a reasonable timeframe, a feat beyond the reach of many existing methods.
*   **Novel Combination:** The combination of a triangle-based representation with a parsimonious model and stochastic variational inference is a sensible and effective approach to addressing the scalability challenges in latent space network analysis.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the modeling choices, particularly the parameter sharing scheme in the PTM, which reduces the model complexity from O(K<sup>3</sup>) to O(K).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world networks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, both in terms of accuracy and runtime performance.
*   **Clear Presentation of Algorithm:** The description of the stochastic variational inference algorithm, including the approximate local update rule, is well-explained and provides sufficient detail for implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Clarity of Motivation:** The statement that the PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is unclear and potentially misleading. While both models deal with network structure, their approaches and interpretations are quite different. Expanding on this comparison and clarifying the relationship would be beneficial.
*   **Interpretability:** The paper lacks a discussion of the interpretability of the inferred latent spaces. While the authors demonstrate the ability to recover latent roles, they do not provide examples of what these roles represent in the context of the analyzed networks, limiting the practical utility of the results.
*   **Subsampling Justification:** The justification for the delta-subsampling procedure could be more thorough. While the authors mention it preserves the distribution of triangles, quantifying the impact of subsampling on accuracy would be helpful.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to the threshold of 0.125 used for latent space recovery is not adequately addressed. Exploring the impact of different thresholds would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Synthetic Data Details:** More detail about the power-law network generator used in the experiments would be useful for reproducibility and comparison with other methods.
* **Inconsistency:** The complexity of PTM CGS stated in the paper is inconsistent with the claim of O(K) parameters. It should be corrected.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Significant scalability improvements over existing methods.
*   Sound theoretical foundation and clear algorithmic description.
*   Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Addresses a relevant and challenging problem in network analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Some lack of clarity in the motivation and explanation of certain modeling choices.
*   Limited discussion of the interpretability of the inferred latent spaces.
*   Minor concerns regarding parameter sensitivity and the justification for subsampling.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in network analysis with a well-motivated and effective approach. The scalability improvements achieved by the authors are significant and represent a valuable contribution to the field. While there are some minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the clarity and interpretability concerns outlined above.
