- Introduces a method to discover latent structure from triangle-count statistics, treating triangles like a bag-of-words.  
- Central idea: use triangle configurations to define K roles (inspired by aMMSB) to reduce parameters and improve scalability.  
- Uses stochastic variational inference, deriving both global and local updates for the proposed model.  
- Modeling novelty is mainly a direct combination of aMMSB and MMTM and involves tedious bookkeeping; presentation needs strengthening.  
- Much of the learning algorithm is a straightforward SVI application, but the paper proposes an intuitive approximation for the local update.  
- Lacks discussion of extending the method to ubiquitous directed networks.  
- Claim that PTM is a “latent space counterpart to ERGMs” is misleading given ERGMs’ broader statistics and ambiguous use of “latent space counterpart.”  
- Unclear whether the inferred latent spaces are interpretable and what they reveal about graph similarities/differences.  
- The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained.  
- No justification shown for why subsampling triangles per node (using delta) yields an adequate approximation.  
- If the goal is to recover which roles are non-zero, the model could benefit from a more explicitly binary role formulation.  
- Unclear sensitivity of latent-space recovery to the chosen 0.125 threshold.  
- The paper does not specify which latent-space model producing power-law networks was used in experiments.  
- Questionable complexity claim: why PTM CGS is K^3 when the model purportedly has O(K) parameters.  
- Concern about performance when fewer edges are observed, since experiments only hold out 10% of edges and preprocessing may bias triangle distribution.  
- Counterintuitive result: PTM outperforming MMSB on synthetic networks generated by MMSB.  
- Overall: the approach learns a mixed-membership blockmodel from triangle counts and achieves a more scalable O(NK) algorithm.
