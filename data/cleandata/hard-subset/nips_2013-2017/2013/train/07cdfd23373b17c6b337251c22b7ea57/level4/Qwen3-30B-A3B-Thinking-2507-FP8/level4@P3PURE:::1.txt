

Review  
This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which constrains the O(K³) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to enhance scalability. Experiments on synthetic data demonstrate that reducing the number of parameters can yield stronger statistical power, while real-world network experiments show competitive accuracy relative to existing methods.  

**Quality**  
PTM represents an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical advantage of achieving scalability in terms of K (the number of roles) is questionable. To evaluate the method’s value, it is critical to address: "How does PTM help if MMTM can be learned with large K?" Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K is less problematic than in single-membership models. For real network experiments, it would be compelling to compare the performance of MMTM (with small K) and PTM (with large K). If PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K, this would demonstrate PTM’s power. Unfortunately, such experiments are absent. Moreover, the paper lacks clarity on how K was selected for real data experiments with PTM and its competitor MMSB, risking biased results due to unaddressed model selection.  

The authors also inadequately discuss the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While scalability is achieved by constraining the parameter space, it is essential to understand when PTM fails. If the true parameter matrix B (in authors’ notation) contains O(K³) distinct values—as in MMSB/MTM—PTM should perform poorly. Yet, the synthetic data experiments do not clarify the choice of B. Testing with diverse B matrices (some favoring PTM, others not) would benefit readers more than solely demonstrating PTM’s success.  

The paper introduces several approximation tricks for scalability but insufficiently describes and analyzes their impact:  
1. What is the effect of threshold δ? Does a larger or smaller δ improve scalability without sacrificing accuracy? How small can δ be set while maintaining accuracy?  
2. The authors claim a-MMSB fails due to downsampling, yet PTM avoids this issue. Why? Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge—why can non-edges be ignored in PTM but not in MMSB?  
3. What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates (p. 5)? How does performance degrade when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples as K increases?  

In summary, the experimental setup is unfair to competitors, and the analysis of the model’s limitations is inadequate.  

**Clarity**  
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized.  

**Originality**  
The modeling approach is somewhat incremental, but the direction of achieving scalability via parameter constraints is original and valuable, especially given recent trends toward model generalization in this domain.  

**Significance**  
If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, PTM could significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large-scale networks, as few existing methods scale effectively.  

**Additional Comments**  
The title is overly broad; PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model, not a general scalable approach.  

Furthermore, PTM’s status as a probabilistic method is questionable. The authors argue it aligns with the bag-of-words assumption in LDA, but this is unpersuasive. LDA generates valid documents (with proper likelihood), whereas PTM’s "bag-of-triples" almost surely corresponds to non-networks. Consequently, the posterior places most mass on modeling non-networks, violating fundamental principles of generative models. The meaning of "probability" in PTM remains unclear, and the paper fails to address this critical issue. While the authors propose useful modeling assumptions and inference tricks, the side-effects of these choices are poorly investigated.
