- Introduces a model that learns latent structure from triangle count statistics (bag-of-words analogy), identifying K roles via triangle configurations instead of modeling all between-community interactions.  
- Modeling innovation, inspired by aMMSB, reduces parameterization and enables a scalable implementation using stochastic variational inference with derived global and local updates.  
- Core contribution is a straightforward combination of aMMSB with MMTM, but the presentation needs improvement due to tedious bookkeeping.  
- Learning largely applies standard SVI; authors propose an intuitive approximation for the local update rule.  
- Lacks discussion of extending the method to directed networks despite their prevalence.  
- The claim that PTM is a “latent space counterpart to ERGMs” is problematic: ERGMs include many statistics beyond triangles and the “counterpart” notion is unclear.  
- Unclear whether inferred latent-space embeddings are interpretable or what insights they provide about graph similarities/differences.  
- The distinction between Δ^1 and Δ^2 is not explained.  
- No justification that subsampling triangles per node using δ is an adequate approximation.  
- If the goal is recovering a “set of non-zero roles,” the model should enforce a stronger binary notion of role membership.  
- Latent-space recovery results hinge on an arbitrary threshold (0.125); robustness to this choice is not assessed.  
- Experimental setup does not specify which latent-space model was used, though multiple models can generate power-law networks.  
- Complexity mismatch: PTM CGS shows K^3 complexity despite claims of O(K) parameters.  
- Experiments are concerning under sparse conditions: only 10% of edges held out, likely because removing more edges biases adjacent triangle distributions.  
- Counterintuitive result: PTM outperforming MMSB on synthetic networks generated by MMSB (Figure 1, left panels).  
- Overall contribution: applies SVI to learn a restricted mixed-membership blockmodel on triangle counts, yielding a more scalable O(NK) algorithm.
