The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. They develop a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior with very large networks. The paper is well written, and well executed. I think it is acceptable.

The authors' main contribution is a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. They propose a succinct representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

The authors provide a thorough discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach, addressing the criteria described in the qualitative evaluation section. They also provide a list of arguments pro and con acceptance, which is particularly useful for the program committee.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to scale to large networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of latent roles. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several large real networks, achieving competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction.

The authors also provide a clear and concise summary of the main ideas of the paper, relating them to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. They demonstrate a good understanding of the relevant literature and provide appropriate citations.

However, there are a few minor issues with the paper. On page 1, the authors use the term "parsimonious" modeling without explaining it. It would be helpful to unpack this term in a sentence, as it is important to the paper. Additionally, the authors' sentence about down-sampling zeros in the introduction is misleading, as it does not accurately reflect the findings of Gopalan et al.

On page 4, the authors appeal to LDA to defend their model, but this analogy is not entirely fair. While LDA can generate documents that are not the original, the generative process of the triangle model can produce objects that are not networks. This does not bother me, as the model is still practical and useful, but the defense by means of LDA is not entirely appropriate.

On page 5, the authors should cite Hoffman et al. with Amari and Sato. In their scenario, where there are "local variables" (in Hoffman's language), Sato's algorithm does not apply. The needed theory is in Hoffman.

On page 6, the authors should provide more details about how they generated power-law networks in the latent space. The paper is vague around this point. Additionally, the authors should compare their algorithm to Gopalan et al.'s algorithm, in addition to (or instead of) MMSB batch.

Overall, the paper is well-written and well-executed, and I think it is acceptable. The authors provide a clear and concise summary of their approach, demonstrate its effectiveness on large networks, and provide a thorough discussion of its strengths and weaknesses.
