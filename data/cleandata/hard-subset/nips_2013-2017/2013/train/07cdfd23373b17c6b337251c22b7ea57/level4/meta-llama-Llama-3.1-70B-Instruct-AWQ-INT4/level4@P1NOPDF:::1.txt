This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation tricks to make it further scalable. Experiments on synthetic datasets demonstrate that reducing the number of variables can lead to stronger statistical power, while experiments on real-world datasets show that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

Quality:
Although PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, it is debatable whether achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) offers significant practical advantages. To empirically evaluate the value of such a method, it is crucial to address the question of how it benefits from learning MMTM with large K. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may not be as problematic as it is in single-membership models. In real network experiments, it would be more informative to compare the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. Specifically, demonstrating that PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K would showcase the power of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear description of how K was chosen in real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB, which raises concerns about potential biases in the experimental results.

The authors are also somewhat reticent about the impact of the simplifying assumptions they make. While it is clear that constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it would be beneficial to discuss the scenarios in which such a model may fail. For instance, if there are indeed O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly. In this regard, it is unsatisfactory that the choice of true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described. The authors should have experimented with various B's, some of which favor PTM and others that are against the model. Providing insights into when PTM would succeed and when it would fail would be more valuable to readers than only demonstrating its successes.

On the other hand, the authors introduce several tricks to make PTM scalable, which I find interesting and effective. However, the paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. Specifically, it would be helpful to know: 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold δ? Does it help to choose larger or smaller δ in real-world and synthetic data experiments? How much can δ be lowered to improve scalability without sacrificing accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy, but why would PTM be immune to this problem? Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge. Why can't non-edges be ignored in MMSB, just like in PTM? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates (on page 5)? What happens if 2K, 4K, or 100K triples are sampled? Would the quality of the approximation suffer as K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm/model is inadequate.

Clarity:
The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

Originality:
While the modeling is somewhat incremental, achieving scalability through parameter constraints is an interesting and original direction to pursue, especially since recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm hold in practice, this work would have a significant impact on the practice of latent-space models on networks, as few of these models currently scale to large data.

Additional Comments:
Personally, I think the title is too broad. There are other scalable approaches to latent space models, and the authors propose a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model, PTM.

I am also unsure whether PTM is truly a probabilistic method. The authors argue that their approach is similar in spirit to the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not convinced. When LDA generates a bag-of-words, there is always a corresponding document, which means it has a proper likelihood function, no matter how simplistic it may be. In contrast, with very high probability, there does not exist a network corresponding to a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM. Therefore, MMTM/PTM is not a generative model for networks, and most of the posterior probability mass is placed on modeling non-networks. It might be perilous to abandon basic principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" methods. I am curious to know what the authors and other reviewers think. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks that make MMTM scalable, but the side effects of these decisions are poorly investigated.
