This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data. The model is fit to triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance is motivated by the aMMSB model, which parameterizes only the configurations of the triangles to identify a set of K roles, rather than all between-"community" interactions. This results in fewer parameters in the model and, in turn, a more scalable implementation. They apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving a global update and local updates for the proposed model.

The novel modeling contribution is a straightforward combination of the aMMSB with the MMTM, although somewhat tedious bookkeeping was required. The presentation of this material could be improved for clarity and readability.

Though most of the learning algorithm is a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule. This approximation is a key component of their approach, allowing for efficient computation and scalability.

Given the prevalence of directed networks, a detailed discussion of how these methods might extend to handle directed networks would be welcome. This would enhance the applicability and usefulness of the proposed approach.

Ongoing concerns and suggestions for improvement:

* The statement "our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs" is somewhat unclear. ERGMs can include more varied statistics than just triangles, and the term "latent space counterpart" is not well-defined. A more precise explanation of the relationship between PTM and ERGMs would be helpful.
* The interpretability of the inferred latent space is not fully explored. If the latent space is indeed interpretable, what insights do we gain about the similarities and differences among these graphs?
* The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained. A more detailed explanation of these terms and their significance in the context of the model would be beneficial.
* The subsampling of triangles for each node using delta is not fully justified. A clearer explanation of why this provides an adequate approximation would be welcome.
* If the goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles," perhaps a more binary notion of "role" should be incorporated into the model.
* The robustness of the latent space recovery results to the threshold of 0.125 is not thoroughly explored. A more comprehensive analysis of the sensitivity of the results to this threshold would be helpful.
* The specific latent space model used to generate power-law networks in the experiments is not clearly stated. This information would be useful for understanding the experimental setup and results.
* The PTM collapsed Gibbs sampler is stated to have K^3 complexity, but this seems inconsistent with the claim that there are O(K) parameters. A clarification of this point would be helpful.
* One concern is how this approach will perform when fewer edges are observed. In the experiments, only 10% of the edges are held out, which may not be representative of more sparse networks. A more comprehensive evaluation of the approach's performance on sparse networks would be welcome.
* It is counterintuitive that the method beats MMSB on a synthetic network generated via MMSB (Figure 1, left 4 panels). A more detailed explanation of this result would be helpful.

Overall, this work uses stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel with a restricted set of parameters on the triangle counts in a graph. Their modeling choices and approximations allow for a more scalable O(NK) algorithm. However, addressing the above concerns and suggestions would strengthen the paper and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach.
