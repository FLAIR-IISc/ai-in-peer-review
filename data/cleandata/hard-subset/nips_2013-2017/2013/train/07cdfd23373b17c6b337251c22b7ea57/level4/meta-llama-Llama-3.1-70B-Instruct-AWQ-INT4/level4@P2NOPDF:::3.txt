The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks, presenting a novel approach to modeling complex network structures. They introduce a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, enabling efficient inference. By leveraging stochastic variational inference, the authors demonstrate the ability to infer the posterior distribution over very large networks.

The paper is well-written and well-executed, making it a pleasure to read. Overall, I think the paper is acceptable.

On page 1, the authors mention "parsimonious" modeling, which may not be a commonly used term in the context of probabilistic models. To improve clarity, it would be helpful to provide a brief explanation of this term, as it plays a crucial role in the paper.

Also on page 1, the authors state that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy, citing Gopalan et al. However, my reading of Gopalan et al. suggests that down-sampling zeros does not necessarily compromise accuracy, making the sentence in the introduction potentially misleading.

On page 4, the authors defend the triangle model by drawing an analogy with Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). While I sympathize with the pragmatic appeal of the triangle model, I do not think the comparison to LDA is entirely fair. Running the generative process of LDA yields a document, albeit a potentially nonsensical one, whereas running the triangle model can produce objects that do not resemble networks. Although this does not detract from the model's practical value, the defense via LDA may not be the most convincing argument.

On page 5, the authors should cite Hoffman et al., in addition to Amari and Sato, as their scenario involves "local variables" that are not addressed by Sato's algorithm. Hoffman's work provides the necessary theoretical foundation for this aspect of the paper.

On page 6, the authors mention generating power law networks in the latent space but do not provide sufficient details about the process. It would be helpful to elaborate on this point to facilitate understanding and reproducibility.

Additionally, the authors cite Gopalan et al. as a means of speeding up the Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB). A comparison to Gopalan et al.'s algorithm, in addition to or instead of MMSB batch, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method.

Some minor comments include: on page 2, "mixture-membership" should be replaced with "mixed-membership" to maintain consistency with standard terminology. On page 8, I suggest removing the last sentence, as the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger conclusion to the paper.

Finally, in the bibliography, the authors should cite the Journal of Machine Learning Research (JMLR) version of "Stochastic Variational Inference".
