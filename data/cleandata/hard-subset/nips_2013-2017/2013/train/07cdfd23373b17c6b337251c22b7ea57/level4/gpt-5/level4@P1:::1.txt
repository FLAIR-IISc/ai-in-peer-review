Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a specialization of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) that reduces the triangle-generating parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K) by sharing parameters across groups of role-triplet configurations. Building on the bag-of-triangles representation, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with additional approximations to achieve scalability to million-node networks and hundreds of roles. On synthetic data, they argue that the reduced parameterization can yield stronger statistical power; on real networks, they report competitive or improved accuracy relative to existing probabilistic methods (e.g., MMSB and a-MMSB) with dramatic speedups.

The work connects to prior latent-space and mixed-membership models for networks (e.g., MMSB, a-MMSB) and to triangle-based representations (e.g., MMTM). It leverages SVI and structured mean-field approximations, in line with scalable Bayesian inference methods.

Quality:
PTM is an interesting and principled specialization of MMTM. However, the practical value of scalability in K (number of roles) is not convincingly demonstrated. Because MMSB/MMTM are mixed-membership models, small K is often less problematic than in single-membership settings. To assess the benefit of scaling in K, it is important to show performance as a function of K on real networks. In particular, a comparison where PTM with large K is pitted against MMTM (or MMSB) with smaller K would directly test the premise that PTM’s parsimony enables better solutions at larger K. Currently, I could not find a clear description of how K was selected for real data for both PTM and the MMSB competitor; model selection may have biased results.

The paper is also too reticent about the consequences of the simplifying assumptions. Parameter sharing reduces variance and cost, but what happens when the true model actually requires O(K^3) distinct triangle parameters (i.e., when the B tensor is not compressible)? In such cases PTM should be misspecified and could perform poorly. The synthetic experiments do not clearly specify how the true B was chosen; one would expect experiments with a range of B’s—some favorable to PTM’s sharing structure and some that violate it. Understanding when PTM succeeds and when it fails would be more informative than only showing favorable regimes.

The paper introduces several clever approximation and subsampling strategies, but their impacts are not analyzed in sufficient depth:
1) δ-subsampling of triangles: What is the empirical effect of δ on accuracy and runtime for both real and synthetic data? How far can δ be reduced before accuracy degrades noticeably? Does performance consistently improve with larger δ, and what are the diminishing returns?
2) Downsampling and omission of non-edges: The paper argues that a-MMSB suffers from downsampling, whereas PTM is robust while ignoring single-edge triples. Why is the loss of information acceptable for PTM but not for MMSB? If MMSB suffers from ignoring non-edges, why is ignoring single-edge triples in PTM acceptable? A clearer explanation of these different sensitivities is needed.
3) O(K) local-update approximation: What is the effect of sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples in the “mixture-of-deltas” approximation? Does approximation quality degrade as K grows? A sensitivity analysis would make the trade-offs transparent.

Overall, I am not convinced the experimental setup is fully fair to competitors, and the paper does not adequately describe the model’s potential weaknesses or failure modes.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written, well organized, and generally easy to follow. The exposition of the model and the algorithm is crisp.

Originality:
Conceptually, the modeling contribution is incremental relative to MMTM, but the parameter-sharing idea and the structured SVI approach for scalability are interesting and timely. This direction is distinct from recent work focused on more general models rather than more parsimonious ones.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in both the model and the inference hold in practice, the contributions could be impactful, since few probabilistic latent-space methods scale to this regime. The demonstrated speedups are impressive. However, without a thorough analysis of when the assumptions are valid and how approximation choices affect accuracy, it is difficult to assess robustness across domains.

Pros:
- Clear model and well-motivated parameter sharing that enables O(K) global parameters.
- Scalable SVI with practical implementation details; strong empirical runtime results.
- Competitive accuracy on several tasks and datasets.
- Clean, readable presentation.

Cons:
- Lack of experiments that systematically vary K on real networks; unclear model selection for K.
- Insufficient analysis of when PTM’s parameter sharing is appropriate; unclear effects when the true B is dense in O(K^3).
- Limited evaluation of the impacts of δ-subsampling and the O(K) local-update approximation.
- Unclear comparison to MMSB regarding information loss from ignoring non-edges vs. ignoring single-edge triples.

Additional comments:
- The title is too broad; it suggests a general scalable approach to probabilistic latent spaces, while the paper focuses on PTM.
- I am not convinced PTM/MMTM are “probabilistic methods” in the same sense as models with a proper likelihood over networks. In LDA, a generated bag of words always corresponds to a document, so the likelihood is well-defined. In PTM/MMTM, a generated bag of triangles may not correspond to any graph; therefore, the model is not a generative model of networks, and most posterior mass may be placed on non-graphs. While the authors state that their goal is latent-space inference rather than network simulation, it would be useful to discuss what “probability” means in this setup, and whether abandoning a proper network likelihood may have unintended effects. At minimum, the paper should more thoroughly examine the side effects and limitations of modeling bags of triangles instead of graphs.
