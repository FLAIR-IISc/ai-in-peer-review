

This paper introduces a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) based on network triangle motifs rather than edge representations. The authors address three key challenges for large-scale network analysis: (1) developing a succinct network representation, (2) creating a parsimonious statistical model, and (3) designing a scalable inference algorithm. Their approach builds on previous work, particularly the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) [8] and the adaptive MMSB (aMMSB) [5], but significantly reduces parameter complexity from O(K³) to O(K) through strategic parameter sharing across latent role configurations. The authors then apply stochastic variational inference with an efficient local update approximation to achieve O(NK) complexity, enabling analysis of networks with over a million vertices on a single machine in hours.

The paper makes several notable contributions. First, it provides a theoretically grounded reduction in model complexity while maintaining competitive accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction. The authors demonstrate that their PTM achieves comparable or better accuracy than state-of-the-art methods (MMTM Gibbs, MMSB variational) while being orders of magnitude faster, particularly for large networks (N > 100,000). Second, their implementation of stochastic variational inference with an O(K) local update approximation is technically sound and well-justified, with clear experimental validation showing convergence in just 2-5 data passes. Third, the work successfully addresses the critical scalability challenge in network analysis, enabling latent space inference on networks previously considered computationally intractable.

The paper excels in quality, with thorough experiments on both synthetic and real networks, including a 1 million-node YouTube social network. The authors provide appropriate comparisons to relevant prior work, including MMSB [1] and MMTM [8], and conduct meaningful ablation studies to validate their modeling choices. The experimental design is rigorous, with careful attention to computational complexity analysis and practical implementation details. The clarity of the paper is generally good, though some technical details about the parameter sharing scheme could be more explicitly explained, particularly regarding the role of the equivalence classes in Table 1.

The originality of the work is moderate. The paper combines established ideas (triangle motifs from [8], parameter sharing from [5]) in a novel way that addresses the scalability gap for large networks. While the core modeling approach represents a logical extension of prior work rather than a radical departure, the specific implementation and empirical validation of this combination for large-scale networks constitutes a significant practical contribution. The paper adequately references relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers on network modeling.

The significance of this work is high. It directly addresses a critical bottleneck in network analysis—scalability to large networks—and provides a practical solution that enables new applications. The authors demonstrate clear improvements in both computational efficiency (orders of magnitude faster) and accuracy (competitive or improved) for latent space recovery and link prediction. This work has the potential to substantially impact practitioners working with large-scale social and information networks.

Key strengths include:
- A compelling solution to a well-known scalability challenge in network analysis
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple network sizes and types
- Clear demonstration of computational advantages over state-of-the-art methods
- Well-justified theoretical and practical contributions to the field

Several concerns warrant attention:
1. The claim that "PTM is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is misleading. ERGMs are a broad class of models that can include various network statistics beyond triangles, and the relationship to latent space models is not clearly articulated. This should be clarified.
2. The interpretation of the inferred latent space is not sufficiently explored. The paper shows good recovery accuracy but does not discuss how these latent roles correspond to meaningful network structures or communities.
3. The threshold of 0.125 for NMI evaluation appears arbitrary. The authors should justify this choice or provide sensitivity analysis.
4. The paper states that PTM CGS has K³ complexity but argues for O(K) parameters. This discrepancy needs clarification—does the complexity arise from the sampling procedure rather than the parameter count?
5. The discussion of triangle subsampling (δ-subsampling) lacks theoretical justification. The paper should better explain why this approximation preserves the necessary network statistics for accurate inference.

The paper would benefit from:
- More explicit discussion of how the latent roles correspond to interpretable network features
- A clearer explanation of the parameter sharing scheme and its impact on model flexibility
- Justification for the thresholding approach used in latent space recovery
- A brief discussion of how the method might extend to directed networks, as suggested in the draft review

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that makes a significant practical advance in scalable network analysis. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in the field. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach achieves both scalability and accuracy where previous methods fail. I recommend acceptance, as this work has clear significance for the NIPS community and practical impact for network analysis practitioners. The minor concerns noted above are not fundamental to the paper's contribution and can be addressed in the camera-ready version.
