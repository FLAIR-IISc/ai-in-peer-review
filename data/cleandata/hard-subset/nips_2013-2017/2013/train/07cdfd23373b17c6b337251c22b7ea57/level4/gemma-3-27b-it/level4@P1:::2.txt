This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data. The model is fit to triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance builds upon the aMMSB: rather than parameterize all between-"community" interactions, it leverages the configurations of triangles to identify a set of K latent roles for parameterization. This results in fewer parameters in the model and, consequently, a more scalable implementation. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving global and local update rules for their proposed model.

The novel modeling contribution is a sensible combination of the aMMSB with the MMTM, though the presentation of this material could be clarified. While much of the learning algorithm is a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors introduce an intuitive and promising approximation for the local update rule. 

Given the prevalence of directed networks, a discussion of how these methods might be extended to handle directed edges would be valuable. 

Several points warrant further consideration:

* The statement that “our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs” seems imprecise. First, ERGMs can incorporate a much broader range of statistics than solely triangles, and second, it’s not immediately clear what is meant by a “latent space counterpart,” unless this applies to all latent space network models.
* Are the inferred latent spaces interpretable? If so, what insights do they provide about the similarities and differences among the analyzed graphs?
* The distinction between the Δ<sup>1</sup> and Δ<sup>2</sup> distributions is unclear.
* The justification for approximating the full triangle statistics via node-centric subsampling (using δ) could be elaborated upon. Why does this approximation provide adequate fidelity?
* Given the goal of recovering the “set of non-zero roles,” a more explicitly binary notion of "role" within the model might be beneficial.
* How sensitive are the latent space recovery results to the chosen threshold of 0.125, which appears somewhat arbitrary?
* For the synthetic experiments, specifying which latent space model was used to generate the power-law networks would strengthen the evaluation.
* The paper states that PTM Gibbs has K<sup>3</sup> complexity. This seems to contradict the argument that the model has O(K) parameters. Could this be clarified?
* A potential limitation is performance when dealing with sparse graphs. The experiments only hold out 10% of the edges, perhaps because a larger removal rate could introduce substantial bias in the distribution of adjacent triangles during the preprocessing step.
* The result that PTM outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network *generated* by an MMSB process (Figure 1, left four panels) is counterintuitive and merits further explanation.

In summary, this work presents a scalable approach—using stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel with a restricted parameter set—applied to triangle counts in a graph. The modeling choices and approximations made allow for an efficient O(NK) algorithm.
