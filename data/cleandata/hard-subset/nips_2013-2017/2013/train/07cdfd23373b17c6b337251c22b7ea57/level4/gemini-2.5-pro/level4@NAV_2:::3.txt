This paper presents a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference in large networks. The authors' central contribution is a method that combines three key principles: a succinct network representation based on a "bag of triangular motifs," a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. The work builds upon the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) of Ho et al. (2013), but introduces a new Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that significantly reduces the model's parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) by sharing parameters across latent role configurations. This parameter reduction is conceptually similar to the improvement a-MMSB (Gopalan et al., 2013) made over the original MMSB. To perform inference, the authors develop a scalable stochastic variational algorithm with a structured mean-field approximation, which enables the analysis of million-node networks with hundreds of latent roles on a single machine.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its impressive scalability. The proposed method achieves orders-of-magnitude speedups compared to the runtimes reported for previous state-of-the-art methods like MMTM and a-MMSB. The ability to infer 100 latent roles on a million-node network in a few hours on a single multi-core machine represents a significant practical advance for researchers and practitioners working with large-scale network data.
2.  **Originality and Model Design:** The PTM is a clever and effective simplification of the MMTM. The parameter sharing scheme is well-motivated and leads to a model that is not only faster to train but also appears more robust, exhibiting better latent space recovery on larger synthetic datasets, likely due to the reduced risk of overfitting and better parameter estimates.
3.  **Technical Quality:** The development of the stochastic variational inference algorithm is technically sound. The use of a structured mean-field approximation to capture dependencies between per-triangle latent roles and the O(K) approximation for the local update are thoughtful design choices that contribute to both the accuracy and efficiency of the final algorithm.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The motivation for the three principles of large-scale inference is clearly laid out, and the model and inference procedure are described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Experimental Comparisons:** A significant weakness is the choice of experimental baselines. The paper motivates its work by discussing the scalability limitations of recent methods like a-MMSB, yet the primary experimental comparison is against the much older and slower batch variational MMSB algorithm. To fully substantiate the claims of superiority in both speed and accuracy, a direct comparison against a-MMSB on at least one of the large-scale datasets would be far more compelling and appropriate.
2.  **Clarity on Key Concepts and Methods:**
    *   The term "parsimonious statistical modeling" is central to the contribution, but it is not a standard expression. While the meaning can be inferred from context, the paper would benefit from a sentence explicitly defining what the authors mean by it (e.g., reducing parameter space complexity to be linear in K).
    *   The description of the synthetic data generation process for power-law networks is vague. To ensure reproducibility, the authors should provide more detail on how the latent space vectors were used to generate networks with this structure.
3.  **Justification and Related Work:**
    *   The discussion of a-MMSB in the introduction could be more precise. The statement that a-MMSB is "not fast enough" because it depends on the O(N^2) adjacency matrix could be misconstrued. The down-sampling of zero-entries is precisely the mechanism that makes the algorithm scalable for sparse networks, often without a significant loss in accuracy. The point is valid, but could be phrased more carefully.
    *   The defense of the model not being a true generative model of networks by analogy to LDA is not entirely appropriate. A generative process for LDA still produces a valid document (a bag of words), whereas the PTM process can produce a set of triangles that is not realizable as a graph. It would be better to state this limitation directly and justify the model based on its pragmatic success in inference, which is a perfectly valid stance.
    *   The paper should cite Hoffman et al. (JMLR, 2013) on Stochastic Variational Inference. This work is foundational for SVI in models with local and global variables (as is the case here) and is more directly applicable than the algorithm of Sato (2001), which does not handle this structure.

**Minor Comments:**

*   p2: There is a typo "mixture-membership" which should be "mixed-membership".
*   p4: The appeal to LDA to justify the non-generative nature of the model is unconvincing (see weakness #3).
*   p8: The final sentence of the paper feels redundant. The second-to-last sentence provides a stronger and more impactful conclusion.
*   Bibliography: The reference for Hoffman et al., "Stochastic Variational Inference," should be updated to the JMLR version.

In summary, this is a well-executed paper presenting a significant contribution to scalable inference for network models. The proposed PTM model and its associated stochastic inference algorithm demonstrate impressive performance. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including a more relevant state-of-the-art baseline, the work is of high quality and likely to be of great interest to the community. I recommend acceptance.
