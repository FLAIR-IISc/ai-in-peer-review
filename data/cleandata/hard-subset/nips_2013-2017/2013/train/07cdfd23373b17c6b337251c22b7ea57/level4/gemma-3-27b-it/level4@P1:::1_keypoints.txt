- Proposes PTM to reduce MMTM’s O(K^3) parameters to O(K) for faster inference with stochastic variational inference and scalability tricks.
- Empirical benefit of enabling large K is not demonstrated; need experiments comparing PTM and MMTM performance as a function of K.
- Model selection and choice of K for PTM and competitors on real data are unclear and may bias results.
- Simplifying assumptions’ failure modes are not analyzed; PTM should perform poorly when true B has O(K^3) distinct values.
- Synthetic experiments lack diversity in true B; authors should test Bs that favor and challenge PTM.
- Several approximation “tricks” are interesting but their impacts are weakly described and analyzed.
- Unclear effects of threshold δ: how varying δ trades off scalability and accuracy is not reported.
- Questionable claim that PTM is immune to downsampling issues in a-MMSB; need explanation why non-edges ignored in MMSB wouldn’t cause similar problems.
- Impact of the O(K) approximation to the local update is unexplored; need studies sampling 2K, 4K, etc., and effect as K grows.
- Experimental setup may be unfair to competitors; overall evaluation is insufficient to prove practical advantage.
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized.
- Modeling contribution is somewhat incremental but the parameter-constraining direction is interesting and original.
- If assumptions hold, PTM could significantly impact scalable latent-space network models.
- Title is overly broad; the method is a scalable algorithm for a specific latent-space model (PTM), not general latent models.
- Doubts about PTM being a genuine probabilistic generative model: bag-of-triples assumption may not correspond to realistic network likelihoods, raising concerns about what probability means in PTM.
- Side effects of modeling assumptions and inference tricks are not thoroughly investigated.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses scalability, competitive accuracy on benchmarks, clear writing, and notable runtime improvements.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete evaluation of assumptions and limitations, potentially unfair experiments, and weak theoretical justification for abandoning likelihood-based modeling.
