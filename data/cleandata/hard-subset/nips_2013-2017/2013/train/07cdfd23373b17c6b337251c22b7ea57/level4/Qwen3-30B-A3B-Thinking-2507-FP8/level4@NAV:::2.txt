

This paper introduces a method for learning latent structure in social network data by modeling triangle count statistics, similar to a bag-of-words representation for text. The core innovation adapts the aMMSB framework: instead of parameterizing all between-community interactions, it identifies K roles via triangle configurations, reducing parameters and enabling scalable implementation. The authors employ recent stochastic variational inference advances, deriving global and local updates for the model.  

The primary modeling contribution combines aMMSB with MMTM, though the presentation could be improved. While most of the learning algorithm directly applies stochastic variational inference, the authors provide an intuitive and compelling approximation for the local update rule. Given the prevalence of directed networks, a detailed discussion of extensions would be valuable.  

Ongoing concerns:  
- "Our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, representing the processes of generating triangular motifs" is unclear. ERGMs encompass far more diverse statistics than triangles, and the "latent space counterpart" claim is ambiguous unless all latent space models qualify.  
- Are inferred latent spaces interpretable? What insights do they provide about graph similarities/differences?  
- The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² is not evident.  
- Why does subsampling triangles per node (using delta) yield a valid approximation?  
- If the goal is recovering "non-zero roles," should the model incorporate a binary role notion?  
- How robust are latent space results to the arbitrary threshold of 0.125?  
- Which latent space model generated the power-law networks used in experiments?  
- Why does PTM (CGS) have K³ complexity when the model claims O(K) parameters?  
- Performance under sparse edges is uncertain; experiments only hold out 10% of edges (to avoid biasing adjacent triangle distributions).  
- It is counterintuitive that PTM outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated via MMSB (Figure 1, left 4 panels).  

This work leverages stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel (with restricted parameters) on graph triangle counts, enabling a scalable O(NK) algorithm.
