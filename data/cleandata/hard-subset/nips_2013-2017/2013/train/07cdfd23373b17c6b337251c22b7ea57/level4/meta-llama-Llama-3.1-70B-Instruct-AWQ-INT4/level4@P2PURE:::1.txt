This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K), enabling faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to further improve scalability. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that the reduction in the number of variables can lead to stronger statistical power and competitive accuracy with existing methods.

Quality:
While PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, its practical advantages in achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) are questionable. To empirically evaluate the value of this method, it is crucial to investigate how it can facilitate learning MMTM with large K. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may not be as problematic as it is in single-membership models. A more comprehensive evaluation would involve comparing the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K, demonstrating whether PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the description of how K values were chosen in real-data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB is lacking, and the method of model selection may have biased the experimental results.

The authors are also reticent about the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it is essential to discuss the potential failure cases of the model. If there are indeed O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly. In this regard, it is unsatisfactory that the choice of true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described. The authors should have experimented with various B values, some of which would favor PTM and others that would not. It would be more beneficial for readers to understand when PTM would succeed and when it would fail, rather than only knowing when it would succeed.

On the other hand, the authors introduce several approximation techniques to make PTM scalable, which are interesting and effective. However, the paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. Specifically, the following questions remain unanswered: 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold δ? How does it affect the results on real-world and synthetic data experiments? How much can δ be lowered to improve scalability without sacrificing accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy, but it is unclear why PTM would be exempt from this problem. Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge, so why can't non-edges be ignored in MMSB while they can in PTM? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates (on page 5)? How does the quality of approximation change when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Would the approximation suffer when K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was fair to competitors, and the description of the potential weaknesses of the algorithm/model is inadequate.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized, making it easy to follow.

Originality: While the modeling approach is somewhat incremental, achieving scalability through parameter constraints is an interesting and original direction, as recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance: If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to networks, as few existing models scale to large data.

Additional Comments:
The title of the paper is too broad, as it implies that the proposed approach is universally applicable to latent space models, whereas it is specifically designed for PTM.

I am also skeptical about whether PTM is a truly probabilistic method. The authors argue that it is similar in spirit to the bag-of-words assumption, but I am not convinced. In LDA, a document corresponds to a generated bag-of-words, providing a proper likelihood function. In contrast, MMTM and PTM generate bags-of-triples that do not correspond to actual networks, placing the majority of posterior probability mass on modeling non-networks. This raises concerns about abandoning basic principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" methods and the meaning of probability in PTM. I would appreciate the authors' and other reviewers' thoughts on this matter. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks to make MMTM scalable, but the side effects of these decisions are poorly investigated.
