

This paper introduces a technique for learning latent structure in social network data by modeling triangle count statistics, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text. The key modeling advance adapts aMMSB by using triangle configurations to identify K roles for parameterization, rather than modeling all between-community interactions. This reduces parameter count and enables a more scalable implementation, leveraging recent stochastic variational inference (SVI) advances with derived global and local updates.  

The core modeling contribution—combining aMMSB with MMTM—is conceptually straightforward but requires tedious bookkeeping. The presentation of this material could be improved for clarity. While most of the learning algorithm directly applies SVI, the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule. However, the work would benefit from a more detailed discussion of extensions to directed networks, which are prevalent in practice.  

Several concerns require clarification:  
1. The claim that "PTM is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs representing triangular motif generation" appears questionable. ERGMs incorporate diverse statistics beyond triangles, and the "latent space counterpart" phrasing is imprecise—unless all latent space models are implicitly "counterparts" to ERGMs.  
2. Are inferred latent spaces interpretable? If so, what insights do they offer about graph similarities and differences?  
3. The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² remains unclear.  
4. The justification for subsampling triangles per node (via Δ) as an adequate approximation is not sufficiently explained.  
5. If the goal is to recover non-zero roles, a more explicit binary role formulation might be preferable.  
6. The robustness of latent space recovery to the threshold of 0.125 (which appears arbitrary) is unaddressed.  
7. The paper does not specify which latent space model generated the power-law networks used in experiments.  
8. The PTM CGS implementation exhibits K³ complexity, contradicting the claim of O(K) parameters.  
9. Performance under sparse edge regimes is concerning; the 10% edge holdout may bias triangle distributions, limiting generalizability to sparser graphs.  
10. It is counterintuitive that PTM outperforms MMSB on synthetic networks generated via MMSB (Figure 1, left panels), warranting deeper analysis.  

The work effectively uses SVI to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel on triangle counts, with modeling choices enabling a scalable O(NK) algorithm. However, the technical claims and methodological details require stronger justification to support the proposed approach’s advantages.
