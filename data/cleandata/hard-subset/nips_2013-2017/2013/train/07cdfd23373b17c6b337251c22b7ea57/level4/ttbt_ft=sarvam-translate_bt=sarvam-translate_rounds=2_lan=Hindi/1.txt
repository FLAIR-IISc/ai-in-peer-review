This research paper proposes a Pragmatic Triangulation Model (PTM) that limits the O(K) parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangulation Model (MMTM) to O(K), enabling faster estimation. The authors develop a stochastic variance estimation algorithm for PTM and additional approximation techniques to make it more scalable. It has been shown from synthetic datasets that reducing the number of variables can provide strong statistical power, and from real-world datasets, the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of achieving good scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) is doubtful. To empirically evaluate the value of such a method, it is important for us to answer, "How does it help if we can learn MMTM with a large K?" Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may not be as difficult as a single-membership model! It would be really interesting to see the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K for real network experiments: if we can actually see that PTM performs better than MMTM with a small K, it will show the power of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments have not been conducted. In fact, I was unable to find good details about how K was chosen for both PTM and its competitor MMSB in real data experiments; the model selection method may have biased the experimental results.

The authors are also a little reluctant to discuss the impact of the simplified assumptions they make. It is clear that we can achieve good scalability by limiting the parameter space, but it would also be good to hear when such a model will fail. If the parameter matrices of MMSB and MMTM (according to the authors' notation) actually have O(K^3) distinct values, then PTM should perform poorly, right? In this regard, it is unsatisfactory that the choice of true B in synthetic data experiments was not clearly described; the authors should have experimented with different B, some of which are in favor of PTM and some against the model. It would be more beneficial for readers to know when PTM will succeed and when it will fail, rather than just knowing when it will succeed.

On the other hand, the authors offer several tricks to scale PTM; while I find them very interesting and effective, this paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of such approximations. 1) What is the effect of choosing a threshold \delta? On real-world and synthetic data experiments, is it helpful to choose a large or small \delta? How much can we reduce \delta to improve scalability without sacrificing too much accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB does not work because downsampling compromises accuracy; why would PTM be free from such a problem? MMTM and PTM are actually only ignoring a biased triangle. Why can't we ignore non-bias in MMTB while we can in PTM? 3) What is the effect of O(K) approximation for local updates (in page 5)? What happens if we sample triangles at 2K, 4K, or 100K? Will the quality of the approximation be affected as K gets larger?

In summary, I am not convinced that the experimental setup was suitable for competitors, and the description of the potential weakness of the algorithm/model is poor.

**Clarity:** The research paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:** It is a little incremental in terms of modeling, but achieving scalability by limiting parameters is an interesting and original direction to follow, as much work has recently been focused on generalizing existing models in this area.

**Importance:** If simplified assumptions remain in practice in the model and estimation algorithm, it will have a good impact on the practice of hidden-state models on networks, as many of them do not actually reach large data.

**Additional Notes:** Personally, I think the title is too broad. There are other scalable approaches for other hidden-state models, and the authors propose a scalable algorithm for a hidden-state model - PTM.

Also, I am not sure that PTM is really a probabilistic method. The authors argue in the last paragraph of Section 3 that this is similar to the word-bag assumption, but I am not sure. When LDA generates a word-bag, there is always a corresponding document; therefore it has a reasonable probability function, no matter how unrealistically simple it is. On the other hand, there is no network corresponding to the bag of triangles generated by MMTM and PTM, which has a very high probability. Therefore MMTM/PTM is not really a generative model for networks, and most backward probability masses are placed on modeling non-networks! Abandoning the basic principles of probability in "probability" methods can be dangerous (what does probability mean in PTM?)... I am curious what the authors and other reviewers think. The authors propose modeling assumptions and estimation tricks that will make estimation scalable on MMTM. However, the side effects of such a decision are poorly investigated.
