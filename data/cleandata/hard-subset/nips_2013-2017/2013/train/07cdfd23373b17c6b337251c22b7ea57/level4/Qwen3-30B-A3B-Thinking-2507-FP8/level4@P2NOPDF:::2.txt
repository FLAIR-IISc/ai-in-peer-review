

This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for learning latent structure in social networks by modeling triangle count statistics, analogous to bag-of-words representations in text analysis. The approach builds on the aMMSB framework, replacing full parameterization of between-community interactions with a focus on triangle configurations to identify K roles. This reduces model parameters and enables a scalable O(NK) implementation via stochastic variational inference (SVI), including novel global and local update rules. While the core modeling integration of aMMSB and MMTM is well-motivated, the presentation requires significant improvement for clarity. The authors’ intuitive approximation for the local update rule is a notable strength, though the paper’s treatment of directed networks remains underdeveloped.

Major concerns require clarification:  
1. **ERGM comparison**: The claim that PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is misleading. ERGMs accommodate diverse statistics beyond triangles, and PTM does not inherently constitute a latent space model (all latent space models could be termed "counterparts"). This warrants rephrasing.  
2. **Latent space interpretability**: The paper does not address whether inferred latent positions are interpretable or what insights they provide about graph similarities/differences.  
3. **Delta notation**: The distinction between Δ₁ and Δ₂ is unclear and inadequately explained.  
4. **Subsampling justification**: The rationale for subsampling triangles per node (using Δ) as an adequate approximation lacks theoretical or empirical support.  
5. **Role definition**: If the goal is recovering "non-zero roles," the model should incorporate a binary role indicator rather than a continuous parameterization.  
6. **Threshold sensitivity**: The robustness of latent space recovery to the arbitrary threshold of 0.125 is untested.  
7. **Power-law model**: The experiment setup uses a latent space model capable of generating power-law networks, but the specific model (e.g., LFR, Kronecker) is unspecified.  
8. **Complexity contradiction**: The paper asserts O(K) parameters but claims PTM-CGS has K³ complexity—this inconsistency requires resolution.  
9. **Edge sparsity**: Performance under low edge density is unexamined. Holding out only 10% of edges (to avoid biasing triangle distributions) limits generalizability to sparse networks.  
10. **Synthetic data paradox**: PTM outperforms MMSB on MMSB-generated synthetic data (Fig. 1, left panels), which is surprising given the data-generation process.  

The paper’s scalability claim is valid but hinges on unaddressed assumptions. For instance, the SVI approach’s effectiveness depends on the triangle subsampling approximation (point 4), which is not justified. Additionally, the synthetic experiment’s result (point 10) contradicts expectations and merits deeper analysis. The authors should clarify whether PTM’s advantage stems from the triangle-count preprocessing or the model’s structure.  

Minor issues include:  
- The phrase "PTM is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs" (line 3) misrepresents both frameworks.  
- The term "roles" is used ambiguously; a binary role definition (as suggested in point 5) would strengthen the model’s interpretability.  
- The presentation of technical details (e.g., the derivation of local updates) lacks sufficient intuition for readers unfamiliar with SVI.  

Overall, the PTM offers a promising path to scalable latent structure learning by leveraging triangle statistics. However, the current formulation contains unresolved technical gaps and insufficient justification for key design choices. Addressing the concerns above—particularly the Delta notation, subsampling rationale, and synthetic data paradox—would significantly strengthen the contribution. The paper’s core idea is compelling but requires more rigorous exposition and validation to demonstrate its advantages over existing methods like MMSB.  

The authors should:  
- Revisit the ERGM comparison to avoid misleading claims.  
- Provide empirical evidence for the triangle subsampling approximation.  
- Clarify the role of the 0.125 threshold and its impact on results.  
- Discuss extensions to directed networks.  
- Resolve the K³ vs. O(K) complexity discrepancy.  

While the SVI adaptation is well-executed, the work’s broader impact hinges on resolving these technical ambiguities. With revisions, PTM could provide a valuable tool for scalable network analysis, but as presented, its advantages remain partially unconvincing.
