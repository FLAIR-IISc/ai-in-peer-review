This paper presents a scalable method for inferring latent structure in large-scale networks. The approach is built on three key principles: a succinct network representation using a "bag of triangular motifs," a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. The core modeling contribution is the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which extends the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) by dramatically reducing the number of parameters. Drawing inspiration from the a-MMSB model, PTM reduces the parameterization of triangle-generating probabilities from O(K³) to O(K) by sharing parameters across configurations of latent roles. To fit the model, the authors derive a stochastic variational inference algorithm with a structured mean-field approximation, which includes a novel and efficient approximation for the local updates. The result is a highly scalable O(NK) algorithm capable of analyzing networks with over a million nodes.

**Strengths**

*   **Scalability:** The most significant strength of this work is its remarkable scalability. The combination of a parsimonious model and a stochastic inference algorithm allows the method to analyze million-node networks with hundreds of latent roles in a matter of hours on a single machine. This represents a significant practical advance over previous methods like MMTM and a-MMSB, which are orders of magnitude slower.
*   **Empirical Performance:** The experiments convincingly demonstrate that the impressive gains in speed do not compromise accuracy. The method achieves competitive or superior performance on both latent space recovery and link prediction tasks across a range of synthetic and real-world datasets. The fast convergence of the stochastic algorithm (typically within 2-5 data passes) is also a notable advantage.
*   **Algorithmic Contribution:** The derivation of the inference algorithm is well-executed. The proposed approximation for the local update step, which reduces its complexity from O(K³) to O(K), is an intuitive and effective solution to a critical computational bottleneck.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is strong overall, but could be improved by addressing the following points regarding clarity, evaluation, and scope.

**Clarity and Justification:**
1.  The authors state that PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs." This connection seems tenuous, as ERGMs can incorporate a much wider variety of graph statistics beyond triangles, and the term "latent space counterpart" is not clearly defined. The paper would be stronger if this claim were either toned down or more thoroughly justified.
2.  The `delta`-subsampling of triangles is a critical preprocessing step inherited from prior work. For the paper to be self-contained, a clearer justification for why this sampling provides an adequate approximation of the true triangle distribution would be beneficial.
3.  The distinction between the simplexes ∆¹ and ∆² in Section 3 was not immediately clear and could be explained more explicitly for the reader.
4.  There appears to be a contradiction regarding the complexity of the PTM Gibbs sampler baseline. The paper argues that PTM has O(K) parameters, yet the PTM Gibbs sampler is stated to have O(K³) complexity. Please clarify why the Gibbs sampler does not benefit from the parsimonious parameterization.
5.  For reproducibility, the specific latent space model used to generate the power-law networks for the experiments should be described in more detail.

**Modeling and Evaluation:**
1.  The link prediction experiments involve holding out only 10% of edges. A potential weakness of triangle-based methods is their reliance on dense local structures. It would be valuable to understand how the method performs in sparser settings or when a larger fraction of edges are missing, as this would significantly alter the input triangle distribution.
2.  It is surprising that PTM outperforms the MMSB model on synthetic data generated from the MMSB model itself (Figure 1). While this may be due to the regularizing effect of PTM's parsimonious structure, this counterintuitive result is interesting and warrants discussion.
3.  The latent space recovery evaluation relies on applying a threshold of 0.125 to the mixed-membership vectors to get a "set of non-zero roles." This threshold seems somewhat arbitrary. An analysis of the results' sensitivity to this threshold would strengthen the experimental claims. Additionally, there is a slight mismatch between the model's continuous output and this discrete evaluation goal.
4.  The practical significance of the work could be further highlighted with a qualitative analysis of the inferred latent spaces on the real-world networks. Are the roles interpretable? What new insights do they provide?

**Scope:**
1.  The proposed method is designed for undirected graphs. Given the prevalence of directed networks in many domains, a discussion of how the triangle-based framework might be extended to handle directed edges would be a welcome addition.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution by developing a highly scalable and accurate method for latent space inference. The modeling choices and algorithmic approximations are well-motivated and lead to impressive empirical results. Addressing the points above would further improve the clarity and impact of this excellent work.
