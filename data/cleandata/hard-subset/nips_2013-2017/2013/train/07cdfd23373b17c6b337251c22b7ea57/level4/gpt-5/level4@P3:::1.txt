Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for latent space inference on large networks. Building on the bag-of-triangles representation (Ho et al., NIPS), the authors reduce the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) in MMTM to O(K) by sharing parameters across equivalence classes of role triplets (all-equal, two-equal, all-distinct). They derive a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients and minibatching over triangles, and further accelerate local updates via an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation. Empirically, PTM is orders of magnitude faster than MMSB and MMTM, scales to >1M nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine, and achieves competitive or better accuracy on latent role recovery and link prediction.

The work is situated alongside MMSB (Airoldi et al.), a-MMSB (Gopalan et al., NIPS) for scalability via parameter tying and SVI, and the triangular/motif-based modeling line (Ho et al., NIPS). The paper’s main novelty is combining the triangles representation with a role-tying scheme that collapses K^3 to K parameters, enabling O(NK) global updates and practical large-K inference.

Strengths:
- Clear modeling idea: principled parameter sharing across role configurations that mirrors a-MMSB’s reduction (from K^2 to K), but adapted to triangle motifs.
- Scalable and practical inference: structured mean-field, natural-gradient SVI, minibatching over motifs, and an O(K) local update approximation. Implementation details (discarding local vars, parallelization) are sensible.
- Strong empirical scaling: convergence in a few passes; runs in hours on million-node graphs with ~100 roles; large speedups over prior methods.
- Solid performance: competitive link prediction and latent role recovery on synthetic and real data.
- Well-written and organized; the algorithmic pipeline is easy to follow.

Weaknesses and questions:
Quality
- Benefit of large K is not convincingly demonstrated. Since both MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership, small-to-moderate K can still express heterogeneous memberships. The paper argues PTM scales in K, but does not show that using larger K materially improves accuracy or interpretability. A study of accuracy vs K (for PTM and baselines) would make the case for large-K modeling.
- Model selection for K is unclear. The real-data experiments do not describe how K was chosen for PTM or MMSB, raising concerns about fairness and possible bias. Providing a consistent selection criterion (e.g., held-out likelihood, validation AUC, or a fixed grid) would strengthen claims.
- Impact of the simplifying assumptions is underexplored. PTM assumes extensive parameter tying across the K^3 space. When does this help or hurt? If the true data require heterogeneous K^3 interactions (e.g., many off-diagonal combinations are genuinely distinct), PTM should degrade. The synthetic experiments do not specify the “true B” and do not include stress tests where PTM’s assumptions are violated.
- Approximation analysis is limited:
  - δ-subsampling of triangles: Sensitivity to δ is not quantified. How do runtime and accuracy trade off as δ varies on synthetic and real graphs? What ranges preserve key motif statistics?
  - Comparison to a-MMSB downsampling: The paper suggests MMSB’s zero-edge downsampling harms accuracy, yet PTM discards non-triangle triples entirely. Why is the triangle-based omission less harmful? A controlled comparison that equalizes the amount of information discarded would be informative.
  - O(K) local update: The mixture-of-deltas approximation is plausible, but there is no sensitivity study on the number/type of off-diagonals sampled (e.g., 2K/4K/100K). Does approximation quality degrade as K grows or in denser role-interaction regimes?

Clarity
- Generally very clear. It would help to specify:
  - How K is set on each dataset, and any hyperparameter tuning.
  - The construction and resampling of the candidate set A for local updates, and the bias/variance implications.
  - The distribution of δ relative to degree across datasets, and practical guidance for choosing δ.

Originality
- Modeling-wise, the parameter-sharing idea is incremental but well-motivated and nontrivial in the triangular setting. The combination with structured SVI and the O(K) local approximation is a useful engineering contribution for scalability.

Significance
- If the parameter-tying assumptions hold broadly, this approach makes large-K latent role inference practical, which is valuable. The link-prediction and scaling results are promising. However, the paper would benefit from a more thorough characterization of when PTM’s assumptions are appropriate.

Pros for acceptance:
- Elegant reduction from O(K^3) to O(K) with a clear intuition and effective implementation.
- Demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedups and ability to handle million-node, hundred-role settings.
- Competitive or improved performance on several tasks and datasets.
- Clear exposition and reproducible algorithmic components.

Cons for acceptance:
- Limited analysis of when the parameter-sharing assumptions fail and how performance degrades.
- No systematic evaluation of performance as a function of K; unclear model selection protocol.
- Missing sensitivity studies for δ and for the O(K) local approximation (size/structure of A).
- Unclear fairness in comparisons when subsampling or discarding information differs across methods.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add accuracy vs K curves for PTM and baselines (including a-MMSB), and report the selection procedure for K (e.g., held-out likelihood or link prediction AUC).
- On synthetic data, vary the true triangle-generating tensor: include cases favoring PTM (strong tying) and cases against it (heterogeneous K^3), and report performance degradation.
- Provide δ-sensitivity analyses (runtime vs accuracy) on synthetic and a few representative real networks; offer practical guidance for choosing δ given degree distributions.
- Evaluate the O(K) local approximation by varying the number and structure of sampled off-diagonals (2K, 4K, 10K, 100K) and by testing larger K; report impact on convergence, bound, and accuracy.
- Clarify the probabilistic interpretation: PTM is a model over bags of motifs and not a generative model of graphs. Position it explicitly as a motif-based likelihood for latent-space inference (akin to composite or pseudo-likelihood), and discuss implications for tasks like link prediction.
- Ensure comparisons control for the amount and type of information subsampled/ignored across methods.

Overall assessment:
A well-written and practically impactful paper that advances scalable latent-space modeling via a thoughtful parameter-tying scheme and efficient inference. With additional analyses on K selection, sensitivity to δ and local approximations, and stress tests against PTM’s modeling assumptions, the contribution would be significantly strengthened.
