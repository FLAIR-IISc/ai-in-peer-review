This paper introduces a method for learning latent structure in social network data by fitting a model to triangle-count statistics obtained via a preprocessing step, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text. The main modeling idea, inspired by aMMSB, is to avoid parameterizing all between-community interactions and instead model triangle configurations to identify a set of K roles. This reduces the number of parameters and yields a more scalable implementation. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving both global and local updates for their model.

The modeling contribution is essentially a straightforward combination of aMMSB with MMTM, albeit with some tedious bookkeeping; the presentation could be clearer. Although much of the learning algorithm follows standard stochastic variational inference, the paper offers an intuitive and interesting approximation for the local update rule. Given the prevalence of directed networks, a detailed discussion of how the approach might extend to directed settings would also be valuable.

Ongoing concerns:
- The claim that “our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs” is puzzling: (1) ERGMs can incorporate far more statistics than triangles, and (2) it is not clear in what sense PTM is a “latent space counterpart,” unless any latent space network model would qualify as such.
- Is the inferred latent space interpretable? If so, what do we learn about similarities and differences among the graphs?
- The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is unclear.
- The paper does not justify why subsampling triangles for each node (using delta) provides an adequate approximation.
- If the goal is to recover the “set of non-zero roles,” the model might benefit from a more explicitly binary notion of “role.”
- How sensitive are the latent space recovery results to the threshold of 0.125 (which seems somewhat arbitrary)?
- Many latent space models can generate power-law networks. Which specific model did you use for the experiments?
- Why does PTM CGS have K^3 complexity? Did you not argue that there are O(K) parameters rather than O(K^3)?
- Performance with fewer observed edges is a concern. The experiments hold out only 10% of edges, presumably because the distribution of adjacent triangles would be heavily biased if too many edges were removed before preprocessing.
- It is counterintuitive that the method outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated by MMSB (Figure 1, left four panels).

In summary, this work uses stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel—with a restricted parameter set—on triangle counts in a graph. The modeling choices and approximations lead to a more scalable O(NK) algorithm.
