- Introduces PTM reducing parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) for scalable inference.  
- Develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm with extra approximations and shows competitive accuracy on synthetic and real data.  
- Practical scalability advantage is unproven: need to show PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K.  
- Choice of K in real-data experiments for PTM and MMSB is unclear and may bias comparisons.  
- Paper fails to analyze when PTM will break: if true B has O(K^3) distinct values, PTM likely performs poorly.  
- Synthetic experiments are biased toward PTM-friendly B matrices and do not probe failure cases.  
- Insufficient evaluation of approximation techniques—impact of the δ threshold on accuracy vs scalability is not explored.  
- Unclear why PTM avoids downsampling issues affecting a-MMSB, given both ignore single-edge triples.  
- No study of how varying the number of sampled triples in the O(K) approximation (e.g., 2K, 4K, 100K) affects results.  
- Experimental setup appears unfair to competitors and potential weaknesses are inadequately described.  
- Paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized.  
- Approach is somewhat incremental but original in targeting scalability via parameter-space constraints.  
- If its simplifying assumptions hold, the method could significantly impact large-scale latent-space modeling.  
- Title is overly broad—the main contribution is a scalable inference algorithm for PTM, not a general latent-space inference approach.  
- Probabilistic claim is questionable: PTM’s "bag-of-triangles" rarely corresponds to valid networks, potentially placing posterior mass on non-networks; the meaning of "probability" in PTM needs clarification.
