This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data by fitting a model to triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance is motivated by the Affiliation Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB), which, rather than parameterizing all between-community interactions, considers the configurations of the triangles to identify a set of K roles to parameterize. This results in fewer parameters in the model and, in turn, a more scalable implementation. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving a global update and local updates for the proposed model.

The novel modeling contribution is a straightforward combination of the aMMSB with the Mixed Membership Matrix Triangulation Model (MMTM), although somewhat tedious bookkeeping was required. However, the presentation of this material could be improved for better clarity.

Although most of the learning algorithm is a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule. Given the prevalence of directed networks, a detailed discussion of how these methods might extend would be welcome.

There are several ongoing concerns with the paper. Firstly, the statement "our Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs), that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs" seems curious. This is because ERGMs can include much more varied statistics than just triangles, and it is unclear how the PTM is a "latent space counterpart," unless all latent space network models are considered "latent space counterparts."

Furthermore, it is unclear whether the inferred latent space is interpretable. If so, what insights do we gain about the similarities and differences among these graphs? Additionally, the difference between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained.

It is also not made clear in the paper why subsampling the triangles for each node (using delta) provides an adequate approximation. If the goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles," perhaps there should be more of a binary notion of "role" in the model.

The robustness of the latent space recovery results to the threshold of 0.125, which seems somewhat arbitrary, is also a concern. Moreover, it is unclear which latent space model was used for the experiments, as a variety of such models can produce power-law networks.

The authors claim that the PTM with collapsed Gibbs sampling (CGS) has K^3 complexity, but this seems inconsistent with their earlier argument that there are O(K) parameters rather than O(K^3).

Another concern is how this method will perform when fewer edges are observed. In the experiments, the authors only hold out 10% of the edges, presumably because the distribution of adjacent triangles will be quite biased if too many edges are removed prior to the preprocessing step.

It is also counterintuitive that the method beats the MMSB on a synthetic network generated via MMSB (Figure 1, left 4 panels). This work uses stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel (with a restricted set of parameters) on the triangle counts in a graph, allowing for a more scalable O(NK) algorithm.
