This paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. Authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce approximation techniques to further improve scalability. Results on synthetic datasets suggest that reducing the number of variables may lead to stronger statistical power, and experiments on real-world datasets show that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but a critical question is the practical advantage of achieving scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles). To empirically evaluate this, it’s crucial to answer how enabling MMTM with large K helps; given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, a small K may not be as limiting as in single-membership models. For real network experiments, it would be valuable to see the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with a large K consistently outperforms MMTM with a small K, it would clearly demonstrate the benefits of the model. Unfortunately, such experiments were not performed. Furthermore, the method of model selection and the choice of K values for both PTM and MMSB in the real data experiments are not thoroughly described, potentially introducing bias into the results.

The authors are somewhat reticent about the implications of their simplifying assumptions. While achieving scalability by constraining the parameter space is clear, it would be beneficial to understand *when* this model might fail. If the true underlying parameter matrix B (following the authors’ notation) of MMSB and MMTM possesses O(K^3) distinct values, PTM should theoretically perform poorly. The lack of experimentation with diverse B matrices—some favoring PTM and others challenging it—is a limitation. It would be more helpful to understand the conditions under which PTM succeeds and fails, rather than only observing success cases.

The paper introduces several techniques to improve PTM’s scalability; however, the description and analysis of the impact of these approximations are insufficient. 1) What is the effect of choosing the threshold δ? Do larger or smaller values of δ perform better in the synthetic and real-world data experiments? How much can δ be reduced to improve scalability without significantly sacrificing accuracy? 2) The authors claim a-MMSB suffers from accuracy compromises due to downsampling; why isn’t PTM subject to the same issue? Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge. Why can’t non-edges be similarly ignored in MMSB? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to the local update (page 5)? What happens if 2K, 4K, or 100K triples are sampled? Would the quality of approximation deteriorate as K increases?

In summary, I am not fully convinced that the experimental setup is fair to competitors, and the discussion of potential model weaknesses is lacking.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:** In terms of modeling, the contribution is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction, particularly as recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

**Significance:** If the simplifying assumptions in the model and the inference algorithm prove practical, it could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to networks, as many existing methods struggle to scale to large datasets.

**Additional Comments:** Personally, I believe the title is overly broad. The authors propose a scalable algorithm *for* a specific latent space model—PTM—rather than a generally scalable approach to all latent space models.

Additionally, I question whether PTM is truly a probabilistic method. The authors draw a parallel to the bag-of-words assumption in the last paragraph of Section 3, but this comparison is not entirely convincing. In LDA, a document corresponds to a generated bag-of-words, possessing a likelihood function, however simple. In contrast, a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM likely does not correspond to a valid network with high probability. Consequently, MMTM/PTM are not genuinely generative models for networks, and the majority of the posterior probability mass is assigned to modeling non-networks. Abandoning fundamental principles of likelihood in “probabilistic” methods raises concerns (what does probability mean in PTM?). I am interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives on this point. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference techniques that make MMTM inference scalable, but the side effects of these decisions are poorly investigated.
