The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. They develop a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior with very large networks. The paper is well written, and well executed.

The main contribution of this paper is a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors propose a succinct representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. This approach allows for the analysis of real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours, which is a significant improvement over existing methods.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem of latent space inference in networks and the limitations of existing approaches. They also provide a detailed description of their proposed method, including the representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, the parsimonious statistical model, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm.

The authors evaluate their method on a variety of synthetic and real networks, and demonstrate its ability to recover latent space roles and perform link prediction tasks. They also compare their method to existing approaches, including the mixed-membership stochastic blockmodel (MMSB) and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM), and show that it is more scalable and accurate.

One potential limitation of the paper is that it does not provide a clear definition of "parsimonious" modeling. While the authors use this term to describe their proposed method, it is not clear what they mean by it. Adding a sentence or two to explain this term would be helpful.
