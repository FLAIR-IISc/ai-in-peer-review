

The authors present a scalable method for latent space inference in large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) and stochastic variational inference. Their approach represents networks as bags of triangular motifs, which enables a more compact parameterization (O(K) instead of O(K³) for previous methods) and a scalable inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity. This allows analysis of networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine within hours, significantly outperforming existing methods like MMSB and MMTM in both speed and scalability.

The paper demonstrates strong technical contributions: the PTM's parameter sharing scheme effectively reduces model complexity while maintaining accuracy, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm converges rapidly (typically within 2-5 passes) even for massive networks. The experimental results on synthetic and real-world networks confirm the method's superior scalability (orders of magnitude faster than previous approaches) while achieving competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction.

Strengths:
- The paper successfully addresses the three key principles for large-scale network analysis: succinct representation (triangular motifs), parsimonious modeling (O(K) parameters), and scalable inference (O(NK) algorithm).
- The parameter sharing strategy for the triangular model is well-motivated and effectively reduces complexity without sacrificing accuracy.
- The stochastic variational inference approach is well-justified and implemented efficiently, with clear convergence properties demonstrated in experiments.
- The experimental section thoroughly validates the method's performance on both synthetic and real-world networks, with compelling speed and accuracy comparisons to state-of-the-art approaches.

Weaknesses:
- The term "parsimonious" is used without sufficient explanation in the introduction; it should be defined as part of the technical contribution to clarify its significance in the probabilistic modeling context.
- The claim that "down-sampling zero elements in the matrix" (for a-MMSB) "is still not fast enough to handle networks with N ≥ 100,000" appears misleading based on Gopalan et al.'s findings, which showed that down-sampling does not compromise accuracy.
- The defense of the model's validity by comparing it to LDA is problematic; while the triangle model may not generate valid networks, this is a pragmatic modeling choice that should be acknowledged without inappropriate comparison to LDA's generative process.
- The paper should cite Hoffman et al.'s stochastic variational inference work rather than Amari and Sato, as the latter does not apply to the local variable setting described in the paper.
- The generation process for power-law networks in the latent space is described too vaguely in the experiments section.
- The paper should include a comparison to Gopalan et al.'s a-MMSB algorithm rather than solely comparing to the original MMSB batch method.

Minor issues:
- "mixture-membership" should be corrected to "mixed-membership" on page 2.
- The final sentence of the paper (on page 8) should be removed as the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger concluding statement.
- The bibliography should cite the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference" by Hoffman et al.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of network analysis with clear technical innovations and significant practical impact. The paper is well-written and well-executed, with thorough experimental validation. The minor issues noted are easily addressable and do not detract from the paper's substantial technical contributions. I recommend acceptance.
