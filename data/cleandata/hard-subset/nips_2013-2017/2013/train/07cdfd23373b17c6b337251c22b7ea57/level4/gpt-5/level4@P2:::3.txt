Summary
This paper proposes a scalable latent-space approach for large networks using a bag-of-triangles representation. Building on prior work on triangular modeling (e.g., MMTM), the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K3) to O(K) by sharing parameters across classes of role configurations. They develop a structured stochastic variational inference algorithm that uses minibatches of triangles and natural gradients, achieving near O(NK) inference. Empirically, the method processes networks with over a million nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine within hours, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over edge-based probabilistic methods (e.g., MMSB) and Gibbs samplers for triangle-based models, with competitive or improved accuracy for latent role recovery and link prediction. This work relates to mixed-membership models for networks (MMSB, a-MMSB), ERGM statistics (triangles), and scalable SVI for hierarchical models.

Evaluation
- Quality: The modeling and inference contributions are technically sound. The structured mean-field choice is appropriate, and the natural-gradient SVI is well motivated. Results are convincing and consistent with the claimed complexity and scalability, though some comparisons are missing (see below).
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. A few terms and claims need clarification, and some citations should be adjusted to reflect the correct theory.
- Originality: The parameter-sharing scheme that yields O(K) triangle parameters is a notable innovation in this modeling family, and combining it with structured SVI for triangle data is a useful contribution.
- Significance: The ability to infer hundreds of roles on million-node networks on a single machine is significant. The triangle representation is pragmatic and aligns with known importance of triadic structure in networks.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for triangle-based representation and its scalability benefits.
- Elegant O(K) parameterization that preserves key structural distinctions while enabling fast global updates.
- Structured variational treatment of local role triples, with an effective O(K) approximation for local updates.
- Thorough empirical evidence of speedups and strong performance in latent role recovery and link prediction.
- Implementation details (minibatching, natural gradients, parallelization) are practical and reproducible.

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- p1: Define “parsimonious” modeling. It is central to your contribution but not standard terminology for this context. A brief explanation (e.g., parameter sharing across role configurations to reduce complexity with minimal loss of expressiveness) would help.
- p1: Your introduction implies that down-sampling zeros in a-MMSB compromises accuracy. Gopalan et al. emphasize that their negative-edge subsampling maintains accuracy under their stochastic variational scheme. Please clarify or soften this statement.
- p4: The analogy to LDA is not persuasive. LDA’s generative process still produces valid documents, whereas your model can generate sets of triangles that do not correspond to a valid network. This is not fatal for inference on observed triangles, but LDA is not an appropriate defense; consider reframing the argument around sufficiency/pragmatism and ERGM practice.
- p5: Cite Hoffman et al. (JMLR SVI) alongside Amari and Sato, and be precise about what theory you rely on. With local variables (triangle-specific role assignments), Sato’s online VB derivations do not apply as stated; the relevant convergence and gradient results are in Hoffman et al. The “natural gradient” connection should also cite Amari.
- p6: The procedure for generating power-law networks from a latent space is under-specified. Please detail the generator, parameters, and references sufficient to reproduce the simulations.
- p6: Since you position a-MMSB (Gopalan et al.) as a scalable MMSB variant, you should compare against their algorithm, not only MMSB batch. Even a smaller-scale comparison would strengthen the empirical case.
- Modeling caveat: While the paper is transparent that the PTM is not a full network generator, a brief discussion of when the triangle bag suffices for accurate downstream tasks (e.g., under what degree/triadic closure regimes, or via empirical ablations) would be valuable.
- Experimental clarity: For the latent role recovery metric, briefly justify the 0.125 threshold and report sensitivity, if possible.
- Minor clarity: Summarize the O(K) local update approximation trade-offs (bias-variance) and how often A is resampled; add a short complexity table contrasting PTM vs. MMTM vs. MMSB.
- Reproducibility: Include key hyperparameters (α, λ, step-size schedule) and thread counts near the main experimental results, not only in the appendix.

Small comments
- p2: “mixture-membership” → “mixed-membership”.
- p8: Consider removing the final sentence; the penultimate sentence is a stronger conclusion.
- Bibliography: Cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference” (Hoffman et al., JMLR 2013).

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Strong, principled scalability via O(K) parameterization and structured SVI.
- Extensive and convincing empirical results, including million-node runs on a single machine.
- Clear practical impact for large-scale network analysis.

Cons:
- Missing direct comparison to a-MMSB.
- Some theoretical attributions/citations should be corrected.
- A few claims in the introduction are overstated; minor clarifications needed.
- Simulation details (power-law generator) are insufficiently specified.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper offers a meaningful modeling advance and an efficient inference scheme that together enable practical large-scale latent space analysis with strong empirical performance. Addressing the citation corrections, clarifying claims, adding details on the power-law generator, and including a comparison to a-MMSB (if feasible) would further strengthen the final version.
