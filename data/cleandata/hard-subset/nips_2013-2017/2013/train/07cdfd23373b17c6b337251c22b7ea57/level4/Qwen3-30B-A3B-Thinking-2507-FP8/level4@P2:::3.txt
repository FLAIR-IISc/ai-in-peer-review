

The authors present a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks, addressing a critical challenge in network analysis. They build upon the triangular motif representation introduced by Ho et al. (NIPS 2013) and propose a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that dramatically reduces the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) through clever parameter sharing. This enables efficient stochastic variational inference with O(NK) complexity, allowing analysis of networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in hours, rather than days required by existing methods.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by successfully addressing all three critical challenges for large-scale network analysis: succinct network representation (via triangular motifs), parsimonious statistical modeling (via parameter sharing), and scalable inference algorithms (via stochastic variational inference). This represents a meaningful advancement over previous work like the mixed-membership stochastic blockmodel (MMSB) and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM), which fail to simultaneously achieve scalability and sufficient accuracy.

Strengths of the paper include:
1. Technical quality: The authors provide a thorough theoretical foundation for their parameter-sharing approach, with clear mathematical formulation of the PTM and its advantages over MMTM. The stochastic variational inference algorithm is well-motivated and rigorously derived.
2. Experimental validation: The authors demonstrate compelling empirical results on both synthetic and real-world networks, showing their method achieves competitive or improved accuracy compared to state-of-the-art approaches while being orders of magnitude faster. The convergence behavior on large networks (e.g., Brightkite and Stanford networks) is particularly impressive.
3. Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of complex concepts and well-structured arguments. The authors effectively communicate the significance of their approach through carefully designed experiments.
4. Originality: While building on the triangular motif representation from Ho et al. (NIPS 2013), the authors introduce a novel parameter-sharing strategy that fundamentally changes the scalability properties of the model, addressing a gap that previous work failed to resolve.

The paper has a few minor weaknesses that could be addressed:
1. The term "parsimonious modeling" appears early without sufficient explanation. While the concept is clear from context, a brief definition would help readers unfamiliar with this specific usage in probabilistic modeling.
2. The claim that "down-sampling zero elements in the matrix" does not compromise accuracy (p.1) appears misleading when compared to Gopalan et al. (2013), who demonstrate that such down-sampling preserves accuracy. The authors should clarify this point to avoid confusion.
3. The paper's defense of their model's generative capabilities by comparing to LDA (p.4) is not entirely appropriate. While the authors correctly note that their model doesn't generate networks in the same way LDA generates documents, the comparison to LDA is misleading since the goal is latent space inference rather than network generation.
4. The paper is somewhat vague about how power-law networks were generated in the synthetic experiments (p.6). A brief technical description would strengthen the reproducibility of their results.

The paper is well-organized and makes a clear contribution to the field of scalable network analysis. The authors have demonstrated both the theoretical soundness and practical utility of their approach through rigorous experiments on large-scale networks. Their method represents a significant step forward in making probabilistic latent space models applicable to real-world social networks with millions of nodes.

I recommend this paper for acceptance at NIPS. It addresses a critical problem in network analysis with a technically sound solution that has clear practical implications. The paper is well-written, well-executed, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field. The authors have successfully balanced theoretical innovation with practical scalability, demonstrating that their approach can handle networks that are orders of magnitude larger than what previous methods could process.

The authors should address the minor points mentioned above in the final version, particularly the clarification about down-sampling accuracy and the explanation of "parsimonious modeling." These are easily addressed and would further strengthen an already strong paper.
