This paper proposes the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) to enable faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and introduce additional approximation techniques to further improve scalability. On synthetic data, they show that reducing the number of variables can increase statistical power, and on real-world datasets, the proposed method is competitive with existing approaches in terms of accuracy.

Quality:
PTM is an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical value of scalability in K (the number of roles) is unclear. To evaluate the utility of such scalability, it is crucial to answer: “How does it help if we can learn MMTM with large K?” Because MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may be less problematic than in single-membership models. For real-network experiments, it would be especially informative to report performance for both MMTM and PTM as a function of K. If PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K, that would substantiate the benefit of PTM. Unfortunately, such experiments are missing. Moreover, I could not find a clear description of how K was selected in the real-data experiments for PTM or for the MMSB competitor; the model-selection procedure could have biased the results.

The authors are also somewhat reticent about the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space obviously improves scalability, it would be valuable to discuss when the model would fail. If the true parameter matrix B (in the authors’ notation) for MMSB/MMTM genuinely has O(K^3) distinct values, PTM should perform poorly, correct? In this light, it is unsatisfactory that the choice of the true B in the synthetic experiments is not clearly described. The paper should evaluate a variety of B’s—some that favor PTM and some that do not. It would be more informative for readers to learn when PTM succeeds and when it fails, rather than only when it succeeds.

The paper also introduces several approximation tricks to scale PTM; while these seem interesting and effective, their effects are not well described or analyzed.
1) What is the impact of the threshold delta? In both real and synthetic experiments, does a larger or smaller delta help? How low can delta go to improve scalability without materially sacrificing accuracy?
2) The authors claim that a-MMSB performs poorly because downsampling harms accuracy. Why is PTM immune to this problem? MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one observed edge. Why is it acceptable to ignore non-edges in PTM but not in MMSB?
3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to the local update (page 5)? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Does the quality of the approximation degrade as K grows?

To sum up, I am not convinced the experimental setup was fair to the competitors, and the discussion of potential weaknesses in the model and algorithm is insufficient.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized.

Originality:
From a modeling perspective it is somewhat incremental, but pursuing scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction, especially given that recent work in this area has focused more on generalizing existing models.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference procedure hold in practice, the work could meaningfully impact the use of latent-space models on networks, many of which do not scale to large datasets.

Additional Comments:
- The title seems too broad. There are other scalable approaches for other latent-space models, and this paper proposes a scalable algorithm for one particular latent-space model—PTM.
- I am not convinced that PTM is truly probabilistic. The authors argue in Section 3 that the setup is analogous to the bag-of-words assumption, but I find this unpersuasive. In LDA, every bag-of-words corresponds to some document, so the model has a well-defined likelihood, even if the assumption is simplistic. In contrast, with very high probability there is no actual network consistent with a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM/PTM. Thus, MMTM/PTM is not really a generative model for networks, and the posterior mass is largely placed on non-networks. It seems risky to abandon basic likelihood principles in a “probabilistic” method—what, precisely, does probability mean in PTM? I would be interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ views. The paper proposes modeling assumptions and inference tricks that make inference in MMTM scalable, but the side effects of these choices are insufficiently explored.
