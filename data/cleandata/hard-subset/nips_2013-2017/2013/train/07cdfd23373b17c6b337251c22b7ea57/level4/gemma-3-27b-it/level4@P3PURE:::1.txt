This paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and incorporate additional approximation tricks to further improve scalability. Results on synthetic data suggest that reducing the number of variables can lead to stronger statistical power, and experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical advantage of achieving good scalability with respect to K (the number of possible roles) remains unclear. To empirically evaluate the value of this method, it is crucial to understand “how does it help if we can learn MMTM with large K?” Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may not be as problematic as it is in single-membership models. For real network experiments, it would be particularly insightful to observe the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K: if PTM with a large K demonstrably outperforms MMTM with a small K, it would strongly support the benefits of the proposed approach. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the rationale behind the choice of K values in the real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB is not clearly explained, raising concerns that the model selection process may have introduced bias into the experimental results.

The authors are also somewhat reserved regarding the implications of their simplifying assumptions. While it is evident that constraining the parameter space leads to scalability, it would be valuable to understand under what conditions this model might perform poorly. If the true parameter matrix B (following the authors' notation) of MMSB and MMTM contains a substantial number of O(K^3) distinct values, PTM’s performance should suffer. In this regard, the description of the true B used in the synthetic data experiments is insufficient; the authors should have experimented with a wider range of B matrices, including some that favor PTM and others that challenge the model. It would be more informative for readers to understand both the strengths and weaknesses of PTM, rather than solely focusing on scenarios where it succeeds. 

The paper introduces several techniques to enhance PTM’s scalability, which are interesting and potentially effective. However, the description and analysis of the impact of these approximations are weak. Specifically: 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold δ? In both real-world and synthetic data experiments, does a larger or smaller δ prove more beneficial? How much can δ be lowered to improve scalability without significantly compromising accuracy? 2) The authors claim that a-MMSB is ineffective due to accuracy loss from downsampling; however, the reasoning behind why PTM avoids this problem is not clearly articulated. Both MMTM and PTM disregard triplets with only one edge. Why can't we similarly ignore non-edges in MMSB? 3) What is the effect of the O(K) approximation on the local update step (page 5)? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K of triples? Would the quality of the approximation degrade as K increases?

In summary, I am not convinced the experimental setup provides a fair comparison to competitors, and the discussion of the algorithm/model’s potential limitations is lacking.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:** In terms of modeling, the contribution is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability by constraining parameters represents an interesting and original direction, particularly given the recent trend toward generalizing existing models in this field.

**Significance:** If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large networks, as many such models struggle with scalability.

**Additional Comments:** I believe the title is overly broad. Numerous approaches exist for scaling other latent space models, and the authors present a scalable algorithm *for* a specific latent space model—PTM. 

Furthermore, I question whether PTM is truly a probabilistic method. The authors argue in the final paragraph of Section 3 that it shares similarities with the bag-of-words assumption. However, in LDA, a bag-of-words always corresponds to an underlying document with a defined (albeit potentially simplistic) likelihood function. In contrast, there is typically no corresponding network associated with a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM; therefore, these models do not genuinely generate networks, and the majority of their posterior probability mass is assigned to modeling non-networks. It may be risky to abandon the fundamental principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" methods – what does probability actually *mean* in PTM? I am interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives on this point. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks to make MMTM scalable, but the side effects of these decisions are not sufficiently investigated.
