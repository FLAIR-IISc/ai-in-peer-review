- Introduces a triangle-count-based latent-structure model (PTM), analogous to bag-of-words for text, using triangle configurations to represent nodes.  
- Adapts assortative MMSB (aMMSB) by using triangle configurations to identify K roles, reducing parameter count versus modeling all between-community interactions.  
- Leverages stochastic variational inference (SVI) with derived global and local updates to enable a scalable implementation.  
- Core contribution (combining aMMSB with MMTM) is conceptually straightforward but involves tedious bookkeeping and could be presented more clearly.  
- Authors provide an intuitive approximation for local updates, but its derivation and properties need more justification.  
- Paper lacks discussion of extending the approach to directed networks, which are common in practice.  
- Claim that “PTM is essentially a latent-space counterpart to ERGMs for triangles” is imprecise and needs clarification.  
- Unclear whether inferred latent spaces are interpretable and what insights they give about graph similarities/differences.  
- The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² is not clearly explained.  
- Justification for subsampling triangles per node (via Δ) as an adequate approximation is insufficient.  
- If the goal is sparse/non-zero role recovery, an explicit binary role formulation might be more appropriate than the current approach.  
- Robustness of latent space recovery to the arbitrary-looking 0.125 threshold is unaddressed.  
- The paper does not state which latent-space model generated the power-law networks used in experiments.  
- PTM CGS implementation reportedly has K³ complexity, contradicting the paper’s O(K) parameter claim and needing reconciliation.  
- Evaluation under sparse edge regimes is concerning: holding out 10% of edges may bias triangle distributions and limit generalizability to sparser graphs.  
- It is counterintuitive that PTM outperforms MMSB on synthetic data generated by MMSB (Figure 1 left); this requires deeper analysis.  
- Overall: SVI and modeling choices enable an O(NK) algorithm, but technical claims and methodological details need stronger justification to support claimed advantages.
