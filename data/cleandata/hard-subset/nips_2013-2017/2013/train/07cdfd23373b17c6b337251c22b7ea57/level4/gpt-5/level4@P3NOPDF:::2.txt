Summary
The paper proposes a parsimonious latent-role model for social networks that operates on triangle-count statistics, analogous to a bag-of-words representation for documents. Building on ideas from assortative MMSB and motif-based models (e.g., MMTM), the authors parameterize interactions via configurations of triangular motifs to identify K roles, yielding fewer parameters and improved scalability. They develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) procedure with global and local updates, including an intuitive approximation for the local step. Empirically, the method appears to scale linearly in N and K.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using triangle statistics to reduce parameterization relative to fully specified blockmodels.
- The role-based parameterization is parsimonious and, if effective, can substantially reduce complexity while capturing higher-order structure.
- The SVI development is mostly standard but well adapted; the local update approximation is appealing and practically useful.
- The overall approach—learning on aggregated triangular motifs—offers a potentially scalable alternative to edge-level modeling.

Weaknesses and Questions
- Novelty: The core modeling idea reads as a relatively straightforward combination of aMMSB with an existing triangle/motif model (MMTM), albeit with nontrivial bookkeeping. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of what is genuinely new beyond this combination and the SVI adaptation.
- ERGM comparison: The claim that “PTM is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs” is misleading. ERGMs can incorporate many statistics beyond triangles, and the notion of “latent space counterpart” is too broad. Please refine or remove this claim, or justify it precisely (e.g., in what sense of sufficiency or equivalence?).
- Interpretability: Are the inferred roles interpretable? What structural patterns do the roles capture across datasets? Providing representative node-role distributions, a role–role interaction summary, and qualitative examples would help readers understand what the model learns.
- Directed graphs: Given the prevalence of directed networks, please discuss how the model and inference would extend to directed motifs (e.g., triads with edge direction), and what computational or modeling challenges arise.
- Delta^1 vs. Delta^2: The distinction is unclear. Please define both rigorously, explain their roles in the updates, and describe how they affect bias/variance, convergence, and runtime.
- Subsampling justification: The paper should justify why subsampling triangles per node using delta yields acceptable accuracy. Is the estimator unbiased or asymptotically consistent? What is the variance as a function of delta and graph sparsity? Empirical sensitivity curves would be helpful.
- Binary/sparse roles: If the objective is to recover a “set of nonzero roles,” the model could profit from sparsity-inducing priors or explicit binary latent variables (e.g., IBP/Beta-Bernoulli, spike-and-slab) to sharpen role selection. Please discuss whether such alternatives were considered.
- Threshold sensitivity: The choice of 0.125 for identifying nonzero roles seems arbitrary. How robust are the conclusions to this threshold? Show sensitivity analyses across a range of thresholds.
- Power-law networks: Many latent-space models produce heavy-tailed degree distributions. Which specific generative model did you use in the experiments, and how were its parameters chosen? Clarify how well it aligns with PTM’s assumptions.
- Complexity mismatch: You state that PTM CGS has K^3 complexity, yet the parameter count is argued to be O(K) rather than O(K^3). Please reconcile these statements. If sampling or evaluating triangle configurations scales as K^3 (even with O(K) parameters), explain where the cubic factor enters and how SVI avoids it.
- Data sparsity and preprocessing: Performance when many edges are missing is concerning, since triangle counts degrade quickly with edge removal. The experiments hold out only 10% of edges; please evaluate higher missingness levels and discuss whether preprocessing introduces bias. How would the method behave for link prediction when only a small fraction of edges are observed?
- Synthetic results: It is counterintuitive that PTM outperforms MMSB on data generated by MMSB (Fig. 1, left). Provide a careful explanation: Is it due to regularization from parsimony, estimation error in MMSB, the triangle aggregation yielding better signal-to-noise, or differences in how inference was tuned? Include diagnostics that rule out optimization or hyperparameter artifacts.

Clarity and Presentation
- The exposition of the triangle configurations and their mapping to K roles should be made more concrete. A concise generative story and a small, worked example would assist readers.
- The derivation of the local update approximation is one of the most interesting technical elements; consider expanding its intuition, approximation error, and when it is expected to help or hurt.
- Provide clearer notation for all triangle-related statistics, and a table mapping symbols (including Delta^1 and Delta^2) to definitions.
- Include runtime and memory complexity per iteration, and wall-clock comparisons on large graphs to substantiate the O(NK) claim.

Relation to Prior Work
- Position the paper more carefully relative to MMSB variants, motif-based latent models, and ERGMs with triad statistics. Distinguish your contribution along the axes of parameter parsimony, inference approach, and empirical scalability.

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add ablations varying delta (triangle subsampling rate), K, and the nonzero-role threshold; report effects on accuracy, calibration, and runtime.
- Explore sparsity-inducing priors to yield sharper role recovery and reduce sensitivity to thresholds.
- Include directed-network experiments or, at minimum, a concrete extension plan with complexity estimates.
- Revisit the ERGM comparison to avoid overclaiming and clarify conceptual connections.
- Provide additional evaluation metrics (e.g., triangle closure prediction, motif count reconstruction) alongside link prediction.

Overall Assessment
The paper presents a sensible and potentially impactful approach to scalable higher-order structure learning via triangle aggregation and SVI. While the technical novelty over combining aMMSB with a motif model is moderate, the parsimony and practical approximation in the local update are appealing. The work would be strengthened by clearer exposition, rigorous justification of the subsampling scheme, sensitivity analyses, and reconciliation of complexity claims. With these clarifications and additional experiments, the contribution could be solid and useful for practitioners working with large-scale network data.
