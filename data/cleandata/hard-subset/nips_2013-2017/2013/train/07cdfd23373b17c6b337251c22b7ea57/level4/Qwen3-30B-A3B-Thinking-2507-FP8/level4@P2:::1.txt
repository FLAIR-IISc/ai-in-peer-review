

This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that reduces the parameter space from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference, along with a stochastic variational inference algorithm featuring several approximation techniques. The method demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over state-of-the-art approaches while maintaining competitive accuracy on both synthetic and real-world networks for latent space recovery and link prediction.

Quality: The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing three key scalability principles for large network analysis: succinct representation (using triangular motifs), parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference. The O(K) parameterization is well-motivated and effectively implemented. However, the experimental evaluation has significant limitations that weaken the claims of superiority. Most critically, the authors fail to demonstrate PTM's advantage over MMTM when K is large, which is the core contribution of the paper. A proper comparison would require PTM with large K against MMTM with small K, but the paper does not provide this comparison. The real-world experiments also lack clarity on how K was chosen for both PTM and MMSB, potentially introducing bias.

The paper does not adequately explore PTM's failure modes. If the true parameter matrix B has O(K^3) distinct values (as might occur in realistic scenarios), PTM would perform poorly, but the authors do not investigate this case. Their synthetic experiments used a specific B that favors PTM without testing cases where PTM would fail. The paper would be much stronger if it addressed when PTM succeeds and when it fails.

The paper also lacks thorough analysis of key parameters. The impact of the triangle subsampling threshold δ is not properly evaluated—how does choosing larger or smaller δ affect accuracy versus scalability? The claim that PTM avoids downsampling accuracy issues while a-MMSB does not is unexplained, especially since both methods ignore single-edge triples. The O(K) approximation to local update (Section 4.3) is not sufficiently analyzed—how does approximation quality change with different numbers of sampled triples (2K, 4K, 100K) as K grows?

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized, with clear technical explanations and sufficient detail for reproduction.

Originality: While the modeling approach builds on MMTM, the focus on parameter space reduction for scalability represents an interesting and novel direction in network analysis. Most recent work has focused on generalizing existing models, whereas this paper takes a different approach by making the model more efficient.

Significance: If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, this work could have significant practical impact by enabling latent space modeling on networks with millions of nodes—addressing a critical gap where many existing methods fail to scale. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications involving large social networks.

Additional Concerns: The title is overly broad, suggesting a general scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference, when the paper specifically addresses a single model (PTM). More critically, the paper's claim that PTM is a probabilistic method is questionable. Unlike LDA, where each bag of words corresponds to a document, PTM generates bags of triangles that rarely correspond to actual networks. This means the model places most probability mass on non-networks, which deviates significantly from standard probabilistic modeling principles. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of what "probability" means in PTM's context.

Arguments for acceptance: The method achieves impressive scalability (1M-node networks in hours on a single machine) while maintaining competitive accuracy, and the paper is exceptionally well-written.

Arguments against acceptance: The experimental design lacks a critical comparison between PTM with large K and MMTM with small K, the model's limitations are not thoroughly explored, and the probabilistic foundation is questionable.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable approach to scalable network analysis with strong empirical results, but the claims of superiority are not fully supported by the evidence presented. The work has practical significance but requires stronger experimental validation to justify the main contribution.
