The authors present a scalable approximate inference framework for triangle-based network models. They introduce a well-designed subclass with thoughtful parameter sharing, and employ stochastic variational inference to perform posterior estimation on very large graphs. The paper is clearly written and carefully executed. I recommend acceptance.

p1: Please clarify what you mean by “parsimonious” modeling. This isn’t a routine term in probabilistic modeling; a brief operational definition would help, especially given its central role here.

p1: My reading of Gopalan et al. suggests that down-sampling zeros does not degrade accuracy. The sentence in the introduction implying otherwise is misleading and should be revised.

p4: I’m sympathetic to the practical merits of the triangle representation, but the analogy to LDA is not appropriate. Sampling from LDA’s generative process yields an object that is still a (possibly odd) document, whereas sampling from your model can produce collections of triangles that are not valid networks. This doesn’t undermine the utility of your approach, but the comparison to LDA should be reconsidered.

p5: Please cite Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato. In the presence of “local variables” (in Hoffman’s terminology), Sato’s algorithm does not apply; the relevant theory for your setting is provided by Hoffman et al.

p6: The procedure for generating power-law networks in latent space is underspecified. Please provide more details.

p6: Since you cite Gopalan et al. as a scalable improvement for MMSB, you should include a comparison to their method in addition to (or instead of) the MMSB batch baseline.

Small comments
- p2: mixture-membership → mixed-membership.
- p8: Consider removing the final sentence; the penultimate sentence provides a stronger conclusion.
- Bibliography: Please cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference.”

Overall, the work is strong: a principled subclass with effective parameter sharing, a scalable SVI algorithm, and compelling empirical results on very large graphs. I recommend acceptance.
