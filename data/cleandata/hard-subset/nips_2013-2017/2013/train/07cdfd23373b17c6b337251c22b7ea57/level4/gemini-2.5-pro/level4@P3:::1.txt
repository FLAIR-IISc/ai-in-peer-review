### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scalable latent space inference for large-scale networks. The authors build upon previous work, particularly the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), which represents networks as a "bag of triangular motifs." The primary contribution is a new model, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which dramatically reduces the parameter complexity of MMTM from O(K³) to O(K), where K is the number of latent roles. This is achieved by sharing parameters for different configurations of latent role triplets. To leverage this parsimonious structure, the authors develop a scalable stochastic variational inference algorithm. This algorithm is further accelerated by several effective approximations, most notably a "mixture-of-deltas" approach for the local updates that reduces its complexity from O(K³) to O(K) per triangle. Experimental results on both synthetic and large real-world networks (up to 1.1M nodes) demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than existing approaches like MMTM and MMSB, while achieving competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The paper tackles a highly significant problem in network analysis. The ability to perform latent space inference on million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine in hours is a major practical achievement. The demonstrated speedup over prior state-of-the-art methods is substantial and impressive.

2.  **Originality and Novel Combination of Ideas:** While the model is an extension of MMTM, the core idea of achieving scalability through aggressive parameter parsimony is an interesting and valuable direction. The combination of this model with a carefully designed stochastic variational inference algorithm and clever approximations (like the O(K) local update) represents a novel and effective engineering contribution.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established, the model is described in sufficient detail, and the inference algorithm is explained logically.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

Despite its impressive performance, the paper could be strengthened by addressing several key issues related to the evaluation and the underlying assumptions of the model and inference procedure.

1.  **Motivation for Scalability in K:** The paper successfully demonstrates scalability in the number of roles, K. However, the practical motivation for needing a very large K is not fully justified. In mixed-membership models, a small K can often suffice since nodes can belong to multiple communities. The most compelling argument for PTM would be to show that on a given task, PTM with a large K (which is now feasible) outperforms MMTM or MMSB with a small K (to which they are limited). Such an experiment is missing, which makes it difficult to assess the true value of scaling to large K beyond just a technical achievement. Furthermore, the method for choosing K in the real-world experiments is not described, which could potentially bias the comparison with competitor methods.

2.  **Analysis of Model and Approximation Limitations:** The paper is reticent about the potential downsides of its simplifying assumptions.
    *   **Model Failure Modes:** The core assumption of PTM is that the O(K³) parameter space of MMTM can be compressed to O(K). It would be highly informative to understand when this assumption might fail. For instance, if a network's true generative process involves complex, asymmetric interactions between many distinct roles, PTM would likely perform poorly. The synthetic experiments could have been used to explore this by generating data from a full-rank O(K³) parameter tensor and evaluating PTM's performance. Providing insight into when the model succeeds *and* fails would be more beneficial to the reader.
    *   **Impact of Approximations:** The authors introduce several effective algorithmic "tricks," but their impact is not analyzed. For example:
        *   What is the sensitivity of the results to the triangle subsampling threshold δ? A study showing the trade-off between speed (lower δ) and accuracy (higher δ) would be valuable.
        *   The paper criticizes a-MMSB for downsampling non-edges, but PTM itself ignores all triplets with fewer than two edges. The justification for why one form of data reduction is acceptable while the other is not warrants a clearer discussion.
        *   How does the quality of the O(K) "mixture-of-deltas" local update approximation change as more role combinations are sampled (e.g., 2K, 4K, etc.)? Does its quality degrade for larger K?

3.  **Fundamental Questions about the Generative Model:** There is a fundamental concern about whether PTM is a valid probabilistic model for networks. The paper acknowledges that the generative process for the bag-of-triangles may produce a set of motifs that do not correspond to any valid network graph. This is a crucial distinction from models like LDA, where a generated bag-of-words always corresponds to a valid document. In PTM, the posterior probability mass is largely concentrated on objects that are not networks. This departure from the basic principles of likelihood-based modeling for graphs is significant and potentially problematic. It raises questions about the statistical interpretation of the model's posterior and likelihood, which should be discussed more thoroughly.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper presents a significant engineering contribution by developing an impressively fast and scalable approach for latent space inference on large networks. The combination of a parsimonious model and a highly optimized stochastic inference algorithm is powerful. However, the scientific evaluation feels incomplete. The paper would be much stronger if it provided a more compelling motivation for needing large K, a more thorough analysis of the limitations of its core assumptions and approximations, and a deeper discussion of the statistical implications of its generative process. While the scalability is a major strength, the lack of robustness in the evaluation and analysis of potential weaknesses is a notable concern.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Presents a method that is orders of magnitude faster than previous state-of-the-art, enabling analysis on a new scale.
*   The combination of model parsimony and scalable inference techniques is novel and of high practical interest.
*   The paper is clearly written and the empirical results, as presented, are strong.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The experimental evaluation lacks depth, particularly in motivating the need for large K and in exploring the model's failure modes.
*   The impact and sensitivity of key approximations in the inference algorithm are not analyzed.
*   There are fundamental questions about the validity of the generative story that are not adequately addressed.
