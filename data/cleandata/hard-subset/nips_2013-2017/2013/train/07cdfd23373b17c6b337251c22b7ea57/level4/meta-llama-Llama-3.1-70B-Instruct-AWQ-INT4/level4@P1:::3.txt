The authors develop a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks. They develop a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way, and they use stochastic variational inference to infer the posterior with very large networks. The paper is well written, and well executed. I think it is acceptable.

Page 1: The term "parsimonious" is used to describe the proposed model. For clarity, it would be helpful to unpack this term in a sentence, as it is crucial to the paper. Parsimonious modeling refers to the approach of using the fewest parameters necessary to achieve a good fit to the data, thereby reducing the risk of overfitting.

Page 1: The statement regarding the down-sampling of zeros in Gopalan et al. is misleading. According to their work, down-sampling the zeros does not compromise accuracy.

Page 4: The comparison between the proposed model and LDA is not entirely accurate. While LDA can generate documents that may not be meaningful, the generative process of LDA still produces something that can be considered a document. In contrast, the proposed model can generate objects that are not networks, which is a different issue. However, this does not detract from the practical value of the model.

Page 5: The authors should cite Hoffman et al. in addition to Amari and Sato. Hoffman's work provides the necessary theory for stochastic variational inference in scenarios with local variables.

Page 6: The paper lacks clarity on how power-law networks were generated in the latent space. This point should be elaborated upon.

Page 6: The authors should compare their algorithm to Gopalan et al.'s scalable a-MMSB algorithm, in addition to or instead of the MMSB batch algorithm.

Minor comments:

Page 2: "Mixture-membership" should be changed to "mixed-membership".

Page 8: The last sentence can be removed, as the second-to-last sentence provides a stronger conclusion.

In the bibliography, the authors should cite the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference".

Quality: The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors are careful and honest in evaluating both the strengths and weaknesses of their work.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and adequately informs the reader. The authors provide enough information for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

Originality: The paper presents a novel combination of familiar techniques, and it is clear how this work differs from previous contributions. The authors adequately reference related work.

Significance: The results are important, and the paper addresses a difficult problem in a better way than previous research. The authors provide unique data, unique conclusions on existing data, or a unique theoretical or pragmatic approach.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper develops a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the "triangle model" of networks.
* The authors develop a powerful subclass of the model that shares parameters in a clever way.
* The paper is well written, and well executed.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The paper may benefit from additional comparisons to other algorithms, such as Gopalan et al.'s scalable a-MMSB algorithm.
* The authors could provide more clarity on how power-law networks were generated in the latent space.
* The last sentence of the paper can be removed to provide a stronger conclusion.
