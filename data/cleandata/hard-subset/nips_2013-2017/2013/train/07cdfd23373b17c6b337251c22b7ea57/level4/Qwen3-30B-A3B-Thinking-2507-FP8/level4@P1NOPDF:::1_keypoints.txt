- Introduces PTM, reducing MMTM parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) for faster inference.  
- Develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm with additional approximations for scalability.  
- Synthetic experiments suggest parameter reduction can improve statistical power.  
- Real-world experiments show PTM is competitive with existing methods in accuracy.  
- Major quality concern: paper fails to justify why PTM should be preferred over MMTM with varying K.  
- Missing experiments comparing PTM (large K) vs MMTM (small K) across K values to demonstrate practical advantage.  
- Unclear how K was selected in real-data experiments, risking biased model comparisons.  
- Authors do not analyze PTM failure modes; synthetic experiments omit descriptions of true B matrices.  
- Need tests on diverse B matrices to show when PTM succeeds or fails, especially when true B has O(K^3) distinct values.  
- Approximation techniques (thresholding, triplet sampling) are interesting but lack justification and quantitative trade-off analysis.  
- Specific unanswered questions: effect of threshold δ on accuracy/scalability; rationale for ignoring single-edge triplets vs a-MMSB; impact of O(K) local-update approximation as K grows.  
- Experimental design appears biased against competitors and lacks adequate analysis of PTM limitations.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized.  
- Modeling approach is somewhat incremental but novel in constraining parameters for scalability.  
- PTM could be significant if assumptions hold, enabling scalable latent-space network models.  
- Title is overly broad—PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific model, not a universal solution.  
- Probabilistic foundation is questionable: the "bag-of-triples" analogy to LDA is flawed and may concentrate posterior mass on non-networks.  
- Authors must clarify the meaning of "probability" in PTM and investigate side effects of their modeling choices.
