- Introduces PTM to reduce MMTM parameters from O(K³) to O(K) with stochastic variational inference and scalability tricks.  
- Main practical advantage unclear: paper claims scalability in K but fails to show why large K matters for mixed-membership models.  
- Missing critical comparison: does PTM with large K outperform MMTM with small K?  
- Lack of transparency on how K was chosen for PTM and competitors, risking biased experimental conclusions.  
- Insufficient analysis of model limitations: PTM’s constraint on B may fail when B has O(K³) distinct entries.  
- Synthetic experiments use a single unspecified B and omit “unfavorable” B cases where PTM would break.  
- Key approximation techniques are poorly justified: impact of threshold δ on scalability vs. accuracy is unexplored.  
- Inconsistent handling of downsampling: PTM ignores many triplets too—why is this acceptable here but not for a-MMSB?  
- Unclear effects of local update approximations (sampling 2K, 4K, 100K triples) and scaling behavior as K grows.  
- Paper is very well written and organized, making technical parts accessible.  
- Constraining parameters for scalability is a novel idea, but the modeling contribution is incremental.  
- Potentially significant if assumptions hold, but lack of failure analysis and experimental issues weaken impact.  
- Title is misleading: PTM is a scalable algorithm for a specific model, not a general scalable method for latent-space models.  
- Probabilistic foundation is unsound: “bag-of-triples” analogy to LDA is invalid and likely concentrates posterior mass on non-networks.  
- Conclusion: three critical flaws—no fair PTM vs MMTM comparison, insufficient failure-mode analysis, flawed probabilistic interpretation—recommend rejection without substantial revisions.
