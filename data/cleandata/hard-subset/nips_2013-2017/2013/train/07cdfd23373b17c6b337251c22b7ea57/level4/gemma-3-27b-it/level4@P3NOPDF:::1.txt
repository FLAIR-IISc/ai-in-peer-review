This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a constrained version of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), designed to reduce the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm with additional approximations to further enhance scalability. Results on synthetic data suggest that reducing the number of variables can improve statistical power, and comparisons on real-world datasets show competitive accuracy with existing methods.

**Quality:**

PTM represents an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical benefit of improved scalability with respect to *K* (the number of possible roles) remains unclear. To adequately evaluate this work, it is crucial to demonstrate the advantages of enabling MMTM with larger *K* values. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, the challenges associated with small *K* are potentially less severe than in single-membership models.  A compelling experiment would assess the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of *K*; demonstrating that PTM with a large *K* outperforms MMTM with a small *K* would strongly support the model's value. Unfortunately, this experiment is absent. Furthermore, the rationale for the chosen *K* values in the real data experiments for both PTM and the competitor, MMSB, is lacking. This lack of clarity regarding model selection could introduce bias into the experimental results.

The authors are also somewhat reserved in discussing the potential limitations of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space undoubtedly improves scalability, a thorough analysis of scenarios where the model might falter is needed. If the true parameter matrix *B* (following the authors’ notation) contains a significant number of distinct values – approaching O(K³) – PTM’s performance is likely to degrade. The synthetic data experiments do not adequately address this point; experiments utilizing diverse *B* matrices, some favorable to PTM and others designed to challenge it, would be highly informative.  It would be more valuable to understand both the strengths and weaknesses of PTM, rather than solely focusing on success cases.

The paper introduces several clever approximation techniques to achieve scalability, but provides insufficient detail and analysis regarding their impact. Specifically: 1) What is the sensitivity of the results to the threshold δ? Do experiments suggest an optimal range for δ on both real and synthetic data? What is the trade-off between scalability and accuracy as δ is lowered? 2) The authors claim that a downsampled MMSB performs poorly due to accuracy loss; however, the rationale for PTM’s immunity to this issue is not fully explained. Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets lacking one edge. Why can’t similar edge-dropping strategies be applied to MMSB? 3) What is the effect of the O(K) approximation in the local update step (page 5)? How do results change when sampling 2*K*, 4*K*, or 100*K* triples? Would the quality of the approximation diminish as *K* increases?

In summary, I am not fully convinced that the experimental setup is fair to competing methods and that the potential weaknesses of the algorithm and model have been sufficiently addressed.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow.

**Originality:** The modeling approach is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability through parameter constraint is an interesting and original direction, especially given the recent trend towards generalizing existing models in this field.

**Significance:** If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, PTM has the potential to significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large networks, a domain where scalability remains a major challenge.

**Additional Comments:** The title is overly broad. The paper presents a scalable algorithm *for* a specific latent-space model (PTM), rather than a universally scalable approach to all such models. 

I also question whether PTM is truly a probabilistic method. The authors draw a parallel to the bag-of-words assumption in Section 3, but this analogy is not entirely convincing. LDA generates a bag-of-words from a corresponding document, and therefore possesses a well-defined (though potentially unrealistic) likelihood function. In contrast, MMTM/PTM often generate a bag-of-triples that do not correspond to any valid network. Consequently, a significant portion of the posterior probability mass is placed on modeling non-networks. Abandoning the principle of likelihood in “probabilistic” methods raises concerns; what does probability signify within the context of PTM? I would be interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives on this point. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks that improve MMTM's scalability. However, the consequences of these decisions are not thoroughly investigated.
