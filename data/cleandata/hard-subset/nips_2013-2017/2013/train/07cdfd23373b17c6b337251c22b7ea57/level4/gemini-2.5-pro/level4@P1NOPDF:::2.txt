This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data. The model is fit to triangle count statistics derived from a preprocessing step, which is analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The primary modeling contribution is motivated by the aMMSB; instead of parameterizing all between-community interactions, the authors use the configuration of triangles to identify a set of K roles. This parsimonious parameterization leads to a more scalable implementation. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving both global and local updates for the proposed model.

The core modeling contribution is a straightforward, albeit non-trivial, combination of the aMMSB and MMTM models, which appears to have required some tedious bookkeeping. The presentation of this material could be improved for clarity. While the learning algorithm is largely a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors introduce an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule that is a valuable contribution.

Given the prevalence of directed networks in many applications, a discussion of how the proposed methods might be extended to handle such data would be a welcome addition.

**Specific Concerns and Questions:**

*   The paper claims that the "Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs." This claim seems overstated. First, ERGMs can incorporate a much wider variety of network statistics beyond triangles. Second, it is unclear what makes PTM a unique "latent space counterpart" to ERGMs in a way that distinguishes it from other latent space models for networks.

*   Is the inferred latent space interpretable? If so, what insights does it provide about the similarities and differences among the graphs analyzed in the experiments?

*   The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² was not clear. This should be clarified in the text.

*   The paper does not provide a clear justification for why subsampling triangles for each node (using δ) yields an adequate approximation. A more detailed explanation or empirical validation of this choice would strengthen the paper.

*   The paper states that a goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles." If this is a primary objective, perhaps the model should incorporate a more explicit (e.g., binary) mechanism for role assignment or selection, rather than relying on post-hoc thresholding of continuous role memberships.

*   The threshold of 0.125 used for latent space recovery appears somewhat arbitrary. How robust are the results to the choice of this threshold?

*   The paper states that a power-law network was used for experiments. Since various models can generate such networks, could the authors specify the exact generative model used?

*   There seems to be a contradiction regarding complexity. The paper argues that the model has O(K) parameters, yet the PTM Collapsed Gibbs Sampling (CGS) baseline is said to have O(K³) complexity. Please clarify why the complexity is cubic in K.

*   A key concern is the model's performance in sparse data settings. The experiments use a 90/10 training/test split. It seems likely that holding out more edges would significantly bias the triangle count statistics derived from the preprocessing step. It would be valuable to see an analysis of the model's robustness as the fraction of held-out edges increases.

*   It is surprising that the proposed method outperforms the standard MMSB on synthetic data generated from an MMSB model (as shown in Figure 1). Is there an explanation for this counterintuitive result?

In summary, this work presents a mixed-membership blockmodel with a restricted parameterization, learned using stochastic variational inference on triangle counts from a graph. The modeling choices and algorithmic approximations result in a scalable O(NK) algorithm for analyzing latent social structure.
