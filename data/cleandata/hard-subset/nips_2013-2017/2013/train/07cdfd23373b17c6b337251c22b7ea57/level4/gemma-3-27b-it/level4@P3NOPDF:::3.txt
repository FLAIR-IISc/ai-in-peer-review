## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission

This paper presents a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the “triangle model” of networks. The authors introduce a parameter-sharing scheme that yields a powerful and tractable subclass of the model, and then apply stochastic variational inference (SVI) to perform posterior inference in large networks. Overall, the paper is well-written and technically sound; I believe it is acceptable for publication.

However, there are several points that could be improved with minor revisions. 

On page 1, the authors use the term “parsimonious” modeling. While potentially accurate, this is not a standard term in the probabilistic modeling literature. I recommend explicitly defining this term in a single sentence, given its importance to the paper’s core contribution.

Also on page 1, the authors state that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy when referencing Gopalan et al. (2012). My understanding, based on a reading of that work, is that down-sampling zeros *does not* negatively impact accuracy. This statement appears to be misleading and should be corrected.

My primary concern lies with the justification presented on page 4 for the triangle model’s generative properties. While I agree that the triangle model is pragmatic and useful, appealing to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) as a defense is not convincing. LDA, when run, will produce a valid “document” – albeit a potentially nonsensical one – even if it is not the original input. However, running the triangle model’s generative process can readily produce outputs that do not represent valid network structures. This distinction is important. The model’s practicality does not require this comparison to LDA, and the analogy weakens the authors' argument.

Regarding related work, on page 5, the authors should cite Hoffman et al. (2013) alongside Amari & Sato (1998) when discussing variational inference for models with local variables. While Amari & Sato provides foundational work, the scenario described in the paper – involving “local variables” in Hoffman’s terminology – falls directly under the scope of Hoffman et al.’s theoretical contribution. 

On page 6, the method used to generate power-law networks in the latent space is not clearly described. The paper is vague on this point, and a more detailed explanation is needed to understand the experimental setup.

Furthermore, the authors cite Gopalan et al. (2012) as a method to accelerate Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling via Batch Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (MMSB). A more direct comparison to the Gopalan et al. algorithm itself, rather than or in addition to MMSB batch, would strengthen the evaluation.

Finally, several minor points:

*   On page 2, “mixture-membership” should be “mixed-membership.”
*   On page 8, I suggest removing the final sentence. The penultimate sentence provides a more concise and impactful conclusion.
*   In the bibliography, please cite the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference" (Blei et al., 2017).



**References:**
