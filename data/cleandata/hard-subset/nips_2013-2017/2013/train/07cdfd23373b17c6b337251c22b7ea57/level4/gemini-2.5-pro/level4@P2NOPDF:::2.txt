This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a method for learning latent social roles from network structure. Instead of analyzing raw edges, the model operates on triangle count statistics, treating them as a "bag-of-triangles" analogous to the bag-of-words model in text analysis. Drawing inspiration from the mixed-membership stochastic blockmodel (MMSB), the PTM achieves parsimony by parameterizing a set of K latent roles based on their participation in different triangle configurations, rather than modeling all K² pairwise community interactions. This reduction in parameters from O(K³) to O(K) is key to the model's scalability. The authors develop an efficient learning algorithm using stochastic variational inference (SVI), deriving both global and local parameter updates for the proposed model.

The primary modeling contribution is a principled combination of concepts from the author-recipient Mixed Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB) and the Mixed-Membership Topic Model (MMTM) to create a parsimonious model for network triangles. While the components are familiar, their integration into the PTM is novel. Furthermore, while the learning algorithm is largely a direct application of SVI, the authors introduce an intuitive and clever approximation for the local update rule, which contributes to the method's efficiency.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several areas that require clarification and improvement.

**Clarity and Justification:**
Several key details of the methodology and experiments could be explained more clearly.
*   The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² was not immediately apparent and requires a more precise definition.
*   The theoretical or empirical justification for why subsampling triangles for each node provides a sufficiently accurate approximation is not thoroughly discussed.
*   The paper claims that PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs." This claim requires further justification. ERGMs are a broad class of models capable of incorporating a wide variety of network statistics beyond triangles. It is unclear what makes PTM a specific "counterpart" in a way that other latent space models for networks are not. Clarifying this comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

**Model Design and Interpretation:**
*   There is a potential mismatch between the model's design and its stated goals. The paper aims to recover a "set of non-zero roles," which suggests a sparse or binary structure. However, the model itself assigns continuous role memberships. A discussion on why a continuous model is suitable for this goal, or how a sparsity-inducing prior could be incorporated, would be beneficial.
*   The paper would be stronger if it included a qualitative analysis of the inferred latent roles. Are they interpretable? What new insights do they provide about the similarities and differences between the networks analyzed?

**Experimental Concerns:**
The experimental setup and results raise several questions that should be addressed.
*   There is an apparent contradiction regarding complexity: the paper argues for O(K) parameters, yet states the collapsed Gibbs sampler (CGS) for PTM has O(K³) complexity. This needs to be clarified.
*   The specific generative model used for the power-law network experiments should be specified, as many such models exist.
*   The result where PTM outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated from the MMSB model (Figure 1) is surprising and counterintuitive. This warrants a more detailed explanation.
*   The model's performance on sparser data is a concern. The experiments use a 90/10 train/test split, holding out only 10% of edges. The distribution of adjacent triangles would likely be heavily biased if a larger fraction of edges were removed. An analysis of the model's robustness to higher levels of data sparsity would be valuable.
*   In the experiments, the robustness of the latent space recovery is not explored with respect to the chosen threshold of 0.125, which seems arbitrary. An analysis of the results' sensitivity to this hyperparameter is needed.

**Future Extensions:**
The paper focuses exclusively on undirected networks. Given the prevalence of directed graphs in social network analysis (e.g., citation networks, "follows" relationships), a discussion of the potential challenges and strategies for extending the PTM framework to directed networks would significantly broaden the paper's impact.

In summary, this paper presents a novel method for learning a mixed-membership blockmodel on a graph's triangle counts. The proposed modeling choices and approximations lead to a scalable O(NK) algorithm based on stochastic variational inference. While the core ideas are promising, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the concerns raised above regarding methodological clarity, experimental validation, and the justification for certain modeling claims.
