This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles network representation, a parsimonious statistical model, and stochastic variational inference. The core idea builds upon the a-MMSB and MMTM, seeking to combine their strengths – scalability and accuracy – by reducing the parameter space and enabling efficient inference. The authors present a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) with O(K) parameters and a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm with O(NK) complexity, demonstrating its ability to analyze networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles.

The work addresses a critical challenge in network analysis: the scalability of probabilistic latent space models. Existing methods, like MMSB and MMTM, struggle with large networks due to computational complexity. This paper directly tackles this limitation with a well-motivated combination of succinct network representation (triangles), parsimonious modeling (parameter sharing), and scalable inference (stochastic variational methods). The proposed PTM effectively reduces the number of parameters compared to previous models, enabling the efficient inference algorithm. The empirical evaluation demonstrates a significant speedup compared to state-of-the-art approaches, particularly on large networks, while maintaining competitive or improved accuracy.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear problem formulation, the technical soundness of the proposed approach, and the compelling experimental results. The authors convincingly demonstrate the scalability of their method and its ability to handle large-scale networks. The use of stochastic variational inference, combined with the efficient parameterization of the PTM, is a significant contribution. The detailed discussion of the algorithm, including the approximations made for computational efficiency, is commendable.  The experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks provide strong evidence of the effectiveness of the proposed method.

However, there are some areas that could be improved. The connection between the PTM and Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs) is not fully clarified and could benefit from further elaboration. While the authors mention the interpretability of the latent space, a more in-depth discussion of the recovered latent roles and their meaning would be valuable. The choice of the δ-subsampling parameter and its impact on accuracy deserves more justification. The paper would benefit from exploring the potential extension of the method to directed networks. Additionally, the impact of the thresholding parameter (0.125) on latent space recovery accuracy warrants investigation, and the specific power-law model used in the synthetic experiments should be explicitly stated. There’s a seeming discrepancy regarding the complexity of the PTM Gibbs sampler which should be addressed. Finally, the surprisingly strong performance of PTM compared to MMSB on data generated by MMSB should be discussed.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The proposed method significantly improves the scalability of latent space inference for large networks.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm are technically sound and well-motivated.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the method on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   **Novelty:** The combination of bag-of-triangles representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference is a novel contribution.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Clarification Needed:** The connection to ERGMs and the interpretability of latent roles could be more clearly explained.
*   **Justification of Choices:** The choice of parameters like δ and the threshold for latent space recovery requires further justification.
*   **Extension to Directed Networks:** The paper does not address the extension of the method to directed networks.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed method offers a practical and scalable solution for latent space inference in large networks, and the experimental results are compelling. Despite the minor areas for improvement, the paper is of high quality and worthy of acceptance.
