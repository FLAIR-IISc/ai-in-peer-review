This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a latent space model for networks that achieves scalability by constraining the O(K^3) parameter tensor of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to be O(K). To leverage this, the authors derive a scalable stochastic variational inference algorithm, incorporating further approximations to improve efficiency. The experimental results on synthetic data suggest that this parameter reduction can improve statistical power, while results on real-world networks show the proposed method achieves competitive accuracy compared to existing approaches, but at a significantly reduced computational cost.

**Quality:**
The paper presents an interesting specialization of the MMTM, but the evaluation of its practical benefits and limitations could be strengthened.
*   The primary motivation is scalability in K, the number of latent roles. However, the practical advantage of being able to model a very large K is not clearly demonstrated. Since MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, a small K is not necessarily as restrictive as in single-membership models. The paper would be more compelling if it included experiments showing the performance of PTM and MMTM as a function of K, specifically demonstrating if a PTM with large K can outperform an MMTM restricted to a smaller K. Furthermore, the method for selecting K in the real-data experiments for PTM and its competitors is not described, which could introduce bias into the comparisons.
*   The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the limitations imposed by its simplifying assumptions. While parameter space reduction is key to scalability, it is important to understand the conditions under which this assumption might fail. For instance, if the true interaction patterns require O(K^3) distinct parameters, the PTM would likely perform poorly. The synthetic experiments would be more informative if they explored a wider variety of generative parameters (the B tensor), including scenarios that are both favorable and unfavorable to the PTM's parsimonious structure. This would help readers understand the model's applicability boundaries.
*   Several algorithmic approximations are introduced to achieve scalability, but their impact is not fully analyzed.
    1.  What is the effect of the subsampling threshold δ? A sensitivity analysis showing how accuracy trades off against scalability for different values of δ would be very informative.
    2.  The paper claims a-MMSB's downsampling of non-edges compromises accuracy. Why is the PTM's approach—which ignores all triples with fewer than two edges—not susceptible to similar issues? A clearer justification for this distinction is needed.
    3.  What is the impact of the O(K) approximation in the local update? An analysis of how approximation quality changes if one samples, for instance, 2K, 4K, or a larger number of role-triples, and how this interacts with an increasing K, would be valuable.

In summary, the experimental setup could be improved to provide fairer comparisons and the paper would be stronger with a more thorough investigation into the potential weaknesses and limitations of the proposed model and inference approximations.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The modeling assumptions and the inference algorithm are explained in a logical and understandable manner.

**Originality:**
While the modeling approach can be seen as an incremental extension of the MMTM, the core idea of achieving scalability by systematically constraining the parameter space is a novel and interesting direction. This contrasts with much recent work that has focused on developing more complex models, and thus represents a valuable contribution.

**Significance:**
The work has the potential for significant impact. If the model's simplifying assumptions hold for a wide range of real-world networks, this approach could enable latent space analysis on a scale previously infeasible for this class of models. The ability to analyze million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine is a notable achievement.

**Additional Comments:**
*   The title may be overly broad. The paper proposes a scalable algorithm for a specific class of latent space models (based on triangular motifs), not a general approach applicable to all such models.
*   A conceptual point worth discussing is whether PTM constitutes a valid probabilistic generative model for networks. As the authors note, the generative process for a bag-of-triangles can produce configurations that do not correspond to any valid network. This contrasts with models like LDA, where a generated bag-of-words always corresponds to a valid document. This implies that for PTM, a significant portion of the posterior mass may be concentrated on modeling "non-networks." A discussion on the implications of this, and how the likelihood should be interpreted in this context, would be a valuable addition to the paper.
