Summary
This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a specialization of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) that reduces the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K), enabling faster inference. The authors derive a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and add further approximations to improve scalability. On synthetic data, they argue that parameter reduction can improve statistical power, and on several real-world networks they report accuracy competitive with existing methods.

Strengths
- The idea of constraining MMTM’s parameterization to achieve O(K) complexity is appealing and timely for large-scale network modeling.
- The stochastic variational inference framework and implementation details are interesting and potentially impactful, especially the practical tricks that make the method scale.
- The paper is clearly written and well organized.

Main Concerns
1) Empirical value of scalability in K is not convincingly demonstrated. Because MMSB/MMTM are mixed-membership models, moderate K often suffices in practice. The key question is: what do we gain by enabling very large K? The current experiments do not answer this. A compelling demonstration would compare performance as a function of K, showing that PTM at large K outperforms MMTM/MMSB at smaller K under a fixed computational budget.

2) Model selection and fairness. The procedure for choosing K on real data is unclear for both PTM and baselines. Without a transparent and uniform model selection protocol, results may be biased. Similarly, the approximations and subsampling strategies used for PTM should be matched with comparable strategies (or equivalent computational budgets) for baselines to ensure fair comparisons.

3) Impact of the modeling constraint. PTM implicitly assumes strong structure in the B tensor (only O(K) degrees of freedom). When the true B is dense with O(K^3) distinct values, PTM should degrade. The paper does not characterize such failure modes. The synthetic experiments also do not clearly specify the true B or explore regimes favorable and unfavorable to PTM. Readers would benefit from a systematic study that varies the structure of B to identify when PTM succeeds or fails.

4) Approximations are insufficiently analyzed.
- Threshold δ: The paper introduces a thresholding scheme but provides no guidance on selecting δ, nor an analysis of how δ trades accuracy for speed. Sensitivity curves on synthetic and real data (accuracy vs. δ; runtime vs. δ) would be very informative.
- Downsampling vs. ignored events: The claim that a-MMSB fails because downsampling harms accuracy, whereas PTM/MMTM can ignore certain events, is not fully justified. PTM/MMTM already discard triplets with only one edge. Why is ignoring non-edges problematic in MMSB but acceptable to PTM? A principled comparison or theoretical rationale is needed.
- O(K) local-update approximation: The effect of sampling O(K) triples for local updates is unclear. What happens when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K? Does approximation quality degrade as K grows? Ablations that vary the sample size relative to K would clarify robustness.

5) Probabilistic semantics. The paper argues that PTM’s treatment is analogous to the bag-of-words assumption in LDA. However, unlike documents, a bag-of-triples may not correspond to any valid network with high probability. This raises concerns about whether PTM/MMTM define a proper likelihood over networks or are better viewed as pseudo-likelihood models over triples. Clarifying the target data-generating object (graphs vs. triple multisets), the likelihood being optimized, and the implications for posterior interpretation and model comparison would strengthen the theoretical footing.

Detailed Questions for the Authors
- How were K and other hyperparameters selected for PTM and for each baseline on real datasets? Please specify search ranges, selection criteria, and whether the same computational budget was enforced.
- Can you provide performance vs. K curves for PTM, MMTM, and MMSB under matched compute? If full MMTM is infeasible at large K, compare PTM at large K against MMTM/MMSB at the best K achievable within the same time or memory constraints.
- Please report sensitivity to δ and provide guidance for practitioners. How small can δ be set to gain speed without a significant loss in accuracy?
- For the O(K) local update, how does performance change when sampling multiples of K triples? Is there a regime of K where the approximation becomes unstable or biased?
- On synthetic data, please vary the structure of B from PTM-friendly (low-rank/structured) to fully dense O(K^3), and report how PTM degrades relative to MMTM.
- Could you formalize the probabilistic model (likelihood and prior) over networks vs. triples, and discuss any mismatch between the inference objective and evaluation metrics on graphs?

Originality
The modeling move from O(K^3) to O(K) is incremental but worthwhile, and the emphasis on scalability via parameter constraints is a useful direction given the field’s recent focus on more general (and often less scalable) models.

Clarity
The manuscript is clear, well structured, and easy to follow. However, critical details about model selection, baseline configurations, and approximation hyperparameters should be made explicit.

Significance
If the simplifying assumptions hold in practice, PTM could materially expand the applicability of mixed-membership latent-space models to larger networks. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more rigorous empirical demonstration of when and why large-K scalability improves outcomes, a fair and transparent experimental setup, and a principled analysis of the proposed approximations.

Minor Comments and Suggestions
- The title is overly broad; consider reflecting the specific contribution (scalable inference for PTM).
- Include runtime and memory versus accuracy trade-off plots for all methods.
- Discuss potential hybrid approaches that relax PTM constraints adaptively when evidence supports more complex interactions.

Overall Assessment
A promising and well-written paper whose central idea—scalability through parsimony—is compelling. To be fully convincing, it needs clearer experimental protocols, stronger ablations, and a candid characterization of failure modes and probabilistic semantics.
