This paper introduces a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model, and stochastic variational inference. The core idea builds upon previous work like a-MMSB and MMTM, aiming to overcome their scalability limitations by reducing the number of parameters needed to model triangle-generating processes. Specifically, the authors propose sharing parameters across groups of latent roles, resulting in a model with O(NK) complexity – a significant improvement over methods with O(N<sup>2</sup>K<sup>2</sup>) or O(Nδ<sup>2</sup>K<sup>3</sup>) complexity. The paper demonstrates competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction on both synthetic and real networks, including a 1 million-node YouTube network.

The main contribution is a practical and efficient algorithm for large-scale latent space analysis. While the combination of existing techniques (a-MMSB and MMTM) isn’t entirely novel, the authors successfully address a critical scalability bottleneck and present a compelling set of experiments demonstrating the method’s effectiveness. The local update approximation within the stochastic variational inference framework is a particularly insightful and impactful contribution. 

However, the presentation could be streamlined. The motivation and connection to Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs) are somewhat unclear – the statement characterizing the PTM as a “latent space counterpart” to ERGMs feels imprecise, given the broader scope of ERGMs. More clarity on the interpretability of the inferred latent spaces would also strengthen the paper. While the experimental results are strong, a deeper discussion of *what* is learned about the networks beyond recovery accuracy would be valuable.

Several specific points warrant clarification. The rationale for the triangle subsampling (δ) and its impact on accuracy requires further justification. The choice of the 0.125 threshold for role assignment seems arbitrary and its sensitivity should be explored. Detailed information about the power-law network generator used in the synthetic experiments is needed. There appears to be a discrepancy in the stated complexity of the PTM Gibbs sampler (K<sup>3</sup>) given the claim of O(K) parameters.  The paper should address potential performance limitations when dealing with sparse graphs (i.e., with a significant proportion of missing edges) and explain why the 10% edge holdout was sufficient. Finally, the superior performance of PTM over MMSB on networks generated *by* MMSB is counterintuitive and warrants investigation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Scalability:** The algorithm demonstrably scales to networks with over a million nodes, a significant advance over existing methods.
*   **Accuracy:** The method achieves competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction.
*   **Novelty:** The parsimonious triangular model and the associated stochastic variational inference algorithm represent a novel combination of techniques that effectively addresses the scalability challenge.
*   **Practicality:** The implementation is efficient and allows for analysis on a single machine.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Clarity:** Some aspects of the model motivation and presentation could be improved for better understanding.
*   **Justification:** The rationale behind certain design choices (e.g., the threshold for role assignment, triangle subsampling) needs further elaboration.
*   **Interpretability:**  The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the interpretability of the inferred latent spaces.
*   **Counterintuitive Results:** The performance of PTM on MMSB-generated networks requires explanation.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in network analysis. While some clarifications and further analysis would be beneficial, the demonstrated scalability and accuracy make this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
