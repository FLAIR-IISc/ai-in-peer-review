- Introduces PTM to reduce parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) and uses stochastic variational inference for scalability.

- Synthetic results suggest parameter reduction can improve statistical power; real-data results show competitive accuracy.

- Practical benefit of scalability with larger K is unclear because experiments do not evaluate performance as a function of K for PTM vs MMTM.

- Selection procedure for K in real-data experiments is insufficiently described and may bias comparisons with MMSB.

- Paper lacks discussion of failure modes: PTM may perform poorly when the true B matrix has many distinct values.

- Synthetic experiments do not describe how B was chosen nor explore configurations that are unfavorable to PTM.

- Impact of key approximations is not analyzed: effects of threshold δ on accuracy–scalability trade-offs are unexamined.

- Claim that a-MMSB loses accuracy due to downsampling is not justified; unclear why PTM would avoid the same issue given triplet ignoring.

- No analysis of the O(K) approximation in local updates or how performance changes when sampling different multiples of K triples.

- Concerns about fairness of experimental comparisons and overall insufficient investigation of modeling and inference choices.

- Paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

- Modeling approach is somewhat incremental but the parameter-constraint strategy is an interesting, original direction.

- If assumptions and inference techniques hold in practice, PTM could significantly impact scalable latent-space modeling for large networks.

- Title may be overly broad; paper presents a scalable algorithm for a specific model (PTM), not a general scalability solution.

- Questionable probabilistic interpretation: the bag-of-triples analogy may not yield a valid likelihood and may assign mass to non-networks, undermining probabilistic claims.
