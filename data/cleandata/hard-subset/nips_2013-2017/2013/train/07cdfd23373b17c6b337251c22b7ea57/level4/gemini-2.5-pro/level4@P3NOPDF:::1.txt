### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a constrained version of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM). By reducing the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K), where K is the number of latent roles, PTM enables significantly faster and more scalable inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm, incorporating additional approximations to further improve scalability. Experimental results on synthetic data suggest that the parameter reduction can lead to greater statistical power, while results on real-world datasets show that PTM is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality and Significance**

The paper addresses the important problem of scalability in latent space models for network analysis. The core idea of achieving scalability by constraining the model's parameter space, rather than solely focusing on inference, is novel and insightful. This offers a valuable alternative perspective to recent trends in the field that often favor increasing model complexity. If the model's simplifying assumptions hold true in practice, PTM could have a significant impact by making sophisticated network analysis accessible for large-scale datasets.

However, the evaluation and analysis presented in the paper have several major weaknesses that currently limit its impact and leave critical questions unanswered.

**Major Concerns**

1.  **Insufficient Motivation and Unconvincing Experimental Design:** The primary motivation for PTM is scalability in K, the number of roles. However, the practical necessity for a very large K in mixed-membership models is not well-established. Unlike single-membership models where a small K can be highly restrictive, mixed-membership models can often represent complex structures with a relatively modest K.
    *   A crucial experiment to validate the paper's premise would be to compare PTM with a large K against the original MMTM with a smaller, computationally feasible K. If PTM (large K) significantly outperforms MMTM (small K), it would provide compelling evidence for the proposed approach. This analysis is unfortunately absent.
    *   Furthermore, the methodology for selecting K in the real-world experiments is not described for either PTM or its competitor, MMSB. This lack of transparency raises concerns that the model selection process could have biased the experimental results.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Model Assumptions:** While the paper successfully demonstrates the benefits of its simplifying assumptions, it is reticent about their potential drawbacks and failure modes. The PTM assumes an O(K) parameter space, which is a strong constraint. The model is likely to perform poorly when the true underlying network structure is governed by a more complex interaction matrix (B) with O(K³) distinct parameters. A thorough evaluation should explore these limitations.
    *   The synthetic data experiments could have been much more illuminating in this regard. The paper does not clearly describe the structure of the true B matrix used for data generation. The authors should have tested PTM on data generated from various ground-truth interaction matrices—some that align with PTM's assumptions and others that violate them. This would help readers understand the model's robustness and its specific failure modes, rather than only demonstrating scenarios where it succeeds.

3.  **Poorly Analyzed Algorithmic Approximations:** The paper introduces several effective algorithmic approximations to further enhance scalability, but their impact on model performance is not sufficiently analyzed. This leaves the reader to guess about the sensitivity and robustness of the inference procedure.
    *   **Thresholding (δ):** What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the threshold δ? An analysis showing the trade-off between accuracy and efficiency as δ varies would be highly valuable. How should practitioners choose this hyperparameter?
    *   **Downsampling Justification:** The paper argues that downsampling harms a-MMSB but is acceptable for PTM. This claim requires stronger justification. Since MMTM and PTM inherently ignore certain triplet configurations (those with only one edge), it is not immediately clear why this is fundamentally different from ignoring non-edges in MMSB.
    *   **Local Update Approximation:** The local updates for the variational parameters rely on an O(K) approximation by sampling triples. How does the quality of this approximation depend on the number of sampled triples (e.g., K, 2K, 100K)? Does its performance degrade as K increases, potentially undermining the model's scalability benefits?

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the model and the inference algorithm.

**Originality**

While the modeling approach is an incremental specialization of MMTM, the strategy of achieving scalability through principled parameter constraint is an original and valuable contribution to the field.

**Additional Comments**

*   **Title:** The title, "A Scalable Approach to Latent Space Models for Networks," seems overly broad. The proposed method is a specific scalable algorithm for a particular class of models (triangular models), not a general framework applicable to all latent space models.

*   **Probabilistic Foundation:** A more foundational question concerns whether PTM qualifies as a true generative model for networks. The model defines a likelihood over a "bag-of-triples." However, unlike LDA's bag-of-words (where a generated bag of words can always form a document), a randomly generated bag-of-triples will, with very high probability, not correspond to any valid graph. This implies the model places most of its posterior mass on non-existent networks. While this may be a pragmatic choice for inference, it deviates from the core principles of likelihood-based probabilistic modeling. The implications of this modeling choice deserve further discussion.

**Conclusion**

The paper proposes an interesting model and a scalable inference algorithm. The core idea is promising. However, the experimental evaluation is not convincing due to a lack of motivating experiments, unclear model selection, and an insufficient analysis of the model's limitations and the impact of its algorithmic approximations. I am therefore not convinced that the claims of the paper are adequately supported.
