- Paper introduces PTM that reduces MMTM parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) with a stochastic variational inference algorithm and shows competitive accuracy and improved power on synthetic data.  
- Practical benefit of supporting large K is unclear; paper lacks experiments showing PTM with large K outperforming MMTM with small K.  
- Criteria for selecting K in real-data experiments are not clearly specified, risking biased comparisons.  
- PTM’s simplifying constraints may fail when the true B matrix has many distinct values (potentially O(K^3)); the paper does not delineate when PTM performs poorly.  
- Synthetic experiments omit details on the true B used; need tests with both B matrices favorable and unfavorable to PTM.  
- The impact of scalability approximations is under-analyzed: effects and trade-offs of the threshold δ are not explored.  
- It is unclear why PTM avoids accuracy loss from downsampling (a-MMSB failure); the claim that PTM is immune lacks explanation.  
- Question why MMSB couldn’t ignore triplets without an edge if MMTM/PTM do the same — the difference is not justified.  
- Unclear impact of the O(K) approximation on local updates; effects of sampling 2K, 4K, or many triples as K grows are not evaluated.  
- Overall experimental setup may not fairly compare to competitors and fails to discuss algorithmic/model limitations adequately.  
- Paper is very clearly written and well-organized.  
- Modeling approach is somewhat incremental but original in using parameter constraints to gain scalability.  
- If assumptions and inference are practical, PTM could meaningfully impact scalable latent-space modeling for large networks.  
- Title is overly broad; should specify a scalable algorithm for PTM rather than a generally scalable approach.  
- Serious conceptual concern: PTM/MMTM may not be truly probabilistic/generative since bags-of-triples likely do not correspond to valid networks, raising questions about the meaning of likelihood and posterior mass.
