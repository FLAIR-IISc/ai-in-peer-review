This paper introduces a model for learning latent structure in social network data by analyzing triangle count statistics, which are treated analogously to a bag-of-words representation. The primary modeling contribution, inspired by the assorted Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB), is to parameterize interactions based on K roles identified from triangle configurations, rather than modeling all between-community interactions directly. This parsimonious parameterization leads to a more scalable model. The authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving both global and local updates for their proposed model.

The novel aspect of the model is a synthesis of the aMMSB and the Mixed-Membership Topic Model (MMTM), though the presentation of this combination could be improved. While the learning algorithm is largely a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors propose an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule.

Given the prevalence of directed networks, a discussion of how the proposed methods might extend to that domain would be a welcome addition.

**Concerns and Questions:**

*   The authors' claim that their "Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is questionable. First, ERGMs can incorporate a much wider range of network statistics beyond triangles. Second, the term "latent space counterpart" is not well-defined; under a broad interpretation, any latent space network model could be considered a counterpart to ERGMs.
*   Is the inferred latent space interpretable? If so, the paper should discuss what these results reveal about the similarities and differences among the analyzed graphs.
*   The distinction between the Δ¹ and Δ² statistics is not clearly explained.
*   The paper does not adequately justify why subsampling triangles for each node (using δ) provides a sufficient approximation for the learning algorithm.
*   If the goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles," the model might benefit from incorporating a more explicitly binary or sparse notion of role assignment.
*   How robust is the latent space recovery to the threshold of 0.125? This value seems arbitrary and warrants a sensitivity analysis.
*   For the experiments involving power-law networks, the paper does not specify which latent space model was used for data generation.
*   There is a potential contradiction regarding complexity. The paper argues that the model has O(K) parameters, yet the PTM CGS algorithm is stated to have O(K³) complexity. This discrepancy requires clarification.
*   The model's performance on sparser networks is a concern. In the experiments, only 10% of edges are held out for testing. Removing a larger fraction of edges would likely bias the triangle count statistics, and the model's robustness to this effect is not explored.
*   It is counterintuitive that the proposed method outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated from the MMSB model itself (as shown in Figure 1). This result requires further explanation.

In summary, this work uses stochastic variational inference to learn a mixed-membership blockmodel with a restricted parameter set from the triangle counts of a graph. The authors' modeling choices and algorithmic approximations result in a scalable O(NK) algorithm.
