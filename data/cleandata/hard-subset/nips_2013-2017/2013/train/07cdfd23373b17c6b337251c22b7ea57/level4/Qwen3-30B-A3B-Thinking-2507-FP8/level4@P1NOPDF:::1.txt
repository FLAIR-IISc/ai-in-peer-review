

This paper introduces the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) from O(K³) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, incorporating additional approximations to enhance scalability. Experiments on synthetic data suggest that reducing parameter count can strengthen statistical power, while real-world network results show PTM is competitive with existing methods in accuracy.

**Quality:** While PTM presents an interesting specialization of MMTM, its practical advantage hinges on scalability with respect to K (the number of roles). Crucially, the paper fails to address whether PTM’s scalability truly matters: *Why should we prefer PTM over MMTM with large K?* For mixed-membership models like MMSB and MMTM, small K is less problematic than in single-membership settings. The most compelling evidence would come from comparing PTM (with large K) against MMTM (with small K) across varying K values. Such experiments are absent. Additionally, the paper lacks clarity on how K was selected for real-data experiments involving PTM and its competitor MMSB, raising concerns about potential bias from model selection methods.

The authors are also vague about the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While scalability is achieved through parameter constraints, the model’s failure conditions remain unexplored. If the true parameter matrix B (in MMSB/MMTM) contains O(K³) distinct values—as implied by the model’s notation—PTM should perform poorly. Yet synthetic experiments do not describe the true B used. Testing PTM against diverse B matrices (some favoring PTM, others not) would better inform readers about the model’s applicability. Knowing *when PTM fails* is more valuable than knowing only *when it succeeds*.

Conversely, the paper’s approximation techniques (e.g., thresholding, triplet sampling) are interesting but poorly justified. Key questions remain unanswered:  
1. How does the threshold δ affect accuracy vs. scalability? Does a smaller δ improve scalability without significant accuracy loss?  
2. The authors dismiss a-MMSB due to downsampling’s accuracy trade-off, yet PTM also ignores triplets with only one edge. Why does PTM avoid this issue while MMSB does not?  
3. What is the impact of the O(K) approximation for local updates (p.5)? How does performance degrade when sampling 2K, 4K, or 100K triplets as K grows?  

In summary, the experimental design appears biased against competitors, and the analysis of PTM’s limitations is inadequate.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized.  
**Originality:** While the modeling approach is somewhat incremental, constraining parameters to achieve scalability represents a novel direction in a field dominated by model generalization.  
**Significance:** If the model’s assumptions hold in practice, PTM could significantly impact the application of latent-space network models by enabling scalability to large datasets—a gap in current literature.

**Additional Comments:** The title is overly broad; PTM is a scalable *algorithm* for a specific latent-space model, not a universal solution. More critically, PTM’s probabilistic foundation is questionable. The authors compare it to LDA’s bag-of-words assumption, but this analogy is flawed: LDA’s generative process always corresponds to a valid document (with a proper likelihood), while PTM’s "bag-of-triples" almost surely generates non-network configurations. Consequently, PTM’s posterior mass concentrates on modeling *non-networks*, undermining its probabilistic interpretation. This is not merely a technical oversight—it risks abandoning fundamental principles of likelihood-based modeling. The authors should clarify what "probability" signifies in PTM. Their focus on inference scalability neglects critical side effects of their modeling choices, which are poorly investigated.
