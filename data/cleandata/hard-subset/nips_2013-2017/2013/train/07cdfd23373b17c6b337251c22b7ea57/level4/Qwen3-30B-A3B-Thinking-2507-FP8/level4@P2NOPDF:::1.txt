

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) from O(K³) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm with additional scaling tricks and demonstrate improved statistical power on synthetic data and competitive accuracy on real networks.  

**Quality:** While PTM is an interesting specialization of MMTM, the practical advantage of its scalability in K (number of roles) remains questionable. A critical gap is the lack of experiments showing PTM with large K outperforming MMTM with small K—a direct comparison essential to justify the model. Without this, it is unclear whether PTM’s scalability truly benefits real applications. The paper also fails to clarify how K was selected for real-data experiments (PTM and competitors like MMSB), risking biased results due to unreported model selection choices.  

Furthermore, the paper inadequately addresses the impact of its simplifying assumptions. If the true parameter matrix B in MMTM/MMSB contains Ω(K³) distinct values (as the model assumes), PTM should fail. However, synthetic experiments do not test this: the authors do not describe B’s structure or vary it to include cases favorable/unfavorable to PTM. Readers need to know *when PTM succeeds/fails*, not just when it succeeds.  

The paper’s description of approximation techniques is similarly superficial. Three key questions remain unanswered:  
1) How does the threshold δ affect scalability vs. accuracy? Is there an optimal range for δ, and how does it scale with K?  
2) Why does PTM avoid the accuracy loss from downsampling in MMSB? Both PTM and MMTM discard triplets with only one edge—why can non-edges be ignored in PTM but not MMSB?  
3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation on local updates (Section 3.2)? How does performance degrade when sampling more triples (e.g., 2K, 100K) as K grows?  

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and logically structured.  
**Originality:** The approach of constraining parameters to achieve scalability is novel in a field dominated by model generalization.  
**Significance:** If the simplifying assumptions hold, PTM could significantly advance scalable network modeling. However, the unaddressed limitations weaken this potential impact.  

**Additional Comments:** The title ("Parsimonious Triangular Model") is overly broad; it describes a scalable *algorithm* for a specific model (PTM), not a general approach. More critically, PTM lacks a proper generative foundation. Unlike LDA (which always corresponds to a valid document), PTM generates "bags of triples" that rarely form valid networks. Consequently, the posterior places most mass on non-networks, undermining its probabilistic interpretation. The authors’ analogy to the bag-of-words assumption is flawed—there is no likelihood function for PTM, making its "probabilistic" label misleading. The paper’s focus on inference tricks neglects this foundational issue.  

**Summary:** The experimental design is unfair (missing K-scaling comparisons), the model’s limitations are poorly characterized, and the probabilistic justification is unsound. While the scalability techniques are intriguing, the paper fails to demonstrate PTM’s *practical* value or identify its failure modes. The technical contributions are modest, and the claims about scalability’s utility require stronger empirical support. I recommend rejection.
