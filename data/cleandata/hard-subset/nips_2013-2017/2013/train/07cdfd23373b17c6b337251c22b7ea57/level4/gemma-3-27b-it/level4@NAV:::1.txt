This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) to address the scalability challenges of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), reducing the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm and employ further approximations to enhance scalability. Evaluation on synthetic data suggests that the parameter reduction can improve statistical power, while results on real-world datasets demonstrate competitive accuracy compared to existing methods.

**Quality:**

The proposed PTM represents an interesting specialization of MMTM; however, the practical benefit of improved scalability with respect to *K* (the number of roles) remains unclear. To fully assess the value of this approach, it is crucial to understand how enabling MMTM with larger *K* values impacts performance. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, limitations associated with small *K* values may not be as significant as in single-membership models.  For network experiments, a particularly insightful analysis would be to observe the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of *K* – demonstrating PTM’s superiority with larger *K* would strongly support its effectiveness.  Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the method used for selecting *K* for both PTM and its competitor, MMSB, in the real-data experiments lacks sufficient description, potentially introducing bias into the results. 

The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of the implications of the simplifying assumptions made. While constraining the parameter space demonstrably improves scalability, it would be valuable to understand scenarios where the model might fail. If the true parameter matrix *B* (following the authors' notation) contains a substantial number of distinct values, PTM’s performance would likely be compromised.  The selection of *B* in the synthetic data experiments was not clearly described, and varying *B* to include configurations favorable and unfavorable to PTM would have provided a more comprehensive evaluation.  Identifying both the strengths and weaknesses of PTM is more informative than solely demonstrating successful scenarios. 

The authors introduce several techniques to improve PTM’s scalability, which are potentially valuable contributions. However, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the impact of these approximations. Specifically: 1) How does the choice of the threshold δ affect performance? Does a larger or smaller δ yield better results on both real-world and synthetic data, and what is the trade-off between scalability and accuracy as δ is adjusted? 2) The claim that a-MMSB suffers from accuracy loss due to downsampling requires further justification. Why is PTM not similarly affected? Both MMTM and PTM disregard triplets with only one edge – why is downsampling problematic for MMSB but not PTM? 3) What is the effect of the O(K) approximation on the local update step (page 5)? How does the performance change when sampling 2*K*, 4*K*, or 100*K* triples? Would the approximation quality diminish as *K* increases?

In summary, I question the fairness of the experimental comparisons and find the discussion of potential model limitations insufficient.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Originality:** While the modeling approach is somewhat incremental, the strategy of achieving scalability through parameter constraints represents an interesting and original direction, particularly given the recent trend toward generalizing existing models in this area.

**Significance:** If the model’s simplifying assumptions and inference techniques prove valid in practice, PTM has the potential to significantly impact the application of latent-space models to large-scale networks, a challenge currently faced by many existing methods.

**Additional Comments:** The title may be overly broad, as it implies a general solution to scalability challenges. The paper actually presents a scalable *algorithm* for a specific latent space model – PTM. 

Furthermore, I am uncertain whether PTM can truly be characterized as a probabilistic method. The authors’ analogy to the bag-of-words assumption in Section 3 is not entirely convincing.  LDA generates a bag-of-words based on an underlying document, retaining a valid (albeit potentially simplistic) likelihood function.  In contrast, a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM rarely corresponds to a valid network. Consequently, a significant portion of the posterior probability mass is assigned to modeling non-networks. Abandoning fundamental principles of likelihood in “probabilistic” methods raises concerns (what is the meaning of probability in PTM?). I would be interested in the authors’ and other reviewers’ perspectives on this point. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks to make MMTM scalable, but the potential consequences of these decisions remain poorly investigated.
