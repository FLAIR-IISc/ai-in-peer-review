This paper proposes a technique for learning latent structure in social network data. The model is fit to triangle count statistics obtained from a preprocessing step, which is analogous to a bag-of-words representation for text documents. The main modeling advance is motivated by the affinity Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (aMMSB); rather than parameterizing all between-community interactions, the authors consider the configurations of triangles to identify a set of K roles to parameterize. This parsimonious approach results in fewer model parameters and, consequently, a more scalable implementation. To fit the model, the authors apply recent advances in stochastic variational inference, deriving corresponding global and local update rules.

While the learning algorithm is largely a direct application of stochastic variational inference, the authors provide an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule. The novel modeling contribution, a combination of the aMMSB with the Mixed-Membership Topic Model (MMTM), is sound, though the presentation could be improved.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing the following points and concerns:

*   The authors claim that their "Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) is essentially a latent space counterpart to ERGMs, that represents the processes of generating triangular motifs." This statement is questionable. First, ERGMs can incorporate much more varied statistics than just triangles. Second, it is unclear what makes the PTM a "latent space counterpart" in a way that distinguishes it from most other latent space network models.

*   Are the inferred latent spaces interpretable? If so, what do the results reveal about the similarities and differences among the graphs analyzed in the experiments?

*   The distinction between Δ¹ and Δ² is not clearly explained.

*   The paper should provide a clearer justification for why subsampling the triangles for each node (using δ) provides an adequate approximation.

*   Given that a stated goal is to recover the "set of non-zero roles," the authors might consider whether a more binary or sparse representation of "role" within the model would be more appropriate.

*   The choice of 0.125 as a threshold for latent space recovery appears arbitrary. How robust are the results to variations in this threshold?

*   The paper mentions using a latent space model to generate power-law networks for experiments. As a variety of such models exist, please specify which one was used.

*   There appears to be a contradiction regarding complexity. The paper argues that the model has O(K) parameters, yet states that the Collapsed Gibbs Sampler (PTM CGS) has K³ complexity. Please clarify the source of this K³ term.

*   A significant concern is how this model will perform when fewer edges are observed. In the experiments, only 10% of the edges are held out. The distribution of adjacent triangles would likely be quite biased if too many edges were removed prior to the preprocessing step. A discussion of the model's robustness to data sparsity is needed.

*   It is counterintuitive that the proposed method outperforms MMSB on a synthetic network generated from the MMSB model itself (Figure 1, left 4 panels). An explanation for this result should be provided.

*   Given the prevalence of directed networks, a detailed discussion of how these methods might be extended to directed graphs would be a welcome addition.

In summary, this work uses stochastic variational inference to learn a parsimonious mixed-membership blockmodel based on triangle counts in a graph. The authors' modeling choices and approximations result in a scalable O(NK) algorithm.
