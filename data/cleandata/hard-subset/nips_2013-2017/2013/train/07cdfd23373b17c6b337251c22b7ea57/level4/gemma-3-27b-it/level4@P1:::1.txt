This paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), which constrains the O(K^3) parameter space of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM and additional approximation tricks to make it further scalable. Results on synthetic datasets suggest that reducing the number of variables may lead to stronger statistical power, and experiments on real-world datasets show the proposed method is competitive with existing methods in terms of accuracy.

**Quality:**

PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, but the practical advantage of achieving scalability in terms of K (the number of possible roles) is not fully demonstrated. To empirically evaluate the value of such a method, it is critical to understand *how* enabling MMTM with large K helps. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using a small K may not be as problematic as in single-membership models! For real network experiments, it would be highly informative to see the performance of both MMTM and PTM as a function of K: if PTM with a large K consistently outperforms MMTM with a small K, this would clearly showcase PTM’s power. Unfortunately, such experiments were not conducted. Furthermore, the method of model selection and the choice of K values in the real data experiments for both PTM and its competitor MMSB are not clearly described, potentially introducing bias into the experimental results.

The authors are somewhat reticent regarding the impact of simplifying assumptions. While achieving scalability by constraining the parameter space is clear, it would be valuable to understand *when* such a model might fail. If there truly are O(K^3) distinct values in the parameter matrix B (following the authors’ notation) of MMSB and MMTM, PTM should perform poorly, correct? In this regard, the description of the true B used in synthetic data experiments is insufficient; the authors should have experimented with various Bs, some favoring PTM and others challenging the model. Knowing when PTM succeeds *and* fails would be more beneficial than only demonstrating its successes.

The paper introduces several tricks to improve PTM’s scalability, which are interesting and effective. However, the description and analysis of the impact of these approximations are weak. 1) What is the impact of choosing the threshold δ? In both real-world and synthetic experiments, does a larger or smaller δ prove more effective? How much can δ be lowered to improve scalability without significantly sacrificing accuracy? 2) The authors claim a-MMSB suffers when downsampling compromises accuracy; why is PTM immune to this problem? Both MMTM and PTM ignore triplets with only one edge. Why can’t we ignore non-edges in MMSB as well? 3) What is the impact of the O(K) approximation to the local update (page 5)? What happens if we sample 2K, 4K, or 100K triples? Would the quality of the approximation suffer as K increases?

In summary, I am not fully convinced the experimental setup is fair to competitors, and the discussion of the algorithm/model’s potential weaknesses is inadequate.

**Clarity:**

The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

**Originality:**

In terms of modeling, the contribution is somewhat incremental. However, achieving scalability by constraining parameters is an interesting and original direction, particularly as recent work in this area has focused on generalizing existing models.

**Significance:**

Provided the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, it could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to networks, as many current models do not scale to large datasets.

**Additional Comments:**

I believe the title is overly broad. There are other scalable approaches to other latent space models, and the authors present a scalable algorithm *for* a specific latent space model – PTM.

I am also uncertain if PTM is genuinely a probabilistic method. The authors argue in the last paragraph of Section 3 that it aligns with the bag-of-words assumption. However, in LDA, a bag-of-words always corresponds to a document with a defined likelihood, however simple. Conversely, with high probability, no network corresponds to a bag-of-triples generated by MMTM and PTM. Consequently, MMTM/PTM doesn't truly model networks generatively, and most of the posterior probability mass is assigned to modeling *non*-networks! Abandoning basic likelihood principles in “probabilistic” methods is concerning (what does probability *mean* in PTM?). I am eager to hear the authors’ and other reviewers’ thoughts on this point. The authors propose modeling assumptions and inference tricks that make MMTM inference scalable. However, the side effects of these decisions are not thoroughly investigated.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical problem in network analysis – scalability to large graphs.
*   Demonstrates competitive performance with existing methods on benchmark datasets.
*   Presents a clear and well-written paper with a novel approach to parameter reduction.
*   Offers potentially significant improvements in runtime compared to existing algorithms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of a comprehensive evaluation of the impact of simplifying assumptions.
*   Insufficient discussion of the model’s limitations and potential failure modes.
*   Experimental setup could be improved to provide a more fair comparison with competitors.
*   The theoretical justification for abandoning traditional likelihood-based probabilistic modeling is weak.
