This paper presents the parsimonious triangular model (PTM), a constrained version of the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM), which reduces the O(K^3) parameter space to O(K) for faster inference. The authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for PTM, along with additional approximation techniques to enhance scalability. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate that the proposed method can achieve stronger statistical power and competitive accuracy with existing methods.

Quality:
While PTM appears to be an interesting specialization of MMTM, its practical advantages in terms of scalability are unclear. To assess the value of this method, it is essential to investigate how it compares to learning MMTM with large K. Given that MMSB and MMTM are mixed-membership models, using small K may not be as problematic as in single-membership models. A more compelling evaluation would involve comparing the performance of MMTM and PTM as a function of K, demonstrating whether PTM with large K outperforms MMTM with small K. Unfortunately, such experiments are not conducted. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a clear description of how K values were chosen for real data experiments, which may have biased the results.

The authors are also reticent about the impact of their simplifying assumptions. While constraining the parameter space can achieve good scalability, it is crucial to discuss when the model may fail. If the parameter matrix B in MMSB and MMTM indeed contains O(K^3) distinct values, PTM is likely to perform poorly. The choice of true B in synthetic data experiments is not clearly described, and the authors should have experimented with various B values to demonstrate the strengths and weaknesses of PTM. Providing insights into when PTM succeeds and fails would be more beneficial than solely showcasing its successes.

The authors introduce several approximation techniques to make PTM scalable, which are interesting and effective. However, the paper does a poor job of describing and analyzing the impact of these approximations. Key questions remain unanswered, such as the effect of choosing the threshold δ, the impact of downsampling on accuracy, and the consequences of ignoring non-edges in MMSB. Additionally, the impact of the O(K) approximation to local updates is unclear, and the quality of approximation when sampling different numbers of triples is not explored.

In summary, the experimental setup may not be fair to competitors, and the description of the algorithm's/model's potential weaknesses is inadequate.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

Originality:
While the modeling approach is somewhat incremental, achieving scalability through parameter constraints is an interesting and original direction, particularly in a field where recent work has focused on generalizing existing models.

Significance:
If the simplifying assumptions in the model and inference algorithm hold in practice, this work could have a significant impact on the application of latent-space models to large networks, as many existing models do not scale well.

Additional Comments:
The title may be too broad, as there are other scalable approaches to latent space models. The authors propose a scalable algorithm for a specific latent space model, PTM.

Furthermore, it is debatable whether PTM is a truly probabilistic method. The authors draw an analogy with the bag-of-words assumption, but this comparison is not convincing. Unlike LDA, which generates bags-of-words corresponding to actual documents, MMTM and PTM generate bags-of-triples that do not necessarily correspond to real networks. This raises concerns about abandoning basic principles of likelihood in "probabilistic" methods and the meaning of probability in PTM. A more thorough investigation into the side effects of these modeling assumptions and inference tricks is necessary.
