Summary
The paper introduces a scalable probabilistic approach for latent space inference in large networks by (i) representing a network as a bag of triangles (open and closed connected triples), (ii) proposing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) via parameter sharing based on multiplicity patterns in a role triplet, and (iii) developing a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and stochastic natural gradients. A key engineering contribution is an O(K) local-update approximation (“mixture-of-deltas”) that maintains a valid lower bound while avoiding O(K^3) computation per triangle. Combined with δ-subsampling of high-degree nodes, the algorithm scales linearly in N and K (treating δ as a constant), requires only a few passes over triangles, and parallelizes trivially.

Relative to prior probabilistic latent space models, MMSB [1], a-MMSB [5], and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) [8], the work addresses all three practical bottlenecks: representation, parameterization, and inference. Empirically, on synthetic and real networks (up to ~1.1M nodes, 100+ roles), the method is orders of magnitude faster than state-of-the-art probabilistic baselines, with competitive or improved accuracy for latent role recovery and link prediction.

Strengths
- Technical novelty: The O(K) parameterization by grouping role triplets into multiplicity classes (all same, two same, all distinct) is elegant and directly enables scalable global updates. The structured mean-field with a fast O(K) local variational family is a practical and thoughtful design that preserves important dependencies among role assignments within a triangle.
- Scalability: Clear complexity analysis; careful use of δ-subsampling; memory-efficient implementation that discards per-triangle variational parameters after accumulating sufficient statistics. Demonstrated million-node runs in hours on a single multi-core machine.
- Empirical validation: Comprehensive synthetic experiments for latent role recovery across increasing N and K, linking accuracy and runtime trends to parameter complexity (O(K), O(K^2), O(K^3)). Real-world link prediction and held-out likelihood curves show fast convergence in 2–5 passes.
- Positioning: Builds on prior “triangles vs. edges” representation [8] and a-MMSB’s parameter-sharing idea [5], connecting the contributions to known challenges in scalable network inference.

Weaknesses and questions
- Expressiveness trade-offs: The PTM’s sharing scheme assumes that all “two-same, one-different” configurations are governed by a single Bxx, regardless of the minority role, and all “all-different” triplets share B0. This is a strong symmetry assumption that may limit modeling of heterogeneous inter-role triangular patterns. Discussion of when such sharing degrades accuracy (and how often in practice) would be useful.
- Comparative baselines: Direct empirical comparisons to a-MMSB [5] on the same datasets are not shown (only wall-clock comparisons reported from prior work). Given a-MMSB is a primary scalable probabilistic baseline, including it in link prediction and recovery accuracy would strengthen the claims.
- Variational objective and unbiasedness: The stochastic gradient claims rely on unbiased gradients of the variational objective. With the mixture-of-deltas and randomly re-sampled off-diagonals A at each local update, the algorithm effectively optimizes a sequence of lower bounds. Clarify whether the stochastic gradients remain unbiased for a fixed objective (or characterize convergence under a changing bound), and provide intuition or guarantees for the re-sampling scheme.
- Link prediction scoring: The method ranks edges by marginalizing over k and roles. The computational strategy and complexity for this ranking (full sum over k vs. sampling/neighbor-restricted sums) are not detailed. Clarifying the practical procedure and its runtime would help assess deployment feasibility.
- Sensitivity and ablations: Limited analysis of the impact of δ, minibatch size, step-size schedule, and the number of off-diagonal tuples in A on accuracy/speed. An ablation comparing (i) full structured mean-field vs. mixture-of-deltas, and (ii) different sizes of A, would illuminate the accuracy–speed trade-off.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The modeling assumptions are clearly stated (not a network generative model; triangles only). Variational derivations are standard; the structured local family is well-motivated. Some theoretical aspects (objective consistency under re-sampling A, unbiasedness) deserve clarification. Experimental methodology is mostly solid, though additional baselines and details would strengthen conclusions.

Clarity
Well-organized and clearly written. The explanation of equivalence classes and the induced parameter tying is concise and helpful. Algorithmic steps and complexity are stated cleanly. Minor areas for clarification include link prediction scoring, preprocessing cost for triangle extraction, and stability of the variational bound when re-sampling A.

Originality
Combines known components (triangles representation [8], parameter sharing in spirit of [5], SVI [10], natural gradients [2,20]) into a coherent design that is new for triangular latent-space models. The specific O(K) parameterization for triangle triplets and the O(K) local update are original and impactful.

Significance
High potential impact for large-scale probabilistic network analysis in 2013-era settings. Demonstrated improvements in speed with competitive accuracy make this a practical advance. The approach should be of interest to both modeling and systems-oriented NeurIPS audiences.

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Include direct comparisons to a-MMSB [5] on at least a subset of datasets (accuracy, runtime, convergence), or provide a compelling reason if infeasible.
- Clarify the theoretical status of the stochastic gradients with mixture-of-deltas and re-sampled A: fixed objective vs. changing bounds; practical guidance for A’s size and composition.
- Detail the computational procedure for link prediction scoring (e.g., restricting k to neighbors, sampling strategies) and report runtime.

Nice-to-have:
- Provide ablations on δ, minibatch size, and A size; compare structured mean-field vs. naive mean-field vs. mixture-of-deltas.
- Discuss extensions that relax symmetry assumptions, e.g., low-rank or clustered parameterizations between roles to recover some heterogeneity while retaining scalability.
- Report sensitivity to α, λ and initialization strategies when no seeds are available.
- Release code and the triangle extraction/subsampling pipeline.

Pros
- Elegant O(K) parameterization and O(K) local updates.
- Strong scalability with practical parallel implementation; million-node runs on a single machine.
- Competitive or improved accuracy on recovery and link prediction; fast convergence.

Cons
- Limited direct comparisons to a-MMSB.
- Strong parameter-sharing assumptions may limit expressiveness in some networks.
- Some theoretical and practical details (objective unbiasedness, prediction complexity) need clarification.

Overall recommendation
A strong contribution in scalable probabilistic network modeling. With added clarity on the variational objective under the local approximation and inclusion of a-MMSB in the empirical comparisons, the work would be very compelling for acceptance.
