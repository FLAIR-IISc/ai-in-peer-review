Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a probabilistic latent-space model over a bag-of-triangles representation of a network. Building on prior work that uses triangular motifs for scalable representation (MMTM; Ho et al., NIPS), the authors reduce the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) (one per role triplet) to O(K) via parameter sharing across configurational equivalence classes: B0 for three distinct roles, Bxx for two-equal-one-different, and Bxxx for all-equal. They derive a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) procedure with natural gradient updates; the local variational posterior over role triplets is approximated via a “mixture-of-deltas” over K diagonal and O(K) randomly chosen off-diagonal configurations, yielding O(K) local updates. The resulting global updates scale as O(NK). Experiments on synthetic networks (with MMSB- and power-law generators) and several large real networks (including a 1M-node YouTube graph) show competitive or improved latent role recovery and link prediction compared to MMSB variational inference and MMTM/PTM Gibbs samplers, at orders-of-magnitude lower runtime.

The work situates itself with respect to MMSB and a-MMSB (Airoldi et al.; Gopalan et al.), ERGM/triadic closure, and Ho et al.’s triangle representation. The main novelty is combining the bag-of-triangles representation with an O(K) parameterization and scalable SVI, contrasted with a-MMSB’s O(K) edge parameterization and MMTM’s O(K^3) tensor.

Quality:
- Modeling and inference: The parameter-tying scheme is well motivated and substantially reduces complexity. The structured variational family over role triplets is appropriate; the mixture-of-deltas restriction appears to be a computationally effective approximation. The SVI derivation (natural gradients, minibatches of triangles) is standard and sound; the claim that any choice of the reduced support yields a valid ELBO is correct, though the practice of re-randomizing the support A each local optimization merits clarification regarding monotonicity of the ELBO across iterations.
- Claims vs. evidence: Synthetic studies convincingly show improved scalability and robust recovery as N and K grow; the ablation against MMSB and MMTM/PTM Gibbs supports the speed/accuracy story. Real data link prediction shows advantage on larger social networks; however, stronger baselines (e.g., triangle/Adamic–Adar heuristics) would better contextualize the gains since the method explicitly models triangles. The reported 1M-node, K=100 result in 4 hours on 8 threads is impressive; more implementation/hardware details would help validate the magnitude of the speedups relative to a-MMSB.
- Potential issues to address:
  - Expressivity: Sharing Bxx across all minority roles ignores pair-specific interactions and may limit modeling of disassortative/heterogeneous triads. Empirical evidence of when this hurts (and when it helps) would be useful.
  - Approximation quality: The O(K) local update restricts support to O(K) off-diagonals; the paper states it “works as well as the full parameterization,” but quantitative comparisons on small graphs where exact O(K^3) updates are feasible would strengthen the claim. Sensitivity to the size/composition of A should be shown.
  - Evaluation setup: For link prediction, clarify whether triangles are re-extracted after masking edges (to avoid leakage) and ensure that training never uses motifs that include held-out edges.
  - Subsampling: δ-subsampling is central to scalability; provide sensitivity analyses w.r.t. δ on accuracy and runtime, and report how often high-degree nodes are truncated in the real datasets.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The model’s equivalence classes and induced probabilities are clearly explained. The algorithmic description is concise, and the role of structured mean-field vs naive mean-field is justified. A few suggestions:
- Move key local update forms (including φxxx and φxxy) from the appendix into the main text for completeness.
- Explicitly state the memory footprint and how sufficient statistics are accumulated and discarded.
- Provide clearer pseudo-code for the construction and resampling of A in the local updates, and discuss its effect on the ELBO.

Originality:
Moderate to high. The bag-of-triangles representation and motif-centric modeling are known, and parameter tying in a-MMSB is known for edges. Applying a principled O(K) parameterization to triangle models plus a structured, scalable SVI with an O(K) local approximation is, to my knowledge, novel and important.

Significance:
High. Achieving O(NK) inference with demonstrated million-node scale and hundreds of roles on a single machine is compelling. The approach should be of broad interest to researchers/practitioners working on scalable network latent variable models, especially when higher-order structure matters.

Pros for acceptance:
- Strong scalability with clear algorithmic innovations (O(K) parameters; O(K) local updates; SVI).
- Competitive or better accuracy on latent role recovery and link prediction.
- Demonstrated results on very large real networks with fast convergence.
- Clear exposition and practical implementation details (minibatches, threading).

Cons / concerns:
- Limited ablation on the O(K) local approximation and on δ-subsampling.
- Expressivity limits from parameter tying not thoroughly evaluated (e.g., disassortative or core–periphery patterns).
- Baselines for link prediction could be stronger and more diverse (simple triangle-based heuristics; degree-corrected/overlapping community models).
- Fairness and details of runtime comparisons need more transparency (hardware, codebases, stopping criteria).

Questions for the authors:
1) Does re-randomizing the support A each iteration guarantee non-decreasing ELBO in expectation? Can you report ELBO traces to confirm stability?
2) How is K chosen on real datasets? Any model selection or held-out ELBO criterion?
3) For link prediction, are triangles re-extracted after masking edges? Are there any motifs in training that could indirectly encode the held-out edges?
4) How sensitive are results to α and λ? Any per-dataset tuning?
5) Can you report memory usage and wall-clock breakdown (triangle extraction vs. inference) for the 1M-node run?

Suggestions to improve the paper:
- Add ablations: exact vs. approximate local updates on small graphs; sensitivity to the size of A; δ-subsampling sensitivity.
- Include stronger link prediction baselines (Adamic–Adar, common neighbors, Katz, personalized PageRank) and, if feasible, degree-corrected blockmodels.
- Provide more details on runtime environments and stopping criteria; include per-iteration complexities in wall-clock.
- Discuss scenarios where PTM’s parameter tying may be too restrictive; consider a middle ground with a small number of inter-role groups.
- Release code or pseudocode sufficient for reproduction.

Overall: A solid, well-executed paper with meaningful algorithmic contributions and strong empirical scalability. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, it would make a valuable contribution.
