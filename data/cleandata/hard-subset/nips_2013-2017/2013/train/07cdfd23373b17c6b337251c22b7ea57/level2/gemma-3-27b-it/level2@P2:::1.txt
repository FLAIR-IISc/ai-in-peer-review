This paper introduces a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, tackling a key bottleneck in many existing methods. The core idea is to combine a succinct network representation (bag of triangles), a parsimonious statistical model, and a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors demonstrate significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art methods while maintaining competitive accuracy on both synthetic and real-world networks, even scaling to networks with over a million vertices and 100 latent roles.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the limitations of existing methods in handling large networks with many latent roles. The three principles outlined – succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference – provide a strong conceptual framework for the proposed approach. The technical contributions, particularly the parsimonious triangular model (PTM) which reduces parameter complexity from O(K3) to O(K), and the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm are significant. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines and demonstrating substantial performance gains in terms of both runtime and accuracy. The convergence analysis, especially showing faster convergence than batch methods and competitive performance with existing work on real datasets, is convincing.

However, several aspects could be strengthened. While the paper mentions δ-subsampling, the sensitivity of the results to the choice of δ isn't thoroughly explored. A more detailed ablation study varying δ and its impact on both performance and runtime would be valuable. The comparison to MMSB is somewhat limited, as MMSB’s performance is only reported for smaller networks. While the authors argue that MMSB cannot scale, demonstrating its performance on somewhat larger networks (even if it’s significantly slower) would provide a more complete comparison. The choice of hyperparameters (α, λ, τ0, τ1, κ) appears largely driven by experimentation; a more systematic exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and justification for the selected values would increase confidence in the results.

The discussion of the structured mean-field approximation could be more detailed. The paper mentions that a naive mean-field approximation performs poorly, but doesn’t fully explain *why* the chosen structured approach is superior. A more in-depth discussion of the limitations of naive mean-field and the benefits of capturing higher-order dependencies would be helpful. The assumption of independence within the equivalence classes in the local update (using the mixture-of-deltas approximation) also warrants further justification; is this a reasonable approximation, and what is the potential impact on accuracy?

The paper references [24] regarding the expectation of many distinct latent roles in large social networks. Providing specific examples of how these roles manifest in the studied datasets (e.g., Youtube) would strengthen the practical relevance of the work. While the authors state the PTM isn’t a generative model *per se*, a brief discussion of potential extensions for generative modeling could be interesting.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The method demonstrably scales to networks significantly larger than those handled by existing probabilistic approaches.
* **Novelty:** The combination of the bag-of-triangles representation, the parsimonious triangular model, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm is a novel and effective approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** Comprehensive experiments show competitive accuracy and significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art methods.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** The choice of hyperparameters is not thoroughly justified.
* **Incomplete Comparison to MMSB:** MMSB performance is only shown for smaller networks.
* **Lack of Ablation Study on δ-subsampling:** The impact of the δ parameter isn't fully explored.
* **Limited Discussion of Mean-Field Approximation:** The rationale behind the structured mean-field approach could be more detailed.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in network analysis – scalable latent space inference.
* The technical contributions are substantial and well-supported by experimental results.
* The method demonstrates significant performance gains compared to existing approaches.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The hyperparameter sensitivity analysis is limited.
* The comparison to MMSB is incomplete.
* The discussion of the mean-field approximation could be more detailed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed method is novel, scalable, and demonstrably effective. While some aspects could be strengthened, the overall quality of the work is high. I recommend acceptance.
