

This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference in large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) and stochastic variational inference. The authors address three key scalability principles: (1) succinct triangular motif representation (replacing O(N²) adjacency matrices with O(Nδ²) motifs), (2) O(K) parameterization of triangle-generating probabilities (vs. O(K³) in prior work), and (3) an O(NK) stochastic inference algorithm. Their method achieves latent space recovery and link prediction on 1M-node networks in hours on a single machine, outperforming state-of-the-art methods [5,8] by orders of magnitude in speed with competitive accuracy.

**Strengths:**  
The paper makes a compelling case for the necessity of combining all three scalability principles, which prior work (e.g., MMSB [1], a-MMSB [5], MMTM [8]) only partially addresses. The PTM’s parameter sharing (Bxxx, Bxx, B0) elegantly reduces the O(K³) complexity of MMTM to O(K), enabling efficient inference. The stochastic variational inference with O(K) local updates (Section 4.2) is a key technical contribution—avoiding the O(K³) cost of exact updates while maintaining accuracy. Experiments (Section 5) convincingly demonstrate scalability: on Brightkite (N=58K), PTM converges in 12 minutes vs. 8 days for a-MMSB [5]; on Stanford (N=1.1M), it takes 4 minutes vs. 18.5 hours for MMTM [8]. The ablation study (Figure 1) shows PTM’s superior convergence behavior and accuracy over baselines as network size grows, with minimal runtime overhead from parallelism.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. The choice of δ=50 (e.g., Section 5.2) lacks theoretical grounding. While the paper states it exceeds the median degree, a brief discussion of how δ impacts accuracy/speed trade-offs for networks with varying degree distributions would strengthen the methodology.  
2. The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation (Section 4.2) is well-motivated but could be better contextualized. A small example illustrating how the O(K) approximation captures dependencies between role triplets (e.g., comparing to naive mean-field) would aid clarity.  
3. The paper does not explore the upper limit of K (e.g., K=500). While Table 3 shows K=64 for Brightkite, it would be valuable to discuss scalability boundaries for extreme role counts.

**Overall:**  
This is a high-quality, impactful contribution to scalable network analysis. The work clearly advances the state of the art by resolving the scalability bottleneck for latent space inference, with rigorous experiments demonstrating both theoretical and practical significance. The technical approach is sound, the presentation is clear (with minor room for improved motivation of design choices), and the results are compelling. The method’s accessibility (single-machine runtime) and strong empirical performance make it highly relevant for practitioners. The authors address all four NIPS criteria effectively: technically sound (Quality), well-organized (Clarity), novel combination of representation/modeling/inference (Originality), and solves a critical scalability problem with broad applicability (Significance).

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper is a strong candidate for NIPS, offering a significant, well-executed solution to a persistent challenge in network analysis.
