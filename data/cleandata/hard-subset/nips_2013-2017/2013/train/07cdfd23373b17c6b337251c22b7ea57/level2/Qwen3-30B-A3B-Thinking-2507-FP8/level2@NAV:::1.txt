

This paper presents a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference of large networks using a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), and a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The main claim is that their method achieves orders-of-magnitude faster inference (hours for million-node networks) compared to state-of-the-art approaches (days for networks of 100,000 nodes) while maintaining competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction.

The paper's technical contribution is well-supported through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real networks. The authors demonstrate that their PTM model (with O(K) parameters versus O(K³) in the MMTM) and stochastic variational inference algorithm (O(NK) complexity versus O(Nδ²K³) for Gibbs sampling) provide significant scalability improvements. Their results on the Brightkite (1M nodes) and Stanford networks show convergence in 12 and 4 minutes respectively, compared to 8 days and 18.5 hours for previous methods. The synthetic experiments further validate that their approach maintains high accuracy (NMI > 0.95) as network size increases, while competing methods degrade or fail to converge.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality through clear theoretical analysis and well-executed experiments. The authors properly contextualize their work against relevant literature (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM), showing how their approach integrates three crucial scalability principles that prior methods only partially address. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

However, the paper could better address the limitations of the triangular representation. While the authors acknowledge it doesn't preserve all network information, they could more thoroughly discuss scenarios where edge-based representations might be necessary or where triangular motifs might fail to capture important structural patterns. Additionally, while they claim "competitive or improved accuracy," a more detailed analysis of when their approach outperforms edge-based methods would strengthen their claims.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that makes a significant step toward scalable network analysis. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear practical utility. The work advances the state of the art in scalable network modeling with a novel combination of succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and efficient inference. I recommend acceptance.
