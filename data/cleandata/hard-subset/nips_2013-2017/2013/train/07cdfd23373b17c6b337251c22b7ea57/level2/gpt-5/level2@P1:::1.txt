Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large, sparse networks by combining (i) a succinct “bag-of-triangles” representation [8]; (ii) a parsimonious triangular latent-role model (PTM) whose triangle-generating parameters scale as O(K) rather than O(K3); and (iii) a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and stochastic natural gradients. Compared to edge-based mixed-membership models (MMSB [1], a-MMSB [5]) and the triangular MMTM [8], the contribution is to retain the informative higher-order structure of triangles while avoiding the O(K3) tensor, enabling O(NK) global updates and O(K) local updates via a mixture-of-deltas approximation. Empirically, the method recovers latent roles on synthetic data generated from MMSB and a power-law latent model, achieves competitive or improved link prediction on several real networks, and scales to 1M nodes and 100 roles within hours on a single multi-core machine—orders of magnitude faster than prior probabilistic approaches.

Quality
- Modeling: The PTM’s parameter sharing groups the O(K3) role-triplet configurations into three families with parameters Bxxx, Bxx, and B0. This sharply reduces complexity and, per results, appears sufficient for accurate role recovery and link prediction on the tested datasets. The authors are transparent that PTM is not a full generative model of networks (triangles only), which is acceptable given the stated objective (latent role inference).
- Inference: The structured mean-field over triad role assignments avoids pathological rank-1 factorizations. The SVI framework with mini-batches is standard and appropriate. The O(K) local update via a randomly refreshed mixture-of-deltas is clever and practically important; the claim that any choice of off-diagonals yields a valid lower bound is reasonable from variational theory.
- Theory: Two issues need clarification. (1) The unbiasedness of the stochastic natural gradient is guaranteed when local factors are optimized for the fixed global parameters and the mini-batch is sampled uniformly. With the mixture-of-deltas approximation and re-sampling of the candidate set A, the objective being optimized changes; convergence to a stationary point of the original bound is not established. Some discussion or an inexact SVI convergence reference would strengthen the paper. (2) In Section 5.2, the step-size exponent is reported as κ = −0.5, which contradicts the Robbins–Monro conditions and would increase step sizes. This appears to be a typo or mismatch with the earlier convergence conditions and should be corrected.
- Empirics: The synthetic recovery experiments are well designed (varying N and K, easy/hard settings) and fairly compare several baselines with identical initializations. The large real-data experiments convincingly demonstrate scalability and fast convergence (2–5 passes). However, some experimental details are underspecified (see below), and ablations (exact local update vs O(K) approximation; effect of δ) would help isolate the contribution of each design choice.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with helpful contrasts to MMSB/a-MMSB and MMTM. The model’s equivalence classes and induced parameter sharing are explained well. Several key update equations and the natural gradients are deferred to the Appendix; that is fine if they are complete. Please fix the step-size parameter inconsistency, and add a few clarifying details noted below.

Originality
The work combines existing ingredients in a novel and effective way: the bag-of-triangles representation [8], an a-MMSB–style parameter sharing idea adapted to triads, structured mean-field within SVI, and an O(K) local approximation. The mixture-of-deltas approximation for triad posteriors and the specific PTM design appear original and practically impactful.

Significance
The ability to perform probabilistic mixed-membership inference with hundreds of roles on million-node networks is valuable. The method substantially improves the scalability frontier for probabilistic latent role models while maintaining competitive accuracy. This should be useful to researchers and practitioners needing interpretable, role-based models beyond simple embeddings.

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Strong scalability with principled probabilistic modeling; orders-of-magnitude speedups over a-MMSB [5] and MMTM [8].
- Sensible structured variational approximation; memory-efficient implementation (discarding local variational factors).
- Competitive or improved accuracy in latent role recovery and link prediction on both synthetic and real networks.
- Clear articulation of modeling choices and their computational consequences.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Missing/unclear theoretical justification for convergence with the inexact local (mixture-of-deltas) updates.
- Potential underfitting due to aggressive parameter sharing: B0 is shared across all all-distinct-role triads, and Bxx is shared across minority roles; PTM may miss role-specific triadic asymmetries.
- Experimental details and ablations are limited (no sensitivity to δ, no comparison of exact vs approximate local updates, limited discussion of initialization without ground-truth seeds).
- Possible issue/typo in the SVI step-size schedule (κ sign).

Questions and suggestions for the authors
1) Convergence and approximation: Please clarify whether the stochastic natural gradient remains unbiased when local updates use the mixture-of-deltas approximation and A is re-sampled. If not, can you cite an inexact SVI result or provide a practical convergence criterion?
2) Step size: Is κ = −0.5 a typo? Please report the actual values used that satisfy ∑t ρt = ∞ and ∑t ρt2 < ∞.
3) Expressiveness: Can PTM be extended to allow role-pair-specific parameters for “two-equal-one-different” triads (e.g., Bx,y for the minority role y), or a small number of B0 components, to capture heterogeneous all-distinct triads? An ablation demonstrating the trade-off between parameter count and accuracy would be informative.
4) Link prediction scoring: When ranking i–j, do you normalize the marginalization over k to avoid degree bias (nodes with more third neighbors)? Please specify negative sampling, metric (AUC/PR), and whether results are averaged over multiple splits with variance.
5) Sensitivity: Please report performance vs δ and mini-batch size, and show memory usage and triangle extraction time. Also include results with the proposed heuristic initialization (no ground-truth seeds).
6) Exact vs approximate local updates: On a mid-sized dataset, compare the O(K3) exact local update to the O(K) mixture-of-deltas to quantify any accuracy gap.

Minor
- Please provide the exact forms of the natural gradients in the Appendix and the size of A used (6K combinations).
- Clarify per-pass complexity: O(mK) with m triangles plus O(NK) global updates, and give m vs N for your datasets.
- If possible, release code and triangle-extraction utilities to encourage reuse.

Overall recommendation
A strong contribution to scalable probabilistic network modeling with clear empirical benefits. Pending clarification of the SVI step size and convergence under the local approximation, I lean toward acceptance.
