This paper presents a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference in large networks, addressing a key bottleneck in existing methods. The core idea is to combine a succinct network representation based on triangular motifs with a parsimonious statistical model and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. The paper argues convincingly that these three principles are crucial for scaling to networks with millions of vertices and hundreds of latent roles.

The work builds on prior research in network modeling, particularly the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB) and the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM). It effectively identifies the limitations of these approaches – the quadratic complexity of MMSB and the cubic complexity of MMTM with respect to the number of latent roles – and proposes a solution that achieves linear complexity in the number of roles. The use of triangular motifs, initially proposed by Ho et al. (2013), is central to this improvement. The proposed Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) and the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm demonstrate a significant step forward in scalability.

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The problem is well-motivated and clearly defined. The proposed approach is technically sound and demonstrably scalable, as evidenced by the experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks. The results show significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art methods, while maintaining competitive accuracy. The thorough experimental evaluation, including a comparison with MMSB and MMTM, is particularly commendable. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details. The discussion of the limitations of the mean-field approximation and the justification for the chosen structured mean-field approach are insightful. The convergence analysis and the detailed explanation of the algorithm’s complexity are valuable contributions. 

However, some areas could benefit from further clarification. While the paper mentions δ-subsampling, a more detailed explanation of the impact of different δ values on accuracy and runtime would be helpful. The connection between the choice of α and λ parameters in the Dirichlet distributions and the expected properties of the latent space is not explicitly discussed. Providing guidance on parameter tuning would be beneficial. The convergence analysis relies on stochastic natural gradient ascent. A more detailed discussion of the step size parameters (τ0, τ1, κ) and their impact on convergence would strengthen the analysis. The paper briefly touches upon the potential for improvement of MMTM/PTM Gibbs samplers but does not elaborate on potential strategies, which would be valuable given their importance as baselines. The reference to Ho et al. (2013) is a bit vague; citing the specific paper would improve clarity. While the paper mentions the limitations of modeling networks only through triangles, a brief discussion of how to incorporate edge-based features or other higher-order statistics could provide a direction for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant scalability improvement over existing methods for latent space inference in large networks.
*   Technically sound approach with a clear and well-justified design.
*   Comprehensive experimental evaluation demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method.
*   Addresses a key bottleneck in network analysis and opens up new possibilities for analyzing large-scale networks.
*   Clear and concise writing, making the paper accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The impact of parameter choices (α, λ, δ) on performance could be explored in more detail.
*   A deeper discussion of the convergence properties of the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm is warranted.
*   The discussion of future directions could be more specific.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed approach is novel, scalable, and well-supported by experimental results. While there are minor areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh its weaknesses. The work is of high quality and is likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners in the field. The paper is recommended for acceptance.
