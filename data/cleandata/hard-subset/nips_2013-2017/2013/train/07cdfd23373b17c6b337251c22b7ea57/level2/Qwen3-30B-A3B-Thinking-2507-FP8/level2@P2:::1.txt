

This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference of large networks by leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors argue that three principles are crucial for large-scale inference: (1) succinct network representation, (2) parsimonious statistical modeling, and (3) scalable inference algorithms. They build on the triangular motif representation from Ho et al. (2012) but introduce a novel parameter sharing scheme that reduces the triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) to O(K), enabling efficient inference with O(NK) complexity.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, the PTM model's parameter sharing scheme is well-motivated and effectively reduces the model's complexity. The authors provide a detailed explanation of how the role triplet configurations induce equivalence classes, allowing them to share parameters across structurally similar configurations. Second, the stochastic variational inference algorithm is carefully designed with a structured mean-field approximation that captures higher-order dependencies between latent variables, addressing a limitation of naive mean-field approaches. Third, the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's scalability, achieving latent space inference on a 1M-node network in just 4 hours on a single machine, while maintaining competitive accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The experimental design is thorough, with comprehensive comparisons across synthetic and real-world networks. The authors appropriately evaluate both latent space recovery and link prediction tasks, and they provide detailed runtime analysis showing orders-of-magnitude speed improvements over existing methods.

However, there are several important weaknesses that need to be addressed. First, the theoretical justification for the parameter sharing scheme is somewhat superficial. While the authors enumerate the equivalence classes and their associated parameters, they don't provide a rigorous argument for why this particular sharing scheme would generalize well to different network structures. A more thorough analysis of the conditions under which this parameter sharing would be effective would strengthen the paper.

Second, the experimental comparison with the MMTM Gibbs sampler is not entirely fair. The authors note that MMTM Gibbs gets stuck in local minima, but they don't provide a thorough comparison of the quality of solutions found by different methods, especially for larger K values. It would be more convincing to show that their method consistently finds better solutions, not just faster ones.

Third, the claim that their method "achieves competitive or improved accuracy" needs more concrete evidence. While Figure 1 shows comparable NMI for synthetic data, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence for real-world networks. The link prediction results in Table 2 are somewhat limited in scope (only three networks), and the paper doesn't discuss whether the accuracy improvements are statistically significant.

The paper does an excellent job of placing their work in context, citing relevant prior work on network modeling and latent space inference. The related work section appropriately distinguishes their contributions from previous methods, particularly highlighting how their approach addresses the three scalability principles that previous methods fail to satisfy simultaneously.

In terms of significance, this work has clear potential impact for large-scale network analysis, addressing a major bottleneck in the field. The ability to handle networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine represents a meaningful advancement over existing methods. The paper's demonstration of convergence on the Brightkite network in 12 minutes compared to 8 days for previous methods is particularly compelling.

The main weakness is that the paper doesn't fully substantiate its claims about accuracy improvements. While the speed improvements are well-documented, the accuracy claims need more rigorous statistical validation across diverse network types. The authors should provide additional experiments showing that their method consistently outperforms competitors in terms of both accuracy and speed, especially for very large networks.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact. The technical contributions are well-executed, and the experimental results are convincing for scalability. With more thorough validation of the accuracy claims and a deeper theoretical justification for the parameter sharing scheme, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS. As it stands, the paper makes a valuable contribution to scalable network analysis, but the claims about accuracy need more substantiation.

Strengths: Novel parameter sharing scheme, well-designed stochastic variational inference algorithm, compelling scalability results, thorough experimental evaluation.

Weaknesses: Superficial theoretical justification for parameter sharing, limited accuracy comparison for real-world networks, fairness of comparison with MMTM Gibbs (local minima issue).
