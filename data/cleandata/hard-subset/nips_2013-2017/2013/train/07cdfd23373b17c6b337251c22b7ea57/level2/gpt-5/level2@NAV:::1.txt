This paper proposes a scalable probabilistic approach to latent space inference for large networks by combining (i) a bag-of-triangles representation, (ii) a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) via role-sharing, and (iii) a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients and an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” local update. Relative to MMSB, a-MMSB (NIPS), and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM, NIPS), the work preserves the advantages of motif-based modeling while addressing the main scalability bottleneck in K and N. Experiments on synthetic and large real networks (up to 1M nodes, 100 roles) report orders-of-magnitude speedups over MMTM and MMSB with competitive or better latent role recovery and link prediction.

Quality: The modeling ideas are sound and well-motivated by the literature on triadic closure and ERGMs. The structured mean-field choice is appropriate; the mixture-of-deltas approximation is a clever computational device. Empirical support is strong in runtime and convergence, with reasonable accuracy on both synthetic role recovery (NMI) and link prediction. However, some aspects need tightening: statistical variability (repeats, error bars), significance tests for link prediction, and clearer fairness controls (e.g., seeding with ground-truth roles). The SVI step-size specification seems inconsistent: Algorithm 1 uses ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ with convergence conditions requiring κ ∈ (0.5, 1], while Section 5 sets κ = −0.5, which would violate diminishing steps; this likely needs correction.

Clarity: Generally well-written and organized, with concrete complexity analyses and algorithmic details. A few notational heavy spots (local update cases) could be streamlined; the triangle extraction/subsampling procedure would benefit from a concise pseudocode and complexity bounds.

Originality: The O(K) parameterization for triangle motifs via equivalence classes is a meaningful and nontrivial extension of MMTM, paralleling the a-MMSB idea for edges. The structured SVI plus O(K) local approximation is novel and practically important.

Significance: High. A method that scales to millions of nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine is valuable to the community, and results indicate the approach may be preferable to edge-based probabilistic models on large social graphs.

Pros:
- Clear, principled route to O(NK) inference; strong empirical speedups.
- Solid probabilistic foundation; structured variational design.
- Practical scalability on real networks.

Cons:
- Step-size/exponent inconsistency; missing variance/robustness analyses.
- Limited baselines on large graphs (no spectral or random-walk embeddings).
- Link prediction metric/reporting could be expanded.

Suggestions: Fix the SVI step-size issue; report multiple runs with error bars; add ablations (δ, size of A, structured vs naive mean-field); compare to strong non-probabilistic baselines; release code and data splits. Overall, a strong and impactful contribution.
