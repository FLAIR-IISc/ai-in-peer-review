Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large graphs by (i) representing the network as a bag of triangles (open/closed), (ii) proposing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) with O(K) triangle-generating parameters via symmetry-based parameter sharing across role-triplet configurations, and (iii) devising a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and a mixture-of-deltas local approximation that reduces per-triangle updates from O(K3) to O(K). The approach is intended to scale in both the number of nodes N and roles K, with empirical demonstrations up to 1M nodes and K=100.

The work builds on mixed-membership latent role models (MMSB), their scalable variants (a-MMSB), and the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM). Compared to MMSB and a-MMSB, the contribution is to replace edge-based likelihoods with motif-based (triadic) likelihoods and to tie parameters so that the number of triangle parameters grows linearly in K rather than cubically as in MMTM. The inference follows SVI in conjugate exponential families with natural gradient updates, similar in spirit to Hoffman et al., but tailored to the structured local dependencies among triad role assignments.

Strengths
- Clear scalability target and principled route: triangle-based data reduction, parsimonious O(K) parameterization, and stochastic natural gradients. The computational story is coherent.
- Elegant parameter sharing: grouping the K3 role-triplet cases by the number of distinct roles (all-same, two-same, all-distinct) is a simple, intuitive symmetry that slashes parameter count while retaining role-specific differentiation through Bxx and Bxxx.
- Inference engineering: structured mean-field for triad roles (vs naive rank-1 factorization) and the mixture-of-deltas approximation are well-motivated and appear to be the right compromises for speed and accuracy.
- Strong empirical scale: demonstrations on million-node graphs and K up to 100 on a single multi-core machine, with 2–5 passes to convergence, are compelling.
- Practical utility: latent role recovery and link prediction show competitive or better accuracy on several datasets; the qualitative argument for motif-based modeling in social graphs is well grounded.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Missing baseline: The most relevant probabilistic baseline is a-MMSB (Gopalan et al.) with SVI. While the paper cites its long runtime on Brightkite, the empirical section does not include a head-to-head comparison of accuracy/speed on the same hardware/settings. Using MMSB Batch Variational (O(N2K2)) and MMTM Gibbs (O(K3)) as main baselines understates the state of the art.
- Expressivity vs. sharing: Tying all configurations with two equal roles to a single Bxx (independent of the minority role) and all-distinct cases to a single B0 may be too coarse for networks where minority-role identity matters. This is a modeling choice, not an inherent necessity of triadic modeling. A low-rank tensor parameterization (e.g., CP/Tucker) could provide an expressivity/speed trade-off; at least a discussion or ablation on the loss of expressivity would be valuable.
- Objective and unbiasedness: The SVI gradient unbiasedness claim ES[∇LS]=∇L holds for mini-batch subsampling of triangles. Once the local family is restricted to the mixture-of-deltas, the algorithm maximizes a different lower bound L̃≤L. The gradients are no longer unbiased for L; they are unbiased for L̃. The text conflates these. Please clarify which bound is being optimized and adjust the convergence claim accordingly.
- δ-subsampling and correction: The δ-subsampling of triangles is said to approximately preserve motif distributions, but the inference objective is then defined on the subsampled bag without importance weighting. This induces a biased objective relative to the full-triangle model. Some analysis of the bias or an importance-weighting correction (when sampling non-uniformly by node degree) would strengthen the claims.
- Equation/notation issues: In Eq. (6), the term Eq[log θi,x + log θj,x + log θk,x] for distinct x,y,z seems incorrect; presumably it should be log θi,x + log θj,y + log θk,z. Also, the step-size schedule states ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ and uses κ = −0.5, which would make the step grow with t; SVI typically uses κ in (0.5, 1]. This looks like a sign error and should be fixed/clarified.
- Generative mismatch and coverage: Like MMTM, PTM is not a generative model of graphs (triangles may be inconsistent; 0/1-edge triples are ignored). This is fine given the goal (latent roles), but it weakens likelihood-based comparisons and complicates link prediction: many real edges are not part of any triangle. The paper should explicitly state how edges with no available third node are handled and quantify the fraction of such cases.
- Evaluation protocol and fairness: The seeding strategy (2 example nodes per role) is not typical in unsupervised settings and can advantage methods differently. Results should include runs with random initializations (with multiple restarts) and a sensitivity analysis to seeding. Also, ablations are missing: effect of A’s size in the mixture-of-deltas, of structured vs naive mean-field, and of δ.
- Complexity statements: The paper claims O(NK) complexity for the global updates (true), but overall per-pass cost is O(mK) with m triangles; with δ-subsampling m=O(Nδ2). This should be consistently emphasized to avoid overclaiming O(NK) total complexity.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized, and the intuition for parameter sharing and the structured inference is clear. However, several typos/equation issues (above), and the lack of some appendix details referenced (e.g., closed-form natural gradients; full local updates for φxxx and φxxy) make it harder for a reader to reproduce results. A more explicit algorithmic complexity breakdown per step would help.

Originality
The combination of triangle-based representation, symmetry-driven parameter sharing to O(K), and a carefully engineered SVI with structured mean-field for triads is novel and non-trivial. While inspired by a-MMSB’s parameter tying and prior MMTM, the specific design and inference approximations appear new.

Significance
If the empirical claims hold under stronger baselines and clarified methodology, the approach is impactful: it offers a practical path to probabilistic latent role inference at million-node scales with interpretable motif semantics. This fills a gap between embedding methods and heavy-weight probabilistic models.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Compare against a-MMSB with SVI on the same datasets; include accuracy, runtime, and convergence passes.
- Fix Eq. (6) and clarify the step-size schedule. Provide the exact natural gradient expressions in the appendix.
- Clarify the theoretical status of the bound being optimized with the mixture-of-deltas local family and provide an ablation on its size/choice A.
- Discuss/quantify the effect of δ-subsampling; consider importance weighting to debias.
- Report link prediction handling for dyads not in any triangle; consider augmenting the likelihood with an edge term.
- Provide ablations: structured vs naive mean-field; effect of seeding; sensitivity to K.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong, simple idea with substantial scalability gains.
- Solid engineering of SVI to exploit structure; parallelizable and memory-efficient.
- Empirically impressive scale and reasonable accuracy.

Cons for acceptance
- Missing key baseline (a-MMSB) and limited ablations.
- Some technical inaccuracies/typos and ambiguous convergence claims.
- Potential loss of expressivity due to aggressive parameter tying without analysis.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on addressing the baseline comparison and clarifying the methodological issues noted above. The paper presents a promising and practically useful contribution to scalable probabilistic network modeling.
