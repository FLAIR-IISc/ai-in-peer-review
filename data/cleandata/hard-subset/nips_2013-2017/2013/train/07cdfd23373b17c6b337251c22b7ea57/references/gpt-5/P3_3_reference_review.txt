Summary
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic latent space model for large networks based on a bag-of-triangles representation. The key modeling idea is the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) in MMTM to O(K) by sharing parameters across equivalence classes of role-triplet configurations: Bxxx for all-equal roles, Bxx for two-equal-one-different (with majority role x), and a global B0 for all-distinct roles. For inference, the authors develop a stochastic variational algorithm with structured mean-field over per-triangle role triplets, mini-batching over triangles, and natural-gradient updates for global parameters. To reduce local-update complexity from O(K^3) to O(K), they introduce a “mixture-of-deltas” variational restriction that concentrates mass on diagonal and a random O(K) subset of off-diagonal role triplets. They combine this with δ-subsampling of triangles to achieve practical scalability and show empirical gains in speed and comparable or better accuracy than MMSB and MMTM on synthetic data and several real networks, including million-node graphs.

Quality
Strengths
- Substantive modeling contribution: the parameter-sharing scheme in PTM is a principled way to impose structure on the O(K^3) triangle tensor, analogous to the a-MMSB reduction of O(K^2) to O(K), and aligns with social-triadic assumptions.
- Inference innovation: the structured mean-field over per-triangle role triplets is appropriate (naive mean-field would be too crude). The random O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” restriction is a clever device to bring local inference to O(K) while still capturing high-rank interactions.
- Algorithmic clarity: use of stochastic natural gradients with mini-batching over triangles is standard but well-motivated here. The discard of local q’s to reduce memory is practical.
- Empirical results: strong speedups over prior triangular/edge models; synthetic evaluations demonstrate robustness to increasing K; link prediction results suggest triangle-based modeling is effective in social networks.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Objective mismatch and convergence guarantees: Stochastic variational inference guarantees rely on unbiased gradients of the same ELBO. Once the local optimization is replaced by the restricted mixture-of-deltas family (random A per triangle), the algorithm optimizes a different, tighter or looser bound than L(η,γ). The paper asserts that any A yields a valid lower bound, but does not clarify whether the stochastic gradients remain unbiased for a fixed objective, nor whether the randomization in A preserves convergence guarantees. A short formal argument or theorem would strengthen the claim.
- Expressivity trade-off: While the O(K) parameterization is the source of scalability, it also imposes strong tying:
  - All-distinct role triplets share B0 irrespective of which three roles appear; thus triads involving, e.g., roles {sports, politics, spam} are indistinguishable from {music, tech, fashion}.
  - For two-equal-one-different, Bxx ignores which minority role is present.
  This can hurt in networks where specific inter-role motifs matter (e.g., role A closing triads only with role B but not C). A comparison with a low-rank tensor parameterization (e.g., CP/Tucker factorization) as a middle ground between O(K^3) and O(K) would be informative.
- Modeling limitations: The PTM is not a generative model of full networks; triangles are independent and may be inconsistent on shared edges. For link prediction, the proposed scoring marginalizes over third nodes, potentially double-counting evidence and overemphasizing high-degree nodes. The paper would benefit from a brief analysis of bias introduced by the independence assumption when converting triangle likelihoods to edge probabilities.
- Scalability details: Complexity alternates between O(NK) and O(Nδ^2K). The latter is correct for m = O(Nδ^2) triangles; O(NK) holds only if δ is constant and small. For δ=50, m≈1225N, which is large for N=1M. The experiments claim 10% mini-batches and a few passes; concrete numbers for m, batch sizes, wall-clock, memory footprint, and triangle materialization strategy are needed. Do the authors store all triangles or sample on-the-fly? Triangle extraction costs are nontrivial on large graphs.
- Baselines: The comparison omits a-MMSB (the main scalable MMSB variant critiqued early). Runtime comparisons on Brightkite are indirect; a head-to-head on shared tasks (including link prediction) would make the case stronger. It would also help to include a simple triangle-augmented edge baseline (e.g., MMSB with a transitivity feature or a wedge/triangle-regularized variant) to isolate the benefit of triangle representation versus parameter sharing.
- Applicability: Triangle-centric models may struggle on low-clustering or bipartite-like graphs where few triangles exist; edges in no connected triple are ignored. An ablation on such graphs (e.g., citation or user–item networks) would clarify the scope.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. However, several vital pieces are in the Appendix (update equations for φxxx and φxxy, expressions for natural gradients) and Table 1 is referenced but missing. Including at least the core φ updates and the form of the natural gradients would aid reproducibility.
- Be consistent about complexity claims (state assumptions on δ explicitly). Specify hardware, exact thread model, and how triangles are stored or sampled at scale.

Originality
- The combination of a triangle-based representation, PTM parameter sharing tailored to triadic equivalence classes, and stochastic variational inference with an O(K) local approximation is novel and well-motivated. There is clear differentiation from MMTM and a-MMSB.

Significance
- If the convergence claims hold under the approximate local updates, the method meaningfully advances scalable latent space inference for large social networks with many roles. The strong practical speedups and competitive accuracy make this of interest to both researchers and practitioners. The work also helps popularize motif-based representations for probabilistic network modeling.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide a convergence statement for the randomized restricted variational family. Are gradients unbiased for a fixed ELBO? If not, what is optimized and what are the guarantees?
- Report m, mini-batch size, passes, wall-clock, memory, and whether you materialize all triangles for the million-node experiments.
- Add a-MMSB baselines on the same tasks/datasets. Consider a low-rank tensor alternative to PTM as a middle expressivity baseline.
- Analyze/link-prediction bias stemming from triangle independence; compare to a wedge-based edge score.
- Discuss performance on low-clustering graphs; include at least one such dataset.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant parameter reduction with clear intuition
- Scalable, practical inference with strong empirical speedups
- Solid structured variational design and engineering

Cons for acceptance
- Missing theoretical clarity on convergence under the approximate local family
- Limited baselines (no a-MMSB) and incomplete scalability details
- Potential expressivity limitations unquantified

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept leaning accept. The paper addresses an important scalability challenge with a thoughtful modeling/inference design and compelling empirical results. Strengthening the convergence discussion and baselines would make it a clear accept.
