Summary
The paper proposes a scalable latent space model for large networks built on the bag-of-triangles representation of Ho et al. (NIPS’11). The key modeling contribution, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), ties together the O(K^3) triangle-generation parameters of MMTM into O(K) groups based on whether the three roles in a triangle are all equal, two equal/one different, or all distinct. For inference, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with a structured mean-field posterior over per-triangle role triples. To reduce local update cost from O(K^3) to O(K), they introduce a “mixture-of-deltas” approximation that concentrates mass on the K diagonal triplets and a random O(K) subset of off-diagonal triplets; global updates use natural gradients. They claim overall O(NK) scaling (with δ-subsampling of triangles) and show results on synthetic data and several real networks, reporting fast convergence and improved or competitive latent space recovery and link prediction relative to MMSB variational, MMTM Gibbs, and PTM Gibbs.

Relation to prior work
The paper directly extends prior work on triangular modeling (MMTM; NIPS’11) and on scalable overlapping blockmodels (a-MMSB; NIPS’12), and uses standard SVI/natural gradient machinery (Hoffman et al., JMLR’13; Amari, 1998). The parameter tying is analogous in spirit to a-MMSB’s O(K) inter-block parameters. The paper positions itself as unifying (1) succinct triangular representation, (2) parsimonious modeling via parameter sharing, and (3) scalable SVI.

Strengths
- Parsimonious parameterization: The O(K) tying scheme is a natural way to reduce the K^3 tensor of MMTM and aligns with the assortative simplification used in a-MMSB.
- Scalable inference design: Structured mean-field plus mini-batching and natural gradients are appropriate and well-motivated. The implementation detail of discarding local q’s to save memory is practical.
- Parallelizability: The per-triangle local updates are trivially parallelizable; memory usage is kept modest.
- Empirical promise: On synthetic data, PTM-SVI reportedly converges in 1–2 passes and recovers roles well; on large networks, the reported wall-clock times are attractive.
- Clarity of the triangular equivalence classes and how they induce shared probabilities is well-explained.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling restrictiveness and lack of analysis: The PTM’s tying (B0, Bxx, Bxxx) is quite coarse—e.g., it ignores which minority role is present and treats all “all-distinct” triplets identically. This sacrifices expressiveness relative to MMTM, but the paper provides limited analysis of when this approximation is appropriate and how it degrades performance. No identifiability or approximation bounds are discussed.
- Not a generative model of networks: The authors acknowledge the model ignores empty/single-edge triples and does not impose consistency across overlapping triangles. Yet likelihoods and perplexities are reported on triangle bags, while downstream claims (e.g., link prediction) involve edges. The connection between triangle-likelihood optimization and edge prediction quality is not theoretically justified.
- Complexity claims and consistency: The paper alternately claims O(NK) and O(Nδ^2K). Since the bag-of-triangles has size Θ(Nδ^2), the per-pass cost is necessarily O(Nδ^2K), with only the global update O(NK). This distinction should be made precise, and the conditions under which δ can be treated as a constant should be stated and empirically explored.
- Local update approximation lacks guarantees: The mixture-of-deltas restriction to O(K) configurations is algorithmically appealing, but (i) it changes the variational family across iterations by re-sampling A, (ii) introduces unquantified bias, and (iii) invalidates the “unbiased gradient” claim unless the randomness over A is handled in the expectation. There is no analysis of how this affects convergence, stability, or the tightness of the bound.
- Empirical baselines and fairness: 
  - No direct comparison to a-MMSB or more recent scalable MMSB variants under matched hardware and implementations. The headline Brightkite speedup is taken from the literature, not from a re-run in the authors’ environment.
  - Missing strong non-probabilistic baselines widely used at scale (e.g., node2vec/DeepWalk, BigCLAM, spectral/triad-based embeddings) for role/community discovery and link prediction.
  - For synthetic experiments, seeding 2% of nodes with true roles is a strong advantage; while acknowledged, this makes comparisons to MMSB/MMTM less informative. Unseeded runs and robustness to initialization are needed.
- Ablations are missing: 
  - No comparison between exact O(K^3) structured local updates and the O(K) approximation for small K to quantify the trade-off.
  - No ablation on parameter tying (e.g., allow a few Bx,y,z groups beyond the three proposed) to assess accuracy vs. complexity.
  - No study of δ-subsampling’s effect on accuracy vs. speed on real graphs with varying degree distributions.
- Evaluation details are insufficient: 
  - Link prediction: specifics (AUC/precision@k?), handling of class imbalance, and computational method for ranking via triangle marginalization are not detailed; edge-ranking by summing over all k may be prohibitively expensive without additional tricks.
  - Perplexities are on triangle bags; their relation to predictive edge performance remains unclear.
  - Reported wall-clock times mix different thread counts and implementations; fair, same-machine comparisons would be more convincing.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters are given, but code availability, dataset preprocessing, and exact train/heldout splits are not included.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you quantify the bias introduced by the mixture-of-deltas restriction and random choice of A? Are gradients still unbiased when averaging over both triangle and A sampling?
2. For small K, how close is the O(K) local approximation to the exact structured update in terms of bound value and task metrics?
3. How sensitive are results to δ? Please provide curves of performance vs. δ and degree distributions (especially on low-clustering graphs).
4. Can you provide same-machine, same-thread comparisons against a-MMSB (and, ideally, node embedding baselines and BigCLAM)?
5. How is the link prediction score computed efficiently? What metric is reported?
6. Under what network regimes (clustering coefficients, role separability) does PTM underperform MMTM or MMSB?

Minor comments
- Clarify the O(NK) vs O(Nδ^2K) complexity statements throughout.
- Some references are incomplete; ensure full bibliographic details.
- Notation: using ∆1 to denote binary simplex is unusual; consider clarifying early.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper addresses an important scalability problem with a sensible parameter tying and an efficient SVI implementation, and the empirical speedups are promising. However, the modeling approximations are strong and insufficiently analyzed; the key local update approximation lacks theoretical justification; and the empirical evaluation omits crucial baselines and ablations, with some fairness concerns. With a more rigorous treatment of the approximation, clearer complexity accounting, and stronger, fair comparisons, this work could become a solid contribution.
