This paper tackles a crucial problem in network analysis – scalable latent space inference – and proposes a genuinely novel approach that merits strong consideration. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods, particularly their inability to handle million-node networks with a reasonable number of latent roles. Their core idea – a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) combined with stochastic variational inference – is both elegant and impactful. The reduction in parameter dimensionality from O(K^3) to O(K) is a significant theoretical advance, and the reported O(NK) complexity is highly promising.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, effectively motivating the problem, outlining the proposed solution, and presenting experimental results. The authors thoughtfully address the trade-offs between model complexity, accuracy, and scalability. The connection to existing work (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM) is clearly explained, and the authors convincingly demonstrate the shortcomings of those methods. The introduction skillfully lays out the three key principles – succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference – and convincingly argues that their approach achieves all three. The detailed explanation of the triangular representation and the parameter sharing strategy in the PTM are particularly commendable.

The strength of this paper lies in its *boldness*. It’s not an incremental improvement; it’s a fundamentally different approach to latent space inference. The theoretical justification for the parameter sharing is sound, and the experimental results, while perhaps not exhaustively comprehensive, are compelling. The speedup observed on the large Youtube network is remarkable, demonstrating a practical impact that many NIPS submissions lack.  The choice of stochastic variational inference, coupled with the structured mean-field approximation, appears well-motivated and effectively addresses the scalability challenges.

However, some areas could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. While the authors mention the limitations of a fully factorized mean-field approximation, a more detailed comparison with other structured mean-field approaches would be valuable.  The choice of hyperparameters (α, λ, ρt parameters) appears somewhat arbitrary, and a more systematic sensitivity analysis would strengthen the results.  The “δ-subsampling” procedure is briefly described, but a more detailed discussion of its impact on accuracy would be helpful, particularly in the context of varying network degree distributions.  The convergence analysis relies on the stochastic natural gradient, and more details on the convergence properties (e.g., a convergence rate) would be appreciated. 

The synthetic data generation is well-described, but it’s unclear how representative these synthetic networks are of real-world networks beyond the Youtube example. It would be helpful to test the algorithm on a wider range of real-world datasets, perhaps including citation networks, biological networks, or knowledge graphs. While the link prediction results are promising, comparing against strong baseline methods specifically designed for link prediction (beyond MMSB) would provide a more robust evaluation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* **Novelty:** The PTM and the O(NK) inference algorithm represent a significant departure from existing approaches and offer a substantial theoretical and practical improvement.
* **Scalability:** Demonstrated ability to handle a 1M-node network in a reasonable timeframe is a major achievement.
* **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
* **Significance:**  Addresses a critical bottleneck in network analysis and opens up new possibilities for studying large-scale networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* **Limited hyperparameter sensitivity analysis.**
* **Relatively limited evaluation on diverse real-world datasets.**
* **Convergence analysis could be more rigorous.**
* **Comparison to state-of-the-art link prediction baselines is lacking.**



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite minor weaknesses, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its shortcomings. The authors have presented a truly novel and scalable approach to a challenging problem in network analysis.  The potential impact of this work is significant, and it aligns well with the spirit of NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.  I believe the minor suggestions outlined above could be addressed in a revision, further strengthening the paper.
