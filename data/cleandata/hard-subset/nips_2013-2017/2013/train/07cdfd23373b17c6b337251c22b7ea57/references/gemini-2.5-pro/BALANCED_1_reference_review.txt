### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a probabilistic latent space model for large-scale network analysis. The work aims to address the key challenge of scalability in existing methods, which often struggle with networks exceeding 100,000 nodes or a large number of latent roles (K). The authors propose an approach built on three principles: (1) a succinct network representation, (2) a parsimonious statistical model, and (3) a scalable inference algorithm.

For representation, the paper adopts the "bag-of-triangles" approach from the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) [8], which is more compact than the standard adjacency matrix for many real-world networks. The core contribution is the PTM itself, which dramatically reduces the parameter complexity of the MMTM. Where MMTM uses an O(K^3) tensor to parameterize triangle formation probabilities based on latent roles, PTM introduces a parameter-sharing scheme that reduces this complexity to O(K). This is achieved by grouping role-triplet configurations based on whether the roles are identical (e.g., x,x,x), have a majority role (e.g., x,x,y), or are all distinct (e.g., x,y,z), and assigning shared parameters to each group. This idea is analogous to the simplification made by a-MMSB [5] over the original MMSB [1].

To perform inference, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm. Key aspects of the algorithm include a structured mean-field approximation to retain dependencies within triangles, the use of natural gradients for efficient updates, and a novel O(K) approximation for the local variational update step, which avoids an O(K^3) bottleneck. The resulting algorithm has a time complexity of O(NK), a significant improvement over prior art.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates that PTM achieves comparable or superior accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks compared to MMTM and MMSB, while being orders ofmagnitude faster. The authors showcase the method's scalability by inferring a 100-role model on a 1.1 million-node network in just 4 hours on a single multi-core machine.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and important problem in machine learning and network science. The ability to perform probabilistic latent space inference on million-node networks with a large number of roles is a significant practical and scientific achievement. The reported runtimes (e.g., 4 hours for a 1M-node graph with K=100) represent a substantial leap forward compared to the baselines cited (e.g., a-MMSB taking days for a smaller task). The O(NK) complexity is a key strength that enables this scalability.

2.  **Novelty and Soundness:** The primary contribution—the parsimonious parameterization of the triangular model—is novel and well-motivated by the goal of scalability. The combination of this model with a carefully designed SVI algorithm, which includes a clever O(K) approximation for the local updates, constitutes a strong and complete piece of work. The technical components, from the structured variational approximation to the use of natural gradients, are sound and appropriate for the problem.

3.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors wisely use synthetic data where the ground truth is known to rigorously evaluate latent space recovery, demonstrating superior performance over baselines, especially as N and K grow. The comparison on real-world link prediction tasks further validates the model's utility. Most importantly, the large-scale experiments effectively substantiate the central claims about scalability and convergence speed, showing convergence in just a few passes over the data.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is clearly written and well-structured. It does an excellent job of motivating the problem, situating the work within the context of prior art (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM), and clearly explaining each component of its contribution. The three guiding principles laid out in the introduction provide a strong narrative frame for the paper.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Justification of Modeling Assumptions:** The parsimony of the PTM model comes from strong parameter-sharing assumptions (e.g., all distinct role-triplets (x,y,z) share a single parameter B0). While this is key to scalability, the paper justifies it primarily on computational grounds. A deeper discussion of the statistical implications would be beneficial. When might these assumptions be too restrictive? For instance, in networks with highly asymmetric or specialized inter-community interactions, forcing all distinct-role interactions to be governed by a single parameter might obscure important structural patterns. Adding a paragraph discussing the limitations of this assumption would make the paper more balanced.

2.  **Limitations of the Triangular Representation:** The paper builds upon the bag-of-triangles representation from [8] and inherits its limitations. It is acknowledged that this representation discards information about isolated edges and cannot model networks with few triangles (e.g., bipartite or tree-like graphs). While this is not a flaw of the core contribution (the PTM itself), these limitations should be stated more prominently for the reader, perhaps in the introduction or conclusion, to clearly define the scope of the method's applicability.

3.  **Details of the O(K) Local Update Approximation:** The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local update is a crucial algorithmic component for achieving scalability. The paper briefly explains that re-sampling the active set of configurations `A` at each step avoids bias. However, this could be elaborated upon. Does this approximation impact the quality of the final solution (e.g., the achieved ELBO) or the variance of the stochastic gradients? A small experiment on a toy problem comparing the full O(K^3) local update to the O(K) approximation in terms of convergence and final objective value would provide stronger evidence for its effectiveness and minimal impact on quality.

4.  **Broader Context of Network Embedding:** The comparison is focused on probabilistic latent space models like MMSB and MMTM. However, the field of large-scale network analysis also includes numerous non-probabilistic embedding methods (e.g., node2vec, DeepWalk, GNN-based approaches). While a direct empirical comparison is not necessary as the goals are different (probabilistic inference vs. feature learning), a brief discussion positioning PTM relative to this broader class of highly scalable methods would enrich the paper's contribution and help readers from different sub-fields understand its place in the literature.

### Decision

**Accept.**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to scalable probabilistic network modeling. The proposed PTM model and its associated SVI inference algorithm are novel, technically sound, and demonstrate impressive empirical results. The paper convincingly shows that its approach is orders of magnitude faster than relevant prior work while maintaining or improving accuracy. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed with additional discussion and clarification, but they do not detract from the core strength and impact of the work. This paper presents a valuable tool for researchers and practitioners working with large-scale networks and is a clear advance for the field.
