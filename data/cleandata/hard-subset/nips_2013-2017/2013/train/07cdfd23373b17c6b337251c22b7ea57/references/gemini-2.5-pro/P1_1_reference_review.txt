**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling probabilistic latent space models to large-scale networks with millions of nodes and many latent roles. The authors argue that previous methods fail because they do not simultaneously satisfy three key principles: succinct network representation, parsimonious statistical modeling, and scalable inference.

Building on the "bag-of-triangles" representation from prior work (MMTM [8]), which satisfies the first principle, this paper introduces two main contributions to address the other two. First, they propose the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a new latent space model that dramatically reduces the parameter complexity of its predecessor, MMTM. Where MMTM required an O(K^3) tensor to parameterize the generation of triangles from K latent roles, PTM uses parameter sharing to reduce this to O(K) parameters by grouping role combinations into three categories: all roles identical, two roles identical, or all roles distinct. This idea is analogous to the simplification that a-MMSB [5] brought to MMSB [1].

Second, the authors develop a highly efficient stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm for the PTM. To avoid a potential O(K^3) bottleneck in the local update step for each triangle, they introduce a clever "mixture-of-deltas" approximation that reduces the complexity of this step to O(K) by considering only a small, randomly sampled subset of the possible role combinations. Combined with the parsimonious model, this results in an overall inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity per pass, which is amenable to parallelization.

The authors provide extensive experimental validation on both synthetic and large-scale real-world networks. Their method demonstrates superior latent space recovery and orders-of-magnitude faster runtimes compared to MMTM and MMSB. Notably, they show their algorithm can infer a 100-role model on a 1.1M-node network in just 4 hours on a single multi-core machine, a significant improvement over previously reported state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed model is well-motivated, and the inference algorithm is sound. The claims of scalability and accuracy are strongly supported by a thorough experimental evaluation.
- The use of a structured mean-field approximation that couples the latent roles within a triangle is a good design choice, and the authors correctly note that a fully-factorized approximation performs poorly.
- The experiments are comprehensive, comparing against relevant baselines (MMTM, MMSB) on both synthetic and real data across multiple tasks (latent space recovery, link prediction, convergence). The direct comparison of runtimes against published results for a-MMSB and MMTM is particularly compelling and effectively highlights the practical impact of the work.
- One minor weakness is the reliance on a seeded initialization for the synthetic experiments. The authors state that 2% of nodes were initialized with one of their true roles. While this provides a fair comparison ground for the different algorithms, it is an optimistic scenario. The paper would be strengthened by showing results using the unseeded initialization strategy mentioned in footnote 3 to demonstrate robustness.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
- The introduction effectively frames the problem using the "three principles" of scalability, which provides a clear roadmap for the paper's contributions.
- The descriptions of the triangular representation, the PTM model, and the SVI algorithm are logical and easy to follow. The distinction between PTM and its predecessor MMTM is made very clear.
- The experimental setup and results are presented lucidly. The figures and tables are informative and support the main claims of the paper.
- A small point for improvement: the description of the O(K) approximation for the local update could be slightly more precise. The text states "we generate A by picking 3K combinations of the form (a, a, b), (a, b, a) or (a, a, b)...". The repetition of "(a, a, b)" is likely a typo. Clarifying exactly how the O(K) off-diagonal combinations are sampled would improve reproducibility.

**Originality:** The work presents a novel and effective combination of existing ideas, tailored to solve a difficult problem.
- While the triangular representation [8], parsimonious parameter sharing (cf. a-MMSB [5]), and stochastic variational inference [10] are not new in isolation, their synthesis is. Applying parameter sharing to a higher-order (triangle-based) model is non-trivial, and the development of the O(K) approximate local update is a key, novel algorithmic contribution that makes the entire framework scalable.
- This work is a clear and significant advance over previous contributions like MMTM [8] and a-MMSB [5], differentiating itself by achieving scalability in both N (number of nodes) and K (number of roles) for a model based on higher-order network structures.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high.
- The problem of analyzing massive networks is of paramount importance in machine learning and applied sciences. This paper provides a practical, principled, and highly scalable tool for this task.
- The demonstrated ability to analyze million-node networks with hundreds of roles in hours on a single machine is a substantial leap forward and makes sophisticated probabilistic modeling accessible for very large datasets.
- The results, showing that a triangle-based model can outperform an edge-based model (MMSB) on link prediction for social networks, also reinforce the value of modeling higher-order network structures. Researchers and practitioners working with large-scale network data are highly likely to use and build upon these ideas.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Significant practical impact:** The paper delivers a method that is orders of magnitude faster than previous state-of-the-art approaches, enabling latent space analysis on a scale that was previously intractable.
2.  **Strong experimental validation:** The claims are backed by thorough experiments on both synthetic and large real-world networks, with clear and impressive results.
3.  **Novel contributions:** The combination of a parsimonious triangle-based model with a bespoke, scalable SVI algorithm (featuring a novel O(K) local update) is a solid research contribution.
4.  **High clarity:** The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and easy to understand, making the contributions accessible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Optimistic initialization:** The main experiments rely on a seeded initialization, which may not reflect real-world performance where no ground truth is available. Adding results with an unseeded strategy would make the evaluation more robust.
2.  **Minor lack of detail:** The description of the sampling strategy for the O(K) local update approximation could be more precise for full reproducibility.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It presents a significant advance in the field of large-scale network modeling. I strongly recommend acceptance.
