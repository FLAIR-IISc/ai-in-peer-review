Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic latent space model for large networks that leverages a bag-of-triangles representation. The key modeling contribution is the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which shares parameters across groups of triangle role configurations so that the number of triangle-generating parameters scales as O(K) instead of O(K^3) (as in MMTM). On the inference side, the paper develops a structured mean-field stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm that uses mini-batches of triangles and natural gradients, with an O(NK) global update and an O(K) approximate local update via a “mixture-of-deltas” variational family. Experiments show improved scalability and competitive or better accuracy on synthetic latent-space recovery and link prediction on real networks, with a reported 1M-node, 100-role run completing in ~4 hours on one 8-core machine. The work builds on and contrasts with MMSB and its scalable variants (e.g., a-MMSB), ERGM ideas, and the MMTM that uses triangle motifs but suffers from O(K^3) complexity.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The modeling idea of parameter sharing by partitioning role-triplet configurations is sensible and clearly reduces complexity. The structured mean-field approximation for per-triangle role variables is well motivated; naive mean-field would indeed collapse high-rank posteriors. The SVI formulation and the use of natural gradients follow standard practice. The “mixture-of-deltas” local variational approximation to achieve O(K) per triangle is plausible and computationally attractive; the claim that any restricted variational family yields a valid lower bound is correct, though the practical bias-variance tradeoff of re-sampling the off-diagonal set A would benefit from deeper analysis.
- Empirical support: The synthetic latent space recovery experiments are thorough in varying N and K, and the results support the main claims about accuracy and speed. Real-data link prediction results suggest advantages over MMSB on larger social networks. The scalability results (e.g., 1M-node YouTube) are compelling. However, several comparisons and ablations are missing (see weaknesses).
- Issues/concerns:
  - Step-size schedule: There is an apparent inconsistency. The paper states ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ with convergence conditions requiring κ∈(0.5,1], but in the experimental setup, κ is set to −0.5. If taken literally, this leads to increasing step sizes, violating the convergence conditions. This is likely a sign error, but should be corrected and clarified.
  - The algorithm is not a generative model for full graphs; the paper is transparent about this, but it means certain network statistics (degree distribution, empty/single-edge triples) are not modeled, which may limit applicability.
  - The mixture-of-deltas approximation is heuristic. While it works well empirically, there is no ablation comparing it to the exact O(K^3) local update on small K to quantify the approximation loss, nor an analysis of how the choice and size of A affects accuracy and convergence.
  - Fairness and breadth of comparisons: The experiments omit a direct comparison to a-MMSB or other scalable probabilistic models on the same hardware/datasets. Also absent are strong nonprobabilistic baselines (e.g., BigCLAM, overlapping community detection methods) and modern embedding-based link predictors; this weakens the significance claims for link prediction.
  - Some evaluation choices could bias results: e.g., seeding 2% of nodes with ground-truth roles during initialization; the NMI threshold at 0.125 is somewhat arbitrary and may interact with sparsity of θ.
  - Triangle extraction and δ-subsampling: While stated to be cheap, practical extraction costs, memory usage, and sensitivity to δ are not quantified; ablations would help.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized, with a clear statement of the three design principles, the PTM generative process, the variational factorization, and the SVI algorithm. The motivations for using triangles and structured variational factors are clear.
- Some details are deferred to the appendix (e.g., exact local updates for all configuration cases, natural gradient sufficient statistics). For reproducibility, more explicit equations in the main text would be helpful.
- Minor issues: the κ sign inconsistency (above); references are sparse/incomplete in the provided text; a brief complexity and memory breakdown (constants, cost of triangle extraction) would improve clarity.

Originality:
- The parameter-sharing scheme over triangle role configurations that reduces O(K^3) to O(K) is a novel and impactful idea in this context, analogous to a-MMSB’s inter-role parameter tying but extended to triangle motifs.
- The structured SVI with mixture-of-deltas for triadic latent roles is a nontrivial, practical contribution.
- The work is a thoughtful synthesis of triangular representations and modern scalable variational inference; while components build on known techniques, the combination and engineering for large-scale networks appear original and useful.

Significance:
- If broadly validated, enabling O(NK) scalable probabilistic latent space inference for million-node networks with hundreds of roles is significant. Many practitioners would value such a tool for community detection, role discovery, and link prediction.
- The empirical results suggest substantial speedups over prior probabilistic models and competitive accuracy. However, the missing comparisons to a-MMSB and modern scalable baselines temper the strength of the state-of-the-art claims, especially for link prediction.

Pros:
- Clear and principled reduction of parameter dimensionality from O(K^3) to O(K).
- Well-motivated structured SVI; practical O(K) local updates; parallelizable and memory-conscious.
- Strong scalability demonstrated; convincing wall-clock results on large networks.
- Transparent about modeling assumptions and limitations.

Cons:
- Apparent bug/inconsistency in step-size exponent κ; needs correction.
- Limited baseline coverage (no a-MMSB comparison; no nonprobabilistic/embedding baselines).
- Heuristic local approximation lacks ablations and theoretical guidance on accuracy.
- Evaluation choices (seeding, thresholding) could bias results; sensitivity analyses missing.
- Not a full generative model of graphs; may underperform in settings where triangle information is sparse or misleading.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Fix and clarify the step-size schedule; report sensitivity to τ0, τ1, κ.
- Add ablations: mixture-of-deltas vs exact local updates (small K); effect of |A|; δ-subsampling sensitivity; structured vs naive mean-field.
- Include comparisons with a-MMSB under comparable conditions and with strong nonprobabilistic baselines (e.g., BigCLAM, node2vec/DeepWalk) for link prediction.
- Report resource usage (time for triangle extraction, memory), and discuss when triangle modeling may fail (e.g., very sparse, low-clustering networks).
- Consider releasing code to aid reproducibility.

Overall recommendation:
This is a strong and timely paper with a clear modeling idea and a well-engineered scalable inference algorithm. Despite some missing comparisons and a few issues to fix, the contribution is significant for NeurIPS. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on addressing the step-size issue and expanding empirical comparisons/ablations.
