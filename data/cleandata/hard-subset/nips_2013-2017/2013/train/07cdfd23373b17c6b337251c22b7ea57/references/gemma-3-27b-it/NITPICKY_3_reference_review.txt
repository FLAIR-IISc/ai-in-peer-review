This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, building on the bag-of-triangles representation. The core contribution lies in the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) which reduces the parameter space from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K), coupled with a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for inference with complexity O(NK). The authors demonstrate promising scalability, achieving inference on a 1M-node YouTube network in 4 hours.

While the motivation for scalability is compelling and the proposed PTM offers a significant reduction in parameter complexity, numerous details and inconsistencies detract from the overall quality of the work. The paper suffers from a pervasive lack of rigor and careful presentation, even extending to formatting errors. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Scalability Focus:** Addressing the scalability bottleneck in latent space network inference is a crucial problem, and the authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods.
*   **PTM Parameter Reduction:** The reduction of the parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K) is a substantial theoretical improvement.
*   **Empirical Results:** Demonstrating inference on a 1M-node network, even with a single machine, is a positive indicator.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Algorithm Description:** The stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm is described at a high level. Crucial implementation details like the specific form of the Fisher information matrix used in the natural gradient calculation, and the adaptive step size schedule parameters (τ0, τ1, κ) are only mentioned briefly and lack justification. The use of a "mixture-of-deltas" approximation to simplify the local update is presented without a clear analysis of its bias or approximation error.
*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** While results are presented, the experimental setup lacks crucial detail. The specific hyperparameters used for each experiment (e.g., α, λ, minibatch size, step size) are not consistently reported, making reproducibility difficult. The δ-subsampling parameter is stated to be set to be larger than the average degree, but the average degrees of the tested networks aren’t reported, hindering proper evaluation of this crucial parameter.
*   **Overstated Claims & Missing Baselines:** The claim of “orders of magnitude faster” compared to [5, 8] requires more careful justification. The runtime comparison lacks detail about hardware specifications and software optimization. Crucially, a direct comparison with optimized implementations of [5, 8] (if available) is missing.
*   **Inconsistent Notation & Formatting:** Throughout the paper, there are inconsistencies in notation (e.g., conflicting definitions of variables) and formatting (e.g., improper equation alignment, missing punctuation). This detracts from readability and suggests a lack of careful editing. For example, the definition of I changes within the same section.
*   **Weak Discussion of Related Work:** While related work is mentioned, the discussion lacks depth. The paper doesn’t adequately contextualize the PTM within the broader landscape of latent space network models, nor does it explore potential connections to other parameter sharing techniques.
*   **Ambiguous Generative Process:** The description of the PTM's generative process mentions it’s “not a generative model *per se*”, but doesn't clearly articulate the implications of this. The statement that the generative process “does not force overlapping triangles to have consistent edge values” is unclear and raises questions about the validity of the model.
*  **Incomplete tables and references:** Table 1 is referred to in the text but appears to be missing. Some references are also not complete, lacking issue numbers or page numbers.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The PTM offers a significant theoretical reduction in parameter complexity, potentially enabling scalable inference.
*   The empirical results demonstrate the feasibility of applying the algorithm to a large real-world network.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of rigor in the algorithm description and experimental setup makes it difficult to assess the validity of the claims.
*   The numerous inconsistencies in notation and formatting suggest a lack of attention to detail.
*   The overstated claims and missing baselines undermine the credibility of the results.
*   The incomplete tables and references.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the promising theoretical contribution, the numerous flaws in presentation, detail, and rigor outweigh the strengths. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the standards of a NIPS publication. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above through a thorough revision, providing a more rigorous and well-documented presentation of their work. Specifically, they should: 1) provide a detailed description of the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm, including the Fisher information matrix and step size schedule; 2) provide a comprehensive experimental setup with complete hyperparameter settings and runtime comparisons; 3) address the inconsistencies in notation and formatting; and 4) provide a more thorough discussion of related work.
