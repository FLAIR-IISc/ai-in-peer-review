Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic latent space model and inference algorithm for large networks using a bag-of-triangles representation. Building on prior work that models networks via triangular motifs (MMTM [8]) and mixed-membership stochastic blockmodels (MMSB [1], a-MMSB [5]), the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) (in MMTM) to O(K) by sharing parameters across groups of role configurations: same-role triplets use Bxxx, two-equal/one-different use Bxx (shared over the minority role), and all-different roles use a single B0. This parsimony enables an efficient stochastic variational inference (SVI) procedure with structured mean-field that models the per-triangle role triplet jointly. To avoid O(K^3) local updates, the authors approximate the local posterior via a “mixture-of-deltas” defined over K diagonal and O(K) randomly chosen off-diagonal triplets, yielding O(K) local complexity. Global updates use stochastic natural gradients with mini-batches of triangles; because the number of triangle parameters is O(K), global updates are O(NK). The approach is evaluated on synthetic data (latent role recovery) and large real networks (heldout link prediction and variational bounds), showing improved scalability over MMSB batch variational and MMTM/PTM Gibbs, and practical convergence on a 1M-node network with K=100 in hours using 8 threads.

Relative to previous work, the paper combines: (i) the triangle-based representation from [8], (ii) parameter-sharing ideas reminiscent of a-MMSB’s treatment of inter-role parameters [5], and (iii) SVI with structured variational families [10,22]. The main novelty lies in the O(K) parameterization for triangles and the O(K) local update via a random subset of off-diagonals.

Strengths
- Addresses a clear scalability bottleneck: reduces triangle parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) and local inference from O(K^3) to O(K), enabling million-node, hundred-role runs.
- Uses a structured mean-field approximation that better captures dependencies among per-triangle role assignments than naive mean-field.
- Sound exploitation of SVI and natural gradients; mini-batching over triangles yields unbiased gradients for the stated objective (given the chosen variational family).
- Empirical results show strong speedups versus MMSB and MMTM/PTM Gibbs; convergence in 2–5 passes is compelling, and link prediction performance is competitive on larger social networks.
- Practical engineering considerations (triangle subsampling, discarding local variables to save memory, multi-threading) are well-motivated.

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality (technical soundness)
- Step-size inconsistency: The SVI step size is defined as ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)^(-κ) with conditions ∑t ρt = ∞ and ∑t ρt^2 < ∞ (standard Robbins–Monro). However, the experiments section specifies κ = −0.5, which violates these conditions (steps would grow with t). This appears to be a typo; please correct and clarify the actual κ used (typically κ ∈ (0.5, 1]).
- Approximation via random subset A: The “mixture-of-deltas” restriction yields a valid lower bound for a reduced variational family. However, unbiasedness claims for the gradient should be stated with respect to this restricted objective (and include expectation over the randomness in A if reused). A brief discussion of the variance/bias trade-off and sensitivity to the size and resampling scheme of A would strengthen the argument.
- Expressivity trade-off: Sharing Bxx across all minority roles and using a single B0 for all-all-different configurations removes inter-role heterogeneity present in MMTM. This may limit interpretability and performance when specific triadic patterns among distinct roles are important. An ablation comparing O(K), O(K^2) (e.g., partial untied parameters), and O(K^3) variants would help quantify the impact.
- Triangle subsampling: While δ-subsampling is argued to approximately preserve open/closed triangle distributions [8], the induced bias for posterior estimates and downstream tasks (e.g., link prediction) is not characterized here. A sensitivity analysis over δ and degree distributions would be informative.
- Link prediction methodology: Ranking by triangle-induced edge probability requires marginalizing over the third node k. The computational cost and any approximations used for this marginalization (especially in large graphs) should be detailed.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized. However, key elements are deferred to the Appendix (e.g., full local update equations for φxxx and φxxy, natural gradient forms for η and γ, details of the power-law simulator). Including at least the core updates in the main text would improve reproducibility.
- Table 1 is referenced but not provided; the conditional probabilities of Eijk under different role configurations are central to understanding PTM.
- Some reference entries are incomplete; please ensure proper bibliographic details.
- Complexity statements could be clearer about the overall dependence on the number of triangles m ≈ O(Nδ^2) after subsampling. Present end-to-end costs per pass in terms of N, K, and δ.

Originality
- The O(K) parameter sharing for triangle configurations and the O(K) local update via random off-diagonals appear novel within triangle-based latent space models. The structured SVI itself is standard, but the particular combination and engineering for triangles is new relative to [8] and [5].
- That said, the work is an incremental advance over [8] and [5] rather than a wholly new modeling paradigm.

Significance
- If broadly adopted, the method could enable probabilistic role inference on networks that were previously out of reach for triangle-based models, a meaningful practical contribution.
- The empirical comparison set is limited on real data: MMSB is a reasonable baseline, but additional strong large-scale baselines (e.g., scalable embeddings or heuristic link predictors) would better contextualize performance. Including simple baselines (common neighbors, Adamic–Adar) and modern scalable embeddings would clarify gains due to modeling triangles.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong scalability advances with principled variational inference.
- Clear improvement over prior triangle-based models in both K and N scaling.
- Solid empirical evidence of speed and reasonable accuracy; convergence behavior is convincing.
- Methodologically clean and likely to be useful for practitioners needing probabilistic mixed-membership at scale.

Cons for acceptance
- A few technical gaps (step-size typo; limited analysis of approximation error and subsampling bias).
- Reduced expressivity from parameter sharing is not empirically ablated.
- Real-data baselines are narrow; broader comparisons would strengthen claims of significance.
- Some critical details are relegated to the Appendix; Table 1 missing in the main text.

Suggestions and questions for the authors
- Correct and clarify the step-size schedule (κ range, τ0, τ1), and report sensitivity.
- Provide ablations: effect of A’s size/resampling; varying degrees of parameter tying; δ sensitivity.
- Expand link prediction baselines and report computational costs for ranking.
- Include main update equations and Table 1 in the paper; ensure references are complete.
- Discuss applicability limits (e.g., bipartite or disassortative graphs where triangles are sparse).

Overall, the paper makes a practical and methodologically sound contribution to scalable latent space inference via triangles, with clear advances in efficiency. Addressing the noted issues would considerably strengthen the submission.
