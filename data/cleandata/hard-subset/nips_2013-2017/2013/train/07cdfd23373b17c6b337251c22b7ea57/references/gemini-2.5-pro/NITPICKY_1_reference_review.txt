### Summary

This paper presents a scalable probabilistic model for latent space inference in large networks, named the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). The work identifies three principles for scalability: succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference. Building upon the "bag-of-triangles" network representation from prior work (MMTM), the authors introduce a novel parsimonious parameterization for the generative model of these triangles. Where the MMTM required O(K³) parameters for K latent roles, the PTM reduces this to O(K) by sharing parameters for role configurations based on the number of distinct roles in a given triple. To perform inference, the authors develop a stochastic natural gradient variational inference algorithm. A key component of this algorithm is a structured mean-field approximation for the local latent variables and a further O(K) approximation for the local update step. The experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method is orders of magnitude faster than previous approaches like MMTM and MMSB, enabling inference on million-node networks in hours, while achieving competitive or improved accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The paper tackles the critical and challenging problem of scaling probabilistic network models. The achieved performance, particularly inferring 100 roles on a 1M-node network in 4 hours on a single machine, represents a substantial practical advance and significantly pushes the state-of-the-art in terms of scalability.
2.  **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The work effectively combines several powerful ideas: the triangular representation for computational efficiency, a parameter-sharing scheme inspired by models like a-MMSB but applied to a higher-order model, and a carefully designed stochastic variational inference procedure. The synthesis of these components is non-trivial and well-motivated.
3.  **Parsimonious Modeling:** The core modeling contribution—the O(K) parameterization of the triangle-generating distribution—is a clever and effective way to control model complexity, which appears to be crucial for both statistical and computational efficiency as the number of roles (K) grows.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising direction and impressive empirical speedups, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological gaps, presentation issues, and experimental design flaws that undermine the work's quality and reproducibility.

**1. Clarity and Presentation:**

*   **Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of care in its preparation. The reference list contains formatting errors (e.g., "Journal of Machine Learning Research,, , ,"). The use of footnotes for crucial details (e.g., footnote 1's dismissal of naive mean-field, footnote 3's alternative initialization) is inappropriate; this information should be integrated into the main text and properly substantiated.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The paper's claims regarding time complexity are confusing and at times inconsistent. The abstract and introduction claim O(NK) complexity, but Section 5.2 states the algorithm has O(Nδ²K) runtime, which leads to an O(N²) conclusion for their specific synthetic data setup (where K ∝ N). The O(NK) claim is misleading as it omits the crucial dependency on the number of sampled triangles per node, which is O(δ²). A more precise and clear analysis is needed.
*   **Undefined Terms:** The term "data pass" (footnote 5) is central to the convergence plots but is defined obscurely. A clear definition in the main text (e.g., one pass corresponds to processing a number of mini-batches equivalent to the total number of triangles in the dataset) would improve clarity.

**2. Methodological Concerns:**

*   **O(K) Local Update Approximation:** The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local update is a key algorithmic speedup, reducing the complexity from O(K³) to O(K) per triangle. However, the claim that it "works as well as the full parameterization" is presented without any empirical evidence. An ablation study comparing the O(K³) and O(K) updates (on a smaller dataset where the former is feasible) is essential to validate this critical approximation. The paper does not analyze the impact of the added variance from this sampling step on convergence.
*   **Model Assumptions:** The parsimonious parameterization (Bxxx, Bxx, B0) makes very strong assumptions about role interactions—namely, that all minority roles are interchangeable and all distinct-role triples behave identically. While this leads to scalability, the paper fails to discuss the modeling trade-offs or the potential limitations of these assumptions. In what scenarios might this simplification fail to capture important network structure?
*   **Conflation of Model and Inference:** The experiments in Section 5.2 show that the "PTM collapsed blocked Gibbs sampler" performs poorly, while the proposed stochastic VI algorithm performs well. The paper then attributes the success to the "parsimonious O(K) parameter structure". This is a contradiction. Both algorithms use the same PTM model. The results strongly suggest that the success is due to the *inference algorithm*, not just the model's parsimony. The paper's narrative oversimplifies this crucial distinction.

**3. Experimental Evaluation:**

*   **Supervised Initialization:** The synthetic experiments rely on providing the algorithm with 2 correctly-labeled nodes for each true latent role. This is a form of semi-supervision that makes the recovery task significantly easier and may not reflect real-world performance. The paper suggests an unsupervised initialization heuristic in a footnote but provides no results using it. The main claims about latent space recovery should be demonstrated in a fully unsupervised setting.
*   **Arbitrary Evaluation Threshold:** The NMI evaluation for latent space recovery depends on thresholding the membership vectors θ̂i at 0.125. This value seems arbitrary and is justified only by the number of roles in the synthetic ground truth. The sensitivity of the results to this hyperparameter is not explored.
*   **Lack of Direct Baselines:** The runtime comparison to `a-MMSB` is anecdotal, citing numbers from the original paper rather than running a controlled experiment on the same hardware. This makes a direct, fair comparison impossible. While the speedup is likely still significant, a rigorous empirical comparison is expected.
*   **Incomplete Link Prediction Analysis:** The link prediction method is described as marginalizing over all possible third nodes 'k'. For a large network, this seems computationally prohibitive. The paper provides no analysis of the complexity of this prediction step, which is a critical detail for a paper focused on scalability.

### Recommendation: Reject

This paper addresses an important problem and presents a method with impressive performance in terms of raw speed. The high-level ideas are promising. However, the work in its current form falls short of the standards for publication at NIPS. The manuscript suffers from a lack of rigor in its methodological descriptions, is not self-contained, and contains significant flaws in its experimental design and reporting. The claims are often not sufficiently supported by evidence (e.g., the O(K) approximation efficacy, the performance with unsupervised initialization). The numerous minor issues in presentation and clarity, combined with the more substantial methodological and experimental gaps, lead me to recommend rejection. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution, but it requires a major revision to address these concerns thoroughly.
