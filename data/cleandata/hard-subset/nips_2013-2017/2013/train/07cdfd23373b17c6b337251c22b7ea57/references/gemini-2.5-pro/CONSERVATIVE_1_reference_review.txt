**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks. The authors identify three key principles for scalability: succinct data representation, parsimonious statistical modeling, and efficient parallel inference. Building on prior work (MMTM [8]) that uses a "bag-of-triangles" representation, they propose a new model, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). The main novelty of PTM is its parameter efficiency: it reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K³) in MMTM to O(K) by enforcing a parameter sharing scheme based on the configuration of latent roles within a triangle. To perform inference, they develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm. A key component of their algorithm is a novel O(K) approximation for the local variational update step, which avoids an O(K³) bottleneck. The authors present experimental results on synthetic and large real-world networks (up to 1.1M nodes), demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups over previous methods like MMTM and MMSB, while achieving comparable or better accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The primary strength of this work is its demonstrated ability to scale to very large networks. The reduction of both parameter complexity (to O(K)) and inference complexity (to O(NK)) is a significant engineering achievement. The empirical results, such as inferring 100 roles on a 1M-node network in 4 hours on a single machine, represent a substantial advance over the state-of-the-art runtimes reported for comparable methods (e.g., a-MMSB [5], MMTM [8]), which require days for smaller problems.

2.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches and systematically designs a solution that addresses each bottleneck. The focus on reducing both data representation size and model parameterization is sound.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method shows impressive empirical results. It converges very quickly (typically in 2-5 data passes) and achieves high accuracy in recovering latent community structure on synthetic datasets, even outperforming established baselines like MMSB on larger, more complex problems. The link prediction results are also competitive, suggesting the model captures useful network structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on a Niche Network Representation:** The entire method is built upon the triangular representation of networks. This is a strong and limiting choice. This representation discards all information about edges that do not participate in triangles, as well as the distinction between empty and single-edge triples. While the authors cite prior work [8] suggesting this is acceptable, this fundamental modeling choice lacks broader validation. The paper does not discuss the classes of networks for which this representation would be inappropriate (e.g., sparse networks, bipartite graphs, or networks with low clustering coefficients), which is a significant omission. This approach seems tailored for networks rich in triadic closures and may not be generally applicable.

2.  **Ad-Hoc Model Parsimony:** The central modeling contribution, the PTM, achieves its O(K) parameterization through a rigid and heuristically motivated parameter-sharing scheme. The decision to group all role triplets into three classes (all-same, two-same, all-different) seems driven entirely by computational convenience rather than any sociological or network-theoretic principle. Such a strong assumption could severely limit the model's expressiveness and its ability to capture complex, asymmetric interactions between latent communities. The paper does not provide an ablation study or analysis to justify this specific structure over other possible parsimonious designs.

3.  **Unanalyzed Inference Approximation:** A key contributor to the algorithm's speed is the O(K) approximation to the local update, where the variational posterior is restricted to a small, randomly sampled subset of O(K) role configurations. This is a novel but unanalyzed heuristic. The authors claim it "works as well as the full parameterization" but provide no empirical evidence (e.g., a comparison on a smaller problem where the O(K³) update is feasible) to support this. The effect of this stochastic approximation on the quality and variance of the final solution is not discussed, making it difficult to assess its soundness. Standard variational inference provides a lower bound, but the quality of that bound depends on the richness of the variational family; this approximation arbitrarily curtails that family at each step.

4.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the runtimes are compared to those reported for a-MMSB [5], a direct experimental comparison on accuracy and objective function value is missing. The a-MMSB is an edge-based model and represents a more established approach to scalable inference. Comparing against the batch version of MMSB and Gibbs samplers, which are known to be slow or have mixing issues, sets a relatively low bar for demonstrating superiority. A more thorough comparison against the most relevant scalable competitor (a-MMSB) would be necessary for a convincing evaluation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   The paper delivers a genuinely massive improvement in scalability for a class of network models, which is a significant practical contribution to the field.
*   The results are impressive and demonstrate the potential of combining succinct representations with parsimonious models.
*   The work is well-written and the approach is clearly explained.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The methodological foundations are questionable. The model relies on a niche representation and a strong, unjustified parameterization scheme.
*   The key algorithmic speedup relies on a new, unproven heuristic approximation whose impact on solution quality is unevaluated.
*   The generality of the approach is limited; it is likely only effective for a specific type of dense, highly clustered network.
*   The experimental comparison against the most relevant scalable baseline (a-MMSB) is incomplete.

**Conclusion:**

This paper presents an ambitious and effective engineering solution to a very challenging problem. The scalability results are striking and undeniably a major practical contribution. However, from a methodological standpoint, the work rests on a series of strong, ad-hoc assumptions and unanalyzed approximations. The reliance on the triangular representation limits its generality, and the parsimonious model structure is not well-justified. While the empirical success is impressive, the lack of rigorous validation for these core innovations makes me hesitant to endorse the approach fully. It feels more like a collection of effective heuristics than a well-principled new model. Given the high standards of NeurIPS, the methodological weaknesses are a significant concern. I am borderline on this paper, but lean towards rejection unless the authors can provide stronger justification for their modeling choices and a more thorough analysis of their inference approximations.
