This paper presents a novel approach to scalable latent space inference in large networks, building upon the bag-of-triangles representation and introducing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) with a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm. The authors correctly identify the scalability limitations of existing probabilistic methods like MMSB and MMTM, and attempt to address these through parameter sharing and efficient inference. While the motivation is strong and the problem important, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing due to a lack of comprehensive comparison with well-established methods, reliance on synthetic data for a significant portion of the evaluation, and a somewhat optimistic presentation of results.

The core idea of parameter sharing in the PTM—reducing the parameter space from O(K<sup>3</sup>) to O(K)—is a conceptually sound and potentially impactful contribution. The proposed stochastic variational inference algorithm, with its claimed O(NK) complexity, is also promising. However, the paper lacks a thorough analysis of the trade-offs introduced by this parameter sharing. How much expressive power is lost by collapsing parameters? Is this loss of expressiveness justified by the gains in scalability? The paper asserts improved accuracy, but doesn’t rigorously demonstrate this across diverse real-world datasets.

A major weakness is the heavy reliance on synthetic data. While these experiments demonstrate the algorithm’s scalability and show promising recovery accuracy, synthetic datasets rarely capture the complexities of real-world networks. The link prediction experiments are a positive step, but the comparison is limited to MMSB, a method known to be less effective than more sophisticated alternatives. A comparison against state-of-the-art community detection and link prediction algorithms (e.g., those based on graph embeddings like node2vec or DeepWalk) would be crucial. Simply stating that triangle-based methods perform better than MMSB on larger networks isn’t sufficient; demonstrating superiority over *multiple* baselines is necessary.

The discussion of the structured mean-field approximation and the justification for avoiding a naive mean-field approach are somewhat hand-wavy. More detailed explanation and empirical validation of the structured approach are required. Similarly, the explanation of the natural gradient ascent and its advantages over standard gradient ascent feels incomplete. 

The presentation, while generally clear, suffers from occasional jargon and lacks sufficient detail in certain areas. For example, the specifics of the hyperparameter tuning (α, λ, step size parameters) are not fully explained, hindering reproducibility. The claim of convergence in 2-5 data passes seems remarkably fast, and warrants further scrutiny. The authors mention a 10% mini-batch size, but the choice of this value, and its impact on convergence and accuracy, isn’t justified.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses a critical problem: scalability of latent space inference.
*   Presents a novel parameter sharing scheme (PTM) to reduce model complexity.
*   Proposes a scalable stochastic variational inference algorithm.
*   Demonstrates reasonable performance on synthetic data and initial results on real networks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Insufficient comparison against strong baselines (beyond MMSB).
*   Overreliance on synthetic data for evaluation.
*   Lack of detailed analysis of the trade-offs introduced by parameter sharing.
*   Incomplete explanation of key algorithmic choices (structured mean-field, natural gradient).
*   Limited discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.
*   Optimistic presentation of results without sufficient rigorous validation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem is important and the proposed solution has the potential to significantly improve scalability.
*   The parameter sharing scheme is a novel and interesting idea.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comprehensive comparison with established methods weakens the claims of improved accuracy.
*   The heavy reliance on synthetic data raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.
*   The paper lacks sufficient detail and analysis to fully convince the reader of the method’s effectiveness.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the authors identify a significant problem and propose a potentially promising solution, the lack of rigorous evaluation and detailed analysis prevents me from recommending acceptance. The paper would benefit significantly from a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved, and more extensive validation on diverse real-world datasets. I encourage the authors to address these concerns and resubmit a revised version in the future.
