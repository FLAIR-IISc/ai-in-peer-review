Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks. Building on the bag-of-triangles representation (Ho et al., NIPS’11), the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which assigns each node a mixed-membership vector over K roles and models triangle motifs via role assignments of triplets. The central modeling idea is to reduce the triangle-generating parameterization from O(K^3) (as in MMTM) to O(K) by tying parameters across role-triplet configurations according to their number of distinct roles: Bxxx for all-same-role, Bxx for two-equal-one-different, and B0 for all-different, each with small Dirichlet-parameterized simplices reflecting open/closed motif probabilities. This trades expressiveness for drastic parsimony.

For inference, the paper derives a structured mean-field variational objective and uses stochastic natural gradient ascent. A key computational contribution is an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation to the per-triangle variational distribution over (si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij), which otherwise is O(K^3). Mini-batch subsampling of triangles yields an unbiased natural gradient estimate. Combined, the algorithm scales roughly linearly in N and K for the global update and in |triangles|×K for the local updates, with triangle subsampling (δ) used to handle high-degree nodes.

Empirically, on synthetic graphs generated from MMSB and a power-law latent-space process, PTM recovers mixed membership roles with high NMI and in substantially less time than MMSB variational (batch) and MMTM/PTM Gibbs samplers. On real networks (up to ~1.1M nodes, 100 roles), the method converges in hours on one multi-core machine and shows favorable link prediction against MMSB. The approach relates to MMSB/a-MMSB (Airoldi et al.; Gopalan et al.) by adopting parsimony in role interactions; to MMTM (Ho et al.) via triangular representations; and to SVI/natural gradients (Hoffman et al.; Amari).

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound variational formulation with structured factors over triplets, and correct use of mini-batch SVI with natural gradients.
- The mixture-of-deltas truncation is a pragmatic, well-motivated way to cut O(K^3) to O(K), and the parameter-tying in PTM is clearly specified.
- Complexity analysis is convincing; the implementation details (discarding local variational parameters, thread-level parallelism) make the method practical.
- Empirical results are extensive, spanning synthetic and several large real networks, with strong runtime gains and competitive or better accuracy.

Concerns/clarifications:
- Convergence/theory of the randomized truncation: The paper states that any choice of A yields a valid lower bound. However, re-sampling A at each local update changes the bound across iterations; monotonicity is not guaranteed, and the unbiasedness argument for ∇LS presumes exact local optima under a fixed variational family. Please clarify whether the gradient estimate remains unbiased for a fixed family per iteration, and whether you can bound the variance or provide references for randomized truncations in SVI.
- Step-size schedule: In Sec. 5.2, κ is set to −0.5, which contradicts the Robbins–Monro conditions stated earlier (and would increase step size with t). This seems like a typo; please correct and report the actual κ used.
- Fairness of baselines: Runtime comparisons mix single-threaded (MMTM/PTM Gibbs) vs 4-thread PTM; MMSB is batch variational rather than a-MMSB (stochastic), which is the more comparable scalable baseline. Also, δ-subsampling reduces data for PTM but not for MMSB. Please add comparisons to a-MMSB (same hardware/threads), and clarify whether δ-like reductions are permitted for baselines. For link prediction, your scoring marginalizes over third nodes (leveraging triangles); MMSB is scored by pairwise link probability—this may favor triangle-based models. Consider also reporting MMSB scores augmented with two-hop features or compare to other scalable community/role methods (e.g., BigCLAM, spectral embeddings) to strengthen claims.
- Expressiveness vs parsimony: Tying all inter-role interactions into B0 and Bxx may underfit when specific triples of roles behave differently. Please characterize when PTM underfits relative to MMTM (e.g., synthetic ablations where inter-role dependencies matter), and consider reporting accuracy vs K with MMTM-variational (if possible) or a partially untied PTM variant.
- Evaluation details: Provide sensitivity to δ and mini-batch size; impact of the number of off-diagonal tuples in A; ablation comparing full structured local updates vs mixture-of-deltas; and memory/runtime breakdown including triangle extraction cost.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable. Algorithm 1 is helpful.
- Some referenced materials are missing in the provided text (e.g., Table 1, Appendix with update equations, simulator details). Ensure these are complete. Define all notation upon first use (e.g., Bxxx,1 vs total open probability; I[·]).
- Be precise about total complexity: the local step scales as O(|S|K) per iteration with the truncation, while the global step is O(NK); earlier text sometimes states O(NK) overall—please reconcile.
- Clarify the link prediction protocol (how held-out edges are excluded from triangle extraction, and whether triangle features involving held-out edges leak information).

Originality
- The O(K) parameter-tying for triangle-generating probabilities is a novel idea within motif-based models and parallels a-MMSB’s parsimony for edges. The structured SVI with a randomized truncation for triadic variables is also a useful technical contribution. The combination is original and addresses a real scaling bottleneck.

Significance
- The ability to infer 100-role latent spaces on million-node graphs in hours on a single machine is impactful. Triangle-based modeling is increasingly relevant, and this work advances the practical frontier for probabilistic motif-based latent space inference. With stronger baselines and clarified theory, this could be a reference approach.

Pros
- Strong scalability with clear algorithmic innovations (O(K) parameters, O(K) local updates).
- Solid empirical performance and large-scale demonstrations.
- Practical implementation details (parallelism, memory footprint).

Cons
- Limited baseline fairness; no a-MMSB runtime/accuracy head-to-head under comparable settings.
- Theoretical justification of the randomized truncation and step-size issue need clarification.
- Potential underfitting due to aggressive parameter tying not fully explored.

Recommendation: Accept (borderline-strong). The paper presents a timely, well-engineered method with novel modeling/inference ideas and compelling scalability results. Addressing the noted clarifications and adding stronger baselines would further strengthen the contribution.

Confidence: High on technical and empirical assessment; medium on completeness pending the Appendix and missing tables.
