

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

## Summary

This paper proposes a "parsimonious triangular model" (PTM) for latent space inference in large networks, building on the triangular motif representation introduced by Ho et al. [8]. The authors claim their model reduces the parameter complexity from O(K³) in the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K), enabling scalable inference with O(NK) time complexity. They introduce a stochastic variational inference algorithm that converges in 2-5 passes on large networks. The paper claims significant speedups over prior work (e.g., 12 minutes vs. 8 days on Brightkite).

## Strengths

The paper addresses a genuine scalability challenge in network analysis, and the parameter sharing approach to reduce O(K³) to O(K) is conceptually sound. The experimental results on real-world networks demonstrate impressive speedups compared to prior work, and the theoretical foundation for the parameter reduction is reasonable. The approach is well-motivated by the need for scalable methods for million-node networks.

## Weaknesses

### Critical Technical Inconsistencies

1. **Parameter Complexity Claim**: The paper claims O(NK) time complexity, but the algorithm description (Section 4) and experimental details (Section 5.2) consistently state O(Nδ²K) complexity. Since δ is a user-specified parameter (set to 50 in experiments), this is not O(NK) in general. The authors should clarify whether they mean "O(NK) for fixed δ" or "O(Nδ²K) with δ = O(1)".

2. **Notation Inconsistency**: The paper states "B0 ∈ ∆1" (equation 3) but later uses B0,1 and B0,2 (e.g., equation 6), implying B0 ∈ ∆2. This is a serious notational error that undermines the mathematical clarity.

3. **Problematic Expectation in Equation (6)**: The local update equation (6) contains "Eq[log(B0,1/3)]" where B0,1/3 is a constant (not a random variable). This is mathematically incorrect since expectations should be taken over random variables. This appears to be a fundamental error in the variational inference derivation.

4. **Algorithm Step Size**: In Algorithm 1, the step size is written as "ρt ← τ0(τ1 + t)−κ" but the paper states "ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ" (with a missing minus sign). This is a critical implementation detail that could affect convergence.

### Experimental Concerns

1. **Synthetic Data Limitations**: The synthetic experiments only test networks up to N=10,000, which is far smaller than the "million-node" claim. The authors should test larger networks to validate their scalability claims.

2. **Lack of Convergence Proof**: The paper claims "converges after 2 passes" but provides no theoretical justification for this empirical observation. The convergence analysis is insufficient for a NIPS paper.

3. **Inconsistent K Scaling**: The paper states "K = N/100" (Section 5.1) but later says "K = O(N)" (Section 5.2), which is inconsistent. If K = N/100, then K = Θ(N), not just K = O(N).

### Minor Issues

1. **References Formatting**: Many references have incomplete journal/conference information (e.g., "Journal of Machine Learning Research,, , ,").

2. **Acknowledgments**: The AFOSR grant number "FA9550010247" appears to be incorrectly formatted (should likely be "FA9550-01-0247").

3. **Terminology**: The paper uses "latent space" to refer to mixed-membership vectors, which is inconsistent with the standard usage of "latent space" for continuous embeddings (e.g., in [9, 19]).

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a relevant problem with potentially significant practical impact, the technical inconsistencies severely undermine its scientific contribution. The O(NK) complexity claim is contradicted by the actual O(Nδ²K) complexity, and the mathematical errors in the variational inference derivation (particularly equation 6) are fundamental. These issues cannot be easily fixed in a revision without major reworking of the theoretical sections.

The paper's claims about scalability (e.g., "100-role latent space on a 1M-node Youtube social network in just 4 hours") are impressive, but the theoretical foundation is too shaky to trust these results. The experimental section lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the results (e.g., how exactly they marginalize over k in the link prediction task).

The paper does not sufficiently distinguish itself from prior work. The parameter sharing approach is similar to a-MMSB [5], and the triangular representation is from Ho et al. [8]. The contribution is incremental rather than groundbreaking.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper's technical flaws are too severe to be considered for NIPS. While the problem is important and the experimental results are promising, the theoretical foundation is compromised by multiple mathematical errors and inconsistencies that would require significant revision. The paper fails to meet the quality standards expected for NIPS, particularly in terms of technical soundness and clarity.
