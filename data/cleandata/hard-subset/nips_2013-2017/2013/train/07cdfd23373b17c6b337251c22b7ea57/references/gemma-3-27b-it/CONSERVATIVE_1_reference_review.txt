This paper addresses the important problem of scalable latent space inference for large networks. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods – namely their inability to simultaneously achieve scalability and accuracy – and propose a novel approach, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), building upon the bag-of-triangles representation. The central idea of parameter sharing within latent roles to reduce model dimensionality is a reasonable one, and the claimed O(NK) complexity is attractive. However, despite the potential, I find the presentation lacking in rigorous validation and comparative analysis, and remain unconvinced of the practical significance of the proposed method.

The introduction does a decent job of outlining the challenges and positioning the work, though it feels somewhat repetitive in highlighting the shortcomings of existing approaches. The motivation for the three principles – succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference – is clear. However, the claim that existing approaches only consider one or two of these principles feels overly simplistic. For example, a-MMSB demonstrably addresses both parsimony and scalability, even if it struggles with network size. 

The description of the PTM itself is reasonably clear, although the rationale behind the specific partitioning of latent roles feels somewhat arbitrary. Why these particular groupings? Is there a theoretical justification, or is it purely empirical? The connection to the a-MMSB parameter sharing is helpful, but a more detailed discussion of how this approach differs and improves upon it would be beneficial. The transition to the variational inference algorithm is smooth, but the explanation of the “structured mean-field” approximation is vague. The authors state it captures higher-order dependencies but don’t adequately explain *how* or *why* it outperforms a naive mean-field approach beyond a brief statement.

My primary concern lies with the experimental evaluation. The synthetic data experiments demonstrate accuracy, but the comparison is limited. While the authors show superiority over MMTM and MMSB, the runtime comparison feels unfair. The MMTM and PTM Gibbs samplers are clearly at a disadvantage being single-threaded, and the statement that they "did not even finish execution" is not a convincing demonstration of scalability – it simply highlights an implementation detail. Furthermore, the initialization strategy is critical for Gibbs sampling, and the authors acknowledge that a more generous initialization improves MMTM/PTM convergence. This raises questions about the fairness of the comparison. The heldout link prediction results are more encouraging, but the gains over MMSB are not substantial enough to justify the increased complexity of the proposed method.

The real-world network experiments are the most compelling, demonstrating that the algorithm can handle a 1M-node network. However, the claim of being “orders of magnitude faster” than previous work requires more careful scrutiny. The authors cite Gopalan et al. (a-MMSB) and Ho et al. (MMTM), but the comparison is presented in a way that favors their method. Specifically, they report convergence times for a-MMSB and MMTM *without* detailing the parameter settings or the specific hardware used for those experiments. A more apples-to-apples comparison is needed. The convergence plots (Figure 2) are helpful, but lack error bars or any indication of the variability in convergence times.

Overall, the paper presents a potentially interesting approach to scalable latent space inference. However, the lack of rigorous comparative analysis, the somewhat arbitrary design choices in the PTM, and the limited experimental validation raise significant concerns. The authors haven’t definitively demonstrated that their method offers a substantial advantage over existing techniques, especially considering the increased complexity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important problem in network analysis.
* Proposes a novel approach with attractive theoretical complexity.
* Demonstrates scalability on a large real-world network.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited and potentially unfair comparative analysis.
* Lack of theoretical justification for key design choices (e.g., parameter sharing).
* Vague explanation of the structured mean-field approximation.
* Insufficient experimental detail and variability analysis.
* The gains over existing methods are not substantial enough to justify the added complexity.



**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper needs significantly more thorough validation and comparative analysis before it can be considered a substantial contribution to the field. The authors should address the concerns raised above and provide a more convincing demonstration of the practical benefits of their approach.
