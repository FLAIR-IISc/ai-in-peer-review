Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks by combining (i) the bag-of-triangles representation (Ho et al., NIPS’12) with (ii) a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) (MMTM) to O(K), and (iii) a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients and a mixture-of-deltas local variational approximation yielding O(N δ^2 K) overall complexity (δ is a degree threshold for triangle subsampling). The key modeling idea is to share parameters across role-triplet configurations based on whether the three roles are all equal (Bxxx), have a majority role (Bxx), or are all distinct (B0). The inference uses a structured mean-field posterior over the triangle role triplet to avoid rank-1 deficiencies of naive mean-field, and a randomized O(K) approximation to the O(K^3) local updates by confining mass to the K diagonals and O(K) randomly sampled off-diagonals. Experiments on synthetic networks (MMSB-like and power-law) and several large real networks report fast convergence (2–5 passes), good latent role recovery, competitive-to-better link prediction than MMSB on larger social networks, and scalability to 1M nodes and K=100 in 4 hours on 8 threads.

This work builds directly on MMTM [Ho et al.], draws ideas from a-MMSB [Gopalan et al.] for parameter sharing, and leverages SVI/natural gradients [Hoffman et al., Amari]. It relates to ERGM/triadic closure literature through the use of triangles as informative network statistics.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The PTM is well-defined, the parameter-sharing scheme is reasonable and reduces variance/complexity, and the structured SVI derivation is standard. The mixture-of-deltas restriction is a principled variational-family reduction, preserves a valid ELBO, and empirically works with O(K) cost. The stochastic natural gradient updates and step-size conditions are appropriate. Complexity claims are consistent: global updates are O(NK) per iteration; end-to-end cost per pass is O(mK) with m≈Nδ^2 triangles after subsampling.
- A few issues need clarification/correction: equation (6) appears to contain a typo for the “all distinct x,y,z” case (it uses log θj,x and log θk,x; it should be log θj,y and log θk,z). The paper asserts “any choice of A yields a valid lower bound,” which is true, but it would help to explain whether re-sampling A across iterations preserves unbiasedness of the stochastic natural gradient and whether the ELBO is tracked despite the changing variational family. The claim that triangle extraction and δ-subsampling are O(1) per sample is terse; a sentence on the algorithmic primitive and complexity in terms of degree distribution would help reproducibility.
- Experiments are thorough on scalability and include both synthetic and real networks. However, fairness and breadth of baselines could be improved (see below).

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a good motivation for triangles and the need for O(K) parameterization. The grouping into B0/Bxx/Bxxx and the equivalence-class reasoning are intuitive. The algorithm presentation is coherent, and the practical details (discarding local vars, mini-batch SVI, step-size schedule) are helpful. Missing pieces referenced to the Appendix (local update formulas, natural gradients) are important for full reproducibility; ensure they are present and complete. Please fix the noted typo, and consider adding a short schematic showing which triangle types contribute to B0 vs Bxx vs Bxxx in each role configuration.

Originality
The combination of (i) triangle-based representation, (ii) role-configuration-based parameter sharing that collapses O(K^3) to O(K), and (iii) a structured SVI with an O(K) mixture-of-deltas local approximation appears novel and useful. While parameter sharing echoes a-MMSB and triangle modeling echoes MMTM/ERGM, the specific PTM parameterization and the scalable inference scheme are distinct contributions.

Significance
Scaling latent role inference to million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine is impactful. The proposed approach offers a pragmatic middle ground: richer than edge-only models (leveraging triadic closure) yet substantially more scalable than previous triangle-based models. The results suggest practitioners can analyze large social networks with probabilistic latent roles in hours. The ideas (parameter sharing by configuration type; mixture-of-deltas structured variational family) are likely to be reused in other higher-order motif models.

Strengths
- Strong scalability via O(K) parameterization and O(K) per-triangle local updates; parallel-friendly and memory-efficient.
- Solid use of structured SVI and natural gradients; clear argument why naive mean-field fails.
- Convincing empirical scaling (1M nodes, K=100) and fast convergence in few passes.
- Competitive or improved link prediction on larger social networks; good latent role recovery on synthetic data.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Model expressiveness: Sharing Bxx across all minority roles may underfit networks where the identity of the minority role matters. An ablation varying the degree of sharing (e.g., a small number of Bxx groups) would clarify the accuracy–complexity trade-off.
- Fairness of baselines: (a) Your method uses 4–8 threads; competitors are single-threaded. Normalize by wall-clock per core or present both. (b) Batch vs stochastic: show a stochastic variant for MMSB if possible, or at least discuss differences. (c) Using triangles benefits your method; MMSB is edge-based. Consider MMSB on a triangle-augmented feature space or compare to other scalable embedding/link-prediction methods prevalent in practice.
- Initialization: Seeding 2% of nodes with ground-truth roles benefits all methods but may not reflect real use. Please include a purely unsupervised initialization for your method (you suggest a heuristic) and report sensitivity.
- Approximation details: Provide experiments varying the size/composition of A in the mixture-of-deltas to quantify the accuracy–speed trade-off; report whether the ELBO is monotone on average despite re-sampling A.
- Evaluation breadth: Add ablations on δ, K, and step-size; report memory usage. Clarify how many passes were needed across datasets and how m scales with δ in practice.
- Not generative of full graphs: This is acknowledged; discuss implications for tasks beyond link prediction and whether a simple post-hoc edge model could be layered for completeness.

Minor comments
- Fix Eq. (6) typo; ensure Table 1 is present and consistent.
- Clarify the “O(1) per triangle sample” claim for δ-subsampling.
- State whether code/data will be released to aid reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-motivated contribution that advances scalability while retaining probabilistic interpretability.
- Novel and practical inference technique with strong empirical results and significant speedups.
- Addresses a relevant NIPS topic (probabilistic modeling, scalable inference, network analysis).

Arguments against acceptance
- Some fairness concerns in experimental comparisons; limited ablations on key approximations.
- Potential loss of expressiveness due to aggressive parameter sharing not deeply analyzed.
- Minor clarity issues and reliance on appendix for key derivations.

Recommendation
Weak accept to accept. The paper presents a substantial and useful advance in scalable latent space modeling for large networks, with thoughtful algorithmic design and compelling empirical evidence. Addressing the experimental fairness and clarity points would strengthen it further.
