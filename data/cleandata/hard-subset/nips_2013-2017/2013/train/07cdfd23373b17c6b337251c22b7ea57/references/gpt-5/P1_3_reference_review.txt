Summary
The paper tackles scalable probabilistic latent space inference for very large networks by combining (i) a succinct “bag-of-triangles” representation, (ii) a parsimonious generative model, and (iii) a stochastic, parallel variational inference algorithm. Building on prior work that uses triangular motifs rather than edges, the proposed Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) shares triangle-generating parameters across classes of role-triplet configurations—(x, x, x), (x, x, y), and (x, y, z)—reducing the parameter count from O(K^3) (as in MMTM) to O(K). The inference uses a structured mean-field posterior over per-triangle role triplets and stochastic natural gradient updates based on minibatches of triangles. To avoid O(K^3) local updates, the authors introduce a “mixture-of-deltas” approximation restricting mass to K diagonal and O(K) sampled off-diagonal role combinations, yielding O(K) per-triangle cost. The method scales effectively: on synthetic and real networks it converges in 2–5 passes, performs competitively or better than MMSB and MMTM in latent-role recovery and link prediction, and handles 1M nodes with 100 roles in hours on a single multi-core machine.

Relation to prior work
- MMSB and a-MMSB: PTM adopts the mixed-membership paradigm and shares the spirit of parameter tying across roles (as in a-MMSB’s single inter-role parameter) but extends it to triangle-based modeling.
- MMTM: PTM builds on the triangle representation but overcomes MMTM’s O(K^3) parameterization and slow Gibbs inference by parameter tying and scalable SVI with structured variational posteriors.
- SVI and natural gradients: The inference approach follows standard SVI/natural gradient methodology, adapted to the triangular setting with an efficient local update approximation.

Strengths
- Scalability: The O(K) parameterization and O(K) local updates deliver large practical speedups; the demonstrated 1M-node, 100-role experiment on a single machine is compelling.
- Modeling choice: The triangle-based representation is well motivated, captures higher-order structure routinely used in ERGM/motif literature, and has shown good empirical fidelity while being compact.
- Inference design: The structured mean-field q(si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij) avoids the rank-1 limitations of naive MF; the mixture-of-deltas local approximation is simple and effective.
- Empirical results: Broad experimental evaluation across synthetic generators and real networks; accuracy and runtime advantages over strong baselines (MMSB, MMTM) are shown; convergence in few passes is valuable in practice.
- Practicality: Clear discussion of δ-subsampling, memory footprint, parallelization, and step-size scheduling.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Expressiveness trade-off: PTM’s parameter tying (one B0 for all-distinct roles; single Bxx per majority role independent of minority) may overly restrict triangle semantics. It would help to quantify the accuracy gap (if any) vs. MMTM on small-K regimes where MMTM is feasible, and to discuss cases where expressiveness is limiting.
- Local update approximation: The mixture-of-deltas construction restricts the variational family at each iteration via a random subset A. While any fixed A induces a valid bound, optimizing with a changing A means the bound itself is randomized and not monotonically improved; more justification and an ablation on the size/choice of A would strengthen the theoretical and empirical case.
- Convergence/step size: The paper states ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ and later uses κ = −0.5, which would yield increasing steps and violates the conditions ∑ ρt^2 < ∞, ∑ ρt = ∞. This appears to be a sign error; please correct and report the actual κ used in experiments.
- Global update complexity: The claim of O(NK) per global update is plausible, but the mechanics for updating γ for nodes not in the current minibatch are not fully specified. Clarify whether γ is updated only for nodes appearing in the minibatch (with appropriate scaling), and provide complexity per pass vs. per iteration.
- Evaluation scope: Link prediction is compared only to MMSB. Given the triangle-centric modeling, a comparison to MMTM (on smaller graphs), degree-corrected SBMs, or other scalable overlapping community/embedding methods (where feasible) would provide a more complete picture. Also, more analysis of δ-subsampling sensitivity (accuracy vs. speed) would be useful.
- Reproducibility details: Some critical update equations are deferred to the appendix (e.g., φxxx and φxxy), and Table 1 is referenced but not fully included here. For a superbly written paper, all ingredients to reproduce results should be present or clearly available.

Quality
Technically sound overall, with solid design choices and a careful integration of SVI into the triangle model. The main potential gaps are the step-size typo and the limited theoretical discussion of the randomized local variational family. Empirically, the work is thorough and honest about trade-offs (e.g., not a full network generative model).

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The generative process and equivalence classes are explained well. The paper would benefit from fixing the step-size exposition, including all local update formulas and the exact natural-gradient sufficient statistics, and clarifying global update complexity. Minor edits: ensure all referenced tables/appendices are accessible and fully specify the link prediction scoring.

Originality
Moderate to strong. Triangle-based representation and SVI are known; the key novelty lies in the O(K) PTM parameter tying tailored to triangles and the O(K) local update via the mixture-of-deltas structured variational family. The combination yields a practically significant advance in scalability.

Significance
High practical impact for large-scale network latent space inference. Demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedups with competitive accuracy make this attractive for practitioners, and the ideas (parameter tying by equivalence classes, structured SVI with restricted local support) are likely to be adopted or extended.

Pros
- Elegant O(K) parameterization for triangle models with strong empirical performance
- Scalable, parallel SVI with structured variational posteriors and efficient local updates
- Convincing large-scale experiments and fast convergence in few passes
- Clear articulation of design principles and practical considerations

Cons
- Limited theoretical justification for the randomized local variational restriction
- Potential loss of expressiveness vs. MMTM not fully quantified
- Step-size/convergence typo; some implementation details deferred
- Comparisons could be broader; δ-subsampling sensitivity analysis is limited

Recommendation
Lean accept. This paper offers a well-motivated, practically important contribution that advances the state of the art in scalable probabilistic latent space modeling for networks. Addressing the noted clarifications (especially step-size and local approximation justification) would further strengthen the paper.
