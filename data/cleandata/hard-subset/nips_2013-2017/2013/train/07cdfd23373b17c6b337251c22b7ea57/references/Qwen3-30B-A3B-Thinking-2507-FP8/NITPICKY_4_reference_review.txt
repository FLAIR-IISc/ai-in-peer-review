

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for latent space inference on large networks, using a bag-of-triangles representation and a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors claim to achieve O(NK) complexity compared to O(N²K²) for MMSB and O(Nδ²K³) for MMTM, with experiments showing faster convergence on large networks.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a genuine scalability challenge in network analysis, and the triangle-based representation is well-motivated. The O(K) parameterization of the triangle-generating parameters is an interesting technical contribution, and the experimental results on synthetic data show promising accuracy improvements over existing methods.

## Critical Weaknesses

### 1. Inconsistent Complexity Claims
The paper claims O(NK) complexity throughout, but Section 5.2 states the runtime complexity as O(Nδ²K), with δ being a subsampling parameter. The paper fails to clarify that δ can be as large as O(N) in high-degree networks, making the worst-case complexity O(N³K). This undermines the scalability claim, as the authors themselves note that δ is chosen to be "larger than the average degree" to minimize data loss.

### 2. Unsubstantiated Claims About Algorithm Convergence
The paper states "our algorithm usually converges after 2 passes on each triangular motif (or up to 4-5 passes at worst)" but provides no empirical evidence beyond Figure 1, which shows NMI as a function of passes without error bars or statistical significance testing. The claim that convergence occurs in "2-5 passes" is presented as a general fact without qualification for different network structures.

### 3. Flawed Local Update Approximation
The "O(K) approximation to local update" (Section 4) is presented as a solution to the O(K³) computational cost, but the paper provides no theoretical justification for this approximation. The claim that "this approximation works as well as the full parameterization" is unsubstantiated. The authors should provide ablation studies showing the impact of this approximation on accuracy.

### 4. Inadequate Baseline Comparisons
The comparison with MMSB is problematic because MMSB is an edge-based model while PTM uses triangle-based representation. The two models make fundamentally different assumptions about network structure, making direct comparison invalid. The authors should acknowledge this limitation or conduct experiments with a fairer comparison (e.g., comparing triangle-based models to other triangle-based models).

### 5. Missing Critical Experimental Details
The paper claims "NMI close to 1" for their method but provides no actual NMI values or error margins. The synthetic data generation in Section 5.1 is insufficiently detailed (e.g., no description of the "Power-Law model" used to generate networks), making reproducibility difficult. The statement that "MMTM Gibbs converges to and becomes stuck in local minima" lacks statistical evidence.

### 6. Formatting and Notational Inconsistencies
- Inconsistent use of "NIPS" vs "NeurIPS" throughout the text
- Inconsistent notation for simplex notation (∆K-1 vs ∆K)
- In Section 3, Bxxx ∈ ∆1 is inconsistent with later use of Bxxx,1 and Bxxx,2
- Equation (1) has inconsistent subscript/superscript notation (φ_{ijk}^{xyz} vs q_{ijk}(x,y,z))

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses an important problem and contains some interesting technical insights, the numerous technical inconsistencies, unsubstantiated claims, and methodological flaws undermine the paper's credibility. The complexity claims are misleading due to inconsistent treatment of the δ parameter, the convergence claims are unsupported by proper empirical evidence, and the baseline comparisons are unfair. The paper's technical contributions are overshadowed by these fundamental issues.

The authors have made an effort to address scalability, but the paper fails to meet the high standards of a NeurIPS publication due to insufficient methodological rigor and poor experimental validation. The claims of "orders of magnitude" faster convergence compared to prior work are not properly substantiated with statistical evidence or clear complexity analysis.

## Recommendation

Reject. The paper's technical flaws and unsubstantiated claims significantly outweigh its potential contributions. While the problem is important and the general approach is interesting, the lack of rigorous experimental validation and inconsistent technical claims make this paper unsuitable for publication at NeurIPS.
