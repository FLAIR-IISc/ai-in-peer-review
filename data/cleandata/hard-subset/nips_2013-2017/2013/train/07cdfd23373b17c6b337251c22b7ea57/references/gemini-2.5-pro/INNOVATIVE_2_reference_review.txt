### Summary
This paper addresses the critical challenge of scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks. The authors identify three key principles for success: a succinct network representation, a parsimonious statistical model, and a scalable inference algorithm. Building on prior work that used a "bag-of-triangles" representation (MMTM), which scales in the number of nodes (N) but not in the number of latent roles (K), this paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). The core innovation of PTM is a drastic reduction in the model's parameter complexity from O(K^3) in MMTM to O(K) by introducing a parameter-sharing scheme inspired by the a-MMSB model. This scheme groups latent role interactions into three categories: all roles the same, two roles the same, or all roles different. To perform inference, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with a structured mean-field approximation. A key algorithmic contribution is a novel O(K) approximation to the local variational update, which would otherwise take O(K^3) time, making the overall inference complexity O(NK). The paper demonstrates through experiments that this approach achieves state-of-the-art scalability, successfully inferring 100 latent roles on a million-node network in a few hours on a single machine, while maintaining or improving accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks compared to previous methods.

### Strengths
This paper presents a bold and highly significant contribution to the field of network analysis. Its primary strength lies in the ambitious and successful integration of multiple ideas to solve a genuinely difficult and important problem.

1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The central idea of creating a parsimonious, O(K)-parameter model for higher-order network structures (triangles) is novel and powerful. While the triangle representation (MMTM) and parsimonious parameterization (a-MMSB) exist in isolation, their combination is non-trivial and solves the critical scalability bottleneck with respect to the number of latent roles, K. The resulting ability to model networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles is a substantial leap forward for the field, opening up new possibilities for analyzing massive real-world graphs. The demonstrated orders-of-magnitude speedup over recent state-of-the-art methods like a-MMSB and MMTM is a testament to the significance of this work.

2.  **Bold and Pragmatic Design:** The authors make several aggressive modeling and algorithmic choices in service of their scalability goal. The O(K) parameter sharing is a strong assumption, but one that pays enormous dividends in performance. Similarly, the "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local variational update is a clever and pragmatic solution to reduce its complexity from O(K^3) to O(K). While not theoretically deep, this kind of creative engineering is exactly what is needed to build systems that work on real-world scales. The paper prioritizes a functional, high-impact system over minor theoretical purities, which is a commendable approach.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The method is shown to be dramatically faster than its predecessors while achieving strong performance on both synthetic latent space recovery and real-world link prediction tasks. The successful application to the 1.1M-node Youtube network is a landmark result. The convergence plots clearly show the efficiency of the stochastic approach, reaching a good solution in just 2-5 passes over the data.

### Weaknesses
While the paper is strong, there are a few points that could be considered weaknesses, though in my view they do not detract from the overall contribution.

1.  **Reduced Model Expressiveness:** The main trade-off for scalability is a loss of model expressiveness. The PTM cannot capture fine-grained interactions between specific pairs or triplets of distinct communities, as all such interactions are governed by a single parameter, B_0. This simplification might be too restrictive for certain networks where specific inter-community relationships are important. The authors should acknowledge this limitation more explicitly as a trade-off. However, given the excellent empirical results, this appears to be a very effective trade-off.

2.  **Incomplete Theoretical Justification:** The model is not a true generative model of networks, as the authors rightly note. More importantly, the justification for the O(K) local update approximation is entirely empirical ("we find that this approximation works as well as the full parameterization"). While the results are convincing, the paper would be stronger with some analysis, however brief, on why this random subsampling of the latent configurations works or under what conditions it might fail.

3.  **Experimental Setup:** The use of a "seeded" initialization (providing 2% of nodes with one of their true roles) in the synthetic experiments makes the latent space recovery task easier than a fully unsupervised scenario. While this is a fair comparison since all methods received the same initialization, it may overstate the absolute performance of all algorithms. It would be valuable to see how performance degrades with a more standard random initialization, even if only briefly discussed.

### Recommendation
I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a novel, bold, and highly effective solution to the crucial problem of scaling probabilistic network models. The conceptual contribution of a parsimonious higher-order model, combined with clever algorithmic approximations, results in a system that pushes the state-of-the-art by orders of magnitude in terms of practical scalability. While there are some methodological trade-offs and minor weaknesses in the experimental setup, these are acceptable compromises in light of the massive gains in performance and the new research directions this work enables. This paper is likely to have a significant impact and will be of high interest to both researchers and practitioners working with large-scale network data.
