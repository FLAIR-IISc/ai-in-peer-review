Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks by combining (i) a succinct bag-of-triangles representation [Ho et al., NIPS’11], (ii) a parsimonious model that reduces the number of triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K), and (iii) a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and natural gradients. The Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) assigns each node a mixed-membership vector over K roles and generates each observed connected triple (open or closed triangle) via role assignments to the three participating nodes. A key modeling contribution is parameter sharing across the O(K^3) role-triplet configurations, grouping them by the number of distinct roles in the triple (all same, two same/one different with a “majority” role, or all distinct), yielding parameter sets {Bxxx, Bxx, B0} of sizes O(K) + O(K) + O(1). This echoes the reduction from O(K^2) to O(K) inter-role parameters in a-MMSB [Gopalan et al., NIPS’12], but in a triangle-based model.

The inference contribution is a structured mean-field variational family that keeps the three within-triangle role variables coupled (as opposed to naïve fully factorized MF), coupled with SVI using mini-batches of motifs and natural gradients [Hoffman et al., JMLR’13]. To avoid O(K^3) local updates per triangle, the authors propose a “mixture-of-deltas” approximation that concentrates mass on diagonal configurations (a,a,a) and a randomly refreshed O(K)-sized subset of off-diagonals, achieving O(K) per-triangle local updates. With triangle subsampling (δ-subsampling) from [Ho et al., NIPS’11], the overall cost per pass scales with the number of kept triangles.

Empirically, on synthetic networks generated by MMSB and a power-law latent-space model, PTM with SVI achieves high NMI for latent role recovery, converges in 1–2 passes, and runs orders of magnitude faster than MMSB batch variational and MMTM/PTM Gibbs. On several large real networks (up to 1.1M nodes), the method converges in a few passes and is reported to fit 100 roles on 1M nodes in 4 hours on 8 threads, with competitive or better link prediction than MMSB.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual advance: principled parameter sharing reduces the triangle parameterization from O(K^3) to O(K), enabling scalability in K while preserving key triangle semantics.
- Inference design matches data structure: structured MF over the three role variables per triangle is appropriate and empirically superior to naïve MF.
- Practical, scalable algorithmic choices: mini-batch SVI with natural gradients, triangle subsampling, discarding local variational parameters to reduce memory, and an O(K) local update via mixture-of-deltas.
- Strong empirical scalability: convincing wall-clock results, including million-node networks and speedups of orders of magnitude compared to prior probabilistic baselines.
- Relevance: latent role inference and link prediction on large networks are central NeurIPS topics; the work connects to established literature on MMSB, ERGMs, and motif-based modeling.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Objective and gradient claims: The SVI gradient unbiasedness is established for mini-batches over triangles, but the paper also replaces the local variational family with a random O(K)-support “mixture-of-deltas.” This changes the variational bound across iterations. While each choice of support yields a valid lower bound, the unbiasedness and convergence claims should be made with respect to the evolving bound (or clarified). A short justification or citation for convergence under randomly changing local families would strengthen the technical soundness.
- Step-size specification: The update specifies ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ and then sets κ = −0.5 in experiments. As written, this implies a growing step size, contradicting standard SVI requirements κ ∈ (0.5, 1]. This looks like a sign error; please correct and report the actual κ used.
- Complexity claims: The paper repeatedly states O(NK) complexity; yet the cost clearly depends on the number of triangles m = O(Nδ^2) (after subsampling) and the O(K) local updates per triangle. Later sections acknowledge O(Nδ^2K). Please unify the complexity discussion and state precisely which steps are O(NK) (global updates) versus O(mK) (local updates), and the net cost per pass.
- Modeling trade-offs: PTM’s parameter sharing imposes strong symmetry assumptions (e.g., the minority role in the two-same/one-different case does not affect probabilities). This reduces expressivity relative to MMTM and may bias role discovery when role interactions are asymmetric. Some ablation (e.g., varying the degree of sharing) or qualitative analysis would help.
- Evaluation choices: 
  - Initialization seeds 2% of nodes with true roles. While this is applied consistently across methods, it is a privileged initialization. Please report sensitivity to less informative initializations (e.g., the heuristic proposed) and quantify variance across random restarts.
  - Comparisons omit a-MMSB runtime/accuracy on the same datasets (only an external runtime report is mentioned). Including direct comparisons would be informative.
  - The link prediction protocol for PTM ranks edges via marginalization over third nodes/roles under the triangle model. This differs from MMSB’s direct edge-probability ranking; the metrics are comparable, but reporting AUC with confidence intervals and including additional baselines would strengthen claims.
- Clarity: Some key equations (e.g., closed-form local updates for φxxx and φxxy) and Table 1 are deferred to the appendix; brief summaries in the main text would aid readability.

Qualitative evaluation
- Quality: Generally sound and thoughtfully engineered. The main technical issues are the step-size typo and the need to clarify convergence under the randomized local variational support. The parameter-sharing assumptions are clearly stated. Empirical methodology is reasonable, though more ablations would bolster conclusions.
- Clarity: Well organized and mostly clear. Improve consistency in complexity claims, fix the step-size notation, and add brief main-text summaries of deferred formulas.
- Originality: Moderate. Triangle-based representation is from [8]; parameter sharing is inspired by a-MMSB; SVI and natural gradients are standard. Novelty lies in the specific parsimonious triangular model, the structured MF with an O(K) local approximation, and the demonstrated scalable implementation.
- Significance: High for scalable network latent role inference. Demonstrated ability to handle million-node graphs with many roles on a single machine is impactful, and triangle-based modeling is increasingly relevant.

Suggestions for improvement
- Correct and clarify the step-size setting and convergence guarantees under the randomized local variational family.
- Unify and precisely state computational complexity per pass and total runtime scaling in N, K, δ, and number of passes.
- Provide sensitivity analyses: initialization, δ-subsampling levels, number of off-diagonal configurations in the local approximation.
- Add direct comparisons to a-MMSB on common datasets if feasible; otherwise provide stronger quantitative references.
- Discuss expressivity trade-offs induced by parameter sharing and when PTM may underperform MMTM.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, well-motivated scalability contributions enabling O(K) parameterization and O(K) local updates.
- Convincing large-scale experiments with substantial speedups and competitive accuracy.
- Method is practical, parallelizable, and memory-efficient.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some technical claims (step size, unbiasedness/convergence under randomized local family) need correction/clarification.
- Limited ablations and missing direct comparisons to the most relevant scalable baseline (a-MMSB) on the same data.
- Modeling expressivity may be curtailed in settings with asymmetric role interactions.

Overall, this is a solid and practically important contribution to scalable probabilistic network modeling; with clarifications and minor fixes, it merits acceptance.
