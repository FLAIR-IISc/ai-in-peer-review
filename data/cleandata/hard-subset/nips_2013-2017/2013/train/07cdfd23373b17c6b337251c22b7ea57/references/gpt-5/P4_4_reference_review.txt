Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks by combining (i) a succinct triangular motif (bag-of-triangles) representation [Ho et al., NIPS ’11], (ii) a parsimonious mixed-membership model (PTM) with only O(K) triangle-generating parameters, and (iii) a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and natural gradients.

Compared to edge-based latent space models such as MMSB [Airoldi et al., JMLR ’08] and its scalable variant a-MMSB [Gopalan et al., NIPS ’12], the approach leverages higher-order structure (triangles), which has a rich history in ERGM modeling and motif analysis. The PTM ties parameters across role triplets by grouping them according to the number of distinct roles in the triangle: Bxxx for all-same roles, Bxx for two-equal-one-different, and B0 for all-different. This reduces the O(K^3) tensor in the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) [Ho et al., NIPS ’11] to O(K) parameters, echoing the a-MMSB idea of tying inter-role link probabilities.

Inference uses structured mean-field over the per-triangle role-triplet variables rather than naive factorization, and introduces a “mixture-of-deltas” approximation that restricts the posterior mass to K diagonal triplets plus O(K) randomly selected off-diagonals, yielding O(K) local updates per triangle. A stochastic natural gradient scheme updates global Dirichlet variational parameters.

Experiments on synthetic data (from MMSB-style and power-law generators) evaluate latent space recovery via NMI and on real networks evaluate link prediction and variational bounds. The method shows large speedups over MMSB variational and MMTM/PTM Gibbs samplers, and reports convergence on a 1M-node, 100-role YouTube graph in 4 hours on 8 threads, substantially faster than prior scalable methods (e.g., a-MMSB on Brightkite).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The model is well-motivated and the parameter tying is coherent with the triangular equivalence classes. The structured variational family is appropriate; restricting to a mixture-of-deltas reduces complexity while maintaining a valid ELBO for the chosen variational family. The SVI setup, mini-batching over triangles, and natural gradients follow standard methodology.
- There are issues needing clarification/correction:
  - Complexity: the overall per-pass cost is O(mK) with m = O(Nδ^2) triangles after δ-subsampling; several places claim O(NK). The global update is O(NK), but the dominant local cost scales with m. The paper should consistently state O(Nδ^2K) (or O(mK)).
  - Equation (6) appears to have a typo: for distinct x, y, z, the term should involve Eq[log θi,x + log θj,y + log θk,z], not repeating x three times.
  - Step-size schedule: text uses ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)^{-κ} but later sets κ = −0.5, which would make step sizes increase with t. This likely should be κ ∈ (0.5, 1], e.g., κ = 0.5.
  - The mixture-of-deltas selects O(K) off-diagonals at random each local update. While any fixed choice yields a valid bound, randomizing A across iterations changes the variational family over time; the paper should clarify the objective under which convergence is claimed and whether the stochastic natural gradient remains unbiased with respect to a fixed ELBO.
  - δ-subsampling preserves triangle distributions approximately; the impact on inference bias and on the heldout bound is not quantified theoretically.

- Empirical support: Results show strong scaling and competitive or improved accuracy in latent role recovery and link prediction compared to MMSB variational and triangular Gibbs baselines. However, link prediction baselines are limited (MMSB only), and the seeding of 2% ground-truth roles for all methods could bias recovery results; robustness to uninformed initialization is only briefly discussed. Details on the computational cost of ranking (summing over all k) and any approximations used would help reproducibility.

Clarity:
- Generally well-written and organized; the model, equivalence classes, and inference procedure are clearly described. Missing elements (Table 1, Appendix derivations) are cited but not shown here.
- Suggested improvements:
  - Correct the noted typos (Eq. (6), step-size κ).
  - Standardize the complexity statements and explicitly separate global vs. local costs.
  - Provide pseudocode or more detail on how A is chosen adaptively vs. randomly, and ablations of its size.
  - Describe link prediction computation and approximations (e.g., sampling k) for large graphs.
  - Report sensitivity to δ and K, and include results without role seeding.

Originality:
- The key novelty is the O(K) parameter tying for triangle-generating probabilities in a mixed-membership triangular model, combined with a structured SVI algorithm and an O(K) local update via mixture-of-deltas. While parameter tying is conceptually similar to a-MMSB’s edge-level tying, applying it to triangle motif modeling with a scalable variational scheme is a new and useful contribution.
- The triangular representation and δ-subsampling build on prior work [Ho et al., NIPS ’11], but the overall modeling and inference package advances scalability to millions of nodes and hundreds of roles.

Significance:
- Addresses an important problem—scalable overlapping role inference in large networks—using higher-order structure. The reported speed and scale (1M nodes, K=100, hours on a single machine) and competitive predictive performance make this contribution practically relevant.
- The parameter-tying approach trades expressiveness for scalability; nevertheless, results suggest it is often sufficient, and the framework could be extended (e.g., grouped role-tying) in future work.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled model reduction from O(K^3) to O(K) enabling substantial scalability.
- Well-designed structured SVI with natural gradients and memory-efficient implementation.
- Strong empirical scaling with good accuracy; impressive wall-clock improvements over prior art.
- Uses higher-order motifs, aligning with known importance of triangles in social networks.

Cons against acceptance:
- Inconsistencies/typos in key equations and step-size schedule.
- Complexity claims occasionally over-simplified; lack of theoretical analysis of δ-subsampling and random A selection’s effect on convergence/bias.
- Limited baselines for link prediction and reliance on seeded initialization for recovery; robustness under fully unsupervised starts not fully demonstrated.
- The strong parameter tying (single B0 for all distinct-role triplets) may limit expressiveness; no ablation exploring this trade-off.

Recommendation:
Within scope for NeurIPS. The work is technically solid overall and practically impactful, with some clarity and experimental breadth issues that seem fixable. If the noted corrections and clarifications are addressed, this would be a strong contribution to scalable network latent space modeling.
