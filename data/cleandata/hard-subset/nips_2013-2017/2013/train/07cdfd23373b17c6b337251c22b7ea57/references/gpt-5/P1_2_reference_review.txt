Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks by combining (i) a succinct “bag-of-triangles” representation, (ii) a parsimonious mixed-membership model for triangle motifs, and (iii) a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field. The main modeling idea is the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the parameterization of triangle-generating probabilities from O(K^3) (as in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model, MMTM) to O(K) by sharing parameters across groups of role-triplet configurations according to the number of distinct roles in the triplet. On the inference side, the paper proposes an SVI procedure with natural gradients. Rather than naive mean-field over per-triangle role assignments, it uses a structured mean-field posterior over the 3-way role tensor; to make local updates O(K) rather than O(K^3), it further restricts the variational family to a “mixture-of-deltas” on diagonal triples and a randomly resampled O(K) set of off-diagonals. The algorithm subsamples triangles in mini-batches and claims overall O(NK) per-iteration cost due to the parsimonious global parameterization. Experiments indicate comparable or better latent role recovery and link prediction than prior work, with very large speedups, e.g., inferring 100 roles on a 1M-node YouTube network in ~4 hours on 8 threads.

This work builds on: MMSB (Airoldi et al.) and its scalable variant a-MMSB (Gopalan et al.), which reduce O(K^2) edge parameters via an assortative prior and use SVI; and MMTM (Ho et al.), which models triangle motifs but has O(K^3) parameters and slow Gibbs inference. It also connects to SVI and natural gradients (Hoffman et al.; Amari), and ERGM/motif-based representations that justify using triangles.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The PTM parameter sharing scheme is clear and yields a well-defined likelihood over triangle motifs, with symmetric Dirichlet priors. The SVI derivation is standard for conditionally conjugate models; the structured mean-field local updates are derived, and the stochastic natural gradient updates are reasonable. The “mixture-of-deltas” restriction provides a scalable O(K) local update. However:
  - The paper asserts unbiased gradients for L via mini-batching S, but the additional restriction of the variational family to the sampled A set changes the objective to a tighter/looser lower bound; gradients are then unbiased for that restricted bound, not the original L. This is fine, but should be stated more precisely (and the impact on accuracy should be quantified).
  - There is a likely sign error in the step-size schedule. Algorithm 1 specifies ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)^{-κ} with conditions ensuring ∑ρt = ∞, ∑ρt^2 < ∞; later κ is set to −0.5 in experiments, which would make ρt grow as (τ1 + t)^{0.5}. Please clarify; presumably κ ∈ (0.5, 1] and κ = 0.5 was intended.
  - Expressivity: PTM shares parameters so aggressively that all “three distinct roles” triplets share the same B0 regardless of which roles appear; and all “one minority, one majority role x” configurations share Bxx. This may limit fidelity for heterogeneous role interactions. The empirical section suggests this is often acceptable, but an ablation (e.g., intermediate parameterizations) or a discussion of when PTM may underfit would strengthen the case.
- Experiments: Synthetic latent recovery (NMI) and link prediction are appropriate, and large-scale timing results are impressive. Some details are missing or need clarification:
  - The link prediction protocol (metric: AUC? precision@k?), scoring complexity (marginalizing over all k), and negative sampling strategy should be specified precisely.
  - Initialization seeds 2% of nodes with ground-truth roles for all methods; this is helpful for fair comparison but not always realistic. Please report results with unsupervised initializations (you suggest a heuristic) and/or sensitivity to initialization.
  - Baselines: a-MMSB with SVI is the most relevant scalable edge-based probabilistic baseline; results are cited from prior work for one dataset but are not included side-by-side across tasks. Additional comparisons to scalable non-probabilistic methods widely used for link prediction (e.g., LINE/DeepWalk/Node2Vec) would help position performance.
  - The δ-subsampling choice and its effect on accuracy vs. speed deserve a sensitivity analysis.

Clarity
The paper is generally well-organized and readable, with a clear description of the model, intuition for equivalence classes, and the inference procedure. Pointers to the Appendix for detailed updates are appropriate, though including at least one worked example of the φ-update beyond the distinct-roles case would help. Minor issues:
- Fix the step-size κ sign and ensure notation is consistent.
- The construction of A for the local posterior mentions (a, a, b) twice; likely you intended (a, b, a) and (b, a, a).
- Complexity statements sometimes ignore dependence on the number of (subsampled) triangles m; stating costs in terms of m and |S| would be clearer.

Originality
The key novelties are: (i) the O(K) parameter sharing for triangle motifs via equivalence classes on distinctness, extending the assortative idea from a-MMSB to triadic settings; (ii) a structured mean-field SVI tailored to triangle motifs; and (iii) the “mixture-of-deltas” O(K) local update with randomized off-diagonal support. Using triangles and δ-subsampling follows prior work [8], and SVI/natural gradients are standard, but the combination and the particular approximations appear original and practically impactful.

Significance
Scalability to million-node networks with hundreds of roles, while maintaining competitive accuracy, is valuable. If broadly validated, the approach provides a practical alternative to edge-based MMSB variants when triangle structures are informative. The modeling simplification may also improve statistical efficiency in the large-K regime. The work is likely to be adopted or extended by researchers working on scalable community detection, mixed-membership models, and motif-based network analysis.

Pros
- Elegant parameter sharing reduces K^3 to O(K); very fast O(NK) global updates and O(K) local updates.
- Structured mean-field avoids rank-1 limitations of naive MF.
- Strong empirical scalability; convincing wall-clock improvements; convergence in few passes.
- Competitive or better accuracy on synthetic and real data.

Cons
- Potential underfitting due to aggressive parameter tying; limited discussion/ablation.
- Theoretical treatment of the restricted variational family is informal; no bounds on approximation error or variance from randomized A.
- Baseline coverage is incomplete (especially a-MMSB SVI; modern scalable link predictors).
- Some experimental details missing; step-size typo.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and fix the step-size schedule; report sensitivity to τ0, τ1, κ.
- Provide ablations: full structured MF vs. mixture-of-deltas on small K; varying size/strategy of A; and sensitivity to δ.
- Include a-MMSB SVI results on the same datasets; optionally add standard embedding baselines for link prediction.
- Detail link prediction scoring, metric, and computational cost; report AUC/ROC and precision-recall.
- Discuss when PTM’s parameter tying may be too restrictive and possible intermediate parameterizations.

Recommendation
Overall, a strong submission with a practical and well-motivated modeling/inference contribution and compelling scalability results. Addressing the clarity and evaluation gaps would make it even stronger. I lean toward acceptance.
