Summary and contributions
The paper tackles scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks by: (i) adopting a bag-of-triangles representation to reduce data size; (ii) introducing a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that ties triangle-generating parameters across equivalence classes of role triplets, reducing parameters from O(K^3) to O(K); and (iii) developing a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients and an O(K) per-triangle local update via a mixture-of-deltas approximation. Empirically, the method is reported to recover latent roles on synthetic graphs and to outperform MMSB on link prediction across several real networks, while scaling to 1M nodes and 100 roles in hours on a single multi-core machine.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds directly on the triangular representation in Ho et al. (NIPS’11), combining ideas from MMSB/a-MMSB on parameter tying (reducing inter-role parameterizations) and SVI with natural gradients (Hoffman et al.). Compared to MMTM (Ho et al.), PTM trades expressivity for scalability by grouping K^3 configurations into three equivalence classes (all-equal, two-equal, all-distinct). The SVI procedure borrows the standard decomposition into local/global updates, but departs in adopting a structured local variational posterior over triangle roles and a bespoke low-rank “mixture-of-deltas” approximation to achieve O(K) local complexity.

Strengths
- Scalability and engineering: The O(K) parameterization and per-triangle O(K) local update are compelling. Discarding local variational parameters between iterations and parallelizing over triangles are practical, memory-efficient choices.
- Structured variational family: Modeling the triad-role tensor jointly rather than with a rank-1 mean-field is well motivated; the mixture-of-deltas is a smart compromise between accuracy and speed.
- Clear modeling intuition: The equivalence-class tying is principled given symmetry of motifs under role multiplicities and matches the spirit of a-MMSB’s parameter tying.
- Empirical evidence: Results suggest faster convergence (few data passes) and competitive or better link prediction than MMSB on large social networks. Demonstrated wall-clock improvements (e.g., Brightkite, Stanford) over prior published baselines are notable.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical guarantees and unbiasedness: SVI convergence guarantees assume exact local optima for the variational factors in the stochastic objective. Here, unbiasedness is established for the exact local update, but the actual algorithm uses an approximate mixture-of-deltas and a random subset A of off-diagonals. This induces a biased gradient; no theory is provided on the resulting stationary points or the tradeoff between subset size and bias/variance. This is a central omission, as the main complexity claim relies on this approximation.
- Step-size schedule inconsistency: The paper states ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ and later uses κ = −0.5 in experiments. With the negative exponent, step sizes grow in t, violating Robbins–Monro conditions. This is likely a sign error, but needs correction as it undermines the convergence claim.
- Expressivity limitations: PTM’s parameter tying collapses many distinct role interaction patterns. In particular, all-distinct triplets share B0; two-equal cases share Bxx independent of the minority role; and all-equal share Bxxx. This prevents modeling role-specific triadic closure, heterophily among specific role triples, or nuanced disassortative/assortative mixtures that MMTM could capture. A discussion/ablation quantifying this bias vs. speed tradeoff is missing.
- Evaluation methodology:
  - Initialization: Seeding 2% of nodes with ground-truth roles favors convergence; baselines are sensitive to initialization too. A fair comparison should include (i) unsupervised initializations for all methods; and (ii) reporting variability across random seeds.
  - Computational fairness: Some baselines are single-threaded while PTM uses 4–8 threads; runtimes per data pass are reported but not end-to-end wall-clock with matched cores. a-MMSB comparisons are limited to one dataset; broader comparisons to scalable SBMs or Poisson factorization blockmodels would be helpful.
  - Metrics and baselines: Link prediction is compared only with MMSB. Given that triadic signals are strong in social networks, comparisons to triangle-aware heuristics, higher-order tensor factorizations, or modern scalable embeddings (e.g., Node2Vec/DeepWalk, higher-order random walks) would better contextualize performance. AUC/precision@k and proper calibration reporting would be useful.
  - Ablations: No ablation on the size of A in the mixture-of-deltas, on δ-subsampling, or on using the exact O(K^3) local update on small graphs to quantify approximation error.
- Modeling concerns:
  - The bag-of-triangles model is not a generative model of graphs; selection bias (conditioning on connected triples) can shift posterior mass and affect link prediction. This is acknowledged but not analyzed. Importance weighting of δ-subsampled triangles or a correction for degree-induced sampling bias would strengthen the approach.
  - Complexity claims: The paper alternates between O(NK) and O(Nδ^2K). The global update is O(NK), but per pass over all triangles is O(Nδ^2K). Clarify the per-iteration cost as a function of mini-batch size and report the number of passes required for convergence across datasets.

Quality
Technically sound in its variational construction and engineering, but incomplete on theoretical analysis of the approximate local update and step-size issue. Empirical results are promising but would benefit from broader, fairer comparisons and ablations.

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized, with intuitive explanations for parameter tying and symmetry classes. However, key derivations (natural gradients, exact local updates) are deferred to the appendix. The step-size sign and complexity statements need clarification. Some references are incomplete.

Originality
Reasonably novel combination: a new parameter tying scheme for triangular latent-role models, and a tailored structured SVI with an efficient mixture-of-deltas local approximation. Building on prior triangular representations and a-MMSB-style parsimony is appropriate and clearly differentiated.

Significance
If the reported scalability and accuracy hold under stricter comparisons and corrected SVI details, this is a meaningful advance for probabilistic higher-order network modeling at scale. The ability to infer 100-role latent spaces on million-node graphs on a single machine is impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix and clarify the learning-rate schedule; provide convergence diagnostics and sensitivity.
- Provide analysis (or at least empirical bounds) on the bias introduced by the mixture-of-deltas and the choice/size of A; include ablations and a small-graph comparison to exact local updates.
- Quantify the expressivity gap vs. MMTM through controlled synthetic experiments varying role-interaction patterns.
- Broaden baselines (a-MMSB with matched threading, scalable SBMs/Poisson models, and simple/higher-order embedding baselines); ensure fair threading and initialization across methods.
- Analyze δ-subsampling bias and consider importance weighting.
- Clarify computational complexity per iteration and report wall-clock with matched resources.

Pros
- Elegant parameter tying yielding O(K) global parameters
- Structured SVI with practical O(K) local updates
- Strong scalability and memory footprint
- Empirically competitive on large networks

Cons
- Convergence theory gaps and step-size error
- Limited expressivity vs. full MMTM not quantified
- Evaluation fairness and missing ablations/baselines
- Selection bias from triangle-only modeling not addressed

Overall: Promising and practically important, but I would like to see the convergence/step-size issue corrected and additional empirical validation/ablations before acceptance.
