This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, addressing a key limitation of existing methods. The core idea is to combine a bag-of-triangles network representation with a parsimonious statistical model and a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for inference. This combination yields a method with O(NK) complexity, enabling analysis of million-node networks with reasonable computational resources. The work builds on existing literature like MMSB, a-MMSB, and MMTM, specifically addressing their scalability issues. The authors convincingly argue that existing approaches fall short in simultaneously achieving scalability and accuracy, and they propose a solution that tackles all three crucial principles: succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The primary strength of this work lies in its scalability. The O(NK) complexity is a significant improvement over existing methods, as demonstrated by the experimental results on large networks (up to 1 million nodes). This addresses a critical bottleneck in latent space analysis of large graphs.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed PTM and stochastic variational inference algorithm appear theoretically well-founded. The authors provide clear explanations of the model and the inference procedure, with appropriate references to related work on variational inference and natural gradients. The justification for the parameter sharing in PTM is logical and contributes to the scalability of the model.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides extensive experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world networks. The results demonstrate that the proposed method achieves comparable or better accuracy than existing approaches while being significantly faster. The comparison with a-MMSB and MMTM is particularly compelling.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The motivation for the work is clearly stated, and the technical details are presented in a comprehensible manner. The authors make a conscious effort to explain complex concepts in an accessible way.
* **Novelty:** While building on existing techniques, the combination of the bag-of-triangles representation, the parsimonious model, and the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm is novel and effectively addresses the scalability challenges in latent space inference.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Mini-batch Size Sensitivity:** The performance of stochastic variational inference can be sensitive to the choice of mini-batch size. The paper mentions using a 10% mini-batch size, but a more thorough investigation of the impact of different mini-batch sizes on the accuracy and convergence speed would be beneficial.
* **δ-subsampling Parameter:** The performance also depends on the δ-subsampling parameter. A deeper analysis of the optimal setting of δ would strengthen the results. It's not clear how to choose δ effectively without knowing the degree distribution beforehand.
* **Structured Mean-Field Approximation:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of a naive mean-field approximation, a more detailed justification for the chosen structured mean-field approach would be helpful. It would be valuable to compare the performance of the structured mean-field approximation with other possible approximations.
* **Assumptions of PTM:** The assumptions behind the PTM regarding parameter sharing, while leading to scalability, might introduce bias. A discussion of the potential impact of these assumptions on the accuracy of the inferred latent spaces would be valuable.
* **Convergence Criteria:** The convergence criterion is not explicitly stated. A clearer definition of convergence and the monitoring of relevant metrics would improve reproducibility.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a critical limitation in latent space network analysis – scalability.
* Presents a technically sound and novel approach.
* Provides strong empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world networks.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.
* Offers significant performance improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Sensitivity to hyperparameter choices (mini-batch size, δ-subsampling).
* Limited discussion of potential biases introduced by the PTM's assumptions.
* Lack of detailed justification for the chosen structured mean-field approximation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed approach addresses a critical challenge – scalability – and offers a practical solution for analyzing large-scale networks. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance.
