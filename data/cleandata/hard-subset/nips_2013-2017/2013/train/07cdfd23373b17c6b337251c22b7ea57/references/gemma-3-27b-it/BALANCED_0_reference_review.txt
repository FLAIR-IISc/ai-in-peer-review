This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, addressing a critical bottleneck in applying these techniques to real-world datasets. The core idea is to combine a succinct network representation (bag-of-triangles) with a parsimonious statistical model and a scalable stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors convincingly argue that previous methods fall short by focusing on only some of these principles, and their proposed approach demonstrably overcomes these limitations, achieving significant speedups while maintaining competitive accuracy.

**Strengths:**

* **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The primary strength of this work is the substantial improvement in scalability compared to existing methods. The reported results on large networks (up to 1M nodes) are impressive, particularly the comparison to [5, 8] which struggled with similar datasets. The O(NK) complexity is a major achievement.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The authors clearly articulate the challenges of scaling latent space inference and present a compelling argument for their three-pronged approach (succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, scalable inference). Each component is well-justified and builds upon prior work.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) appears well-designed, effectively reducing the parameter space while still capturing essential network structure. The stochastic variational inference algorithm, with its use of natural gradients and structured mean-field approximation, seems theoretically sound. The detail regarding the local update approximation (δ-subsampling) is particularly valuable.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The convergence results on large networks are particularly noteworthy. The comparison to established baselines like MMSB and MMTM provides a clear benchmark.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the technical details and motivating their design choices. The inclusion of relevant citations is appropriate.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Discussion of Mini-Batch Size:** While the authors mention using a 10% mini-batch size, the sensitivity of the algorithm to this parameter is not thoroughly investigated. A more detailed analysis of mini-batch size effects on convergence and accuracy would be beneficial.
* **Structured Mean-Field Approximation Justification:** The authors state that a naive mean-field approximation performs poorly, but the justification for choosing the specific "structured" approximation is somewhat limited. More explanation of why this specific structure is expected to be beneficial would strengthen the paper.
* **Dependence on δ-Subsampling:** The approach relies on the δ-subsampling technique, which introduces a parameter that needs to be tuned. While the authors provide guidance on setting δ, a more systematic evaluation of its impact on performance across different network topologies would be helpful. Is there a “sweet spot” or a range of effective values?
* **Link Prediction Evaluation:** The link prediction evaluation, while positive, is relatively simple. Exploring more sophisticated link prediction metrics or comparing to more recent state-of-the-art link prediction methods would add further credibility.
* **Lack of Comparison to Graph Neural Networks (GNNs):** While the paper focuses on probabilistic latent space models, a brief discussion of how the results compare to recent advances in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which also aim to learn node embeddings for large-scale networks, would be valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant challenge in network analysis – scalability of latent space inference.
* The proposed approach achieves a substantial improvement in scalability compared to existing methods.
* The technical contributions are sound and well-motivated.
* The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The sensitivity analysis of key parameters like mini-batch size and δ-subsampling is limited.
* The justification for the specific structured mean-field approximation could be more detailed.
* The link prediction evaluation is relatively simple, and a comparison to GNNs is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed approach effectively addresses the scalability challenges of probabilistic latent space inference, and the empirical results are compelling. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

* Conduct a more thorough sensitivity analysis of the mini-batch size and δ-subsampling parameter.
* Provide a more detailed justification for the chosen structured mean-field approximation.
* Explore more sophisticated link prediction metrics and/or compare to more recent link prediction methods.
* Briefly discuss how the results compare to recent advances in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs).
* Consider adding a small ablation study to highlight the importance of each component of the proposed approach (succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, scalable inference).
