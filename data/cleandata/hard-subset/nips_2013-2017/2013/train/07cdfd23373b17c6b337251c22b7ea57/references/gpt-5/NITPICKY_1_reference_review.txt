Summary
The paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for latent space inference on large networks using a bag-of-triangles representation. PTM reduces the O(K^3) triangle-configuration parameters of MMTM to O(K) by sharing parameters across equivalence classes of role triplets (all equal, two equal, all distinct). The authors develop a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with a stochastic natural gradient, and an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation for local updates. They report faster convergence and comparable or better accuracy than MMSB and MMTM on synthetic and real networks, including million-node scale.

Relation to prior work
- Builds directly on MMSB [Airoldi et al. 2008] and a-MMSB [Gopalan et al. NIPS], and on the triangular representation/MMTM [Ho et al. NIPS]. Uses SVI and natural gradients [Hoffman et al. JMLR; Amari 1998].
- The parameter sharing idea resembles a-MMSB’s inter-role simplification from K^2 to 1; here generalized to K^3→O(K).
- Higher-order motif-based network modeling is motivated by ERGM and motif literature.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea: share parameters across role-triplet equivalence classes to bring K^3→O(K).
- Structured mean-field for triplet roles is sensible; naive mean-field is indeed too restrictive here.
- Using minibatches of triangles to scale inference is appropriate for the representation.
- Empirical scale claims (up to 1M nodes, 100 roles) are compelling if substantiated.
- Parallelization and discarding local variational parameters to control memory are practical contributions.

Weaknesses and detailed concerns
Technical correctness and reproducibility:
- Complexity claims are inconsistent. Early sections claim O(NK) complexity; the algorithm per pass is O(|I| K) with |I|=O(N δ^2). Only if δ is treated constant do you get O(NK); this should be stated consistently. The “O(NK) inference” claim is misleading otherwise.
- The SVI objective and unbiasedness: You define LS by subsampling triangles (standard) but then change the local variational family by randomly selecting a new set A of off-diagonals at each local update. If A changes across iterations, the optimization target itself changes. The claim “any choice of A yields a valid lower bound” is true for a fixed family, but stochastic switching of the family breaks standard Robbins–Monro assumptions. Please either (a) fix A per triangle (or use an incremental expansion/truncation with monotone bounds) and justify convergence, or (b) provide a convergence argument for the random-A scheme.
- The “mixture-of-deltas” construction text appears to have a typo when enumerating combinations: it lists (a, a, b), (a, b, a) or (a, a, b) (duplicated), likely intending (a, b, b) as well. This must be clarified.
- Equation (6) is given only for distinct x,y,z; updates for (xxx) and (xxy) are deferred to the Appendix, which is not provided. The natural gradients for η,γ are also omitted. This prevents reproduction.
- Link prediction: The method ranks edges by the probability that triangle (i,j,k) includes edge i–j, marginalized over k and roles. This is ad hoc for a model that is not generative for edges; no explicit formula, computational strategy, or complexity analysis is provided (marginalizing over all k is O(N) per pair). Clarify how triangles involving held-out edges are handled in the training data to avoid leakage, and ensure computational feasibility for large N.
- δ-subsampling: No details on how triangles are extracted and sampled in O(1) per sample; counting triangles is nontrivial. Are training statistics reweighted to correct subsampling bias? Without this, the estimator could be biased.
- Initialization and evaluation: Recovery experiments seed 2% of nodes with ground-truth roles. This favors convergence and is unrealistic; include results under random or heuristic initialization. The threshold 0.125 for NMI is ad hoc; sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Baselines: A fair comparison should include DC-SBM (Karrer & Newman 2011) and scalable overlapping community detection baselines (e.g., BigCLAM) and, if possible, an SVI variant of MMTM for small K to test the accuracy/parsimonious tradeoff.
- Claims of “orders of magnitude faster” partly mix single-threaded baselines with 4-thread runs; also earlier the paper claims “8 threads” on YouTube vs later “4 threads” elsewhere—be consistent and report wall-clock and hardware for all methods fairly.

Clarity and presentation:
- Numerous missing elements: Table 1 is referenced but not present; Figures 1–2 and Tables 2–3 are referenced but absent. The Appendix is essential for the algorithm but missing.
- Many formatting/typo issues: “stateof-the-art,” “Θ(N2)”, inconsistent math spacing, “( δ 2 )” binomial formatting, “3-triangles,” duplicated tuple as noted above, etc.
- Step size: You set ρt=τ0(τ1+t)^{-κ} with κ=−0.5; this makes step sizes increase with t and violates convergence. Probably κ=0.5; please correct.
- References are incomplete (missing years/venues/pages) and include placeholders “, , ,”. Several citations need standard bibliographic details.

Originality
- The specific parameter-sharing over triangle role configurations is a neat and natural reduction, and the structured SVI with O(K) local approximation is a practical novelty. However, the idea of parameter tying for scalability is not new (e.g., a-MMSB), and higher-order motif modeling has precedent. The combination is incremental but useful.

Significance
- If validated, the scale benefits are substantial. However, the current version lacks enough methodological rigor (convergence under random A, exact updates in Appendix) and complete experiments to confidently assess significance. The link prediction evaluation needs clearer methodology and stronger baselines.

Pros
- Elegant parameter reduction from O(K^3) to O(K).
- Sensible structured variational family; avoids rank-1 artifacts.
- Practical, parallel SVI implementation ideas; memory-aware.
- Promising empirical scale.

Cons
- Incomplete/missing derivations, tables, figures, and Appendix.
- Convergence/consistency concerns with changing variational support A.
- Ambiguous complexity claims; step size typo.
- Evaluation relies on seeded ground truth; baselines and metrics could be more rigorous.
- Anonymity and formatting: author names, acknowledgments, and grants appear; references incomplete.

Recommendation
Lean reject in current form due to (i) missing essential content (Appendix, tables/figures), (ii) methodological issues around the local approximation and convergence, (iii) inconsistencies and typos (including a critical step-size sign), and (iv) inadequate experimental rigor. The core idea is promising; with complete derivations, corrected and clarified algorithmic details, stronger and fairer evaluations, and polished presentation, this could become a strong submission.
