- Proposes Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that reduces triangle-parameterization from O(K³) to O(K) via parameter sharing.  
- Develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm with O(NK) per-iteration complexity, converging in 2–5 passes on million-node networks.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical speedups and competitive or improved accuracy on synthetic and real-world networks, including a 1M-node YouTube experiment.  
- Core innovation (O(K) parameterization) is well-motivated and effectively implemented.  
- Runtime analysis is convincing and supported by empirical measurements.  
- Lacks theoretical analysis quantifying approximation error introduced by parameter sharing compared to full MMTM.  
- The "mixture-of-deltas" local-update approximation is clever but under-supported; approximation error is not sufficiently quantified.  
- Paper omits discussion and evaluation of memory requirements, which are critical at million-node scale.  
- Explanation of the parameter-sharing partitioning (why grouping is valid) is opaque and needs clearer intuition.  
- Use of "latent space" is imprecise and may confuse non-expert readers.  
- Insufficient explanation of why structured mean-field is necessary versus naive mean-field; implications of high-rank q(x,y,z) are not elaborated.  
- Doesn’t sufficiently distinguish contributions from related work (e.g., a-MMSB) despite acknowledging similarities.  
- Limited comparisons to other scalable network methods (e.g., GraphSAGE, DeepWalk) that would help situate the work.  
- Paper under-discusses limitations of triangle-based representation, especially for low-clustering networks where triangles are rare.  
- Recommendation: Major Revision — address theoretical error analysis, memory usage, parameter-sharing justification, limitations of triangle representation, and broader comparisons.  
- Specific revision requests: add theoretical approximation-error analysis, clearer justification of parameter-sharing cases, discussion of triangle-representation failure modes, memory comparisons with baselines, broader method comparisons, and stronger evidence for the mixture-of-deltas approximation.
