This paper addresses the important problem of scalable latent space inference in large networks. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods – specifically their inability to simultaneously achieve scalability and accuracy – and propose a “Parsimonious Triangular Model” (PTM) built upon the bag-of-triangles representation, combined with a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm. The motivation is sound, and the identified bottlenecks in prior work (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM) are accurately described. However, despite the clear presentation and ambitious goals, I find the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant advance warranting acceptance to NIPS.

The core contribution, the PTM, relies on parameter sharing to reduce complexity from O(K^3) to O(K). While conceptually appealing, the justification for *how* this sharing is implemented – partitioning based on the number of distinct latent roles – feels somewhat arbitrary. The authors state this is analogous to the parameter sharing in a-MMSB, but the context and impact are quite different. In a-MMSB, the sharing simplifies inter-role link probabilities; here, it fundamentally alters the triangle-generating process. The explanation relating this to equivalence classes of triangles, while detailed, feels overly complex and doesn’t fully justify the chosen parameterization. Is this the *most* parsimonious representation, or simply *a* parsimonious representation? A more thorough exploration of alternative parameter sharing strategies would strengthen the argument.

A major concern is the level of empirical validation. The synthetic experiments, while demonstrating the method's performance on generated data, lack a comparative analysis against truly strong baselines *at scale*. The MMSB variational algorithm is included, but its known limitations are well-established. MMTM and PTM Gibbs sampling are presented, but fail to complete on the larger networks, making a direct comparison impossible. The claim that the stochastic variational algorithm converges faster than a batch implementation is interesting, but doesn’t address the core issue of scalability relative to other scalable methods. The real-world network experiments showcase speed improvements over previous work, which is positive, but focus primarily on runtime. A more rigorous comparison of accuracy (e.g., link prediction performance, community detection metrics) against established methods – even if they are slower – is crucial. The statement that the triangle-based method performs better on larger social networks than edge-based MMSB requires substantial justification; simply stating it matches literature is insufficient. The provided results aren’t sufficiently detailed to assess the *magnitude* of these improvements.

The clarity of the paper is generally good, but the mathematical notation and derivations in Section 4 are dense and could benefit from more explanation. The choice to use a “mixture-of-deltas” variational approximation to accelerate inference, while understandable, adds complexity and feels like a somewhat ad-hoc solution. A clearer justification for this choice, and perhaps an ablation study demonstrating its impact on performance, would be beneficial.

The originality of the work is moderate. The combination of bag-of-triangles representation, parameter sharing, and stochastic variational inference isn’t entirely novel; rather, it appears to be a clever integration of existing techniques. The significance of the work is potentially high, given the importance of scalable network analysis. However, the limited empirical evidence weakens this claim.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in network analysis.
*   Clear motivation and identification of limitations in existing methods.
*   Demonstrates significant speed improvements over previous work on real-world networks.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Parameter sharing strategy in PTM lacks strong justification.
*   Insufficient comparative evaluation against strong baselines, particularly at scale.
*   Empirical results lack sufficient detail and analysis of accuracy metrics.
*   The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation feels ad-hoc and requires further justification.



Overall, while the paper presents a promising approach, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation and the somewhat arbitrary nature of the parameter sharing strategy prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. I believe the authors have a solid foundation, but significant revisions and more rigorous experimentation are needed to demonstrate a clear and substantial contribution to the field. I suggest the authors focus on a more thorough comparative evaluation, a stronger justification for the PTM parameterization, and a more detailed analysis of the results.
