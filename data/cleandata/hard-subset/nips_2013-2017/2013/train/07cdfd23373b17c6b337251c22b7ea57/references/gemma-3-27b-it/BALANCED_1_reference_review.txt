This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, addressing a critical challenge in network analysis. The authors build upon the bag-of-triangles representation and introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), coupled with a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm, achieving O(NK) time complexity. This represents a significant improvement over existing methods like MMSB and MMTM, which struggle with scalability due to quadratic or cubic complexity in network size or latent space dimension. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and demonstrates compelling results on both synthetic and real-world networks, including a 1M-node YouTube network.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** The primary strength lies in the demonstrated scalability. Achieving inference on a 1M-node network with 100 latent roles in 4 hours with a single machine is a substantial accomplishment, and a clear advantage over prior work. The O(NK) complexity is a significant theoretical result.
* **Novelty:** The PTM's parameter sharing mechanism is a clever and effective way to reduce model complexity while maintaining accuracy.  Combining this with stochastic natural gradient ascent is a novel approach.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including experiments on both synthetic data (designed to test different aspects of the model) and real-world networks. The comparison with existing methods (MMSB, MMTM) is fair and highlights the advantages of the proposed approach. The convergence analysis and link prediction task are valuable additions.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to understand the proposed method and its advantages. The mathematical notation is used effectively, and the explanations are concise and informative.
* **Practical Considerations:** The authors address practical considerations such as triangle subsampling and the choice of hyperparameters, demonstrating a strong understanding of the challenges of applying the method to real-world datasets. The discussion of the impact of initialization on Gibbs sampling is also insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Structured Mean-Field Approximation:** The justification for the "structured mean-field" approximation feels a bit hand-wavy. While the authors state that the naive mean-field performs poorly, a more detailed explanation of *why* the structured approximation is superior would be beneficial. What specific dependencies are being captured, and how does this improve the approximation?
* **Mini-Batch Size Sensitivity:** The choice of the mini-batch size (10%) is not thoroughly justified. A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different mini-batch sizes on performance and convergence would strengthen the results.
* **Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While comparisons with MMSB and MMTM are relevant, a comparison with more recent scalable graph embedding techniques (e.g., GraphSAGE, GCN) would provide a more complete picture of the method's performance relative to the current state-of-the-art.  These methods operate in a different framework, but addressing their relative strengths and weaknesses would be valuable.
* **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** Although the authors provide complexity analysis, a more detailed theoretical understanding of the convergence properties of the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm would be desirable.
* **Delta Approximation:** The use of the “mixture-of-deltas” approximation for faster local updates is a clever idea, but the justification for randomly picking the O(K) triples seems a bit ad hoc.  A more rigorous analysis of the impact of this approximation on the quality of the solution would be helpful.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Significant improvement in scalability compared to existing methods.
* Novel approach to parameter sharing and inference.
* Comprehensive and convincing experimental results.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Addresses an important problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited theoretical analysis of the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm.
* Lack of comparison with more recent scalable graph embedding techniques.
* Justification of key design choices (e.g., mini-batch size, delta approximation) could be stronger.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed method offers a compelling solution to the problem of scalable latent space inference, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the points raised above, particularly a more detailed justification for the structured mean-field approximation and a discussion of how this work relates to more recent advances in graph embedding techniques.  Specifically, the authors could briefly compare the runtime and scalability of PTM to GraphSAGE on a medium-sized real-world network.
