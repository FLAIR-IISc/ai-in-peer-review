This paper presents a novel approach to scalable latent space inference for large-scale networks, addressing a significant challenge in network analysis. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing probabilistic methods concerning scalability and propose a solution based on a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm. The motivation is sound, and the problem is clearly articulated within the context of existing literature, such as MMSB and MMTM.

However, despite a well-defined problem and a seemingly promising approach, I find myself skeptical. While the authors claim to address all three principles – succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference – the evidence presented doesn’t fully convince me of their simultaneous achievement, particularly concerning accuracy trade-offs. The emphasis on scalability is strong, but at the potential cost of significant modeling simplification.

The core idea of parameter sharing in the PTM is clever and addresses the limitations of MMTM. However, the justification for the specific partitioning scheme (grouping by the number of distinct roles) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a strong theoretical basis.  Why *this* specific sharing structure, and not another? The authors mention inspiration from a-MMSB, but the connection isn't deeply explored. Further analysis regarding the impact of this parameter sharing on model expressiveness is needed.

The inference algorithm, employing stochastic natural gradient ascent, appears well-motivated and designed for scalability.  However, the details surrounding the “structured mean-field approach” are somewhat vague. The authors state a naive mean-field approach performs poorly, but provide limited justification *why*.  A more thorough comparison against simpler variational inference techniques is required. Furthermore, the use of a "mixture-of-deltas" approximation to simplify the local update introduces another layer of approximation that needs careful scrutiny. While the authors claim it performs as well as the full parameterization, the potential for bias and loss of accuracy isn’t sufficiently addressed. The choice of A being randomly re-picked every iteration is a reasonable mitigation, but its effectiveness should be demonstrated more convincingly.

The experimental results are presented, but the comparisons are somewhat imbalanced. The MMTM/PTM Gibbs samplers failing to complete on larger networks is not surprising given their complexity, but it doesn’t necessarily *validate* the PTM’s scalability. Demonstrating a speedup against a non-converging method is not a strong argument. The claim of “competitive or improved accuracy” on synthetic data needs more rigorous backing. The NMI scores shown in Figure 1 are comparable, but not overwhelmingly superior, to baselines, especially given the model simplifications in PTM. The initialization strategy employed for the baselines seems designed to highlight their weaknesses; a more fair comparison would involve multiple random initializations and averaging the results. The reported runtime improvements on the real-world networks are impressive, but the specific experimental setup (e.g., hardware, number of threads) needs to be more clearly described to allow for proper replication.

The paper’s clarity is reasonable, although the notation is dense and requires careful attention.  The organization is logical, but the appendix contains significant detail that could be integrated into the main text for better readability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a crucial problem in network analysis: scalable latent space inference.
*   Presents a novel and potentially efficient approach based on a bag-of-triangles representation and parameter sharing.
*   Demonstrates significant speedups compared to existing methods on real-world networks.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The parameter sharing scheme in PTM lacks strong theoretical justification.
*   The “structured mean-field approach” and the “mixture-of-deltas” approximation are not thoroughly explained or validated.
*   The experimental results, while promising, are not conclusive and potentially biased by the experimental setup.
*   The authors haven't sufficiently demonstrated that the gains in scalability don't come at a significant cost to accuracy.
*   The paper relies heavily on implementation details (e.g., δ-subsampling) without a broader analysis of their impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards rejection. While the paper tackles an important problem and proposes an interesting solution, the lack of strong theoretical justification, limited experimental validation, and potential trade-offs between scalability and accuracy raise significant concerns. The authors have identified a niche for improvement but haven't sufficiently demonstrated a compelling advantage over existing methods to warrant acceptance into NIPS. I would encourage the authors to address the concerns raised above, particularly focusing on a more thorough theoretical analysis and a more rigorous experimental evaluation. A more comprehensive comparison against stronger baselines and a detailed analysis of the accuracy-scalability trade-off are crucial.
