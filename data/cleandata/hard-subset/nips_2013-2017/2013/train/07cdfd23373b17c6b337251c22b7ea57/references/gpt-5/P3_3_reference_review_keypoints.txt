- Proposes Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that models networks via bag-of-triangles and reduces triangle parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) using three equivalence classes (Bxxx, Bxx, B0).  
- Develops stochastic variational inference with structured mean-field over per-triangle role triplets, mini-batching over triangles, and natural-gradient updates for global parameters.  
- Introduces a “mixture-of-deltas” variational restriction that concentrates mass on diagonals plus a random O(K) subset of off-diagonals to lower local-update cost to O(K).  
- Uses δ-subsampling of triangles and other engineering tricks to achieve practical scalability and demonstrates speedups and competitive accuracy on synthetic and large real networks (including million-node graphs).  
- Strength: principled parameter-sharing in PTM is an elegant, socially-motivated reduction analogous to a-MMSB’s O(K^2)→O(K) move.  
- Strength: the structured mean-field and random O(K) restriction are a clever, effective way to capture high-rank interactions while maintaining O(K) local inference.  
- Strength: algorithmic design is clear and practical—stochastic natural gradients, triangle mini-batching, and discarding local q’s reduce memory and are well-motivated.  
- Strength: empirical results show strong speedups, robustness to larger K, and effective triangle-based link prediction in social networks.  
- Concern: convergence guarantees are unclear because the randomized restricted variational family may change the ELBO; need formal argument about unbiased gradients and preserved convergence.  
- Concern: expressivity trade-offs from O(K) tying can erase role-specific triad patterns (e.g., all-distinct B0 ignores which specific roles appear), potentially harming networks where such motifs matter.  
- Suggestion: compare PTM to low-rank tensor parameterizations (CP/Tucker) as a middle ground between O(K^3) and O(K).  
- Concern: PTM treats triangles independently, causing possible inconsistencies on shared edges and link-prediction bias (double-counting third-node evidence, favoring high-degree nodes).  
- Concern: scalability claims depend on δ; for realistic δ (e.g., 50) the number of triangles m becomes large—paper lacks concrete m, batch sizes, passes, wall-clock, memory, and triangle-materialization strategy.  
- Concern: triangle extraction/storage costs on large graphs are nontrivial; unclear if triangles are stored or sampled on-the-fly.  
- Baselines missing: no a-MMSB comparison, and lacking simple triangle-augmented edge baselines to isolate benefits of triangle modeling vs. parameter sharing.  
- Applicability limitation: triangle-centric approach may fail on low-clustering or bipartite-like graphs; recommend ablation on such datasets (e.g., citation or user–item networks).  
- Clarity: generally well written but key update equations and natural-gradient forms are relegated to the appendix (Table 1 missing); include core φ updates and gradient forms for reproducibility.  
- Clarity: be explicit about complexity assumptions (δ), hardware, threading, and triangle storage/sampling in experiments.  
- Originality: combination of triangle-based representation, PTM parameter sharing, and O(K) local approximation in stochastic variational inference is novel and distinct from MMTM and a-MMSB.  
- Significance: if convergence holds, the method significantly advances scalable latent inference for large social networks and popularizes motif-based probabilistic modeling.  
- Request: provide a formal convergence statement for the randomized restricted variational family and clarify the exact objective being optimized.  
- Request: report concrete experimental details (m, mini-batch size, passes, wall-clock, memory) and whether triangles were materialized.  
- Request: add a-MMSB and low-rank tensor baselines, analyze link-prediction bias from triangle independence, and evaluate on low-clustering graphs.  
- Pros for acceptance: elegant parameter reduction, scalable practical inference, strong empirical speedups, and solid structured variational design.  
- Cons for acceptance: lacking theoretical convergence clarity, incomplete baselines and scalability details, and unquantified expressivity limitations.  
- Overall recommendation: borderline accept leaning accept; strengthen convergence analysis and baselines to make a stronger accept case.
