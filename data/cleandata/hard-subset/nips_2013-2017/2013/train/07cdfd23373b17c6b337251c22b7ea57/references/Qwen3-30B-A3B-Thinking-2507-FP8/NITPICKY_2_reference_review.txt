

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

## Summary
This paper proposes a scalable approach to latent space inference for large networks using a "parsimonious triangular model" (PTM) that leverages triangular motif representations instead of full adjacency matrices. The authors argue that existing methods fail to simultaneously achieve scalability and accuracy due to limitations in network representation, statistical modeling, or inference algorithms. The PTM reduces the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) through parameter sharing, and develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity. The authors demonstrate significant speedups on both synthetic and real-world networks compared to prior methods.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important problem in network analysis with clear motivation. The key technical innovation of reducing the parameter space to O(K) is well-justified and represents a meaningful improvement over the O(K³) parameterization of MMTM. The experimental results demonstrate impressive scalability, with the algorithm converging on a 1M-node network in 4 hours. The comparison with prior work is thorough, and the authors provide a clear explanation of why their approach is more scalable.

## Weaknesses
Despite the significant contribution, I identified several minor but important technical inconsistencies and presentation issues that undermine the paper's rigor:

1. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for mathematical expressions. For example, "O(N2K2)" appears multiple times without proper superscripts, which should be "O(N²K²)" for consistency with standard mathematical notation. Similarly, "O(K3)" should be "O(K³)".

2. **Technical Ambiguity in Parameter Reduction**: The paper claims the PTM has "just O(K) parameters" (B0, Bxx, Bxxx for each x), but the description is confusing. Bxx is a vector of size 2, and Bxxx is a vector of size 1, so the total number of parameters is 1 + 2K + K = 3K + 1, which is indeed O(K). However, the phrasing "B0, Bxx, Bxxx, x ∈ {1, . . . ,K}" suggests Bxx and Bxxx are scalars, when they are actually vectors. This ambiguity could mislead readers.

3. **Inconsistent Complexity Claims**: The paper states "our method has time complexity O(Nδ²K)" but then claims "our method runs in O(N²) on these synthetic networks" (Section 5.2). While mathematically consistent when K = N/100, the phrasing is confusing and should be clarified to avoid reader confusion.

4. **Methodological Ambiguity in Local Update**: The paper describes an O(K) approximation to the local update by sampling "3K combinations of the form (a, a, b), (a, b, a) or (a, a, b)" which is redundant (should be "picking 3K combinations of the form (a, a, b), (a, b, a), or (b, a, a)"). More importantly, it states "another 3K combinations of the form (a, b, c)" which would be O(K³) combinations, contradicting the O(K) claim. The paper should clarify this.

5. **Vague Experimental Details**: The paper states "we used δ = 50 subsampling (i.e. (50 2) = 1225 triangles per node)" but this is technically incorrect. The number (50 2) represents the maximum possible triangles per node when degree ≤ 50, not the average. The paper should clarify this distinction.

6. **Missing Mathematical Formulation**: The paper describes link prediction ranking as "ranking possible links i−j by the probability that the triangle (i, j, k) will include edge i−j," but fails to provide the exact mathematical formulation. This lack of detail reduces reproducibility.

7. **Inconsistent Terminology**: The paper uses both "δ-subsampling" and "δ-subsampling" without consistency.

## Recommendation
While the paper presents a significant contribution to scalable latent space inference with clear experimental validation, the numerous minor technical inconsistencies and presentation issues undermine its rigor as a NIPS submission. The paper should be polished to ensure consistent notation, clarify ambiguous claims, and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. These are not fatal flaws, but as a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance in their current form.

The paper demonstrates strong technical content and meaningful progress in the field, but the attention to detail required for a NIPS submission is lacking in several places. The authors should address the notational inconsistencies, clarify the parameter reduction claim, and provide the missing mathematical details before reconsideration.

I recommend rejection on the grounds of insufficient technical precision and attention to detail, despite the paper's significant contribution to the field. The authors have good ideas, but the presentation does not meet NIPS standards for meticulousness.
