This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, addressing a critical limitation of existing methods. The core idea is to combine a succinct network representation (bag-of-triangles) with a parsimonious statistical model and a scalable stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors convincingly argue that these three principles are crucial for handling networks with millions of nodes and a significant number of latent roles.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulating the challenges with existing methods like MMSB and MMTM, and outlining how their proposed approach overcomes these limitations. The technical contributions – the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) and the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm – are significant. The reduction in parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) for the triangle-generating parameters is a substantial improvement, and the resulting algorithm’s O(NK) complexity is a key enabler for scaling to large networks.  The use of a structured mean-field approximation, rather than a naive one, demonstrates careful consideration of model accuracy. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world networks are compelling, showing substantial speedups compared to existing methods, while maintaining competitive accuracy. The comparison to a-MMSB and MMTM, highlighting the runtime differences, is particularly impactful.

However, several areas could benefit from further clarification and refinement. 

Firstly, the justification for the specific parameter sharing strategy in the PTM (grouping based on the number of distinct roles) could be strengthened. While the authors demonstrate its effectiveness, a more theoretical justification or exploration of alternative sharing schemes would be valuable. How sensitive is performance to this particular sharing strategy?

Secondly, the details of the stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm, while described, are somewhat dense. A more intuitive explanation of the natural gradient concept and its benefits in this context would be helpful for readers less familiar with optimization techniques.  Furthermore, the choice of hyperparameters (τ0, τ1, κ) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of these parameters and their impact on performance would be beneficial.

Thirdly, the discussion of the δ-subsampling procedure could be expanded. While the authors state that δ = 50 works well, a more detailed analysis of how the choice of δ impacts both runtime and accuracy is warranted. A figure illustrating the trade-off between these two factors would be informative. Also, mentioning computational cost of the preprocessing step of δ-subsampling would be useful.

Fourthly, the experimental evaluation focuses heavily on recovery of latent roles. While important, demonstrating the utility of the learned latent space for downstream tasks beyond link prediction (e.g., node classification, community detection) would further strengthen the paper's impact.

Finally, while the authors acknowledge limitations of their generative model, a discussion of how these limitations might affect the inferences drawn from real-world networks would be valuable.  How robust is the approach to violations of the model assumptions?



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The proposed approach demonstrably scales to larger networks than existing methods, enabling analysis of million-node graphs.
*   **Novel Technical Contributions:** The PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm are novel and well-motivated.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:**  The experiments show competitive or improved accuracy compared to existing methods, with substantial runtime reductions.
*   **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   **Important Problem:** Addressing scalability limitations in latent space inference is a crucial step toward analyzing large-scale networks.

**Arguments for rejection:**

*   **Parameter Sharing Justification:** The rationale for the specific parameter sharing strategy in the PTM is not fully convincing.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The impact of hyperparameter choices on performance is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Limited Downstream Task Evaluation:** The evaluation primarily focuses on latent role recovery and link prediction, lacking exploration of other downstream tasks.
* **Lack of preprocessing cost analysis**: The computational cost of the preprocessing step of delta-subsampling is not mentioned.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in network analysis. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results are compelling. While there are some areas for improvement, the benefits of the proposed approach – particularly its scalability – outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, I suggest adding a more detailed discussion of the parameter sharing strategy in the PTM, exploring the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices, and including an evaluation on additional downstream tasks.
