This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, building on the bag-of-triangles representation and introducing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) with a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for inference. The core idea—reducing the parameter space through sharing and employing a stochastic inference scheme—is relevant to the NIPS community, given the increasing scale of network datasets. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing methods (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM) in handling both large numbers of nodes and latent roles. 

However, despite the potentially significant contribution of addressing scalability in latent space modeling, the paper suffers from a multitude of flaws, ranging from minor inconsistencies to concerning methodological choices and a lack of thorough experimental validation. While the authors acknowledge some limitations, they frequently downplay their impact and fail to provide sufficiently detailed justifications for design choices. The writing, while generally clear, is occasionally imprecise and repetitive.

**Strengths:**

*   **Addresses a Relevant Problem:** Scalability of latent space models is a crucial challenge.
*   **Novel Parameter Sharing:** The PTM’s parameter sharing strategy to reduce dimensionality (O(K) vs O(K3) in MMTM) is a potentially valuable contribution.
*   **Stochastic Inference:** The stochastic natural gradient ascent approach is appropriate for large-scale datasets.
*   **Demonstrated Scalability:** The reported runtime on a 1M-node YouTube network is impressive.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient Justification for δ-subsampling:** The choice of δ=50 is stated as being larger than the average degree, but the impact of this choice on the accuracy of the results is not thoroughly explored. The authors claim it preserves the distribution, but provide limited empirical support, especially across varying network structures.
*   **Oversimplification of Local Update:** The “O(K) Approximation to Local Update” introduces a significant simplification using randomly chosen "off-diagonals". The authors gloss over the potential bias introduced by this choice and offer only a superficial justification. The claim that it "works as well as the full parameterization" needs stronger evidence.
*   **Vague Convergence Criteria:** The paper states convergence occurs after 2-5 data passes, but doesn't define a precise convergence criterion (e.g., change in variational lower bound). This makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the results.
*   **Limited Baseline Comparison:** While MMSB and MMTM are included, a more comprehensive comparison with other scalable graph embedding techniques (e.g., node2vec, DeepWalk, GraphSAGE) would strengthen the evaluation.  These methods don't necessarily fall into the probabilistic latent space modeling category, but are relevant competitors.
*   **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Experiment results (figures and tables) lack statistical significance measures (e.g., standard deviations, p-values). This makes it hard to determine if observed performance differences are meaningful.
*   **Inconsistent Notation:** The use of "Eijk" to represent both the type of triangular motif and an event is confusing.
*   **Minor Typographical Errors:** Several minor typos and grammatical inconsistencies exist throughout the paper.
*   **Unclear Table 1:** Table 1, describing the generative process, is difficult to interpret without constant reference back to the surrounding text. It lacks sufficient self-contained explanation.
*   **Poorly Defined Synthetic Data Generation:** Details about how the “power-law networks” are generated are scant, making reproducibility difficult.
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** While acknowledging sensitivity to initialization, the chosen approach (seeding 2% of nodes) feels arbitrary and may unduly favor the presented algorithm. The authors downplay its significance.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Against:**

*   Numerous methodological simplifications lack sufficient justification or empirical validation.
*   Insufficiently rigorous experimental evaluation with missing statistical significance measures.
*   Limited baseline comparison.
*   Multiple minor flaws that detract from the overall quality of the presentation.
*   The claimed scalability advantage, while promising, isn't fully convincing without a broader, more thorough evaluation.

**For:**

*   The problem is important and the authors propose a novel approach.
*   The reported runtime on the 1M-node network is encouraging.
*   The parameter sharing strategy offers a potential solution to the scalability bottleneck.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a borderline submission. While the core idea is promising and addresses an important problem, the numerous flaws and lack of rigorous validation raise serious concerns. The methodological shortcuts and lack of thorough experimentation significantly weaken the contribution. I lean towards **rejection** due to the large number of issues requiring substantial revision. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above – particularly providing a more rigorous justification for the approximations, expanding the experimental evaluation, and adding statistical significance measures – would significantly improve the paper.  A more careful and detailed presentation is also necessary.
