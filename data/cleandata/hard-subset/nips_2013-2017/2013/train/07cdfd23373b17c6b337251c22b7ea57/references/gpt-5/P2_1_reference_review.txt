Summary
The paper proposes a probabilistic latent-space model for large networks that leverages a bag-of-triangles representation to reduce data size and computation. The Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) ties triangle-generating parameters so that the number of global parameters scales as O(K) (vs. O(K3) in MMTM), by sharing across equivalence classes of role configurations: all-equal (Bxxx), two-equal (Bxx), all-distinct (B0). The authors develop a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients. To avoid O(K3) local updates, they introduce a “mixture-of-deltas” variational family that restricts mass to O(K) configurations per triangle and resamples these configurations at each local update. Empirically, the method converges in a few passes and scales to 1M nodes and 100 roles on a single machine, with competitive latent-space recovery on synthetic data and improved link prediction on several real networks, compared to MMSB and MMTM baselines.

Quality
- Soundness: The parameter-tying scheme is reasonable and clearly specified; the structured mean-field family grouping the three role variables per triangle is appropriate. The SVI formulation is standard, and the natural-gradient updates are plausible. However:
  - The claim of an unbiased gradient estimator holds when local variational parameters are exactly optimized. In practice, the paper uses the O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation. This still optimizes a valid lower bound, but the unbiasedness statement should be qualified (you are optimizing a different bound); please clarify.
  - The step-size schedule appears inconsistent: ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ with κ set to −0.5 violates Robbins–Monro conditions (it makes the step size increase). I suspect a sign typo and that κ ∈ (0.5, 1]; please correct and state the actual values used in code.
  - Complexity claims oscillate between O(NK) and O(Nδ2K). Given δ-subsampling, per-pass cost is O(Nδ2K) plus O(NK) for globals; please present complexity precisely and discuss sensitivity to δ.
  - The model is intentionally not a full network generative model (triangles only). This is stated, but it limits applicability to likelihood-based tasks; the link prediction formulation is adapted accordingly. That is acceptable given the stated goals, but should be more explicitly discussed as a trade-off.
- Evidence: Synthetic latent-space recovery (with NMI) and real-data link prediction/perplexity are appropriate. The 1M-node, 100-role experiment is impressive. However:
  - Comparisons omit a-MMSB’s stochastic variational implementation on the same datasets/hardware (you cite its runtime on Brightkite from the paper). A head-to-head comparison would strengthen the case.
  - Latent-space recovery uses 2% seeded ground truth roles for initialization. This may exaggerate differences and is atypical in unsupervised settings. Please include results from random initializations and report variability across runs.
  - Clarify the link prediction metric (AUC? pairwise accuracy?) and ensure comparisons are fair given PTM ranks using triangle probabilities while MMSB uses edge probabilities.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, with a clear motivation, model specification, and algorithm outline. The equivalence-class argument is intuitive and helpful.
- Some key items need clarification/correction:
  - Fix the step-size typo and state the theoretically valid parameter ranges; report the actual schedule used.
  - Make explicit that the SVI with mixture-of-deltas optimizes a modified bound; explain how you ensure stability when resampling the A set each iteration.
  - Provide the exact per-iteration complexity in terms of N, K, δ, mini-batch size, and threads; distinguish “per pass” from “wall-clock”.
  - Include more detail on triangle extraction cost and memory footprint for the largest graphs.
  - Move essential update equations currently in the appendix (e.g., for φxxx and φxxy) into the main text or summarize them.

Originality
- The work builds on known ideas: bag-of-triangles representation (Ho et al., 2011), parameter tying as in a-MMSB, and SVI with natural gradients. The main novelty is combining triangle-based modeling with an O(K) parameterization and an O(K) local-update variational approximation that preserves higher-order dependencies among triangle role variables.
- While incremental conceptually, the package is well engineered and addresses a real bottleneck (scaling in K). This is a meaningful methodological contribution.

Significance
- Practical significance is high: scaling to millions of nodes and hundreds of roles on a single multicore machine with competitive or better accuracy is valuable to the community. The triangle-based modeling aligns with sociological motifs and can outperform edge-based MMSB for link prediction on large social networks.
- The reduction in parameter count may improve generalization in high-K regimes; this is supported empirically. The approach will likely be used or extended, e.g., with alternative low-rank parameterizations (CP/Tucker) to recover some expressivity beyond full tying.

Pros
- Clear, principled parameter-tying scheme reducing O(K3) to O(K).
- Structured SVI with efficient O(K) local updates and natural gradients.
- Strong scalability and convincing large-scale experiments (1M nodes, 100 roles).
- Competitive accuracy on synthetic recovery and improved link prediction on large real graphs.
- Practical engineering choices (mini-batching, parallelization, discarding local variational states).

Cons
- Theoretical clarity gaps: step-size inconsistency; unbiased gradient claim vs approximate local optimization.
- Expressivity trade-off: collapsing all inter-role interactions into B0 may limit modeling of heterophilous patterns among distinct roles; discuss or compare to low-rank alternatives.
- Evaluation limitations: reliance on seeded initialization; lack of direct comparison to a-MMSB SVI on same hardware; incomplete reporting of link prediction metric and extraction costs.

Questions and suggestions for authors
- Please correct and clarify the step-size schedule and theoretical guarantees for convergence with your actual settings.
- Provide ablations on the size/composition of the A set in the mixture-of-deltas approximation and its impact on accuracy/speed.
- Add results with random initializations and report variance across runs.
- Include head-to-head comparisons with a-MMSB SVI on the same datasets and hardware.
- Report triangle extraction time and memory for each dataset; discuss behavior on high-degree networks and δ sensitivity.
- Consider a comparison to a low-rank tensor parameterization (e.g., CP) as a middle ground between O(K3) and O(K).

Overall evaluation
The paper addresses an important scalability problem with a well-motivated model and an efficient inference scheme, and demonstrates substantial empirical gains. With clarifications and stronger comparisons, it would be a solid contribution. I lean to accept based on significance and practicality, while encouraging the authors to address the noted theoretical and empirical clarity issues.
