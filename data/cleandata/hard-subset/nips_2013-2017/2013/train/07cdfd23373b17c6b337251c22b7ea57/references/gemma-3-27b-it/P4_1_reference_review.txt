This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, addressing a critical bottleneck in existing methods. The core idea is to combine a succinct bag-of-triangles network representation with a parsimonious statistical model and a scalable stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors argue convincingly that existing approaches fall short in simultaneously achieving scalability and accuracy, and demonstrate significant improvements in both aspects through their proposed method.

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the limitations of existing techniques (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM). The motivation for each design choice – succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference – is clearly explained. The technical details of the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) and the stochastic variational inference algorithm are presented with sufficient clarity, and the use of natural gradients is a notable strength. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world networks demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showcasing its superior scalability and competitive accuracy compared to state-of-the-art methods. The ablation study, comparing different inference algorithms, highlights the benefits of their proposed stochastic variational method.

A major strength lies in addressing the computational complexity issues of prior work. By leveraging the triangle representation and parameter sharing in the PTM, the authors achieve a runtime complexity of O(NK), significantly improving scalability. The experimental results strongly support this claim, showing substantial speedups on large networks compared to existing methods. The ablation studies also clearly show the benefits of the parsimonious model. The convergence analysis and the results on the 1M-node YouTube network are particularly compelling.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. While the motivation for choosing the bag-of-triangles representation is well-explained, a more detailed discussion of the information loss associated with this representation compared to edge-based models would be beneficial. Although the authors mention that it captures crucial structural features like triadic closure and network motifs, quantifying the impact of this information loss on the accuracy of latent space inference would add further weight to their approach. The choice of hyperparameters (α, λ, step sizes for stochastic gradient ascent) feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of the hyperparameter space, and a discussion of their sensitivity, would strengthen the robustness of the results.  While the paper demonstrates good performance on networks with a specific structure (power-law), testing on a wider variety of network topologies would increase the generalizability of the findings.  The section describing the synthetic data generation could be expanded to provide more details on the ground truth parameters used. The paper would also benefit from a discussion of potential limitations, such as the applicability of the triangle representation to networks with very sparse connectivity or specific structural properties.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The proposed approach demonstrably scales to networks with millions of nodes, a significant advancement over existing methods.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results show competitive or improved accuracy on both synthetic and real-world datasets.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm are well-designed and theoretically sound.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is clearly written and effectively communicates the key ideas and contributions.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Information Loss in Triangle Representation:**  A deeper discussion of the trade-offs involved in using the bag-of-triangles representation is needed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices is not thoroughly investigated.
*   **Generalizability:** Testing on a wider range of network topologies would increase confidence in the general applicability of the method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in network analysis. The proposed approach is technically sound, scalable, and empirically validated. While there are a few areas for improvement, the overall contribution is significant and warrants acceptance to NIPS. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
