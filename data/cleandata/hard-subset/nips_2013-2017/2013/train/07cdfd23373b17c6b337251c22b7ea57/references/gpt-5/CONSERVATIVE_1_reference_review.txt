Summary
The paper tackles scalable probabilistic latent space inference for large networks by (i) adopting the “bag-of-triangles” representation from prior work, (ii) proposing a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that collapses the K^3 triangle-parameter tensor into O(K) parameters via role-sharing (B0, Bxx, Bxxx), and (iii) developing a stochastic variational inference (SVI) procedure with a structured mean-field posterior and an O(K) per-triangle “mixture-of-deltas” local approximation. The method uses δ-subsampling of triangles for high-degree nodes and parallelizes updates across triangles. Empirically, the authors report fast convergence (2–5 passes), competitive or improved latent-role recovery on synthetic data, better link prediction than MMSB on some real networks, and scalability to a 1M-node network with K=100 in ~4 hours on 8 threads.

Relation to prior work
The modeling foundation (triangular representation and mixed-membership roles over triangles) builds directly on Ho et al. (NIPS’12), while the parameter-parsimony idea (collapsing off-diagonal parameters) echoes a-MMSB (NIPS’12) for edges. The inference framework (SVI with natural gradient and structured mean-field) follows Hoffman et al. (JMLR’13), adapted here to triangular motifs with a constrained local family. The paper positions itself primarily as a pragmatic combination and scaling of existing principles to triangle-based modeling with an O(K) global parameterization.

Strengths
- Scalability focus with a clear pathway: O(K) parameterization; local O(K) updates via a restricted variational family; mini-batch SVI; embarrassingly parallel local steps; discarding local variational parameters to save memory.
- Practicality: The δ-subsampling strategy and a structured mean-field posterior are reasonable choices to mitigate the curse of K and motif count.
- Empirical results suggest useful speed-ups, with convergence in a few passes, and large-scale demonstrations (1M nodes).
- The structured mean-field (joint q(s_i,jk, s_j,ik, s_k,ij)) is more faithful than naive mean-field for triangles, and the mixture-of-deltas approximation is a sensible heuristic to cut O(K^3) local cost.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theoretical rigor of the inference scheme is underdeveloped where it matters most. The SVI convergence guarantees require unbiased gradients for a fixed variational family. Here, the local family is stochastically restricted per triangle by sampling a set A of “off-diagonals” and re-sampling A at each local update; hence the effective lower bound being optimized changes across iterations. The paper asserts unbiasedness and convergence based on standard SVI theory, but this does not directly apply when the variational family (and bound) itself is randomized per iteration. A formal argument (or at least an empirical ablation showing stable monotone ascent of a fixed lower bound) is needed.
- Expressivity vs. parsimony trade-off: Collapsing all distinct-role triples into a single B0 and all “two same, one different” into role-wise Bxx may be too restrictive for networks exhibiting nuanced triadic patterns or heterophily between particular role triples. There is no ablation quantifying accuracy loss vs. MMTM as K grows (beyond a single Gibbs baseline that reportedly gets stuck). This sharing may also bias link prediction, since only triangles are modeled and off-diagonal role interactions are homogenized.
- Not a generative network model: By design, the model does not enforce edge consistency across overlapping motifs and ignores empty and single-edge triples. This limits interpretability of likelihood/perplexity and complicates fair comparison to edge-based generative models. The paper acknowledges this but still uses likelihood-based metrics; a careful discussion of what is actually being measured (triangle likelihood vs. network likelihood) is needed.
- Evaluation gaps and fairness issues:
  - Baselines: Link prediction compares only to MMSB. At minimum, include a-MMSB (the closest scalable edge-based probabilistic baseline) and, if feasible, other established approaches for the period (e.g., Hoff et al. latent distance, nonparametric latent feature models). For completeness, ablate against MMTM with a scalable variational method if possible (even if expensive) on medium-sized graphs.
  - Implementation fairness: The proposed method uses multi-threading while baselines are single-threaded; report wall-clock normalized per thread and provide apples-to-apples comparisons where possible.
  - Synthetic evaluation relies on ad hoc thresholding of θ at 0.125; results may be sensitive to this choice. Provide sensitivity or use established continuous-similarity metrics (e.g., AUC over support recovery at varying thresholds).
  - Seeding: The method (and baselines) benefit from seeding with true roles for 2% of nodes. Realistic behavior without seeding should be reported, or at least a robust unsupervised initialization compared fairly across methods.
- Clarity and correctness issues:
  - Equation (6) appears to use log θi,x + log θj,x + log θk,x for distinct x,y,z; likely a typo and should be θi,x + θj,y + θk,z. This is critical.
  - The step-size schedule uses κ = −0.5 with ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ, which makes ρt increase in t. Standard SVI requires κ ∈ (0.5, 1]. This undermines the claimed convergence guarantees and should be corrected.
  - Some key details are deferred to a missing Table 1 and Appendix, hindering reproducibility (e.g., exact conditional probabilities, natural gradient forms).

Quality
Technically, the high-level ideas are sound and aligned with established SVI practice and parameter sharing strategies, but there are nontrivial gaps: the randomized local family and step-size typo cast doubt on the theoretical claims; clarity issues in the updates; limited and potentially unfair baselines; and reliance on a non-generative objective.

Clarity
Generally clear, but critical derivations are elided, and there are likely typos in core equations and the step-size schedule. The paper would benefit from a more careful presentation of the local update, the exact lower bound being optimized, and the computational cost of link prediction.

Originality
Moderate. The triangular representation and structured mean-field over triangle roles are known; the main novelty is the specific O(K) parameter sharing and the O(K) local approximation. This is an incremental but potentially useful contribution.

Significance
If validated and corrected, the scalability gains are attractive. However, given the methodological concerns and evaluation gaps, the current evidence does not yet meet the bar for NeurIPS.

Suggestions for improvement
- Fix the step-size schedule and provide a convergence analysis appropriate for the randomized local variational family, or remove the claim of standard SVI guarantees and justify empirically.
- Correct and clarify the local update equations; include the full Table 1 and Appendix derivations.
- Add ablations: effect of parameter sharing vs. MMTM, size of A in the mixture-of-deltas, δ-subsampling sensitivity, and seeding sensitivity.
- Strengthen baselines (include a-MMSB and other established methods) and ensure fair resource usage.
- Clarify link prediction computation and report standard metrics with confidence intervals.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability problem with a simple, implementable approach.
- Promising empirical speed and convergence behaviors; large-scale demonstration.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical and algorithmic correctness concerns (step-size, randomized variational family).
- Expressivity limitations of the O(K) parameterization not thoroughly quantified.
- Evaluation and baselines are insufficient to substantiate superiority.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues (missing details, likely typos).

Recommendation
At this stage, I lean to reject. With the above issues addressed and a stronger, fair empirical comparison, the contribution could be impactful.
