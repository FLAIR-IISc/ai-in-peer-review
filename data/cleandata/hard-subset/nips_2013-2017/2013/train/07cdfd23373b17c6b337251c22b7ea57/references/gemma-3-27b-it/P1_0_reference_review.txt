## Peer Review for "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the crucial problem of scalable probabilistic latent space inference in large networks, a bottleneck for many network analysis tasks. The authors argue that current methods fall short due to limitations in network representation, statistical modeling, and inference algorithms. They propose a novel approach, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), building upon the bag-of-triangles representation. The PTM achieves scalability through parameter sharing, reducing the complexity from O(K^3) to O(K), and utilizes a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm for efficient variational inference with a complexity of O(NK). The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on both synthetic and real-world networks, showcasing significant speedups compared to existing methods, including a compelling 1M-node YouTube network analysis completed in 4 hours.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper positions itself well within the existing landscape of latent space network modeling. It correctly identifies the limitations of MMSB and a-MMSB regarding scalability due to their reliance on the adjacency matrix. It also builds upon the work of Ho et al. (2017) with the MMTM, highlighting its scalability issues stemming from the high-dimensional triangle-generating parameters.  The authors effectively differentiate their approach by addressing all three principles – succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference – which previous work has only tackled partially.  The citation of relevant work appears comprehensive, including foundational papers in network modeling (Granovetter, Newman), variational inference (Hoffman & Blei), and stochastic gradient methods (Bottou).

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability:** This is the primary strength. The O(NK) complexity is a significant advance, allowing analysis of networks previously intractable for probabilistic latent space methods. The empirical results demonstrating this scalability are compelling.
* **Novelty:** The parsimonious triangular model, with its parameter sharing strategy, is a genuinely novel contribution. It cleverly addresses the dimensionality issue that plagued previous approaches.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear derivation of the PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm. The use of natural gradients is a theoretically justified optimization technique.
* **Empirical Validation:** The extensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison with existing algorithms is thorough and highlights the performance gains.
* **Clarity of Presentation:**  Despite the technical complexity, the paper is generally well-written and organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the key ideas and motivating the design choices.
* **Structured Mean-Field Approximation:** The rejection of a naive mean-field approach, coupled with the explanation of why it fails, is a valuable insight.  The chosen structured approach appears well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Triangle Representation Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the triangular representation loses some network information. While the authors claim minimal performance degradation, a more detailed discussion of the trade-offs involved in this representation would be beneficial.  Specifically, are there network structures where the triangular representation performs significantly worse?
* **Subsampling Parameter Sensitivity:** The choice of the subsampling parameter δ (δ=50) seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of the impact of δ on performance and runtime would strengthen the analysis.
* **Mini-Batch Size:** The authors use a 10% mini-batch size. Exploring other mini-batch sizes and their effect on convergence speed and final accuracy would be valuable.
* **Initialization Dependence:** While the authors attempt to mitigate initialization sensitivity, it remains a potential concern. More robust initialization strategies or techniques to assess the robustness of the results would be helpful.
* **Lack of Comparison to Deep Learning Approaches:** While the paper rightly focuses on probabilistic latent space methods, a brief discussion of how this work compares to more recent deep learning-based network embedding techniques (e.g., Graph Neural Networks) would be beneficial for contextualizing the contribution.  Are there scenarios where PTM outperforms these methods?



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant bottleneck in network analysis: scalable latent space inference.
* The proposed PTM offers a genuinely novel and theoretically sound solution.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate substantial performance gains over existing methods.
* The paper is well-written and clearly explains the key ideas and contributions.
* The work has the potential to impact a wide range of applications, including community detection, link prediction, and exploratory network analysis.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The triangular representation introduces a potential loss of information, requiring a more thorough discussion of its limitations.
* The sensitivity of the method to parameters like δ and mini-batch size needs further investigation.
* The lack of comparison to deep learning-based methods leaves a gap in the contextualization of the work.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed PTM is a clever and effective solution to the problem of scalable latent space inference. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper clearly outweigh them.  I **strongly recommend acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the discussion of the triangular representation limitations and the sensitivity analysis of key parameters.

**Confidence:** 9/10
