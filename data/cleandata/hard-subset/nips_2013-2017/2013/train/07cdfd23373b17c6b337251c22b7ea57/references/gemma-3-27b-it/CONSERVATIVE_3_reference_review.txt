This paper proposes a new probabilistic latent space model, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), and a stochastic variational inference algorithm designed for scalability to very large networks. The core idea is to represent networks using triangular motifs, combined with parameter sharing to reduce the dimensionality of the model, and a stochastic gradient ascent approach for inference. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of existing methods (MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM) in scaling to networks with millions of nodes and a large number of latent roles.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-articulated, and the technical details are presented in a logical manner. The authors do a good job of positioning their work within the existing literature and explaining the trade-offs involved in their design choices. However, while the description of the method is clear, the justification for some of the design choices (e.g., the specific way parameters are shared) feels somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from further theoretical grounding.

My primary concern with this paper is the lack of compelling evidence that the proposed method represents a significant advance over existing approaches. While the authors claim substantial speedups compared to MMTM and MMSB, the experimental evaluation is limited. The synthetic experiments, while thorough, don’t fully demonstrate superiority—the improvement is primarily in runtime, and the reported NMI scores are competitive but not drastically better than alternatives. The real-world experiments focus on runtime comparisons and lack detailed analysis of the quality of the recovered latent spaces. The claim of “competitive or improved accuracy” feels overstated given the evidence presented. Specifically, the comparison to a-MMSB, which is identified as a strong baseline, is missing. The authors state a-MMSB isn’t fast enough for their largest network but don’t report any runtime comparisons on networks where both methods could be applied. Furthermore, the performance gain on Brightkite and Stanford is presented only in terms of runtime; demonstrating similar or improved latent space recovery quality would be essential. 

The parameter sharing strategy, while intuitively appealing for scalability, introduces a potentially strong inductive bias. The paper doesn't adequately address the potential consequences of this bias on the accuracy of the inferred latent spaces. What happens if the underlying network structure doesn't align well with the parameter sharing scheme? Is there a theoretical justification for why this sharing is beneficial in general, or is it simply an empirical observation? The reliance on δ-subsampling introduces another potential source of approximation error, and a more careful analysis of its impact on performance would be beneficial.

**Strengths:**

*   Addresses an important problem: scalability of probabilistic latent space models for large networks.
*   Clearly written and well-organized.
*   Novel combination of triangular motif representation, parameter sharing, and stochastic variational inference.
*   Demonstrates significant speedups compared to existing methods on larger networks.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Limited experimental evaluation. Missing comparison to a-MMSB and insufficient analysis of latent space quality.
*   Lack of theoretical justification for parameter sharing scheme.
*   Potential impact of δ-subsampling on accuracy not adequately addressed.
*   Claims of “competitive or improved accuracy” are not fully supported by the evidence.
*   The reliance on specific initialization conditions to avoid local minima in Gibbs sampling for baseline methods raises concerns about the robustness of the comparison.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a relevant and important problem in network analysis.
*   The proposed method offers a significant improvement in scalability compared to existing approaches.
*   The technical details are well-presented and the paper is clearly written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation is limited and doesn’t provide strong evidence of improved accuracy.
*   The parameter sharing strategy lacks theoretical justification.
*   The potential impact of approximations (δ-subsampling) on performance is not adequately addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

I am currently leaning towards **reject**. While the paper presents a technically sound method with a clear motivation, the lack of compelling experimental evidence and theoretical justification for key design choices prevents me from recommending acceptance at this time. The speedups are valuable, but they need to be accompanied by evidence that the accuracy of the recovered latent spaces is not significantly compromised. I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above by conducting a more thorough experimental evaluation, providing a theoretical justification for their parameter sharing scheme, and carefully analyzing the impact of their approximations. A direct comparison to a-MMSB on networks where both methods can be run efficiently is crucial.
