

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper proposes a "Parsimonious Triangular Model" (PTM) for scalable latent space inference in large networks. The authors argue that existing methods fail to simultaneously achieve scalability and accuracy due to limitations in network representation, statistical modeling, and inference algorithms. They propose representing networks as a bag of triangular motifs (instead of adjacency matrices) and introduce a parameter-sharing scheme to reduce the triangle-generating parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K), followed by a stochastic variational inference algorithm with claimed O(NK) complexity. The authors claim their method can handle 1M-node networks in 4 hours on a single machine.

The paper builds on prior work, particularly the triangular representation from [8] and the a-MMSB approach from [5]. The key novelty appears to be combining these ideas with a parameter-sharing scheme and a stochastic inference algorithm. However, the paper's main claims require careful scrutiny.

**Quality concerns:**

The most significant issue is the claimed time complexity. The paper states the algorithm has "time complexity O(NK)", but the actual complexity is O(Nδ^2K), where δ is the subsampling threshold. The authors note they choose δ > median degree, but for many real networks (e.g., power-law networks), median degree grows with N, making the complexity effectively O(N^2) rather than O(N). This undermines their scalability claims for networks where degree grows with size.

The parameter sharing scheme (reducing from O(K^3) to O(K)) is theoretically sound but may be too restrictive. For example, their grouping of role triplets (case 2: "only two role indices exhibit the same state x") shares parameters across different minority roles, which may not be justified in real networks where different minority roles likely have distinct behaviors. The paper fails to provide evidence that this simplification doesn't degrade accuracy.

The experimental validation is insufficient. The authors compare against MMSB (O(N^2K^2)), MMTM Gibbs (O(Nδ^2K^3)), and PTM Gibbs (O(Nδ^2K^3)), but omit the most relevant baseline: the a-MMSB algorithm from [5], which they cite as the state-of-the-art for large networks. They claim a-MMSB "is not fast enough to handle networks with N ≥ 100,000" but don't provide direct comparisons. Their synthetic experiments only go up to N=10,000, while their real-world claims are for N=1M networks—this gap is not adequately addressed.

The initialization procedure is highly unrealistic. The authors seed 2% of nodes with their true roles, which is infeasible for real applications. Their proposed alternative ("rank nodes by number of 3-triangles") is not tested in experiments.

**Clarity:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The technical details of the model and inference algorithm are presented with sufficient mathematical rigor. The experimental setup is described with reasonable detail, though some implementation specifics (e.g., exact hardware for real-world experiments) are missing.

**Originality:**

The bag-of-triangles representation builds directly on prior work [8], and the parameter sharing scheme resembles the a-MMSB approach [5]. The main contribution is combining these ideas with a stochastic inference algorithm, but the novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The paper doesn't sufficiently clarify how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods.

**Significance:**

The scalability claims are potentially significant, but they're not convincingly supported by the evidence provided. The paper fails to demonstrate that their method actually achieves the claimed speedup on networks approaching 1M nodes. The comparison against the most relevant baseline (a-MMSB) is missing, making it impossible to assess whether the claimed speedup is real or an artifact of the experimental setup.

**Strengths:**

- The paper identifies a genuine challenge in scalable network analysis.
- The parameter-sharing scheme is theoretically sound.
- The experimental results on synthetic data are consistent with the claimed improvements over MMSB and MMTM.
- The stochastic variational inference approach is a reasonable adaptation of established methods.

**Weaknesses:**

- Overstated complexity claims (O(NK) instead of O(Nδ^2K)).
- Inadequate comparison against the most relevant baseline (a-MMSB).
- Unrealistic initialization procedure.
- Limited experimental validation on networks approaching 1M nodes.
- Insufficient justification for the parameter-sharing scheme's validity in real networks.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents an incremental improvement on prior work in network analysis. While the parameter-sharing scheme is theoretically sound, the paper's scalability claims are not sufficiently validated. The claimed O(NK) complexity is misleading, and the experimental evidence does not convincingly demonstrate the claimed speedup on networks of the scale the authors claim to handle. The omission of the most relevant baseline (a-MMSB) is particularly troubling. Without more rigorous validation of the scalability claims and a comparison against the state-of-the-art, the paper does not provide compelling evidence to support its central claims.

I recommend rejection. The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Correcting the complexity claims to O(Nδ^2K)
2. Adding direct comparisons against a-MMSB on networks of size N ≥ 100,000
3. Providing a more realistic initialization method and evaluating its performance
4. Conducting experiments on networks closer to 1M nodes

The paper's contribution is interesting but lacks the rigorous validation required for NIPS. The claims about scalability are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the paper does not clearly demonstrate how it advances the state of the art in a demonstrable way.
