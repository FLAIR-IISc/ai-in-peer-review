This paper presents a novel approach to scalable latent space inference for large-scale networks, building upon the bag-of-triangles representation and introducing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) with a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm. The core idea – reducing the parameter space and leveraging stochastic optimization – is a valuable contribution to a field plagued by scalability issues. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing methods like MMSB, a-MMSB, and MMTM, and convincingly argue for the need for a model that simultaneously addresses succinct network representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference.

However, despite the promising premise and substantial effort, I find myself leaning towards rejection due to a multitude of minor, yet persistent, flaws that collectively detract from the paper’s overall rigor and clarity. These are not necessarily individually devastating, but their accumulation creates a concerning impression of sloppiness and a lack of meticulousness.

**Strengths:**

* **Scalability Focus:** Addressing scalability in latent space modeling is crucial, and the paper rightly emphasizes this.
* **Parsimonious Modeling:** The parameter sharing strategy in the PTM is a clever way to reduce dimensionality and improve scalability.
* **Stochastic Optimization:** Utilizing stochastic natural gradient ascent is appropriate for handling large datasets.
* **Experimental Validation:** The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method can handle larger networks and achieve competitive results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Notational Inconsistency:** Throughout the paper, there are subtle inconsistencies in notation. For example, the definition of *Eijk* changes slightly between sections 2 and 3, causing confusion. While minor, these inconsistencies accumulate.
* **Lack of Rigorous Justification for Approximations:** The "O(K) approximation" to the local update, while motivated by computational constraints, lacks a thorough justification. The authors state it "works as well," but provide limited empirical evidence demonstrating the impact of this approximation on accuracy *beyond* the synthetic data, which is presented quite favorably. The effect of choosing different *A* sets is glossed over.
* **Vague Description of Hyperparameter Tuning:** The selection of hyperparameters (α, λ, step size parameters) is not adequately explained.  The authors mention values used, but lack discussion on sensitivity analysis or the rationale for choosing those specific parameters.
* **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** While MMSB is discussed, a more comprehensive comparison with more recent, scalable network modeling approaches (beyond those explicitly criticized) is missing.  There's no discussion of how PTM compares to methods leveraging graph neural networks, for instance.
* **Unclear Presentation of Algorithm 1:** Algorithm 1 is quite high-level, and crucial details of the stochastic natural gradient update (specifically equations (6) and (7) mentioned as references) are relegated to the Appendix. This makes it difficult to fully grasp the algorithmic flow.
* **Minor Typographical Errors & Grammatical Issues:** The paper contains several minor typographical errors (e.g., “fundamentally, however,” in the introduction) and awkward phrasing, which detract from readability.
* **Overstated Claims:** Statements like “orders of magnitude faster” require precise quantification, which is not always provided.  The comparison to Gopalan et al. and Ho et al. feels somewhat selective.
* **Redundancy:** Certain concepts are repeated excessively throughout the paper, particularly the motivation for the PTM.

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a critical problem (scalability) in network modeling.
* Presents a novel and potentially effective approach (PTM and stochastic optimization).
* Demonstrates promising results on synthetic and real datasets.

**Con:**

* Numerous minor flaws (notational inconsistencies, vague justifications, lack of rigorous analysis of approximations).
* Insufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods.
* Overstated claims and imprecise language.
* Lack of detailed algorithmic description.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a potentially valuable contribution, but the multitude of minor flaws and the lack of complete rigor diminish its impact. While the authors demonstrate that their method *can* work, they haven’t fully convinced me that it *reliably* works and that it represents a significant advance over existing techniques. The overall impression is one of rushed execution, with a focus on achieving scalability at the expense of meticulousness. 

I recommend rejection.  The authors should address the concerns outlined above, focusing on improving the clarity, rigor, and completeness of their presentation before resubmission.  A more thorough comparison with other scalable network modeling approaches is essential, along with a more detailed analysis of the approximations made in the algorithm. A meticulous proofread to eliminate notational inconsistencies and typographical errors is also necessary.
