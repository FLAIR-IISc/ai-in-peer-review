This paper addresses the critical challenge of scaling probabilistic latent space models for network analysis to graphs with millions of nodes and a large number of latent roles (K). The authors propose a novel approach, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), and a corresponding stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm, which together achieve impressive scalability and performance.

The core idea builds upon three principles: (1) using a succinct bag-of-triangles representation of the network, as in the prior MMTM model, which avoids the O(N²) cost of adjacency matrices; (2) introducing a parsimonious statistical model (PTM) that reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K³) in MMTM to O(K) through parameter sharing; and (3) developing a scalable SVI algorithm with a novel O(K) approximation for local updates, bringing the overall time complexity to a manageable O(NK) per pass (after triangle extraction).

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed PTM model is a well-motivated simplification of the MMTM, drawing inspiration from similar parsimonious assumptions in models like a-MMSB. The derivation of the variational inference framework is standard, but the key contribution lies in the "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local updates. This approximation is crucial for scalability, as it avoids an O(K³) bottleneck within the SVI loop. The claims of scalability and accuracy are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments on both synthetic and large-scale real-world networks. The authors are honest about the limitations of the model, noting that it is not a true generative model for networks and does not enforce edge consistency across triangles. The experimental setup is fair, particularly the use of a shared, seeded initialization to provide a level playing field for the different locally-optimal algorithms.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts by clearly motivating the problem and articulating the three principles that guide the work. The description of the PTM is lucid, contrasting it effectively with the MMTM to highlight the key innovation in parameterization. The inference section logically presents the structured mean-field approach, the stochastic optimization framework, and the crucial O(K) approximation. The experiments are presented clearly with informative plots and tables, making the performance gains immediately apparent. The description is detailed enough that an expert reader should be able to grasp the core mechanics and potentially reproduce the work.

**Originality:** The work combines several existing ideas in a novel and powerful way. While the use of triangular representations (from MMTM) and parsimonious parameter sharing (conceptually similar to a-MMSB) are not new in isolation, their synthesis in the context of a highly scalable SVI algorithm is a significant contribution. The most original component is arguably the "mixture-of-deltas" O(K) approximation to the local variational update. This is a clever algorithmic trick that directly addresses the primary computational bottleneck of applying SVI to a model with O(K³) latent configurations per data point. This specific contribution is what unlocks the scalability for large K. The paper clearly delineates how it builds upon and improves MMTM and a-MMSB, with adequate referencing of related work.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is very high. The inability of most probabilistic network models to scale to massive real-world graphs has been a major barrier to their practical application. This paper provides a concrete and effective solution, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups over recent state-of-the-art methods. The ability to infer 100 latent roles on a million-node network in just a few hours on a single machine is a substantial leap forward. This will likely enable researchers and practitioners to apply mixed-membership models to a much wider range of problems in social network analysis, biology, and other domains. The ideas presented here—particularly the combination of parsimonious modeling and approximated local updates in an SVI framework—are likely to be influential and built upon in future work on scalable probabilistic modeling.

**Strengths:**
*   **Massive Scalability:** The primary strength is the demonstrated ability to scale to million-node networks and a large number of roles (K=100), a task that was previously infeasible for similar models. The runtime improvements are not incremental but represent a leap of orders of magnitude.
*   **Novel Algorithmic Contribution:** The O(K) approximation for the local update is a clever and crucial innovation that enables the overall scalability of the inference procedure.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental section covering role recovery on synthetic data, link prediction, and convergence on large real-world networks. The comparison against relevant baselines (MMTM, MMSB) is convincing.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very easy to follow, with the motivation, model, and algorithm presented in a logical and compelling manner.

**Weaknesses and Questions for Authors:**
*   **Implications of Not Being a True Generative Model:** The authors correctly state that PTM is not a generative model of a network graph due to the potential for inconsistent edges across triangles. This point could be elaborated upon. What are the theoretical and practical implications of this? Does this limitation affect the interpretation of the latent space or the model parameters in any fundamental way?
*   **Details of the O(K) Approximation:** The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation is presented as sampling O(K) random off-diagonal role combinations. The performance seems excellent, but this feels somewhat heuristic. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the results to the number and method of choosing these combinations? Would a more principled approach, such as beam search for the most likely combinations, offer further improvements?
*   **Link Prediction Mechanism:** The description of the link prediction task for the PTM is brief. It states that links i-j are ranked by marginalizing over all choices of a third node k. For a large network, iterating through all N-2 nodes for k seems computationally prohibitive. Could the authors clarify if an approximation or a more efficient computation was used here?

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and practical contribution to the field of network analysis. It presents a well-motivated model and a novel, highly scalable inference algorithm, backed by strong empirical evidence. The weaknesses are minor and mostly relate to requests for clarification that would further strengthen the manuscript. The work directly addresses a major bottleneck in the field and provides a tool that many researchers are likely to use and build upon.

**Recommendation:** Strong Accept.
