

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for scalable latent space inference in large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation and a parameter-sharing scheme to reduce the model's complexity from O(K³) to O(K). The authors claim their stochastic variational inference algorithm achieves O(NK) complexity, enabling analysis of million-node networks in hours.

**Strengths:** The paper addresses a genuine scalability challenge in network analysis, and the use of triangular motifs is a reasonable approach to reduce data representation. The experimental results on synthetic and real networks demonstrate significant speed improvements over prior work, with competitive accuracy. The authors provide a well-structured comparison with relevant baselines (MMSB, MMTM).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Fundamental Notation Errors:** The paper contains critical inconsistencies in simplex notation. The authors claim "B0 ∈ ∆1" and "Bxx ∈ ∆2" but then refer to B0 having two components (B0,1 and B0,2) and Bxx having three components (Bxx,1, Bxx,2, Bxx,3). A 1-dimensional simplex can only represent a single probability (not two), and a 2-dimensional simplex can only represent two probabilities (not three). This suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of simplex notation that undermines the model's theoretical foundation.

2. **Inconsistent Complexity Claims:** The paper claims O(NK) complexity for their inference algorithm, but their runtime results (Section 5.2) show that for N=10,000, their method takes ~10x longer than for N=5,000. If K = O(N) and the complexity were truly O(NK), it should scale as O(N²), meaning the runtime should increase by approximately 4x (not 10x) when N doubles. This discrepancy is unexplained.

3. **Missing Details on Key Approximations:** The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation (Section 4) is described as working "as well as the full parameterization" but without evidence. The authors don't compare their method to the full PTM (without the O(K) approximation) in experiments. The set A of triples is chosen randomly, but the paper doesn't specify how this randomness is controlled or why it avoids bias, despite claiming "re-pick A every time."

4. **Inconsistent Terminology:** The paper uses "triangular motifs" and "triangles" interchangeably, which is confusing since "triangular motifs" specifically refer to three-node subgraphs with particular edge patterns, while "triangles" typically refer to three-node cliques (three edges). This inconsistency appears throughout the paper.

5. **Incomplete Experimental Details:** The runtime claims (e.g., "4 hours for 1M-node network") lack critical hardware specifications (CPU, memory, number of threads). The initialization method ("seed 2% of nodes with true roles") is poorly described—how are these roles chosen, and how does this affect the results? The paper states they use "δ = 50" but doesn't explain why this value is optimal or how it was selected.

6. **Theoretical Oversight:** The paper claims "any choice of A yields a valid lower bound" but provides no proof or reference. This is a non-trivial claim that requires justification in a theoretical machine learning paper.

7. **Inconsistency in Model Complexity:** In Section 5.2, the authors claim PTM Gibbs has "O(Nδ²K³) time complexity," but since PTM is claimed to have O(K) parameters (not O(K³)), this complexity should be O(Nδ²K), not O(Nδ²K³). This suggests a fundamental misunderstanding of their own model's complexity.

8. **Misuse of Latent Space Terminology:** The paper uses "latent space" incorrectly to describe a discrete set of roles (a "latent membership" model), rather than a continuous embedding space. This is a significant conceptual misalignment with standard network analysis terminology.

**Overall Assessment:** While the paper addresses an important problem and shows promising empirical results, the fundamental notation errors, inconsistent complexity claims, and missing theoretical details undermine the technical credibility of the work. The paper's claims about scalability and model structure are inconsistent with the presented methodology and results. The errors are not merely minor formatting issues but represent misunderstandings of core mathematical concepts that affect the paper's validity.

The paper would benefit significantly from a complete revision to address these theoretical inconsistencies, provide complete experimental details, and clarify the terminology. As written, the technical flaws are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's potentially valuable application. The authors should first ensure the mathematical foundation is sound before proceeding with empirical claims.
