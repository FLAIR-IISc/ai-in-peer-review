**Summary:**
This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a probabilistic latent space model for large-scale network analysis. The work builds directly upon the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) by Ho et al. (2013), adopting its unconventional "bag-of-triangles" network representation. The key contributions are twofold: first, a parameter reduction scheme that shrinks the MMTM's O(K³) triangle-generating tensor to O(K), inspired by a similar idea in a-MMSB; and second, a scalable stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with an O(K) approximate local update, resulting in an overall time complexity of O(NK). The authors demonstrate impressive scalability, running the model on a million-node network with 100 latent roles in a few hours on a single machine, and show strong performance on synthetic latent space recovery tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Demonstrated Scalability:** The primary strength of this work is its empirical demonstration of scalability. The ability to infer a 100-role latent space on a million-node network in four hours is a significant engineering achievement. The runtime results, particularly the comparisons to cited runtimes of MMTM and a-MMSB, are compelling and address a well-known and important bottleneck in probabilistic network modeling.

2.  **Parsimonious Model:** The parameter-sharing strategy to reduce the triangle-generating tensor from O(K³) to O(K) is a well-motivated and sensible extension of ideas from prior work (e.g., a-MMSB). This not only enables the scalable inference algorithm but also, as the authors suggest, may improve statistical efficiency and prevent overfitting, especially when K is large.

3.  **Efficient Inference Algorithm:** The development of a stochastic natural gradient ascent algorithm is appropriate for this problem scale. The O(K) approximation for the local variational update is a clever and necessary step to achieve the claimed overall complexity, avoiding the O(K³) bottleneck that would otherwise render the SVI approach impractical.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Fundamental Reliance on an Unconventional Representation:** My primary concern lies with the foundational choice of the bag-of-triangles representation. This approach, inherited from MMTM, discards a significant amount of network information, namely all isolated edges and the broader connectivity patterns beyond 3-cliques. The authors claim this leads to "little or no degradation of performance," but this is a strong claim supported by limited evidence. This representation seems tailored for networks with high clustering coefficients where triadic closure is the dominant structural feature. For sparse networks, or networks where dyads and longer paths are more informative, this model is fundamentally misspecified. The paper would be much stronger if it included a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this representation and characterized the types of networks for which it is (and is not) suitable.

2.  **Lack of a True Generative Process:** The authors correctly state that PTM is not a generative model of networks, as the process does not guarantee that the generated triangles form a consistent graph. This is a significant theoretical weakness compared to established models like the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB), which defines a valid probability distribution over the adjacency matrix. While the authors' goal is inference, not simulation, this limitation undermines the probabilistic coherence of the model and makes its interpretation less clear. It operates on a pre-processed summary statistic of the graph, not the graph itself.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The experimental validation, while impressive in scale, is lacking in rigor regarding baseline comparisons. The most relevant and direct competitor for a scalable latent space model is the a-MMSB [5], which is discussed in the introduction. However, a-MMSB is conspicuously absent from the head-to-head comparisons for latent space recovery (Figure 1) and link prediction (Table 2). The authors only compare their runtime on Brightkite to the one reported in the a-MMSB paper, which is not a direct, controlled comparison. To convincingly claim state-of-the-art performance, a direct empirical comparison against a-MMSB on both accuracy and runtime under identical conditions is essential. The current baselines (batch MMSB, MMTM Gibbs) are known to be unscalable, so outperforming them on runtime is expected and does not sufficiently validate the proposed method against current scalable alternatives.

4.  **Concerns about Experimental Protocol:**
    *   **Initialization:** The synthetic experiments rely on providing the algorithms with a "seeded" initialization using a small number of ground truth labels. This is an unrealistic assumption for most real-world applications and may mask convergence issues. The performance of stochastic optimization methods can be highly sensitive to initialization. A more convincing evaluation would demonstrate robustness to random initializations, perhaps using the strategy mentioned in footnote 3 and evaluating it empirically. The current setup makes it difficult to assess whether the superior NMI of PTM is due to the model itself or its convergence properties under favorable starting conditions.
    *   **Link Prediction Methodology:** The description of the link prediction task is brief. Ranking links by marginalizing over all possible third nodes `k` seems computationally prohibitive, scaling as O(N) for every candidate edge. It is unclear how this was implemented for the large networks in Table 2. More detail is needed to assess the practicality and validity of this evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a significant engineering contribution to scaling a specific class of latent space models. The runtime and scalability results are impressive. However, the work is built on a questionable and specialized network representation and lacks a proper generative story. More importantly, the experimental evaluation falls short of the rigorous standards expected at NeurIPS by omitting a direct comparison with the most relevant state-of-the-art scalable baseline (a-MMSB) and by using a potentially confounding seeded initialization protocol.

While the scalability is a major achievement, the methodological and empirical weaknesses prevent a confident endorsement. The paper advances a very specific approach but does not provide sufficient evidence to establish its general utility or its superiority over other established scalable methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Demonstrates impressive, orders-of-magnitude speedup for latent space inference on very large graphs.
*   The parsimonious modeling and approximate SVI algorithm are solid technical contributions.
*   Addresses the important and challenging problem of scalability in network analysis.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The model relies on a non-standard "bag-of-triangles" representation that discards significant network information, limiting its general applicability.
*   The model is not a valid generative process for networks, weakening its theoretical foundation.
*   Crucial experimental comparisons to the state-of-the-art scalable baseline (a-MMSB) are missing.
*   Synthetic experiments use a seeded initialization, which may inflate performance and obscure issues with convergence from random starts.
