Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a probabilistic latent space model for large networks that operates on a bag-of-triangles representation. Building on prior work that advocates triadic statistics for scalable representation (e.g., MMTM; Ho et al. NIPS’12), the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which shares parameters across classes of triadic role configurations to reduce the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) to O(K). They derive a structured variational inference algorithm with stochastic natural gradient updates. To avoid O(K^3) local updates, they propose a “mixture-of-deltas” variational family that restricts mass to K diagonal and O(K) randomly sampled off-diagonal role triples, yielding O(K) per-triangle cost. They combine this with node-centric δ-subsampling of triangles. Empirically, they report faster convergence and competitive or better accuracy than MMSB (batch variational), MMTM (Gibbs), and a PTM Gibbs variant on synthetic data and several real networks, including 1M-node YouTube.

This paper sits at the intersection of MMSB-style mixed membership models (Airoldi et al.), scalable SVI (Hoffman et al.), and motif-based network modeling (triangles in ERGMs and MMTM). The parameter sharing idea is reminiscent of a-MMSB’s inter-/intra-community collapse to O(K) from O(K^2), adapted here to the triadic setting.

Strengths
- Clear scalability motivation: moving from edges to triangles reduces data size for sparse networks, and the O(K) parameterization is attractive for large K.
- The structured variational formulation (joint per-triangle role factors q(x,y,z)) is better founded than naive mean-field for triads.
- The O(K) local approximation yields practical speedups and fits naturally with SVI and mini-batching.
- Empirical results show large wall-clock gains and convergence in a few passes; handling 1M nodes with K=100 on a single machine is practically relevant.
- The paper is generally well written and organized; the high-level ideas are easy to follow.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling expressiveness: The PTM’s parameter sharing assumes all “two-equal-one-different” configurations share the same Bxx regardless of the minority role, and all “all-different” share B0. This is a strong restriction that removes role-specific triadic patterns. The paper does not quantify the loss in fit vs. MMTM nor provide diagnostics demonstrating that this simplification is harmless beyond a few tasks/datasets.
- Approximation gap and guarantees: The “mixture-of-deltas” local variational family changes the objective (tightness of the ELBO). The paper asserts unbiasedness of stochastic gradients due to minibatching but does not clarify that this unbiasedness holds only for the constrained objective. There is no analysis of variance or convergence behavior introduced by re-sampling the set A each iteration, nor an empirical ablation comparing exact vs. approximate local updates on moderate K.
- Evaluation breadth and fairness: Baselines are limited to MMSB (batch var), MMTM Gibbs, and PTM Gibbs. More established, stronger baselines for link prediction and latent space recovery (e.g., degree-corrected blockmodels, spectral/tensor methods for triads, nonparametric MMSB variants) are not included. For link prediction, comparing an edge-based model (MMSB) to a triangle-based scoring rule may be unfavorable to MMSB; modern embedding methods are absent. The synthetic setup sets K=N/100, making baselines with O(K^2)/O(K^3) parameters scale poorly by design; this needs complementing with fixed-K regimes.
- Use of δ-subsampling: The paper relies heavily on δ-subsampling to claim O(NK) scalability, but the effect on bias/variance of estimates and on downstream link prediction is not analyzed; sensitivity plots w.r.t. δ are needed.
- Metric choices: The latent recovery evaluation thresholds θ at 0.125 and uses NMI on binarized roles. This choice can distort differences between algorithms that produce calibrated posteriors vs. sparse/multi-modal ones. Alternatives (AUC-PR on role supports, per-node precision/recall, calibration plots) would be more informative.
- Link prediction with a non-network generative model: PTM does not define a generative model over edges, yet the method proposes a heuristic ranking via marginalizing triangles. The mismatch between the model and the prediction task deserves more justification and head-to-head evaluation against established link predictors.
- Reproducibility details: Critical pieces are deferred to the appendix (e.g., exact local updates for different equivalence classes; dataset preprocessing; hyperparameter sensitivity). No code or exact hardware specs are provided; a-MMSB comparisons are via numbers from prior work with potentially different preprocessing.

Quality
Technically competent in its use of SVI and structured variational approximations, but key claims (unbiasedness under approximation, negligible loss of expressiveness) are not rigorously supported. Empirical results are promising but limited in baseline coverage and ablations.

Clarity
Generally clear, but important derivations are deferred; Table 1 and appendix details are essential for full reproducibility. The link prediction scoring rule should be specified precisely (computational cost, approximations).

Originality
The parameter sharing for triads and the O(K) local approximation are novel adaptations of known ideas (a-MMSB-style parsimony, SVI) to triangle models. The contribution is more on engineering/scalability than on fundamentally new modeling.

Significance
If validated more broadly, the approach could be useful for large-scale role discovery. As is, the significance is constrained by the modeling restrictions and the limited evaluation against established baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide ablations: exact vs. approximate local updates; with/without δ-subsampling; sensitivity to K and δ; effect of re-sampling A.
- Quantify expressiveness loss vs. MMTM on moderate K with likelihood/perplexity and predictive tasks.
- Include stronger baselines: degree-corrected blockmodels, spectral/tensor triad methods, and at least one strong link prediction baseline.
- Clarify convergence theory under the constrained variational family and random A; correct the unbiasedness claim as needed.
- Justify the link prediction mechanism and report its computational cost.
- Release code and detailed experimental settings.

Pros
- Scalable, practical method; strong wall-clock results.
- Sensible structured variational design and parsimony.
- Clear writing.

Cons
- Strong modeling restrictions with limited validation.
- Approximation and convergence claims insufficiently justified.
- Baseline coverage and evaluation choices weaken the evidence.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The scalability is impressive, but given the heavy reliance on restrictive parameter sharing and approximations, I would require stronger empirical validation (broader baselines, ablations, sensitivity) and clearer theoretical grounding before recommending acceptance. Confidence: high.
