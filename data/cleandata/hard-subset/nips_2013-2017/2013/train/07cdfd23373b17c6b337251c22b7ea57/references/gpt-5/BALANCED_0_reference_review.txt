Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic latent-space method for large networks based on a bag-of-triangles representation. Building on Ho et al. (NIPS’11) who showed that triangles can succinctly capture informative higher-order structure, the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) in MMTM to O(K) via parameter sharing across equivalence classes of role configurations. They then develop a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with (i) grouped variational posterior over per-triangle role triplets, (ii) a mixture-of-deltas approximation that brings local updates from O(K^3) to O(K), and (iii) stochastic natural gradients for global updates. Using δ-subsampling of triangles, mini-batches, and multi-threading, they report convergence on networks with up to 1.1M nodes and 100 roles in hours, outperforming MMSB/a-MMSB (Airoldi et al.; Gopalan et al.) and MMTM (Ho et al.) in speed, with competitive or better latent role recovery and link prediction.

Quality
Strengths
- Sound modeling motivation: triangles encode triadic closure/motifs central to social network structure and ERGM statistics.
- Careful parsimony: the grouping of role-triplet configurations (B0, Bxx, Bxxx) is principled, analogous to the reduction in a-MMSB, and makes global updates O(NK).
- Inference design: structured mean-field for role triplets is appropriate; the mini-batch SVI and natural gradients are standard and effective; the O(K) mixture-of-deltas local approximation is a practical idea.
- Empirical scale: convincing demonstrations up to 1M nodes and K=100; fast convergence in 2–5 passes; clear wall-clock improvements over reported times for a-MMSB and MMTM.
- Practicality: discarding local variational factors and parallelizing over triangles are thoughtful engineering choices.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Modeling expressiveness: PTM’s parameter sharing assumes that all “two-equal-one-different” role configurations behave the same regardless of the minority role identity, and that all-all-different configurations share a single parameter. This could wash out important heterogeneity across roles and limit interpretability or predictive power in heterogeneous graphs. Please analyze/quantify the loss vs. MMTM (e.g., ablation varying the number of “off-diagonal” combinations allowed).
- Approximation and guarantees: The stochastic gradients are unbiased for the objective defined with exact local optimization. After restricting the local family via mixture-of-deltas and re-sampling the set A, the algorithm optimizes a different lower bound. Please (i) state clearly which bound is being optimized, (ii) provide a proof or citation that the resulting natural gradients remain unbiased for that bound, and (iii) add sensitivity experiments to the size/composition of A and to re-sampling frequency, including accuracy–speed trade-offs.
- Evaluation protocol:
  - Synthetic latent-space recovery uses seeding (2% nodes provided with one true role). This is a strong assumption that advantages methods susceptible to local minima. Please include an unsupervised initialization (your heuristic) and/or multiple random restarts, with aggregated results.
  - Baselines: comparisons are primarily to MMSB and MMTM/PTM Gibbs. Given the focus on scalability and link prediction, comparisons to scalable overlapping community/embedding methods (e.g., BigCLAM; spectral or embedding methods) would strengthen the case.
  - Metrics: the link prediction setup is under-specified (metric not reported; dataset table not shown). Please report standard metrics (AUC, precision@k) with confidence intervals, and control for class imbalance. Clarify how triangle-based scores are converted to edge probabilities and whether this induces bias.
- Fairness of runtime comparisons: the proposed method is multi-threaded while baselines are single-threaded; some plots show time per pass rather than total time. Please report both single-thread and multi-thread times for your method, and wall-clock times for all methods where feasible. Also discuss the cost of triangle extraction (often non-trivial) and include it in end-to-end timing or justify its omission with concrete measurements.
- Choice of K and model selection: K is fixed or set proportional to N. Please discuss how to choose K in practice, sensitivity to K, and whether simple criteria (held-out bound or VB model evidence) can guide selection.
- Triangle subsampling: claims that δ-subsampling preserves distributional properties are cited to [8]; please quantify its effect on accuracy and convergence across δ, and report results on high-degree networks where subsampling is most aggressive.
- Reproducibility: several crucial update equations and the equivalence-class table are deferred to the appendix; ensure these are complete. Including memory usage and detailed hyperparameter settings will help replication.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. The intuition behind the parameter sharing and structured variational family is clear. However, some important details (Table 1, local update forms for φxxx and φxxy, objective with O(K) approximation, full evaluation tables) are missing from the main text. Figures referred to in the text need to be visible and readable; include axis labels and units. Please also improve the references formatting and completeness.

Originality
Moderately original. The triangle representation is from prior work [8], and parameter parsimony is inspired by a-MMSB. The key contributions are (i) the particular O(K) parameterization for triangle motifs; (ii) the structured SVI with an O(K) mixture-of-deltas local approximation; and (iii) an engineering blueprint to make triangle-based latent-space inference scale. This constitutes a meaningful, incremental advance.

Significance
High practical significance if results hold: enabling probabilistic latent-space inference with K up to hundreds on million-node graphs is valuable. The approach could be a useful alternative to edge-based MMSB in settings where triangles are abundant and informative. The theoretical contribution is limited, but the scalability and empirical performance can impact practice.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important scalability bottleneck with a principled, parsimonious model.
- Careful inference design that leverages structured variational families and stochastic natural gradients.
- Strong empirical scaling with compelling speedups and acceptable accuracy.
- Builds on and advances a NIPS line of work on triangle-based network modeling.

Arguments against acceptance
- Restrictive parameter sharing may limit expressiveness; limited analysis of its impact.
- Evaluation has potential biases (seeding, baseline coverage, metrics) and missing details.
- Theoretical guarantees for the O(K) local approximation and its stochastic gradients are not fully established.
- Limited comparisons to other scalable community/embedding approaches.

Recommendation: Weak accept
The paper offers a practical and scalable probabilistic approach with solid engineering and promising empirical results on large graphs. With improved evaluation rigor, clearer theoretical framing of the approximation, and more comprehensive baselines, it would be a strong contribution. Even as is, the scalability advance on triangle-based latent-space inference is likely to interest the NeurIPS community.
