## Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the critical scalability challenge in probabilistic latent space inference for large networks. The authors argue that a combination of succinct network representation (bag-of-triangles), parsimonious statistical modeling (sharing parameters in a triangular model), and scalable inference (stochastic natural gradient ascent) is crucial. They propose the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the parameter space compared to existing methods like MMTM, and demonstrate its effectiveness on both synthetic and real-world networks, achieving significant speedups and comparable or improved accuracy.  The authors show a dramatic improvement in scalability, running inference on a 1M-node YouTube network with 100 latent roles in just 4 hours.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper builds upon a strong foundation of prior work in network modeling. It correctly identifies the limitations of existing methods like MMSB and a-MMSB (scalability issues due to edge-based representations) and MMTM (scalability limited by the O(K^3) parameter space). It effectively positions itself as an advancement over these methods, specifically addressing the parameter explosion in MMTM while maintaining a succinct network representation akin to that used in MMTM. The reference to stochastic variational inference, natural gradients, and relevant network statistics (triadic closure, motifs) demonstrates a strong understanding of the field.  The comparison to Gopalan et al. (a-MMSB) and Ho et al. (MMTM) is especially valuable, providing concrete evidence of the proposed method’s improvements.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a Critical Problem:** Scalability remains a significant bottleneck in network analysis. This paper directly tackles this issue with a well-motivated and technically sound approach.
* **Novel Parameter Sharing:** The core contribution of parameter sharing in the PTM is clever and effectively reduces the complexity from O(K^3) to O(K), a substantial improvement.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate both the improved scalability (orders of magnitude faster than competing methods) *and* competitive accuracy. The results on the 1M-node YouTube network are particularly impressive.
* **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the methodology is explained in detail, and the experimental setup is well-described.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of stochastic variational inference with natural gradients is justified and appropriate for the problem.
* **Detailed Ablation Studies:** Comparing PTM against MMTM and MMSB provides a thorough evaluation of the contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Complexity of the Stochastic Natural Gradient Ascent:** While the authors mention the use of natural gradients, the explanation could be more accessible to readers unfamiliar with this technique. A clearer, more intuitive explanation of *why* natural gradients are beneficial in this context would strengthen the paper.
* **Mini-Batch Size Sensitivity:** The choice of 10% mini-batch size is somewhat arbitrary. An analysis of the sensitivity of performance to different mini-batch sizes, and a justification for the chosen value, would be beneficial.
* **δ-Subsampling:** While δ-subsampling is mentioned and referenced, its potential impact on accuracy isn’t fully explored.  A more detailed discussion of how the choice of δ affects the results and how to select an appropriate value for different network characteristics would be valuable.
* **Local Update Approximation:** The O(K) approximation to the local update is crucial for scalability. However, the potential trade-off between accuracy and speed introduced by this approximation could be quantified more rigorously.
* **Limited Network Diversity:** The experiments rely primarily on synthetic and a few publicly available datasets. Evaluation on a broader range of real-world networks with varying characteristics (e.g., different degree distributions, community structures) would strengthen the generalization of the findings.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a crucial problem in network analysis with a novel and effective solution.
* The demonstrated scalability improvements are significant and practically relevant.
* The empirical results are compelling and support the authors' claims.
* The paper is well-written, organized, and grounded in a solid theoretical foundation.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The explanation of the stochastic natural gradient ascent could be more accessible.
* A more thorough analysis of the mini-batch size and δ-subsampling parameters is warranted.
* The evaluation could benefit from a broader range of real-world networks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. The proposed PTM and associated inference algorithm address a critical scalability challenge while maintaining competitive accuracy. While there are minor areas for improvement (particularly concerning clarity of technical details and parameter sensitivity analysis), the strengths of the paper far outweigh its weaknesses.  I believe this paper is well-suited for NIPS.

**Rating:**  Strong Accept.

**Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.  Specifically, I would suggest adding a more detailed explanation of the natural gradient, a discussion on mini-batch size sensitivity, and a slightly more comprehensive evaluation on diverse real-world networks.
