Summary
This paper proposes a new probabilistic latent-space model and scalable inference algorithm for large networks based on a bag-of-triangles representation. The key modeling idea—Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM)—ties together the O(K^3) triangle-generating parameters of prior triangle-based mixed-membership models (e.g., MMTM) into just O(K) parameters by grouping role triplets according to whether all roles are equal, two are equal, or all are distinct, and sharing parameters within each group. On the inference side, the authors develop a structured stochastic variational inference scheme that (i) uses a structured mean-field family over the triplet of per-triangle latent roles rather than naive mean-field; (ii) introduces a “mixture-of-deltas” O(K) approximation to the O(K^3) local updates; and (iii) performs stochastic natural gradient updates over global parameters with triangle mini-batches. Combined with triangle subsampling, the method claims O(NK) global updates and overall practical scalability to million-node graphs and hundreds of roles on a single multi-core machine. Experiments on synthetic and real networks show improved or comparable latent role recovery and link prediction to MMSB and MMTM, with dramatic speedups (e.g., 1M nodes, K=100 in ~4 hours; Brightkite in minutes vs days for a-MMSB).

Relation to prior work
The paper builds directly on MMSB/a-MMSB (edge-based, O(K^2) blocks) and MMTM (triangle-based, O(K^3) tensor). The novelty is (1) the parameter-tying scheme that collapses O(K^3) triangle parameters to O(K) by role-equality pattern, akin in spirit to a-MMSB’s cross-block tying but for triadic motifs; (2) a structured mean-field family for triangle role triplets; and (3) an O(K) local-update approximation via a randomly refreshed set of off-diagonal role triplets, coupled with stochastic natural gradients. The triangle-centric representation connects to ERGM and motif literature; the paper positions itself as a latent-space inference tool rather than a generative network simulator.

Strengths
- Novel and bold modeling simplification: The O(K) parameterization for triads is a compelling conceptual advance that directly addresses the K^3 bottleneck of prior motif models while preserving key triadic distinctions (all-equal, majority/minority, all-distinct).
- Scalable and practical inference: The combination of structured variational family, O(K) local approximation, and natural-gradient mini-batch updates is well-motivated and yields impressive wall-clock performance.
- Clear empirical value: Strong scaling results (up to 1M nodes, 100 roles) with competitive or superior accuracy on latent role recovery and link prediction. Convergence in 2–5 passes is particularly appealing.
- Thoughtful engineering: Triangle subsampling, discarding local variational factors between iterations, and simple parallelization are pragmatic choices that make the approach usable.
- Good ablations vs MMTM Gibbs/PTM Gibbs/MMSB: The experiments illuminate how PTM’s parsimony yields robustness and speed.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Expressivity trade-off: Collapsing all “all-distinct” role triplets into a single B0 and all “two-equal with majority x” patterns into Bxx sacrifices heterogeneity in triadic closure across specific role identities. It is plausible that many networks exhibit role-specific triadic tendencies that this model cannot capture. An empirical ablation showing where this loss of expressivity matters would strengthen the paper.
- Theoretical guarantees under O(K) local approximation: While each restricted variational family yields a valid lower bound, the algorithm re-randomizes the candidate off-diagonals A at each local update. This implies optimization of a sequence of changing bounds; convergence properties (and potential bias in the stochastic natural gradients relative to the maximization of a fixed evidence lower bound) are not clarified. A brief discussion or lemma about convergence to a stationary point of a stationary surrogate objective, or empirical stability diagnostics, would help.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: Link prediction is compared to MMSB, but not to modern scalable embedding or tensor/motif-based methods (e.g., DeepWalk/node2vec/LINE; tensor factorization of 3-node motifs). While the paper’s focus is probabilistic latent-space inference, the predictive claims would be more convincing with these baselines.
- Not a generative network model: As acknowledged, the model ignores empty/single-edge triples and does not enforce edge consistency across overlapping triangles. This limits interpretability for some applications and complicates likelihood-based comparisons to edge-based methods.
- Some details deferred to the appendix: Key update equations for φxxx and φxxy and the exact forms of the natural gradients are postponed; including compact versions in the main text (or a more extensive algorithmic box) would improve reproducibility.
- Triangle availability: In very sparse or low-clustering graphs, the number of triangles may be limited; sensitivity to δ and to networks with few triangles is not deeply explored.

Quality
Overall technically sound and careful. The structured mean-field and natural-gradient derivations follow established variational methods; the local O(K) approximation is plausible and effective in practice, though its theory should be clarified. Experimental protocol is mostly reasonable; initialization fairness is appreciated. Some evaluation choices (e.g., thresholding θ at 0.125 for NMI) could be complemented with continuous measures (AUPRC/AUC for role recovery), but the story remains convincing.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. The explanation of role-equivalence classes and their induced probabilities is intuitive. Minor suggestions: unify notation for Bxxx/Bxx/B0 in a compact table; explicitly state the computational cost per mini-batch iteration; and briefly explain how edge ranking is computed from triadic posteriors (complexity and any approximations).

Originality
High. The parameter-tying scheme for triadic models and the O(K) local update via a mixture-of-deltas variational family strike me as genuinely new and impactful directions.

Significance
High. If widely adopted, this approach could make probabilistic motif-level latent-space inference feasible at industry scales, with likely follow-on work on richer motif families and selective untieing of parameters.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide any convergence statement or empirical evidence (e.g., monotone surrogate bound) for the stochastic optimization with changing A sets?
- How sensitive are results to the size/composition of A? Any guidance on 3K vs 6K vs adaptive schemes?
- Can you show an experiment where role-specific triadic closure (which PTM cannot model) is present, and quantify the gap vs a less-parsimonious model?
- How does performance change on graphs with low clustering (few triangles)? Any fallback to edges/k-stars?

Pros
- Elegant O(K) parameterization for triadic models
- Scalable, parallel, memory-efficient inference with strong wall-clock results
- Solid empirical performance on both recovery and prediction
- Clear path to broader use in large-scale network analysis

Cons
- Potential loss of expressivity; limited analysis of when it hurts
- Convergence theory under changing variational support not fully addressed
- Baselines could be broader for prediction
- Not a full generative model of networks
