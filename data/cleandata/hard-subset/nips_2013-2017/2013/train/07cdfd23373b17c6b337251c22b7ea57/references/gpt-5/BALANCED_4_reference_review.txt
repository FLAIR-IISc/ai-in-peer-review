Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic latent-space model and inference algorithm for large networks. Building on the “bag-of-triangles” representation (Ho et al., NIPS’11), it introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which ties parameters across equivalence classes of role triplets, reducing the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) (as in MMTM) to O(K). The model uses mixed-membership vectors θi for nodes and defines triangle probabilities via shared Dirichlet parameters B0, Bxx, and Bxxx depending on whether role triplets are all-equal, two-equal, or all-distinct. The paper develops a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with stochastic natural gradients; crucially, it uses a “mixture-of-deltas” approximation to the local variational distribution to reduce local updates to O(K) per triangle in a mini-batch. The method supports triangle subsampling (δ-subsampling) to control data size. Experiments show high latent role recovery on synthetic data (MMSB-like and power-law), competitive link prediction vs. MMSB on real networks, and impressive scalability (e.g., 1M nodes, 100 roles in 4 hours on 8 threads). The work relates to MMSB (Airoldi et al., JMLR), a-MMSB (Gopalan et al., NIPS), MMTM (Ho et al., NIPS’11), ERGM notions of higher-order statistics, and SVI (Hoffman et al., JMLR). The parameter-tying idea echoes a-MMSB’s O(K) inter-role parameterization but applied to triangle motifs.

Strengths
- Scalability: The O(K) parameterization and O(K) local updates per triangle, combined with mini-batching and natural gradients, yield excellent wall-clock performance and memory footprint. Demonstrating 1M-node, K=100 runs on a single machine is compelling.
- Algorithmic design: Sensible structured mean-field (joint q(si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij)) avoids the pitfalls of naive mean-field; discarding local φ between iterations reduces memory; natural gradients simplify global updates.
- Empirical results: Strong latent space recovery on synthetic data with growing K and N; convergence in 2–5 passes; link prediction improvements over MMSB on larger social networks.
- Practicality: Clear engineering choices (δ-subsampling, mini-batch sizing, step-size schedule), evidence of parallel speedups, and discussion of memory considerations.

Weaknesses
- Missing head-to-head comparisons to the most relevant baseline a-MMSB under identical conditions. The paper cites reported convergence times, but does not provide controlled comparisons on the same datasets/hardware. Similarly, there is no comparison to an MMTM variant with variational inference (which would remove MCMC’s mixing issues and better isolate the benefit of O(K) parameterization).
- Expressivity trade-offs of parameter sharing are under-analyzed. Tying all “two-equal-one-different” triangles into Bxx irrespective of the minority role may mask heterophily/heterogeneity patterns across specific role triplets; likewise, all-distinct roles share B0. The paper lacks ablations quantifying accuracy loss vs. MMTM when inter-role specificity matters.
- The “mixture-of-deltas” local variational family is ad hoc and lacks theoretical error control or principled selection of the sampled off-diagonals A. There is no sensitivity analysis of the number/structure of sampled combinations and its effect on accuracy/stability.
- Evaluation caveats: latent role recovery uses seeded initialization with 2% ground-truth labels, which may not reflect real unsupervised conditions; no robustness across random seeds is shown. Link prediction baselines are limited to MMSB; there is no comparison to triangle-based baselines (MMTM) or to simple scalable methods (even if non-probabilistic), and details on computational complexity for the triangle-marginalized edge scoring are sparse.
- Some complexity claims are inconsistent. While the global update is O(NK), per-pass cost is O(mK) with m ≈ O(Nδ^2). The abstract and text occasionally suggest O(NK) overall, which could be misleading unless δ is treated as a constant and the number of passes is demonstrably small.
- Clarity gaps: key update equations are deferred to the appendix, Table 1 is critical to understanding the generative probabilities, and the link prediction scoring/marginalization details are brief.

Quality
Technically sound overall with appropriate use of SVI and natural gradients. The structured variational formulation is reasonable, and the engineering to reduce per-triangle costs is thoughtful. However, empirical validation would be stronger with direct comparisons to a-MMSB and MMTM-SVI, ablations (e.g., without mixture-of-deltas, varying size/structure of A, with/without parameter tying), and robustness analyses (initialization, δ). Some claims (orders-of-magnitude vs a-MMSB) rely on external reports rather than controlled experiments.

Clarity
Generally well written; the model intuition and equivalence-class parameterization are clear. Still, readers must consult appendices for essential math. Sharper statements on computational complexity would help.

Originality
Moderate. The main novelty lies in the specific equivalence-class parameter tying for triangle motifs and the O(K) local update approximation. The overall scaffolding (triangle representation, SVI, natural gradients, parameter tying) builds on prior work.

Significance
Potentially high for practitioners needing scalable probabilistic role inference. The demonstrated scalability is impressive, but the limited comparative evidence against the most relevant baselines and the underexplored expressivity-accuracy trade-off reduce confidence in broad impact without further validation.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong scalability with thoughtful variational/engineering design.
- Demonstrated million-node runs with competitive accuracy.
- Clear, implementable approach; structured mean-field well motivated.
- Advances triangle-based modeling toward practical large-K, large-N regimes.

Cons for acceptance
- No controlled comparison to a-MMSB or MMTM with variational inference.
- Expressivity trade-offs from parameter tying not quantified; lack of ablation studies.
- Ad hoc local variational approximation without theoretical guarantees or sensitivity analysis.
- Reliance on seeded init; limited robustness reporting.
- Some ambiguity in complexity statements and link prediction methodology.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide head-to-head comparisons to a-MMSB and a variational MMTM on shared datasets/hardware (runtime, accuracy, convergence).
- Add ablations: (i) full φ vs. mixture-of-deltas; (ii) varying the size/structure of A; (iii) with/without parameter tying (or a sparse correction term to partially reintroduce inter-role specificity).
- Analyze scenarios where Bxx/B0 tying limits modeling power; synthetic stress tests with role-specific triadic patterns.
- Clarify per-iteration and per-pass complexity, including δ’s role; report wall-clock per pass and total passes across datasets.
- Remove seeding or report robustness across random initializations; consider hyperparameter learning for α, λ.
- Detail link prediction scoring, computational cost, and compare against triangle-based baselines.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is promising and could be impactful, but needs stronger experimental comparisons to the most relevant baselines and deeper analysis of the approximation/expressivity trade-offs to meet the NeurIPS bar. With these additions, it could become a strong submission.
