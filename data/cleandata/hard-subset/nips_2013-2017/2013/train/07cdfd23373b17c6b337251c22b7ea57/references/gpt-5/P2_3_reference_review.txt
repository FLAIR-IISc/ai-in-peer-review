Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic approach for latent space inference in large networks by (i) representing networks as a bag of triangular motifs (open/closed triangles), (ii) introducing a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) with O(K) triangle-generating parameters via role-sharing across equivalence classes of role-triplet configurations, and (iii) developing a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and natural gradients that is parallelizable and memory-efficient. This extends prior triangular modeling (MMTM [8]) by compressing the parameterization from O(K^3) to O(K), addressing scale in both N and K. Compared to edge-based mixed-membership models (MMSB [1] and its scalable a-MMSB variant [5]), the approach leverages higher-order structure (triangles; cf. ERGM motivation [11,16]) to reduce data size and improve informativeness. The SVI framework (Hoffman et al. [10]) and natural gradients (Amari [2]) are used to enable stochastic, convergent optimization. Empirically, the method reportedly recovers latent roles on synthetic data, achieves superior link prediction on larger social networks relative to MMSB, and scales to 1M nodes and 100 roles on a single 8-thread machine in hours.

Quality
Strengths:
- Sound modeling idea: The PTM’s parameter sharing across role-triplet equivalence classes (B0, Bxx, Bxxx) is well-motivated and reduces variance and cost, while still capturing key triadic distinctions (all-same, two-same, all-distinct). This directly addresses MMTM’s K^3 blow-up.
- Algorithmic design: The structured mean-field for the role triplet retains key dependencies that naive mean-field loses. The mixture-of-deltas local variational family provides an O(K) per-triangle update that mirrors the model’s structure and enables practical SVI with mini-batches. Discarding local variables after accumulating sufficient statistics is a good memory optimization.
- Scalability: Clear analysis of per-pass cost and parallelism; the global update is O(NK) due to the O(K) parameterization. Practical runtimes (e.g., 1M nodes, K=100 in ~4 hours, 8 threads) are compelling.
- Empirics: The synthetic latent role recovery and convergence plots convincingly show faster convergence (1–2 passes) and robustness versus baselines. Large-scale experiments report orders-of-magnitude speedups over MMTM Gibbs [8] and literature-reported a-MMSB runtimes [5]. Link prediction gains over MMSB on larger networks are plausible given triangle information.

Weaknesses/concerns:
- Theoretical guarantees: The SVI convergence discussion relies on unbiased gradients of the variational objective L(η,γ). However, the algorithm uses (a) a restricted local variational family (mixture-of-deltas) and (b) approximate local maximization, and re-samples the off-diagonal support A. This yields unbiasedness with respect to the mini-batch sampling, but not necessarily with respect to the original L(η,γ); effectively, the method optimizes a different bound. The paper would benefit from explicitly stating the objective actually optimized and giving a convergence statement for that objective (or an empirical ablation showing negligible loss relative to exact O(K^3) local updates on smaller graphs).
- Expressiveness trade-off: The O(K) parameter sharing treats all all-distinct role triplets identically (B0), and all “two-same” configurations via Bxx shared across minority roles. This limits the model’s ability to capture heterogeneous cross-role triadic patterns. While this is the point of parsimony, it would be useful to discuss when this hurts (e.g., networks with strong role-specific triadic closure differences).
- Comparisons: Empirical comparisons omit a direct implementation of a-MMSB [5] under the same hardware and stopping criteria; the paper cites literature runtimes. Including a direct comparison (or at least a well-documented attempt) would strengthen the claim. Also, link prediction is compared only against MMSB; triangle-based approaches might also be compared with other scalable community/embedding methods (even if non-probabilistic) to contextualize performance.
- Evaluation details: The synthetic experiments seed 2% of nodes with true roles. Seeding is realistic in some applications but could bias outcomes; presenting results without seeding or with the proposed heuristic initialization would help assess robustness. For δ-subsampling, the effect on the objective and predictions is asserted from [8]; a brief accounting of whether reweighting is needed to correct subsampling bias would clarify.
- Minor technical issues: The step-size schedule is written as ρt = τ0(τ1 + t)−κ, with conditions ∑ ρt = ∞, ∑ ρt^2 < ∞; later, experiments state κ = −0.5, which would make steps increase over time and violate these conditions. This looks like a sign typo; please correct and report the actual κ used (typically 0.5 < κ ≤ 1). There are a few small notational slips (e.g., repeated tuple form) and reliance on an Appendix/Table 1 for key update details; ensure these are complete.

Clarity
Overall well-organized and readable. The motivation for triangular representation and the PTM design is clear. The variational factorization and optimization flow are described crisply, although key derivations are deferred to the Appendix. Please correct the step-size typo, tidy tuple enumeration, and clarify the precise objective optimized under the mixture-of-deltas approximation.

Originality
The combination of triangle-based representation with a parsimonious O(K) parameterization and a structured SVI algorithm is novel relative to MMTM [8] and a-MMSB [5]. The specific equivalence-class sharing and the O(K) local-update approximation are original and well-justified.

Significance
The contribution is practically important: scaling latent role inference to million-node graphs with hundreds of roles on modest hardware, with competitive or improved accuracy, addresses a pressing need. The approach is likely to be adopted or extended by practitioners working with large social or information networks.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong, principled scalability via model parsimony and SVI; compelling runtime and memory profile.
- Clear, useful idea (triangle-based, role-sharing) advancing prior triangular models.
- Solid empirical evidence on both synthetic and large real graphs; faster convergence and better or comparable accuracy.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of formal convergence guarantees for the approximate local updates and restricted variational family; unclear exact objective optimized.
- Limited baselines (no direct a-MMSB comparison) and reliance on seeded initialization in synthetic experiments.
- Potential expressiveness limitations of the O(K) parameterization not deeply analyzed.

Questions/suggestions for authors
- Provide an ablation comparing exact O(K^3) local updates vs the mixture-of-deltas approximation on smaller datasets to quantify any loss.
- Include a direct a-MMSB baseline if feasible, or a more thorough discussion of attempts and obstacles.
- Clarify and correct the step-size parameter κ and report sensitivity to τ0, τ1, κ.
- Discuss when the PTM’s parameter sharing may be too restrictive, and whether a modestly richer sharing scheme (e.g., a small number of B0 clusters) helps.
- Clarify how δ-subsampling affects the optimization objective and whether any reweighting is used.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept. The paper presents a thoughtful and impactful advance in scalable probabilistic latent space modeling with strong empirical results. Addressing the noted clarity and evaluation issues would further strengthen it.
