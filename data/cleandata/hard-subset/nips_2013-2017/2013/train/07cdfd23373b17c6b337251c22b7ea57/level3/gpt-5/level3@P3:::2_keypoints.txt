- Summary: Proposes Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that models triangles instead of edges, reducing triangle-configuration parameters from O(K3) to O(K) via parameter sharing and an SVI algorithm with structured mean-field and natural-gradient global updates.  
- Scalability: Introduces an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” local-update approximation and demonstrates million-node, 100-role runs with competitive link prediction and faster convergence.

Strengths:
- Motivates triangles as a compact, informative bag-of-words analog amenable to mini-batching.  
- Main modeling advance: principled O(K) parameter sharing across triangle configurations enabling O(NK) global updates.  
- Uses scalable SVI with structured variational family that better captures triad coupling than naive mean-field.  
- O(K) local-update approximation is simple, valid for variational bounds, and empirically effective.  
- Strong empirical scaling and competitive predictive performance with faster convergence than batch methods.

Weaknesses / Questions:
1) Novelty and positioning: PTM is largely a combination of a-MMSB-style sharing and MMTM likelihood; need clearer statement of what is genuinely new beyond configuration grouping and the O(K) local approximation.  
2) Directed networks: No discussion of extension to directed triads or how parameter sharing and complexity generalize for directed graphs.  
3) ERGM analogy: Claim of being a “latent-space counterpart to ERGMs” is overstated—PTM only targets triangle/open-triple statistics, not the broader ERGM sufficient statistics.  
4) Interpretability: Unclear if roles are interpretable; need qualitative analyses (top nodes per role, role-triangle profiles, correlations with metadata).  
5) Δ1 vs Δ2 clarity: The distinction is hard to follow; must state Δ1 is 2-category simplex (open vs closed) and Δ2 is 3-category simplex (majority/minority/closed) and explain motif equivalence.  
6) Triangle subsampling (δ): Insufficient evidence that δ-subsampling preserves triangle distributions; need ablations across δ and analysis of bias/variance effects.  
7) Local update approximation: Random support selection needs comparison to full O(K3) updates at small K and deterministic top-L schemes; report how often mass is off-diagonal.  
8) Complexity clarity: Explain why collapsed Gibbs sampling remains O(K3) despite O(K) parameters and contrast end-to-end costs of SVI vs CGS across K and δ.  
9) Evaluation robustness: Questions on arbitrary thresholding, unspecified synthetic generator, light 10% holdout, and surprising MMSB-outperformance that need sensitivity analyses and diagnostics.  
10) Scope: No discussion of extensions to directed/typed/weighted edges or combining edge+triangle modeling for low-triad regions.

Quality:
- Variational derivations and use of natural gradients are sound, but key empirical claims (δ adequacy, threshold robustness, MMSB results) need stronger ablation/evidence.

Clarity:
- Generally readable but needs clearer exposition of Δ1/Δ2, equivalence-class logic, centralized complexity statements, stronger motivation/analysis of the local approximation, and toned-down ERGM analogy.

Originality:
- Moderate: main novelties are the triangle parameter-sharing pattern and the O(K) local approximation; other elements are standard SVI applications.

Significance:
- High practical significance for scalable motif-based mixed-membership inference if robustness and interpretability issues are addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Add a directed-network extension sketch with parameter/complexity counts.  
- Provide ablations for δ, threshold sensitivity, exact vs approximate local updates, and deterministic vs random support.  
- Specify the power-law latent-space simulator and add other simulators (e.g., degree-corrected SBM).  
- Evaluate under heavier edge sparsity and cold-start scenarios.  
- Explore sparse priors or post-hoc sparsification for binary role recovery.  
- Include qualitative analyses to demonstrate role interpretability.

Arguments pro acceptance:
- Scales motif-based mixed-membership inference to million-node graphs with competitive accuracy.  
- Effective O(K) parameter reduction enabling O(NK) global updates.  
- Practical and intuitive O(K) local update approximation with strong empirical performance.  
- Timely contribution to scalable network inference practice.

Arguments contra acceptance:
- Incremental novelty; much of the method is standard SVI machinery and bookkeeping.  
- Several empirical claims lack sufficient substantiation (δ, thresholding, MMSB-simulated results).  
- Limited discussion of applicability beyond undirected, untyped graphs.  
- Presentation and clarity issues (ERGM analogy, Δ1/Δ2 explanation, complexity narrative).
