## Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper presents a novel approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large-scale networks, focusing on scalability through a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference (SVI). The core idea – parameterizing triangle configurations to reduce model complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) – is a significant contribution towards enabling analysis of networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of latent roles. The authors successfully demonstrate improved scalability compared to existing methods like MMSB and MMTM, achieving competitive accuracy on synthetic data and showcasing impressive performance on a 1M-node YouTube network.

The work builds upon prior research in latent space modeling (e.g., MMSB [1], a-MMSB [5]) and network representation (e.g., triangular motifs [8]). It addresses a critical limitation of existing methods – their inability to scale to truly large networks – by cleverly leveraging triangle counts and a parameter sharing scheme. The combination of aMMSB with MMTM, while straightforward in concept, is presented as a novel contribution, though the implementation details and bookkeeping appear complex. The intuitive approximation for the local update rule within the SVI framework is a noteworthy achievement, especially given the standard nature of the overall SVI procedure.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The analogy between PTM and ERGMs is questionable, as ERGMs utilize a broader range of network statistics. The interpretability of the inferred latent space is not discussed; it remains unclear what insights the model reveals about graph structure. The rationale behind subsampling triangles (using delta) and the arbitrary threshold of 0.125 require further justification. The model’s ability to recover a distinct “set of non-zero roles” might benefit from a more binary role membership representation. The reported O(K) parameter complexity of PTM seems inconsistent with the K^3 complexity reported for PTM CGS, raising concerns about the analysis of computational cost. 

Furthermore, the experiments primarily focus on networks with a relatively high edge density (10% held-out edges), and the method’s performance on sparser networks is unknown. The counterintuitive result of outperforming MMSB on networks *generated* by MMSB warrants deeper investigation. The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is unclear, hindering full comprehension of the methodology. Finally, the paper lacks discussion on extending the method to directed networks, limiting its applicability.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical scalability issue in latent space network modeling.
*   Novel parameterization of triangle configurations significantly reduces model complexity.
*   Demonstrates impressive performance on large-scale networks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Several methodological concerns regarding justification of design choices (delta, threshold).
*   Inconsistencies in complexity analysis.
*   Limited discussion of interpretability and applicability to directed networks.
*   Unclear presentation of certain aspects (Delta^1/2, PTM CGS complexity).



**Overall:** This is a promising work with a strong technical contribution. However, addressing the identified weaknesses and clarifying the presentation would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
