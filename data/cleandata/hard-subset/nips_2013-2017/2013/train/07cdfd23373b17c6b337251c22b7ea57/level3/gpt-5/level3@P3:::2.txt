Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a scalable latent-space model for large networks by replacing edge-level modeling with a bag-of-triangles representation. The Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) parameterizes triangle configurations so that the number of generative parameters grows as O(K) rather than O(K3) (as in MMTM), borrowing the “tie-sharing” idea from a-MMSB’s O(K) inter-block parameterization. The authors derive a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field: per-triangle role triples are coupled locally, and global updates for role proportions and triangle parameters use natural gradients. To address the O(K3) local update cost, they introduce an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation that concentrates variational mass on diagonals and a small random set of off-diagonals. The approach scales to million-node graphs and hundreds of roles, with competitive or improved link prediction and latent-role recovery compared to MMSB (Airoldi et al., JMLR), a-MMSB (Gopalan et al., NIPS), and MMTM (Ho et al., NIPS). The triangular representation aligns with prior work on triadic closure, ERGMs (Hunter/Handcock), and motif-based modeling, and the algorithmic framework builds on SVI (Hoffman et al., JMLR) and natural gradients (Amari).

Strengths
- Clear motivation for triangles as a bag-of-words analog: more compact than edges, often more informative for social graphs, and amenable to mini-batching.
- Main modeling advance: principled parameter sharing across triangle configurations (all-same, two-equal majority/minority, all-distinct), reducing parameters to O(K) and enabling O(NK) global updates.
- Scalable SVI with explicit global and local rules; the structured variational family is more appropriate than naive mean field for triads.
- The O(K) local update approximation is simple, intuitive, and practically effective, with an argument that any restricted support still yields a valid variational bound.
- Strong empirical scaling: million-node, 100-role runs in hours on one machine; competitive link prediction on real graphs; faster convergence (few passes) than batch variants.

Weaknesses and questions
1) Novelty and positioning. Technically, PTM is a straightforward combination of a-MMSB-style parameter sharing with MMTM’s triangle likelihood. The contribution is useful but incremental; the paper would benefit from a crisper comparison clarifying what is new beyond (i) the specific grouping of triangle configurations, and (ii) the O(K) local update approximation.

2) Directed networks. No discussion of extension to directed graphs. Directed triads have many isomorphism classes; how would the parameter sharing generalize, and what happens to complexity?

3) ERGM analogy. The claim that PTM is a “latent space counterpart to ERGMs” is overstated. ERGMs typically use a broader set of sufficient statistics (edges, k-stars, GWESP, etc.). Please clarify scope: PTM focuses on triangle/open-triple ratios, not general ERGM statistics.

4) Interpretability. It is unclear whether the inferred latent space is interpretable. What do roles correspond to in real networks? Provide qualitative analyses (top nodes per role, role-to-triangle profiles, correlation with metadata) to show what similarities/differences among graphs the roles reveal.

5) Δ1 vs Δ2. The distinction between Δ1 and Δ2 is easy to miss and not clearly explained. Spell out that Δ1 is a 2-category simplex (open vs closed) and Δ2 is a 3-category simplex (open centered at majority, open centered at minority, closed), and how this interacts with equivalence classes of motifs.

6) Triangle subsampling (δ). The justification for δ-subsampling as an “adequate approximation” needs more evidence. What conditions preserve the open/closed triangle distribution? How does δ affect bias/variance of the SVI gradients and downstream metrics? Ablate δ over orders of magnitude and report accuracy/runtime trade-offs.

7) Local update approximation. The mixture-of-deltas approximation is intriguing, but support selection is random. Compare against: (i) full O(K3) updates at small K, and (ii) a deterministic top-L scheme (e.g., product of per-node responsibilities) to probe bias/variance. Quantify how often mass sits off the diagonal in practice.

8) Complexity clarity. The paper states PTM has O(K) parameters yet the collapsed Gibbs version is O(K3). Explain why the sampling step remains cubic (joint role triples) despite parameter sharing, and contrast the end-to-end cost of SVI vs CGS for typical K and δ.

9) Evaluation details and robustness.
- Thresholding at 0.125 to recover “set of non-zero roles” seems arbitrary. Provide sensitivity analysis across thresholds or adopt a sparse/Beta–Bernoulli prior to induce near-binary memberships when that is the goal.
- Synthetic data: specify precisely the “power-law latent-space” generator; many models yield power laws. Without this, it is hard to gauge the match between simulator and inference model.
- Held-out edges: only 10% removal is a light stress test. Please evaluate performance in sparser regimes (e.g., 50–90% holdout) and cold-start nodes to emulate missing-data conditions common in large graphs.
- Counterintuitive finding: outperforming MMSB on MMSB-simulated graphs suggests either beneficial inductive bias/regularization or a mismatch in evaluation. Diagnose with matched hyperparameters, multiple initializations, and exact-vs-approx local updates.

10) Scope. How would the method handle directed/typed edges, weighted graphs, or combine edges and triangles to avoid losing information from low-triad regions?

Quality
- Sound variational derivations and sensible use of natural gradients; structured mean-field is appropriate. However, several claims (δ-subsampling adequacy, robustness to thresholding, MMSB-outperformance on MMSB-data) require deeper analysis or ablations to be fully convincing.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but some presentation can improve: clarify Δ1 vs Δ2; make the equivalence-class logic more explicit with a compact figure; centralize complexity statements; better motivate and analyze the local approximation; tone down ERGM analogy.

Originality
- Moderate. The parameter sharing pattern for triangles and the O(K) local approximation are the main novel pieces. The rest is a solid application of existing SVI machinery to a motif-based model.

Significance
- High practical significance: the method enables O(NK) global updates and near-linear-time training on very large graphs, which is valuable. If robustness and interpretability issues are addressed, this will be useful to practitioners.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add directed-network extension sketch, with parameter counts and complexity.
- Provide ablations: δ, threshold sensitivity, exact vs approximate local updates, deterministic vs random support sets.
- Clarify the power-law generator; consider adding another well-specified simulator (e.g., degree-corrected SBM).
- Report results under heavier edge sparsity and cold-start.
- Explore sparse priors or post-hoc sparsification for binary role recovery.
- Include qualitative role interpretability analyses.

Arguments pro acceptance
- Scales motif-based mixed-membership inference to million-node graphs with competitive accuracy.
- Clear modeling idea that reduces parameters to O(K), enabling O(NK) global updates.
- Practical, intuitive local update approximation with strong empirical performance.
- Timely contribution to scalable network inference.

Arguments contra acceptance
- Incremental novelty; much of the algorithm is standard SVI and bookkeeping.
- Several claims insufficiently substantiated (δ-subsampling adequacy, threshold robustness, MMSB-data results).
- Limited discussion of applicability beyond undirected, untyped graphs.
- Presentation issues (ERGM analogy, Δ1/Δ2 clarity, complexity narrative).
