

This paper presents a scalable approach for latent space inference on large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) and stochastic variational inference. The main contribution lies in developing a parameter-sharing subclass of the triangle model that reduces the parameter dimension from O(K³) to O(K), combined with an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm that achieves O(NK) time complexity. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles, significantly outperforming existing approaches in both speed and scalability.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in network analysis. The authors effectively demonstrate the superiority of their approach through comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks, showing competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction while achieving orders-of-magnitude speedup.

However, several specific revisions are needed for clarity and accuracy. First, the term "parsimonious modeling" should be defined or unpacked on page 1, as it's central to the paper's contribution. Second, the introduction's claim about down-sampling zeros compromising accuracy (page 1) is misleading; Gopalan et al. (2014) showed this doesn't compromise accuracy, so the sentence should be revised. Third, the LDA analogy on page 4 is inappropriate since triangle-model samples can produce non-networks, making the comparison invalid.

Theoretical foundations require attention: on page 5, the authors should cite Hoffman et al. (2013) alongside Amari and Sato, as Sato's algorithm doesn't apply with local variables while Hoffman provides the necessary theory. The paper should also clarify how power-law networks were generated in the latent space (page 6), and compare against Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm in addition to batch MMSB for baseline comparison.

Minor corrections include changing "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" (page 2), removing the last sentence on page 8, and citing the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference" in the bibliography.

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper while maintaining its strong technical contributions. The core idea of parameter-sharing in the triangular model is novel and well-executed, making this an important contribution to scalable network analysis. With the suggested revisions, this paper would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
