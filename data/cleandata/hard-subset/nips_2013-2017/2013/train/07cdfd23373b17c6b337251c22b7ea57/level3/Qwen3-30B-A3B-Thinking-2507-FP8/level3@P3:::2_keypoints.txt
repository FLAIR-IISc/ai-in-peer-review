- Proposes Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) using triangle-count statistics as a bag-of-words analog, reducing parameters from O(K³) to O(K) via parameter sharing and using stochastic variational inference with O(NK) complexity.  
- Builds on a-MMSB and MMTM by combining parameter-sharing ideas with triangle-based representations.  

Strengths:
- Demonstrates scalable latent-space inference on networks up to 1M nodes, a substantial practical advance.  
- O(NK) complexity claim is empirically supported, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups over MMSB and MMTM.  
- Stochastic variational inference converges quickly (2–5 passes) on large datasets.  
- Experimental results show improved accuracy on large networks versus existing methods.  

Weaknesses:
- Unsupported and potentially misleading claim that PTM is a latent-space counterpart to ERGMs, given ERGMs’ broader statistic use.  
- Poor explanation of the distinction between Δ^1 and Δ^2, causing confusion about parameterization.  
- Arbitrary role-membership threshold (0.125) presented without justification or robustness analysis.  
- No discussion of extension to directed networks, limiting applicability to many real-world graphs.  
- Unclear interpretability of the inferred latent space and what it reveals about graph similarities/differences.  
- Inconsistency between claiming O(K) parameters and reporting PTM CGS as K³ complexity, causing scalability confusion.  
- Experiments only hold out 10% of edges, leaving performance with sparser observations unknown.  
- Counterintuitive result that PTM outperforms MMSB on MMSB-synthesized data is unexplained and unvalidated.  

Clarity and presentation:
- Generally well-written but parameter-sharing strategy, notation, and differences from MMTM/a-MMSB need clearer, more intuitive exposition.  

Originality:
- Novel combination of a-MMSB and MMTM for triangle-based networks; parameter-sharing idea is practical but not fundamentally new.  

Significance:
- Addresses scalability for large-network latent inference meaningfully, enabling million-node analysis on a single machine, though limitations (directed networks, methodological clarifications) reduce immediate applicability.  

Overall assessment:
- Promising, technically sound contribution with clear scalability gains but hindered by methodological concerns and unclear exposition.  

Recommendation:
- Weak accept pending major revisions to clarify parameter complexity, justify thresholds, address directed networks, and better position the work relative to prior literature.
