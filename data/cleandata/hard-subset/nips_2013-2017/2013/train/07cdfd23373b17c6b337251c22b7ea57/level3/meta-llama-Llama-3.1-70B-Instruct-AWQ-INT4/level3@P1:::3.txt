The paper "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks" presents a novel method for making inference about latent spaces of large networks. The authors propose a scalable approach that combines a succinct representation of networks as a bag of triangular motifs, a parsimonious statistical model, and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. This approach enables the analysis of real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the triangle model, a parameter-sharing subclass, and the use of stochastic variational inference for very large networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on synthetic and real-world networks, showing that it achieves competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction compared to state-of-the-art probabilistic approaches.

The paper is well-written and well-executed, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. However, there are a few areas that require clarification or correction. Firstly, the authors should define and unpack the term "parsimonious modeling" to provide a clearer understanding of their approach. Additionally, the authors should correct the misleading claim that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy, as Gopalan et al. have shown that this is not the case.

Furthermore, the authors' analogy with Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is unfair, as the triangle model can produce non-networks, whereas LDA is designed to produce coherent topics. The authors should also cite Hoffman et al. along with Amari and Sato, as Sato's algorithm does not apply with local variables, and Hoffman provides the necessary theory for stochastic variational inference.

In terms of experimental details, the authors should provide a clearer explanation of how power-law networks were generated in the latent space. Additionally, the authors should compare their approach to Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm, in addition to or instead of batch MMSB.

Minor wording changes, such as changing "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership," can also improve the clarity of the paper. Finally, the authors should remove the last sentence of the manuscript, as it is unnecessary and can be misleading.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of network analysis and provides a scalable approach for making inference about latent spaces of large networks. With some minor revisions and clarifications, the paper can be further improved to provide a clearer and more accurate representation of the authors' approach.
