This paper proposes a scalable probabilistic approach to infer mixed-membership latent roles from large networks by representing graphs as a bag of triangular motifs rather than edges. Building on prior work that showed triangles preserve important higher-order structure with far fewer sufficient statistics than edges [8], the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). PTM ties triangle-generating parameters by grouping role-triplet configurations into three equivalence classes: all-same role (Bxxx), two-same-one-different (Bxx), and all-distinct (B0). This reduces the parameterization from O(K3) in MMTM [8] to O(K). For inference, they develop a structured mean-field stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm [10] with natural gradients [2], using a “mixture-of-deltas” approximation to compute local updates in O(K) time per triangle instead of O(K3). Experiments show orders-of-magnitude speedups over MMSB [1] and a-MMSB [5] (which model edges) and over MMTM (which models triangles but has O(K3) parameters), with comparable or better latent role recovery and link prediction on synthetic and real networks (including a 1M-node YouTube graph with K=100 in ~4 hours on one machine).

Relation to prior work: The paper closely relates to MMSB [1], a-MMSB [5] (parameter tying for inter-block links and SVI on edges), and MMTM [8] (bag-of-triangles). Its main novelty is combining triangle-based modeling with parameter tying akin to a-MMSB to achieve O(K) global parameters and an efficient structured SVI scheme.

Strengths
- Clear modeling idea that retains the benefits of triangle-based representations [8] while removing the O(K3) bottleneck through principled parameter sharing.
- A practical and well-engineered SVI algorithm with natural gradients, mini-batching over triangles, and a mixture-of-deltas local approximation that reduces local updates to O(K) without sacrificing accuracy.
- Strong scalability: O(NK) global updates and overall training that converges in 2–5 passes; results demonstrated on million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine.
- Empirical performance competitive or superior to MMSB/a-MMSB and MMTM on latent role recovery and link prediction, with orders-of-magnitude runtime gains.
- Good parallelization story and memory footprint (discarding local variational parameters after accumulating sufficient statistics).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Scope and claims: The analogy to ERGMs as a “latent-space counterpart” is overstated. ERGMs use a broader family of statistics, and PTM is not a generative network model (as the authors themselves note). Consider softening the claim and clarifying the relationship.
- Directed/weighted graphs: No discussion of directed networks or signed/weighted edges. Clarify whether the equivalence-class construction and parameter tying extend to directed 3-node motifs, and what additional parameters would be needed.
- Clarity of notation: The use of ∆1 and ∆2 is not explained; explicitly state that ∆d denotes a d-simplex (i.e., 2 and 3 outcome multinomials for ∆1 and ∆2, respectively), and map components Bxxx,1/2 and Bxx,1/2/3 to the corresponding motif events.
- Subsampling rationale: The δ-based node-centric triangle subsampling is briefly justified via [8]; include a concise argument or empirical sensitivity analysis showing how varying δ affects downstream tasks (latent role recovery and link prediction), especially on heavy-tailed degree distributions.
- Interpretability: The paper emphasizes efficiency and accuracy but gives little insight into what the learned roles capture. Provide qualitative analyses (e.g., top nodes per role, correlation with metadata, motif composition per role) to demonstrate interpretability and differences across graphs.
- Evaluation choices: The recovery metric thresholds θ̂ at 0.125 because “no more than 4 roles per θi.” Report sensitivity to the threshold and/or adopt a calibrated measure (e.g., AUC for per-role detection, or evaluate KL divergence between θ and θ̂). If the goal is recovering a set of non-zero roles, consider a sparse prior (e.g., small α, spike-and-slab) or feature-based alternatives [14] and discuss tradeoffs.
- Synthetic data: The “power-law latent space” simulator is unspecified. Provide a precise model or citation to ensure reproducibility and interpretability of results.
- Complexity confusion: The paper states PTM has O(K) parameters yet reports PTM collapsed Gibbs as O(K3). Clarify that despite parameter tying, a blocked Gibbs sampler still enumerates O(K3) role triplets per triangle (hence K3 time), whereas the proposed SVI avoids this via the mixture-of-deltas approximation, yielding O(K) local updates.
- Robustness to missingness: Link prediction holds out 10% of edges. Assess performance under higher sparsity (e.g., 50–90% holdout), cold-start nodes, and networks with fewer observed edges, to probe generalization.
- Counterintuitive synthetic result: PTM outperforming MMSB on MMSB-generated graphs warrants discussion. Plausible reasons include: (i) O(K)-parameter tying reduces variance and improves estimation at large K; (ii) the triangle representation amplifies clustering signal; (iii) the thresholded NMI may favor sparser role supports; (iv) different initializations and optimization dynamics. A small ablation (e.g., varying K, α, and thresholds) would clarify.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The structured mean-field, SVI with natural gradients, and local mixture-of-deltas approximation are reasonable and well-motivated. Claims about scalability are supported empirically. Stronger theoretical justification for δ-subsampling fidelity and for the quality of the mixture-of-deltas approximation would improve rigor.
- Empirical support: Results are extensive on size and runtime; additional sensitivity analyses and interpretability would strengthen the paper.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized, but some notational and conceptual points (∆-notation, equivalence classes, subsampling rationale) need clearer exposition. Include a figure summarizing equivalence classes and their mapping to B parameters, and a table clarifying computational complexity at each step.

Originality
- Moderate. The key ideas—triangle-based modeling [8], parameter tying like a-MMSB [5], and SVI [10]—are known; the novel contributions are their combination, the specific equivalence-class parameterization, and the efficient O(K) local update via mixture-of-deltas.

Significance
- High practical significance: enables latent role inference on million-node graphs on a single machine, which is valuable for both research and applications. The approach should be broadly useful for scalable community/role discovery and link prediction.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong scalability with clear algorithmic innovations enabling O(NK) inference.
- Comprehensive empirical validation with large real datasets and substantial speedups over strong baselines.
- Methodologically clean and likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited theoretical treatment of subsampling and local approximation quality.
- Missing discussion and experiments on directed/weighted graphs, higher missingness, and interpretability.
- Some presentation issues and ambiguous claims (ERGMs analogy, ∆-notation, synthetic generator).

Recommendation
- Accept, contingent on revisions to clarify notation and claims, add brief sensitivity analyses (thresholding, δ), and include a discussion of directed extensions and interpretability.
