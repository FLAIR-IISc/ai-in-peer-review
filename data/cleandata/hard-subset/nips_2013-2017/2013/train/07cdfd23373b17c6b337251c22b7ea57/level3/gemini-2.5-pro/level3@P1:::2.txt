### Review

**Summary**

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a scalable method for inferring latent mixed-membership roles in large-scale networks. The core idea is to represent a network not by its edges, but as a "bag of triangles," building upon the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) by Ho et al. (2012). The main contribution is a novel, parsimonious parameterization for the generative probabilities of these triangles. Where MMTM required O(K³) parameters for K latent roles, PTM reduces this to O(K) by grouping role configurations and sharing parameters, in a spirit similar to the a-MMSB of Gopalan et al. (2013). To perform inference, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with a structured mean-field approximation. A key aspect of their algorithm is an O(K) approximation for the local variational updates, which avoids an O(K³) bottleneck. The experiments demonstrate that PTM is orders of magnitude faster than previous methods like MMTM and MMSB, enabling inference on million-node networks in hours, while achieving competitive or improved accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The paper's primary strength is its success in scaling latent space network modeling. By combining the triangle representation with a parsimonious O(K) parameterization and an efficient SVI algorithm, the authors achieve a runtime complexity of O(NK). The empirical results are compelling, showing the ability to analyze a million-node network with 100 roles in just a few hours on a single machine. This is a major practical advance over prior work and addresses a critical need in the field.

2.  **Novel and Effective Model Parameterization:** The reduction of the triangle-generating parameter tensor from O(K³) to O(K) is the key modeling insight. This not only enables scalability but also appears to act as a form of regularization, potentially explaining the strong performance on latent space recovery tasks. The idea of sharing parameters based on the number of distinct roles in a triplet is intuitive and effective.

3.  **Efficient Inference Scheme:** The application of SVI is well-suited to the problem. The authors' decision to use a structured mean-field approximation that models dependencies within a triangle's latent roles is sound. Furthermore, the "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local update, which reduces its complexity from O(K³) to O(K), is a clever and practical contribution that makes the overall algorithm viable.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Improvement)**

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper could be clearer in several areas.
    *   The distinction between the probability spaces Δ¹ and Δ² in Section 3 is not explicitly defined, leaving the reader to infer their meaning from context.
    *   The complexity claim for the PTM Collapsed Gibbs Sampler (CGS) is listed as O(Nδ²K³), which seems to contradict the central claim of an O(K) parameter model. While the Gibbs sampler's complexity comes from iterating over all role assignments, this apparent contradiction should be explicitly explained for clarity.
    *   The connection to Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs) is overstated. While both use graph statistics, ERGMs typically incorporate a much wider variety of statistics (k-stars, cycles, etc.) and model the entire graph distribution. The analogy is tenuous and could be misleading.

2.  **Methodological Justification and Limitations:**
    *   The δ-subsampling of triangles is presented as a preprocessing step inherited from MMTM, but its statistical implications are not discussed. It is unclear how this node-centric subsampling affects the resulting inference and whether it introduces significant bias.
    *   The paper lacks a discussion on the model's applicability to directed networks, which is a common and important use case.
    *   The interpretability of the discovered latent space is not explored. While quantitative metrics are good, a qualitative analysis showing what the inferred roles represent would greatly strengthen the paper's claims of utility.

3.  **Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   The most surprising result is that PTM outperforms the standard MMSB variational algorithm on networks generated from the MMSB model itself (Figure 1). This is highly counterintuitive and requires explanation. Is it due to the specific initialization strategy, or does PTM's parsimonious structure act as a powerful regularizer that prevents overfitting even when the model is misspecified? This finding needs to be addressed directly in the text.
    *   The choice of thresholding the role vectors θᵢ at 0.125 for the NMI calculation seems arbitrary. The robustness of the results to this threshold should be evaluated to ensure the conclusions are not dependent on this specific choice.
    *   For the link prediction task, the method is only tested by holding out 10% of edges. Its performance in sparser settings, where triangle motifs would be much rarer, is a major practical concern that is not addressed.

**Originality and Significance**

The paper's originality lies in the effective combination of ideas from MMTM (triangle representation) and a-MMSB (parsimonious parameter sharing and SVI). While not a radical departure from previous work, the synthesis is novel and the resulting system is a significant engineering achievement. The work is highly significant due to its demonstrated ability to scale latent space inference to networks that were previously out of reach for such probabilistic models. This contribution is likely to be impactful for practitioners and researchers working with large-scale network data.

---

**Summary of Pro/Con Arguments:**

**Pro:**
*   Achieves impressive, state-of-the-art scalability for latent space network inference.
*   The parsimonious O(K) parameterization is a clever modeling contribution that drives the scalability and seems to improve recovery accuracy.
*   The O(K) approximation to the local VI update is a novel algorithmic contribution that is crucial for the overall efficiency.

**Con:**
*   Several aspects of the model and algorithm lack clarity and rigorous justification (e.g., Δ¹/Δ², δ-subsampling).
*   The experimental evaluation contains surprising results (PTM beating MMSB on its own generative model) that are not adequately explained.
*   Key evaluation choices, like the NMI threshold, seem arbitrary and are not tested for robustness.
*   The work's scope is limited (undirected graphs only) and its claims of interpretability are not supported with qualitative evidence.
