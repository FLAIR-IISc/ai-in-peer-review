

This paper proposes a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) to address scalability limitations in mixed-membership triangular models (MMTM) for large-scale network analysis. The authors reduce the parameter complexity from O(K³) to O(K) by sharing parameters across role configurations and develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity. The approach claims to enable latent space inference on million-node networks with hundreds of roles in hours, outperforming existing methods like MMSB and MMTM.

The paper's strengths include clear writing, good organization, and a compelling scalability claim supported by experiments on real-world networks. The core technical idea of parameter sharing for scalability is a reasonable direction that could impact practice if assumptions hold. The writing quality is excellent, and the method represents a moderate increment over prior work (e.g., a-MMSB [5]).

However, several critical weaknesses undermine the paper's contribution. The practical advantage of large-K inference is unclear—why should we care about MMTM with large K when mixed-membership models often tolerate small K? The paper fails to demonstrate the benefit of PTM through performance comparisons as a function of K, which is essential to validate the core claim. Model selection for real datasets is unexplained, risking biased comparisons. The authors neglect to analyze scenarios where PTM might fail (e.g., when true B has O(K³) distinct values), and synthetic experiments lack proper description of true B choices or unfavorable cases for PTM.

The approximation analysis is insufficient: the impact of threshold δ on accuracy vs. scalability is unexplored, and the local-update approximation's impact with varying sampling sizes (2K, 4K, 100K) as K grows remains unaddressed. The paper's claims about downsampling and ignoring non-edges lack justification compared to MMSB. Experimental fairness is questionable due to missing analyses and selection biases.

The title is overly broad for the specific contribution, and the probabilistic validity of the bag-of-triples assumption is questionable, as it may not correspond to any real network and could place mass on "non-networks." Overall, the paper's side-effects of modeling and inference decisions are poorly investigated.

While the method is promising for scalable inference under specific assumptions, the lack of comprehensive analysis and validation significantly weakens the contribution. The paper would benefit from addressing these concerns before acceptance.
