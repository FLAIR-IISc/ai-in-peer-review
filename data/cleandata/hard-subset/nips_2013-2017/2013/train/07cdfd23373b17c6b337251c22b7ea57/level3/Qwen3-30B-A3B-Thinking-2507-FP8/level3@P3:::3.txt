

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper introduces a novel approach for scalable latent space inference of large networks, addressing a critical gap in the field of network analysis. The authors propose the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which represents networks as a bag of triangular motifs rather than an adjacency matrix, and develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm that scales linearly with network size. The main contribution lies in the parameter-sharing mechanism that reduces the triangle-generating parameter dimension from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K), combined with a stochastic variational inference procedure that achieves O(NK) complexity.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that their method can analyze networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in hours, whereas previous methods require days or are infeasible for such scale. The authors effectively argue that three principles are crucial for large-scale inference: succinct network representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable inference algorithms. They successfully address all three, while prior work like MMSB [1] and a-MMSB [5] fail to address all three simultaneously.

The paper is well-written, with clear exposition of the technical contributions. The experimental evaluation demonstrates clear advantages in both speed and accuracy across synthetic and real-world networks. The authors convincingly show that their method outperforms MMSB and MMTM Gibbs on large networks, with convergence in 2-5 data passes versus days or weeks for baselines.

**Strengths:**
- Clear articulation of the problem and the three key principles for scalable inference
- Effective parameter-sharing mechanism in PTM that reduces complexity from O(K^3) to O(K)
- Well-executed stochastic variational inference algorithm with clear complexity analysis
- Strong experimental results demonstrating scalability to 1M+ node networks
- Good comparison with relevant baselines, though additional comparison to Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB would strengthen the claims

**Weaknesses and suggested revisions:**

1. **Terminology clarification:** The term "parsimonious modeling" (p1) is not clearly defined. The authors should provide a precise definition or at least a clearer explanation of what makes their model parsimonious.

2. **Misleading claim correction:** The claim that "a-MMSB mitigates [the O(N^2) issue] by downsampling zero elements in the matrix, but is still not fast enough" is misleading. Per Gopalan et al. [5], downsampling does not compromise accuracy, so the introduction should be revised to state that downsampling helps but is insufficient for networks with N ≥ 100,000.

3. **LDA analogy is inappropriate:** The paper defends the model by comparing it to LDA (p4), but this is inappropriate because triangle-model samples can produce non-networks (i.e., inconsistent edge values across overlapping triangles). This comparison should be removed.

4. **Theory/citation fix:** The paper cites Amari and Sato [2] for natural gradient ascent, but Sato's algorithm doesn't apply with local variables. The authors should cite Hoffman et al. [10] along with Amari and Sato to properly credit the relevant theory.

5. **Experimental detail:** The paper mentions "power-law networks" in Section 5.1 but fails to explain how these were generated in the latent space. A brief description of the power-law generation process would improve clarity.

6. **Baseline comparison:** The paper should compare against Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm [5] in addition to or instead of the batch MMSB, as this is the most relevant recent scalable method.

7. **Minor wording:** The term "mixture-membership" should be changed to "mixed-membership" (p2).

8. **Manuscript ending:** The last sentence on p8 should be removed, and the paper should end with the second-to-last sentence.

9. **Bibliography:** The reference for "Stochastic Variational Inference" should cite the JMLR version [10] rather than the conference version.

**Overall assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of scalable network analysis. The technical content is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the results are compelling. With the suggested revisions (particularly the correction to the downsampling claim, removal of the LDA analogy, and proper citation of Hoffman et al.), the paper would be even stronger.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant scalability challenge in network analysis
- Provides a clear and effective solution with theoretical and empirical justification
- Demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedup over previous methods
- Well-written with clear technical contributions

**Arguments against acceptance (minor):**
- Some minor technical claims require correction
- A few methodological details need clarification
- The LDA analogy is inappropriate

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of network analysis with clear technical advances and strong empirical results. The suggested revisions are relatively minor and would improve the paper's precision and clarity. The work represents a significant step forward in scalable network modeling that would be of interest to the NIPS community.
