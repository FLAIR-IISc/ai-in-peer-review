This paper introduces a scalable approximate inference algorithm for large-scale networks. The approach builds on the triangle-based network representation (Ho et al., 2013) by proposing the Parsimonious Triangle Model (PTM), a parameter-sharing subclass that reduces the model's parameter complexity from O(K^3) to O(K). To enable inference on very large networks, the authors develop an efficient stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm based on the framework of Hoffman et al. (2013). The combination of a succinct representation, a compact model, and a scalable inference algorithm is the paper's main contribution.

The primary strength of this work is its well-executed and compelling demonstration of scalability. The PTM, combined with SVI, achieves impressive speed-ups, enabling the analysis of million-node networks in hours on a single machine—a significant improvement over prior art. The experimental results on both synthetic and real-world data are strong, showing competitive performance on latent space recovery and link prediction. The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are clearly communicated.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses that should be addressed. The experimental evaluation is not entirely fair. The proposed stochastic method is compared against the slow, batch MMSB algorithm, which is not a state-of-the-art scalable baseline. A more appropriate comparison would be against the fast a-MMSB algorithm from Gopalan et al. (2015), which the authors mention but do not compete against. Furthermore, a claim in the introduction—that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy—misrepresents the findings of Gopalan et al. and should be revised.

On the theoretical side, the justification for the model's generative limitations feels weak; the fact that samples can produce non-networks (p4) is a significant caveat that should be acknowledged more directly. There are also several points requiring correction for clarity and rigor. The term "parsimonious modeling" should be explicitly defined (p1). The citation for the natural gradient update (p5) should include Hoffman et al. (2013), as Sato's (2001) algorithm does not apply to models with local variables. For reproducibility, the authors must explain how the synthetic power-law networks were generated (p6). Minor corrections include changing "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" and citing the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference."

Overall, this is a solid paper presenting a valuable contribution to scalable network analysis. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the issues above, especially the experimental baseline comparison.
