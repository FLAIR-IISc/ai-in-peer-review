This paper proposes a scalable latent-space model for large networks that combines (i) a succinct “bag-of-triangles” data representation, (ii) a parsimonious parameterization of triangle-generation probabilities, and (iii) a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients. The key modeling idea is the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which shares parameters across equivalence classes of role-triplet configurations, reducing triangle parameters from O(K^3) in MMTM [Ho et al., NIPS’12] to O(K) via B0, Bxx, and Bxxx. The inference contribution is a scalable, structured mean-field SVI that optimizes local three-node role assignments jointly and approximates local updates in O(K) using a “mixture-of-deltas” support, paired with O(NK) global updates. Empirically, PTM scales to million-node graphs and hundreds of roles on a single machine and reports competitive or improved latent-role recovery and link prediction relative to MMSB [Airoldi et al., JMLR’08] and MMTM.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds directly on the triangle-based representation of Ho et al. (NIPS’12) and on mixed-membership blockmodels (MMSB) and their scalable variants (e.g., a-MMSB, Gopalan et al., NIPS’12). It also connects to ERGM’s use of higher-order motifs and to the SVI literature (natural gradients, Robbins-Monro step sizes). In contrast to MMSB’s O(K^2) block matrix and MMTM’s O(K^3) tensor, PTM’s parameter sharing mirrors the dimensionality reduction used by a-MMSB for inter-block probabilities and is well motivated by exchangeability/symmetry of motif configurations.

Strengths
- Scalability: Elegant parameter sharing plus structured SVI yields O(NK) global updates and practical O(K) local computations; demonstrated on 1M+ nodes, K≈100, converging in a few passes.
- Methodological clarity: The equivalence-class partition of motif configurations and structured mean-field over (si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij) are well justified; the natural-gradient SVI is appropriate for this conjugate-exponential setting.
- Empirical results: Strong runtime improvements with competitive or superior accuracy for role recovery and link prediction; convergence in 2–5 passes is compelling.
- Practical engineering: Triangle subsampling, mini-batching, and discarding local variational states keep memory modest and enable parallelization.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Clarify “parsimonious modeling”: Explicitly define it as parameter sharing driven by symmetry/indistinguishability of role-triplet configurations that reduces degrees of freedom from O(K^3) to O(K), thereby improving statistical efficiency and stability at large K (p1).
- Correct introduction about zero-downsampling: The statement implying that downsampling zeros compromises accuracy should be revised; a-MMSB shows that careful negative (zero) subsampling can preserve accuracy while improving speed. Please cite Gopalan et al. (NIPS’12) and temper the claim (p1).
- LDA analogy: The defense that “PTM is like LDA on bags of words” is not fully apt because PTM can generate inconsistent triangle sets that do not correspond to any graph. Please acknowledge this limitation more directly and explain why non-network samples do not undermine posterior inference on real triangle bags, or add constraints/discussion to mitigate this concern (p4).
- Convergence theory/citations: When invoking stochastic natural gradients with local and global variables, cite Hoffman et al., “Stochastic Variational Inference” (JMLR 2013) in addition to Amari. Sato’s online VB focuses on settings without local variables and is not the right primary reference for your convergence guarantees. Also connect to Robbins-Monro conditions already used in your step-size schedule (p5).
- Local update approximation: You assert any choice of the O(K) support A yields a valid lower bound. Please formalize this claim (e.g., show the restricted-support variational family preserves a valid ELBO and discuss variance/bias from resampling A), and briefly quantify the impact on accuracy vs. the full O(K^3) local optimum.
- Baselines: Include a direct comparison to the fast a-MMSB of Gopalan et al. (NIPS’12) with edge subsampling and SVI, which is the most relevant probabilistic baseline. If code is unavailable, reimplement the published method or reproduce key settings; at minimum, discuss expected runtime/accuracy trade-offs relative to your approach (p6).
- Experimental detail: Provide the exact generator for the “power-law latent-space” networks (e.g., degree distribution mechanism, role assignment, edge formation), or cite a precise source; this is needed for reproducibility (p6).
- Fairness of link prediction evaluation: Because your method uses triangles while MMSB uses edges, clarify how held-out edges are treated in triangle extraction and whether triangle availability around test edges differs across methods. Consider adding an edge-subsampled a-MMSB baseline to control for this representational difference.
- Sensitivity/robustness: Briefly report sensitivity to δ, mini-batch size, K selection, and initialization (beyond seeding 2% of nodes). Consider ablations for structured vs. naive mean-field to quantify its contribution.

Quality
- Technically sound overall; the parameter-sharing design and structured SVI are appropriate and well executed. Theoretical claims about the O(K) local approximation and unbiasedness of gradients would benefit from a short, formal justification.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized. Please: define “parsimonious” explicitly; fix “mixture-membership” to “mixed-membership” (p2); improve the discussion of the non-generative-network issue; add missing experimental details; and clarify evaluation protocols. Consider moving some derivations to an appendix for readability.

Originality
- The combination of triangle-based representation with an O(K) parameter-sharing scheme and structured SVI is novel and practically important, extending MMTM to large K and N in a principled way.

Significance
- High practical impact: million-scale networks and hundreds of roles in hours with competitive accuracy. The approach should be useful to researchers and practitioners interested in overlapping communities/roles and higher-order structure.

Specific minor comments
- Change “mixture-membership” to “mixed-membership” (p2).
- Remove the last sentence on p8 and end with the second-to-last sentence for a stronger conclusion.
- Cite the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference” rather than only conference versions.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong scalability with clear methodological contributions.
- Competitive or improved accuracy on synthetic and real tasks.
- Clean, well-presented algorithm likely to be adopted by the community.

Arguments against acceptance
- Missing comparison to the most relevant fast MMSB baseline (a-MMSB).
- Some theoretical/clarity gaps: formalization of the local support restriction, treatment of non-network generative issue, and reproducibility details for synthetic data.

Overall: A well-executed, clearly motivated contribution that materially advances scalable probabilistic modeling of higher-order network structure. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on improved positioning versus a-MMSB, clarified theory/citations, and added experimental detail.
