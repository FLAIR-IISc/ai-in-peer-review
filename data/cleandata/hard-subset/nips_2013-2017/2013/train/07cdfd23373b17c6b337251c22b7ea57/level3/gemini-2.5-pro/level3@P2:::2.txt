### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a scalable probabilistic model for latent space inference in large-scale networks. The approach is built on three core principles: 1) representing networks succinctly as a "bag-of-triangles," following Ho et al. (NIPS 2012); 2) a parsimonious model that reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K); and 3) a scalable stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with an O(NK) time complexity per iteration. By sharing parameters for different latent role configurations, the model avoids the combinatorial explosion of parameters that plagues similar models. The authors derive an SVI algorithm with an innovative O(K) approximation for the local updates, enabling it to analyze networks with over a million nodes and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world data demonstrate that PTM is orders of magnitude faster than previous methods like MMTM and MMSB, while achieving competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The primary contribution of this work is a framework that makes latent space inference on triangular motifs practical for massive networks. The combination of the parsimonious O(K) parameterization and the efficient O(NK) SVI algorithm is a significant engineering and modeling achievement. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate speedups of several orders of magnitude over prior state-of-the-art methods like a-MMSB and MMTM, reducing runtimes from days to minutes/hours.

2.  **Novel Combination of Ideas:** The paper effectively synthesizes two powerful ideas from recent literature: the bag-of-triangles representation from MMTM, which captures important higher-order network structure, and the parameter-sharing and SVI techniques from a-MMSB, which enable scalability. While the combination may seem straightforward in retrospect, the execution, including the derivation of the variational updates, is non-trivial and well-executed.

3.  **Innovative Inference Approximation:** A key technical contribution is the "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local variational update. Instead of computing the full O(K^3) posterior over role assignments for each triangle, the algorithm approximates it using a distribution over a randomly selected O(K) subset of configurations. This is an elegant and pragmatic solution that dramatically speeds up the most expensive part of the per-triangle computation without sacrificing empirical performance.

4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The algorithm demonstrates fast convergence (typically within 2-5 passes), strong performance on latent space recovery across different network generation models, and competitive link prediction accuracy. The ability to scale to a million-node network with 100 roles is particularly impressive.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

Despite its strengths, the paper has several areas where clarity could be improved and methodological choices could be better justified.

1.  **Clarity and Presentation:**
    *   The relationship between the model's parameter complexity and the inference algorithm's complexity is confusing. The PTM model is stated to have O(K) parameters, yet the PTM Gibbs sampler is reported to have O(K^3) complexity. Is this high complexity inherent to any Gibbs sampling scheme for this model, or is it a feature of the specific blocked sampler used? A clearer explanation would help readers understand the source of the computational bottlenecks.
    *   The notation for the parameter space (e.g., distinguishing between simplexes Δ¹ and Δ²) is not well-explained, making the model specification in Section 3 harder to parse without referring to supplementary material.
    *   The claim that PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" feels overstated. ERGMs often incorporate a much wider variety of network statistics beyond triangles, and the analogy is not sufficiently developed to be convincing.

2.  **Methodological Concerns:**
    *   The paper does not discuss how the proposed triangle-based method would extend to directed networks, where triangular motifs have more complex configurations (e.g., cycles). This is a significant limitation for a general network analysis tool.
    *   The justification for δ-subsampling of triangles is brief. While citing prior work, it would be helpful to include more intuition or analysis on why this local, node-centric sampling scheme provides a good approximation of the global triangle distribution, especially for networks with complex degree distributions.
    *   The evaluation of latent space recovery relies on an arbitrary threshold of 0.125 to binarize the mixed-membership vectors. The robustness of the NMI scores to this choice is not explored. Given the goal of recovering a "set of non-zero roles," a model with more explicit sparsity assumptions on the θ vectors might be a more natural fit.

3.  **Experimental Questions:**
    *   A surprising result in Figure 1 is that PTM outperforms the MMSB variational algorithm on networks generated from the MMSB model itself. This is counterintuitive and warrants discussion. Is this an artifact of the initialization strategy, or does the parsimonious structure of PTM act as a regularizer that prevents overfitting, even when the model is misspecified?
    *   The link prediction experiments hold out only 10% of edges. Since triangle-based methods rely on higher-order structure, their performance may degrade more sharply than edge-based models in sparser settings. Evaluating the method with a larger fraction of held-out edges would provide a more stringent test of its robustness.
    *   The paper lacks a qualitative analysis of the learned latent roles. While quantitative metrics are important, showing what these roles represent in a real network (e.g., by examining the nodes with high membership in a specific role) would make the contribution more compelling and demonstrate the model's utility for exploratory analysis.

In summary, this paper presents a valuable and significant contribution to scalable network analysis. The proposed model and inference algorithm achieve impressive scalability and performance. Addressing the points of clarity and providing further justification for some of the methodological choices would strengthen the paper considerably.
