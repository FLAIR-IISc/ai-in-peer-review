### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference in large-scale networks. The authors identify three key principles for scalability: succinct network representation, parsimonious statistical modeling, and efficient inference algorithms. Building on the "bag-of-triangles" network representation from Ho et al. (2012), they propose the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). PTM is a variant of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) that dramatically reduces the number of model parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) by sharing parameters across different latent role configurations, a technique inspired by the a-MMSB model. To perform inference, the authors develop a stochastic natural gradient variational inference algorithm. This algorithm processes mini-batches of triangles and uses a structured mean-field approximation to maintain dependencies within latent role triplets, coupled with a clever O(K) approximation for local updates. The combination of the parsimonious model and the stochastic inference algorithm results in a time complexity of O(NK) per pass. The authors demonstrate their method on synthetic and real-world networks, including one with over a million nodes, showing it is orders of magnitude faster than batch variational inference for MMSB and Gibbs sampling for MMTM, while achieving competitive or superior accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

This is a well-executed and well-written paper that makes a significant contribution to the analysis of large-scale networks.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The proposed PTM model is a sensible extension of prior work, and the derivation of the stochastic variational inference algorithm appears correct. The key strength is the impressive empirical validation. The ability to infer a 100-role latent space on a million-node network in a few hours on a single machine is a substantial practical achievement. The experiments on synthetic data convincingly show that the method not only scales well but also maintains high accuracy in recovering ground-truth latent structure.

However, the quality of the experimental comparison could be improved. The paper compares its stochastic algorithm primarily against batch algorithms (batch variational for MMSB, Gibbs sampling for MMTM/PTM). A more direct and compelling baseline would be the scalable stochastic inference algorithm for a-MMSB by Gopalan et al. [5], which is mentioned as prior work. Comparing against another state-of-the-art stochastic method would provide a much stronger validation of the claimed performance improvements. While the paper does mention the runtime of a-MMSB on the Brightkite network, a direct comparison on the same machine and tasks would be more rigorous.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is compelling, and the authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing literature. The three principles for scalability provide a strong narrative framework. The model and algorithm are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the key ideas.

A few minor points could enhance clarity:
*   In the introduction, the term "parsimonious statistical modeling" is central to the contribution but is not explicitly defined until Section 3. A brief forward-looking explanation in the introduction would be helpful.
*   The description of the synthetic power-law network generation is missing from the main text. Including these details (perhaps in the appendix, as noted) is important for reproducibility.
*   The characterization of a-MMSB in the introduction could be more precise. The text states that down-sampling zero elements in a-MMSB "is still not fast enough," which is subjective. A more neutral phrasing that acknowledges the contributions of that work while motivating the current approach would be better.

**Originality:** The work represents a novel and effective synthesis of existing ideas. The core components—triangle-based representations, parameter sharing for parsimony, and stochastic variational inference—have been explored before in different contexts. However, their combination into the PTM model and the development of a tailored, structured SVI algorithm with an O(K) local update approximation is a novel and significant contribution. This work clearly advances the state of the art in scalable network modeling.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant. The problem of analyzing massive social and information networks is of great practical and scientific importance. By providing a method that is orders of magnitude faster than previous probabilistic approaches, this work makes latent space analysis accessible for networks at a scale that was previously out of reach for these models. Researchers and practitioners working with large-scale network data are very likely to find this method useful and to build upon it.

**Arguments For and Against Acceptance**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses the critical and challenging problem of scaling probabilistic network analysis to million-node graphs.
*   Presents an effective and novel combination of a parsimonious model (PTM) and a highly efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm.
*   The empirical results are extremely impressive, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups over relevant baselines with competitive or improved accuracy.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and positions itself well within the literature.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is weakened by the absence of a direct comparison with the most relevant state-of-the-art baseline, the a-MMSB algorithm of Gopalan et al. [5]. This makes it difficult to fully assess the performance gains relative to the best existing edge-based stochastic method.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement**

Overall, I am strongly in favor of accepting this paper. The weaknesses are addressable and do not detract from the core contribution. I encourage the authors to consider the following points for the final version:

1.  **Baseline Comparison:** As noted above, the most important improvement would be to include an experimental comparison with the a-MMSB algorithm from Gopalan et al. [5]. This would make the paper's claims of superiority much more robust.
2.  **Citation for SVI:** In the "Global Update" section, the paper should cite Hoffman et al. ("Stochastic Variational Inference," JMLR 2013) alongside Amari and Sato. The theory in Hoffman et al. is more directly applicable to models with local random variables, as is the case here, whereas Sato's algorithm is not. The JMLR version of the SVI paper should also be used in the bibliography.
3.  **Model Justification:** The argument on page 4 that the model is not a true generative process for networks is valid, but the analogy to topic modeling for documents could be strengthened. The issue of generating inconsistent triangles is a fundamental property of the modeling choice, and it might be better to discuss this limitation and its implications for inference directly, rather than relying on an analogy.
4.  **Minor Corrections:**
    *   Please correct the typo "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" (e.g., in the abstract and Section 3).
    *   The final sentence of the paper feels slightly redundant. The paper could end more strongly with the second-to-last sentence, which effectively summarizes the key achievements.
