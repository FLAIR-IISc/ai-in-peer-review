This paper proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference in large networks. The core idea is to represent networks as a "bag-of-triangles" and learn a mixed-membership model on these higher-order structures. The primary modeling contribution, which builds on ideas from the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) and alpha-MMSB, is a parsimonious parameterization of triangle-generating probabilities. By grouping latent role configurations, the model reduces the number of parameters from O(K^3) to O(K), enabling a highly efficient stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with O(NK) complexity.

The main strength of this work is its impressive scalability. The proposed method makes latent space inference on million-node networks feasible on a single machine, representing a significant practical advance over prior art. The parameter reduction is a clever and effective modeling choice for this domain. While much of the SVI algorithm is standard, the paper introduces an intuitive "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local update step, which is an interesting technique for maintaining scalability without sacrificing performance. The empirical results demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups over baselines with competitive accuracy on link prediction and latent space recovery tasks.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form. The clarity of the presentation could be improved; for instance, the distinction between ∆1 and ∆2 is not well explained, and the justification for why subsampling triangles provides an adequate approximation is insufficient. Several claims are questionable, such as the analogy to ERGMs, which use far more varied statistics. Furthermore, there is a confusing discrepancy between the claimed O(K) parameters and the reported O(K^3) complexity for the PTM Gibbs sampler.

The evaluation also raises concerns. The robustness of the latent-space recovery is not tested with respect to the arbitrary threshold of 0.125. A key result—that the method outperforms MMSB on networks generated from the MMSB model—is counterintuitive and warrants deeper investigation. The paper lacks a crucial discussion on the interpretability of the inferred latent space and fails to address how the method would extend to directed networks.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and highly scalable model for network analysis. However, its significant contributions are undermined by issues with clarity, methodological justification, and experimental rigor. I would encourage the authors to address these points to strengthen what could be an impactful contribution to the field.
