This paper presents a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference in large networks. The method builds upon the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), which represents a network as a bag of triangular motifs. The main contributions are twofold: (1) a new model, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) in MMTM to O(K) through aggressive parameter sharing, and (2) a stochastic variational inference algorithm with an O(K) approximate local update, enabling inference with O(NK) complexity. The authors demonstrate experimentally that their method is orders of magnitude faster than MMTM and MMSB on large synthetic and real-world networks, achieving competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Scalability:** The paper tackles the crucial problem of scaling probabilistic network models. The ability to perform inference on million-node networks with hundreds of latent roles in a few hours on a single machine is a significant practical achievement. The reported speed-ups over prior state-of-the-art methods like a-MMSB and MMTM are impressive and address a clear need in the community.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of positioning their work relative to MMTM and a-MMSB, explaining the limitations of each and how their proposed method addresses them. The generative process for PTM and the inference algorithm are described in sufficient detail.

3.  **Originality:** While the approach is an evolution of MMTM and employs standard techniques like stochastic variational inference, the specific way the model is made parsimonious (the PTM parameterization) is novel. This constraint on the parameter space is a simple yet effective idea for achieving scalability in both K (number of roles) and N (number of nodes). The "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for the local variational update is also a clever algorithmic contribution to reduce complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) per triangle.

### Weaknesses

The primary weaknesses of this paper lie in the insufficient analysis and experimental validation of the numerous simplifying assumptions and approximations made to achieve scalability. The side-effects of these choices are not adequately explored.

1.  **Motivation for Large K is Unclear:** The central motivation for the PTM is to enable scaling to a large number of roles (K). However, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate why this is necessary. Mixed-membership models can often capture complex structure with a relatively small K, as nodes can combine roles. The experiments do not include a comparison between PTM and MMTM where performance is shown as a function of K, which would be the most direct way to justify the need for a model that scales well in K.

2.  **Analysis of Model Assumptions:** The O(K) parameterization of PTM relies on very strong assumptions (e.g., all triplets of distinct roles share a single parameter B0; all pairs of a majority role `x` and a minority role share Bxx). The paper does not discuss the potential negative impact of these assumptions. It would be valuable to analyze scenarios where this parsimonious structure would fail to capture the true underlying network structure (e.g., if the true interaction tensor has O(K^3) distinct, meaningful values).

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Synthetic Data:** The description of the synthetic data generation is incomplete. The paper does not state how the ground truth B tensor was generated. Were the synthetic networks generated from a model that satisfies PTM's assumptions? To properly assess the model, experiments should include cases that are both favorable and unfavorable to its core assumptions.
    *   **Impact of Approximations:** The paper introduces several key approximations but does not study their impact. For instance, the δ-subsampling of triangles is a critical preprocessing step, yet there is no analysis of the trade-off between δ, runtime, and model accuracy. Similarly, the O(K) "mixture-of-deltas" local update is a significant approximation, but its effect on inference quality is not evaluated. How does it compare to the full O(K^3) update on a smaller problem? How does the choice of the size of set A (e.g., 3K vs 6K vs 10K) affect the results, especially for large K?
    *   **Fairness of Comparisons:** The experimental comparisons with baselines are undermined by a lack of clarity on model selection. The paper does not state how K was chosen for the real-world datasets. This is a critical hyperparameter, and without a clear and consistent selection strategy (e.g., based on held-out likelihood), the reported performance improvements might be due to a more favorable choice of K for the proposed model.

4.  **Fundamental Modeling Issues:** The paper inherits a conceptual issue from MMTM. The model is defined over a "bag of triangles," and the generative process does not guarantee that the generated triangles can be assembled into a valid graph. This means the model does not define a proper likelihood over the space of networks. While the authors acknowledge this, its implications should be discussed more seriously, as it calls into question the model's validity as a generative model for networks.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

*   The title "A Scalable Approach..." is quite broad. A more specific title reflecting the contribution of the parsimonious triangular model (PTM) might be more appropriate.
*   The paper criticizes a-MMSB for performance degradation due to downsampling zero-entries, but the triangular representation itself ignores all non-edges and single edges, which constitute the vast majority of potential interactions in a sparse network. The authors should provide a more nuanced argument for why their information-discarding approach is superior.
*   To strengthen the paper, I would strongly recommend adding experiments that directly test the core contributions. For example, show a plot of link prediction accuracy for PTM and MMTM vs. K on a moderately sized dataset. This would simultaneously motivate the need for large K and demonstrate PTM's advantage.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and impressively fast method for latent space inference. The core ideas for achieving scalability are interesting. However, the work is currently undersold by an experimental evaluation that does not sufficiently investigate the trade-offs and limitations of the proposed model and inference approximations. While the scalability results are strong, the claims of competitive or improved accuracy are not fully substantiated by the current experiments.
