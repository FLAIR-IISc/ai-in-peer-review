This paper proposes a scalable approach for making inference about latent spaces of large networks. The authors present a probabilistic latent space model, called the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which is based on a bag-of-triangles representation of networks. They also develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for performing approximate inference under this model.

The PTM is a mixed-membership model that defines the generative process for a bag of triangular motifs. The model reduces the number of parameters by partitioning the configuration space into several groups and sharing parameters within the same group. This yields a more compact and lower-dimensional model that allows for more efficient inference algorithms.

The authors also present a stochastic variational inference algorithm for performing approximate inference under the PTM. The algorithm uses a structured mean-field approximation method and has a time complexity of O(NK), making it scalable to large networks.

The paper presents several experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors show that their method achieves latent space recovery accuracy comparable to or better than prior work, but in only a fraction of the time. They also demonstrate that their approach is capable of scaling to large real-world networks, achieving convergence in a fraction of the time reported by recent work on scalable network modeling.

The strengths of the paper include the development of a scalable probabilistic latent space model and a stochastic variational inference algorithm that can handle large networks. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their method on several synthetic and real-world networks.

However, there are several weaknesses and areas for improvement. One of the main concerns is the lack of discussion on how the method would extend to directed networks. The authors also claim that the PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs," but this analogy is unclear and requires further explanation. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of the distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2.

The paper also raises several questions, such as whether the inferred latent space is interpretable and what it reveals about graph similarities/differences. The authors also do not evaluate the robustness of latent-space recovery to the chosen threshold (0.125) and its impact on the results.

Furthermore, the experiments do not specify which latent-space model generates power-law networks, and many models can do so. The paper also reports a counterintuitive result where the method outperforms MMSB on networks synthetically generated by MMSB.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but some sections could be improved. For example, the explanation of the PTM and the stochastic variational inference algorithm could be more detailed and easier to follow.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of a mixed-membership model with a triangular representation of networks. The authors also develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm that is specifically designed for this model.

In terms of significance, the paper addresses a difficult problem in network analysis and provides a scalable solution that can handle large networks. The results of the paper are important and can be used in various applications, such as community detection, link prediction, and exploratory analysis.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of network analysis and provides a scalable solution for latent space inference. However, there are several areas for improvement, and the authors should address the concerns and questions raised by this review.
