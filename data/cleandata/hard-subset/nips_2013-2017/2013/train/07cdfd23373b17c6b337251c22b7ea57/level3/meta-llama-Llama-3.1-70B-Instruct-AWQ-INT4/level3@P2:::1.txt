The paper "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks" proposes a novel approach to latent space inference for large-scale networks. The authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the number of parameters from O(K^3) to O(K), allowing for faster inference. They also present a stochastic variational inference algorithm and scalability tricks to further improve the efficiency of the model.

The main idea of the paper is to represent networks as a bag of triangular motifs, which is a more succinct representation than the traditional edge-based representation. The authors then apply a parsimonious statistical model and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm to perform latent space inference. The PTM model is shown to be more efficient than existing methods, with a time complexity of O(NK) compared to O(N^2K^2) for the Mixed-Membership Stochastic Blockmodel (MMSB) and O(NK^3) for the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM).

The authors evaluate their method on synthetic and real-world networks, demonstrating its ability to recover latent space roles and perform link prediction. The results show that the PTM model outperforms existing methods in terms of accuracy and scalability. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the model's limitations and potential biases, discussing scenarios where the PTM model may fail and the impact of simplifying assumptions.

However, there are several concerns and limitations with the paper. Firstly, the practical advantage of the PTM model is unclear, as it is not shown how learning MMTM with large K helps, since mixed-membership models may tolerate small K. Secondly, the experiments are incomplete, as the true B choices are not described, and cases both favorable and unfavorable to PTM are not included. Additionally, the impact of threshold δ on accuracy vs. scalability is not analyzed, and the local-update approximation is not sufficiently studied.

Furthermore, the reviewer is not convinced that the competitors were evaluated fairly, given the missing analyses and selections. The paper's originality is moderate, as the modeling is incremental, but constraining parameters for scalability is an interesting direction. The significance of the method is conditional, as it could impact practice if the simplifying assumptions hold, since scalability is rare for latent-space network models.

In terms of writing quality, the paper is clear and well-organized. However, the title is too broad relative to the paper's specific contribution (scalable PTM). The probabilistic validity of the PTM model is also questioned, as the bag-of-triples assumption may not correspond to any real network, so PTM/MMTM might lack a proper generative likelihood and place mass on "non-networks."

Overall, the paper presents a novel approach to latent space inference for large-scale networks, but there are several concerns and limitations that need to be addressed. The authors should provide more thorough analyses and experiments to demonstrate the practical advantage of the PTM model and address the concerns raised by the reviewer.
