This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) and a stochastic variational inference algorithm to scale latent space network inference to networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of latent roles. The core contribution lies in reducing the parameter count from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) by sharing parameters across latent roles, enabling significantly faster inference. The writing is clear and well-organized, presenting the methodology and experiments logically.

However, despite the technical soundness and clarity, several concerns limit the paper's overall impact. The primary issue is a lack of convincing demonstration that the scalability afforded by PTM is actually *necessary* or *beneficial* in practice. The authors argue for the need to analyze networks with many latent roles (K), but don't fully justify why existing mixed-membership models that may tolerate smaller K are insufficient.  The empirical evaluation primarily focuses on demonstrating speedup, rather than showing a tangible advantage in latent space recovery or downstream tasks *because* of the ability to use a large K.

A critical missing experiment is a direct comparison of PTM and MMTM performance as a function of K. Showing how accuracy degrades in MMTM as K increases, and how PTM maintains accuracy at larger K, would strongly support the claims.  Currently, the results suggest PTM is faster, but not necessarily *better* at recovering the underlying latent structure when K is reasonably sized for MMTM.

Model selection is also unclear. The paper doesn’t explain *how* K was chosen for the real datasets. This is a significant omission, as an arbitrarily chosen K could bias comparisons with competing methods. Without justification for K, it’s difficult to assess whether the observed performance gains are due to the method itself or simply to a more favorable parameter setting.

The simplifying assumptions of PTM, particularly the parameter sharing, are under-analyzed. The authors should discuss scenarios where this simplification might fail, for example, if the true underlying structure (B) has a large number of distinct values (approaching O(K^3)).  Exploring such scenarios would strengthen the robustness analysis.

The synthetic experiments, while useful, are incomplete. The paper states true B choices aren’t described, which hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess whether the chosen synthetic settings are representative or biased towards PTM.  Including cases specifically designed to be unfavorable to PTM (e.g., highly non-sparse B matrices) would increase confidence in the results.

The impact of the δ threshold on the accuracy-scalability trade-off isn’t sufficiently studied. The paper mentions δ-subsampling but doesn’t analyze its effect on performance, making it difficult to determine an optimal setting.

The claims about downsampling avoiding accuracy loss are questionable. The authors contrast PTM’s behavior with a-MMSB, claiming PTM avoids the accuracy loss associated with downsampling. However, the justification for why ignoring non-edges is acceptable here but problematic in MMSB is not compelling. A more thorough explanation is needed.

The local-update approximation (using O(K) parameters instead of O(K^3)) raises concerns. The impact of this approximation on quality, especially as K grows, remains unexplored.  The use of different sampling sizes (2K, 4K, 100K) isn't adequately justified. How does the quality of the approximation change with these different sizes?

Experimental fairness is also doubtful. The reviewer isn't fully convinced that the competitors were evaluated fairly, given the missing analyses and model selection details described above.

The originality is moderate. While constraining parameters for scalability is an interesting direction, the modeling approach is largely incremental. The significance is conditional – the method could be impactful if the simplifying assumptions hold, as scalability is a major challenge in latent space network models. The title, "A Scalable Approach…," is too broad, as the primary contribution is the scalable *PTM* specifically, not scalability in general.

Finally, the probabilistic validity of the model is questionable. The bag-of-triples assumption might not correspond to any real network, potentially leading to PTM/MMTM lacking a proper generative likelihood and assigning probability mass to invalid "non-networks."

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound and well-written approach to scalable latent space network inference. However, the lack of compelling evidence demonstrating a practical advantage over existing methods, combined with several analytical and experimental shortcomings, raises significant concerns about its overall contribution.  The side-effects of modeling and inference decisions are not thoroughly investigated.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Demonstrates a significant speedup over existing methods.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses a relevant and important problem (scalability).

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of compelling evidence of a practical benefit due to scalability.
*   Missing comparison of PTM and MMTM as a function of K.
*   Unclear model selection (K) and potential for biased comparisons.
*   Under-analyzed simplifying assumptions and potential failure scenarios.
*   Incomplete synthetic experiments.
*   Questionable probabilistic validity of the model.



I recommend rejection with encouragement to address the identified weaknesses, particularly by providing a more thorough empirical evaluation demonstrating the benefits of using a large K with PTM.
