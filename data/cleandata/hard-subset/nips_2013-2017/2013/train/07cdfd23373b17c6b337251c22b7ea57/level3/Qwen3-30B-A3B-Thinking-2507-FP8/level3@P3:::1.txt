

# Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference of large networks using a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that reduces the parameter complexity from O(K³) in the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) to O(K). The authors argue that by representing networks as a bag of triangular motifs, applying a parsimonious parameterization, and using stochastic variational inference, they achieve scalability to networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in hours. The paper builds on the triangular representation of networks [8] and extends the a-MMSB [5] by replacing the O(K²) parameterization with O(K) while maintaining a structured mean-field approximation.

The paper makes several technical contributions: (1) a PTM that uses a parameter-sharing scheme to reduce the triangle-generating parameter tensor from O(K³) to O(K); (2) a stochastic variational inference algorithm with an O(K) approximation for local updates; and (3) extensive experiments demonstrating scalability on large networks.

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it accessible to readers.
- The authors demonstrate significant computational improvements over prior work, with experiments showing PTM's ability to handle networks with 1M nodes in hours.
- The technical contributions to scalability through parameter sharing and stochastic inference are well-executed and well-motivated.
- The paper addresses a genuine challenge in network analysis: scaling probabilistic latent space models to large networks.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Practical advantage unclear:** The paper fails to demonstrate why learning MMTM with large K is beneficial. Mixed-membership models often tolerate small K, so the authors should show how larger K improves performance for real-world networks.
- **Missing experiments:** Critical experiments comparing PTM and MMTM performance as a function of K are missing. Without these, it's impossible to validate the claim that PTM's O(K) parameterization enables effective large-K learning.
- **Model selection unclear:** The paper does not explain how K was chosen for real datasets, risking biased comparisons with competitors. The choice of K should be justified with respect to the data characteristics.
- **Simplifying assumptions under-analyzed:** The authors should discuss scenarios where PTM fails (e.g., when the true B has O(K³) distinct values) and how these might manifest in real data.
- **Synthetic experiments incomplete:** The true B choices in synthetic experiments are not described, and the experiments should include both favorable and unfavorable cases for PTM to properly evaluate its limitations.
- **Approximation tricks insufficiently studied:** The impact of threshold δ on accuracy vs. scalability is not analyzed, leaving readers uncertain about the trade-offs involved in subsampling.
- **Downsampling claims questionable:** The paper claims PTM avoids accuracy loss that harms a-MMSB, but does not explain why ignoring non-edges is justified here but not in MMSB.
- **Local-update approximation concerns:** The impact of the O(K) approximation and different sampling sizes (2K, 4K, 100K) on quality as K grows is unexplored.
- **Experimental fairness doubted:** Given the missing analyses and model selection concerns, the fairness of comparisons with competitors is questionable.
- **Probabilistic validity questioned:** The bag-of-triples assumption may not correspond to any real network, meaning PTM/MMTM might lack a proper generative likelihood and place mass on "non-networks."
- **Title too broad:** The title claims a "scalable approach" but the contribution is specifically about scalable PTM, making the title misleading.

**Originality and Significance:**
The modeling approach is incremental, with the main novelty being the parameter sharing strategy to reduce complexity. While the constraint of parameters for scalability is an interesting direction, the overall contribution is moderate in originality. The significance is conditional on the simplifying assumptions holding in practice, which the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate. If the assumptions hold, this could impact practice since scalability is rare for latent-space network models.

**Quality Assessment:**
The paper is technically sound but has several gaps in experimental design and analysis. The theoretical claims are supported by experiments, but the experimental design lacks critical comparisons and thorough analysis of trade-offs. The paper does not adequately address its own limitations, particularly regarding the simplifying assumptions.

**Recommendation:**
The paper makes a valuable contribution to scalable network modeling, but several critical weaknesses undermine its scientific contribution. The lack of proper experiments to validate the key claim about large-K performance, the unclear model selection, and the insufficient analysis of assumptions significantly weaken the paper's claims. While the technical approach is sound and the writing is clear, these weaknesses prevent the paper from making a strong case for acceptance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Demonstrates significant computational improvements over prior work
- Introduces a novel parameter-sharing scheme for scalability
- Extensive experiments on large real-world networks

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Fails to demonstrate the practical advantage of large-K learning
- Missing critical experiments comparing PTM and MMTM as a function of K
- Inadequate analysis of model assumptions and their limitations
- Unclear model selection process for real datasets
- Questionable claims about downsampling without justification

In summary, this paper presents an interesting approach to scalable network modeling with a clear technical contribution. However, the lack of proper validation of the key claims about large-K performance and the insufficient analysis of the method's limitations prevent it from being a strong contribution to the field. I recommend rejection due to the critical experimental gaps and unaddressed theoretical limitations.
