

This paper proposes a scalable approach to latent space inference for large networks by leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The authors address a critical gap in network analysis: existing probabilistic methods struggle with networks having over 100,000 nodes due to computational complexity. Their approach achieves O(NK) time complexity (compared to O(N²K²) for MMSB and O(Nδ²K³) for MMTM), enabling analysis of million-node networks with hundreds of latent roles in hours on a single machine.

The paper makes strong technical contributions. The PTM model significantly reduces parameter dimensionality by sharing parameters across role configurations (O(K) vs. O(K³) in MMTM), which is crucial for scalability. The structured mean-field variational inference with stochastic natural gradient ascent further enhances efficiency, converging in just 2-5 data passes. The authors demonstrate compelling empirical results: on synthetic networks, their method achieves higher accuracy than competitors as network size increases, while being orders of magnitude faster. On real-world networks like Brightkite (58K nodes) and Stanford (1.1M nodes), their algorithm converges in minutes versus days for prior methods.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The key innovation—using triangular motifs as a succinct network representation—builds logically on prior work by Ho et al. (2013) while addressing their scalability limitations. The authors provide sufficient experimental details, including comparisons to multiple baselines (MMTM Gibbs, PTM Gibbs, and batch MMSB) across synthetic and real networks. The evaluation metrics (NMI for latent recovery, link prediction accuracy) are appropriate and well-justified.

However, several points require clarification or correction. First, the term "parsimonious modeling" should be explicitly defined (as requested in key points) to distinguish it from standard statistical parsimony. Second, the claim that "down-sampling zero elements in the matrix does not compromise accuracy" (p.1) is misleading; the authors should cite Gopalan et al. (2013) who demonstrated that downsampling preserves accuracy in their a-MMSB approach, rather than making this unsupported assertion.

The analogy to LDA (p.4) is problematic: the authors defend their triangle model by noting it's analogous to LDA, but the triangle model can generate non-networks (as the authors acknowledge), making this comparison inappropriate. The theoretical foundation for the stochastic variational inference algorithm should cite Hoffman et al. (2013) alongside Amari and Sato, as Sato's work doesn't apply to local variables, while Hoffman provides the necessary theoretical grounding.

In the experiments, the authors should clarify how they generated power-law networks in the latent space (p.6), as this is critical for evaluating their method's performance on realistic network structures. Additionally, they should compare against Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm (a-MMSB), which was specifically designed for scalability, rather than solely comparing to the batch MMSB.

Minor but important corrections include changing "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" (p.2), removing the final sentence on p.8, and citing the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference" in the bibliography.

The paper's significance is clear: it enables latent space analysis of networks at scales previously unattainable, which is crucial for modern social network analysis. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method achieves competitive or improved accuracy compared to state-of-the-art while being orders of magnitude faster. Their approach represents a meaningful advance in scalable network modeling, with potential applications in social network analysis, recommendation systems, and community detection.

The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria. The technical approach is sound with thorough experimental validation. The clarity is excellent, with well-structured arguments and sufficient detail for reproducibility. The originality is strong: while building on Ho et al. (2013), the authors introduce novel parameter sharing and inference techniques that address the scalability limitations of prior work. The significance is demonstrated through concrete results on networks of unprecedented scale.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to network analysis. With minor revisions to address the points above, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The authors have successfully developed a method that bridges the gap between theoretical network models and practical large-scale applications, advancing the field significantly. I recommend acceptance.
