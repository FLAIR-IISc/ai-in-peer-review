Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for latent space inference in large networks represented as a bag of triangular motifs. PTM groups the K^3 role-triplet configurations into classes based on the number of distinct roles involved, reducing the number of triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) (as in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model, MMTM) to O(K). It then develops a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with natural gradients that scales in O(NK) for global updates and uses an O(K)-cost local approximation via a “mixture-of-deltas” support restriction. Combined with node-centric δ-subsampling of triangles, the method handles million-node graphs and hundreds of roles on a single machine.

Relative to MMSB and a-MMSB (Airoldi et al.; Gopalan et al.), this work moves from edges to triangles, aligns with ERGM intuition that higher-order motifs add information, and builds on Ho et al.’s triangular representation (MMTM) by imposing parameter sharing and using structured SVI instead of Gibbs. The empirical results show orders-of-magnitude runtime improvements over MMSB variational and MMTM/PTM Gibbs, competitive latent-role recovery on synthetic data, and improved link prediction on several large networks.

Strengths
- Clear, well-organized manuscript; technical ideas are easy to follow.
- Elegant parameter sharing that dramatically reduces the core tensor to O(K), enabling O(NK) global updates.
- Structured variational treatment of per-triangle latent variables (vs naive mean-field) is appropriate and principled; natural-gradient SVI is a solid choice.
- Strong scalability results (up to 1M nodes, 100 roles) with multi-threading; convergence in few passes.
- Empirical speedups of several orders of magnitude over established probabilistic baselines; competitive or better link prediction on larger social networks.

Weaknesses and detailed evaluation

Quality
- Model misspecification and scope: PTM (like MMTM) is explicitly not a generative model of networks; it defines a likelihood over bags of triangles, possibly placing mass on sets of triangles that do not correspond to any graph. While stated, the implications for inference quality and downstream tasks (e.g., link prediction) are not analyzed. A discussion of conditions under which the bag-of-triangles posterior over θ is consistent or robust would strengthen the claims.
- Simplifying assumptions under-explored: The key assumption that many Bxyz entries can be shared (collapsed to B0, Bxx, Bxxx) is not stress-tested. There is no experiment where the ground-truth triangle probabilities actually require O(K^3) distinct patterns; no analysis of failure modes or performance degradation when PTM is misspecified.
- Local-update approximation: The O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” support restriction is appealing, but there is no sensitivity analysis to the number and type of off-diagonal configurations included. It is unclear how accuracy and the evidence lower bound vary with, say, 2K/4K/10K candidates or with full O(K^3) updates on small graphs.
- δ-subsampling: The effect of δ on accuracy vs. runtime is not quantified. Given that δ controls the amount of information discarded, a systematic study (varying δ) is needed to justify default choices and to compare fairly with edge-based methods that use non-edges.
- Model selection for K: Real-data experiments do not describe how K was chosen (validation splits, held-out bound, or standardized heuristic). Without this, comparisons can be biased in favor of PTM.
- Fairness and comparability: Some baselines are single-threaded while PTM uses multi-threading; compute budgets and convergence criteria across methods are not normalized. Also unclear whether the same train/validation/test splits (and identical negative sampling) are used for link prediction across methods.

Clarity
- Overall writing quality is high. However, several crucial details are deferred or missing:
  - Precise synthetic data generation, especially the specification of triangle probabilities (B) and whether cases favorable and unfavorable to PTM are included.
  - Full derivations for local updates (including xxy and xxx cases) and the exact natural-gradient sufficient statistics (appendix is referenced but not visible here).
  - The definition of link prediction scoring under PTM (marginalizing over k) needs to be fully specified to ensure reproducibility.
  - Stopping criteria, step-size schedule tuning, and hardware specifics for all methods.
  - Clear description of how A is sampled in the local approximation and whether the bound is monotone.

Originality
- The core novelty is the particular parameter sharing over triangle roles and the O(K) local approximation. These are reasonable but incremental extensions of prior triangle-based modeling (Ho et al.) and SVI techniques (Hoffman et al.). The paper’s contribution is pragmatic and engineering-focused rather than conceptually transformative.

Significance
- If the simplifying assumptions hold (i.e., triangle probabilities are largely class-dependent rather than role-triplet specific), the method could enable scalable latent-role analysis beyond the reach of prior probabilistic models. However, the current evidence is incomplete: the practical advantages of using large K are asserted but not demonstrated (e.g., no study of performance as a function of K comparing PTM vs MMTM where the latter is feasible). The claim that triangle representations avoid the accuracy degradation seen in a-MMSB with downsampling is plausible but unsubstantiated experimentally.

Requested experiments/analyses
- PTM vs MMTM as a function of K on datasets where MMTM is tractable (small/medium K), including ground truths where B has:
  - Shared structure (favorable to PTM).
  - Rich, heterogeneous O(K^3) structure (unfavorable to PTM).
  Report NMI, held-out triangle likelihood, and link prediction.
- Sensitivity to δ (e.g., δ ∈ {20, 50, 100, ∞}) on multiple graphs, plotting runtime vs. accuracy.
- Sensitivity to the local approximation (number/type of off-diagonals in A): 1K, 2K, 4K, 10K, and full O(K^3) on small graphs.
- Transparent model selection for K (held-out ELBO or standardized heuristic applied to all methods); report performance vs. K and ensure fair K across baselines.
- Normalize compute budgets and threading across methods, or report wall-clock/time-per-pass fairly with matched resources; include tuned a-MMSB where applicable.
- Discussion/theory: When does ignoring non-edges not hurt? When does triangle subsampling preserve enough information to recover θ? Provide either bounds or diagnostic checks.

Minor comments
- Title is broader than the contribution; consider a more specific title highlighting triangle-based modeling and PTM.
- Clarify memory footprint and the gain from discarding local variational parameters between iterations.
- Add a brief discussion on identifiability and potential role permutation/merging issues with large K.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong scalability with simple, elegant parameter sharing and structured SVI.
- Clear exposition; practically useful if assumptions hold.
- Promising empirical speedups and some accuracy gains on large social networks.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incomplete experimental validation: missing sensitivity analyses, K selection procedure, and fairness controls.
- Unanalyzed failure modes under model misspecification (when B truly requires O(K^3) variation).
- The bag-of-triangles likelihood and δ-subsampling’s impact on inference and prediction are insufficiently justified.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The paper is well-written and proposes a promising, scalable approach, but key empirical and methodological questions remain unanswered. With the additional analyses above, this could become a strong contribution.

Reviewer confidence
High on the methodological/empirical concerns; moderate on the breadth of related work in triangle-based models.
