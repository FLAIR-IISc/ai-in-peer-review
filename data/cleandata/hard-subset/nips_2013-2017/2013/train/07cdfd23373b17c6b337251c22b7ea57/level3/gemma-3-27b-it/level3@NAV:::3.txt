## Review of “A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks”

This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference. The core contribution lies in enabling analysis of networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine, a significant improvement over existing methods. The work is well-written and executed, demonstrating strong results on both synthetic and real-world datasets, and is generally acceptable for NIPS.

The authors effectively address the scalability challenges inherent in latent space modeling. By moving from an edge-based representation to a triangle-based one and employing parameter sharing in the PTM, they achieve a significant reduction in computational complexity, scaling linearly with the number of latent roles (K). The proposed stochastic variational inference algorithm further enhances scalability, enabling rapid convergence even for large networks. The experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method’s performance, highlighting its speed advantage over state-of-the-art approaches like Gopalan et al.’s a-MMSB and the MMTM Gibbs sampler.

However, several points could be clarified or revised. Firstly, the term “parsimonious modeling” on page 1 requires a clearer definition or unpacking for readers unfamiliar with the concept. Secondly, the introduction makes a misleading claim about down-sampling zeros not compromising accuracy; this should be revised considering the findings of Gopalan et al. Furthermore, the analogy drawn to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) on page 4 is flawed, as the triangle model can generate samples that are not valid networks.

The theoretical grounding could also be strengthened by citing Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato when discussing natural gradient methods on page 5; Sato’s algorithm doesn’t directly apply with local variables, and Hoffman et al. provides the necessary theoretical framework for the variational inference approach used. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of how power-law networks were generated in the latent space on page 6.  In the experimental comparisons, including a direct comparison with Gopalan et al.’s fast MMSB algorithm, in addition to or instead of batch MMSB, would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.  Finally, the last sentence on page 8 is unnecessary and should be removed, ending the manuscript with the preceding sentence. The bibliography should also be updated to cite the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference."

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Significant scalability improvement over existing methods.
*   Strong experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world data.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Addresses a challenging and important problem in network analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Minor terminological and factual inaccuracies.
*   Theoretical grounding could be strengthened with additional citations.
*   Experimental setup could benefit from more comprehensive baseline comparisons.
