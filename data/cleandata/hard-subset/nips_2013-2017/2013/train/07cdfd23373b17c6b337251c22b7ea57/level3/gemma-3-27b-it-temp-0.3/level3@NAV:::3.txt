## Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper presents a scalable approach for probabilistic latent space inference in large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference. The core contribution lies in achieving scalability to networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles, a feat beyond many existing methods. The work is well-motivated, addressing a critical bottleneck in network analysis – the computational cost of inference. The proposed PTM, through parameter sharing, significantly reduces model complexity compared to prior work like the MMTM, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm further enhances scalability. 

The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the challenges and proposed solutions. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach, showcasing significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art methods (MMTM, MMSB) on both synthetic and real-world networks. The comparison to Gopalan et al. is particularly valuable, highlighting the practical advantages of the proposed method. 

However, several points require attention. First, the term "parsimonious modeling" (p1) should be explicitly defined or unpacked for clarity. Second, the introduction contains a misleading claim regarding down-sampling zeros not compromising accuracy; this should be revised in light of Gopalan et al.’s findings. The analogy drawn to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) on p4 is inappropriate, as triangle-model samples can produce non-network structures, weakening the defense of the model. 

Regarding theoretical foundations, the paper should cite Hoffman et al. alongside Amari and Sato (p5). While Sato’s algorithm is relevant, it doesn’t directly apply with local variables, and Hoffman et al. provide the necessary theoretical framework for stochastic variational inference in this context.  Further experimental detail is needed regarding how power-law networks were generated in the latent space (p6).  Finally, comparing to Gopalan et al.’s fast MMSB algorithm, rather than batch MMSB, would provide a more direct and relevant benchmark (p6). Minor edits include changing "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" (p2) and removing the final sentence on p8 for a more concise conclusion. The bibliography should also include the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference."

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant scalability challenge in network analysis.
*   Novel combination of bag-of-triangles, parsimonious modeling, and stochastic variational inference.
*   Strong experimental results demonstrating significant speedups and competitive accuracy.
*   Well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Minor clarity issues with terminology and potentially misleading claims.
*   Theoretical citations could be more complete and accurate.
*   Experimental details could be more thorough.



**Overall Assessment:** Acceptable, pending minor revisions to address the points raised above. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to researchers working on large-scale network analysis.
