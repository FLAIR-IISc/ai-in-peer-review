Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a scalable approach to latent space inference on very large networks by: (i) representing networks as a bag of triangular motifs; (ii) introducing a parameter-sharing subclass of the mixed-membership triangular model—the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM)—that reduces triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K); and (iii) developing a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and an O(K) local update approximation, enabling analysis of million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine. The work builds on triangle-based representations (Ho et al., NIPS 2012) and mixed-membership models such as MMSB and a-MMSB (Gopalan et al., NIPS 2012), and uses natural-gradient SVI (Hoffman et al., JMLR 2013; Amari 1998). Compared to MMTM Gibbs and MMSB batch VI, the proposed method reports large speedups with competitive or improved latent role recovery and link prediction.

Quality
Technical soundness is generally strong: the variational objective and mini-batch construction are standard; the structured mean-field over (si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij) is appropriate; and the natural-gradient updates are well motivated. The O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” approximation for the local updates is a key engineering contribution—plausible and effective empirically—but it is heuristic. Please add an ablation (full K^3 local updates on small graphs versus the O(K) approximation) to quantify the tradeoff in ELBO and accuracy. Convergence guarantees should be stated carefully to reflect that the ELBO is optimized under the restricted variational family induced by the approximation. Claims about down-sampling zeros in a-MMSB should be corrected (see below). The experimental section is comprehensive on synthetic data and several real graphs, but a crucial baseline (a-MMSB) is missing.

Clarity
The paper is well written and organized. Suggestions to further improve clarity:
- Define “parsimonious statistical modeling” at first mention (p1): here it means using parameter tying/sharing induced by model symmetries to drastically reduce the number of free parameters (from O(K^3) to O(K)), thereby lowering variance and computational cost without materially sacrificing expressiveness for the targeted tasks.
- LDA analogy (p4): Using LDA as a defense for modeling triangles is not appropriate because samples from PTM/MMTM need not correspond to any valid simple graph (overlapping triangles need not have consistent edges). Please temper or remove the LDA analogy and justify the model on its own merits for latent space inference.
- Minor wording: replace “mixture-membership” with “mixed-membership” (p2).

Originality
The parameter-sharing PTM and the structured-SVI with the O(K) local approximation are novel and non-trivial extensions of prior triangle models. Applying SVI to triangle-motif data at this scale is a meaningful advance.

Significance
Scaling latent role inference to million-node networks with hundreds of roles is impactful. The approach may be adopted by practitioners dealing with large social or information networks. The model’s parsimony is also of conceptual interest.

Specific comments and requested fixes
- Introduction (p1): Correct the discussion of a-MMSB. Gopalan et al. show that subsampling zeros yields unbiased stochastic gradients and, in practice, does not compromise predictive accuracy. Suggested rewrite: “a-MMSB mitigates the O(N^2) cost by subsampling non-edges to obtain unbiased stochastic gradients with little or no loss in predictive accuracy; however, even with this, it remains slower than our triangle-based approach on million-node graphs.”
- Theory/citations (p5): When justifying stochastic natural-gradient variational inference with local variables, please cite Hoffman, Blei, Wang, and Paisley (JMLR 2013) alongside Amari (1998). Sato’s online VB does not directly apply in the presence of per-datum local variables in the same way; Hoffman et al. provide the required convergence theory and algorithms for global–local models.
- Experimental detail (p6): Move a brief, self-contained description of the power-law network generator into the main text. Specify the latent-space mechanism: how hubs are created, the exponent of the degree distribution, role popularity priors, how edges/triangles are generated from roles, and the exact parameters used. This is important for reproducibility and for interpreting the “power-law” claim.
- Baseline comparison (p6): Include Gopalan et al.’s fast a-MMSB baseline (code is available), with identical threading and hardware, and report both runtime and accuracy for latent role recovery and link prediction. If batch MMSB cannot scale, prioritize a-MMSB over batch MMSB for fairness.
- LDA analogy (p4): As noted, please avoid defending PTM via LDA, since triangle-model samples can be non-networks.
- Bibliography: Ensure the citation for “Stochastic Variational Inference” is the JMLR version (Hoffman et al., 2013) in both references and first in-text mention. Also add the a-MMSB NIPS citation where you discuss that baseline.
- Minor edits:
  - p2: “mixture-membership” → “mixed-membership.”
  - p8: Remove the last sentence and end with the second-to-last sentence, which is a clearer conclusion.

Strengths
- Clear main contribution: a scalable approximate inference algorithm for a parameter-sharing triangle model subclass, enabling SVI on very large networks.
- Strong empirical results with dramatic speedups and competitive accuracy.
- Careful engineering (structured mean-field, O(K) local approximation) and practical parallel implementation.
- Good discussion of when triangle representations are beneficial.

Weaknesses
- Missing a-MMSB baseline weakens the experimental comparison.
- Heuristic O(K) local approximation lacks ablation/diagnostics.
- Some claims about a-MMSB zero subsampling are misleading and should be corrected.
- The LDA analogy is not suitable for a model that can sample non-networks.

Pro/con list for acceptance
Pros:
- Significant scalability advance with solid empirical evidence.
- Well executed, clearly written, and relevant to NeurIPS community.
- Conceptually neat parameter-sharing design with practical impact.

Cons:
- Incomplete baseline comparison (a-MMSB).
- Limited analysis of the approximation’s effect.
- A few citation/terminology issues.

Overall assessment
Accept. The paper is well written and well executed. With the suggested fixes—especially adding a-MMSB comparisons, clarifying the power-law generator, correcting the a-MMSB claim, and tightening citations—this will be a strong and useful contribution.
