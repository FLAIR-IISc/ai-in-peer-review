## Review of “A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks”

This paper tackles the crucial problem of scaling probabilistic latent space models for network analysis to networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of latent roles. The authors propose the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), building upon the bag-of-triangles representation and introducing parameter sharing to reduce the model complexity from O(K^3) to O(K), coupled with a stochastic variational inference algorithm. The core idea of reducing parameters for scalability is interesting and addresses a significant bottleneck in existing methods like MMTM. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the paper easy to follow.

However, despite the technical soundness and clear presentation, the practical advantage of PTM remains unclear. While the authors demonstrate a reduction in parameters, they don’t convincingly show *why* learning with large K is beneficial. Many mixed-membership models function effectively with relatively small K, and the paper lacks a compelling argument for the necessity of scaling to hundreds of roles. This is further compounded by the lack of experiments systematically varying K to demonstrate the benefit of the PTM’s scalability. 

Several experimental aspects raise concerns. The model selection process for K on real datasets is not explained, potentially introducing bias in comparisons with competing methods. The impact of the δ-subsampling threshold on the trade-off between accuracy and scalability is insufficiently studied. Furthermore, the justification for ignoring non-edges during subsampling, while acceptable in PTM, is questioned in relation to its predecessor a-MMSB, without sufficient explanation. The local-update approximation, utilizing O(K) parameters, also lacks a thorough analysis of its impact on quality, particularly as K grows. The claims regarding downsampling avoiding accuracy loss, unlike a-MMSB, require more robust justification. 

The originality of the work is moderate; the modeling approach is incremental. However, the parameter constraint for scalability is a valuable direction. The significance is conditional, hinging on the validity of the simplifying assumptions made and the applicability of the bag-of-triples assumption to real-world networks. The title is overly broad, as the paper focuses specifically on scalable PTM, not a general scalable approach. A fundamental concern is the limited investigation into the side-effects of modeling and inference decisions. The probabilistic validity is also questionable, as the bag-of-triples assumption may not correspond to real networks, potentially leading to the model assigning mass to non-networks.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a critical scalability issue in latent space network modeling.
*   Clear and well-written presentation.
*   Demonstrates promising performance on large networks.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Unclear practical advantage of scaling to large K.
*   Insufficient experimental validation of scalability benefits.
*   Concerns regarding experimental fairness and model selection.
*   Simplifying assumptions under-analyzed.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject with encouragement to address the experimental shortcomings and better justify the practical benefits of the proposed method. A more thorough investigation of the model’s limitations and a clearer articulation of its advantages are needed before it can be considered a significant contribution.
