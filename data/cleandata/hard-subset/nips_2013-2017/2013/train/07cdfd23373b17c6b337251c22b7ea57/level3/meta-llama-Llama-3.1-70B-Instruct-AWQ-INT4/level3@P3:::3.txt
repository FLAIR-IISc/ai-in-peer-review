This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors introduce a parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that defines the generative process for a bag of triangular motifs, and develop a fast stochastic variational inference algorithm for performing approximate inference under this model. The PTM has a more compact and lower-dimensional nature than the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM), allowing for more efficient inference algorithms.

The main contribution of this paper is the development of a scalable approximate inference algorithm for the triangle model, a parameter-sharing subclass, and the use of stochastic variational inference for very large networks. The authors demonstrate that their algorithm achieves latent space recovery accuracy comparable to or better than prior work, but in only a fraction of the time.

The paper is well-written and well-executed, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach. The experimental results are impressive, showing that the algorithm can handle large-scale networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles in hours on a single multi-core machine.

However, there are a few areas where the paper could be improved. First, the authors could provide a clearer explanation of the term "parsimonious modeling" (p1). This term is not well-defined in the paper, and it would be helpful to provide a more detailed explanation of what it means and how it is used in the context of the PTM.

Second, the authors claim that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy, but this is not supported by the work of Gopalan et al. The authors should revise the introduction sentence (p1) to reflect this.

Third, the analogy between the PTM and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is not entirely fair. The PTM is a more complex model that can produce non-networks, whereas LDA is a simpler model that is specifically designed for text analysis. The authors should be more careful in their comparison of these two models.

Fourth, the authors should cite Hoffman et al. along with Amari and Sato, as Sato's algorithm does not apply with local variables, and Hoffman provides the needed theory (p5).

Fifth, the authors should provide more details on how the power-law networks were generated in the latent space (p6). This is an important aspect of the experimental setup, and it would be helpful to provide more information on how it was done.

Sixth, the authors should compare their algorithm to Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm in addition to (or instead of) batch MMSB (p6). This would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the algorithm's performance.

Seventh, the authors should change "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" (small comments/p2). This is a minor point, but it would improve the clarity of the text.

Eighth, the authors should remove the last sentence on p8 and end with the second-to-last sentence (small comments/p8). This would improve the flow of the text and make the conclusion more concise.

Finally, the authors should cite the JMLR version of "Stochastic Variational Inference."

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. With a few revisions to address the above points, it has the potential to be an even stronger paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks.
* The experimental results are impressive, showing that the algorithm can handle large-scale networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles in hours on a single multi-core machine.
* The paper is well-written and well-executed, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach.

* The authors should revise the introduction sentence to reflect the fact that down-sampling zeros does not compromise accuracy.
* The analogy between the PTM and LDA is not entirely fair, and the authors should be more careful in their comparison of these two models.
* The authors should provide more details on how the power-law networks were generated in the latent space.
* The authors should compare their algorithm to Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm in addition to (or instead of) batch MMSB.
