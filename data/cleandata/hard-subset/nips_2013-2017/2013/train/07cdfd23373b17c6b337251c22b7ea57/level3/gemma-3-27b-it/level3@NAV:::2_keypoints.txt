- Proposes a scalable probabilistic latent-space inference method using triangle-count representation, PTM, and SVI to handle million-node networks.  
- Novel idea: learning latent structure from triangle statistics (bag-of-words analogy) to dramatically reduce parameter space versus models like MMTM.  
- Technical contribution: combines aMMSB with MMTM and derives global/local SVI update rules, including an intuitive local-update approximation.  
- Empirical result: shows large runtime speedups and competitive accuracy on synthetic data, converging on a 1M-node YouTube network in reasonable time.  
- ERGM analogy is weak: ERGMs use many network statistics beyond triangles, so the comparison feels forced.  
- Binary role membership claim is unclear; recovering a “set of non-zero roles” suggests thresholding and possibly binary assignments would help.  
- Robustness to the chosen threshold (0.125) for role selection is not evaluated and requires further study.  
- Delta^1 vs Delta^2 distinction and the subsampling strategy are poorly explained, hurting reproducibility and understanding.  
- Counterintuitive finding: outperforming MMSB on MMSB-generated networks raises questions about model assumptions or experimental setup.  
- No discussion on extending the method to directed networks, limiting applicability to many real-world graphs.  
- Complexity/confusion: claim of O(K) parameters conflicts with reported PTM CGS complexity of K^3; complexity analysis needs clarification.  
- Sparse-network performance is untested: only 10% edge holdout is evaluated, leaving robustness to sparsity unknown.  
- Interpretability of the inferred latent space is unclear; the paper lacks insights on what the latent roles reveal about graph structure.  
- Acceptance positives: strong scalability, novel triangle-based approach, and seemingly sound SVI implementation.  
- Acceptance negatives: clarity, methodological robustness, thresholding, and interpretability concerns warrant a major revision before strong acceptance.
