

This paper proposes a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) for scalable latent space inference in large networks, addressing the scalability challenges of existing probabilistic approaches. The authors build on the triangular motif representation [8] to reduce the parameter complexity from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K) through parameter sharing across latent roles, enabling a stochastic variational inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity. The method demonstrates promising scalability, processing 1M-node networks in hours rather than days.

The paper's main strengths lie in its technical execution and clear presentation. The authors provide a well-structured description of their model and inference algorithm, with detailed experimental validation on both synthetic and real-world networks. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical contributions accessible. The method does achieve significant speedups over competitors (up to orders of magnitude faster), and the experimental results demonstrate competitive accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction.

However, several critical concerns undermine the paper's contribution and claims. First, the practical advantage of scaling to large K remains unclear. The paper does not demonstrate how learning with large K (e.g., K=100) provides tangible benefits over smaller K, which is common in mixed-membership models. The authors should explicitly show how PTM's performance improves with increasing K, rather than assuming it's inherently beneficial.

The experimental design has significant gaps. The comparison between PTM and MMTM as a function of K is missing, which is crucial to demonstrating the claimed benefit of PTM's parameter reduction. The paper also fails to explain how K was chosen for real datasets, risking biased comparisons with competitors. The synthetic experiments lack details about the true parameter configurations (B), making it impossible to assess whether the experiments were designed to favor PTM's assumptions.

The paper's modeling assumptions require deeper analysis. The authors claim that PTM's parameter sharing is justified by "real-world" network structures, but they do not explore scenarios where this assumption fails (e.g., when the true B matrix has O(K^3) distinct values). The probabilistic validity of the bag-of-triangles representation is also questionable, as the authors acknowledge this does not correspond to any real network, potentially invalidating the likelihood-based inference.

The paper's claims about downsampling require clarification. The authors assert that PTM avoids accuracy loss from downsampling that affects a-MMSB, but provide no theoretical or empirical justification for why ignoring non-edges is justified here but not in edge-based models. The impact of the δ threshold on accuracy vs. scalability is also not analyzed, leaving readers uncertain about the tradeoffs.

The local-update approximation (reducing O(K^3) to O(K) work per triangle) lacks sufficient exploration. The authors mention using random sampling of O(K) role combinations but do not analyze how different sampling sizes (2K, 4K, 100K) affect accuracy, especially as K grows. This is critical for understanding the method's limitations.

The experimental fairness is questionable. Competitors were not evaluated under identical conditions (e.g., MMSB was single-threaded while PTM used 4 threads), and the paper does not explain why certain baselines were excluded from larger experiments (e.g., MMTM Gibbs not finishing on large networks).

While the paper's technical execution is strong and the writing is clear, the contribution appears incremental rather than transformative. The parameter reduction technique is interesting but not sufficiently novel, and the experimental validation is incomplete. The significance is conditional on the validity of their modeling assumptions, which are not adequately justified.

Pro arguments: Strong technical execution, clear writing, significant speedups over existing methods, well-structured experiments on synthetic and real networks.

Con arguments: Missing critical experiments comparing PTM and MMTM as a function of K, unclear practical advantage of large K, unaddressed assumptions about network structure, insufficient analysis of modeling tradeoffs, questionable experimental fairness, and limited demonstration of why PTM's specific parameter sharing is superior to alternatives.

The title is overly broad, as the contribution is specifically about the PTM, not a general scalable approach to latent space inference. The paper would benefit from a more precise title reflecting the specific model and inference technique.

Overall, while the paper presents a technically sound and well-executed method for scalable latent space inference, the contribution is limited by unaddressed assumptions, incomplete experimental validation, and unclear practical advantages. The method could be significant if the modeling assumptions hold for a wide range of networks, but the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate this. The reviewers recommend rejection due to the critical gaps in experimental validation and justification of key modeling choices.
