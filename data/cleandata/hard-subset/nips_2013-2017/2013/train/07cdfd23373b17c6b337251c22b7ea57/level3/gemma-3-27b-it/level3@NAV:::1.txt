## Review of “A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks”

This paper tackles the important problem of scalable latent space inference for large networks, proposing the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) to reduce the parameter complexity of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) from O(K³) to O(K). The authors achieve this through parameter sharing and employ stochastic variational inference for efficient learning. While the approach demonstrates promising scalability, achieving inference on a 1M-node network with 100 latent roles, several concerns regarding practical advantages, experimental validation, and modeling assumptions need addressing.

The core contribution – reducing MMTM’s parameter count – is valuable, especially given the scalability challenges of existing latent space models like MMSB and MMTM. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the method accessible. However, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* learning with large K is beneficial. Mixed-membership models often perform well even with smaller K, so the practical impact of enabling significantly larger latent spaces remains unclear. 

A significant weakness lies in the experimental setup. The paper lacks a direct comparison of PTM and MMTM performance *as a function of K*. While the authors show PTM scales to larger K, they don’t demonstrate whether the benefits of doing so outweigh the increased computational cost. The choice of K for real datasets is also not explained, raising concerns about biased comparisons. Furthermore, the synthetic experiments, while demonstrating accurate recovery, lack detail regarding the generation of ground truth B values and don't adequately explore scenarios unfavorable to PTM (where the true B might exhibit O(K³) distinct values). The impact of the δ-subsampling threshold on accuracy is insufficiently studied, and the justification for ignoring non-edges in PTM (while being detrimental in a-MMSB) feels incomplete. 

The local-update approximation and its sensitivity to sampling sizes (2K, 4K, 100K) as K grows also require further investigation. The authors claim to avoid accuracy loss in downsampling, but the reasoning isn’t fully convincing. Finally, the probabilistic validity of the “bag-of-triples” assumption is questionable, potentially leading to the model assigning mass to non-network structures. 

In summary, the paper presents an interesting incremental advance in scalable latent space modeling. However, the lack of thorough experimental validation, particularly regarding the benefits of large K, and the under-analysis of simplifying assumptions, weaken the overall impact. The title is too broad, given the specific contribution focusing on scalable PTM.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Demonstrates a clear path to scaling latent space models; clear writing and organization; potentially significant if simplifying assumptions hold.
**Arguments against Acceptance:** Unclear practical advantages; insufficient experimental validation; under-analyzed assumptions; questionable probabilistic validity; experimental fairness concerns.
