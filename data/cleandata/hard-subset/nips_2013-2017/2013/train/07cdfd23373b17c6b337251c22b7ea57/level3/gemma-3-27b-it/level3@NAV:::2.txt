## Review of “A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks”

This paper presents a novel approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large networks, focusing on scalability through a triangle-count representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference (SVI). The core idea – learning latent structure from triangle statistics, analogous to a bag-of-words approach for text – is intriguing and addresses a critical limitation in existing methods that struggle with networks exceeding 100,000 nodes. By parameterizing triangle configurations to identify K roles, the authors significantly reduce the parameter space compared to models like MMTM, enabling analysis of million-node networks.

The technical contribution of combining aMMSB with MMTM is interesting, though the authors acknowledge it's implementation requires tedious bookkeeping. The derived global and local update rules for the SVI algorithm, particularly the intuitive approximation for the local update, represent a valuable addition to the toolbox for large-scale network analysis. The experimental results demonstrate significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art methods, achieving competitive accuracy on synthetic data and demonstrating convergence on a 1M-node YouTube network in a reasonable timeframe. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The analogy between PTM and ERGMs feels forced; ERGMs incorporate a much wider range of network statistics beyond just triangles. The claim of recovering a "set of non-zero roles" suggests a more binary role membership might be beneficial, and the robustness of the latent-space recovery to the chosen threshold (0.125) requires further investigation. The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained, hindering understanding of the subsampling strategy. The counterintuitive result of outperforming MMSB on networks generated *by* MMSB raises concerns about model assumptions or experimental setup. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks discussion of how the method would extend to directed networks, limiting its applicability. There is also confusion regarding complexity – O(K) parameters are claimed, yet the PTM CGS is reported as K^3. The robustness to sparse networks (with fewer observed edges) is not evaluated; the experiments only hold out 10% of edges. Finally, it is unclear whether the inferred latent space is interpretable and what specific insights it provides about graph structure and similarity.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Scalability: Significant improvement in runtime compared to existing methods.
*   Novelty: Interesting approach to latent space inference using triangle counts.
*   Technical Soundness: SVI implementation and derived updates appear correct.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Clarity: Several aspects (Delta^1/2, ERGM analogy) are poorly explained.
*   Methodological Concerns: Robustness, threshold selection, and sparse network performance are unaddressed.
*   Interpretability: Lack of discussion on the meaning of the latent space.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising approach to scalable latent space inference, offering a valuable contribution to the field. However, the methodological concerns and clarity issues need to be addressed before strong acceptance can be recommended. A major revision focusing on clarifying the presented concepts, rigorously evaluating robustness, and providing insights into the interpretability of the latent space is warranted.
