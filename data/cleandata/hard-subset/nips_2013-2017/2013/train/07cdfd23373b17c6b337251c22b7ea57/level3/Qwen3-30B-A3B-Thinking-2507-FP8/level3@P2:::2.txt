

# Review for "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"

This paper proposes a scalable method for latent space inference in large networks by leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference. The authors argue that three principles are crucial for large-scale inference: (1) succinct network representation, (2) parsimonious statistical modeling, and (3) scalable inference algorithms. They build upon the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM) [8] and the a-MMSB [5] to develop PTM, which reduces the parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) by sharing parameters across role configurations. The resulting inference algorithm achieves O(NK) complexity, enabling analysis of networks with over 1 million vertices on a single machine.

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound approach that successfully addresses scalability challenges in latent space inference. The stochastic variational inference algorithm is well-motivated, with careful derivation of both local and global updates. The experimental evaluation demonstrates significant speed improvements (orders of magnitude faster) compared to state-of-the-art methods while maintaining competitive accuracy on synthetic and real networks. However, some claims need clarification: the PTM is claimed to have O(K) parameters, but the PTM collapsed Gibbs sampler (used in experiments) has O(K³) complexity. This inconsistency weakens the argument for scalability. The evaluation of latent space recovery uses an arbitrary threshold (0.125) without justification or sensitivity analysis, which raises concerns about the robustness of the results. Additionally, the claim that PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is questionable given that ERGMs use more varied statistics and the analogy is not clearly established.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, though some sections could benefit from improved exposition. The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 in the parameterization is confusingly explained, and the rationale for the triangle subsampling procedure (using delta) is not sufficiently justified. The presentation of the local update approximation (O(K) vs O(K³)) is somewhat misleading, as the authors acknowledge the full parameterization would require O(K³) work but then claim the approximation "works as well as the full parameterization" without thorough validation. The intuition behind the approximation is compelling but could be better motivated.

**Originality**: The core contribution—combining a-MMSB's parameter sharing with MMTM's triangular representation—is not entirely novel, as the authors acknowledge it's a "straightforward combination" of existing techniques. However, the specific implementation of parameter sharing for triangular motifs and the development of the efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm represent meaningful improvements that enable scalability to much larger networks than previously possible. The paper adequately references related work, including the MMTM [8] and a-MMSB [5], and demonstrates how their approach differs from these methods.

**Significance**: The paper addresses an important challenge in network analysis—scalability to large networks with many latent roles. The demonstrated ability to analyze million-node networks in hours represents a significant practical advance over existing methods. The improved accuracy on link prediction for large social networks (Table 2) further demonstrates the value of the approach. However, the paper does not sufficiently explore the interpretability of the inferred latent space or what it reveals about network structure, which limits its significance for practitioners. The lack of discussion on extending the method to directed networks also represents a notable gap for many real-world applications.

**Arguments for acceptance**: 
- Demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speed improvements while maintaining competitive accuracy
- Enables latent space inference on networks with over 1 million nodes
- Provides a theoretically sound and well-motivated inference algorithm
- Includes comprehensive experiments on both synthetic and real-world networks

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- Inconsistent claims about parameter complexity (O(K) vs O(K³))
- Arbitrary threshold (0.125) for latent space recovery without justification
- Questionable analogy to ERGMs
- Limited discussion of method limitations (directed networks, interpretability)
- Counterintuitive results (outperforming MMSB on MMSB-generated networks) without adequate explanation

Overall, this is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable contribution to scalable network analysis. While the core idea is a combination of existing techniques, the specific implementation and empirical results demonstrate clear practical advantages. The paper would benefit from addressing the clarity issues noted above, particularly the parameter complexity confusion and threshold justification. The significance would also be enhanced by discussing interpretability of the latent space and potential extensions to directed networks.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the clarity concerns and strengthen the discussion of limitations. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of scalable network analysis, with the potential to significantly impact how large networks are analyzed in practice.
