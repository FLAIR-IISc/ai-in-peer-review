- Proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that models networks as a "bag-of-triangles" and learns mixed-membership on triangle motifs.  
- Main modeling contribution: a parsimonious parameterization that groups latent role configurations, reducing parameters from O(K^3) to O(K).  
- Enables a highly efficient stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with O(NK) complexity.  
- Major strength: impressive scalability—latent-space inference on million-node networks feasible on a single machine.  
- Parameter reduction is a clever and effective modeling choice for large networks.  
- Introduces a "mixture-of-deltas" approximation for local updates to maintain scalability.  
- Empirical results show orders-of-magnitude speedups over baselines with competitive link-prediction and latent-recovery accuracy.  
- Presentation clarity issues: distinction between ∆1 and ∆2 is not well explained.  
- Methodological justification lacking: insufficient rationale for why subsampling triangles is an adequate approximation.  
- Questionable claims: analogy to ERGMs is weak given ERGM statistic diversity.  
- Confusing discrepancy between claimed O(K) parameters and reported O(K^3) complexity for the PTM Gibbs sampler.  
- Evaluation concerns: robustness of latent-space recovery not tested with respect to the arbitrary 0.125 threshold.  
- Counterintuitive empirical finding—PTM outperforming MMSB on MMSB-generated networks—needs deeper investigation.  
- Lacks discussion on interpretability of inferred latent space.  
- Does not address extension to directed networks.  
- Overall: promising, highly scalable approach but weakened by clarity, methodological justification, and experimental rigor issues that should be addressed.
