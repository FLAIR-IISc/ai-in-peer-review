This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors represent networks as a bag of triangular motifs, use a parsimonious statistical model, and develop an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm. The method is shown to be several orders of magnitude faster than existing approaches while maintaining competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction.

The main idea of the paper is to learn latent structure from triangle-count statistics as a bag-of-words analog for graphs. The authors parameterize triangle configurations to identify K roles, reducing the number of parameters and improving scalability. They apply stochastic variational inference, deriving global and local update rules for the model. The novel contribution of the paper is a straightforward combination of aMMSB with MMTM, although it requires tedious bookkeeping and could be better presented.

The paper provides an intuitive and intriguing approximation for the local update rule, despite most of the algorithm being standard SVI. However, there are several concerns and limitations. Firstly, the method lacks discussion of how it would extend to directed networks. Secondly, the claim that PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs" is questionable, as ERGMs use more varied statistics and the analogy is unclear. Thirdly, it is unclear whether the inferred latent space is interpretable and what it reveals about graph similarities/differences.

Additionally, the paper has several methodological concerns. The distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2 is not clearly explained. It is also unclear why subsampling triangles per node (using delta) yields an adequate approximation. Furthermore, if the goal is recovering a "set of non-zero roles," the model might need a more binary notion of role membership. The robustness of latent-space recovery to the chosen threshold (0.125) is not evaluated and seems arbitrary.

The experiments in the paper also have some limitations. They do not specify which latent-space model generates power-law networks, as many models can do so. There is also confusion over complexity, as PTM is claimed to have O(K) parameters, but PTM CGS is reported as K^3 complexity. Moreover, the method's performance with fewer observed edges is not evaluated, as experiments only hold out 10% of edges.

One counterintuitive result in the paper is that the method outperforms MMSB on networks synthetically generated by MMSB (Figure 1). This suggests that the method may be capturing some aspect of the data that MMSB is not.

Overall, the paper proposes a mixed-membership blockmodel on triangle counts that enables a more scalable O(NK) algorithm. However, it has several methodological and presentation concerns that need to be addressed.
