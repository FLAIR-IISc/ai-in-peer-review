This paper presents a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model, and stochastic variational inference. The method achieves significant speedups compared to state-of-the-art techniques while maintaining competitive accuracy. The problem of scalable latent space inference is crucial for analyzing the increasingly large networks encountered in various domains. Previous work, such as MMSB and a-MMSB, has struggled with scalability due to computational complexity related to adjacency matrix representation and parameter dimensionality. This paper directly addresses these limitations.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a well-written and well-executed paper. The technical contributions are significant, and the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The paper is acceptable for NIPS.

**Detailed Comments:**

* **Clarity & Terminology (p1):** The term "parsimonious modeling" is introduced on page 1. While the general idea is clear, a brief unpacking of this term would improve readability for those less familiar with the concept. Specifically, stating that parsimonious modeling aims to represent complex phenomena with a minimal set of parameters would be beneficial.

* **Correcting Misleading Claim (p1):** The introduction states that down-sampling zeros in a-MMSB does not compromise accuracy (referencing Gopalan et al.). This is a misleading statement. While Gopalan et al. demonstrate *some* resilience to downsampling, it *does* impact accuracy – even if the impact is mitigated. The sentence should be rephrased to acknowledge that downsampling can affect accuracy but is used as a trade-off to improve computational efficiency. For example: “The a-MMSB improves upon MMSB by applying principles (2) and (3): it reduces the dimension of the parameter space from O(K2) to O(K), and applies a stochastic variational algorithm for fast inference, employing downsampling of zero elements in the adjacency matrix to improve speed, albeit potentially at a cost to accuracy.”

* **Inappropriate LDA Analogy (p4):** The defense of the model by analogy to Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is inappropriate. The authors state that “the analogy to LDA does not hold, as PTM samples can produce non-networks.” This is a crucial point. If the model can generate structures that are not valid networks, invoking LDA as justification for certain modeling choices is misleading and weakens the argument. This section should be revised to remove the LDA comparison.

* **Theory/Citation Fix (p5):** The discussion of natural gradient ascent requires a more complete citation of relevant theoretical work. While Amari and Sato’s work is foundational, Sato’s algorithm doesn’t directly apply with local variables. The authors should cite Hoffman et al. (2013) – *Stochastic Variational Inference* – alongside Amari and Sato. Hoffman et al. provide the necessary theoretical foundation for stochastic natural gradient ascent in the context of variational inference with local variables.

* **Experimental Detail (p6):** The description of how power-law networks were generated in the latent space lacks detail. Clarification on the specific parameters and process used to generate these networks (e.g., the power-law exponent, the method for assigning latent roles, the edge probability function) is necessary for reproducibility. A more precise description would be valuable.

* **Baseline Comparison (p6):** Comparing the proposed method to Gopalan et al.’s fast MMSB algorithm would be a more relevant benchmark than batch MMSB, given the focus on scalability. Batch MMSB is impractical for the network sizes considered in the experiments. Including a comparison to the a-MMSB algorithm would more directly highlight the improvements achieved by the proposed approach.

* **Minor Wording (p2):** On page 2, “mixture-membership” should be changed to “mixed-membership” for consistency and common usage.

* **Manuscript Ending (p8):** The last sentence on page 8 is redundant and detracts from the conclusion. The paper should end with the second-to-last sentence, which provides a concise summary of the contributions and achievements.

* **Bibliography:** The bibliography should include a citation to the JMLR version of “Stochastic Variational Inference” (Hoffman et al., 2013).

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Scalability Improvement:** The proposed method demonstrates orders of magnitude speedup compared to existing approaches for large-scale network analysis.
*   **Competitive Accuracy:** The method achieves comparable or improved accuracy on both synthetic and real-world networks.
*   **Novel Technical Contributions:** The parsimonious triangular model and stochastic variational inference algorithm are novel and contribute to the field.
*   **Well-Supported Claims:** The experimental results provide strong support for the claims made in the paper.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Misleading Statement:** The initial claim regarding downsampling accuracy requires clarification.
*   **Inappropriate Analogy:** The LDA analogy is flawed and should be removed.



**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, with rigorous derivations and well-designed experiments. Claims are well-supported. It represents a complete piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized, but the points outlined above regarding terminology and clarity should be addressed.

**Originality:** The approach is novel in its combination of a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious model, and stochastic variational inference.

**Significance:** The results are significant as they address a critical challenge in network analysis – scalability – and provide a practical solution for analyzing very large networks. This has the potential to impact research in various fields that rely on network analysis.
