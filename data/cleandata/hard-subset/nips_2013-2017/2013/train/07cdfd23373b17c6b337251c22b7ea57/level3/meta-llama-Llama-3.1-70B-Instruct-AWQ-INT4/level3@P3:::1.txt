This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks, which is a crucial task in network analysis. The authors introduce a new model, called the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), which reduces the number of parameters from O(K^3) in the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) to O(K), allowing for faster inference and better scalability.

The PTM is based on a triangular representation of networks, which has been shown to be effective in extracting informative mixed-membership roles from networks. The authors also develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for the PTM, which has a time complexity of O(NK) and can be parallelized across triangles. The algorithm is shown to be efficient and effective in recovering latent space roles and predicting links in synthetic and real-world networks.

The paper is well-written and well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and algorithm. The experimental results are also impressive, showing that the PTM can achieve competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction on large-scale networks.

However, there are some concerns and limitations that need to be addressed. First, the paper does not provide a clear explanation of how the number of latent roles K was chosen for the real-world networks. This is an important issue, as the choice of K can significantly affect the performance of the model. Second, the paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the simplifying assumptions made in the PTM, such as the assumption that the triangle-generating parameters can be shared among certain groups of latent roles. It is not clear how these assumptions affect the performance of the model, and whether they are valid in all scenarios.

Additionally, the paper does not provide a comparison of the PTM with other state-of-the-art methods for latent space inference, such as the a-MMSB model. This would be useful in understanding the strengths and weaknesses of the PTM and how it compares to other methods.

Finally, the paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the impact of the approximation tricks used in the stochastic variational inference algorithm, such as the O(K) approximation to the local update. It is not clear how these approximations affect the performance of the model, and whether they are necessary for achieving good performance.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of network analysis. However, there are some concerns and limitations that need to be addressed in order to fully understand the strengths and weaknesses of the PTM and its potential applications.
