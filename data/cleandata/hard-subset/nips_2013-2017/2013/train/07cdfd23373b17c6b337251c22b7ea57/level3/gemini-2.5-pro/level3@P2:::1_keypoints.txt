- Proposes the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), reducing MMTM's O(K^3) triangle parameters to O(K) by parameterizing triangle probabilities by number of distinct roles.  
- Introduces scalable SVI with node-centric triangle subsampling and an O(K) approximation for local variational updates.  
- Demonstrates orders-of-magnitude speedups on synthetic and real networks (up to 1.1M nodes) with competitive latent recovery and link-prediction.  
- Technically sound derivation and inference; empirical results show massive scalability gains.  
- Strong clarity and organization; motivation, model, and algorithm are well presented.  
- Moderate originality: combines existing ideas (triangular motifs, parsimonious parameterization, SVI) in a novel, practical way; O(K) local update is creative.  
- High potential significance if PTM assumptions hold broadly; can scale to million-node graphs with many roles.  
- Core assumption (parameter sharing across role configurations) is strong and unexplored; may fail when interactions are complex/asymmetric.  
- Experiments lack ablations: impact of triangle subsampling (δ) on accuracy vs speed is not analyzed.  
- Effects of the O(K) "mixture-of-deltas" local-update approximation versus exact O(K^3) update are not evaluated.  
- Fundamental probabilistic concern: modeling as a "bag of triangles" may assign mass to invalid graphs, risking theoretical inconsistency.  
- Unclear justification for necessity of large K; no experiments showing when large K outperforms smaller K mixtures.  
- Fairness of comparisons is questionable: model selection procedure for K is unspecified and synthetic data generation details are missing.  
- Suggested experiments: vary K with performance metrics, generate full MMTM synthetic data, plot NMI/AUC vs runtime for δ, and compare final lower bounds between approximations.  
- Title is overly broad and should explicitly reference the Parsimonious Triangular Model or its key contribution.  
- Arguments for acceptance: tackles a hard scalability problem, achieves huge speedups, clear presentation, and practical novelty.  
- Arguments against acceptance: strong untested modeling assumptions, incomplete/biased evaluation, missing ablations and robustness checks, and potential overstatement of contributions.
