### **Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"**

**Summary**

This paper proposes a scalable method for latent space inference in large-scale networks. The approach is built on three key ideas: (1) representing the network as a bag of triangular motifs, following Ho et al. (2012); (2) proposing a "parsimonious" version of the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM) where the O(K³) tensor of interaction parameters is reduced to O(K) by sharing parameters across role configurations; and (3) developing a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with a structured mean-field approximation to fit the model. The combination of these ideas results in an inference algorithm with a time complexity of O(NK), a significant improvement over previous methods like the batch MMSB (O(N²K²)) and the MMTM Gibbs sampler (O(NK³)). The authors demonstrate impressive empirical results, analyzing a network with over a million nodes in a few hours on a single machine and showing competitive performance on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks.

**Strengths and Weaknesses (Qualitative Evaluation)**

**Quality & Significance:**
The paper's primary strength is its significant contribution to the scalability of probabilistic network modeling. The ability to infer hundreds of latent roles on million-node networks is a major advance and addresses a critical need in the field. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating orders-of-magnitude speedups over relevant prior work. The work is technically sound, combining several powerful techniques (triangle representation, parameter sharing, SVI) into a cohesive and effective framework. The choice of a structured mean-field approximation that retains dependencies within a triangle's latent roles, rather than a fully-factorized one, is well-justified and likely key to the model's good performance.

**Originality:**
While the core components are not entirely new—the triangle representation comes from MMTM, parameter sharing is analogous to that in a-MMSB, and SVI is a known technique—the application of these ideas to create a scalable triangular model is novel. The specific parameter-sharing scheme for a triangle model and the development of an efficient, approximate O(K) local update step for the SVI algorithm are original contributions. This paper represents a thoughtful and well-executed synthesis of existing ideas to solve an important open problem.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, and the model and inference algorithm are described in sufficient detail. However, there are several points that could be clarified or corrected to improve the manuscript.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Misleading Characterization of Prior Work:** The introduction states that the a-MMSB algorithm "mitigates this issue [dependence on the O(N²) adjacency matrix] by downsampling zero elements in the matrix, but is still not fast enough...". This framing suggests that down-sampling zeros inherently compromises the method. However, Gopalan et al. (2012) show that this is a principled approximation to the full likelihood and does not necessarily harm accuracy. The sentence should be revised to more accurately represent the contribution of a-MMSB without implying a fundamental flaw.

2.  **Weak Justification for Non-Generative Model:** On page 4, the authors defend the fact that their model can generate sets of triangles that do not form a valid network by drawing an analogy to topic models like LDA. This analogy is inappropriate. A sample from a trained LDA model is always a valid document (a collection of words). In contrast, a sample from the PTM is not guaranteed to be a valid network, as edge consistency is not enforced across triangles. This is a key difference and a limitation of the model that should be stated more directly rather than being defended with a flawed comparison.

3.  **Incomplete Theoretical Grounding:** On page 5, the paper cites Amari (1998) and Sato (2001) for stochastic natural gradient ascent. While foundational, these citations are insufficient here. Sato's algorithm applies to models without local random variables. The algorithm presented relies on integrating out local variational parameters, for which the theoretical justification is provided by Hoffman et al. (2013) in their work on Stochastic Variational Inference. This is a critical citation that must be included to correctly ground the inference method.

4.  **Insufficient Experimental Baseline:** The experiments compare the proposed method against the batch MMSB and Gibbs samplers for MMTM/PTM. While these comparisons highlight the scalability benefits, they are not against the current state-of-the-art in scalable inference for the mixed-membership blockmodel family. A much more compelling and appropriate baseline would be the fast a-MMSB algorithm from Gopalan et al. (2012). Including a comparison to a-MMSB would provide a much stronger assessment of the method's relative performance in terms of both speed and accuracy.

**Detailed Comments for the Authors**

*   **p1, "parsimonious modeling":** This term is central to the contribution but is not clearly defined in the introduction. Please briefly explain that it refers to reducing the parameter space by sharing parameters (e.g., from O(K³) to O(K)), which makes the model more scalable and statistically efficient.
*   **p2, Minor Wording:** In the section "O(K) Approximation to Local Update," the phrase "mixture-of-deltas" is used, but the description refers to "mixture-membership" vectors for nodes. Please correct "mixture-membership" to "mixed-membership" for consistency with standard terminology (e.g., Airoldi et al., 2008).
*   **p6, Experimental Detail:** The manuscript states that a model producing power-law networks was used for synthetic data generation but refers to the appendix for details which are not present. To ensure reproducibility, please explicitly describe how these power-law networks were generated in the latent space.
*   **p8, Manuscript Ending:** The final sentence ("These results are orders of magnitude faster than recent work...") is repetitive of claims already made. The paper would have a stronger conclusion by ending with the preceding sentence: "Our implementation allows network analysis with millions of nodes N and hundreds of roles K in hours on a single multi-core machine, with competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction."
*   **Bibliography:** Please update the citation for Hoffman et al. ("Stochastic Variational Inference") to the 2013 JMLR version.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a well-executed and valuable paper that presents a significant step forward in scalable probabilistic network analysis. The proposed model and inference algorithm are effective and the results are impressive. Despite some weaknesses in the exposition and experimental comparison, the core contributions are strong. The paper is acceptable for publication, but addressing the points above, particularly the inclusion of the Hoffman et al. citation and a comparison to the a-MMSB baseline, would substantially strengthen the final manuscript.
