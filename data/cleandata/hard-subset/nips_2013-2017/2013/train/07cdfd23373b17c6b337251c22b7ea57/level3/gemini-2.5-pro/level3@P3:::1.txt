### **Review of "A Scalable Approach to Probabilistic Latent Space Inference of Large-Scale Networks"**

#### **Summary**

This paper presents a scalable method for latent space inference in large networks. Building on the Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM), which uses a "bag-of-triangles" network representation, the authors introduce the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM). The key contribution is a parameter-sharing scheme that reduces the MMTM's triangle-generating parameter tensor from O(K³) to O(K), where K is the number of latent roles. This parsimony is designed to make the model tractable for large K. To perform inference, the authors develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm that incorporates a structured mean-field approximation and several efficiency tricks, including a novel O(K) approximation for local variational updates. Experiments on synthetic and large-scale real-world networks demonstrate that the proposed method is significantly faster than previous approaches like MMTM and MMSB, while achieving competitive performance on latent space recovery and link prediction tasks. The method is shown to scale to a network with over a million nodes and infer 100 latent roles in a few hours on a single machine.

#### **Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses the critical and challenging problem of scalability in probabilistic network modeling. The ability to infer a large number of latent roles (K) is a valuable goal, as many existing methods are computationally constrained to small K. If the model's assumptions hold in practice, this work could be an important tool for analyzing massive networks.
*   **Originality:** While the work is an incremental improvement on MMTM, the specific parameter-sharing scheme to achieve an O(K) parameterization is a novel and clever idea. The broader concept of enforcing model parsimony to enable scalable inference is a promising research direction for complex probabilistic models.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors effectively explain the motivation, the proposed model, the inference algorithm, and the experimental setup.

#### **Weaknesses**

The paper's primary weaknesses lie in the insufficient analysis of its core assumptions and the incompleteness of the experimental evaluation, which leaves several key claims inadequately supported.

1.  **Under-analyzed Modeling Assumptions:**
    *   **Parsimony Assumption:** The central assumption that reduces O(K³) parameters to O(K) is very strong. The paper lacks a discussion of the potential limitations of this choice. In what types of networks or for what kinds of role interactions would this assumption break down? For instance, if the true interactions between roles are complex and cannot be grouped into the three categories proposed (all-same, one-majority, all-different), the PTM would be a poor approximation. This modeling choice needs more justification and analysis of its potential failure modes.
    *   **Probabilistic Validity:** The paper inherits the "bag-of-triangles" representation from MMTM, which does not define a generative process for a valid network graph (i.e., the set of generated triangles may not be consistent with any single graph). This is a fundamental issue that questions the model's probabilistic coherence and should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.

2.  **Incomplete Experimental Evaluation:**
    *   **Missing Core Comparison:** The main motivation for PTM is to enable scaling to large K where MMTM is intractable. However, the paper is missing the most critical experiment to validate this: a direct comparison of PTM and MMTM on a task (e.g., link prediction or held-out likelihood) as K increases. Such a plot would demonstrate the performance trade-off PTM makes for its speed advantage and is essential for understanding the practical benefit of the parsimonious model.
    *   **Lack of Model Selection Details:** The authors do not explain how the number of roles, K, was selected for the real-world data experiments. This is a crucial detail for reproducibility and fairness. Without a clear and principled method for choosing K, the comparisons to competitors could be biased if K was chosen to specifically favor PTM.
    *   **Synthetic Experiments:** The description of the synthetic data generation is insufficient. Critically, the structure of the true B tensor used to generate the data is not described. The evaluation would be far more convincing if it included experiments where the data-generating process both matches PTM's assumptions (a favorable case) and violates them (an unfavorable case) to test the model's robustness.

3.  **Insufficient Analysis of Inference Approximations:**
    *   **Local Update Approximation:** The O(K) approximation for the local variational updates is a key algorithmic trick for scalability. However, its impact on the quality of the final inference is not studied. How much does this approximation degrade the variational lower bound compared to the exact O(K³) update? Does its accuracy depend on the size of the random sample of role triplets, and does this dependency change as K grows?
    *   **Subsampling Hyperparameter:** The analysis of the `δ`-subsampling of triangles is also lacking. While this technique comes from prior work, its interaction with PTM is new. A sensitivity analysis showing the trade-off between accuracy and runtime as `δ` varies would provide valuable practical guidance.
    *   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper claims that its approach avoids accuracy loss from downsampling that affects a-MMSB. This is a strong claim that needs empirical or theoretical evidence. It is also not immediately clear why ignoring non-edges is justified here but problematic for edge-based models that must subsample zeros.

#### **Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you provide a qualitative discussion on the types of network structures or role interactions for which the PTM’s parameter-sharing scheme would be a poor fit?
*   The title "A Scalable Approach..." is quite broad. Consider a more specific title that highlights the parsimonious triangular model, such as "Scalable Latent Space Inference via Parsimonious Triangular Models."
*   Regarding experimental fairness, it is not clear that competitors were given a fair evaluation. The MMTM/PTM Gibbs samplers appear to get stuck in local optima. While this is a valid weakness of the samplers, it may not be a weakness of the models themselves. Better MCMC methods might exist. The comparison feels more like an algorithm-to-algorithm comparison than a model-to-model one.
*   In Section 4, the O(K) approximation to the local update involves randomly choosing a set A of off-diagonal role combinations. The quality of this approximation seems highly dependent on this random choice. Have you explored the variance in results due to this stochasticity?

#### **Conclusion**

This paper introduces an interesting idea for scaling probabilistic network models by enforcing parameter parsimony. The proposed PTM model and its associated stochastic inference algorithm demonstrate impressive runtime performance on very large networks. However, the work in its current form is incomplete. The core assumptions of the model and the approximations in the inference algorithm are not sufficiently analyzed, and the experimental evaluation is missing key comparisons that would be necessary to fully substantiate the paper's claims. While the direction is promising, the paper requires significant additional analysis and experimentation to be considered a robust and complete contribution.
