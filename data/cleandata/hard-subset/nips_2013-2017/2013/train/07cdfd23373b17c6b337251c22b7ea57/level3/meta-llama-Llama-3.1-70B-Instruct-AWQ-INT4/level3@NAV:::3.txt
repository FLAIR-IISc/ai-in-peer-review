This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors present a novel parsimonious triangular model (PTM) that represents networks as a bag of triangular motifs, allowing for efficient inference algorithms scalable to larger networks. The PTM model is a latent-space model that defines the generative process for a bag of triangular motifs, and it has a more compact and lower-dimensional nature than the mixed-membership triangular model (MMTM).

The authors also develop a stochastic variational inference algorithm for performing approximate inference under the PTM model. The algorithm has a runtime complexity of O(NK) and is capable of handling large networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of roles. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on synthetic and real-world networks, showing that it achieves competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction.

The paper is well-written, well-executed, and acceptable. However, there are a few minor issues that need to be addressed. Firstly, the term "parsimonious modeling" is not clearly defined, and it would be helpful to provide a brief explanation of what it means in the context of the paper. Secondly, the claim that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy is not supported by the work of Gopalan et al., and this statement should be revised.

Additionally, the analogy between the PTM model and Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is not entirely fair, as the PTM model can produce non-networks, whereas LDA is designed to model text data. Furthermore, the theory and citation for the stochastic variational inference algorithm could be improved by citing Hoffman et al. along with Amari and Sato.

In terms of experimental details, it would be helpful to provide more information on how the power-law networks were generated in the latent space. Additionally, a comparison to Gopalan et al.'s fast MMSB algorithm would be useful to demonstrate the scalability of the PTM model.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of network analysis, and the proposed PTM model and stochastic variational inference algorithm have the potential to be widely adopted. However, addressing the minor issues mentioned above would further strengthen the paper.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks.
* The proposed PTM model has a more compact and lower-dimensional nature than the MMTM, allowing for efficient inference algorithms.
* The stochastic variational inference algorithm has a runtime complexity of O(NK) and is capable of handling large networks.
* The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach on synthetic and real-world networks.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The term "parsimonious modeling" is not clearly defined.
* The claim that down-sampling zeros compromises accuracy is not supported by the work of Gopalan et al.
* The analogy between the PTM model and LDA is not entirely fair.
* The theory and citation for the stochastic variational inference algorithm could be improved.
* More information on the experimental details would be helpful.
