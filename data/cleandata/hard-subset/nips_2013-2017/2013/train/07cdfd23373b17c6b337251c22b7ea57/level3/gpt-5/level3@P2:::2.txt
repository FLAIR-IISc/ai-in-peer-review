Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a scalable probabilistic approach to latent space inference in large networks by: (i) representing a graph as a bag of triangular motifs (open and closed triads), (ii) introducing a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that reduces triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) (as in MMTM) to O(K), and (iii) developing a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with a structured mean-field family and a mixture-of-deltas approximation that brings per-triangle local updates down to O(K). The method supports mini-batching over triangles and natural-gradient global updates, and empirically scales to N ≈ 10^6 nodes and K ≈ 100 roles on a single multi-core machine in hours. The approach builds on prior triangular representation work (Ho et al., NIPS 2012), mixed-membership stochastic blockmodels (Airoldi et al., JMLR 2008), their scalable a-MMSB variant (Gopalan et al., NIPS), and stochastic variational inference (Hoffman et al., JMLR 2013). The paper shows competitive or better latent role recovery and link prediction versus these baselines, while being orders of magnitude faster.

Strengths
- Clear scalability story: from O(K^3) to O(K) parameterization and O(NK) global updates, with an O(K) local update approximation. Strong empirical scaling (1M-node Youtube in 4 hours, 8 threads) and fast convergence (2–5 passes).
- Uses informative higher-order motifs (triangles) with δ-subsampling to reduce data size while largely preserving signal; aligns with motif-based network modeling and ERGM practice.
- Structured variational family captures within-triangle dependencies that naive MF would miss; the mixture-of-deltas local approximation is simple, parallelizable, and effective.
- Extensive experiments on synthetic and real networks with comparisons to MMSB variational and MMTM Gibbs; competitive or improved NMI and link prediction accuracy.
- Solid engineering: mini-batching, natural gradients, memory efficiency (discarding local variational parameters after accumulating sufficient statistics).

Weaknesses and detailed comments
- Positioning vs. ERGMs: The claim that PTM is a “latent space counterpart” to ERGMs is overstated. ERGMs use a broad set of statistics (k-stars, triangles, covariates) to model graph distributions, whereas PTM models a bag of triads for latent role inference and is not a generative network model (as the authors note). Please soften and clarify this analogy, and cite ERGM work on triad terms appropriately.
- Directed networks: The approach and experiments are undirected. Many real networks are directed; the paper should discuss how PTM and its parameter sharing would extend to directed triad types (e.g., triad census classes such as transitive vs. cyclic triads) and the computational implications.
- Interpretability of latent space: While scalability and predictive performance are strong, it is unclear what learned roles capture. Provide qualitative analyses: top nodes per role, role prevalence across open vs. closed triangles, role-role interaction patterns, and case studies comparing roles across datasets.
- δ-subsampling: The argument that δ-subsampling “approximately preserves” triad distributions cites [8]; in this paper, please quantify the effect on inference (e.g., bias/variance tradeoff, accuracy vs. δ curves, sensitivity to degree heterogeneity, and large hubs).
- Evaluation thresholding: Latent role recovery is assessed by thresholding θ̂ at 0.125. This seems arbitrary. Please report robustness to the threshold (sweeps, PR/AUC for multilabel recovery) or use threshold-free metrics tailored to overlapping memberships.
- Binary vs. continuous roles: If the stated goal is recovering the “set of nonzero roles,” consider a more explicit sparsity-inducing prior or post-processing that induces binary memberships, and compare.
- Synthetic data details: The “power-law latent space” simulator is not specified; many models can yield power-law degree distributions. Please provide the exact generative process and parameters.
- Complexity statements: There is confusion between “PTM has O(K) parameters” and “PTM collapsed Gibbs is O(K^3).” The latter is due to enumerating (x, y, z) role triplets in blocked sampling despite the O(K) global parameterization. Please clarify this distinction and make the complexity accounting consistent throughout.
- Counterintuitive synthetic result: PTM outperforms MMSB on data generated by MMSB. Offer a careful explanation (e.g., better optimization via SVI, robustness from parameter sharing reducing variance, or mismatch between evaluation metric and MMSB objective) and, if possible, a controlled study (e.g., small N,K where exact inference is feasible).
- Link prediction stress tests: Only 10% edges are held out. Report performance under more severe sparsity (e.g., 50% holdout), cold-start nodes, and across networks with different clustering coefficients to illustrate when triangle-based modeling helps or hurts.

Quality
Overall technically sound. The variational objective, structured mean-field family, exact local update (Eq. 6), and natural-gradient updates are standard and correctly applied. The O(K) mixture-of-deltas approximation is intuitive and empirically effective, but theoretical guarantees (beyond standard variational lower bound arguments) about its bias would strengthen the paper. Claims about δ-subsampling adequacy would benefit from additional empirical quantification.

Clarity
Generally well written, but several definitions and notational choices could be clearer:
- Explain Δ^1 and Δ^2 early: Δ^1 is the 2-simplex (Bernoulli/2-category), Δ^2 is the 3-simplex (3-category).
- Improve the exposition of parameter sharing and induced equivalence classes; a small figure with examples would help.
- Unify complexity statements and explicitly separate global parameter dimension from per-triangle inference cost.
- State the power-law simulator precisely.

Originality
Moderate. The work combines known ingredients (triad-based representation, parameter sharing akin to a-MMSB, and SVI) into a coherent, scalable package, with a neat local update approximation. The main novelty is in the parsimonious parameterization tailored to triangles and the practical SVI design.

Significance
High practical impact. The method makes previously infeasible scales (N≈10^6, K≈100) tractable on a single machine with competitive accuracy. The triangle-based perspective is timely and aligns with recent interest in higher-order structures.

Questions for the authors
- How would PTM extend to directed graphs and weighted graphs? What are the parameter and runtime implications?
- How sensitive are results to δ and to the choice/size of the off-diagonal set A in the mixture-of-deltas approximation?
- Can you report recovery metrics across thresholds and/or introduce sparsity in θ to better match the “set of roles” objective?
- Please reconcile why PTM beats MMSB on MMSB-generated data and provide ablations isolating the effects of parameter sharing vs. SVI.

Arguments for acceptance
- Strong scalability with convincing empirical evidence and careful variational engineering.
- Competitive or better accuracy on role recovery and link prediction.
- Clear contribution beyond prior triangular and MMSB-based models via O(K) parameterization and practical O(K) local updates.
- Of broad interest to the NIPS community working on networks, inference, and large-scale learning.

Arguments against acceptance
- Some methodological and presentation gaps (directed extension, δ-subsampling justification, interpretability).
- Novelty is incremental: a combination of known ideas with one key approximation.
- Evaluation choices (thresholding, light holdout) leave open questions about robustness.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a valuable, scalable contribution with solid empirical results. Addressing the clarity issues and deepening the analysis (especially interpretability, δ-subsampling effects, and robustness) would strengthen the final version.
