This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference of large-scale networks. The authors introduce a parsimonious statistical model, the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM), and an efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm, which together enable the analysis of real networks with over a million vertices and hundreds of latent roles on a single machine in a matter of hours.

The PTM is based on a bag-of-triangles representation of networks, which requires much less computational cost compared to edge-based models. The model reduces the number of parameters by partitioning the configuration space into several groups and sharing parameters within the same group. This yields a dramatic reduction in the number of parameters, from O(K^3) to O(K), allowing the PTM to scale to far more latent roles than previous models.

The authors apply stochastic variational inference to the PTM, deriving global and local update rules for the model. The local update rule is approximated using a mixture-of-deltas variational distribution, which requires only O(K) work per sampled triangle. The global update rule is optimized using stochastic natural gradient ascent, which greatly simplifies the update rules while maintaining the same asymptotic convergence properties as classical stochastic gradient.

The authors evaluate their approach on synthetic and real networks, demonstrating that it achieves competitive or improved accuracy for latent space recovery and link prediction. They also show that their algorithm converges and infers a 100-role latent space on a 1M-node Youtube social network in just 4 hours, using a single machine with 8 threads.

The paper makes several contributions to the field of network analysis. The PTM is a novel and efficient model for latent space inference, and the stochastic variational inference algorithm is a significant improvement over previous methods. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, demonstrating its scalability and accuracy on a range of networks.

However, there are several limitations and concerns with the paper. The authors claim that the PTM is a "latent space counterpart to ERGMs," but this analogy is unclear and requires further explanation. Additionally, the authors do not discuss how the method would extend to directed networks. The paper also lacks a clear explanation of the distinction between Delta^1 and Delta^2, and the subsampling of triangles per node using delta is not clearly justified.

Furthermore, the experiments do not specify which latent-space model generates power-law networks, and many models can do so. The paper also reports a counterintuitive result, where the method outperforms MMSB on networks synthetically generated by MMSB. Finally, the robustness of latent-space recovery to the chosen threshold (0.125) is not evaluated and seems arbitrary.

Overall, the paper presents a significant contribution to the field of network analysis, but requires further clarification and justification of several methodological and presentation concerns.
