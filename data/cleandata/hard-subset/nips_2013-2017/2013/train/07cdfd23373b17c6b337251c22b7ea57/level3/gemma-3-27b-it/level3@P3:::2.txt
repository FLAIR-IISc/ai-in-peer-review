This paper proposes a scalable approach to probabilistic latent space inference for large networks, leveraging a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference (SVI). The core idea – parameterizing triangle configurations to reduce dimensionality – builds on prior work in mixed-membership models (MMSB, a-MMSB) and triangular representations (MMTM), aiming to achieve scalability to networks with millions of nodes and hundreds of latent roles.

The paper’s strength lies in its focus on scalability, a significant bottleneck in existing latent space modeling techniques. The combination of a succinct network representation (triangles) and a reduced-parameter model (PTM) achieves an O(NK) complexity, a substantial improvement over O(N2K2) (MMSB) or O(Nδ2K3) (MMTM Gibbs). The experimental results demonstrate this scalability, successfully inferring a 100-role latent space on a 1M-node YouTube network in a reasonable timeframe. The use of stochastic variational inference is also appropriate for handling the size of the datasets involved.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses in clarity, presentation, and theoretical justification. The novel contribution – the combination of aMMSB principles with MMTM – feels somewhat understated and the bookkeeping involved isn’t adequately explained. The description of Delta^1 and Delta^2 is absent, hindering understanding of the triangle subsampling strategy and its theoretical basis. While the authors claim the δ-subsampling approximates the triangle distribution, the rationale and a more formal justification for why this holds are missing.  The choice of 0.125 as a threshold for role membership appears arbitrary, and a sensitivity analysis to this parameter is lacking. 

Furthermore, the claim that PTM is a “latent space counterpart to ERGMs” is questionable. ERGMs incorporate a wider range of network statistics than just triangle counts, making the analogy unclear and potentially misleading.  The interpretability of the inferred latent space is also unclear. While the authors demonstrate the model can recover synthetic roles, it's not obvious what these roles *mean* in the context of real-world networks, and how the latent space captures similarities or differences between them.

There's a contradiction in the reported complexity: PTM is initially stated to have O(K) parameters, but PTM CGS (presumably the Gibbs sampler version) is reported as having K^3 complexity. This needs clarification. Concerns remain about performance with sparse networks. Experiments only hold out 10% of edges, which may not adequately test the model’s robustness in scenarios with significantly fewer observed edges. The counterintuitive result—PTM outperforming MMSB on networks *generated* by MMSB—raises questions about the experimental setup or the model’s assumptions; further investigation is warranted.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Scalability: The O(NK) complexity is a significant advance, enabling analysis of networks previously intractable.
*   Empirical Performance: The method demonstrates competitive or improved accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction.
*   Novel Combination: The integration of ideas from aMMSB and MMTM, while not perfectly presented, offers a potentially valuable new approach.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Clarity and Presentation: Several aspects of the method and experiments are unclear or poorly explained.
*   Theoretical Justification: The rationale behind certain design choices (e.g., δ-subsampling, the threshold of 0.125) lacks sufficient theoretical support.
*   Unclear Interpretability: The meaning of the inferred latent space remains elusive.
*   Potential for Misleading Claims: The analogy to ERGMs and the complexity discrepancy are problematic.



**Overall:** This paper presents a promising approach to scalable latent space inference for large networks.  While the technical contribution is valuable, significant improvements in clarity, theoretical justification, and presentation are needed before acceptance. The paper reads as a solid engineering effort with interesting empirical results, but lacks the polish and rigor expected of a NIPS publication. A revision addressing these concerns would greatly strengthen the manuscript.
