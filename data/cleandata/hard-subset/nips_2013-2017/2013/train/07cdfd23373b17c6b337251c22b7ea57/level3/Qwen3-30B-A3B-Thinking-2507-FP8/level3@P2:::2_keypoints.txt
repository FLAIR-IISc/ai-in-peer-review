- Proposes scalable latent-space inference using a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious triangular model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference.  
- Argues three principles for large-scale inference: succinct representation, parsimonious modeling, and scalable algorithms.  
- Builds on MMTM and a-MMSB, sharing parameters across role configurations to reduce nominal parameterization from O(K^3) to O(K).  
- Claims resulting inference achieves O(NK) complexity and can analyze networks with over 1 million nodes on a single machine.  
- Empirically shows orders-of-magnitude speed improvements while maintaining competitive accuracy on synthetic and real datasets.  
- SVI algorithm is technically sound with careful derivation of local and global updates.  
- Inconsistency: PTM is claimed to have O(K) parameters but the collapsed Gibbs sampler used in experiments has O(K^3) complexity, weakening scalability claims.  
- Evaluation concern: latent-space recovery uses an arbitrary threshold (0.125) with no justification or sensitivity analysis.  
- Questionable analogy: calling PTM a "latent-space counterpart to ERGMs" is not well-supported given ERGMs' more varied statistics.  
- Clarity issues: confusing explanation of Delta^1 vs Delta^2 and insufficient rationale for the triangle subsampling procedure (delta).  
- Misleading presentation of local update approximation—authors claim O(K) approximation "works as well" as full O(K^3) without thorough validation.  
- Originality is limited (straightforward combination of prior ideas), but the parameter-sharing implementation and efficient SVI enabling much larger scale are meaningful contributions.  
- Significance: practically important advance—enables million-node inference in hours and improves link prediction on large social networks.  
- Limitations underexplored: interpretability of inferred latent space and extensions to directed networks are not sufficiently discussed.  
- Arguments for acceptance: large speedups, scalability to million-node graphs, sound algorithmic development, and comprehensive experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: complexity inconsistency (O(K) vs O(K^3)), arbitrary threshold choice, weak ERGM analogy, limited discussion of limitations, and unexplained counterintuitive results.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to clarify parameter complexity, justify thresholding, and strengthen discussion of limitations and interpretability.
