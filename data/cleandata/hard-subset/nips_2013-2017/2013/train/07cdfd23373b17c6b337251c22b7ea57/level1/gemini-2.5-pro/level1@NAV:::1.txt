This paper presents a scalable probabilistic model for latent space inference in massive networks. The approach builds on the triangular motif representation from Ho et al. (NIPS 2012) but introduces a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) to overcome the scalability limitations of the original Mixed-Membership Triangular Model (MMTM). The key idea is a dramatic reduction in model complexity from O(K^3) to O(K) parameters by sharing parameters across latent role configurations, a principle also seen in models like a-MMSB (Gopalan et al., NIPS 2012). This parsimonious model is coupled with a highly efficient stochastic variational inference algorithm that uses a structured mean-field approximation and a novel O(K) local update, achieving an overall complexity suitable for million-node graphs.

The primary strength of this work is its outstanding significance and demonstrated impact on scalability. The authors tackle a critical bottleneck in network analysis, enabling the modeling of million-node graphs with hundreds of latent roles in mere hours on a single machine. This represents an orders-of-magnitude speedup over prior state-of-the-art methods. The quality of the work is substantiated by a comprehensive experimental evaluation, showing that the proposed method is not only faster but also achieves competitive or superior accuracy in latent space recovery and link prediction tasks compared to established baselines like MMSB and MMTM. The originality lies in the effective synthesis of known techniques—triangular representations, parameter sharing, and stochastic inference—into a novel and powerful system. The paper is well-written and clearly positions its contributions.

A minor weakness is that the modeling framework, as the authors note, is not a generative model of networks *per se*, as a collection of triangles may not form a valid graph. While the focus is on inference, a more thorough discussion of the implications of this choice would be beneficial. Furthermore, the O(K) approximation for the local variational update, while empirically effective, could be better motivated. A brief analysis of this approximation's sensitivity would strengthen the technical contribution.

Overall, this is an excellent paper making a strong practical contribution.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Achieves a significant, orders-of-magnitude speedup in latent space inference for large networks.
*   Novel and highly effective combination of established techniques.
*   Strong and convincing empirical results across multiple tasks and datasets.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The model is not strictly generative for networks, a limitation that could be explored further.
*   The justification for the O(K) local update approximation is primarily empirical.

The contributions in scalability far outweigh these minor limitations, making this a valuable addition to the conference.
