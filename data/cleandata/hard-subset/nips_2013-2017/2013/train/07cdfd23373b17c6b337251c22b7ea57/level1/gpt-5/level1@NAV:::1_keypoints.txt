- Proposes scalable latent-space inference by representing graphs as a bag of triangular motifs.  
- Introduces Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that groups triangle role configurations to reduce triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K).  
- Develops structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) with natural gradients and an O(K) local-update “mixture-of-deltas” approximation.  
- Targets million-node networks and hundreds of roles, demonstrating substantial speedups over MMSB/a-MMSB and MMTM.  
- Reports competitive or improved accuracy in latent role recovery and link prediction on synthetic and real data.  
- Builds on prior work: MMSB, a-MMSB, motif-based MMTM, ERGM statistics, and SVI/natural gradients.  
- Grouping triangle role configurations by equivalence classes is well-motivated and yields a coherent PTM likelihood.  
- Structured mean-field plus stochastic natural gradients is standard and appropriate for scalability.  
- The O(K) local approximation is pragmatic but its approximation error (bias/variance) is not quantified.  
- Empirical results are extensive, though some comparisons (e.g., triangle-based link scoring vs. MMSB edge probabilities) may not be apples-to-apples.  
- Reliance on seeded initialization for synthetic experiments could bias recovery comparisons; robustness to unseeded starts is only briefly addressed.  
- Paper is generally clear and well-organized; parameter-sharing explanation is helpful.  
- A compact figure mapping role configurations to shared parameters would improve readability.  
- Some algorithmic details are deferred to the appendix; a concise pseudo-code block for local/global updates would aid reproducibility.  
- Key novelty: achieving O(K) parameterization for triangle-based latent role models and coupling it with scalable SVI.  
- Parameter-sharing echoes a-MMSB but the combination with motif-based modeling and the O(K) local approximation is original and practically impactful.  
- Significant: scales to ~1M nodes and ~100 roles on a single machine with convincing speedups.  
- Pros for acceptance: clear modeling advance, demonstrated scalability with competitive accuracy, and careful engineering (mini-batching, structured mean-field, natural gradients, parallelism).  
- Cons against acceptance: limited theoretical analysis of the O(K) approximation and identifiability, seeding on synthetic data, missing comparisons to strong non-probabilistic baselines (DeepWalk/node2vec, GNNs), and mixed wall-clock/thread-count reporting.  
- Suggested additions: ablations (structured vs naive mean-field, mixture-of-deltas size), evaluation of unseeded initialization, comparisons to popular embedding baselines, per-core timing and memory profiles, and sensitivity analyses for δ and K.
