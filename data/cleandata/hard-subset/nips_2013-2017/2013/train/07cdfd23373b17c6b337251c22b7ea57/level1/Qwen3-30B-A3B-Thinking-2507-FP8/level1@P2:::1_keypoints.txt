- Proposes a scalable latent-space inference method using a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious triangle-generating PTM, and stochastic variational inference.  
- Claims to satisfy three scalability principles: succinct triangle-based representation, O(K) parameters vs O(K^3), and O(NK) inference complexity.  
- Provides solid theoretical justification for parameter sharing that reduces parameter space from O(K^3) to O(K).  
- Introduces a structured mean-field stochastic variational algorithm that captures key latent dependencies.  
- Experimental results on synthetic data show near-perfect latent recovery (NMI ≈ 1) and superior convergence behavior.  
- Real-world experiments (Brightkite, Stanford) demonstrate orders-of-magnitude speedups (minutes vs hours/days) while maintaining or improving link-prediction accuracy.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations: triangle representation loses some network information and requires preprocessing.  
- Paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized, with reproducible details, figures, and hyperparameter choices.  
- Novelty lies in combining bag-of-triangles and SVI with a new O(K) parameterization and a structured mean-field local update.  
- Significance is high: enables probabilistic analysis of networks with >1M nodes and hundreds of roles on a single machine.  
- Strength: clear identification of scalability bottleneck and a principled, effective solution.  
- Strength: comprehensive validation across synthetic and real datasets with strong speed/accuracy tradeoffs.  
- Weakness: limited exploration of trade-offs between triangular representation and full adjacency information.  
- Weakness: δ-subsampling procedure and its behavior across network types are under-analyzed.  
- Weakness: scalability to beyond 1M nodes and distributed settings is not discussed.  
- Weakness: initialization that seeds 2% of nodes with true roles may be unrealistic in practice.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses critical scalability challenge, strong theory (O(K) parameterization), and convincing empirical gains.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete analysis of triangle-representation trade-offs, lack of extensions to dynamic networks, and limited discussion of δ-subsampling robustness.  
- Overall recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, significant impact, and well-validated despite minor limitations.
