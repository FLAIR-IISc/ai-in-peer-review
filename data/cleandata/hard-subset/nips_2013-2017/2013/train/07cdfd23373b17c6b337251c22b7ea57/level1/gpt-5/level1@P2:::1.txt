Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper tackles scalable latent space inference for large networks by combining (i) a succinct “bag of triangles” network representation, (ii) a parsimonious probabilistic model—the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM)—that shares parameters across role configurations, reducing triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K), and (iii) a stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm with structured mean-field and natural gradients, further approximated via an O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” local variational family. The approach enables inference on networks with >1M nodes and hundreds of roles on a single multi-core machine.

The work builds directly on Ho et al. (NIPS’12) who introduced triangular representations and the MMTM (O(K^3) tensor), and on scalable inference for MMSB (e.g., a-MMSB of Gopalan et al.) that reduced O(K^2) to O(K) parameters for inter-block links and used SVI but still operated on edges. The use of ERGM-inspired triangular statistics is well-motivated. The key novelties here are (a) the role-configuration partitioning and parameter sharing for triangles (B0, Bxx, Bxxx) to regain tractability, and (b) a structured SVI with per-triangle joint variational distributions and an O(K) local update via mixture-of-deltas. Experiments compare to MMSB variational inference and MMTM/PTM Gibbs, showing orders-of-magnitude speedups and competitive or improved accuracy in latent role recovery and link prediction.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The PTM is clearly specified; the parameter-sharing scheme is sensible and mirrors the a-MMSB simplification for edges. The SVI derivation is standard; unbiasedness of the stochastic gradients w.r.t. triangle subsampling is correct. The structured mean-field for the role triplet addresses a well-known failure mode of naive mean-field on high-rank posteriors. The mixture-of-deltas restriction gives O(K) local updates and preserves a valid variational lower bound; however, two aspects deserve more rigor: (1) the analysis of convergence when the local variational family is randomly resampled (A) across iterations; and (2) empirical ablations quantifying the gap between exact K^3 local updates and the O(K) approximation in terms of the bound and downstream performance. Claims about “usually converges after 2 passes” are well supported by plots.
- Empirical evaluation: The synthetic recovery task is carefully set up with both MMSB-like and power-law generators, scaling K with N. The seeding strategy (2% nodes initialized with ground-truth roles) equalizes initialization across methods but is optimistic for all, and may favor methods sensitive to initialization; it would be useful to report results with fully random initializations or a data-driven seeding (the authors suggest one). Runtime comparisons per pass are fair and highlight asymptotics. On real networks, convergence curves and wall-clock times are compelling; comparisons against published times (a-MMSB on Brightkite; MMTM on Stanford) strengthen the case. Link prediction results against MMSB are promising, particularly on social graphs with high clustering; nevertheless, additional baselines (e.g., nonprobabilistic overlapping community methods) and results on low-transitivity graphs would clarify scope.
- Limitations: The model is not a generative model of full networks; the paper is clear about this. The triangular representation discards dyads not in connected triples and treats overlapping triangles independently, which may bias inference on graphs with few triangles or strong non-triadic structure. The PTM’s shared cross-role parameter (B0) and shared “majority/minority” parameters (Bxx) trade expressiveness for scalability; this could hurt performance when specific inter-role patterns matter.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and organized. The generative process and the equivalence-class argument for triangle probabilities are clear. The SVI algorithm and step sizes are specified; appendices (not included here) are referenced for derivations. Two clarity suggestions:
- Complexity: At places the text states O(NK) complexity; elsewhere it acknowledges O(Nδ^2K) per pass due to m=O(Nδ^2) triangles. Make this distinction explicit early to avoid confusion.
- Convergence theory: Provide a brief justification or citation for convergence with changing local variational support (random A sets), or fix A per iteration and discuss the implications.

Originality:
Moderately high. Triangular representations and mixed-membership latent roles are known; parameter sharing to collapse O(K^3) to O(K) in triangle models and the specific structured SVI with O(K) local updates are novel and practically impactful. The combination of these ideas to hit the million-node regime is a noteworthy contribution for NIPS 2013.

Significance:
High practical significance. The ability to perform latent role inference with K up to hundreds on million-node networks in hours is compelling, and the method outperforms or matches strong baselines in speed and, often, accuracy. The approach is most relevant for highly clustered networks (e.g., social graphs). Its reduced expressiveness relative to full MMTM or MMSB may limit applicability in domains where specific inter-role interactions or non-triadic structures dominate; demonstrating robustness beyond social networks would broaden impact.

Pros:
- Elegant parameter sharing reduces O(K^3) to O(K) without ad hoc heuristics.
- Structured SVI with O(K) local updates; simple and parallelizable implementation.
- Strong scalability: million-node, 100-role inference in hours on a single machine.
- Competitive or improved accuracy on latent space recovery and link prediction.
- Clear exposition of model assumptions and limitations.

Cons:
- Theoretical guarantees for the mixture-of-deltas approximation with random A are not discussed.
- Evaluation relies on seeded initializations; robustness to uninformed starts is not shown.
- Limited baselines on real data; no comparison to nonprobabilistic scalable community detection.
- Applicability to low-clustering or bipartite-like graphs is not evaluated.
- The PTM’s expressiveness is constrained (single shared cross-role parameter), which may miss structured inter-role heterogeneity.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide ablations comparing exact K^3 local updates vs. O(K) mixture-of-deltas on small graphs to quantify the approximation cost.
- Add experiments on networks with low transitivity (e.g., technological or web graphs) to delineate scope.
- Report results with random/data-driven initializations and sensitivity to δ, minibatch size, and K.
- Compare to at least one strong nonprobabilistic scalable overlapping community baseline.
- Clarify complexity and memory footprints; include wall-clock breakdown for preprocessing vs. inference.

Overall assessment:
A solid and timely contribution that advances scalable probabilistic latent space inference by a careful mix of representation, modeling, and inference design. Despite some theoretical and empirical gaps, the work meets the NIPS criteria well, with strong significance and good technical quality. I lean toward acceptance.
