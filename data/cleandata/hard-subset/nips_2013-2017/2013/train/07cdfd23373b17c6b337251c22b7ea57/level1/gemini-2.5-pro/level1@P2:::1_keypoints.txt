- Tackles scalable latent-space inference for large networks using a succinct triangle-based representation, a parsimonious model, and scalable inference.
- Introduces the Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that reduces triangle-generation parameters from O(K^3) to O(K) via parameter sharing.
- Develops a stochastic variational inference algorithm with natural gradients that processes mini-batches of triangles for scalability.
- Proposes an O(K) approximation to the O(K^3) local variational update by restricting the posterior to a small, randomly sampled subset of role configurations.
- Achieves overall O(NK) complexity and demonstrates scaling to million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine.
- Extensive experiments on synthetic and real data show orders-of-magnitude speedups over baselines (MMSB, MMTM Gibbs) while keeping or improving accuracy.
- Paper is technically sound, well-justified, and the system design choices (e.g., structured mean-field) are clearly explained.
- Authors transparently acknowledge limitations, including focus on inference (not simulation) and reliance on δ-subsampling of triangles.
- Main weakness: the O(K) heuristic approximation lacks formal analysis of approximation error or tightness relative to the full O(K^3) update.
- Exceptionally clear writing and organization; key ideas, tables, and algorithm summaries are accessible to experts.
- Some detailed mathematics are deferred to the appendix, but the main text provides necessary intuition and detail.
- Original contributions include the PTM parameter-sharing scheme and the O(K) local-update approximation; the combination of ideas is novel and effective.
- Work is broadly incremental within a research line (building on MMSB, a-MMSB, MMTM) rather than a wholly new paradigm.
- High practical significance: makes probabilistic latent-space modeling feasible for truly large networks and lowers computational barriers.
- Method effectiveness depends on triangular motifs being informative; may perform poorly on graphs where triangles are uninformative (e.g., bipartite or grid-like networks).
- Arguments for acceptance: solves a crucial scalability problem, provides state-of-the-art runtime/accuracy tradeoffs, high-quality and high-impact work.
- Arguments against acceptance: primarily incremental novelty and a key heuristic component without theoretical guarantees.
- Conclusion: strong, well-evaluated contribution with strengths outweighing minor weaknesses; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
