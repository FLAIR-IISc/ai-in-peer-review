Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a scalable probabilistic approach for latent space inference in large networks by: (i) representing the graph as a bag of triangular motifs (open/closed triads), (ii) introducing a Parsimonious Triangular Model (PTM) that reduces triangle-generating parameters from O(K^3) in MMTM [Ho et al., NIPS’12] to O(K) by sharing parameters across equivalence classes of role triplets, and (iii) deriving a structured stochastic variational inference (SVI) algorithm that uses natural gradients and mini-batches of triangles with an O(K) local approximation. Empirically, the method recovers latent roles on synthetic networks, predicts held-out links competitively, and scales to 1M nodes and 100 roles in hours on a single machine, outperforming MMSB [Airoldi et al.] and the triangular MMTM Gibbs sampler [Ho et al., NIPS’12] in speed and often in accuracy. The work builds on three strands: (1) mixed-membership blockmodels and scalable variants (MMSB, a-MMSB [Gopalan et al., NIPS’12]), (2) motif-based network statistics and ERGM, and (3) stochastic variational inference [Hoffman et al., JMLR]. It extends the triangular representation of Ho et al. (NIPS’12) by drastically simplifying the parameterization and marrying it with SVI.

Quality
- Modeling: The PTM’s parameter sharing is well-motivated and clearly defined: Bxxx for fully homogeneous triplets, Bxx for “two-equal” majority cases, and B0 for all-distinct roles. This yields only O(K) Dirichlet-distributed parameters, a dramatic reduction relative to the O(K^3) MMTM tensor, plausibly improving estimation and stability. The structured variational family keeps (si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij) coupled per triangle, addressing the high-rank posterior that breaks naive mean-field. The stochastic natural gradient updates and mini-batch scheme are standard and appropriate.
- Algorithms: The exact local update is O(K^3); the “mixture-of-deltas” approximation brings it to O(K) by focusing on diagonal and a small random set of off-diagonals. This is a pragmatic engineering choice; the paper asserts that any choice of A yields a valid lower bound, which is correct at the level of variational theory. However, the effect of resampling A on gradient variance is not analyzed; some discussion of variance control or diagnostics would be helpful.
- Correctness and assumptions: The model is not a generative model of full networks (triangles may be inconsistent, singles/empties omitted); the authors are explicit about this and focus on latent inference rather than simulation. δ-subsampling of triangles for high-degree nodes is described and claimed to approximately preserve triangle distributions (citing [8]), but more quantitative analysis of the induced bias on downstream inference would strengthen the claims.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are well designed to vary N and K jointly, with “easy/hard” mixtures. Latent recovery is measured by NMI after thresholding, which makes sense given mixed-membership. However, all methods are seeded with 2% ground-truth nodes per role; while this controls for local optima across methods, it is a relatively strong supervision signal and may not reflect realistic unsupervised use. On real data, link prediction compares only to MMSB; including a-MMSB [Gopalan et al., NIPS’12] and MMTM (at smaller K) would make the comparison more compelling. It is also unclear what metric (AUC, precision@k) or calibration is used for link ranking; please clarify. The convergence plots in passes and wall-clock times are convincing and consistent with the complexity analysis.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The rationale for the equivalence classes and parameter sharing is lucid. Notation is heavy in places; a figure illustrating the equivalence classes and the correspondence between (si,jk, sj,ik, sk,ij) and motif types would aid readers. Important update equations are deferred to the appendix (e.g., φxxx and φxxy); providing at least the form of these updates in the main text would improve reproducibility. The definition of the link prediction score should be formalized. Reporting mini-batch size, δ choices, and step-size schedules is helpful; releasing code or pseudocode for triangle extraction and subsampling would further aid replication.

Originality
The work is original in combining motif-based triangular representations with a parsimonious parameterization that collapses O(K^3) to O(K), and in tailoring a structured SVI procedure with an O(K) local approximation. The idea of parameter sharing echoes a-MMSB’s reduction from O(K^2) to O(K) for inter-block links, but the application to triangle motifs and the specific equivalence-class construction is novel. Using triangles rather than edges continues a line started in NIPS’12 [8], but the contribution here is a distinct model and an inference scheme that scales in both N and K.

Significance
If broadly validated, the ability to infer 100-role latent spaces on million-node graphs in hours is impactful. Triadic information is known to be informative in social graphs; showing improved link prediction at scale strengthens the case. The method can plausibly become a practical tool for exploratory analysis and as a prior for downstream tasks. However, expressivity is limited by the PTM’s shared parameters: it cannot capture role-specific asymmetries among distinct-role triplets (all-distinct configurations share B0) or minority-role heterogeneity within “two-equal” cases. A discussion of when this inductive bias helps or hurts (e.g., networks with strong inter-role structure beyond transitivity) would contextualize the significance.

Suggestions and questions
- Please include a comparison to a-MMSB on both runtime and accuracy, and, where feasible, MMTM with smaller K, to disentangle the benefits of triangle representation versus parameter sharing.
- Analyze the impact of δ-subsampling on estimation bias and link prediction (e.g., vary δ, report performance/variance curves).
- Provide details and metrics for link prediction (AUC/ROC or PR curves), and compare to non-probabilistic scalable baselines available at the time (e.g., degree-corrected SBM, spectral or modularity-based methods, overlapping community detection such as Lancichinetti et al.).
- Discuss identifiability and potential role collapse under the PTM sharing; report failure cases where PTM underfits.
- Quantify the variance introduced by the O(K) “mixture-of-deltas” local approximation and whether control variates or importance resampling improve stability.
- Clarify memory and wall-clock budgets, and whether performance holds beyond 100 roles.

Pros
- Strong and principled scalability story: O(NK) global updates with an O(K) local approximation; parallelizable; compelling wall-clock results.
- Clear modeling innovation: equivalence-class based parameter sharing for triangles.
- Structured variational family avoids rank-1 pitfalls; natural gradient SVI is appropriate and efficient.
- Competitive or improved accuracy on synthetic latent recovery and real-data link prediction.

Cons
- Limited comparative evaluation (missing a-MMSB and stronger baselines; unclear link prediction metrics).
- Potential underfitting due to aggressive parameter sharing (all-distinct configurations share one parameter).
- The δ-subsampling and local approximation introduce biases/variance not fully analyzed.
- The model is not generative for full graphs; implications for tasks beyond link prediction are limited.

Overall
A well-motivated and carefully engineered contribution that materially advances scalable probabilistic latent space inference by combining motif-level modeling with parsimonious parameterization and SVI. With stronger baselines and additional analysis of approximation effects, this would be a high-impact NIPS paper. I lean toward acceptance.
