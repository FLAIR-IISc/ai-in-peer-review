

This paper proposes a scalable approach for latent space inference of large networks using a bag-of-triangles representation, a parsimonious statistical model (PTM), and stochastic variational inference. The authors address a key limitation of prior work: existing methods either scale poorly with network size (O(N²) for edge-based models like MMSB [1]) or with the number of latent roles (O(K³) for MMTM [8]), making them infeasible for million-node networks. The PTM reduces parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) through parameter sharing across role configurations, enabling an inference algorithm with O(NK) complexity.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with thorough theoretical analysis and comprehensive experiments on synthetic and real networks. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method achieves competitive or better accuracy than baselines while being orders of magnitude faster. However, the paper doesn't sufficiently explore the limitations of the triangular motif representation—particularly for networks with weak triadic closure patterns, which could limit applicability.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The model description, algorithm, and experimental setup are presented with sufficient detail for reproducibility. The comparison to prior work [5, 8] is particularly effective.

Originality: The core innovation—reducing parameter space from O(K³) to O(K) through parameter sharing—is novel and well-motivated. While triangular motifs [8] have been used before, the authors' parsimonious model represents a significant advancement that enables scalability to large networks.

Significance: The ability to analyze million-node networks with hundreds of roles on a single machine in hours is highly significant for real-world applications. The Brightkite and Stanford network experiments demonstrate 100-1000x speedup over prior scalable methods [5, 8], with competitive link prediction accuracy.

Strengths: Strong theoretical foundation, comprehensive experiments, clear presentation, and significant scalability improvements. The stochastic variational inference approach with O(K) local updates is particularly elegant.

Weaknesses: Limited discussion of when the triangular representation might fail (e.g., networks with minimal triadic closure), and no comparison to recent non-probabilistic embedding methods (e.g., DeepWalk, Node2Vec) that might offer different trade-offs.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to scalable network analysis with clear theoretical and empirical validation. The method's practical impact on real-world network analysis is substantial, and the work aligns well with NIPS' focus on high-quality scientific contributions to machine learning. I recommend acceptance.
