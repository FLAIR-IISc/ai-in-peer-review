- Proposes a novel LDS-based framework to "stitch" sequentially recorded partial neural populations to infer larger-circuit dynamics.  
- Builds on Pillow & Latham (2008) by extending missing-data treatment from within a session to across multiple sequential sessions.  
- Demonstrated on synthetic data and mouse somatosensory calcium imaging, showing stitched model can predict noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs.  
- Major empirical result: stitched model predicts near-fully-observed correlations (Fig. 3d, r=0.84) vs. naive approach (r=0.15).  
- Strength: Addresses an important experimental bottleneck (limited simultaneous imaging) with a conceptually elegant method.  
- Strength: Synthetic experiments are rigorous with sensible metrics (coupling recovery, noise-correlation prediction) and scalable EM inference.  
- Weakness: Strong linear-Gaussian model assumption with no analysis of failure modes under nonlinear dynamics or non-Gaussian noise (spiking, calcium transients).  
- Weakness: Unclear incremental benefit—stitching may add little over collecting more data from a single subset, as Fig. 4b shows only slight accuracy gains with population size.  
- Weakness: Requires substantial overlap between subsets (≥40%) for success, but offers no theoretical explanation for this overlap requirement.  
- Weakness: Key technical details (derivations of M-step updates, handling of correlated noise, convergence guarantees) are omitted, hurting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Stitched couplings correlate poorly with fully-observed couplings for non-simultaneous pairs (r=0.38), and noise correlations could reflect common inputs rather than direct coupling.  
- Originality: High for the multi-session stitching application, though the core LDS modeling is standard.  
- Overall assessment: Promising and impactful idea but currently limited by assumptions, ambiguous benefits, unexplained overlap requirement, and missing technical/robustness analyses.  
- Recommendation: Weak reject/borderline pending major revisions—authors should test non-Gaussian/nonlinear robustness, quantify incremental gains vs. single-subset recording, theoretically justify overlap needs, disentangle common-input vs. direct-coupling interpretations, and provide full derivations.
