The paper "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit" presents a novel approach to inferring the dynamics of large neural populations by "stitching" together sequentially recorded subnetworks. The authors propose a statistical method for combining multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit, allowing for the estimation of joint models of population activity that capture correlation structures and stimulus selectivity.

The paper is related to previous work on inferring functional connectivity from neural activity data, but it differs in its focus on non-simultaneously observed neurons. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using both simulated and real data from the mouse somatosensory cortex. They show that their approach can accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, even when the stimulus drive is weak.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a statistical method for stitching together multiple partial recordings of a neural circuit. This method allows for the estimation of a joint model of population activity that captures correlation structures and stimulus selectivity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach using both simulated and real data, and they show that their method can accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The paper's strengths include its novel approach to inferring neural population dynamics, its ability to accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, and its potential for extending to point-process data and being broadly applicable. However, the paper also has some limitations, including the unclear robustness of the method, the unclear incremental benefit of using multiple subsets, the requirement for overlapping populations, and the ambiguity of the real-data results.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, but it could benefit from more detailed derivations and explanations of the estimation procedure. The presentation is also somewhat imbalanced, with a lot of introductory and discussion material, but insufficient technical exposition. Overall, the paper presents a promising idea, but it needs clearer explanation and derivations to fully establish its novelty and impact.
