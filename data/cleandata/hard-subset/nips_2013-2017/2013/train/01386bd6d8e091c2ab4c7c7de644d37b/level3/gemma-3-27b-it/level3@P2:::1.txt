This paper proposes a statistical method to address a fundamental limitation in neural circuit analysis: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons comprising a circuit, particularly in structures like cortical barrels containing thousands of neurons. The authors frame this challenge as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, enabling the “stitching” together of sequentially imaged neuron populations to infer circuit dynamics beyond the scope of individual recordings. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, demonstrating an ability to predict noise correlations between neurons never simultaneously recorded.

The core idea of leveraging sequential recordings and modeling the problem as a latent dynamical system is well-motivated and builds logically upon existing work in neural inference and functional connectivity mapping [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]. Numerous studies have tackled the problem of inferring connectivity, but this work differentiates itself by explicitly focusing on *non-simultaneous* recordings, a scenario not addressed by prior methods like those in [12, 13, 14, 15]. The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is pragmatic, allowing for tractable inference, and the EM algorithm provides a standard framework for parameter estimation. 

The paper’s strengths lie in its novel approach and demonstrated ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The results on simulated data are compelling, showing good recovery of the coupling matrix and accurate prediction of noise correlations (Figure 2). Applying this method to real calcium imaging data and demonstrating similar predictive power, even if reliant on a degree of overlap between imaging sessions, is a significant achievement. The authors clearly articulate the potential significance of this work, highlighting its ability to overcome limitations in current imaging technology and enable analysis of larger, more biologically relevant neural circuits.

However, several weaknesses require attention. While the paper discusses related work, the connection between the proposed method and existing approaches for handling missing data in dynamical systems [22, 23, 21] could be more explicitly drawn. While the authors acknowledge these theoretical foundations, a deeper discussion of how their implementation relates to these broader principles would strengthen the paper. 

A concerning discrepancy exists between the results observed in simulated versus real data. While the simulated data shows strong recovery of couplings for non-simultaneously recorded neurons (correlation of 0.73, Figure 2c), the real data demonstrates a significantly lower correlation (0.38, Figure 3b). The authors do not adequately explain this difference. Is this due to the inherent complexity of the biological data, limitations of the linear Gaussian model in capturing real neural dynamics, or potentially issues in the experimental data itself? Addressing this discrepancy is crucial.

Furthermore, the requirement for substantial overlap between imaging sessions to achieve good performance in the real data is a limitation that needs further discussion. The authors mention that some overlap is “critical,” but don't sufficiently explore the trade-offs between overlap, total coverage, and performance. How does the amount of overlap impact the accuracy of correlation predictions? Is 60% overlap optimal, or could different percentages yield better results?

Clarity also suffers in certain areas. While the overall writing is good, the figure legends and related text are often lacking in detail. For example, Figure 3a’s caption could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the coupling matrix visualization and how the different methods contribute to its structure. Similarly, the description of the “fictional imaging scenario” in Section 2.3 could be more precise. More descriptive figure captions and enhanced explanations of the experimental setup and analysis would greatly improve the paper's accessibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a fundamental problem in neural circuit analysis.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
*   Solid theoretical foundation and a well-defined statistical framework.
*   Potential to significantly expand the scale of circuit-level analyses.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Discrepancy between results on simulated and real data needs to be addressed.
*   Requirement for substantial overlap between imaging sessions limits applicability.
*   Insufficient explanation of the discrepancy between simulation and experimental results.
*   Clarity issues regarding figure descriptions and experimental details.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis. The proposed method offers a promising approach to overcome the limitations of simultaneous imaging and scale up circuit-level studies. However, the discrepancy between simulation and real data, the need for substantial overlap, and the clarity issues need to be addressed before this work can be considered truly impactful. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon the authors addressing the points raised above. Specifically, a thorough discussion of the discrepancy in results, a more detailed exploration of the overlap requirement, and improved clarity in figure legends and experimental descriptions are essential.
