### **Review**

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, sequential recordings of neuronal subpopulations to infer the dynamics of the entire, larger neural circuit. The central challenge addressed is a fundamental limitation of current technologies like 2-photon calcium imaging, which can only record from a fraction of the neurons in a circuit simultaneously. The authors frame this problem as one of inference in a latent dynamical system with missing observations. Specifically, they model the entire neural population with a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS), where each sequential recording session provides a partial, noisy observation of the full system's state. Using a variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, they estimate the model parameters, including the coupling matrix that captures functional interactions.

The method is validated on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The primary claim is that this approach can successfully recover statistics of the joint dynamics, most notably the pairwise noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded at the same time. This work is novel compared to prior studies on inferring functional connectivity (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011; Pillow et al., 2008), which typically require simultaneous recordings. It presents a concrete computational strategy to overcome experimental limitations and scale up circuit-level analysis.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper tackles a highly significant and practical problem in systems neuroscience. The ability to computationally integrate non-simultaneous recordings to model a larger circuit would be a major advance, allowing researchers to study population dynamics at scales beyond the physical limits of current imaging technology. The proposed "stitching" approach, framing sequential recordings as a missing data problem in a single dynamical system, is an original and elegant contribution to this area. It provides a principled way to infer interactions that are otherwise unobservable.

*   **Technical Quality:** The methodological approach is sound. Formulating the problem within the well-established LDS framework is appropriate, and the use of EM for parameter estimation is a standard and suitable choice. The paper's core strength lies in its empirical validation. The authors smartly designed a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data to create a ground truth for evaluation. The use of "fully observed" (upper bound) and "naive" (lower bound) models provides a clear and effective benchmark for the method's performance, convincingly demonstrating its value.

*   **Strong Core Result:** The method's ability to recover pairwise noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is impressive and well-supported by the results (Fig. 3d, Fig. 4a). For this key metric, the stitching model performs nearly as well as the upper-bound model trained on fully observed data and substantially better than the naive baseline. This is the paper's most compelling finding and highlights its practical potential.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Discrepancy Between Simulated and Real Data Performance:** There is a stark, unexplained difference in the quality of the recovered coupling matrix (`A`) between the simulated and real data experiments. In simulation, the correlation between true and estimated couplings for non-simultaneous pairs is high (0.73, Fig. 2c), suggesting the underlying system parameters are well recovered. However, on real data, this correlation drops to 0.38 (Fig. 3b). While the authors report this result, they do not discuss the reasons for this significant performance degradation. This could be due to model mismatch (the linear Gaussian assumption being too simple for real neural dynamics), non-stationarities in the data, or other challenges. This omission weakens the claim that the model accurately recovers the underlying functional couplings, even if it successfully predicts the emergent correlations.

*   **The Role of Overlapping Neurons:** The paper is not fully transparent about a crucial practical detail. The method is shown to work on simulated data with two completely *non-overlapping* sets of neurons. However, for the real data, the authors state they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" and subsequently used a setup where the two fictional sessions shared 20% of the neurons (each session containing 60% of the total, creating a 20% overlap). This is a critical finding for any experimentalist wishing to apply this technique. The necessity of this "anchor" population should be highlighted and discussed more thoroughly as a practical requirement or limitation of the method, rather than being mentioned in passing.

*   **Clarity in Figures:** While the paper is generally well-written, the clarity of the figures could be improved. The figure legends and associated text are often too sparse, requiring the reader to hunt for details in the main body. For instance, in Figure 3, it would be helpful to explicitly label the quadrants in the correlation/coupling matrices as "simultaneously observed" and "non-simultaneously observed" to make the visual point more immediate. Similarly, the scatter plots (e.g., Fig 3b) could be more clearly annotated to guide interpretation.

**Conclusion and Suggestions**

This paper presents a novel and significant contribution towards overcoming a major bottleneck in neuroscience research. The proposed method for "stitching" partial recordings is promising, and the results demonstrating the recovery of cross-session noise correlations are strong. The work is technically sound and addresses a problem of high interest to the NIPS community.

Therefore, I recommend acceptance. However, I strongly encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses in the final version to strengthen the paper:
1.  Please add a discussion regarding the discrepancy in coupling matrix recovery between simulated and real data. A brief exploration of potential causes (e.g., model mismatch) would add valuable context.
2.  Please be more upfront about the requirement for overlapping neurons in real-world applications. If possible, a brief analysis showing how performance degrades with decreasing overlap would be extremely valuable for guiding future experimental design.
3.  Please revise the figures and legends to be more self-contained and descriptive, thereby improving the overall clarity and impact of the paper.
