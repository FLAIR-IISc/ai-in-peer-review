This paper proposes a statistical method for combining multiple, sequential recordings of different subsets of neurons from the same neural circuit into a single, unified dynamical model. The central idea is to frame this "stitching" problem as parameter estimation in a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The authors model the joint activity of the entire neuron population using a linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS). For each experimental session, the observed neurons are treated as direct measurements of a subset of the latent state, while the non-recorded neurons are treated as missing data. The model parameters (coupling matrix, stimulus response, noise covariances) are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors validate this approach on both simulated data and two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main claim is that this method can accurately predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, a task that is impossible with conventional, separate analyses of each recording session. This work is related to a body of literature on inferring functional connectivity from neural data, but it is novel in its specific focus on inferring interactions between non-simultaneously observed neurons, thereby computationally extending the effective size of populations that can be studied.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and the proposed method is a logical application of latent state-space models to a challenging problem in neuroscience. The experimental validation is well-designed, using a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data to create well-defined upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") performance bounds. This provides a clear and convincing demonstration of the method's utility. The core claim—that the method recovers pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons (Fig 3d)—is well-supported by the results on the experimental datasets, showing performance approaching the upper bound.

However, there are two significant issues concerning the quality and completeness of the work:
1.  A critical discrepancy exists between the performance on simulated and real data regarding the need for overlap between recorded subsets. The simulation successfully stitches two *non-overlapping* sets of neurons. In contrast, the authors state that for the real data, they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" and used subsets with a 20% overlap (c=60% means 20% of the total population is in both subsets). This is a crucial practical limitation that is mentioned only in passing. The paper would be much stronger if it investigated and explained why overlap is necessary for real data. Is it due to model mismatch (the LDS being too simple for real neural dynamics), non-stationarities in the recordings, or other factors not present in the simulation?
2.  There is a large, unexplained performance drop in recovering the underlying coupling matrix parameters between the simulated and real data. In the simulation, the correlation between true and estimated couplings for non-simultaneously recorded pairs is high (r=0.73, Fig 2c). On real data, the correlation between the stitched model's couplings and the upper-bound model's couplings is substantially lower (r=0.38, Fig 3b). While the paper rightly focuses on the recovery of correlations (which are a function of the parameters), this drop in parameter identifiability is concerning and warrants discussion. It suggests that while the model can reproduce the population's covariance structure, the specific parameters it finds may not be as meaningful on real data, potentially due to the simplicity of the linear-Gaussian assumption.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the method is explained clearly. The motivation is strong and the problem is well-defined. However, the clarity could be improved in the presentation of the results. The figure legends and the accompanying text could provide more detail to guide the reader. For instance, the discussion of Figure 3 moves between analyzing the coupling matrix (A) and the resulting correlation matrix without explicitly drawing the connection for the reader, which could make the logic harder to follow. A more explicit narrative explaining how the structure in the recovered coupling matrix leads to the accurate prediction of the correlation matrix would strengthen the presentation.

**Originality:** The paper's contribution is original. While latent dynamical systems and EM are standard tools, their application to the problem of stitching non-simultaneous neural recordings is novel. The introduction does a good job of differentiating this work from previous approaches that either require simultaneous recordings or make stronger assumptions (e.g., relating noise and signal correlations). The work addresses a problem of growing importance as experimentalists seek to understand larger and larger neural circuits.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is potentially very high. Two-photon imaging and other recording techniques face fundamental physical trade-offs between the number of neurons recorded, spatial resolution, and temporal resolution. A robust computational method to combine sequential recordings could effectively break through the barrier of how many neurons can be analyzed as a single circuit, enabling system-level analysis at scales previously out of reach. If the method proves robust and generalizable, it could become a standard tool for neuroscientists, allowing them to build more complete models of circuits like cortical columns from achievable experimental data. The successful demonstration on real cortical data, despite the model's simplicity, suggests this is a promising research direction.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and practical bottleneck in experimental neuroscience.
*   The proposed method is novel, elegant, and theoretically grounded.
*   The primary claim—recovering correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons—is demonstrated convincingly on real data using a well-conceived validation strategy.
*   The work has the potential for high impact, enabling the study of larger neural circuits than is physically possible to record simultaneously.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The unexplained need for overlapping neuron sets in real data (but not in simulation) is a major weakness that undermines the method's generality.
*   The significant performance drop in parameter recovery between simulated and real data is not discussed, raising questions about the model's fidelity and the interpretability of its parameters.
*   The clarity of the results section could be improved to better connect the different performance metrics and figures.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. The primary weaknesses relate to a lack of discussion around the discrepancies between performance on simulated and real data. I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to address these discrepancies in the final version to provide a more complete and honest assessment of the method's current capabilities and limitations.
