- Introduces a method to "stitch" sequential, partially overlapping neuronal recordings by fitting a single latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations.  
- Demonstrates on simulations and 2-photon calcium imaging that the method can predict noise correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously.  
- Elegant formulation: session-specific observation matrices (Ck) in a shared LDS is a natural and powerful approach.  
- Technical approach is sound in principle and provides a strong proof-of-concept on real data.  
- Core novelty: first computational attempt to merge sequential recordings into a single coherent dynamical model.  
- High potential impact: could enable modeling of much larger neural populations and estimating interactions between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.  
- Learned coupling matrix A is ambiguous: modest correlation (0.38) with fully observed model suggests couplings may reflect latent/shared inputs rather than direct interactions.  
- Robustness is insufficiently explored; reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions is a major simplification with no discussion of failure modes.  
- Methods section lacks technical detail: EM algorithm and M-step updates for A, B, Q are omitted and need explicit derivation for this masked-observation scenario.  
- Critical practical finding—that overlap between sessions is necessary—is stated but not theoretically explained or intuitively justified.  
- Presentation is imbalanced: strong intro/discussion but underdeveloped technical/methods exposition.  
- Significance is high and the idea is generalizable to more complex models (e.g., spiking point-processes).  
- Arguments for acceptance: novelty/significance, elegant latent-variable framing, and convincing real-data predictions.  
- Arguments against acceptance: missing technical depth, unclear robustness/limitations, ambiguity in parameter interpretation, and unclear incremental benefit over repeated measurements of a single subset.  
- Recommendation: lean toward acceptance contingent on major revisions—expand methods with full derivations, explain role of overlap, and address interpretation and robustness concerns.
