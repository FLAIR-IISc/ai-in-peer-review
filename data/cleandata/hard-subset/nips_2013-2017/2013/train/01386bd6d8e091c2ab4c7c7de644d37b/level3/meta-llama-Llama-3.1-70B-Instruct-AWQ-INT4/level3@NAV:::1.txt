This paper proposes a novel statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model, allowing for the expansion of population-sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging in mice, demonstrating the recovery of correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear and well-organized presentation of the method and its application. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the model formulation, parameter estimation, and inference procedures. The use of simulated data and experimental calcium imaging in mice provides a robust validation of the method.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neuroscience, as it addresses the fundamental limit of simultaneous imaging and provides a means for expanding our limited capabilities. The method has the potential to be widely applicable, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs. The authors demonstrate that their method can accurately reconstruct these correlations, even in the absence of simultaneous imaging. This is a significant achievement, as it has the potential to reveal new insights into the dynamics of neural populations.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. The authors note that the method requires substantial overlap between the imaged populations for reasonable results, which may limit its applicability in certain situations. Additionally, the authors do not explain the large difference in correlation results between simulated and real data, which may indicate a need for further refinement of the method.

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel combination of techniques for inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons. The use of a latent dynamical system model with missing observations is a unique approach that sets the paper apart from previous work in the field.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide sufficient detail for the reader to understand the method and its application. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neuroscience and has the potential to be widely applicable.
