This paper addresses a significant limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from very large neural populations. The authors propose a novel statistical method to “stitch” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons by framing the problem as a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations. While inferring functional connectivity from imaging or electrophysiological data is an active area of research, this work is distinguished by its specific focus on inferring correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a challenging problem largely unaddressed by previous methods.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its originality and potential significance. The proposed stitching method offers an elegant computational solution to a fundamental experimental bottleneck, potentially enabling circuit-level analyses at scales currently out of reach. The model formulation is sound, and the authors demonstrate convincingly on simulated data that their Expectation-Maximization-based inference can effectively recover pairwise correlations between neuron pairs that were never measured together—a non-trivial achievement.

However, the paper's main weakness is a critical and unexplained discrepancy between its performance on simulated versus real-world data, which impacts the quality and robustness of its claims. The model performs remarkably well on simulated, non-overlapping populations, yet the authors state that real calcium imaging data required substantial neuron overlap between sessions to yield reasonable results. The paper fails to investigate or even hypothesize why this performance gap exists. The large difference in the quality of recovered coupling parameters between the simulated results (Fig. 2c) and the real data (Fig. 3b) is stark and undermines confidence in the method's immediate applicability.

In terms of clarity, while the main text is well-written, the figure legends and accompanying descriptions are insufficient. They lack the necessary detail for a reader to fully interpret the results without making assumptions, which would hinder reproducibility.

In summary, the paper introduces a highly original and significant idea. The method is promising and performs well in an idealized setting. However, its claims of practical utility are weakened by the unexplained performance drop on real data and the need for significant overlap, a key constraint that is not fully explored. The clarity of the figures must also be improved. This is an important contribution, but the gap between simulation and experiment needs to be thoroughly addressed to solidify the paper's claims.
