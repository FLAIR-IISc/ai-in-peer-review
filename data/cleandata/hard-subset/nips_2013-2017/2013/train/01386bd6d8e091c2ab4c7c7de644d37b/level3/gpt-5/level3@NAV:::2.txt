This paper proposes “stitching” sequential partial recordings into a single population model by fitting a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations and known selection matrices. Using EM (with an online variant), the method estimates couplings and predicts cross-session noise correlations. On synthetic data and two mouse S1 calcium datasets, it reports strong prediction of noise correlations for neuron pairs never observed simultaneously, benchmarking against “fully observed” (upper bound) and “naive” (lower bound) baselines. The work relates to partially observed population models and latent common-input approaches (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS’08; Vidne et al., 2011; Macke et al., 2012) but is distinctive in explicitly aligning submodels across sessions via overlap.

Strengths
- Original and broadly useful framing: inferring large-network properties by sequentially recording subnetworks and stitching submodels. Conceptually powerful and practically relevant for calcium/ephys where full simultaneity is infeasible.
- Clear empirical target (noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs) with sensible upper/lower-bound evaluation.
- Simple, scalable LDS instantiation makes the idea accessible and suggests extensions to point-process models.

Weaknesses
Quality: The approach appears limited to near linear-Gaussian regimes; failure modes under model mismatch (nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian spiking/fluorescence) are not analyzed. The real-data gains could reflect latent common inputs rather than direct couplings; indeed, stitched couplings correlate poorly with “fully observed” couplings (Fig. 3b). The paper does not quantify whether multiple subsets add information beyond a single long recording of one subset (identifiability suggests much may be recoverable up to a latent transform from one subset). Robustness to degree of overlap, observation noise, and mis-specified R/Q is unclear.

Clarity: Important estimation details are missing (precise M-step updates, regularization/priors, stability constraints, initialization, convergence criteria), hindering reproducibility and obscuring novelty. Presentation is intro-heavy with insufficient technical exposition.

Originality: Few directly comparable works; closest is Pillow & Latham (partially observed GLMs). The stitching alignment via overlap is novel, but the LDS/EM core is standard.

Significance: Potentially high impact if robustness and identifiability are clarified; current evidence is strongest for correlation prediction, weaker for interpretable coupling recovery.

Suggestions
- Theorize observability/identifiability conditions and explicitly justify the role of overlap; report performance vs overlap (including 0%) and session count.
- Stress-test on nonlinear/point-process simulations; compare to PLDS/latent-GLM baselines (Pillow & Latham; Vidne et al.) and subspace ID with missing data.
- Disentangle sparse couplings vs low-rank common input (sparse+low-rank models); report whether added sessions improve beyond longer single-session data.
- Provide full derivations, hyperparameters, and code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and practical idea; convincing correlation prediction; broadly applicable; timely.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient technical detail; unclear robustness and identifiability; ambiguous interpretation of learned couplings; reliance on overlap without principled explanation.
