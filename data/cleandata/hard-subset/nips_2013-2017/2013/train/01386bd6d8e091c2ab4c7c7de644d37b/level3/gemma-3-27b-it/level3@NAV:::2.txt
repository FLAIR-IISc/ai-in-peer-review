## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a novel method for extending population dynamics characterization beyond the limits of simultaneous recording by “stitching” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, framed as a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The core idea – leveraging overlapping recordings to infer dynamics across a larger network – is compelling and addresses a significant practical limitation in large-scale neural recording. This work is distinct from existing approaches focused on functional connectivity from complete or interpolated data, and most closely relates to Pillow & Latham’s work on partially observed spiking populations, though extending to point-process data and offering broader applicability.

The paper’s strengths lie in its innovative approach and initial demonstration on both simulated and real (mouse somatosensory cortex) data. The successful prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a particularly promising result. The authors clearly articulate the problem and motivate the method within the context of current experimental limitations. 

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The authors acknowledge a potential limitation to near linear-Gaussian data, but fail to adequately address the method’s robustness or provide intuition for failure modes when these assumptions are violated. A critical point is the unclear incremental benefit of multiple subsets; if the full model can be recovered from a single subset (up to a latent transform), the value added by additional data from other subsets isn't clearly established. The reliance on overlapping populations in the real-data analysis is also problematic, with insufficient explanation as to *why* overlap is necessary for constraining the model. Furthermore, the authors’ interpretation of recovered noise correlations is potentially confounded by common input or latent variables, particularly given the discrepancies between predicted and true couplings in Fig. 3b. 

Critically, the paper lacks detailed derivations and estimation details (especially the M-step), hindering both understanding and assessment of the method's novelty. There is a significant imbalance in presentation, with extensive introduction and discussion overshadowing the technical exposition. While the idea is promising, a more thorough and accessible explanation of the underlying mathematics is required. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Novel approach to a significant problem, initial success on both simulated and real data, potential to unlock analysis of larger networks than currently feasible.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Limited robustness analysis, unclear incremental benefit of multiple subsets, potential for confounding interpretations, insufficient technical detail, presentation imbalance.

**Overall:** While the idea presented is potentially impactful, the paper, in its current form, falls short of NIPS quality due to the weaknesses outlined above. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to revise and resubmit after addressing the concerns regarding robustness, justification of design choices, technical detail, and presentation.
