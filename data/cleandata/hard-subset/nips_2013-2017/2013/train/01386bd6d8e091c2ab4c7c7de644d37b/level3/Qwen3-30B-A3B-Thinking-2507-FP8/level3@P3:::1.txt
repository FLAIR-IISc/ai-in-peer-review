

# Review: "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in neural circuit analysis: the inability to simultaneously image large populations of neurons (thousands) with current 2-photon calcium imaging technology (which typically captures hundreds). The authors propose a statistical "stitching" method to combine sequentially imaged neuron sets into a unified model of population dynamics. The core idea frames the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations using a linear Gaussian model, allowing inference of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. This approach builds on previous work in neural population modeling [6-11] but uniquely addresses the challenge of combining non-simultaneous recordings, unlike prior methods that either require simultaneous measurements or make strong assumptions about correlation structure [12-15].

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a compelling case for the method's ability to recover pairwise correlations between neuron pairs not measured simultaneously. The simulated data results (Fig. 2) are particularly convincing, showing the method successfully recovers coupling matrices and noise correlations (correlation 0.92 between predicted and true correlations) where naive approaches fail entirely. The real data validation on mouse somatosensory cortex (Fig. 3) demonstrates the method's practical utility, with stitched model correlations matching the fully observed model (0.84 correlation) while the naive approach performs poorly (0.15 correlation). The paper's theoretical grounding in latent dynamical systems [21] provides a solid foundation, and the clear connection to the experimental limitations of current imaging technology makes the work highly relevant.

**Weaknesses:**
The most significant weakness is the unexplained discrepancy between simulated (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b) correlation results. The simulated data shows strong recovery (0.92 correlation) while real data shows much weaker recovery (0.38 correlation for stitched model vs fully observed model), with no explanation for this substantial difference. The requirement for substantial overlap in real data (c=60% in experimental validation) is noted but not adequately justified, especially since the simulation used non-overlapping populations (c=50% with no overlap) and worked well. The figure legends and related text lack sufficient detail to fully understand the experimental setup, particularly regarding the fictional imaging scenario (Section 3.2).

**Quality:**
The method is technically sound, with a well-formulated model and appropriate inference via a variant of EM. However, the unexplained discrepancy between simulated and real data results is a significant quality concern. The authors do acknowledge limitations (e.g., requiring overlap), but the substantial gap in performance between simulated and real data needs deeper analysis.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized, but the figure legends and related text lack sufficient detail to fully understand the experimental setup and results, particularly regarding the fictional imaging scenario in the real data analysis. For instance, it's unclear how the "60% overlap" was implemented in the experimental data.

**Originality:**
The core contribution is novel in addressing the specific challenge of inferring functional connections for non-simultaneously imaged neurons. The method differs significantly from prior work on sub-sampled observations [12-15], which either require strong assumptions or don't scale to large population sizes. The paper adequately references related work, though it could better clarify the distinction between their approach and [12].

**Significance:**
The significance is high. This method offers a promising approach to overcome fundamental limits of simultaneous imaging, potentially enabling circuit-level analyses of thousands of neurons in cortical circuits. The authors convincingly argue that this could expand the scope of neural circuit analysis beyond current capabilities.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Novel approach to a significant problem in neural circuit analysis
- Strong theoretical foundation and technical soundness
- Demonstrated success in both simulated and real data
- Addresses a clear limitation of current experimental technology
- High potential impact for scaling up population-level neural analyses

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Unexplained discrepancy between simulated and real data results (Fig. 2c vs Fig. 3b)
- Requirement for substantial overlap in real data not adequately justified
- Insufficient detail in figure legends and related text
- The 0.38 correlation for stitched model vs fully observed model in real data (Fig. 3b) is substantially lower than the simulated result (0.92)

**Recommendation:**
This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to neural data analysis. The core idea is novel, technically sound, and addresses a significant limitation in the field. However, the unexplained performance gap between simulated and real data is a serious weakness that needs to be addressed. I recommend acceptance with major revisions, specifically requiring the authors to:
1) Provide a thorough explanation for the performance discrepancy between simulated and real data
2) Better justify the requirement for substantial overlap in real data
3) Improve figure legends and related text for clarity

The paper has high potential impact, and with these revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
