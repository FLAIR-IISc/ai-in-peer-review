- Proposes an LDS-based "stitching" method using EM/Kalman smoothing to combine partially overlapping calcium imaging sessions into a joint model.  
- Claims it can recover cross-population couplings and noise correlations for neuron pairs never observed simultaneously by leveraging latent dynamics.  
- Novel relative to prior work by explicitly inferring functional relationships across non-simultaneously imaged neurons; relates to LDS/GPFA and system ID but targets cross-session alignment.  

Strengths:
- Clear, principled modeling with specified parameters and EM updates.  
- Strong motivation: extends analyzable population beyond simultaneous imaging limits.  
- Simulations show recovery of off-block couplings and noise correlations for non-overlapping subsets.  
- Real-data "fictional" splits show improved cross-session correlation recovery versus a naive baseline.  
- Framework is extensible (e.g., latent inputs, sparsity priors) and practically significant.  

Weaknesses / Clarification points:
- Method succeeds on fully non-overlapping simulations but real data required substantial overlap—this discrepancy is unexplained.  
- Lacks analysis of identifiability and required overlap/session length/stimulus diversity for reliable stitching.  
- Noise modeling treats Q and R as diagonal, ignoring common calcium measurement correlations and neuropil/motion artifacts.  
- Scalability concerns: dense A has O(N^2) parameters, overfitting risk, and unclear runtime/complexity.  
- Evaluation uses fictional splits; real sequential data with drift, registration errors, and nonstationarity are not tested.  
- Figures and methods lack detail on units, noise-correlation computation, d estimation, stationarity assumptions, and preprocessing.  

Quality:
- Core modeling and inference are technically sound, but robustness issues (overlap dependence, correlated noise, nonstationarity) are under-analyzed.  

Clarity:
- Generally well written but figure legends and methodological assumptions need more explicit description; request for stitching schematic and EM schedule details.  

Originality:
- High novelty in stitching non-simultaneous recordings; should cite broader LDS/GPFA/system ID literature (e.g., Yu et al., Smith & Brown).  

Significance:
- Potentially impactful for scaling circuit analyses, contingent on robustness to experimental complications like overlap, drift, and correlated noise.  

Key suggestions for improvement:
- Systematically evaluate performance versus overlap fraction, session length, SNR, coupling strength, and stimulus richness.  
- Analyze sensitivity to correlated measurement noise and low-rank common inputs; consider R = diagonal + low-rank or added latent factors.  
- Include regularization (sparse/low-rank A) and report effects on stability and accuracy.  
- Test robustness to registration errors and mis-specified selection matrices C.  
- Provide at least small-scale real sequential recordings with known overlaps to complement fictional splits.  
- Report parameter counts, runtime/memory scaling, initialization, and convergence diagnostics.  

Pros:
- Addresses a novel and important problem with a simple, well-motivated model and promising simulation and real-data results.  

Cons:
- Dependence on overlap in real data undermines non-overlapping claims; measurement noise likely misspecified; limited identifiability and scalability analyses.  

Overall recommendation:
- Promising and timely work meriting acceptance if authors address the simulation–real discrepancy, broaden robustness analyses (especially overlap and noise modeling), and improve clarity of figures and methods.
