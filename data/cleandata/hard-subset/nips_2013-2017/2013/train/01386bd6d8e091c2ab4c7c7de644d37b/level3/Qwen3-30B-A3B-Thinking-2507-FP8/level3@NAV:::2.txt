

This paper proposes an intriguing approach to infer large-scale neural population dynamics by stitching together sequentially recorded subnetworks, addressing a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience where simultaneous imaging of large neural circuits is challenging. The core idea—using a latent dynamical system with missing observations to combine partial recordings—builds upon Pillow & Latham's work on partially observed spiking populations but extends it to the practical context of calcium imaging data, where overlapping subnetworks provide critical constraints.

The paper demonstrates promising results on both simulated data and real mouse somatosensory cortex recordings, showing that the method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs with accuracy approaching that of a "fully observed" model. The conceptual novelty is strong, and the application to neuroscience is compelling, addressing a genuine experimental bottleneck.

However, the paper has several significant weaknesses that undermine its scientific contribution. The robustness claims are unsupported: the method appears limited to near-linear-Gaussian data, and the paper lacks analysis of failure modes when assumptions are violated (e.g., non-Gaussian dynamics or strong nonlinearities). The incremental benefit is unclear—since the full model can be recovered from one subset (up to latent transform), it's ambiguous how much extra information the additional subsets provide versus simply more data from one subset. The requirement for overlapping populations is critical for real data but unexplained, and the real-data interpretation of noise correlations as direct pairwise couplings is questionable, as Figure 3b shows poor match with true couplings.

Technical presentation is deficient: the M-step updates are glossed over, and the paper lacks sufficient derivation details, making it difficult to assess the novelty and soundness of the method. The presentation is imbalanced, with extensive introduction/discussion but insufficient technical exposition. While the idea is promising, these weaknesses significantly reduce the paper's quality and clarity.

In summary, this work has strong potential but requires substantial revisions to address robustness, clarify incremental benefits, provide technical details, and better justify the real-data interpretation. The significance is high for neuroscience, but the current paper fails to fully demonstrate the method's utility and limitations. I recommend rejection due to insufficient technical depth and unresolved methodological concerns.
