Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “stitching” sequential, partial recordings of the same neural circuit into a single joint model by formulating the problem as inference in a latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations and known, session-specific selection matrices Ck. The authors fit parameters with EM (using an online-EM variant) and demonstrate on simulations and 2-photon calcium data from mouse S1 that the stitched model can predict noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded simultaneously, outperforming a naïve baseline and approaching a fully observed “upper bound.”

The key conceptual contribution is to infer large-network properties by aggregating multiple subnetwork recordings in time, effectively leveraging overlaps (shared neurons) and temporal structure to constrain cross-population couplings and correlations. While the paper instantiates a linear-Gaussian LDS on fluorescence, the framework could, in principle, extend to point-process/GLM models for spikes.

Related work: There are many methods for estimating functional connectivity from simultaneous recordings and calcium data (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al.), and substantial work on latent state-space models and common-input modeling (e.g., Vidne et al., Macke et al.). Fewer papers address sub-sampling across sessions; the closest in spirit is Pillow & Latham (NIPS 2008) on partially observed spiking populations/latent GLMs. Subspace/system identification ideas (Katayama) underlie the identifiability arguments. The proposed “stitching” of sequentially recorded populations, with explicit treatment of missing observations across sessions and evaluation on real imaging data, appears to be novel.

Quality
- Empirics: Simulation results convincingly show recovery of cross-block couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs. On real data, stitched models predict correlation structure substantially better than the naïve baseline and close to the fully observed upper bound. However, direct coupling estimates align only modestly with the fully observed model for non-simultaneously recorded pairs (reported r ≈ 0.38), raising ambiguity about whether recovered “couplings” reflect direct pairwise interactions versus common latent drive.
- Assumptions and robustness: The approach relies on a near linear-Gaussian approximation (diagonal Q and R in practice), stationary dynamics, and stable alignment across sessions. The paper does not analyze failure modes when these assumptions are violated (e.g., strong nonlinearities, non-Gaussian spike-driven calcium, nonstationarity across sessions, or correlated process noise). Reported performance degradation without overlap suggests identifiability challenges not fully explained theoretically.
- Estimation details: Key M-step derivations, handling of missing data across sessions for A, B, Q, initialization, regularization choices (e.g., sparsity), and computational complexity are not sufficiently described for reproduction.

Clarity
The paper is clearly motivated and well-situated in the literature, but the presentation is imbalanced: long introduction and discussion with too little technical exposition. The EM updates are only partially shown (R, d), leaving the central updates (A, B, Q) and the multi-session smoothing/sufficient statistics underspecified. The rationale for the overlap requirement is not explained. More intuition on identifiability and on what information additional sessions contribute would help.

Originality
The core idea—inferring properties of a large circuit by stitching sequentially imaged subsets—is novel and broadly useful. Framing the problem as an LDS with known selection matrices is elegant. Extension to point-process data is suggested but not demonstrated.

Significance
If robust, the method could substantially expand the effective population sizes that can be characterized with current imaging. Predicting noise correlations for nonsimultaneously recorded pairs is a valuable capability for systems neuroscience. However, practical impact hinges on (i) robustness beyond linear-Gaussian settings, (ii) principled guidance on required overlap and session design, and (iii) clearer disentanglement of direct couplings from common input.

Strengths
- Novel and powerful idea with broad applicability; conceptually simple and potentially compatible with many latent-dynamics models.
- Clear empirical benefit: accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-overlapping pairs on real data, outperforming a strong naïve baseline and approaching fully observed upper bounds.
- Sensible evaluation protocol including synthetic “ground truth” and “fictional session” tests on real data.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Robustness unclear: results may rely on near linear-Gaussian behavior; failure modes when assumptions are violated are not explored.
- Incremental benefit of multiple subsets vs. more data from one subset is not theoretically articulated; identifiability and what extra constraints are gained by additional non-simultaneous sessions remain opaque.
- Overlap requirement: stitching “failed” without overlap in real data, but the paper does not explain why overlap better constrains the model (intuitively to align latent similarity transforms). Formal conditions on overlap, observability/identifiability, and performance vs. overlap size are missing.
- Real-data ambiguity: explained noise correlations could reflect common inputs/latent factors rather than pairwise couplings, consistent with the weak match of learned couplings (r ≈ 0.38) to the fully observed model.
- Estimation/derivation details are sparse (notably the full M-step and sufficient statistics), undermining reproducibility and perceived novelty.
- Presentation imbalance: insufficient technical clarity relative to the length of intro/discussion; point-process extension is claimed but not shown.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the formal identifiability conditions for recovering cross-block couplings from multiple sessions, with and without overlap? Can you characterize how overlap anchors the similarity transform across sessions?
2. How does performance scale with overlap size, SNR, stimulus richness, and degree of nonlinearity/non-Gaussianity? Please provide sensitivity curves and failure cases.
3. How do results change if Q is allowed to be low-rank plus diagonal (to capture common input), and how do you then interpret pairwise “couplings”?
4. Can you provide full EM updates for A, B, Q with missing observations across sessions, initialization strategy, regularization (e.g., sparsity), and runtime complexity?
5. Can you demonstrate a point-process/GLM version on spikes to support the claim of broader applicability?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a theory section with identifiability/observability analysis, explicitly addressing the role of overlap and multiple sessions.
- Include full estimation details and a reproducible algorithmic description (pseudocode).
- Evaluate robustness to model mismatch (nonlinear/Poisson simulations, nonstationarity across sessions) and to correlated process noise.
- Disentangle direct couplings vs. common input by adding latent low-dimensional factors or using partial correlations; report how this affects coupling recovery and correlation predictions.
- Clarify the incremental information contributed by additional subsets beyond simply collecting more data from one subset.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, broadly applicable idea with a clear practical need.
- Strong empirical gains on a real dataset for an important target metric (noise correlations across sessions).
- Simple, extensible framework that many groups could adopt.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited technical depth and missing derivations; unclear identifiability and overlap rationale.
- Ambiguity in interpreting learned couplings vs. common input; modest match to fully observed couplings.
- Unknown robustness beyond linear-Gaussian settings; point-process extension not demonstrated.

Overall
Promising and potentially impactful, but the paper needs stronger technical exposition, clearer identifiability/overlap analysis, and robustness studies. I lean toward borderline reject/weak accept, contingent on addressing the above concerns.
