This paper proposes a novel statistical method for inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The method, referred to as "stitching," involves fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations to combine sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach using both simulated and real calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper's main contribution is its ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, which is a significant advancement in the field of neural population dynamics. The authors' method is also broadly applicable, as it can be used with various types of data, including point-process data.

One of the strengths of the paper is its clear and well-organized presentation. The authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem and their proposed solution, and the methods section is detailed and easy to follow. The results section is also well-structured, with clear figures and tables that effectively communicate the findings.

However, there are some limitations to the paper. One of the main concerns is the robustness of the method, as it may be limited to near-linear Gaussian data. The authors do not provide sufficient intuition on failure modes when assumptions are violated, which could be a significant issue in practice. Additionally, the paper lacks a clear explanation of the overlap requirement, which is necessary for the method to work with real data.

Another concern is the incremental benefit of the method. If a full model can be recovered from one subset of data, it is unclear how much extra coupling information other subsets provide versus simply more data from one subset. The authors do not provide a clear answer to this question, which could impact the method's applicability in practice.

In terms of originality, the paper builds upon previous work by Pillow and Latham on partially observed spiking populations. However, the authors' approach is distinct and provides a novel solution to the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings.

Overall, the paper is well-written and presents a significant contribution to the field of neural population dynamics. However, there are some limitations and concerns that need to be addressed in future work.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and powerful idea for inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings.
* The method is broadly applicable and can be used with various types of data.
* The authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their work.
* The results section is well-structured and effectively communicates the findings.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The method may be limited to near-linear Gaussian data, and the authors do not provide sufficient intuition on failure modes when assumptions are violated.
* The paper lacks a clear explanation of the overlap requirement, which is necessary for the method to work with real data.
* The incremental benefit of the method is unclear, and it is not clear how much extra coupling information other subsets provide versus simply more data from one subset.
* The paper builds upon previous work, but the authors' approach is not entirely novel.
