This paper introduces a practical and conceptually clear method for “stitching” sequentially imaged neural subsets into a single dynamical model, enabling inference of population interactions and noise correlations beyond the limits imposed by simultaneous 2-photon imaging. The authors cast the problem as learning a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations across sessions: neural fluorescence x evolves via x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t with diagonal Q, and observations y_t = C (x_t + d) + ε_t with diagonal R, where C encodes which neurons are visible in each session. Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant), and inference is performed by Kalman smoothing with session-specific observation matrices. On synthetic data, the method recovers cross-session couplings and accurately predicts noise correlations and unobserved activity. On mouse S1 calcium imaging, the authors construct a “fictional” two-session scenario from simultaneously recorded data and show that stitching predicts correlations between non-simultaneously imaged neurons nearly as well as a “fully observed” upper bound, and substantially better than a naive baseline that fits each session separately.

Relation to prior work: Unlike methods focused on inferring connectivity from simultaneous imaging or electrophysiology (e.g., GLM-based and information-theoretic approaches), this work directly addresses the cross-session setting, where key pairs are never observed together. It differs from approaches that assume monotonicity between stimulus- and noise-correlations [12] and from latent GLM methods [13] that do not scale or were not designed for session stitching. The modeling framework is in line with state-space approaches used at NIPS and elsewhere (e.g., LDS/PLDS/GPFA families), but the contribution here is leveraging missing-data LDS structure to align and combine partially overlapping recordings from the same circuit to estimate cross-session covariances.

Quality:
- Strengths: The modeling and inference are technically sound for LDS with missing data; the EM derivations and session-specific C matrices are standard yet effective. The synthetic experiments demonstrate identifiability intuition (up to linear transformations in the hidden part) and show strong recovery of noise correlations for non-overlapping subsets. The real-data results convincingly show that stitching recovers cross-session correlations with performance approaching a simultaneously observed upper bound and surpasses a naive model. Cross-prediction analyses are informative.
- Weaknesses: The approach assumes stationarity (same A, B, Q) across sessions; the real-data evaluation uses a fictional split of a single recording, which largely guarantees stationarity and may overstate performance compared to truly separate sessions (e.g., across time, depth, or behavioral context). The method struggles without overlap on real data (works in sim, not in practice), but the paper does not quantify performance vs. degree of overlap nor explain the discrepancy. Measurement and process noise are assumed diagonal, which is unlikely for calcium imaging (neuropil/motion induce correlations; unmodeled common input leads to off-diagonal Q), and the impact of these assumptions is not analyzed. Parameterization is large (A is N×N), yet regularization is minimal; risk of overfitting and stability constraints on A are not fully addressed. Evaluation against a “fully observed” LDS upper bound is useful, but the primary target—empirical pairwise noise correlations computed on held-out simultaneously recorded data—should be used as ground truth rather than model-on-model comparisons. Finally, figure legends and text do not always give sufficient detail to reproduce settings (e.g., parameter counts, runtime, initialization, convergence criteria, sensitivity).

Clarity:
- Overall writing is clear; the motivation and modeling choices are well presented. However, figure legends should be expanded (axes, units, population sizes, overlap c, number of trials, evaluation windows). Include pseudocode or a concise algorithm box for the stitching EM with mini-batches; specify initialization, stability enforcement (e.g., spectral radius), and stopping rules. Clarify how stimulus regressors were defined in the awake experiment and how “noise correlations” were computed (e.g., partialing out B u via posterior means vs. regression). Explicitly describe the withheld test data and whether empirical correlations from the held-out segment were used.

Originality:
- The key novelty is inferring cross-session correlated dynamics and noise correlations for pairs never imaged simultaneously by treating unobserved neurons as latent variables and then aligning sessions through overlap. To my knowledge, this specific “stitching” formulation and demonstration on 2-photon data is new relative to prior work on connectivity inference, partial observations, and anatomical superposition.

Significance:
- This is a timely and impactful contribution: the method directly addresses an experimental bottleneck and enables population-scale analyses that are otherwise infeasible. If robust to realistic session-to-session variability, it could substantially expand our capacity to model cortical circuits and guide experimental design (trade-offs between overlap and coverage).

Specific suggestions:
- Quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction (including c=0%, 20%, 40%, 60%) on real data by constructing splits with controlled overlap, and report both model-to-model and model-to-empirical correlation recovery on held-out simultaneously imaged segments.
- Evaluate robustness to session nonstationarities by creating pseudo-sessions that differ in baseline fluorescence, gain, or stimulus context; consider including session-specific offsets/gains and slow drift terms.
- Compare to an alternative baseline that aligns sessions via shared low-dimensional latent trajectories with session-specific loadings (e.g., factor LDS/PLDS), which may need fewer parameters than a full A.
- Add regularization (e.g., L1 sparsity on A, stability constraints, distance-dependent priors) and report its effect; discuss sample complexity vs. N.
- Relax diagonal R and/or include a small number of never-observed latent dimensions to capture common input (as noted by the authors); report gains.
- Provide empirical ground truth: correlations computed from the simultaneously recorded held-out test set, not only from the “fully observed” model.
- Explain the discrepancy between simulated coupling recovery (high) and real-data coupling correlation (lower), and the necessity of overlap for real data.

Pros for acceptance:
- Addresses an important and practical problem with a simple, general framework.
- Strong synthetic and encouraging real-data results; predicts cross-session noise correlations.
- Clear path to broader use and extension; aligns with NIPS interest in latent dynamical modeling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Real-data evaluation relies on fictional splits; no demonstration on truly separate sessions.
- Dependence on overlap is not quantified; non-overlap fails in practice.
- Modeling assumptions (diagonal Q/R, linearity, stationarity) may limit validity; limited regularization and few ablations.
- Clarity gaps in figures and evaluation protocol.

Overall, this is a promising and original contribution. With strengthened empirical validation on genuinely separate sessions, more thorough baselines/ablations, and clearer presentation, it would make a strong addition to the program.
