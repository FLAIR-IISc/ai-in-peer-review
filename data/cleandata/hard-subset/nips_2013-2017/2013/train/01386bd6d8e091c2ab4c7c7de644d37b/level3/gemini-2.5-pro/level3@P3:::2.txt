This paper introduces a novel and powerful statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential, partial recordings of that population. The core idea is to "stitch" together these partial observations by framing the problem as parameter estimation in a latent dynamical system where, in any given recording session, the unobserved neurons are treated as latent variables. The authors demonstrate this approach using a linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LG-LDS) model, with parameters fit via an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is validated on both simulated data and on 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main result is that this approach can accurately predict statistics of the joint population, such as noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never simultaneously recorded.

The problem of inferring functional connectivity is well-studied, but most prior work assumes simultaneous observation of the entire population of interest. This work addresses a critical and practical experimental limitation. The approach is conceptually related to general system identification from partial observations, and the authors correctly situate their work relative to prior neuroscience literature. The most similar prior work may be that of Pillow & Latham (2008) on latent variable models for partially observed spiking populations, but that work did not address the specific problem of stitching together distinct, sequential recording sessions to form a unified model of a larger circuit.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of stitching together sequential recordings to model a circuit larger than the experimental field-of-view is highly original and significant. It directly addresses a fundamental bottleneck in systems neuroscience and offers a computational path forward to characterize larger-scale circuit dynamics. The potential impact is high, as it could enable new analyses and data-driven modeling of complex neural circuits.
*   **Novel Application of LDS:** The framing of the problem as an LDS with missing data is elegant and powerful. It provides a principled statistical foundation for combining information across different recording sessions. The generality of the framework suggests it could be extended beyond the LG-LDS model to more complex, nonlinear, or point-process models relevant to spiking data.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a convincing demonstration of the method's utility. On simulated data with known ground truth, the model successfully recovers coupling parameters and noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs. The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real calcium imaging data is a clever and effective way to benchmark the method's performance against both a "naive" lower bound and a "fully observed" upper bound. The results, showing that the stitched model’s predictions approach the upper bound, are impressive.

### Weaknesses

*   **Unclear Robustness and Failure Modes:** The method is demonstrated using a linear-Gaussian model, which is a significant simplification of real neural dynamics. It is unclear how robust the method is to violations of these assumptions (e.g., strong nonlinearities, non-Gaussian spike statistics). The paper lacks a discussion of the method's potential failure modes, leaving the reader to wonder about its applicability to data that deviates significantly from the model's assumptions.
*   **Insufficient Technical Detail:** The paper is light on the technical details of the model estimation. The M-step update equations are presented without derivation, making it difficult for an expert to fully assess the implementation or reproduce the results. A more thorough exposition of the algorithm, perhaps in an appendix, would greatly strengthen the paper's clarity and perceived novelty. This lack of detail is a notable weakness in a NIPS paper.
*   **Ambiguity in Interpretation of Results:** On the real data (Fig. 3b), the learned coupling parameters from the stitched model correlate only moderately (r=0.38) with those from the fully-observed model, whereas the predicted noise correlations are a much better match (r=0.84 in Fig. 3d). This discrepancy suggests that the model may be capturing shared variability via latent dynamics rather than accurately identifying direct, pairwise functional couplings. This raises questions about the interpretability of the learned `A` matrix and suggests the model might be better viewed as a good phenomenological description rather than a tool for inferring circuit structure.
*   **Unclear Incremental Benefit of Multiple Subsets:** The paper's intuition is that observing one subset of neurons constrains the full system's dynamics up to a linear transform of the latent variables, and a second observation resolves this ambiguity. However, it's not made clear precisely how much *new information* about cross-subset couplings is provided by the second recording, versus simply providing more data to better constrain the latent dynamics that could have been inferred from the first recording alone.
*   **Lack of Justification for Overlap Requirement:** The authors state that on real data, some overlap between the recorded subsets was "critical for stitching to work." This is a crucial practical finding for experimental design, but the paper offers no intuition or explanation as to why. The overlapping neurons likely serve as anchors to align the latent representations across sessions, but this mechanism should be explicitly discussed.

### Summary and Recommendation

This paper presents a creative, powerful, and highly significant idea for overcoming a major experimental hurdle in neuroscience. The proposed method is elegant and the empirical results are compelling. However, the work is let down by a presentation that prioritizes high-level discussion over crucial technical details and a deeper analysis of the method's assumptions and limitations. The lack of derivations, the ambiguity in the interpretation of the learned parameters, and the missing explanation for the critical role of overlapping neurons detract from an otherwise excellent contribution.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the core idea are substantial. This work is likely to inspire follow-up studies and could become an important tool for neuroscientists. Therefore, I recommend acceptance, but I strongly urge the authors to revise the manuscript to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by adding more technical detail regarding the estimation procedure and providing a more nuanced discussion of the model's limitations and the interpretation of its parameters.
