

This paper introduces a novel statistical approach for "stitching" together sequentially recorded neural subpopulations into a unified dynamical model, enabling inference of joint population dynamics and noise correlations beyond the scope of simultaneous recordings. The core idea treats the problem as fitting a latent linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations across sequential imaging sessions. By leveraging overlapping neuron subsets between sessions, the method recovers functional couplings and noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed neuron pairs, demonstrated on simulated data and 2-photon calcium imaging from mouse somatosensory cortex. The approach extends prior work on partially observed neural data (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008), which focused on stimulus-driven correlations and required strong monotonicity assumptions, by addressing general noise correlations without such constraints.

**Strengths:**  
- **Originality & Significance:** The stitching framework is conceptually novel for neural population dynamics, offering a practical path to expand population sizes beyond current imaging limits. It addresses a critical experimental bottleneck (simultaneous recording of large circuits) and demonstrates clear utility in predicting noise correlations (Fig. 3c), which are key to understanding circuit computation. The real-data application to mouse barrel cortex is compelling and timely.  
- **Technical Soundness (for assumptions):** The LDS framework is well-motivated for the task, and results on simulated data (Fig. 2) show strong recovery of couplings and noise correlations relative to a naive baseline. The method’s extension to point-process data (via latent dynamics) is broadly applicable beyond neural data.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Quality & Robustness:** The method’s reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions is poorly justified. The paper lacks analysis of failure modes under non-Gaussian, nonlinear, or non-stationary data (e.g., spiking dynamics, calcium kinetics). Real data (Fig. 3b) shows poor coupling recovery, suggesting the model may misinterpret common input as direct coupling—a critical limitation for neural interpretation. The absence of robustness tests (e.g., on non-linear synthetic data) undermines confidence in general applicability.  
- **Clarity & Technical Depth:** Key estimation details (e.g., the M-step updates for *R* and *d* in Section 2.1) are oversimplified, omitting derivation and justification. The requirement for overlapping populations (Section 3.2, Fig. 4a) is asserted but not explained: why does overlap *constrain* the model better than non-overlapping subsets? The argument that "more data from one subset" suffices (Section 1) is unaddressed, weakening the incremental benefit claim.  
- **Real-Data Ambiguity:** Fig. 3b reveals a low correlation (0.38) between stitched couplings and the "fully observed" model, yet the paper claims noise correlations are "explained" (Fig. 3d). This inconsistency suggests the predicted correlations may reflect common input (e.g., shared stimuli or latent variables) rather than direct pairwise couplings—a misinterpretation that risks misleading neural conclusions.  
- **Presentation Imbalance:** The paper dedicates excessive space to motivation (Section 1) and results (Section 3) but provides minimal technical exposition (e.g., EM derivation, assumptions for identifiability). The method’s promise is clear, but the lack of clarity on *why* it works (beyond the linear-Gaussian toy example) hinders evaluation.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Borderline Accept (with major revisions).** The core idea is promising and addresses a significant gap in neural data analysis, warranting publication *if* the authors address critical gaps. Specifically:  
1. **Add robustness analysis** on non-linear/non-Gaussian synthetic data (e.g., spiking models) and discuss failure modes.  
2. **Clarify the overlap requirement** via a theoretical argument (e.g., identifiability conditions) and test non-overlapping cases.  
3. **Reconcile the real-data ambiguity** by comparing stitched couplings to independent benchmarks (e.g., electrophysiological connectivity) or explicitly modeling common input.  
4. **Expand technical details** with derivations of M-step updates and discussion of identifiability.  
5. **Refine the incremental benefit argument** by quantifying how much *additional* information overlapping subsets provide versus simply increasing sample size from one subset.  

The paper’s significance is high for neural circuit analysis, but its current quality is hampered by unexamined assumptions and insufficient technical rigor. With revisions addressing these points, it would make a strong contribution to NIPS. As written, it risks overclaiming results while under-delivering on methodological transparency.
