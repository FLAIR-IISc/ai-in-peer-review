This paper proposes a novel statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model, allowing for the expansion of population-sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations and demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using simulated data and experimental calcium imaging in mice.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method and its application to both simulated and real data. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and provides context for the proposed solution. The methods section is detailed and provides a thorough explanation of the linear Gaussian dynamical system model used to "stitch" together the imaged neuron sets.

The results section is divided into two parts, with the first part focusing on the simulated data and the second part on the experimental data. The authors demonstrate that their method can effectively recover the true coupling matrix, including the off-diagonal blocks corresponding to pairs of neurons that were not imaged simultaneously. They also show that their method can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to effectively recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs. The authors demonstrate that their method can accurately reconstruct these correlations, even when the naive approach fails to do so. The paper also provides a thorough comparison of the proposed method with the naive approach and the fully observed model, highlighting its advantages and limitations.

However, there are some areas where the paper could be improved. The figure legends and figure-related text lack sufficient description and elaboration, making it difficult to fully understand the figures without reading the main text. Additionally, the authors do not explain the large difference in correlation results between simulated (fig. 2c) and real data (fig. 3b), which could be an important point of discussion.

In terms of originality, the paper is novel in its approach to inferring functional connections for non-simultaneously imaged neurons. The authors provide a clear explanation of how their method differs from previous contributions and demonstrate its effectiveness in addressing a difficult problem in the field.

The significance of the paper lies in its potential to overcome fundamental limits of simultaneous imaging and scale up circuit-level analyses. The authors demonstrate that their method can be applied to real data and provide promising results, highlighting its potential for future research and applications.
