This paper introduces a statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential, partial recordings of that population. The core idea is to "stitch" together these partial observations into a single coherent model of the entire circuit. The authors formulate this problem as one of fitting a latent dynamical system—specifically, a linear dynamical system (LDS) with Gaussian noise—where each recording session corresponds to observing a different subset of the system's state variables. They demonstrate the method on both simulated data and two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main finding is that this approach can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, a task that is impossible with conventional methods.

The problem of inferring network properties from subsampled data is of great importance in modern neuroscience. This work is related to, but distinct from, previous research. While many studies infer functional connectivity from simultaneously recorded data, and others model partially observed populations (e.g., Pillow & Latham, 2008), this paper is one of the first to explicitly tackle the challenge of integrating non-simultaneous recordings to recover a unified dynamical model. It proposes a direct and powerful solution to a practical limitation of current recording technologies.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Originality and Significance:**
The primary strength of this paper is its novel and highly significant contribution. The idea of "stitching" together partial recordings to characterize a larger, unobserved circuit is powerful and addresses a fundamental bottleneck in systems neuroscience. If robust, this method could substantially expand the effective scale of neural recordings, allowing researchers to build models of circuits that are orders of magnitude larger than what can be measured at once. The framework is general and could, in principle, be extended to other model classes (e.g., point-process models), making it broadly applicable beyond the calcium imaging context presented. The problem is timely and important, and the proposed solution is both creative and elegant.

**Quality:**
The paper demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data, showing that the "stitching" model significantly outperforms a naive baseline and approaches the performance of a model fit to fully observed data. However, there are several concerns regarding the technical quality and the robustness of the method.

1.  **Model Assumptions:** The method relies on a linear-Gaussian LDS. While this is a reasonable starting point for tractability, real neural dynamics are nonlinear and non-Gaussian. The paper lacks a discussion of the method's failure modes. How would performance degrade with strong nonlinearities or different noise statistics? The success on real data is encouraging, but it's unclear if this is due to the dynamics being sufficiently linear or the model being flexible enough to capture second-order statistics despite mismatched assumptions.

2.  **Ambiguity of Learned Parameters:** On the real dataset (Fig. 3b), the correlation between the couplings inferred by the "stitch" model and the "fully observed" model is quite low (0.38), even though the resulting noise correlations are well-matched (Fig. 3d). This suggests that the model may be capturing the correlation structure through mechanisms other than accurate recovery of direct pairwise functional couplings, perhaps by attributing shared variability to effective connections that are actually mediated by unobserved common input. This ambiguity in what the learned parameters represent should be discussed more thoroughly.

3.  **Role of Overlapping Neurons:** A critical practical detail is that for the real data, the authors "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This empirical finding seems to contradict the core intuition provided, which suggests that two non-overlapping sets should be sufficient to resolve the latent linear transformation. The paper does not offer a clear explanation for why this overlap is necessary. Is it a theoretical requirement for identifiability under noisy conditions, or a practical one related to signal-to-noise? This is a key limitation that needs to be better understood and articulated.

**Clarity:**
While the high-level idea is presented clearly, the manuscript suffers from a lack of technical detail that hinders clarity and reproducibility.

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** The methods section is too brief. Key details of the estimation procedure, particularly the M-step updates for the EM algorithm, are not provided. The authors simply state the updates are "as in standard linear dynamical systems," but given the novel structure of the missing data (entire blocks of neurons missing for entire sessions), a more explicit derivation is warranted.

2.  **Unclear Intuition for Information Gain:** The paper states that one partial recording can identify the system up to an invertible linear transformation. A crucial question that is not clearly answered is what, precisely, the second recording contributes. Does it simply provide the necessary constraints to uniquely identify that transformation, or does it provide fundamentally new information about the couplings? It is unclear how much of the performance gain comes from resolving this ambiguity versus simply having more data to constrain the parameters of the first observed subset and its latent extensions.

3.  **Presentation Imbalance:** The paper dedicates significant space to introduction and discussion but comparatively little to the technical exposition of the model and fitting procedure. This imbalance makes it difficult for an expert reader to fully assess the technical novelty and soundness of the work.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper introduces a highly novel and creative approach to a fundamentally important and practical problem in neuroscience.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method shows compelling performance on both synthetic and real neural data, demonstrating its potential utility.
*   **High Potential Impact:** The work could enable a new class of analyses for large-scale neural data and could significantly influence future experimental design.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Lack of Technical Depth:** The methods section is insufficiently detailed, hindering reproducibility and a full assessment of the work's technical contributions.
*   **Unclear Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a hand-wavy intuition but fails to clearly explain key aspects of the method's success and limitations, such as the necessity of overlapping populations on real data.
*   **Limited Exploration of Assumptions:** The robustness of the method to violations of the linear-Gaussian assumption is not discussed, leaving its applicability to more complex, realistic neural dynamics in question.

In summary, this paper presents a promising and important idea. However, the lack of technical clarity and the unaddressed questions regarding the model's assumptions and limitations are significant weaknesses. While the potential impact is high, the paper in its current form feels more like a proof-of-concept than a complete and rigorously presented piece of work.
