## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a novel statistical method for stitching together sequentially imaged neuron sets to expand analyzable population sizes, addressing a key limitation in circuit analysis – the inability to simultaneously record from all relevant neurons. The authors frame this as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, utilizing a linear Gaussian model and an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for parameter estimation. This approach aims to recover correlations between neurons not measured concurrently, a significant challenge in understanding circuit dynamics.

The work builds upon existing methods for inferring neural connectivity from calcium imaging and electrophysiology, but distinguishes itself by specifically tackling the problem of *non-simultaneous* recordings. While previous studies have focused on complete or partially observed data within a single recording session, this paper uniquely addresses the challenge of combining information across multiple, disjoint recordings. The validation on both simulated and experimental data (mouse somatosensory cortex) demonstrates the potential of the method. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a particularly promising result.

A key strength of the paper lies in its effective recovery of pairwise correlations in simulated data. However, a significant weakness arises when applying the method to real data. The authors acknowledge a substantial discrepancy between the performance on simulated data (strong correlation recovery) and real data (requiring substantial overlap between imaging sessions for reasonable results). This discrepancy is not adequately explained, and the reasons behind the reduced performance in the real data remain unclear. This is a critical issue that needs to be addressed. 

Furthermore, while the paper is generally well-written, the figure legends and text relating to the figures lack sufficient detail. Providing more context and elaboration within the figures and their descriptions would significantly improve clarity. For example, the specific parameters used in the simulations and the criteria for selecting neurons for analysis in the experimental data are not fully transparent. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a significant problem in neural circuit analysis.
*   Promising results on simulated data demonstrating the feasibility of stitching sequential recordings.
*   Potential to overcome fundamental limits of simultaneous imaging.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Large discrepancy between simulated and real data performance without adequate explanation.
*   Lack of clarity in figure descriptions and related text.
*   Limited exploration of the impact of varying overlap between imaging sessions.



**Overall:** This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field, offering a promising approach to overcome limitations in simultaneous imaging. However, the unexplained discrepancy between simulation and real data, coupled with clarity issues, prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection with request for revision**, contingent on addressing the discrepancy in performance and improving the clarity of the presentation. The authors should thoroughly investigate the factors contributing to the difference between simulated and real data and provide a compelling explanation for the observed results.
