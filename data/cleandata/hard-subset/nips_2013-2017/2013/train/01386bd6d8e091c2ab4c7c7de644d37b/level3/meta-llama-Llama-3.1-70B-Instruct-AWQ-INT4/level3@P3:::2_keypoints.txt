- Introduces a novel statistical "stitching" method framing sequentially imaged neurons as a latent dynamical system with missing observations.  
- Demonstrates method on simulated and real mouse somatosensory cortex data to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.  
- Paper is well-motivated and situates itself relative to prior work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging.  
- High-level explanation is clear and concise, with stated assumptions and limitations.  
- Use of simulated data to evaluate performance is appropriate and justified.  
- Results are promising, particularly for predicting noise correlations and outperforming a naive per-session baseline.  
- Robustness to violations of linearity and Gaussianity assumptions is unclear and not empirically characterized.  
- Unclear incremental benefit of stitching versus simply using more data from a single subset when the full model may be recoverable from one subset up to a latent transform.  
- Requirement for overlapping populations in real-data modeling is not well-explained; unclear how method performs without overlap.  
- Estimation details—especially derivation and explanation of the M-step updates—are insufficiently presented.  
- Presentation is imbalanced: extensive intro/discussion but inadequate technical exposition and derivations.  
- Reviewer scores: Quality 7/10, Clarity 6/10, Originality 8/10, Significance 8/10.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel stitching approach, potential significant contribution to neuroscience, and promising predictive results.  
- Arguments for rejection: unclear robustness, unclear incremental benefit, poorly explained estimation/M-step, and imbalanced presentation.
