

This paper proposes a novel statistical framework for "stitching" together sequentially recorded partial neural population data to infer dynamics of larger circuits beyond the simultaneously imaged population size. The core idea—modeling the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations—builds upon Pillow & Latham (2008), who addressed *partially observed* neural activity within a *single* recording session. The authors extend this to *multiple sequential sessions* with non-overlapping (or partially overlapping) neuron subsets, demonstrating the method on simulated data and mouse somatosensory cortex calcium imaging. Crucially, they show the stitched model predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, which conventional approaches cannot.

**Strengths:**  
*Originality (High):* The method directly addresses a critical experimental bottleneck (limited simultaneous imaging) in neuroscience. While Pillow & Latham (2008) handled missing data *within* a session, this work tackles missing data *across sessions*, a distinct and significant challenge. No prior NIPS work solves this specific problem.  
*Significance (High):* The approach enables studying large-scale circuit dynamics (e.g., thousands of neurons in cortical barrels) with current 2-photon imaging technology. The demonstration on real data showing near-fully-observed correlation prediction (Fig. 3d, r=0.84 vs. naive r=0.15) is compelling for the field.  
*Quality (Moderate-High):* The synthetic data experiments are rigorous (Fig. 2), with clear metrics (coupling recovery, noise correlation prediction). The EM-based inference is sound, and the method scales to realistic population sizes (e.g., 80 neurons).  

**Weaknesses:**  
*Quality (Moderate):*  
- **Robustness Uncertainty:** The linear-Gaussian assumption is strong. The paper lacks analysis of failure modes under non-linear dynamics or non-Gaussian noise (e.g., spiking, calcium transients), which are central to neural data. This limits confidence in real-world applicability.  
- **Incremental Benefit Ambiguity:** Why is stitching *multiple subsets* better than simply recording *more data from one subset*? The paper claims stitching provides "extra coupling information," but Fig. 4b shows prediction accuracy increases only slightly with population size. If more data from one subset suffices, stitching adds little value.  
- **Overlap Requirement:** The method requires substantial overlap (≥40%) between subsets (Section 3.2). The paper states this is "critical" but provides no theoretical explanation (e.g., why overlap enables unique parameter recovery vs. a latent transform). This is a major practical limitation.  

*Clarity (Moderate):*  
- **Technical Gaps:** The M-step updates (Section 2.1) are presented without derivation, undermining reproducibility. Key details (e.g., how correlated noise is handled, convergence guarantees) are omitted.  
- **Real-Data Interpretation:** Fig. 3b shows stitched couplings poorly correlate with fully-observed couplings (r=0.38) for non-simultaneously recorded pairs. The paper claims these predict noise correlations (Fig. 3d), but noise correlations could arise from common unobserved inputs (e.g., as in Vidne et al., 2011), not direct couplings. This ambiguity weakens the significance claim.  

*Originality (High, but context matters):* While novel for *sequential* stitching, the core LDS model is standard. The true contribution is the *application to multi-session neural data*, which is well-motivated and distinct from prior work (e.g., [12] requires monotonicity; [13] doesn’t scale).  

**Pro/Con Acceptance Summary:**  
*Pros:* Solves a genuine experimental bottleneck; validated on synthetic and real data; enables new analyses of large circuits; conceptually elegant.  
*Cons:* Assumptions limit applicability; incremental benefit over single-subset data is unclear; overlap requirement unexplained; technical details insufficient; real-data interpretation conflates noise correlations with direct couplings.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a promising method with high potential impact for neuroscience. The core idea is novel and addresses a critical gap in leveraging existing imaging technology. However, the paper’s weaknesses—particularly the lack of robustness analysis, ambiguous incremental benefit, and unclear overlap requirement—prevent it from being a strong contribution. The technical gaps also reduce confidence in the method’s reliability.  

**Recommendation:** **Weak Reject** (or **Borderline** with major revisions). The work is significant and original, but the current presentation fails to adequately justify the method’s necessity and scope. The authors should: (1) rigorously test robustness to non-Gaussian dynamics; (2) quantify the *incremental* gain of stitching vs. single-subset data; (3) explain the overlap requirement theoretically; (4) clarify whether noise correlations reflect direct couplings or common inputs; (5) provide full M-step derivations. Without these, the contribution is overstated. The significance is real but not yet demonstrably superior to simpler alternatives.
