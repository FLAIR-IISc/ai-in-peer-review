This paper proposes a principled “stitching” approach to infer neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to model the full population as a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where each imaging session reveals a different subset of neurons through a known selection matrix. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned via EM with Kalman smoothing across sessions, yielding a single joint model from which one can predict cross-session statistics, notably noise correlations between neuron pairs never observed simultaneously. On simulations, the method recovers off-diagonal couplings and noise correlations; on two in vivo two-photon datasets (mouse S1), it predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs nearly as well as a fully observed upper-bound model and much better than a naive baseline that fits separate session-wise LDSs.

Relation to prior work: The paper is closely related to latent dynamical models for partially observed populations (e.g., Pillow & Latham NIPS’08 latent GLMs; Vidne et al. 2011; population latent-factor models like GPFA/PLDS) and system identification with missing data (EM/Kalman, subspace ID). It differs by directly treating the “state” as the full N-neuron vector observed in pieces across sessions, using known selection matrices to couple sessions, rather than relying purely on a low-dimensional latent shared across neurons. It also complements work that infers connectivity or correlations from calcium imaging (Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012) by addressing cross-session inference. The framing as an LDS with missing observations and the demonstration that noise correlations between non-simultaneous pairs can be predicted on real data appear novel and valuable for current imaging regimes.

Strengths
- Clear, timely problem: practical path to expand beyond simultaneous field-of-view limitations in calcium imaging.
- Conceptual simplicity and generality: the stitching view applies broadly (could extend to spike count/point-process observations, multi-session ephys).
- Empirical value: on real S1 data, accurately predicts noise correlations across non-simultaneously imaged pairs, approaching a fully observed upper bound.
- Solid synthetic validation: recovers off-diagonal couplings and predicts latent activity of unobserved neurons; performance bracketing with upper and lower bounds is informative.
- Scalability tactics: online EM initialization then batch EM.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Assumptions and failure modes: The method hinges on linear-Gaussian dynamics on fluorescence, diagonal Q/R, stationarity, and consistent dynamics across sessions. Please describe robustness when these are violated (e.g., nonlinear calcium/spiking transforms; correlated process noise; nonstationarities across sessions such as photobleaching or state changes). A sensitivity study varying nonlinearity, sampling rate (3 Hz), process/measurement noise correlation, and mild drift would strengthen claims.
- Role of overlap: In real data, stitching worked only with overlapping subsets; without overlap, performance degraded. Provide an explicit identifiability argument: with disjoint sessions, parameters are only determined up to an invertible transform on the unobserved subspace; overlap anchors the coordinate system. Derive conditions (e.g., minimal overlap, excitation by stimuli) and quantify how performance scales with overlap; show controlled experiments sweeping overlap from 0–50–80%.
- Incremental information from additional sessions: If the first session identifies the unobserved block only up to a transform, the second session “reveals” those coordinates. Clarify this point with a brief proof or citation to system ID literature and explain why new sessions provide more than just more data for the first subset. Consider a multi-session alignment view that estimates the linear transforms explicitly.
- Interpreting couplings vs correlations: On real data, stitched couplings correlate weakly with fully observed couplings, while correlation predictions are strong. Emphasize that A encodes statistical couplings, not synaptic connectivity, and is confounded by common input. Consider adding a low-rank-plus-sparse prior (common drive + sparse direct couplings) or a low-dimensional latent process noise (à la Vidne/Macke) and report whether this improves both coupling interpretability and correlation prediction.
- Estimation details and clarity: The derivations are too terse. Please include the complete-data log-likelihood, closed-form M-step updates for A, B, Q (and any regularization/stability constraints), initialization, convergence criteria, and computational complexity in N, T, and number of sessions. The measurement model equation has notational issues; clarify y^k_t = C^k(x_t + d) + ε^k_t, ε^k_t ~ N(0, R).
- Baselines: The naive model is a weak lower bound. Add comparisons to low-dimensional latent models that naturally handle missing data (e.g., GPFA/PLDS with EM; subspace ID with missing outputs; covariance completion of time-lagged statistics), and to methods that infer cross-session covariance using shared stimuli (Machens/Wohrer).
- Practical considerations: Discuss regularization of A (stability, sparsity), hyperparameter selection, and runtime/scaling to thousands of neurons; report whether sparsity penalties or stability constraints improved performance on real data.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound within the LDS framework; EM/Kalman methodology is standard. Empirical results are convincing for correlation prediction, less so for couplings. Missing derivational detail and identifiability discussion reduce rigor.
- Clarity: Writing is readable but imbalanced: long motivation/discussion, sparse technical exposition. Tighten notation, expand estimation details, and add a section on identifiability/overlap.
- Originality: Framing multi-session stitching for population dynamics with demonstrated cross-session correlation prediction on real data is novel and impactful relative to prior partially observed/latent GLM work.
- Significance: High. If robust, this is immediately useful to labs doing multi-field imaging, enabling inference of across-field correlations and activity prediction. Extensions to spike train models would broaden impact further.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, common experimental constraint with a simple, general tool.
- Strong empirical evidence that cross-session noise correlations can be recovered on real data.
- Method likely to be adopted and extended (e.g., to PLDS/point-process settings).

Cons for acceptance
- Limited technical detail; identifiability and overlap requirements insufficiently analyzed.
- Robustness to violations of linear-Gaussian assumptions not established.
- Weak interpretability of learned couplings; baseline comparisons could be stronger.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide identifiability conditions and a quantitative analysis of how overlap affects parameter recovery and correlation prediction?
- How does performance change with correlated process noise, low-rank latent inputs, or a PLDS observation model?
- What regularization/stability constraints are used for A? Any spectral radius control?
- How sensitive are results to nonstationarities across sessions? Did you attempt session-specific offsets or drift terms?

Overall recommendation: Promising and practical contribution with strong empirical results on correlation prediction but lacking technical depth in derivations and identifiability analysis. With added clarity and robustness studies, this could be a solid NIPS paper. Borderline accept.
