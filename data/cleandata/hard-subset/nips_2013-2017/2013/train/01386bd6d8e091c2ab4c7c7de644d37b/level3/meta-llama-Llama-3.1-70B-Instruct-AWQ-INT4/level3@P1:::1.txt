This paper proposes a novel statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model, effectively expanding the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging in mice, demonstrating the recovery of correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons.

The paper's main contribution is the formulation of the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations using a linear Gaussian model. This approach allows for the estimation of a joint model of the activity of a neural population, capturing the correlation structure and stimulus selectivity of the population from partial observations of the population activity. The model parameters, including the coupling matrix, stimulus receptive-field matrix, neural and measurement noise covariances, are estimated via a variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

One of the paper's strengths is its ability to effectively recover pairwise correlations between neuron pairs not measured simultaneously. The method is demonstrated to work well on both simulated and real data, although the real data requires substantial overlap between the imaged populations for reasonable results. The paper also highlights the potential of the method to overcome the fundamental limits of simultaneous imaging and scale up circuit-level analyses.

However, there are some unexplained discrepancies in the results, such as the large difference in correlation results between simulated (fig. 2c) and real data (fig. 3b). Additionally, the paper could benefit from more detailed figure legends and figure-related text to facilitate a clearer understanding of the results.

In terms of originality, the paper's approach to inferring functional connections for non-simultaneously imaged neurons is novel compared to prior work. The significance of the paper lies in its potential to overcome the limitations of simultaneous imaging and enable the analysis of larger neural populations.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel and effective method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons.
* The method is validated on both simulated and real data, demonstrating its potential to recover correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons.
* The paper highlights the potential of the method to overcome the fundamental limits of simultaneous imaging and scale up circuit-level analyses.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from more detailed figure legends and figure-related text to facilitate a clearer understanding of the results.
* The unexplained discrepancies in the results, such as the large difference in correlation results between simulated and real data, may raise concerns about the method's robustness.
* The paper's reliance on a linear Gaussian model may limit its applicability to more complex neural systems.
