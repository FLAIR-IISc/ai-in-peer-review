This paper introduces a novel method for inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The authors propose a statistical tool for "stitching" together measurements from multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit, allowing for the characterization of the joint dynamics of large populations of neurons even if many of the neurons have not been imaged simultaneously.

The paper relates to previous work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data and electrophysiological measurements. However, the authors' approach is distinct in that it attempts to infer functional connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons. The method is based on a latent dynamical system model, which is fitted to the data using an expectation-maximization algorithm.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using both simulated and real data from the mouse somatosensory cortex. They show that the method can accurately recover the true coupling matrix, including the off-diagonal blocks which correspond to pairs of neurons that were not imaged simultaneously. They also demonstrate that the method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

The paper's main strength is its novel idea of stitching together sub-models to infer large-network properties. This approach is broadly applicable and could be extended to point-process data. However, the paper has several limitations. The method may be limited to near linear-Gaussian data, and the paper lacks intuition on failure modes when assumptions are violated. Additionally, the paper does not explain why overlap between populations is necessary for the method to work.

The paper's results are promising, but the presentation is imbalanced, with too much emphasis on the introduction and discussion, and not enough technical exposition. The estimation and derivation details are not fully explained, which undermines the clarity and perceived novelty of the method.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. However, the paper could benefit from more detailed derivations and explanations of the estimation procedure. The writing is clear, but the organization could be improved to better balance the technical and non-technical sections.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written, but some sections could be improved. The introduction is clear, but the technical sections could benefit from more detailed explanations and derivations. The results are well-presented, but the discussion could be more concise.

In terms of originality, the paper's idea of stitching together sub-models is novel and powerful. However, the method is not fully explained, and the estimation procedure could be improved.

In terms of significance, the paper's results are promising, and the method could be broadly applicable. However, the paper's limitations and potential failure modes need to be more fully explored.
