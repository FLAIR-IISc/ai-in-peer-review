Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a statistical “stitching” method to combine sequential, partially overlapping calcium imaging recordings of the same neural circuit into a single joint model, thereby expanding the analyzable population beyond simultaneous imaging limits. The authors cast the problem as a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations: x_t evolves via x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t, η_t ~ N(0,Q), and observations y_t are subsets of x_t with additive Gaussian noise y_t = C (x_t + d) + ε_t, ε_t ~ N(0,R). The coupling matrix A captures effective (functional) interactions and shapes noise correlations; B captures stimulus receptive fields; Q and R model neural and measurement noise (Q diagonal by default; R estimated elementwise, implying a diagonal form). Parameters are fit with a variant of EM (online mini-batches followed by batch EM), performing Kalman smoothing E-steps with session-specific selection matrices C.

The key claim is that by integrating multiple sessions from overlapping subsets of neurons, the method can recover cross-population couplings and, more robustly, pairwise noise correlations for neuron pairs never observed simultaneously. Validation is shown on simulated data (two non-overlapping subsets) and on two mouse S1 calcium imaging datasets (awake/anesthetized), where “fictional imaging” experiments split simultaneously recorded populations into two partially overlapping sessions. Relative to prior work on inferring connectivity from imaging or spikes (e.g., GLMs, latent GLMs, and information-theoretic approaches), this paper is novel in explicitly inferring functional relationships and correlations for non-simultaneously imaged neurons by leveraging latent dynamical structure. It also relates to subspace/system identification and latent factor models (e.g., LDS/GPFA), but targets the cross-session alignment and missing-data stitching problem, which has been less explored.

Strengths
- Clear, principled modeling: simple LDS with missing data, leveraging well-understood EM/Kalman machinery; parameters and updates are specified.
- Compelling goal and strong motivation: overcoming fundamental simultaneous imaging limits to access larger circuits.
- Empirical validation: simulated data demonstrate recovery of off-block couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs; real data show improved recovery of cross-session correlations versus a naive baseline and approaching a “fully observed” upper bound.
- Practical significance: predicts cross-population correlations and unobserved activity; framework is extensible (e.g., unobserved latent inputs, sparsity priors).
- Originality: explicitly addresses inference across non-simultaneously imaged neurons, differentiating from prior connectivity-from-calcium and latent GLM work.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Simulated vs. real data gap: On simulations, the method works with non-overlapping sessions; on real data, substantial overlap was necessary for reasonable results (authors “failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons”). This discrepancy is not adequately explained, and the large difference between simulated (Fig. 2c) and real (Fig. 3b) correlation recovery merits analysis (e.g., role of model mismatch, noise correlations in R, session drift, nonstationarity, stimulus alignment, calcium nonlinearity).
- Identifiability and required conditions: Theoretical intuition is given (recover up to a linear transform then align), but practical identifiability with finite noisy data is not analyzed. What overlap fraction, session lengths, and stimulus diversity are minimally required? How sensitive is stitching to weak cross-population coupling?
- Noise modeling: Both Q and R are treated effectively as diagonal, yet real calcium imaging exhibits significant shared measurement/neuropil noise and motion artifacts. How does correlated R (or unmodeled low-rank latent inputs) affect estimates, and can the approach incorporate low-rank R or additional latent LDS inputs (as briefly mentioned)?
- Scalability and regularization: A is dense with O(N^2) parameters; scalability to thousands of neurons and overfitting risks are concerns. Some unreported sparsity penalties are mentioned—results should include regularized variants (sparse A, low-rank + sparse) and complexity/runtime scaling.
- Evaluation design: The real-data evaluation uses “fictional” splits from simultaneously recorded data. While helpful for benchmarking, it does not fully reflect true sequential recordings with session-to-session drift, registration errors, and nonstationarities. A small real sequential dataset (with documented overlap and alignment) would strengthen claims.
- Clarity: Figures/legends lack detail; axes units, definitions (e.g., how noise correlations are computed after stimulus regression), and dataset/task specifics should be clearer. The role and estimation of d, stationarity assumptions, and preprocessing (filtering) deserve more detail.

Quality
- Technically sound core modeling and inference; claims about correlation recovery are supported on simulations and partially on real data. However, key limitations (overlap requirement, correlated noise, nonstationarity) are under-analyzed. Additional ablations (varying overlap, SNR, session drift; mis-registered C; correlated R; stimulus strength) would improve rigor.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with a crisp model statement. Figure legends and methods related to figures need more elaboration, and several assumptions (diagonal Q/R, stationarity, preprocessing) should be stated more prominently. Please add a schematic of the stitching scenario with overlap, and detail the “online EM” schedule and convergence checks.

Originality
- High within this space: inferring functional relationships for non-simultaneously recorded neurons using LDS stitching is novel relative to prior calcium/GLM work. Related LDS/GPFA/system ID literature should be acknowledged more broadly (e.g., Yu et al., 2009 GPFA; Smith & Brown state-space), clarifying differences.

Significance
- Potentially impactful: provides a path to scale circuit analyses beyond simultaneous imaging limits and to estimate cross-population noise correlations. Adoption will hinge on robustness to real experimental complications (overlap, drift, noise), which requires further empirical characterization.

Suggestions for improvement
- Systematically study performance versus overlap fraction, session length, SNR, cross-population coupling strength, and stimulus richness; report when non-overlapping stitching works or fails on real data.
- Analyze and report sensitivity to correlated measurement noise and to low-rank common inputs; consider modeling R as diagonal + low-rank or adding latent input factors.
- Include regularization (sparse/low-rank A) in main results; report improvements and stability.
- Address registration errors and neuron identity uncertainty; test robustness to mis-specified C.
- Provide real sequential recordings (even small-scale) with known overlaps to complement fictional splits.
- Clarify computation: parameter counts, runtime/memory scaling, initialization, and convergence diagnostics.

Pros
- Novel and important problem; simple, well-motivated model.
- Strong simulation results; meaningful real-data improvements over a naive baseline.
- Practical framework likely to stimulate further methodological development.

Cons
- Dependence on overlap in real data undermines the non-overlapping claim; discrepancy unexplained.
- Noise/measurement model likely misspecified for calcium imaging; correlated noise ignored.
- Limited analysis of identifiability, robustness, and scalability; evaluation based on fictional splits.

Overall assessment
Promising and timely contribution with clear novelty and potential impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the simulated–real discrepancy, expanding robustness analyses (especially overlap and noise modeling), and improving clarity of figures and methodological details.
