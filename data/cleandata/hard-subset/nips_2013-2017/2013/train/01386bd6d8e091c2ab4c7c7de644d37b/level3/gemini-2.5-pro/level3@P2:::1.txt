### Summary

This paper presents a novel statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population by "stitching" together multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, partially overlapping subsets of neurons. The core idea is to frame this as a latent variable problem, where the full population's activity is modeled as a latent state in a linear dynamical system (LDS), and each recording session provides partial, noisy observations of this state. The model parameters, including the functional coupling matrix between all neurons, are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors demonstrate their approach on both simulated data and two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main contribution is showing that this method can predict "noise correlations" between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, thereby overcoming a fundamental limitation of current experimental techniques. This work builds upon the literature of latent dynamical systems and functional connectivity inference, but is novel in its direct application to combining non-simultaneous recordings to infer unobserved joint statistics.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as an LDS with missing data is elegant and appropriate. The use of EM for parameter fitting is standard and well-justified. The validation strategy, which includes a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound, provides a clear and effective way to benchmark the method's performance on the experimental data.

The main strength lies in the successful demonstration on simulated data (Figure 2). The method impressively recovers the underlying coupling matrix and, more importantly, the noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with high accuracy (correlation of 0.92). This serves as a strong proof-of-concept for the proposed framework.

However, a significant weakness arises from the discrepancy between the performance on simulated and real data. The authors state that the method worked on simulated non-overlapping populations, but for the real calcium imaging data, they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" and had to use subsets with substantial (60%) overlap. This is a critical limitation that is not sufficiently explored or explained. The success of the method seems highly dependent on this overlap in practice, which should be highlighted and discussed as it impacts the experimental design implications.

Furthermore, there is a large, unexplained performance gap in the recovery of the coupling matrix between the simulated results (Figure 2c, correlation 0.73) and the real data results (Figure 3b, correlation 0.38). While perfect recovery is not expected on real data, the paper would be much stronger if it offered potential reasons for this substantial drop, such as model mismatch (the linear Gaussian assumption being too simple for real neural dynamics), non-stationarity of the recordings, or lower signal-to-noise ratios.

**Clarity:**
The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the methods section provides a concise description of the model.

However, the clarity could be improved in the presentation of the results. The figure legends and the accompanying text are somewhat sparse. For instance, the description of the "fictional imaging scenario" could be more detailed to aid reproducibility (e.g., were the overlapping neurons chosen randomly? How does this choice affect results?). The axes in Figure 3d and Figure 4 could be more explicitly labeled in the captions. A more thorough walkthrough of each figure panel in the main text would help the reader fully appreciate the results.

**Originality:**
The work is highly original. While previous studies have used latent variable models to infer functional connectivity or account for unobserved common input from simultaneously recorded data, this paper is the first to our knowledge to explicitly use this framework to combine non-simultaneous recordings and infer statistics for unobserved pairs. It directly addresses a novel and important problem in computational neuroscience, clearly distinguishing its contribution from prior work which either made strong assumptions (e.g., relating noise and signal correlations) or was not applicable to this specific problem setting.

**Significance:**
The potential significance of this work is high. Experimental techniques for recording neural activity are fundamentally limited in the number of neurons that can be monitored simultaneously. If this method proves robust, it offers a powerful computational tool to scale up our models of neural circuits far beyond the physical limitations of the recording technology. The ability to estimate the correlation structure of a circuit comprising thousands of neurons, as the authors suggest, would be a major advance for testing theories of neural computation. This approach could change how experiments are designed, encouraging sequential recordings of overlapping populations to build a more complete picture of circuit dynamics.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Addresses a major problem:** The paper tackles the significant and practical problem of limited simultaneous recording capacity in neuroscience.
2.  **Novel and elegant approach:** The formulation as an LDS with missing observations is a creative and powerful way to frame the problem.
3.  **Strong proof-of-concept:** The method is shown to work exceptionally well on simulated data, providing clear evidence for the validity of the core idea.
4.  **Promising real-world application:** The application to real calcium imaging data, despite limitations, demonstrates feasibility and produces results that significantly outperform a naive baseline, suggesting real-world utility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Crucial limitation is under-reported:** The method's failure on real data without substantial neuron overlap is a major weakness that is mentioned only briefly. This significantly tempers the claim of stitching together separate populations and needs a more thorough investigation and discussion.
2.  **Unexplained performance gap:** The large drop in parameter recovery performance between simulated and real data is not discussed, leaving the reader to question the robustness and practical applicability of the model.
3.  **Minor clarity issues:** The descriptions of the experimental setup and figures lack sufficient detail, which could hinder understanding and reproducibility.

In conclusion, this is a very interesting paper with a high-impact idea. The weaknesses, particularly the unexplained dependency on population overlap in real data, should be addressed to strengthen the paper. However, the novelty of the approach and its potential significance make it a strong candidate for acceptance. I would encourage the authors to revise the manuscript to include a discussion on the potential causes for the performance discrepancies between simulated and real data.
