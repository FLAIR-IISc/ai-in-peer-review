- Proposes stitching sequential, partially overlapping neural recordings into a single linear-Gaussian latent dynamical system to infer joint dynamics and cross-session noise correlations.
- Uses session-specific selection matrices to handle missing observations and learns parameters with EM/Kalman smoothing plus an online-EM variant.
- Evaluated on simulations and 2-photon calcium imaging (awake and anesthetized), using “fictional sessions” to benchmark against full-observation upper bounds and naive lower bounds.
- Frames work relative to classical system identification and prior latent models for partially observed populations; novelty lies in the stitching application and cross-session correlation prediction.
- Strength: timely and broadly applicable idea for leveraging overlapping recordings to infer large-network dynamics beyond field-of-view limits.
- Strength: demonstrates accurate prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, outperforming naive baselines and approaching fully observed bounds.
- Strength: simple, computationally tractable LDS formulation that is extensible and readily implementable.
- Strength: careful benchmarking design with upper/lower bounds and exploration across population sizes.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions and limited discussion of failure modes under nonlinearities, non-Gaussian noise, or calcium-model mismatch.
- Weakness: identifiability and the role of overlap are asserted but not formalized; no theory or sufficient intuition for when overlap is necessary/sufficient.
- Weakness: possible conflation of direct couplings and shared low-dimensional inputs; modest correspondence in inferred couplings suggests correlations may be driven by latent inputs.
- Weakness: key estimation details (full M-step derivations, initialization, stability constraints on A, regularization, model selection, computational complexity) are missing or only sketched.
- Quality: technically sound within LDS framework, but identifiability and overlap claims need theoretical backing or systematic empirical analysis.
- Clarity: generally well written but lacking important derivations, algorithmic details, runtime/memory scaling, and precise procedures for computing noise correlations.
- Originality: stitching application to calcium imaging is novel; the underlying LDS+missing observations approach is incremental.
- Significance: high practical relevance for modern imaging and experimental design if made more robust and theoretically justified.
- Suggestion: provide identifiability analysis detailing conditions (overlap size, stimulus richness, SNR) under which stitching recovers global parameters.
- Suggestion: systematically vary overlap in experiments and test zero-overlap scenarios with additional constraints (shared stimulus regressors, anchors, low-rank inputs).
- Suggestion: include regularization (sparsity, low-rank Q), model selection, and sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters.
- Suggestion: test robustness to model mismatch (nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, calcium convolution, spike-based outputs) and compare to deconvolved-spike LDS or latent GLMs.
- Suggestion: disentangle contributions of explicit inter-population couplings vs. low-rank common inputs to correlation-prediction performance.
- Suggestion: expand reproducibility details (full M-step, initialization, online-EM schedule, convergence criteria, code availability).
- Pros for acceptance: practical, impactful idea with strong empirical gains in cross-session correlation prediction and a simple, scalable method others can adopt.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient theoretical grounding on stitching/overlap, limited robustness analyses, weak correspondence in inferred couplings, and incomplete algorithmic details.
- Recommendation: borderline reject/weak reject — promising but requires stronger identifiability theory, robustness tests, and fuller technical exposition before acceptance.
