

This paper proposes a statistically principled method to "stitch" together sequentially recorded subpopulations of neurons into a unified dynamical model, enabling inference of large-scale network properties from limited simultaneous imaging data. The core idea—treating non-simultaneous recordings as missing observations in a latent dynamical system—builds on prior work in partially observed neural systems (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008), but extends it to predict noise correlations across non-overlapping neuron pairs. The authors demonstrate this on simulated data and two in vivo calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing improved correlation prediction over naive baselines.  

**Strengths:**  
The method addresses a genuine experimental bottleneck: current 2-photon imaging captures only hundreds of neurons, while cortical circuits involve thousands. The real-data validation in awake/anesthetized mice is compelling, showing stitching predicts noise correlations (Fig. 3d, r=0.84) significantly better than the naive approach (r=0.15). The framework is broadly applicable beyond calcium imaging (e.g., to spike trains via latent Gaussian models) and generalizes to more than two subpopulations. The synthetic data analysis (Fig. 2) clearly illustrates the core mechanism: stitching recovers cross-couplings between non-simultaneously imaged pairs by leveraging latent dynamics, a non-trivial contribution.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Overly restrictive assumptions:** The linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) model is insufficient for neural data, which exhibits strong nonlinearities (e.g., spiking, calcium transients). While the authors acknowledge this, the paper lacks analysis of how violations degrade performance. The method’s success on real data may stem from the data’s approximate linearity (e.g., slow calcium dynamics), but without testing on nonlinear synthetic data or discussing failure modes, the scope remains unclear.  
2. **Unclear role of overlap:** The method requires overlapping neurons between sessions (Fig. 4a), but the paper does not explain *why* overlap is necessary beyond empirical observation. Theoretical justification—e.g., how overlap resolves identifiability issues in the latent space—would strengthen the contribution. Without this, the requirement seems ad hoc.  
3. **Questionable incremental benefit:** The authors claim stitching enables inference beyond single-session limits. However, if a single session (with sufficient data) can recover the full model up to a latent transform (as suggested by theoretical results), stitching adds little beyond simply gathering more data from one session. The paper fails to compare stitching to this alternative (e.g., "would stitching outperform a single session with double the duration?").  
4. **Ambiguous interpretation of results:** The stitched couplings correlate poorly with the "fully observed" model (Fig. 3b, r=0.38), yet noise correlations are well-predicted. This suggests the model may capture common-input effects (e.g., shared external drive) rather than true pairwise couplings. The discussion does not address whether the predicted correlations reflect direct interactions or indirect influences.  
5. **Technical gaps:** The M-step updates (Eq. 3) are presented without derivation, undermining reproducibility. The online-EM implementation details are sparse, and the paper omits how hyperparameters (e.g., noise covariance) were selected. For a NIPS paper, this lack of methodological rigor is concerning.  

**Overall assessment:**  
The paper presents a valuable *experimental* contribution—enabling new analyses of large-scale neural dynamics with existing technology. However, the *theoretical* contribution is incremental: it adapts known LDS techniques to a new application without resolving fundamental identifiability or model-accuracy issues. The real-data results are promising but lack critical validation (e.g., against nonlinear models, overlap ablation studies). The method’s utility hinges on the linearity assumption, which is untested and potentially limiting. While the approach could be useful for practitioners, its scientific impact is diminished by the unaddressed weaknesses above.  

**Recommendation:**  
**Weak Reject**. The work is technically sound *for the specific application* but lacks the theoretical novelty or robustness required for NIPS. The key limitations (assumptions, overlap necessity, coupling interpretation) prevent it from advancing the state of the art. With deeper analysis of model failures, theoretical grounding for overlap, and comparison to single-session scaling, this could be a strong contribution. As-is, it falls short of NIPS’ quality and significance criteria.
