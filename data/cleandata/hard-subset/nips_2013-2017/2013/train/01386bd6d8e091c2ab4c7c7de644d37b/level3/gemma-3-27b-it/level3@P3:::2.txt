This paper introduces a novel method for inferring neural population dynamics by “stitching” together sequentially recorded subnetworks into a unified model. The core idea – framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations – is elegant and addresses a critical limitation in current imaging techniques, namely the inability to simultaneously record from entire neural circuits. The approach extends to point-process data and demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, successfully predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The work builds upon a rich history of attempts to infer connectivity and dynamics from partial neural observations (e.g., [8, 9, 10, 11]). However, unlike these prior efforts, this paper specifically tackles the challenge of combining *sequential* observations, a problem not addressed by studies focused on simultaneously observed subsets or reconstructions from subsampled connectivity matrices [14, 15]. The closest related work, Pillow & Latham [13], is acknowledged, but the current method differs in its scalability and applicability to larger population sizes.

Despite the novelty and potential impact of the idea, several weaknesses temper my assessment. A primary concern is the robustness of the method. The paper focuses on a relatively simple linear-Gaussian dynamical system. While this choice is motivated by tractability, it raises questions about the method’s performance when applied to more complex, non-linear neural dynamics. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but fail to provide intuition regarding failure modes or conditions under which the linear-Gaussian assumption breaks down. A more thorough exploration of these limitations, perhaps through simulations with varying degrees of non-linearity, would significantly strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the incremental benefit of sequentially recording multiple subnetworks is not fully convincing. If the full model can be recovered from a single subset (up to a latent transformation), as suggested by the authors, it remains unclear how much additional information is gained by observing other subsets. The paper hints at improved estimation accuracy with more data, but doesn't rigorously demonstrate that the coupling information obtained from additional subsets surpasses what could be achieved with a longer recording from a single subset.

A key requirement for successful application to real data is the overlap between recorded populations. The paper observes that the model only performs well with overlapping populations, but offers no clear explanation for *why* overlap is necessary. A theoretical justification for this requirement would strengthen the paper considerably. 

The interpretation of the learned noise correlations is also ambiguous. While the authors successfully predict these correlations, the possibility that they reflect common input or latent variables rather than direct pairwise couplings is not adequately addressed. The fact that the learned couplings poorly match true couplings in Fig. 3b raises concerns about the biological interpretation of the model's parameters.

From a technical perspective, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the estimation procedure, particularly the M-step of the EM algorithm. This omission hinders reproducibility and undermines the perceived novelty of the method. A more complete derivation of the update equations would be highly beneficial.

Finally, the paper suffers from a presentation imbalance. The introduction and discussion sections are lengthy and provide ample context, but the technical exposition is comparatively sparse. While the core idea is presented clearly, the mathematical details are insufficient, making it difficult to fully grasp the method’s intricacies. More detailed derivations and a more rigorous presentation of the statistical framework are needed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Novel and potentially impactful idea for leveraging partial recordings to infer large-scale neural dynamics.
*   Successful demonstration of the method on both simulated and real data.
*   Addresses a significant challenge in current neuroimaging techniques.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited robustness analysis and unclear failure modes.
*   Unclear incremental benefit of sequential recordings.
*   Lack of explanation for the overlap requirement.
*   Ambiguous interpretation of learned noise correlations.
*   Insufficient technical details and a presentation imbalance.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a promising approach to a challenging problem in computational neuroscience. However, the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of robustness analysis, unclear incremental benefit, and insufficient technical detail, prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance at this time. I believe the paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field, but requires substantial revision to address these concerns. I would recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit. A score of 6/10 seems appropriate.
