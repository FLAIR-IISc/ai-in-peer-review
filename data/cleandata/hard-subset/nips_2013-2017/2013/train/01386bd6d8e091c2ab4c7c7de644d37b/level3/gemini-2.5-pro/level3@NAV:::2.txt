This paper introduces a novel and powerful statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population by “stitching” together multiple, sequential, partial recordings. The core idea is to frame the problem as fitting a latent linear dynamical system where each recording session provides observations of a different subset of neurons. This approach allows the model to estimate properties, such as noise correlations, between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The work addresses a critical experimental limitation in neuroscience. While there are theoretical precedents for identifying systems from partial observations, and related work on partially observed spiking populations (e.g., Pillow & Latham, 2008), this paper’s direct application to stitching experimental recordings is highly original.

**Strengths:**
*   **Originality and Significance:** The central idea of computationally expanding the effective field of view is a significant contribution. It offers a practical way to model neural circuits at a scale beyond the limits of current recording technology. The method is broadly applicable and could be extended to other types of point-process data.
*   **Quality:** The approach is validated on both simulated and real-world calcium imaging data, with a well-designed evaluation that benchmarks performance against clear upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Clarity and Technical Detail:** The paper’s primary weakness is its lack of technical depth. While the introduction and discussion are extensive, the methods section is light on estimation and derivation details, notably for the M-step of the EM algorithm. This lack of clarity undermines reproducibility and obscures the precise technical novelty.
*   **Robustness and Assumptions:** The method relies on a linear-Gaussian model, but the paper lacks a discussion of its robustness. There is little intuition provided about potential failure modes when these assumptions are violated by complex, non-linear neural dynamics.
*   **Conceptual Clarity:** The paper could be strengthened by clarifying key conceptual points. For instance, if a full model can be recovered from one subset (up to a latent transformation), the incremental benefit of adding more subsets versus simply recording more data from a single subset is unclear. Furthermore, the empirical requirement of overlapping populations for real data is mentioned but not explained, leaving the reader to wonder why overlap is critical for constraining the model.
*   **Interpretation of Results:** The learned couplings in the real-data application may reflect common latent inputs rather than direct pairwise interactions. This ambiguity is highlighted by the relatively poor match between learned and true couplings shown in Figure 3b, a point that warrants more careful discussion.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and important idea. However, its potential is currently undermined by an unbalanced presentation that prioritizes concept over technical execution. A thorough revision with detailed derivations and a more critical discussion of the model's assumptions and limitations would be necessary to make this a strong contribution.
