## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a novel method for inferring the dynamics of large neural populations by “stitching” together sequentially recorded subnetworks using a latent dynamical system framework. The core idea – leveraging overlapping recordings to extend population size beyond simultaneous imaging limits – is compelling and addresses a significant challenge in modern neuroscience. The approach extends to point-process data and has broad applicability, particularly for circuits exceeding current imaging capabilities, like cortical barrels. This work builds upon, but significantly extends, previous efforts like Pillow & Latham (2008) on partially observed spiking populations, moving beyond restrictive assumptions about stimulus correlations.

The strengths of this paper lie in its innovative approach and potential impact. The demonstration on both simulated and real (mouse somatosensory cortex) data is encouraging, showing the method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The ability to recover coupling matrices and predict activity in unobserved neurons, even with limited overlap, is a significant achievement. The comparison to a naive approach clearly highlights the benefits of the stitching method.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. A major concern is the lack of robustness analysis. The method appears to perform well under specific conditions (near linear-Gaussian data), but the paper lacks intuition regarding failure modes when these assumptions are violated. The real-data results are also ambiguous; the learned couplings don’t strongly match true couplings (Fig. 3b), and the explained noise correlations could be driven by shared input rather than direct couplings. The overlap requirement is also not adequately explained – why does overlap better constrain the model, and what are the limitations of minimal overlap? 

Furthermore, the incremental benefit of multiple subsets is unclear. If the full model can be recovered from a single subset (up to a latent transform), the value added by additional subsets isn't convincingly demonstrated. The paper also lacks sufficient technical detail, particularly regarding the M-step of the EM algorithm, hindering reproducibility and assessment of novelty. The presentation is imbalanced, with a disproportionate amount of space devoted to introduction and discussion compared to the technical exposition. 

**Arguments for acceptance:** Novel and potentially impactful method addressing a key limitation in neural population analysis. Successful demonstration on both simulated and real data.

**Arguments against acceptance:** Lack of robustness analysis, ambiguous real-data results, unclear incremental benefit of multiple subsets, insufficient technical detail, and imbalanced presentation.



**Overall:** While the idea is promising, the paper requires significant revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. I recommend **rejection with request for revision**. Addressing the robustness concerns, providing more detailed derivations, and clarifying the benefits of multiple subsets are crucial for elevating this work to a publishable standard.
