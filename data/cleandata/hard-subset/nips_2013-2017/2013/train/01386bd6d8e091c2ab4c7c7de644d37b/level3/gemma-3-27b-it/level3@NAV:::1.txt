## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses the fundamental limitation in circuit neuroscience – the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons comprising a circuit. The authors propose a statistical method, framed as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, to “stitch” together sequentially imaged neuron sets, effectively expanding analyzable population sizes and enabling the inference of correlations beyond the scope of simultaneous imaging. This is a compelling problem, and the approach – leveraging a linear Gaussian model and Expectation-Maximization (EM) – is a reasonable starting point. 

The work builds upon existing methods for inferring neural connectivity from calcium imaging data (e.g., [6, 7]) and addressing incomplete observations [12, 13, 14, 15], but uniquely focuses on inferring functional connections specifically for *non*-simultaneously imaged neurons. The significance of this contribution lies in its potential to overcome limitations of current imaging technology and enable larger-scale circuit analyses. The validation on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex is a positive aspect. 

A key strength is the demonstrated ability to effectively recover pairwise correlations between neurons not measured simultaneously, particularly in the simulated data (Fig. 2). However, a significant weakness arises from the discrepancy between results obtained on simulated versus real data. The method works well when simulations have non-overlapping populations, but requires substantial overlap in real data to yield reasonable results. The authors acknowledge this difference (Fig. 3), but importantly, do *not* offer a clear explanation for it. This unexplained difference significantly weakens the claims made about the applicability of the method to real-world datasets.

While the paper is generally well-written, clarity suffers from insufficiently descriptive figure legends and figure-related text. More elaboration on the specifics of the data processing and parameter choices would be helpful. For example, detailing the justification for the chosen overlap percentage (60%) in the real data analysis would strengthen the presentation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a significant problem in circuit neuroscience.
*   Demonstrated ability to recover correlations between non-simultaneously imaged neurons in simulations.
*   Potential to scale up circuit-level analyses beyond the limits of current technology.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Significant and unexplained discrepancy between simulation and real data results.
*   Lack of clarity in figure descriptions and related text.
*   Reliance on substantial overlap in real data, limiting the practical applicability of the method.



Overall, this paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful approach. However, the unexplained discrepancy between simulation and real data, and the clarity issues, prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection with a request for revision**, specifically addressing the discrepancy and improving the clarity of the presentation. The authors should also explore and discuss the limitations imposed by the required overlap in real data and potential solutions to mitigate this issue.
