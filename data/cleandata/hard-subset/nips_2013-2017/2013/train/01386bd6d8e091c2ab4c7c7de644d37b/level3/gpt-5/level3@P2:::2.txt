Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a method to “stitch” together sequential recordings of partially overlapping neural populations into a single latent dynamical system (LDS), enabling inference of joint population dynamics and, critically, noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The model is a linear-Gaussian state-space model with stimulus drive; missing observations across sessions are handled by session-specific selection matrices. Parameters are learned via EM/Kalman smoothing, with an online-EM variant for efficiency. The method is evaluated on simulations and on 2-photon calcium imaging from mouse S1 (awake and anesthetized), using a “fictional sessions” scheme to benchmark against a fully observed upper bound and a naive lower bound.

The framing relates to classical system identification and observability (e.g., subspace methods and identifiability of linear systems up to a similarity transform). In the NIPS literature, the closest is Pillow & Latham (latent GLMs for partially observed spiking populations) and Wohrer et al. (predicting correlations from partial data under restrictive assumptions). The contribution here is a practically scalable LDS formulation tailored to stitching sequential calcium-imaging cohorts, with an emphasis on predicting cross-session noise correlations—an application not addressed by prior connectivity-inference papers [6–11, 14–15].

Strengths
- Timely, broadly applicable idea: leveraging overlapping sequential recordings to infer large-network dynamics beyond the simultaneously imaged field of view.
- Clear practical value: accurate prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs on real data; substantial improvement over a naive baseline and close to a fully observed upper bound for correlations.
- Simple, computationally tractable approach: LDS with EM/Kalman smoothing; readily extensible to point-process models and to adding latent common-input dimensions.
- Careful benchmarking setup with upper/lower bounds on synthetic and real data; exploration across population sizes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Scope/robustness: Reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions with fluorescence states; limited discussion of failure modes under nonlinearity, non-Gaussianity, or model mismatch (e.g., calcium dynamics, nonstationarity).
- Incremental benefit and identifiability: The paper asserts that a second session resolves the similarity-transform ambiguity, but the conditions under which overlapping neurons are necessary and sufficient are not formalized. With zero overlap, performance degrades, yet the paper does not provide intuition or theory explaining when and why overlap is required.
- Interpretation: The model conflates direct couplings and shared low-dimensional inputs. Real-data results show modest correspondence between stitched and fully observed couplings for off-diagonal blocks, suggesting that successful correlation prediction may be driven by latent common inputs rather than accurate inter-population couplings. This should be clarified and tested.
- Estimation details: Key M-step derivations are only sketched; there is little on initialization, stability constraints on A, regularization (e.g., sparsity), model selection (latent dimensions, noise covariance structure), or computational complexity.
- Presentation balance: Introduction and discussion are strong, but core technical exposition is thin given the centrality of identifiability and estimation.

Quality
- Technically sound within the LDS framework; EM/Kalman approach is appropriate. However, claims about identifiability via stitching (and the necessity/benefit of overlap) need theoretical backing or systematic empirical analysis. The paper would benefit from regularization, model selection, and stability constraints, which are briefly mentioned but not reported.
- Experiments convincingly show correlation prediction gains; however, the discrepancy in inferred couplings (Fig. 3b) and the overlap requirement deserve deeper analysis.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but important derivations and algorithmic details are missing. Please include: full M-step updates, treatment to ensure stability of A, initialization strategy, online-EM schedule, runtime and memory scaling, and precise procedures for computing “noise correlations” (e.g., how stimulus effects are subtracted).
- Provide a conceptual explanation (and, if possible, a proposition) for why overlap anchors the similarity transform and how much overlap is needed as a function of noise and dynamics.

Originality
- The “stitching” application to calcium imaging is novel and useful. The LDS with missing observations and EM is not new per se; the contribution is the problem framing and the demonstration that cross-session noise correlations can be predicted.

Significance
- High practical relevance for modern imaging where FOV limits prevent full simultaneous coverage. If made more robust and better justified theoretically, this approach could influence experimental design (e.g., planned overlap) and broaden analyses to circuit scales.
- At present, the main demonstrated advance is accurate correlation prediction; claims about reconstructing couplings should be tempered or strengthened with additional evidence.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide identifiability analysis: conditions under which two (or more) partial recordings with overlap uniquely determine the global model parameters (up to trivial symmetries). Analyze how overlap size, stimulus richness, and signal-to-noise affect this.
- Systematically vary overlap in both synthetic and real data; test zero-overlap with additional constraints (e.g., shared stimulus regressors, anchor neurons, or low-rank latent inputs).
- Include regularization (e.g., sparsity on A, low-rank Q) and model selection; report sensitivity to these choices.
- Test robustness to model mismatch: nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, calcium-convolution effects, or spiking point-process outputs; compare to a deconvolved-spike LDS or latent GLM where feasible.
- Clarify the source of correlation-prediction accuracy: add models with explicit low-rank common input vs. dense A, and report which component explains cross-session correlations.
- Expand estimation details and reproducibility information (hyperparameters, initialization, convergence criteria, code availability).

Pros for acceptance
- Practical and impactful idea addressing a widespread experimental constraint.
- Strong empirical gains in predicting cross-session noise correlations.
- Simple, scalable method that others can adopt and extend.

Cons for acceptance
- Theoretical underpinnings of stitching/overlap insufficiently developed; identifiability unclear.
- Limited robustness analysis; unclear performance under realistic nonlinearities and nonstationarity.
- Weak correspondence for inferred couplings; risk of over-interpreting functional connectivity.
- Insufficient algorithmic details for full reproducibility.

Recommendation
Promising contribution with clear potential impact, but currently limited by missing theoretical justification and insufficient technical and robustness details. I lean toward a borderline reject/weak reject, with encouragement to revise: strengthen identifiability analysis, expand estimation details, and provide robustness tests that clarify when stitching works and what aspects of the dynamics are truly recovered.
