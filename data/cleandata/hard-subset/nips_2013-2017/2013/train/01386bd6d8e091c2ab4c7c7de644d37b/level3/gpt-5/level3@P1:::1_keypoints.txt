- Introduces a practical method for “stitching” sequentially imaged neural subsets into a single dynamical model to infer population interactions and noise correlations beyond simultaneous imaging limits.  
- Models data as a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations across sessions: x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t, y_t = C (x_t + d) + ε_t, with diagonal Q and R and session-specific C.  
- Learns parameters via EM (including an online variant) and performs inference with Kalman smoothing using session-specific observation matrices.  
- Synthetic experiments show recovery of cross-session couplings (up to linear transforms) and accurate prediction of noise correlations and unobserved activity.  
- Real-data (mouse S1) evaluation uses a fictional two-session split and finds stitching predicts correlations between non-simultaneously imaged neurons nearly as well as a fully observed upper bound and much better than per-session fits.  
- Differs from prior connectivity and GLM-based approaches by directly addressing cross-session pairs never observed together, leveraging missing-data LDS structure.  
- Strength: modeling and inference are technically sound; EM and session-specific C are effective; synthetic results validate identifiability intuition and recovery for non-overlapping subsets.  
- Strength: real-data results are encouraging and cross-prediction analyses are informative.  
- Weakness: assumes stationarity (same A, B, Q across sessions); fictional split from one recording may overstate performance versus truly separate sessions.  
- Weakness: method fails without overlap on real data despite success in simulation; paper does not quantify performance vs degree of overlap or explain this discrepancy.  
- Weakness: assumes diagonal measurement/process noise (Q, R), which is unrealistic for calcium imaging due to neuropil/motion and common inputs; impact not analyzed.  
- Weakness: large parameterization (full N×N A) with minimal regularization; risk of overfitting and missing stability constraints (e.g., spectral radius) not fully addressed.  
- Weakness: primary evaluation compares to a “fully observed” LDS upper bound rather than empirical pairwise noise correlations from held-out simultaneous data.  
- Weakness: insufficient detail in figures/text for reproducibility (parameter counts, runtime, initialization, convergence criteria, sensitivity analyses).  
- Clarity: overall writing and motivation are clear, but figure legends need expanded details (axes, units, N, overlap c, trials, windows).  
- Clarity: request inclusion of pseudocode/algorithm box for stitching EM with mini-batches and specification of initialization, stability enforcement, and stopping rules.  
- Clarity: ask to clarify stimulus regressor definitions, how noise correlations were computed (e.g., partialing out B u), and exact withheld test-data protocol.  
- Originality: stitching formulation for inferring cross-session correlated dynamics and noise correlations for never-simultaneously-imaged pairs appears novel.  
- Significance: timely and potentially impactful for scaling population analyses if robust to session variability; can inform experimental design trade-offs.  
- Suggestion: quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction (including c=0%, 20%, 40%, 60%) on real data and report model-to-model and model-to-empirical recovery.  
- Suggestion: evaluate robustness to session nonstationarities by creating pseudo-sessions with baseline/gain/context differences and consider session-specific offsets/gains/drift terms.  
- Suggestion: compare to baselines that align sessions via shared low-dimensional latent trajectories with session-specific loadings (e.g., factor LDS/PLDS).  
- Suggestion: add and report effects of regularization (L1 sparsity, stability constraints, distance-dependent priors) and discuss sample complexity vs N.  
- Suggestion: relax diagonal R and/or include unobserved latent dimensions to capture common inputs, and report improvements.  
- Suggestion: use empirical ground truth correlations from simultaneously recorded held-out test sets rather than only model-model comparisons.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important practical problem with a simple, general framework; strong synthetic and encouraging real-data results; clear path for extensions.  
- Cons for acceptance: real-data relies on fictional splits without true separate-session demonstration; overlap dependence not quantified; modeling assumptions and limited ablations may limit validity.  
- Overall: promising and original contribution that needs stronger empirical validation on real separate sessions, more thorough baselines/ablations, and clearer presentation to be a strong paper.
