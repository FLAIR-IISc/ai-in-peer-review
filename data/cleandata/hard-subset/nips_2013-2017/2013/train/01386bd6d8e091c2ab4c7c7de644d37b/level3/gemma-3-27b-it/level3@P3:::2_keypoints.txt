- Introduces a novel method to infer neural population dynamics by stitching sequentially recorded subnetworks into a unified latent dynamical system with missing observations.  
- Elegant framing addresses inability to record entire neural circuits simultaneously and extends to point-process data.  
- Demonstrates promising results on simulated and real calcium imaging from mouse somatosensory cortex, predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.  
- Builds on prior work inferring dynamics from partial observations but specifically targets combining sequential recordings, differing from studies on simultaneous subsets or subsampled connectivity.  
- Claims improved scalability and applicability to larger populations compared to closest related work (Pillow & Latham).  

- Main concern: robustness — method is evaluated on a simple linear-Gaussian model, raising questions about performance on nonlinear neural dynamics.  
- Paper lacks exploration of failure modes and conditions where the linear-Gaussian assumption breaks down; needs simulations with varying nonlinearity.  
- Unclear incremental benefit of sequential recordings: unclear how much extra coupling information multiple subsets provide beyond longer recordings of a single subset.  
- Overlap requirement: model only performs well with overlapping populations, but no theoretical justification is provided for why overlap is necessary.  
- Ambiguous interpretation of learned noise correlations — could reflect common input/latent variables rather than direct pairwise couplings; learned couplings poorly match true couplings in Fig. 3b.  
- Technical omission: insufficient detail on estimation procedure, particularly the M-step of the EM algorithm, hindering reproducibility.  
- Presentation imbalance: long introduction/discussion but sparse mathematical/technical exposition and derivations, making the method hard to fully grasp.  

- Arguments for acceptance: novel and potentially impactful idea; successful demonstrations on simulated and real data; addresses a key limitation in neuroimaging.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited robustness analysis, unclear incremental benefit of sequential recordings, lack of overlap explanation, ambiguous noise-correlation interpretation, and insufficient technical detail.  
- Overall assessment: promising approach but requires substantial revision to address robustness, theoretical justification, and technical clarity; recommend rejection with encouragement to revise and resubmit (score 6/10).
