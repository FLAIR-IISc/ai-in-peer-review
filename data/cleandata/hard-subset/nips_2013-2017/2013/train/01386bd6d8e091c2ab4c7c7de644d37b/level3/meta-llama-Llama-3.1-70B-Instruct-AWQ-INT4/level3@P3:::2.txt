This paper introduces a novel statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model by phrasing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method in predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs using both simulated and real data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper is well-motivated and relates to previous work in the field, particularly in the context of inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their method, including the underlying assumptions and limitations. The use of simulated data to evaluate the performance of the method is also well-justified.

The results presented in the paper are promising, particularly in terms of the method's ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. The comparison to a "naive" approach, which separately fits dynamical system models to each of the two imaging sessions, provides a useful baseline for evaluating the performance of the stitching method.

However, there are some limitations and unclear aspects of the paper that need to be addressed. Firstly, the robustness of the method to violations of its assumptions, particularly the linearity and Gaussianity of the data, is unclear. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide any intuition on how the method might fail when these assumptions are violated.

Secondly, the incremental benefit of the stitching method over simply using more data from one subset is unclear. If the full model can be recovered from one subset (up to latent transform), it is not clear how much extra coupling information other subsets provide.

Thirdly, the requirement for overlapping populations in the real-data model is not well-explained. The authors do not provide any intuition on why overlap is necessary to constrain the model, and it is unclear how the method would perform without overlap.

Fourthly, the estimation details, particularly the M-step, are not well-explained. The authors provide some details on the updates for the model parameters, but the derivation of these updates is not provided.

Finally, the presentation of the paper is somewhat imbalanced, with a lot of space devoted to the introduction and discussion, but not enough to the technical exposition. The idea is promising, but it needs clearer explanation and derivations to be fully appreciated.
