This paper addresses the important challenge of inferring the dynamics of large neural populations, exceeding the limitations of current simultaneous recording techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging. The authors propose a statistical method to “stitch” together data acquired from sequentially imaged subsets of neurons, effectively creating a larger, unified model of the circuit. This is achieved by formulating the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically employing a linear Gaussian model. The method is demonstrated on both simulated data and experimental recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex, with the primary claim being the ability to predict noise correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously.

The work builds upon a substantial body of research attempting to infer functional connectivity from neural data. Previous studies have focused on detailed biophysical modeling of calcium dynamics [6], information-theoretic approaches [7], or predicting connections within *simultaneously* recorded populations [8-11].  This paper differentiates itself by explicitly tackling the scenario of non-simultaneous recordings. While some prior work addresses subsampled observations [12-15], these methods either rely on strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic stimulus-correlation relationship [12]) or are not scalable to the population sizes considered here [13, 14].  The authors rightly position their contribution as filling a gap in the existing literature.

The strengths of this paper are considerable.  The problem is well-motivated and biologically relevant, especially given the growing interest in understanding circuit-level computations in the brain. The mathematical formulation, while relatively simple (linear Gaussian dynamical system), is clearly presented and justified for tractability. Importantly, the results demonstrate a compelling ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, both in the simulated data and, to a lesser extent, in the real experimental data. The use of a fully observed model as an upper bound and a naive approach as a lower bound provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating performance. The inclusion of both anesthetized and awake mouse data adds further weight to the findings.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. While the method performs well on simulated data, the results from the experimental data, while promising, seem less robust and require substantial overlap between the imaged subsets to work effectively. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a more thorough discussion of the factors influencing the required degree of overlap would be beneficial.  A significant unexplained discrepancy exists between the performance in the simulated data (Fig. 2c; correlation of 0.73 for non-simultaneously recorded pairs) and the experimental data (Fig. 3b; correlation of 0.38). The authors don't fully address this difference, which raises questions about the transferability of the method from simulation to real-world scenarios.  

Furthermore, the clarity of the presentation could be improved. Several figure legends are sparse, lacking detailed descriptions of the parameters or metrics being displayed. The discussion of the results often refers to figures without adequately explaining the key takeaways. For example, the details of how the 'fictional imaging scenario' was implemented could be clarified.  While the paper is generally well-written, a more detailed elaboration in the figure-related text would improve readability and understanding. 

In terms of originality, the paper presents a novel approach to a significant problem. While the underlying techniques (latent dynamical systems, EM algorithm) are well-established, their application to this specific context – inferring connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons – is a valuable contribution.  The method extends previous work by enabling the analysis of larger circuits than previously possible.

The significance of this work is potentially high. Overcoming the limitations of simultaneous imaging is crucial for advancing our understanding of neural circuit function. If validated and refined, this method could become a valuable tool for neuroscientists studying complex neural systems. The ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons opens up exciting possibilities for building more comprehensive and accurate models of brain activity.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a significant problem in computational neuroscience.
*   Promising results demonstrating the ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
*   Clear mathematical formulation and justification of the model.
*   Validation on both simulated and experimental data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Performance on real data is less robust and requires substantial overlap between imaged subsets.
*   Unexplained discrepancy between simulation and experimental results.
*   Clarity of presentation could be improved with more detailed figure legends and explanations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the weaknesses mentioned above warrant consideration, the novelty, potential significance, and overall quality of the work outweigh these concerns.  I recommend acceptance, with a request for the authors to address the clarity issues and provide a more thorough discussion of the discrepancy between simulation and experimental results, and the conditions under which the method performs best.
