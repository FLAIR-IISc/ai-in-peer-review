

This paper proposes a novel statistical method to "stitch" sequentially imaged neuron sets into a unified model, addressing a fundamental limitation in neural circuit analysis where simultaneous imaging of large populations is experimentally constrained. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations using a linear Gaussian model, which they validate on simulated data and experimental calcium imaging in mouse somatosensory cortex. The key contribution lies in recovering correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons, a capability absent in prior work that typically focuses on simultaneously recorded populations [6,7,8,9,10,11].

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-structured methodology using a variant of EM for inference. The model effectively recovers pairwise correlations between neuron pairs not measured simultaneously in simulated data (Figure 2c), showing 0.92 correlation between predicted and true noise correlations. The significance is high, as this approach could substantially expand population sizes for circuit-level analyses beyond current imaging limitations.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses. The most critical issue is the unexplained discrepancy between simulated (Figure 2c) and real data (Figure 3b) results, where the model performs substantially better on simulated data than on real experimental data. While the authors note that real data requires substantial overlap for reasonable results, they fail to address why this discrepancy exists or how it might be resolved. Additionally, the paper lacks sufficient detail in figure legends and related text, making it difficult to fully assess the experimental validation.

The method shows promising originality in addressing the specific challenge of non-simultaneous imaging, differentiating it from related work on sub-sampled observations [12,13,14,15]. The significance is compelling, particularly for neuroscience applications where large-scale population dynamics are crucial.

Pro: Novel approach to a significant problem; strong theoretical foundation; promising experimental results on simulated data; clear significance for neuroscience.

Con: Unexplained performance gap between simulated and real data; insufficient detail in figures; model requires substantial overlap in real data that isn't well-justified.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution to overcoming experimental constraints in neural circuit analysis, but requires clarification of the real data discrepancy and improved presentation to fully realize its potential. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing these concerns.
