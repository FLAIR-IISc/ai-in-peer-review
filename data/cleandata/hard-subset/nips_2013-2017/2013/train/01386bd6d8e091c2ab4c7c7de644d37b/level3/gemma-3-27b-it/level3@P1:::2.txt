This paper introduces a method for inferring the dynamics of large neural populations by “stitching” together data from sequentially recorded, overlapping subnetworks. The core idea is to formulate the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, allowing inference of connections and correlations beyond the scope of simultaneous recordings. This is a potentially powerful approach, particularly given the limitations of current 2-photon imaging technology in capturing the activity of entire neural circuits (e.g., cortical barrels). The work relates to prior efforts in inferring functional connectivity from neural data [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11], but distinguishes itself by explicitly addressing the problem of combining non-simultaneously observed neurons. It also differs from approaches requiring strong assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations [12] or being limited in scalability [13, 14, 15].

The strengths of the paper are considerable. The problem addressed is important and well-motivated, and the proposed solution is both elegant and potentially impactful. The demonstration on both simulated and real (mouse somatosensory cortex) data is encouraging, showing the ability to predict noise correlations between neurons never simultaneously recorded. The authors acknowledge limitations (linear Gaussian assumption) and demonstrate a clear awareness of related work. The results on simulated data are particularly compelling, showing recovery of ground truth coupling matrices and correlations. Applying the method to real calcium imaging data and demonstrating improvement over a naive approach further strengthens the contribution.

However, there are several weaknesses. The method’s robustness is a concern. The authors primarily demonstrate performance under relatively linear-Gaussian conditions. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of potential failure modes when these assumptions are violated – how does the performance degrade with increased non-linearity or non-Gaussianity in the underlying neural activity?  While the theoretical basis suggests a method for extending beyond these assumptions, this isn’t fully explored or demonstrated. Relatedly, the incremental benefit of adding more subnetworks is not fully clear. If the full model can be recovered (up to a latent transformation) from a single, sufficiently large subset of neurons, the added value of sequential recordings beyond simply increasing the total data volume is not explicitly addressed. 

A crucial aspect needing further explanation is the requirement for overlapping populations. The authors state that overlap is critical for performance, yet the reason for this is not fully elucidated. Why does overlap better constrain the model than, for example, strategically sampling non-overlapping regions believed to be functionally connected? A more detailed theoretical or empirical analysis of the role of overlap would be highly valuable.

Furthermore, the interpretation of the inferred correlations is somewhat ambiguous. The authors acknowledge the possibility that the observed correlations might reflect common input or latent variables, rather than direct pairwise couplings – this is particularly relevant given the relatively poor match between inferred and true couplings in Figure 3b. Disentangling these possibilities would require further investigation and potentially the incorporation of additional modeling assumptions.

Finally, the technical exposition lacks detail in several areas. The M-step of the EM algorithm, a key component of the method, is presented rather briefly, hindering a complete understanding of the implementation and potential for modifications. This deficiency also impacts the assessment of the method’s novelty; greater clarity in the derivations would strengthen the claim of a significant contribution. The presentation is also somewhat imbalanced, with a disproportionate amount of space devoted to introduction and discussion relative to the technical details.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and potentially impactful method for leveraging incomplete neural recordings.
*   Strong results on both simulated and real data, demonstrating the ability to predict noise correlations.
*   Clear connection to existing literature and careful consideration of related work.
*   Addresses an important practical challenge in analyzing large-scale neural data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of robustness to violations of the linear-Gaussian assumption.
*   Unclear justification for the overlap requirement between recorded populations.
*   Ambiguous interpretation of inferred correlations.
*   Insufficient technical detail in key aspects of the method, hindering reproducibility and assessment of novelty.
*   Incremental benefit of sequential recordings is not clearly established.



**Overall assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a potentially significant contribution to the field. However, the weaknesses related to robustness, explanation of key design choices (overlap), and technical detail prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejecting** the paper, but encouraging the authors to address the identified weaknesses through more detailed analysis, explanation, and possibly additional experimentation. A revised version addressing these points would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.
