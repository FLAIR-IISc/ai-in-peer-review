

# Review for "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a novel statistical approach to overcome a fundamental limitation in neural circuit analysis: the inability to simultaneously record from large populations of neurons. The authors propose a "stitching" method that combines sequentially imaged subsets of neurons into a single latent dynamical system model, enabling the prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. The core idea builds on theoretical results about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21, 22, 23], but extends them to the neuroscience context by framing the problem as latent dynamical system inference with missing observations. This addresses a gap in the literature: while previous work has focused on inferring connectivity from simultaneous recordings [6-11], none have tackled the problem of combining non-simultaneously recorded data.

The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that their linear Gaussian dynamical system model can effectively "stitch" together partial neural recordings. The authors validate their approach on both simulated data (where they can measure performance against ground truth) and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. Their results show that the stitching method substantially outperforms a naive baseline that simply averages parameters from separate sessions, particularly in predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

**Quality:** The method is technically sound and well-motivated. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and derive appropriate parameter updates using a variant of the EM algorithm. The experimental validation is comprehensive, with careful comparison to appropriate baselines (fully observed and naive models). The paper adequately addresses limitations, noting the requirement for substantial overlap between imaging sessions (60% in real data) and the linear model's potential limitations. However, the paper does not adequately explain the significant performance gap between simulated data (0.73 correlation for non-simultaneously recorded couplings in Figure 2c) and real data (0.38 in Figure 3b), which weakens the argument for the method's general applicability.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The method is clearly described, and the flow from problem statement to solution to validation is coherent. However, the figure legends and related text lack sufficient detail to fully interpret some key results. For instance, Figure 3b's correlation of 0.38 between stitched and fully observed couplings for non-simultaneously recorded pairs is not sufficiently contextualized, and the paper does not explain why this is substantially lower than the simulated data performance.

**Originality:** The paper's core contribution—using latent dynamical systems to stitch together non-simultaneously recorded neural populations—is novel in the context of neuroscience data analysis. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, noting that methods like [12] require strong assumptions about noise correlations, [13] does not scale to large populations, and [14] addresses a different problem where observations can be interpolated across time.

**Significance:** This work addresses a fundamental bottleneck in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations. If the method proves robust across diverse neural circuits, it could substantially expand the scale of neural circuit analysis, moving beyond the current limitation of hundreds to thousands of neurons. The authors demonstrate promising results with real data from mouse somatosensory cortex, suggesting the approach could be applied to other neural systems where simultaneous recording is limited.

**Strengths:** 
- Novel application of latent dynamical systems to a key neuroscience problem
- Strong validation on both simulated and real data
- Clear demonstration of the method's ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons
- Well-justified comparison to appropriate baselines
- Potential to significantly impact neural circuit analysis

**Weaknesses:**
- Insufficient explanation for the performance gap between simulated and real data
- Requires substantial overlap (60%) between imaging sessions, which may limit applicability
- Linear Gaussian model may be too simplistic for complex neural dynamics
- Figure legends and text lack sufficient detail for full interpretation of results

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**

*Pro:* This paper presents a novel, well-motivated solution to a fundamental problem in neuroscience. The method is technically sound, validated on real data, and has the potential to significantly expand the scale of neural circuit analysis. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation and demonstrate practical utility.

*Con:* The significant performance gap between simulated and real data is not adequately addressed, raising questions about the method's generalizability. The requirement for substantial overlap between imaging sessions may limit its applicability to many experimental paradigms. The linear model may be too simplistic for capturing complex neural dynamics.

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental limitation in neural circuit analysis. While there are some weaknesses to address (particularly regarding the performance gap between simulations and real data), the core idea is novel, the method is sound, and the potential impact is significant. The authors should address the performance discrepancy in their revision and provide more detailed explanations of their real-world results. This work has the potential to become a valuable tool for neuroscientists seeking to analyze large neural circuits with current imaging technology.
