### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, partially overlapping subpopulations. The core idea is to frame this "stitching" problem as one of fitting a single latent dynamical system (an LDS, in this case) with missing observations, where each recording session corresponds to a different observation mask on the full state space. The authors demonstrate this method on both simulated data and 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main result is that this approach can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, a feat not possible with standard analysis techniques.

The work is situated within the broader field of inferring functional connectivity from neural data. While previous work has tackled inference from partial observations (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008), this paper's contribution is unique in its explicit goal of combining multiple *different* partial recordings to reconstruct a single, larger population model. This directly addresses a fundamental practical limitation of modern neural recording technologies, which have a limited field-of-view.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The technical approach is sound in principle, and the application of an LDS with different observation matrices (Ck) for each session is an elegant formulation. The experimental results, particularly the prediction of cross-session noise correlations on real data (Fig 3c, 3d), provide a compelling proof-of-concept. However, the paper is somewhat incomplete as a technical work.

*   The interpretation of the learned coupling matrix A is a key weakness. While the model successfully predicts noise correlations, the learned couplings for non-simultaneously recorded pairs have only a modest correlation (0.38 on the example in Fig 3b) with those from a fully observed model. This suggests the model may be capturing the correct second-order statistics through latent dynamics or effective couplings that do not correspond to direct pairwise interactions, but rather to shared input from unobserved sources. The paper should discuss this ambiguity more explicitly.
*   The robustness of the method is not thoroughly explored. The demonstration relies on a linear-Gaussian model, which is a strong simplification of real neural dynamics. There is no discussion of the method's potential failure modes when these assumptions are violated, which is critical for assessing its practical utility.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the high-level motivation is very clear. The figures effectively illustrate the core concepts and results. However, the manuscript suffers from a lack of technical detail in the methods section.

*   The description of the EM algorithm is too brief. Key details, such as the full M-step update equations for A, B, and Q, are omitted and simply referred to as "standard". While true for a fully observed LDS, the extension to this multiple-observation-mask scenario warrants a more explicit derivation to ensure clarity and reproducibility.
*   The paper states that for real data, "some overlap was critical for stitching to work." This is a crucial practical finding, but the authors provide no intuition or theoretical explanation for why this is the case. Clarifying how overlap helps constrain the parameter space would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   The presentation feels imbalanced, with a large portion devoted to introduction and discussion but an underdeveloped methods section.

**Originality:** The core idea is highly original. To my knowledge, this is the first attempt to computationally merge sequential recordings of different neural subpopulations into a single coherent dynamical model. This directly tackles a pressing and practical problem in systems neuroscience, offering a way to expand the effective scale of neural recordings beyond current technological limits. The approach is conceptually novel and significant.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is very high. If the method proves robust and generalizable, it could become a standard tool for analyzing large-scale neural recordings, enabling researchers to build models of neural circuits (e.g., a full cortical column) that are currently too large to record from simultaneously. The ability to estimate interactions between non-simultaneously recorded neurons opens up new avenues for testing theories of population coding and circuit function. The idea is also general enough that it could be extended to more complex models (e.g., point-process models for spiking data), increasing its potential reach.

### Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a highly original and powerful idea that addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience. The potential impact is substantial.
2.  **Elegant Formulation:** The problem is elegantly framed as inference in a latent variable model, which is a natural and powerful approach.
3.  **Strong Proof-of-Concept:** The results, especially on real calcium imaging data, convincingly demonstrate that the core idea works and can yield predictions (i.e., noise correlations between unseen pairs) that are otherwise unattainable.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Lack of Technical Depth:** The methods section is underdeveloped, omitting key derivations and details which hinders reproducibility and full technical evaluation.
2.  **Unclear Robustness and Limitations:** The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the linear-Gaussian assumption or provide intuition about how the model might fail. The unexplained requirement for population overlap on real data is also a significant loose end.
3.  **Ambiguity of Interpretation:** There is a potential disconnect between the model's ability to predict correlations and its ability to identify the true underlying coupling parameters. This ambiguity is not sufficiently addressed.
4.  **Unclear Incremental Benefit:** The paper's intuition states that the full dynamics can be recovered from one subset up to a linear transformation. It is not made sufficiently clear what specific information the second recording provides that could not be obtained by simply collecting more data from the first subset of neurons.

### Recommendation

This paper introduces a fantastic and important idea. The potential significance for neuroscience and the originality of the approach are clear strengths. However, the execution in the manuscript is lacking in technical depth and a thorough discussion of the method's limitations and assumptions. It currently reads more like a proof-of-concept than a complete piece of work.

Given the high potential of the core contribution, I lean toward acceptance but with strong encouragement for revision. The authors should significantly expand the methods section to include full derivations, provide a more detailed discussion on the role of overlapping neurons, and address the ambiguity in the interpretation of the learned parameters. Clarifying these points would turn a promising paper into an excellent one.
