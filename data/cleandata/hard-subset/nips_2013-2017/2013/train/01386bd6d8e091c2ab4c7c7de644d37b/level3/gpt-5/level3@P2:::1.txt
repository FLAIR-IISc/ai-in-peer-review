Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a principled approach to “stitch” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit into a single model, enabling inference of population dynamics and noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never observed simultaneously. The authors cast the problem as learning a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations, where each session observes a different subset of neurons via a known selection matrix. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are estimated via EM (with an online EM variant for efficiency), and inference is performed via Kalman smoothing. The method is validated on simulations and on two two-photon calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1), using a “fictional” split to benchmark against a fully observed upper bound and a naïve per-session baseline.

This work builds on a rich literature on latent dynamical/state-space models for neural populations and system identification (e.g., [8, 13, 17, 18, 21]) and on spike/calcium modeling [6, 16]. Distinct from prior work on connectivity or decoding from subsampled data [12–15], it addresses the specific and practically important problem of combining sequentially imaged, partially overlapping populations to infer cross-session noise correlations.

Quality
- Soundness and empirical support: The LDS formulation with missing data is standard and the EM learning/inference procedure is appropriate. The synthetic experiments convincingly show recovery of cross-session couplings and, more robustly, of noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs (r ≈ 0.92). On real data, the method substantially outperforms a naïve baseline in predicting noise correlations for unobserved pairs and approaches the “fully observed” upper bound (e.g., r ≈ 0.84 vs. 0.15 in Fig. 3d). Activity cross-prediction results also support practical utility.
- Assumptions and limitations: The model assumes linear, Gaussian dynamics over continuous-valued calcium traces, diagonal Q (although extensions are discussed), stationary dynamics, and known neuron identity across sessions (known Ck). These are pragmatic but restrictive, especially for awake data with nonstationarity and potentially structured noise. The paper notes that performance on real data required overlap between sessions, unlike in simulation; this important discrepancy is not analyzed in depth.
- Methodological details: EM updates are briefly described; however, initialization, regularization (e.g., stability constraints on A, sparsity), and convergence diagnostics are only sketched. Computational complexity and scaling with N, K, T are not reported.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally well written and accessible. The problem setup and model are clearly stated, and the fictional split is a helpful evaluation device. However:
- Figure legends and related text need more detail (data preprocessing, exact train/test splits, overlap fraction c and its effect, error bars/CI).
- Please specify initialization, step sizes and minibatch schedule for online EM, stopping criteria, and any constraints on A (e.g., spectral radius) to ensure stable dynamics.
- Clarify whether R and Q are constrained to be diagonal in all experiments, and whether allowing low-rank shared noise improves stitching.
- Explicitly report the fraction of explained variance attributed to stimulus vs. latent dynamics on real data.

Originality
The central contribution—stitching sequentially imaged populations by treating unobserved neurons as missing entries in a single LDS and identifying the latent alignment via overlapping observations—is novel and useful. Prior related work on latent GLMs/LDS and subsampled data does not directly solve this cross-session alignment/prediction of noise correlations at the scale targeted here. The paper situates itself appropriately relative to [12–15] and the LDS/GLM literature.

Significance
If robust, this approach addresses a central bottleneck in systems neuroscience: limited simultaneous field-of-view. Accurately predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously imaged neurons would enable richer circuit-level analyses and hypotheses with current imaging technology. The simulated results are strong; on real data, the method provides clear gains over the baseline and approaches the fully observed model. The requirement for overlap and the gap between simulated and real performance temper the impact; nonetheless, the contribution is timely and likely to be used and extended (e.g., to nonlinear dynamics, spike-level models, structured noise).

Strengths
- Clear formulation of stitching as an LDS with missing observations and practical EM-based learning.
- Strong synthetic validation including recovery of cross-session couplings and noise correlations.
- Real-data demonstrations on awake and anesthetized S1 showing improved correlation prediction and activity cross-prediction over a naïve baseline.
- Thorough benchmarking using an upper bound (fully observed) and a lower bound (naïve).
- Practical relevance for 2p imaging studies.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Overlap dependence: Real-data stitching required substantial overlap (c = 60%), while simulations worked with no overlap. Please analyze systematically how performance depends on overlap fraction, session length, SNR, and stimulus drive. Provide curves and guidance for experimental design.
- Model mismatch: Discuss and, if possible, quantify the impact of nonlinearity, nonstationarity, and correlated noise (non-diagonal Q/R). Consider a low-rank shared-noise extension [17, 18] and report performance.
- Baselines: Include multi-view latent models (e.g., probabilistic CCA/FA across sessions with shared latent dynamics or GPFA-like LDS with per-session loadings), and a subspace identification baseline [21].
- Regularization and identifiability: Report use of sparsity or stability regularization on A and its effect (the discussion mentions sparsity helping in simulations). Provide insight into identifiability with partial overlap (theory or ablations), and quantify parameter uncertainty (e.g., bootstrap CIs on couplings).
- Calcium-specific issues: Justify working directly on fluorescence vs. deconvolved spikes [16], and show whether deconvolution changes stitching accuracy.
- Computational aspects: Provide run-time/memory scaling and limits on N, K, T; discuss practicality for thousands of neurons.

Questions for authors
- What is the minimum overlap needed for reliable correlation prediction under realistic noise? How does this vary with SNR and stimulus richness?
- Did you constrain A to be stable (spectral radius < 1)? If so, how enforced in EM?
- How sensitive are results to initialization? Any failure cases or local maxima issues?
- Can you provide results with low-rank Q (common input) and/or non-diagonal R?
- How robust is neuron identity matching across sessions in practice?

Pros for acceptance
- Novel, practical solution to a pressing experimental limitation.
- Strong synthetic results; meaningful gains on real data.
- Simple, scalable modeling framework likely to be adopted and extended.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited analysis of the crucial overlap requirement and simulation–real gap.
- Missing baselines and ablations that would strengthen claims of robustness.
- Incomplete methodological and computational details.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The contribution is original and practically impactful, with solid empirical evidence and clear avenues for strengthening. Addressing overlap dependence, adding baselines/ablations, and clarifying methodology would substantially improve the paper.

Reviewer confidence
Medium.
