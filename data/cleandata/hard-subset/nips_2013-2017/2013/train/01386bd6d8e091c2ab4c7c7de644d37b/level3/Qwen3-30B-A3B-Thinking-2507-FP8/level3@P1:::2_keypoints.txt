- Proposes a statistically principled method to "stitch" sequentially recorded neural subpopulations into a unified latent dynamical model by treating non-simultaneous recordings as missing observations.  
- Extends prior partially observed LDS work to predict noise correlations across non-overlapping neuron pairs.  
- Validated on simulated data and two in vivo calcium imaging datasets, showing improved noise-correlation prediction versus naive baselines.  
- Addresses an important experimental bottleneck: limited simultaneous coverage of 2-photon imaging versus large cortical populations.  
- Real-data validation is compelling (e.g., stitching predicts noise correlations with r=0.84 vs naive r=0.15).  
- Framework is broadly applicable beyond calcium imaging (e.g., spike trains via latent Gaussian models) and generalizes to >2 subpopulations.  
- Synthetic experiments illustrate that latent dynamics can recover cross-couplings between non-simultaneously imaged pairs.  
- Model assumption is overly restrictive: using a linear-Gaussian dynamical system ignores neural nonlinearities (spiking, calcium transients), with no analysis of performance degradation under violations.  
- The necessity and theoretical role of overlap between sessions is unclear and not justified; identifiability explanations are missing.  
- Incremental benefit is questionable: paper doesn't compare stitching to simply collecting longer single-session data or clarify gains beyond latent-transform ambiguities.  
- Stitched coupling estimates correlate poorly with the fully observed model (r=0.38), suggesting predicted correlations may arise from common input rather than true pairwise interactions.  
- Methodological details are insufficient: M-step derivations, online-EM implementation specifics, and hyperparameter selection are omitted, hurting reproducibility.  
- Overall: useful experimental contribution but incremental theoretical novelty; lacks robustness analyses and deeper validation.  
- Recommendation: Weak Reject — promising but needs stronger theoretical grounding, failure-mode analysis, overlap justification, and methodological detail.
