This paper proposes a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model, allowing for the characterization of population dynamics beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian model. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging in mice, demonstrating the recovery of correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons.

The authors' approach is novel in inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons, which sets it apart from prior work in the field. The method's ability to effectively recover pairwise correlations between neuron pairs not measured simultaneously is a significant contribution to the field of neuroscience. The authors demonstrate the robustness of their findings by applying their method to multiple populations of neurons in both anesthetized and awake mice.

The paper is well-written overall, with clear explanations of the model formulation, parameters, and inference procedures. However, the figure legends and figure-related text could benefit from more detailed descriptions and elaboration. For example, the figures depicting the coupling matrices and noise correlations could be accompanied by more explicit explanations of the results.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to address a significant limitation in current imaging techniques, which are unable to simultaneously measure the activity of large populations of neurons. The authors' method offers a promising approach to overcome this limitation and scale up circuit-level analyses.

However, there are some issues that the authors do not fully address. For instance, the large difference in correlation results between simulated (Figure 2c) and real data (Figure 3b) is not explained. Additionally, the authors note that their method requires substantial overlap between the imaged populations to produce reasonable results, but they do not provide a clear explanation for why this is the case.

In terms of originality, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by proposing a novel method for inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using both simulated and real data, which adds to the paper's significance.

In conclusion, this paper proposes a novel and effective method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons to characterize population dynamics. While there are some areas that could be improved, such as the figure legends and the explanation of the discrepancy between simulated and real data results, the paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neuroscience.
