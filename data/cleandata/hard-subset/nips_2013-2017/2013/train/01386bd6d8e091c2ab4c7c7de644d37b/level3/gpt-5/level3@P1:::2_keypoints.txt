- Contribution: Proposes "stitching" approach modeling full neural population as an LDS with missing observations to infer dynamics from multiple partial recordings.  
- Core method: Learn shared A, B, Q, R, d via EM with Kalman smoothing across sessions using known selection matrices.  
- Key capability: Predicts cross-session statistics, including noise correlations between neuron pairs never recorded simultaneously.  
- Empirical result (sim): Recovers off-diagonal couplings and noise correlations; predicts latent activity of unobserved neurons.  
- Empirical result (real data): On two mouse S1 two-photon datasets, predicts non-simultaneous pairwise noise correlations close to a fully observed upper bound and far better than session-wise LDS baselines.  
- Novelty: Frames multi-session stitching as an LDS with missing observations and demonstrates predicting correlations for non-simultaneous pairs on real data.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on latent dynamical models and system ID with missing data but treats the state as the full N-neuron vector observed in pieces instead of only low-dimensional latent factors.  
- Strength — relevance: Addresses a timely, practical limitation in calcium imaging (limited simultaneous field-of-view).  
- Strength — simplicity/generalizability: Conceptually simple and extensible to other observation types (spike counts/point processes, multi-session ephys).  
- Strength — empirical validation: Solid synthetic validation and informative performance bounds (upper/lower) used.  
- Strength — scalability: Uses online EM initialization followed by batch EM to aid scalability.  
- Weakness — modeling assumptions: Relies on linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q/R, stationarity, and consistent dynamics across sessions; robustness unclear when violated.  
- Weakness — role of overlap: Performance degrades without overlap; identifiability with disjoint sessions is ambiguous and needs formal analysis.  
- Weakness — lack of identifiability analysis: Request explicit identifiability conditions (e.g., minimal overlap, stimulus excitation) and experiments sweeping overlap.  
- Weakness — incremental session information: Needs clearer explanation/proof of how additional sessions resolve transform ambiguities and improve identification.  
- Weakness — couplings vs connectivity: Learned A correlates weakly with fully observed A; A reflects statistical couplings confounded by common input, not synaptic connectivity.  
- Suggestion — modeling priors: Consider low-rank-plus-sparse or low-dimensional latent process noise to improve interpretability of couplings.  
- Weakness — derivation details: Derivations are terse; include full complete-data log-likelihood, closed-form M-step updates, initialization, convergence criteria, and complexity scaling.  
- Weakness — baselines: Naive baseline is weak; add comparisons to GPFA/PLDS, subspace ID with missing outputs, and covariance-completion methods.  
- Practical suggestions: Discuss regularization/stability of A, hyperparameter selection, runtime, and scaling to thousands of neurons; report effects of sparsity/stability penalties.  
- Evaluation summary — quality: Sound within LDS framework but missing derivations and identifiability analysis reduce rigor.  
- Evaluation summary — clarity: Readable but imbalanced; needs tighter notation and expanded technical exposition.  
- Evaluation summary — originality & significance: Novel framing and high potential impact for multi-field imaging; likely to be adopted and extended.  
- Pros for acceptance: Addresses important experimental constraint, strong empirical correlation recovery, and practical utility.  
- Cons for acceptance: Insufficient technical detail, unclear robustness to model violations, and weak interpretability/baselines.  
- Questions for authors: Provide identifiability conditions and overlap sensitivity; test performance with correlated/low-rank noise or PLDS observations; detail regularization/stability for A; assess sensitivity to session nonstationarities.  
- Overall recommendation: Promising, practical, and borderline accept — strong empirical results but needs added technical depth and robustness studies.
