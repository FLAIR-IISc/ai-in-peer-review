This paper proposes a principled “stitching” framework that combines multiple, partially overlapping calcium-imaging sessions into a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. The model comprises a coupling matrix A, stimulus drive B, and noise covariances Q (neural) and R (measurement), estimated with a mini-batch EM variant using session-specific selection matrices Ck. The goal is to recover correlations—and by proxy functional couplings—between neuron pairs not recorded simultaneously. The paper situates itself against prior NIPS work on latent GLMs/LDS and subspace identification for partially observed populations, as well as methods inferring connectivity from calcium data; unlike prior approaches, it targets correlations across non-simultaneously measured neurons without assuming a monotonic link between stimulus- and noise-correlations.

Quality: The formulation is sound, the inference scheme is standard and appropriate, and the synthetic experiments are convincing: the method recovers off-diagonal couplings and accurately predicts noise correlations for never-co-recorded pairs. On real mouse S1 data, the approach substantially outperforms a naive baseline and approaches a fully observed upper bound for correlations. However, there are notable weaknesses: (i) a strong gap between simulated (high) and real-data (modest) coupling recovery is not explained; (ii) model assumptions (linear Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q, simple observation model ignoring calcium/spiking and heteroscedastic noise) likely drive the need for substantial overlap in practice, yet this sensitivity is not analyzed; (iii) baselines are limited—comparisons to PLDS/GPFA, subspace-ID, or deconvolution+GLM would strengthen claims; (iv) no held-out likelihood or predictive metrics beyond correlations are reported, and stability/identifiability conditions for A are not discussed.

Clarity: The paper is well written overall, but figure legends and figure-referred text are too terse; panels, axes, and evaluation metrics require more explicit description. Please also report CIs/error bars and dataset-specific preprocessing choices.

Originality: High. Inferring cross-session correlations without simultaneity is a novel and timely contribution.

Significance: High potential. If robustness to overlap, noise, and model mismatch is clarified, this can become a standard tool to scale population analyses beyond simultaneous imaging limits.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation of stitching as an LDS with missing data; scalable EM implementation.
- Strong correlation recovery on synthetic and meaningful gains on real data.
- Addresses an important experimental bottleneck with a general framework.

Cons for acceptance:
- Real-data performance depends on substantial overlap; no systematic analysis of overlap vs. accuracy.
- Unexplained discrepancy between simulated and real coupling/correlation results.
- Limited baselines and ablations; simplified observation/noise model.

Suggestions: Quantify trade-offs vs. overlap fraction, stimulus strength, and noise; add stronger baselines; report predictive likelihood and activity cross-prediction; examine stability constraints and sparsity priors on A; discuss calcium dynamics or deconvolution in the observation model.
