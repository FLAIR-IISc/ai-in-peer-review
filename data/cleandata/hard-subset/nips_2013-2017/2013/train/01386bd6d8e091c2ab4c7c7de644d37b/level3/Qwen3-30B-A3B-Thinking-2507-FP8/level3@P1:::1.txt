

This paper addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience: characterizing neural population dynamics when simultaneous recording of all neurons in a circuit is experimentally infeasible. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit into a unified latent dynamical system model, enabling inference of dynamics and correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neuron pairs. The approach builds on latent dynamical systems theory [21] but extends it to handle missing observations across sequential imaging sessions, which is distinct from prior work on sub-sampled neural data [12-15]. While previous studies have focused on inferring functional connectivity from simultaneously recorded neurons [6-11], this work uniquely tackles the problem of connecting non-simultaneously recorded populations.

The paper's main strength lies in its effective demonstration of the method's ability to recover noise correlations between neuron pairs not recorded simultaneously. In the simulated data (Section 3.1), the stitching method successfully recovers coupling matrices (correlation 0.73 for non-simultaneously recorded pairs) and accurately predicts noise correlations (correlation 0.92 with ground truth), substantially outperforming a naive approach that would set such correlations to zero. On experimental data from mouse somatosensory cortex (Section 3.2), the method produces coupling matrices with structure similar to the "fully observed" model (correlation 0.38 for non-simultaneously recorded pairs), and predicts pairwise correlations with correlation 0.84 relative to the upper bound model compared to 0.15 for the naive approach. This represents a significant advancement over existing methods that cannot infer correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons [12].

The technical quality of the paper is strong. The authors clearly formulate the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian model for computational tractability. The EM-based inference procedure (Section 2.1) is well-explained, and the authors provide careful experimental design (Section 2.2) including both simulated data with ground truth and real calcium imaging data. The authors also appropriately benchmark against a naive approach and a theoretical upper bound, providing a clear picture of the method's performance.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need addressing. First, the real data experiments require substantial overlap (60%) between imaging sessions to work effectively, as noted in Section 3.2. The authors do not sufficiently explain why this overlap is necessary or provide a theoretical justification for this requirement, which is critical for experimental design. Second, there is a significant unexplained discrepancy between the simulated (Figure 2c: correlation 0.73) and real data (Figure 3b: correlation 0.38) results for non-simultaneously recorded couplings. The authors should discuss possible reasons for this difference, such as differences in neural dynamics between simulated and real data, or limitations of the linear model in capturing real neural circuit complexity.

The clarity of the paper could be improved. While the overall writing is clear, several figure legends (e.g., Figure 2, Figure 3) lack sufficient detail to fully understand the presented results. For instance, Figure 3a's legend does not explain how the "50 most correlated neurons" were selected or what the different coupling matrices represent. The text should more explicitly connect the figures to the main results, particularly regarding the significance of the correlation values presented.

Regarding originality, the paper makes a clear contribution to the field by addressing a specific gap in the literature: inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. While the model framework (latent dynamical system) is not novel, the application to this specific problem of stitching partial recordings is original. The authors adequately distinguish their work from prior approaches in the related work section.

The significance of this work is high. Current imaging technology limits simultaneous recording to hundreds of neurons, while neural circuits of interest often contain thousands. The proposed method effectively expands the analyzable population size, potentially enabling more comprehensive circuit-level analyses. The authors correctly note that this approach could help overcome fundamental physical limits of current imaging technology. The paper's application to mouse somatosensory cortex demonstrates clear practical utility for neuroscience research.

The paper would benefit from addressing the following limitations: 1) The authors should discuss the applicability of their method to more than two imaging sessions and the impact of session order; 2) They should explore whether the linear Gaussian model could be improved with more biologically realistic components [6, 16]; 3) The paper should more thoroughly discuss the experimental design implications of the required overlap between imaging sessions.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the neuroscience and machine learning communities. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates clear utility on both simulated and real data. While the paper has some clarity issues and the real data results show a larger discrepancy than expected, these do not undermine the core contribution. The work has the potential to significantly impact how neural population data is analyzed, making it a valuable addition to the field. I recommend acceptance.
