This paper introduces a novel and potentially impactful method for inferring the dynamics of large neural populations by “stitching” together data acquired from sequentially recorded, overlapping subnetworks. The core idea – framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations – is clever and addresses a significant challenge in contemporary neuroscience: the limitations of simultaneous recording capabilities when studying circuits comprising thousands of neurons. The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on both simulated and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing its ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The work builds upon a growing body of literature focused on inferring functional connectivity from neural data [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]. However, unlike these approaches, this method specifically targets the problem of combining information across non-simultaneous recordings. While some related work addresses incomplete observations [12, 13, 14, 15], the authors convincingly argue that these methods are not directly applicable to their setting, particularly highlighting the limitations of assuming monotonic relationships between stimulus and noise correlations [12] or requiring high temporal sampling [14]. The paper appropriately references and distinguishes itself from these prior efforts.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The method represents a significant step towards analyzing larger neural circuits than previously possible with existing imaging technology. The approach of leveraging sequential recordings and framing it as a missing data problem within a latent dynamical system is a conceptually elegant solution.
* **Potential Impact:** The ability to accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons has the potential to significantly improve our understanding of circuit dynamics and function, moving beyond what can be inferred from isolated, partial recordings.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating the method’s effectiveness on both simulated and real data strengthens the claims. The results on the mouse somatosensory cortex data are particularly encouraging.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and its importance, connecting it to the limitations of current experimental techniques and the need to model larger circuits.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions and Robustness:** The method relies on several assumptions, notably linearity and Gaussianity. While the authors acknowledge these, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the method’s robustness to violations of these assumptions. What are the failure modes? Under what conditions might the method break down, and how can these be mitigated?
* **Incremental Benefit:** The paper doesn’t fully address the question of *why* stitching provides additional information beyond simply acquiring more data from a single, larger recording. If a fully accurate model can be recovered from one subset of neurons (up to a latent transformation), what crucial information is gained by incorporating subsequent subsets? A more thorough theoretical justification would be valuable.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The observation that the method requires substantial overlap between sequential recordings (60% in the experimental data) is significant but not adequately explained. Why is overlap necessary to constrain the model and achieve accurate results? The paper needs to provide a more intuitive explanation for this dependency.
* **Interpretation of Correlations:** The predicted noise correlations could be driven by shared, unobserved inputs or latent variables, rather than direct pairwise couplings. The authors acknowledge this possibility but should more carefully discuss this ambiguity, especially given the limited agreement between predicted and true couplings (Fig. 3b).
* **Technical Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the mathematical derivations, particularly the M-step of the EM algorithm. This omission hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the validity of the method’s implementation. A more thorough presentation of the technical aspects would enhance clarity and confidence in the results.
* **Presentation Imbalance:** The introduction and discussion sections are relatively extensive, while the technical details are comparatively sparse. A more balanced presentation, with a greater emphasis on the mathematical foundations and implementation, would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The method addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience and offers a novel solution with promising results.
* The empirical validation on both simulated and real data supports the claims.
* The work is well-motivated and clearly presented, despite some technical omissions.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The lack of robustness analysis and discussion of failure modes raises concerns about the method’s practical applicability.
* The unclear incremental benefit and the overlap requirement need further justification.
* The insufficient technical detail hinders reproducibility and thorough evaluation.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. However, several weaknesses, particularly regarding robustness, theoretical justification, and technical detail, prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. I recommend **rejection with request for revision.** The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above by: (1) providing a more thorough analysis of the method’s robustness to violations of its assumptions; (2) clarifying the incremental benefit of stitching over simply acquiring more data; (3) explaining the necessity of substantial overlap between recordings; (4) discussing the potential confounding effects of shared inputs; and (5) providing a more complete and detailed description of the mathematical derivations and implementation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact on the field.
