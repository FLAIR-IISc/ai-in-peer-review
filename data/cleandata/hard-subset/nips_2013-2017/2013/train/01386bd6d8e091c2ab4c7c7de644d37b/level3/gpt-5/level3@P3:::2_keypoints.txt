Summary: Proposes "stitching" sequential partial recordings into a joint LDS with session-specific selection matrices, fit by (online) EM, to predict noise correlations for non-simultaneous neuron pairs.

Conceptual contribution: Infers large-network properties by aggregating overlapping subnetworks and temporal structure, constraining cross-population couplings.

Relation to prior work: Builds on latent state-space and partial-observation system ID ideas; most similar to Pillow & Latham (2008) but stitching sequential sessions with explicit missing-data treatment is presented as novel.

Empirics — simulations: Convincing recovery of cross-block couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations in simulations.

Empirics — real data: Stitched model predicts correlations substantially better than naïve baseline and near a fully observed upper bound, but learned couplings for non-simultaneous pairs correlate modestly (r ≈ 0.38) with fully observed model.

Assumptions and robustness: Relies on near linear-Gaussian approximation, diagonal Q/R, stationarity, and stable alignment; failure modes under nonlinearity, non-Gaussianity, nonstationarity, or correlated process noise are not explored.

Identifiability/overlap: Performance degrades without overlap; paper lacks theoretical conditions explaining why overlap is required or how it anchors similarity transforms.

Estimation details missing: M-step derivations for A, B, Q, handling of missing data, initialization, regularization, and computational complexity are underspecified, hindering reproducibility.

Clarity: Well-motivated and situated, but presentation is imbalanced with insufficient technical exposition of core EM updates and identifiability intuition.

Originality: Core idea of stitching sequentially imaged subsets as an LDS is novel and elegant; extension to point-process data is suggested but not demonstrated.

Significance: Potentially impactful for expanding effective population size in imaging, especially for predicting non-simultaneous noise correlations, but practical impact depends on robustness and guidance on session design.

Strengths: Novel, broadly applicable idea; clear empirical benefits on real data; sensible evaluation including synthetic ground truth and fictional-session tests.

Weaknesses: Unclear robustness to model mismatch; opaque identifiability gains from multiple subsets; overlap requirement unexplained; ambiguity between direct couplings and common input; sparse estimation/derivation details; claimed point-process extension not shown.

Key questions for authors: 1) Formal identifiability conditions with/without overlap? 2) How does performance scale with overlap size, SNR, stimulus richness, nonlinearity? 3) Effects of allowing low-rank+diagonal Q to capture common input? 4) Provide full EM updates, initialization, regularization, runtime? 5) Can you demonstrate a point-process/GLM version?

Suggested improvements: Add identifiability/observability theory addressing overlap; include full estimation details and pseudocode; test robustness to nonlinear/Poisson/nonstationary scenarios and correlated noise; disentangle direct couplings vs. common input (e.g., latent factors or partial correlations); clarify incremental information from additional subsets.

Arguments for acceptance: Novel, useful idea with real-data gains on an important metric; simple, extensible framework.

Arguments against acceptance: Missing technical depth and derivations; unclear identifiability and overlap rationale; ambiguity in interpreting couplings; unknown robustness and no demonstrated point-process extension.

Overall verdict: Promising and potentially impactful but needs stronger technical exposition, identifiability/overlap analysis, and robustness studies; reviewer leans borderline reject/weak accept pending revisions.
