This paper presents a novel statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of neuronal subsets. The core contribution is to frame this "stitching" problem within a latent dynamical system framework, treating the full population activity as a latent state and each recording session as a partial observation. Using a linear dynamical system (LDS) model, the authors demonstrate that this approach can effectively estimate functional connectivity and, crucially, predict noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never simultaneously recorded. This work builds on a foundation of system identification and latent variable modeling but applies it to the specific and important challenge of overcoming the limited field-of-view in modern neuroscience imaging techniques.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its elegant and effective solution to a significant, practical problem. As technologies for recording neural activity increase in neuron count but remain limited relative to circuit size, methods for computationally integrating partial datasets are critically important. The originality of the approach is clear, and the validation is methodologically sound. The use of both simulated data with ground-truth and a clever "fictional imaging scenario" on real 2-photon data provides compelling evidence for the method's efficacy. The comparisons against "fully observed" (upper bound) and "naive" (lower bound) models clearly contextualize the performance and highlight the substantial improvement offered by the proposed technique. The paper is well-written, and the concepts are communicated with excellent clarity.

The main weakness is the reliance on a linear Gaussian model, which is a simplification of the underlying non-linear and non-Gaussian neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this and the model proves surprisingly effective for predicting correlations, its limitations in capturing more complex dynamics are not fully explored. Furthermore, the authors state that some overlap between recorded subsets was "critical" for the method to work. This is a key practical constraint, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more systematic analysis quantifying how performance degrades with decreasing overlap. This would provide invaluable guidance for experimental design.

Overall, the strengths of this paper—its novelty, high significance for experimental neuroscience, and rigorous evaluation—outweigh its limitations. It presents a valuable computational tool with the potential to substantially advance the analysis of large-scale neural recordings by computationally expanding the effective population size beyond what is experimentally possible in a single session. I strongly recommend acceptance.
