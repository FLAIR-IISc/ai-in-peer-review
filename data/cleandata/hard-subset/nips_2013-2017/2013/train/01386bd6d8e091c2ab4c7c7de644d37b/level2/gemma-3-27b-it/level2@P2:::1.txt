This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring the dynamics of a larger neural population from sequential, partial recordings. The authors propose a method based on fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, effectively “stitching” together data from multiple imaging sessions. The core idea is sound and well-motivated, particularly in the context of current limitations in 2-photon calcium imaging where simultaneously recorded populations are often smaller than the functionally relevant circuit size. The method is demonstrated effectively on both simulated data and real recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing a clear ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem in systems neuroscience.  The ability to infer dynamics beyond the scope of simultaneous recordings is crucial for understanding circuit function.
* **Sound Methodology:**  The proposed approach of framing the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing data is a natural and principled way to address the challenge. Using a linear Gaussian system simplifies the computation while still demonstrating the core concept.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the model and methods. The equations are well-defined, and the workflow is easy to follow.
* **Convincing Results:** The simulation results convincingly demonstrate the method's ability to recover the underlying coupling matrix and predict noise correlations. The application to real calcium imaging data strengthens the findings, showing the method’s applicability to a complex biological system. The comparison to a naive approach and the 'fully observed' model provides a useful benchmark.
* **Thorough Ablation:** The authors clearly define upper and lower bounds on performance and demonstrate that their method performs well within these bounds, showing good performance relative to what is theoretically possible and what can be achieved with simpler methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** While justified for tractability, the linear Gaussian dynamical system is a strong simplification of the underlying neural dynamics.  The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of this assumption on the results would be valuable. For example, how might the method perform with more nonlinear models, even if computationally more demanding?
* **Limited Exploration of Model Complexity:** The paper primarily focuses on a specific implementation (linear Gaussian). A brief exploration of other latent dynamical system models (e.g., GLMs, state-space models with nonlinearities) or different noise structures might strengthen the argument that the core stitching idea is robust, rather than being dependent on the choice of a specific model.
* **Parameter Tuning/Sensitivity:** The paper does not delve deeply into the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices (e.g., the regularization parameters used during fitting).  A discussion of how robust the results are to different parameter settings would be beneficial.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between imaging sessions is crucial is important, but the paper doesn't fully explore the optimal degree of overlap. Further investigation into how performance varies with overlap could inform experimental design.
* **Missing Related Work:** While the authors mention related work, they omit citations to recent work on state-space models specifically designed for calcium imaging data (e.g., the CaImAn package: https://github.com/flatironinstitute/CaImAn). Acknowledging and discussing these works would provide a more complete contextualization of the contribution.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and valuable method for analyzing neural population dynamics. The approach is well-motivated, the results are convincing, and the paper is clearly written. The limitations are primarily related to the simplifying assumptions made in the model, but these are acknowledged by the authors. The potential impact of this work is significant, as it opens up new possibilities for studying neural circuits that are too large to be imaged simultaneously with current technology.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem in systems neuroscience.
* Proposes a principled and well-motivated solution.
* Demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real data.
* Provides a clear and thorough analysis of the method's strengths and limitations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* The strong simplifying assumptions (linear Gaussian model) may limit the applicability of the method to certain types of neural data.
* Further investigation into the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices and the optimal degree of overlap between imaging sessions is needed.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend accepting this paper. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh them. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field and has the potential to be highly impactful. Addressing the minor suggestions for improvement would further strengthen the manuscript.
