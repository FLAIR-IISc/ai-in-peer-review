This paper addresses the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The authors propose a statistical method, framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. The motivation is clear – current 2-photon imaging techniques limit the number of simultaneously recorded neurons, while computations likely involve larger circuits. The proposed method aims to overcome this limitation, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness both on simulated and real data (mouse somatosensory cortex), showing that it can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The paper is well-motivated and tackles an important problem in the field of neural data analysis. The approach of utilizing a latent dynamical system is reasonable and builds on existing work in this area (e.g., [13]). The demonstration on both simulated and real data is a significant strength. The authors clearly explain their method and its advantages, and the results presented are compelling. The inclusion of both simulated data allows for a controlled evaluation of the method's performance, while the analysis of real data demonstrates its applicability to a biologically relevant scenario. The comparison with a naive approach and an upper bound (“fully observed” model) is particularly useful in evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed stitching method. The authors also carefully consider and discuss limitations and potential extensions of their work.

However, there are several areas where the paper could be improved. 

First, while the authors mention related work, the discussion feels somewhat superficial.  For example, the reference to [12] is accurate in pointing out the assumption of monotonic relationships between stimulus and noise correlations. However, a broader discussion of other methods for dealing with partial observability, especially in the context of state-space models (e.g., Kalman filtering, particle filtering), could strengthen the positioning of this work. While the authors specifically address differences with [13] regarding scalability, a brief comparison to more recent scalable methods for latent GLMs would be beneficial. 

Second, the choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification. While the authors acknowledge this and motivate it by computational tractability, a discussion of the potential impact of this simplification on the accuracy of the results would be helpful.  Exploring the sensitivity of the results to the linearity assumption, or briefly investigating more complex models, could address this concern. The authors mention that more realistic models could improve accuracy, but do not delve into the computational challenges of implementing such models.

Third, the description of the experimental data and pre-processing steps could be more detailed. While the authors provide some information, a more thorough description of the imaging parameters, ROI definition, and filtering procedures would enhance reproducibility.  Specifically, the rationale for the 1s high-pass filter is not stated, and could be elaborated upon.

Fourth, the paper relies heavily on correlation coefficients as a measure of performance. While informative, it would be beneficial to present other metrics, such as explained variance or prediction error, to provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s accuracy. This is particularly important given the potential for spurious correlations to arise in high-dimensional data.

Finally, while the authors address potential limitations, the discussion of near degeneracy of parameters for larger populations in the experimental results (last paragraph of section 3.2) feels somewhat abrupt and could be expanded. What are the practical implications of this degeneracy? How might it be mitigated?

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in neural data analysis.
*   The proposed method is well-motivated and based on sound statistical principles.
*   The results are compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the method on both simulated and real data.
*   The comparison with alternative approaches (naive method, fully observed model) provides a valuable benchmark.
*   The code is available.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The related work discussion could be more comprehensive.
*   The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification that could limit the accuracy of the results.
*   The description of the experimental data and pre-processing steps could be more detailed.
*   Reliance on correlation coefficients as the primary performance metric.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. While there are some areas for improvement, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. The proposed method is novel and effective, and the results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to expand the related work discussion, elaborate on the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption, provide more detail on the experimental data and pre-processing, and consider adding additional performance metrics.
