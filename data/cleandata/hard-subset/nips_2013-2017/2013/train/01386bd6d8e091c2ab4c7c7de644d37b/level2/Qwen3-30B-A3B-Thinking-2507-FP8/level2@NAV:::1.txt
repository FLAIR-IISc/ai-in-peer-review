

This paper presents a compelling statistical framework for "stitching" together sequential, non-overlapping neural recordings into a unified dynamical model to infer noise correlations across non-simultaneously observed neuron pairs. The core claim is that a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations can accurately reconstruct population dynamics beyond the limits of simultaneous imaging, as demonstrated on simulated data and mouse somatosensory cortex recordings. The authors support this with rigorous comparisons against a "fully observed" upper bound (impossible in practice) and a naive baseline that cannot predict non-simultaneous correlations. Results show their method achieves 0.92 correlation for noise correlations in simulations and 0.84 in real data versus the upper bound, significantly outperforming the naive approach (0.15 correlation).

The work addresses a critical bottleneck in neuroscience—scaling population analysis beyond current imaging limits—while building on relevant NIPS literature (e.g., [6,7,13] on neural dynamics and sub-sampling). Novelty is strong: unlike prior methods [12,13], this avoids restrictive assumptions (e.g., monotonic stimulus-correlation relationships) and scales to large populations (100+ neurons). The LDS approach is well-motivated for its computational tractability, and the experimental validation on two distinct datasets (anesthetized/awake mice) adds practical credibility.

Strengths include clear problem formulation, thorough quantitative evaluation, and insightful discussion of limitations (e.g., performance degradation with larger populations, linear model simplifications). However, the reliance on a linear Gaussian model is acknowledged as a simplification; the authors could strengthen claims by testing more biologically realistic models (e.g., incorporating calcium dynamics [16]) or quantifying how much performance drops as population size increases. The "fictional imaging" test on real data is clever but could benefit from a discussion of how it maps to real-world experimental constraints (e.g., session-to-session variability).

This work is highly significant for neuroscience and ML, offering a practical path to expand population-level analysis. It meets all NIPS criteria: technically sound (well-supported experiments), clear (logical structure), original (distinct from prior work), and impactful (enables new circuit studies). While the model’s simplicity is a limitation, the authors appropriately frame it as a starting point. The paper is well-written and reproducible. **Recommendation: Accept.**
