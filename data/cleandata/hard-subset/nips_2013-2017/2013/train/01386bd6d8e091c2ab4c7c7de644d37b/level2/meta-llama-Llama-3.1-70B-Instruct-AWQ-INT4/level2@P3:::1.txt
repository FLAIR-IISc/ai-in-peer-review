This paper presents a novel statistical method for inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The method, referred to as "stitching," combines sequentially imaged sets of neurons into one model by fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method using both simulated and real calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound, and the authors provide a clear and well-organized presentation of their method. The claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The authors also carefully evaluate the performance of their method using various metrics and compare it to a conventional approach.

**Clarity**: The paper is clearly written, and the authors provide a thorough introduction to the problem and the proposed method. The text is well-organized, and the figures are informative and help to illustrate the main points. The authors also provide a detailed description of the methods used, making it possible for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

**Originality**: The paper presents a novel approach to inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings. While the idea of using latent dynamical systems is not new, the authors' application of this approach to the problem of stitching together non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs is original and innovative.

**Significance**: The results presented in the paper are significant, as they demonstrate the ability to accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs using the stitching method. This has important implications for the study of neural circuits and the development of data-driven models of neural function. The authors also provide a thorough discussion of the limitations and potential extensions of their method, which will be useful for future research.
