Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the practical and important problem of inferring joint population dynamics from multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same neural circuit. The authors formulate “stitching” as parameter estimation in a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, sharing a global A, B, Q, R across sessions and using known selection matrices Ck to indicate which neurons were imaged in each session. Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant followed by batch EM). The key claim is that, given partial overlaps and sufficient data, one can recover cross-couplings and hence predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

The approach is conceptually grounded in classic system identification and EM with missing data (e.g., Dempster et al. 1977) and consistent with identifiability ideas for LDS up to coordinate transforms. The paper situates itself relative to prior work on inferring connectivity from calcium imaging (e.g., Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012), PLDS/latent-population models (Pillow & Latham 2008; Macke et al. 2012), and sub-sampling problems (Wohrer et al. 2010), highlighting that previous methods did not infer cross-session couplings between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The technical contribution is not a new model class per se, but a careful application of LDS with missing data to a practically relevant acquisition paradigm, with empirical validation on simulated and real 2p datasets.

Strengths:
- Clear and useful problem formulation: a shared LDS across sessions with known selection matrices elegantly captures multi-session partial recordings.
- Empirical validation shows that stitched models can recover cross-session couplings and, more robustly, predict noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs. Performance approaches an upper bound (“fully observed” model) and substantially exceeds a “naive” baseline.
- Demonstrated on both simulated networks with known ground truth and on real mouse S1 datasets (awake and anesthetized) using a controlled “fictional split” that enables benchmarking.
- Practical algorithmic details (online EM followed by batch EM) and sensible performance metrics (parameter recovery, correlation prediction, cross-prediction of activity).

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Validation on real data relies on splitting simultaneously recorded datasets; there is no demonstration on truly sequentially acquired fields of view, where session-to-session nonstationarities (e.g., gain drift, behavioral state changes) and cell registration issues arise. This limits real-world generality.
- The model assumes a single global A, B, Q, R shared across sessions, stationarity, and diagonal R. Calcium imaging often exhibits correlated measurement noise (neuropil contamination) and per-session/ROI-specific gains. A single offset d may be insufficient; per-session (and even per-neuron per-session) gains/offsets could be important in practice.
- Stability and identifiability: The method presumes a stable A (Lyapunov equation for Q0) but does not state how stability is enforced during EM. Identifiability conditions (amount of overlap, stimulus richness, controllability/observability) are discussed qualitatively but not analyzed. The empirical note that overlap is “critical,” and that performance deteriorates without overlap, warrants a more systematic treatment.
- Parameterization vs data regime: With N up to ~200, A has N^2 parameters; regularization is only briefly mentioned. Overfitting risks are nontrivial, particularly as population size grows; cross-session holdout results are encouraging but additional controls would help.
- Evaluation metrics focus on correlation between predicted and “fully observed” LDS quantities. It would be informative to include direct empirical correlation comparisons computed from the simultaneously recorded data (after removing stimulus effects) as an additional reference, not just model-vs-model.
- External inputs: the framework allows u^k_t, but many experiments have unobserved inputs and internal state fluctuations. Low-dimensional latent drives (e.g., adding unobserved LDS factors) are mentioned as an extension but not evaluated.

Quality:
The model and inference are sound and standard; the application is careful, with appropriate baselines and held-out tests. Some key assumptions (stationarity across sessions, diagonal R, stability constraints) should be made explicit, with sensitivity analyses. Runtime/scaling details are limited; for larger N, cubic Kalman steps become costly unless structure is exploited.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. The role of overlap, the identifiability intuition, and the evaluation protocol are explained well. It would help to explicitly list model assumptions, detail initialization and stability enforcement, and include more specifics on convergence diagnostics and computational cost.

Originality:
The novelty lies in framing and validating stitching of multi-session partial recordings via a shared LDS with missing observations, rather than inventing a new model. This is a useful and timely application, distinct from prior connectivity-inference and latent-population works that did not address cross-session prediction of noise correlations.

Significance:
The contribution is practically significant for calcium imaging and other modalities where full simultaneous coverage is infeasible. Accurate prediction of cross-session noise correlations and activity cross-prediction could meaningfully expand the scope of population analyses. Wider adoption will depend on robustness to real multi-session complications; demonstrating stitching on actual sequential fields of view would strengthen impact.

Questions for the authors:
- What are the precise identifiability and observability conditions (e.g., minimum overlap size, stimulus richness) needed for reliable stitching? Can you provide a diagnostic for when stitching is expected to succeed?
- How do you enforce stability of A during EM? Did you observe divergence or unstable estimates, and how was this handled?
- How sensitive are results to the diagonal-R assumption and to per-session gain/offset mismatches? Have you tried per-session affine calibration parameters?
- Can you report computational complexity and wall-clock time as a function of N, T, and number of sessions? Any use of sparsity in A during inference?
- On the real data splits, how do stitched-model-predicted noise correlations compare to empirical correlations computed directly from the simultaneous data (after regressing out stimulus effects)?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Include regularization (e.g., L1 or sparsity-promoting priors) and/or low-rank plus sparse structure on A; report its effect systematically.
- Add per-session affine calibration (offsets and gains) and consider correlated R (or latent observation noise factors) to better capture imaging noise.
- Provide a small theoretical or empirical study of identifiability vs overlap fraction, including cases with no overlap and more than two sessions.
- Report results on at least one truly sequential-imaging dataset (with partial overlap), including robustness to modest nonstationarity.
- Compare stitched predictions not only to a fully observed LDS but also to empirical correlations and to simpler baselines (e.g., regression on PSTHs, multiset CCA/factor models).

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses a relevant and practically important problem with a simple, principled approach.
- Strong empirical evidence that stitching can accurately predict cross-session correlations.
- Clear potential for broad applicability in systems neuroscience.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Lack of validation on truly sequential recordings; reliance on fictional splits may overestimate robustness.
- Limited treatment of identifiability, stability, regularization, and measurement noise structure.
- Incremental methodological novelty.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and useful contribution; with additional analyses addressing robustness and real multi-session data, it would be a strong candidate.
