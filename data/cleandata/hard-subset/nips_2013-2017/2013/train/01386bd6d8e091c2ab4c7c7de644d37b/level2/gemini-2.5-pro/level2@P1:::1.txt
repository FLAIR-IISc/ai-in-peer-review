This paper presents a statistical method for modeling the joint dynamics of a large neural population from a series of partial recordings where different subsets of neurons are observed in sequential sessions. The key idea is to model the entire population's activity as the latent state of a linear dynamical system (LDS). Each recording session is then treated as a partial, noisy observation of this full latent state. The model parameters, including the functional connectivity matrix, are inferred using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. This "stitching" framework allows the authors to infer interactions and predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

The problem of limited fields-of-view in neural recording technologies like 2-photon calcium imaging is a major bottleneck in systems neuroscience. This work is therefore well-motivated and timely. It builds upon a long history of work in system identification from partial observations, but its specific application to combine non-simultaneous neural recordings is novel and highly relevant to the NIPS community. The authors correctly position their work as an advance over methods that either require single, fully-observed sessions or make stronger assumptions about the relationship between signal and noise correlations.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a problem of great practical importance. The ability to computationally expand the effective size of a recorded neural population beyond the physical limitations of the recording device is a significant contribution. The proposed solution, framing the problem as inference in an LDS with structured missing data across sessions, is both elegant and novel in this context.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed method is well-founded in standard statistical machine learning techniques (LDS, EM). The adaptation of these tools to the specific problem of "stitching" is clear and appropriate.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The validation of the method is a major strength.
    *   On simulated data, where the ground truth is known, the authors demonstrate that the method can successfully recover both the underlying coupling matrix and, more robustly, the resulting noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed neuron pairs.
    *   The evaluation on real calcium imaging data is particularly clever. By creating a "fictional imaging scenario" from a larger, simultaneously recorded dataset, the authors are able to create a test case with a known ground truth ("fully observed" model). Comparing their method against this upper bound and a "naive" lower bound provides a clear and compelling demonstration of the method's efficacy on real neural data.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the method is described with sufficient detail, and the results are presented logically. The intuition provided in section 3.1 for why the method works (using one session to identify the system up to a linear transform, and the second to resolve the ambiguity) is helpful for the reader.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** A crucial practical detail mentioned is that some overlap of neurons between the two "fictional" sessions was critical for the method to work well on the experimental data. This is an important finding that deserves more exploration. The initial intuition provided suggests that as long as the two populations are dynamically coupled, the latent state model should be able to link them. Could the authors clarify why overlap is necessary? Is it a fundamental identifiability issue, or a practical one related to optimization (e.g., avoiding poor local minima in EM)? A more detailed discussion on the trade-offs between the number of shared neurons, the strength of coupling between populations, and the quality of the fit would significantly increase the paper's practical value.

2.  **Limitations of the Linear-Gaussian Model:** The authors correctly acknowledge that the LDS is a simplified model of neural dynamics. While it appears sufficient for predicting pairwise correlations, a brief discussion of the potential failure modes would be beneficial. For what types of neural dynamics would this model be insufficient? For instance, how would it handle strongly non-linear interactions or bursting dynamics characteristic of some neural circuits?

3.  **Identifiability and Parameter Estimation:** The paper notes that while correlation structures are recovered well, the estimates of the individual coupling parameters degrade as the population size increases. This is an interesting result that suggests a potential identifiability issue. Could the authors expand on the potential "near degeneracy of parameters for large systems"? Does this imply that many different connectivity matrices can produce similar correlation structures, and that the model is simply finding one of them? A more formal discussion of the conditions required for unique parameter identifiability would strengthen the theoretical underpinnings of the work.

4.  **Scalability:** The paper proposes a method that could, in principle, be applied to stitch together many sessions to model very large circuits (e.g., an entire cortical barrel). It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the computational scalability of the method. How does the runtime of the EM algorithm scale with the total number of neurons N, the number of recording sessions, and the duration of each session? This would provide insight into the practical limits of the approach.

In summary, this is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and significant contribution to the fields of computational neuroscience and machine learning. It tackles a key experimental bottleneck with a method that is principled, well-executed, and convincingly validated. The weaknesses identified are primarily requests for clarification and further discussion that would strengthen an already excellent paper. The work is likely to be of high interest to researchers and practitioners in neuroscience and is a strong candidate for acceptance.
