### **Summary**

This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, partially overlapping subsets of neurons. The central problem addressed is a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously monitor all neurons in a functional circuit. The proposed solution frames this as a problem of inference in a latent dynamical system with missing data. The activity of the entire population is modeled as the latent state of a linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS), and each experimental recording session is treated as a partial observation of this state. The model parameters, including the functional connectivity matrix, are estimated using the Expectation-Maximization algorithm.

The work's main contribution is to demonstrate that this "stitching" method can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded at the same time. This is a non-trivial accomplishment that goes beyond previous work on functional connectivity, which typically requires simultaneous observations. The method is validated on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, where a "fictional imaging scenario" is cleverly used for benchmarking.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound and complete piece of work. The application of an LDS model to this problem is appropriate for establishing a proof of concept, and the claims are well-supported by the evidence provided. The evaluation is rigorous and convincing. Using simulated data allows for direct comparison with ground-truth parameters, clearly demonstrating the method's ability to recover the underlying connectivity structure. For the real experimental data, the use of a "fully observed" model as an upper bound and a "naive" model as a lower bound provides a clear and fair assessment of the method's performance. This evaluation strategy is a significant strength of the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is compelling, the methodology is explained concisely, and the results are presented logically. The figures are illustrative and effectively support the main claims, particularly the visualizations of the recovered connectivity and correlation matrices. The paper provides enough information for an expert to understand the approach and its evaluation.

**Originality:** The primary contribution is highly original. While LDS and the EM algorithm are standard tools, their application to the problem of combining non-simultaneous neural recordings is novel and insightful. The paper successfully carves out a unique niche, differentiating its approach from previous work that either did not address non-simultaneous recordings or made strong assumptions that are not required here. The idea of computationally expanding the scope of neural recordings in this manner is a new and valuable concept.

**Significance:** The work has the potential for high impact. It offers a practical solution to a pressing problem in experimental systems neuroscience. The ability to build a unified dynamical model from a series of partial recordings could enable the study of circuit-level computations at a scale that is currently inaccessible. If the approach proves generalizable, it could become a standard tool for analyzing data from techniques like 2-photon imaging, guiding both data analysis and future experimental design. The results are important and are likely to be used and built upon by other researchers in the field.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be addressed to further improve it.

1.  **Model Assumptions**: The choice of a linear-Gaussian dynamical system is well-justified for its tractability and for demonstrating the core idea. However, neural dynamics are inherently non-linear and spiking activity is non-Gaussian. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential limitations imposed by these simplifying assumptions. How robust might the inference of correlations be to violations of linearity or Gaussianity? While implementing a more complex model is likely beyond the scope of a revision, a discussion of these issues would add valuable context.

2.  **Scalability to Multiple Sessions**: The experiments are confined to stitching two recording sessions. The ambition stated in the discussion is to map entire circuits, which would require stitching many sessions together. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief analysis, perhaps on simulated data, of how performance degrades as the number of "stitched" populations increases. For instance, if populations A and C are only connected via an intermediate recording of population B, how well can A-C correlations be inferred?

3.  **Role of Overlap**: The work notes that for real data, having some overlapping neurons between sessions was critical for good performance. This is a very important practical insight for experimentalists who might use this method. The paper could be made even more useful by quantifying this dependency more systematically. An analysis showing how stitching performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) varies as a function of the percentage of overlapping neurons would provide invaluable guidance for experimental design.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**

*   **Pro:** Addresses a significant, practical problem in experimental neuroscience with a novel and elegant computational solution.
*   **Pro:** The methodology is sound, and the experimental validation is thorough and convincing, using both simulated and real neural data.
*   **Pro:** The results are strong, demonstrating the ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons, which is a key and non-trivial claim.
*   **Pro:** The paper is well-written, clear, and has the potential for high impact on how large-scale neural data is collected and analyzed.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**

*   **Con:** The linear-Gaussian model is a simplification of real neural dynamics, and the robustness of the method to model mismatch is not fully explored.
*   **Con:** The demonstration is limited to stitching two populations, leaving questions about scalability to larger, more complex experimental designs unanswered.

In summary, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The proposed suggestions are intended to further strengthen an already excellent paper.
