

This paper presents a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural populations into a single coherent dynamical model. The authors address a significant practical limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations (thousands of neurons) due to imaging constraints. Their approach models the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, allowing them to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The paper's main strength is its clear demonstration of the method's utility on both simulated and real neural data. The authors show that their stitching method can recover coupling matrices and predict noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed neuron pairs with accuracy approaching a "fully observed" model (which would require recording all neurons simultaneously). The experimental results on mouse somatosensory cortex data are convincing, showing that their method substantially outperforms a naive approach that simply averages parameters across sessions.

The theoretical foundation is sound, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with expectation maximization for parameter estimation. The paper provides a clear explanation of the model and fitting procedure. The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their simple LDS model, noting that more complex models could improve performance.

However, I have two major concerns that affect the paper's quality and significance. First, the paper does not adequately address whether the linear model is sufficient for the task. Neural dynamics are inherently nonlinear, and calcium imaging data has known nonlinearities (as acknowledged in the paper's references [6, 16]). While the authors show that their simple model works reasonably well, they don't provide a strong theoretical justification for why a linear model would be appropriate for predicting noise correlations in neural populations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough analysis of the model's limitations and potential improvements.

Second, the paper's claim of "predicting noise correlations" needs more precise justification. The authors define noise correlations as "correlations in variability of neural activity after subtracting contributions due to the stimulus." However, their method actually predicts the correlation structure of the latent dynamical system, not necessarily the true neural noise correlations. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the model's predicted correlations relate to the biologically meaningful noise correlations.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear figures and appropriate experimental design. The related work section is thorough and properly situates the contribution within the literature. The authors cite relevant prior work, including recent NIPS papers on neural population dynamics.

I have a minor concern about the experimental design. The authors create a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data by splitting the simultaneously recorded neurons into two subsets. However, they don't explain why they chose the specific split (60% overlap), which could significantly impact the results. A more systematic exploration of how the method performs across different split ratios would strengthen the experimental validation.

The significance of this work is high, as it addresses a real experimental limitation in neuroscience. The ability to "stitch" together partial recordings could substantially expand the population sizes for which neural dynamics can be characterized. However, the paper could better articulate how this method would change experimental design in neuroscience, particularly regarding the trade-offs between imaging time, coverage, and overlap between recording sessions.

The paper's main contribution is valuable, but the claim that this method can "predict noise correlations" needs to be more precisely qualified. The method predicts correlations within the latent dynamical system model, which may not perfectly correspond to true neural noise correlations due to the simplifying assumptions of the model. The authors should temper their claims to reflect this limitation.

In conclusion, this paper presents a useful method for addressing a significant experimental challenge in neuroscience. The approach is technically sound, and the experimental results are convincing. However, the paper would be significantly improved by a more thorough discussion of the model's limitations and a more precise explanation of how the predicted correlations relate to biologically meaningful noise correlations. The authors should also better justify why their linear model is appropriate for this task, given the known nonlinearities in neural dynamics.

The paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS if the authors address these concerns, particularly regarding the model's limitations and the precise interpretation of their results. As it stands, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, but the claims about predicting noise correlations could be overstated.
