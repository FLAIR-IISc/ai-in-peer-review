- Formulates "stitching" as parameter estimation in a shared latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations using known selection matrices and EM (online then batch).

- Key claim: with partial overlaps and sufficient data, one can recover cross-couplings and predict noise correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously.

- Approach is grounded in classic system identification/EM and consistent with LDS identifiability up to coordinate transforms; not a new model class but a careful application.

- Situates work relative to prior connectivity and latent-population methods, highlighting novelty in cross-session coupling inference.

- Clear and useful problem formulation: shared LDS across sessions elegantly models multi-session partial recordings.

- Empirical results show stitched models recover cross-session couplings and predict noise correlations better than naive baselines and close to a fully observed model.

- Validated on simulated networks with ground truth and on real mouse S1 datasets via controlled "fictional split" benchmarking.

- Practical algorithmic details (online EM followed by batch EM) and sensible metrics (parameter recovery, correlation prediction, cross-prediction) are provided.

- No demonstration on truly sequential fields of view; reliance on fictional splits misses session nonstationarities and cell registration challenges.

- Strong modeling assumptions: single global A,B,Q,R shared across sessions, stationarity, and diagonal R; these may not reflect neuropil contamination or per-session/gain differences.

- Stability and identifiability are not enforced or systematically analyzed; overlap is empirically critical but lacks formal conditions and diagnostics.

- Overfitting risk: A has O(N^2) parameters (N~200) and regularization is only briefly mentioned; scalability and generalization concerns remain.

- Evaluation focuses on model-vs-model correlation metrics; direct empirical comparisons to correlations from simultaneous data are missing.

- Unobserved/external inputs are not fully addressed; low-dimensional latent drives are suggested but not evaluated.

- Model and inference are technically sound but key assumptions, sensitivity analyses, and stability enforcement should be made explicit.

- Paper is generally clear, but needs more detail on initialization, stability constraints, convergence diagnostics, and computational cost.

- Novelty lies in framing and validating stitching with shared LDS rather than introducing new methodology.

- Contribution is practically significant for calcium imaging, but impact depends on robustness to real multi-session complications.

- Key questions: precise identifiability/observability conditions and diagnostics for success; how stability of A is enforced; sensitivity to diagonal-R and per-session gains; runtimes and scalability; comparison of stitched predictions to empirical correlations.

- Suggested improvements: add regularization (L1/sparsity or low-rank+ sparse), per-session affine calibration and correlated observation noise, identifiability vs overlap study, validation on real sequential datasets, and additional baseline comparisons.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important problem with a principled, empirically supported approach that can expand population analyses.

- Arguments against acceptance: lacks validation on true sequential recordings, limited treatment of identifiability/stability/noise structure, and is methodologically incremental.

- Overall: thoughtful and useful contribution that would be much stronger with additional robustness analyses and demonstration on real multi-session data.
