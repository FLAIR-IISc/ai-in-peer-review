This paper presents a novel statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, partially overlapping subsets of that population. The core idea is to model the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS), where each recording session corresponds to a partial, noisy observation of the full system state. By fitting this model using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm across all recording sessions, the authors show that it is possible to "stitch" the partial views together. This allows them to infer the functional connectivity and predict noise correlations even between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This work builds on a rich literature of inferring functional connectivity from neural data but is, to my knowledge, the first to provide a practical and effective solution for combining non-simultaneous recordings of this type into a single dynamical model.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Originality:** The problem addressed is of great importance to experimental neuroscience. The field of view of techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging is a fundamental bottleneck, limiting simultaneous observation to hundreds of neurons while circuits of interest can comprise thousands. This paper offers a principled and elegant computational solution to partially overcome this physical limitation. The approach of framing this "stitching" problem as inference in a latent dynamical system with block-structured missing observations is novel and powerful. It moves beyond previous work that either required simultaneous recordings or made strong assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Validation:** The authors provide a very thorough evaluation of their method on both simulated and real neural data. The use of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is particularly clever. By splitting a fully observed dataset into two fictional, partially overlapping sessions, they create a scenario where a ground-truth performance benchmark (a model trained on the fully observed data) is available. This allows for a clear and quantitative comparison against both an achievable upper bound and a "naive" lower-bound model. This experimental design is a major strength of the paper.

3.  **Impressive Results:** The results strongly support the paper's claims. On both simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets (from anesthetized and awake mice), the "stitching" method successfully predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The performance, as measured by the correlation between predicted and "true" correlation matrices, is remarkably close to the upper bound achieved by a model with access to all data simultaneously (e.g., correlation of 0.84 for the stitched model vs. 0.15 for the naive model in Fig 3d). This is a compelling demonstration of the method's practical utility.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The method is explained clearly, building on the standard LDS framework. The motivation is well-established, and the results are presented logically and effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Limitations of the Linear-Gaussian Model:** The LDS model is a significant simplification of true neural dynamics, which are nonlinear and produce non-Gaussian spike trains. The authors rightly state that their model is a statistical description and that it works well for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), which is the paper's main goal. However, the paper would be stronger if it discussed these limitations more explicitly. The success of the LDS may be partly due to the slow, low-pass filtered nature of the GCaMP6s calcium signals. It would be valuable to discuss how the model might perform on direct, high-temporal-resolution spiking data and to speculate on how the framework could be extended to more realistic nonlinear or point-process models in the future.

2.  **Importance of Overlapping Neurons:** A critical detail is mentioned in passing: the authors "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This suggests that having "anchor" neurons observed across sessions is crucial for aligning the latent state spaces and making the stitching successful. This is a key finding for experimental design. I would strongly encourage the authors to expand on this point. A systematic analysis showing how stitching performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases would be extremely valuable for neuroscientists wishing to apply this method. This would help answer the practical question of how to best allocate a fixed experimental time budget between covering new neurons and re-imaging old ones.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The introduction motivates the work by referring to circuits with "thousands of neurons." While the method is demonstrated on populations of up to 100 neurons, the computational complexity of the Kalman smoother in the E-step (O(N^3)) may become prohibitive for much larger N. A brief discussion of the computational costs and the practical limits on population size for the current implementation would be helpful.

4.  **Parameter vs. Correlation Recovery:** The results show an interesting divergence: the model recovers pairwise correlations very well, but the recovery of the underlying coupling parameters (`A` matrix) is less accurate and degrades with population size. This is an important result that deserves more discussion. It may suggest that the parameter space has near-degeneracies for large systems, but that the overall dynamics (and thus the emergent correlations) are nonetheless well-constrained by the data. Highlighting this robustness is a potential strength of the method.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that addresses a significant and timely problem in computational neuroscience. The proposed method is novel, the validation is rigorous and convincing, and the results are impressive. It provides a valuable tool that could substantially expand the scale at which neural circuit dynamics can be studied. The weaknesses identified are largely avenues for future work or points that could be clarified to strengthen the paper further. I strongly recommend acceptance.
