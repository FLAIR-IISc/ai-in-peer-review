Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses a timely challenge in systems neuroscience: how to infer population dynamics and correlated variability when the full circuit cannot be recorded simultaneously. The core contribution is a “stitching” framework that models multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same circuit as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Parameters are learned with EM (including an online variant), using known correspondences for overlapping cells across sessions. On simulations and two 2-photon datasets from mouse barrel cortex (anesthetized and awake), the approach predicts noise correlations between neuron pairs not recorded simultaneously, substantially outperforming a naïve baseline (separate LDS fits per session) and approaching a “fully observed” upper bound.

This work is related to latent-variable approaches developed for neural populations (e.g., GLM- or LDS-based models; Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008; Macke et al., NIPS 2012; Vidne et al., JCN 2011), connectivity inference from calcium imaging (Mishchenko et al., AOAS 2011), and subspace/system identification methods (e.g., Katayama). The novelty here is not the LDS per se, but the formulation and empirical demonstration that multiple, partially overlapping sessions can be integrated to recover cross-session correlations and cross-couplings, a capability not addressed by typical single-session methods or by prior subsampling work that makes stronger assumptions (e.g., monotone mapping from stimulus to noise correlations).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The LDS with missing observations is a principled and tractable formulation. The EM procedure and observation model are standard; details of the M-step for R and offsets are provided. The intuition and identifiability discussion (alignment of latent subspaces across sessions via overlap) are reasonable, although primarily heuristic.
- Empirical support: Simulations demonstrate recovery of cross-couplings and strong prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs. On real data, the stitched model reliably predicts cross-session correlations better than the naïve baseline and near the “fully observed” model. Activity prediction of unobserved neurons given observed ones is quantitatively assessed.
- Limitations in validation: On real datasets, correlation predictions are compared to a fully observed LDS rather than directly to empirical correlations computed from held-out simultaneous test data. Since simultaneous measurements exist in the fictional scenario, this direct comparison would provide a more objective ground truth and quantify model mis-specification.

Clarity:
The model, assumptions, and fitting procedure are clearly described, with helpful notation and an explicit observation mask Ck. The experimental design (fictional split with overlap, held-out test) is explained. Minor issues: the observation equation has a typographical ambiguity (“d + t” vs additive noise); assumptions on Q and R being diagonal are stated but could be emphasized earlier as modeling choices.

Originality:
The stitching formulation for multi-session, partially overlapping recordings and the demonstration that it can predict noise correlations across non-simultaneous neuron pairs are novel and practically important for current 2-photon workflows. While LDS/EM are standard, the paper contributes a new application and evaluation protocol, showing that overlap across sessions can resolve latent subspace ambiguities to recover cross-couplings.

Significance:
The approach provides a path to expand effective population sizes beyond the simultaneously imaged set, directly addressing a bottleneck in circuit-level studies. Predicting noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs is valuable both for basic science and for designing future experiments. The method is simple, scalable to O(10^2) neurons in practice, and compatible with standard pipelines. Demonstrating scalability and robustness for larger populations and more sessions would further enhance impact.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and practical problem arising in modern calcium imaging.
- Clear, principled formulation as an LDS with missing observations; efficient EM implementation.
- Strong performance on simulations and two real datasets; meaningful metrics (correlations, cross-prediction).
- Careful positioning relative to prior work, including discussion of limitations and potential extensions.

Weaknesses and suggestions (essential):
1) Ground-truth evaluation on real data: In the fictional split, directly compare predicted pairwise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs to empirical correlations computed on a held-out simultaneous test segment (with stimulus effects regressed out consistently). This avoids dependence on a “fully observed model” as an upper bound and clarifies absolute, rather than relative, accuracy.
2) Overlap dependence and identifiability: Provide a systematic analysis of performance as a function of overlap fraction and session count (e.g., 0–60% overlap), including failure modes at zero overlap. A brief statement of identifiability conditions (observability with masked outputs; subspace alignment) or a citation to system-ID/missing-data results would strengthen the theoretical footing.
3) Baselines: Include stronger baselines beyond “naïve,” such as:
   - Low-dimensional shared latent models fit across sessions (e.g., GPFA/FA-LDS) with alignment via overlapping units.
   - Subspace identification methods handling missing outputs.
This will contextualize when a full-N LDS is preferable to lower-rank approaches.

Weaknesses and suggestions (optional but beneficial):
- Regularization: Given the large number of parameters (A is N×N), include sparsity or structured priors (e.g., L1, low-rank plus sparse) and report effects on coupling and correlation recovery; this was mentioned but not shown.
- Noise and measurement model: Explore modeling correlated measurement noise or explicit calcium dynamics/deconvolution; at minimum, report sensitivity to the diagonal-Q/R assumptions.
- Robustness: Assess sensitivity to cell mis-registration and ROI selection (including neuropil contamination), which is relevant in practice.
- Scalability: Report runtime/memory and complexity with N and number of sessions; add simulations at larger N and >2 sessions to support claims about thousands of neurons.
- Notation and reproducibility: Clarify the observation equation and publish code to facilitate adoption.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and practically impactful formulation enabling inference across non-simultaneous recordings.
- Clear method with convincing simulation and real-data demonstrations.
- Addresses a pressing need in population neuroscience; likely to be adopted and built upon.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Validation on real data relies on a model-based upper bound rather than direct empirical ground truth.
- Limited baseline comparisons; identifiability and overlap requirements not systematically characterized.
- Scalability to the intended regime (thousands of neurons, multiple sessions) not yet demonstrated.

Overall, the work presents a compelling and well-executed step toward integrating multi-session imaging data to infer population dynamics. With the essential additions on evaluation and overlap analysis, it would make a strong contribution.
