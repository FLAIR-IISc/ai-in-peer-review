

This paper presents a novel statistical method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural populations into a single coherent model, addressing a significant limitation in current neural recording technology. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, demonstrating that their approach can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons in mouse somatosensory cortex. The core idea is that by leveraging the strong coupling between nearby neurons in a neural circuit, they can combine data from different imaging sessions to infer population dynamics beyond the immediate field of view.

The paper is well-motivated by the current experimental reality where 2-photon calcium imaging can capture hundreds of neurons simultaneously, but cortical circuits may involve thousands of neurons. The authors clearly position their work against related literature, noting that while other methods address functional connectivity from simultaneous recordings or sub-sampled observations, none solve the specific problem of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings. The comparison to the "naive" approach (averaging parameters from separate sessions) and the "fully observed" model (theoretical upper bound) is particularly effective for demonstrating their method's advantages.

The technical contribution is sound, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system that is computationally tractable while capturing the essential dynamics. The authors provide sufficient detail on the model, including the EM algorithm implementation with Kalman smoothing. Their evaluation on both simulated data (where ground truth is available) and real experimental data from anesthetized and awake mice is comprehensive, with multiple metrics (coupling matrix recovery, noise correlation prediction, activity prediction) across different population sizes.

Strengths of the paper include:
- Clear articulation of the problem and its significance for neuroscience
- Rigorous evaluation framework with appropriate baselines
- Demonstrated applicability to real experimental data from two different conditions (anesthetized vs. awake)
- Careful discussion of limitations and potential extensions (e.g., the need for some overlap between imaging sessions)
- Good integration of related work and clear distinction from previous methods

Potential weaknesses to consider:
1. The paper focuses on a linear Gaussian model, which is a simplification of the complex nonlinear dynamics of neural systems. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it would strengthen the paper to provide more specific discussion about how their method might be extended to more complex models (e.g., incorporating nonlinearities or spike-related dynamics) without significantly increasing computational complexity.

2. The paper states that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work," but doesn't provide sufficient guidance on how much overlap is needed or how to determine optimal overlap in experimental design. This is a practical consideration for neuroscientists planning experiments, so more concrete recommendations would be valuable.

3. While the results are impressive for predicting correlations, the paper doesn't fully explore the potential limitations when the underlying assumptions of the model (e.g., linear dynamics, Gaussian noise) are violated. It would be useful to see a brief analysis of how the method performs under more challenging conditions.

The significance of this work is substantial. By enabling researchers to effectively expand the population sizes they can study, this method has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of neural circuit dynamics. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly valuable for studying neural computation, as noise correlations can reveal important aspects of circuit function.

The paper is well-written and clear, with good organization that guides the reader through the problem, method, evaluation, and results. The figures are well-designed and effectively communicate the key findings. The authors have addressed the relevant prior work thoroughly and have made a strong case for the novelty of their approach.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It represents a significant methodological contribution to the neuroscience community that addresses a genuine experimental limitation. The approach is technically sound, well-evaluated, and has clear potential for broad impact. The method is immediately applicable to current experimental practices in neuroscience, and the authors have demonstrated its value through both simulations and real data analysis. While there are opportunities for future work (particularly in extending the model to capture more complex dynamics), the current work is a substantial contribution that meets the quality, clarity, originality, and significance criteria for NIPS.
