This paper tackles an important experimental bottleneck in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics when only partial subsets of neurons can be imaged at a time. The authors cast “stitching” multiple, partially overlapping two-photon recordings as learning a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations, trained by EM with Kalman smoothing. They claim (i) accurate recovery of functional couplings and, more robustly, (ii) accurate prediction of noise correlations between neuron pairs never observed simultaneously, and (iii) useful cross-prediction of unobserved activity. Simulations support identifiability up to the expected linear transforms, and experiments on mouse S1 (awake/anesthetized) using a fictional split show performance close to a fully observed upper bound and far above a naive baseline. Relative to prior NIPS work on PLDS/GPFA and partially observed GLMs, the novelty is the explicit cross-session stitching using shared cells to resolve latent indeterminacies.

Quality: The model and estimation are technically sound and appropriate; the evaluation is carefully bounded with upper/lower references. However, real-data validation relies on artificial session splits, leaving open robustness to true cross-session nonstationarities (drift, photobleaching, behavioral changes). Conditions for successful stitching (overlap size, observability/stability) are discussed qualitatively but not quantified.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized; a few notational hiccups aside, the method is reproducible at the LDS level, though initialization, regularization, and model selection details could be expanded.

Originality: While LDS with missing data is standard, applying it to stitch non-simultaneous neural recordings and to predict cross-session noise correlations is a timely and useful contribution.

Significance: High practical value for imaging labs and for building larger-scale, data-driven circuit models.

Pros (arguments for acceptance):
- Addresses a real and common limitation with a simple, scalable tool.
- Strong empirical gains on both simulations and real data versus a reasonable baseline.
- Clear link to theory and to prior NIPS literature, with a novel application.

Cons (arguments against acceptance):
- No evaluation on genuinely sequential recordings; sensitivity to nonstationarity and registration errors untested.
- Dependence on overlap is acknowledged but not systematically characterized.
- Limited comparisons (e.g., GPFA/PLDS with shared latents, deconvolved spikes, non-Gaussian observations); scalability to thousands not profiled.

Constructive suggestions:
- Validate on truly sequential datasets (or interleaved scans) and quantify robustness to drift and misalignment.
- Systematically vary and report performance versus overlap size and spectral radius constraints on A.
- Compare to PLDS/GPFA with shared latents across sessions and to spike-based observation models.
- Report runtime/memory and release code to enhance impact.

Overall: A promising, practically useful contribution; I lean toward acceptance contingent on strengthened real-data validation and baseline comparisons.
