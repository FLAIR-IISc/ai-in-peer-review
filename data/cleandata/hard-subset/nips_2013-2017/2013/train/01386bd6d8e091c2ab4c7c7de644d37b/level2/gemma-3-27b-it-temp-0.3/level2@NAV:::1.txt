## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses the important problem of extending the scope of population dynamics analysis beyond the limits of simultaneous recording capabilities. The authors propose a statistical method, framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, to “stitch” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. This is a compelling approach, particularly relevant given the increasing use of 2-photon calcium imaging and the limitations in simultaneously recording from large neural populations. The method is well-motivated by the context of cortical barrel circuits, where strong local coupling is expected.

The core strength of the paper lies in its clear presentation of a statistically sound method and its demonstration of efficacy on both simulated and real data. The simulation results convincingly show the method’s ability to recover coupling matrices and, importantly, predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. Applying the method to mouse somatosensory cortex data further validates its potential, with performance approaching that of a “fully observed” model – a crucial benchmark. The comparison to a naive approach highlights the value of incorporating information across imaging sessions. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of limitations. While the authors acknowledge the linear Gaussian assumption, a more detailed exploration of its potential impact on the results would be valuable. For example, how might non-linearities in neural dynamics affect the accuracy of correlation predictions? Additionally, the sensitivity of the method to the degree of overlap between imaging sessions could be further investigated. The statement that some overlap is “critical” warrants more quantitative analysis. 

Regarding originality, the approach builds on established techniques (latent dynamical systems, EM algorithm) but cleverly adapts them to a novel problem. The key contribution is the specific application to stitching together partial recordings and demonstrating its effectiveness in predicting correlations. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but could benefit from briefly mentioning recent advances in methods for dealing with noisy or incomplete calcium imaging data beyond those explicitly cited.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential. The method presented is a valuable contribution to the field, offering a practical solution to a common challenge in systems neuroscience. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the limitations outlined above.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practical problem in neural data analysis.
*   Statistically sound method with clear theoretical justification.
*   Convincing results on both simulated and real data.
*   Demonstrates the ability to predict noise correlations, a key aspect of population dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the impact of the linear Gaussian assumption.
*   Insufficient quantitative analysis of the sensitivity to overlap between imaging sessions.
*   Related work section could be slightly more comprehensive.
