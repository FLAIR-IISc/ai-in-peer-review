This paper addresses the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from multiple, sequentially recorded subsets of neurons. The motivation is compelling: current imaging technology limits the number of simultaneously recorded neurons, while computations likely involve larger circuits. The paper proposes a statistical method based on fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations to “stitch” together these partial recordings, enabling predictions of correlations between neurons never simultaneously observed. The work is clearly written and well-motivated, and the experimental validation using calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex is a significant strength.

The core idea of formulating the problem as a latent dynamical system is sound and builds upon existing work in state-space modeling of neural activity. The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a reasonable starting point for tractability, and the paper acknowledges the limitations of this simplification. The connection to previous work is relatively well-covered, mentioning relevant studies on functional connectivity and dealing with subsampled data, and clearly delineating where this work differs (i.e., predicting correlations between *non*-simultaneously observed neurons). The experiments, both simulated and on real data, are well-designed and the results presented are convincing. The performance gains over a naive approach are clearly demonstrated, and the comparison to a fully observed model provides a useful benchmark.

However, some areas could be strengthened. While the paper mentions the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption, it would be valuable to briefly discuss how extensions to nonlinear models might address these limitations. Furthermore, the choice of the linear Gaussian model is presented as being primarily for computational tractability. A more in-depth discussion of why this model performs *well* despite its simplicity, particularly concerning the underlying assumptions about neural circuit dynamics, would be beneficial. 

Regarding the experimental details, while the description of the calcium imaging data is adequate, providing more specific details about the imaging setup (e.g., laser power, objective magnification) and data preprocessing steps (e.g., denoising, drift correction) would enhance reproducibility. It’s also not entirely clear how the “fictional imaging scenario” was constructed and why the 60% overlap was chosen – a justification for this choice would be helpful.  The statement that the naive approach overestimates correlations due to biases from averaging couplings could benefit from further explanation.

The paper demonstrates impressive performance, but the impact could be greater with a clearer discussion of potential challenges and future directions. For example, how does the performance of the method degrade with increasing temporal correlations in the underlying neural activity? What are the implications of model misspecification? Exploring these questions would further solidify the contribution of this work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is important and relevant to current challenges in neuroscience.
*   The proposed method is novel and provides a practical solution for expanding the population sizes accessible to analysis.
*   The experimental validation using real calcium imaging data is convincing and demonstrates the effectiveness of the approach.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a limitation, and a more thorough discussion of potential extensions is needed.
*   Some experimental details could be more comprehensive to facilitate reproducibility.
*   A deeper exploration of the limitations and potential failure modes of the method would strengthen the paper.



Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential to impact the field of neural population dynamics. Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further enhance its quality and increase its impact.  The proposed method is a valuable contribution and warrants acceptance.
