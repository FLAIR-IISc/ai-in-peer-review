

The paper introduces a method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons into a single model by framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. This approach addresses a critical limitation in neuroscience where current imaging techniques can only capture a small fraction of neurons in a neural circuit (e.g., hundreds out of thousands in cortical barrels), yet the goal is to characterize dynamics across the entire circuit.

The core contribution is a statistical framework that predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs by combining multiple partial observations. The authors model the problem using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, which can be fit using an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing in the E-step. The method allows researchers to effectively expand the population size for which dynamics can be characterized beyond what is possible with simultaneous imaging.

The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a strong emphasis on both theoretical motivation and practical validation. The authors demonstrate their method using both simulated data (where ground truth is available) and real experimental data from mouse somatosensory cortex. They establish a rigorous benchmark by comparing their method to a naive approach (which simply averages parameters across sessions) and an upper bound (a fully observed model trained on all neurons simultaneously). In simulated data, the stitching method achieved correlation coefficients of 0.92 between predicted and true noise correlations, while the naive method could not predict these correlations at all. In real experimental data, the stitching method achieved correlation coefficients of 0.84 with the fully observed model, compared to 0.15 for the naive method.

The significance of this work is substantial. Current imaging technology limits the size of neural populations that can be simultaneously recorded, but this method effectively expands the population size that can be studied by leveraging sequential recordings. This could enable more comprehensive studies of neural circuits that are too large to be imaged simultaneously, advancing our understanding of neural computation. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly valuable, as these correlations are critical for understanding population dynamics.

The method is technically sound, with appropriate statistical foundations and careful experimental design. The authors acknowledge limitations of the current approach (e.g., using a simple LDS model rather than more complex ones) and provide clear suggestions for future work. They also note that some overlap between imaging sessions is critical for stitching to work, which is an important consideration for experimental design.

Strengths include the clear articulation of the problem and solution, the rigorous evaluation with both simulated and real data, the appropriate comparison to baselines, and the practical significance of the contribution. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results.

Weaknesses include limited exploration of how the method performs with different levels of overlap between imaging sessions (beyond noting that some overlap is necessary), and insufficient discussion of the computational complexity of the method as population sizes grow. The authors could also provide more specific guidance on when the simple LDS model is sufficient versus when more complex models would be needed.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to computational neuroscience by providing a practical solution to a major experimental limitation. The method is novel in its application to stitching together non-simultaneous recordings, and the results demonstrate clear utility for real experimental data. The authors have thoroughly validated their approach, and the paper meets all the criteria for a strong NIPS submission.

The paper's main limitation is the use of a relatively simple model, but the authors appropriately acknowledge this and suggest avenues for future work. The significance of overcoming the population size limitation in neural imaging is high, and the method has clear potential for immediate application in neuroscience research. The paper is well-positioned to advance the field by enabling more comprehensive studies of neural circuits that are currently beyond the reach of simultaneous imaging techniques.
