## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses the important problem of extending the scope of population dynamics analysis beyond the limits of simultaneous recording capabilities. The authors propose a statistical method – framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations – to “stitch” together sequential recordings of neurons within a cortical circuit, specifically in the mouse somatosensory cortex. The core idea is sound and addresses a clear bottleneck in current neuroscience research; moving beyond hundreds to thousands of simultaneously recorded neurons is challenging, and this work provides a compelling approach to bridge that gap.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear motivation, well-defined problem statement, and a sensible methodological approach. The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system, while simple, provides a tractable starting point and allows for a rigorous statistical framework. The validation through both simulated data and real experimental recordings is commendable. The simulation demonstrates the method’s ability to recover underlying coupling matrices and noise correlations, even for non-simultaneously recorded neurons. Importantly, the application to real data shows promising results, predicting correlations that align with a ‘fully observed’ benchmark and substantially outperform a naive approach. 

However, the paper isn't without weaknesses. While the authors acknowledge limitations, a deeper discussion of the assumptions inherent in the linear Gaussian model would be beneficial. The reliance on this model limits the applicability to circuits exhibiting strong non-linear dynamics. The sensitivity of the results to the overlap between the imaging sessions could also be explored further. The comparison to existing methods ([12, 13, 14, 15]) feels somewhat cursory; a more detailed discussion of why those methods *specifically* fall short in this context would strengthen the argument for the proposed approach. Reproducibility is hampered by a lack of publicly available code.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
* Addresses a significant challenge in population neuroscience.
* Presents a statistically principled and well-motivated method.
* Demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real data.
* Offers a potential path towards analyzing larger, more complete neural circuits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
* Relies on a simplifying linear Gaussian model.
* Limited exploration of the impact of model assumptions.
* Lack of publicly available code hinders reproducibility.
* Comparison to related work could be more in-depth.

**Overall:** Despite some limitations, this paper presents a valuable contribution. The proposed method offers a practical and promising approach to leveraging existing imaging technologies to study larger neural circuits. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically a more thorough discussion of model assumptions and a stronger emphasis on reproducibility by making code available.
