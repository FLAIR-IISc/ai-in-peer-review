Summary and contributions
The paper tackles an important practical limitation in large-scale neural population recording: only a fraction of neurons in a local circuit can be imaged simultaneously. The authors propose “stitching” multiple partially overlapping imaging sessions into a single model by fitting a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. The state dimension equals the number of neurons, and the observation matrices select the subset imaged in each session; parameters are learned by EM (with a mini-batch online variant). The key claimed contribution is the ability to infer cross-couplings and predict noise correlations between neurons that were not observed simultaneously. The approach is validated on simulations and on two 2-photon datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1), using a “fictional imaging scenario” in which simultaneously imaged data are temporally partitioned into two sessions with some overlap, and the stitched model is compared to a “fully observed” LDS upper bound and a “naive” lower bound that fits sessions separately.

Relation to prior work
The paper situates itself among methods for inferring functional connectivity from imaging and electrophysiology (e.g., GLMs, penalized likelihood, information-theoretic approaches) and among latent variable models for partially observed populations. The LDS with missing data and EM/Kalman smoothing are classical; the novelty lies in the application to stitching non-simultaneous populations with known cell identity and in demonstrating prediction of noise correlations across sessions. Some relevant latent factor approaches (e.g., GPFA/Yu et al. 2009; subspace identification with missing data) and state-space calcium deconvolution (Vogelstein et al. 2009) could be discussed more thoroughly, as they offer alternative ways to capture shared variability and may inform modeling choices (e.g., low-dimensional common input).

Quality and correctness
- Modeling: The LDS assumptions (linear dynamics on fluorescence, diagonal process noise, Gaussian measurement noise) are simple but reasonable as a first pass. With diagonal innovations, correlated variability arises via A; instantaneous correlated noise is not modeled. The authors note possible extensions (shared latent inputs), which would be important in practice.
- Identifiability and necessity of overlap: The simulation demonstrates that cross-couplings can be recovered without overlap when neurons are re-identifiable across sessions. In real data, performance degrades without overlap. This is an important empirical caveat, and a more explicit discussion of identifiability conditions (e.g., stability of A, observability/controllability with partial measurements, stimulus richness, degree of overlap) would strengthen the paper.
- Evaluation: The simulation analyses are sound. For real data, the “fictional imaging scenario” is a pragmatic way to create ground truth for benchmarking, and the use of a fully observed LDS as an upper bound is reasonable. It would be even stronger to compare reconstructed noise correlations to the empirical correlations on held-out data (not only to those predicted by a fully observed LDS), and to demonstrate stitching across truly separate imaging fields/sessions with minimal overlap to support the main claim.
- Scalability and overfitting: The method fits a full N×N A; no explicit regularization is used (sparsity priors are mentioned but not reported). With N up to ~100–200, this is tractable, but the paper aspires to thousands of neurons. Complexity and memory requirements of smoothing and EM with missing data should be discussed, along with regularization needed to avoid overfitting. The observed drop in coupling estimation accuracy with population size suggests near-degeneracy.
- Practicalities: Stability constraints on A (spectral radius < 1) and how stationarity is enforced during EM should be made explicit; otherwise the Lyapunov initialization may be ill-posed. The decision to work directly on fluorescence is convenient, but calcium dynamics convolve spikes and may inflate apparent temporal correlations; at least a control using deconvolved activity or an LDS that includes calcium filtering would increase confidence.

Clarity
The manuscript is generally clear and well organized. The notation around the observation model (Equation 2) has minor typographical glitches. More detail on initialization, convergence diagnostics, and runtime would help reproducibility. The description of the naive baseline could be clarified (e.g., how averaging across overlap induces bias).

Originality
While the LDS/EM machinery is standard, the specific stitching application to sequentially imaged, partially overlapping neural populations and the demonstration that noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs can be predicted are novel and timely. The framing as missing-data LDS with known selection matrices is simple and elegant.

Significance
The problem is important for systems neuroscience, and the ability to expand effective population size beyond simultaneous imaging limits is valuable. The empirical results on two datasets show promising performance for correlation prediction and cross-prediction. The current validation uses synthetic splits of simultaneous data; demonstrating performance on genuine multi-session recordings and addressing scalability/regularization would considerably increase impact.

Strengths
- Clear, simple probabilistic formulation; uses well-understood inference.
- Convincing simulations with ground truth; strong correlation recovery for unobserved pairs.
- Real-data benchmarks on awake and anesthetized S1; robust correlation prediction approaching fully observed upper bound.
- Practical insight that some overlap is helpful/necessary; discussion of experimental design trade-offs.

Weaknesses
- Validation relies on “fictional” splits; no demonstration on truly separate sessions/fields.
- Lack of explicit theoretical conditions for when stitching works (role of overlap, stimulus richness, stability/observability).
- Scalability and regularization are underdeveloped; full A estimation may not scale to thousands of neurons.
- Modeling choices (diagonal Q, no calcium dynamics) may limit fidelity; potential confounds (slow drifts/brain state) not controlled.
- Limited baselines; e.g., low-dimensional latent factor models (GPFA), covariance completion, or LDS with shared latent inputs.

Questions for the authors
- Do you enforce stability of A during EM? How often does EM yield unstable A, and how is this handled?
- How sensitive is performance to the amount and identity of overlap? Can you quantify the trade-off between overlap and coverage?
- How does a low-dimensional latent LDS (common inputs) compare to full-state LDS for correlation prediction?
- Can you report correlation recovery relative to empirical held-out correlations, not only vs the fully observed LDS?
- What are the computational costs for N≈200 vs N≈1000? Any structure (sparsity/low-rank) exploited?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on truly separate sessions (ideally with minimal overlap), or separate fields within a session, to validate stitching in realistic conditions.
- Include regularization on A (e.g., sparsity or stability-promoting priors) and report its effects; discuss computational scaling.
- Consider modeling calcium dynamics or using deconvolved spike trains as a control.
- Provide a brief analysis of identifiability/observability with partial measurements and overlap, and practical guidelines for experimental design.
- Compare against a low-dimensional latent factor LDS/GPFA baseline and, if feasible, simple covariance imputation baselines.
- Clarify notation and add details on initialization, convergence, and code availability.

Overall: A timely and potentially impactful contribution with a clear formulation and promising results, but in need of stronger validation on real multi-session data and a more thorough treatment of scalability and modeling assumptions.
