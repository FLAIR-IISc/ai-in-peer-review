- Summary: Proposes stitching partially overlapping imaging sessions by fitting an LDS with missing observations to infer cross-couplings and predict noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
- Method: State dimension equals neuron count; observation matrices select imaged subsets; parameters learned by EM with a mini-batch online variant.
- Validation: Tested on simulations and two 2-photon datasets using synthetic splits into overlapping sessions, compared to fully observed LDS (upper bound) and separately fitted sessions (naive lower bound).

- Relation to prior work: Positions itself among functional connectivity and latent-variable methods; novelty is applying missing-data LDS to stitching with known cell identities.
- Prior work gaps: Could discuss GPFA, subspace ID with missing data, and calcium deconvolution approaches more thoroughly as alternative baselines or modeling choices.

- Modeling correctness: LDS with diagonal process noise and Gaussian measurement noise is a reasonable first pass, but instantaneous correlated noise and calcium dynamics are not modeled.
- Identifiability & overlap: Simulations show cross-couplings recoverable without overlap if cells are re-identifiable, but real-data performance degrades without overlap; identifiability conditions need clearer discussion.
- Evaluation: Simulation design is sound and the fictional split pragmatic, but stronger validation would compare reconstructed noise correlations to empirical held-out correlations and to truly separate sessions.
- Scalability & overfitting: Full N×N A estimation lacks explicit regularization; scalability to thousands of neurons, memory and smoothing costs, and overfitting risks need discussion.
- Practicalities: Need explicit handling of stability constraints on A, stationarity enforcement during EM, and consideration of calcium filtering or deconvolution effects.

- Clarity: Manuscript is generally clear; minor notation glitches and missing details on initialization, convergence diagnostics, and runtime hinder reproducibility.
- Originality: Using missing-data LDS to stitch sequentially imaged populations and predict correlations for non-simultaneous pairs is a simple, elegant, and novel application.
- Significance: Addresses an important neuroscience limitation and could expand effective population size, but impact depends on validation on real multi-session data and improvements in scalability.

- Strength: Clear probabilistic formulation leveraging well-understood inference techniques.
- Strength: Convincing simulations showing ground-truth recovery of cross-couplings.
- Strength: Real-data benchmarks show robust correlation prediction approaching fully observed upper bound.
- Strength: Practical insight that some overlap aids stitching and informs experimental design.

- Weakness: Validation relies on synthetic (“fictional”) splits rather than truly separate sessions or fields.
- Weakness: Lacks theoretical conditions for stitching success (overlap, stimulus richness, observability).
- Weakness: Scalability and regularization for large N are underdeveloped; full A may not scale.
- Weakness: Simplifying modeling choices (diagonal Q, no calcium dynamics) may limit fidelity and confound interpretation.
- Weakness: Limited comparison to alternative baselines like GPFA, covariance completion, or shared-latent LDS.

- Question: Do you enforce stability of A during EM, and how do you handle unstable iterations?
- Question: How sensitive is stitching performance to amount and identity of overlap; can you quantify the overlap–coverage trade-off?
- Question: How does a low-dimensional latent LDS (shared inputs) compare to the full-state LDS for correlation prediction?
- Question: Can you report correlation recovery relative to empirical held-out correlations, not just the fully observed LDS?
- Question: What are computational costs and memory requirements for N≈200 versus N≈1000, and is any structure exploited?

- Suggestion: Add experiments on truly separate sessions or separate fields to validate stitching under realistic conditions.
- Suggestion: Incorporate regularization on A (sparsity, stability priors) and report effects on performance and scaling.
- Suggestion: Model calcium dynamics or use deconvolved spikes as a control to assess temporal confounds.
- Suggestion: Provide an analysis of identifiability/observability with partial measurements and practical experimental guidelines.
- Suggestion: Compare against low-dimensional latent factor methods (GPFA) and simple covariance-imputation baselines.
- Suggestion: Clarify notation, and add details on initialization, convergence criteria, runtime, and code availability.

- Overall: Timely and potentially impactful work with a clear formulation and promising results, but needs stronger real multi-session validation, scalability/regularization treatment, and broader baseline comparisons.
