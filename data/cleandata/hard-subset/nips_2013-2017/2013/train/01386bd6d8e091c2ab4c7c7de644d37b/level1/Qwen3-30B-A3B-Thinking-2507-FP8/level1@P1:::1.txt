

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a neural circuit due to experimental limitations. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit by modeling the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The key insight is that by recording from overlapping subsets of neurons across multiple sessions, one can infer the joint dynamics of the full population, including noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors demonstrate their approach using both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their method substantially outperforms naive approaches and approaches the performance of a hypothetical "fully observed" model.

The work builds on previous research in neural population dynamics (e.g., [6,7,8,9,10,11]) but addresses a specific gap: the inability of existing methods to infer functional connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from related work on sub-sampled observations ([12,13,14,15]), emphasizing that their method leverages the temporal dynamics of neural activity rather than assuming monotonic relationships between stimulus and noise correlations.

## Quality

The paper presents a technically sound approach that is well-supported by both theoretical justification and empirical validation. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the latent dynamical system model with missing observations, and the implementation details are sufficiently detailed for replication. Their experimental validation is comprehensive, including both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real neural data (where they establish meaningful baselines by creating a "fictional imaging scenario" that allows comparison against a hypothetical "fully observed" model). The comparison against the naive approach (which sets couplings between non-simultaneously recorded pairs to zero) is particularly valuable for demonstrating the limitations of current practice.

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, including the requirement for some overlap between imaging sessions (60% in their experiments) and the potential for parameter degeneracy in larger systems. They also appropriately note that while their linear Gaussian model provides a statistical description, more complex models could potentially improve performance.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and its significance, the methods section is detailed without being overwhelming, and the results are presented with clear visualizations. The authors provide sufficient technical detail for experts to replicate their experiments, including the specific parameters used in their simulations and the exact experimental protocols for the calcium imaging data. The figures are well-designed and effectively communicate the key findings.

## Originality

The core contribution of this paper—using latent dynamical systems to stitch together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit—is novel. While latent dynamical systems have been used in neuroscience before ([13,17,18]), the specific application to combining non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit is new. The authors effectively contrast their approach with related work, demonstrating that previous methods for handling sub-sampled observations cannot address their specific problem. The paper's contribution represents a meaningful advance in how we can leverage current experimental capabilities to study larger neural circuits.

## Significance

This work addresses a critical bottleneck in systems neuroscience: the inability to record from large populations of neurons simultaneously. By enabling researchers to combine multiple partial recordings, the method significantly expands the population sizes for which dynamics can be characterized, potentially allowing for the study of circuits with thousands of neurons rather than hundreds. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly valuable, as noise correlations are known to be important for understanding neural computation. The authors demonstrate that their method works on real neural data, suggesting immediate applicability to ongoing experiments.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a significant, well-motivated problem in neuroscience
- Provides a theoretically sound and empirically validated solution
- Demonstrates clear improvement over naive approaches on both simulated and real data
- Well-written and well-organized with sufficient detail for replication
- Thoughtful discussion of limitations and future directions

**Weaknesses:**
- The linear Gaussian model might be too simplistic for complex neural dynamics, though the authors acknowledge this
- The requirement for substantial overlap (60%) between imaging sessions might limit applicability in some experimental designs
- The paper doesn't explore how the method would scale to much larger populations (thousands of neurons)
- Limited discussion of alternative approaches for handling missing data in time series

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of computational neuroscience and presents a method with clear practical value. It is technically sound, well-motivated, and addresses an important limitation in current experimental capabilities. The method has the potential to significantly impact how researchers analyze neural population data.

While the linear Gaussian model is a simplification, the authors demonstrate that it provides accurate predictions of noise correlations, which is the primary goal of the method. The requirement for some overlap between sessions is a reasonable constraint given the experimental realities, and the authors provide useful guidance on how to balance overlap with coverage.

The paper meets all four NIPS criteria: it is of high quality, clear, original, and significant. It represents a meaningful advance in our ability to analyze neural population dynamics with current experimental technology.

**Recommendation: Accept**
