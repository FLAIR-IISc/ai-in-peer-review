- Summary: Proposes "stitching" sequential partial 2p recordings into a single linear-Gaussian LDS with missing observations to recover joint dynamics and predict noise correlations across non-simultaneous neurons, learned via EM/Kalman smoothing.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on functional connectivity, latent GLMs/LDS, and partially observed system ID, but explicitly uses neuron identity and overlap to estimate cross-session couplings—distinct from methods assuming monotonic noise–stimulus relationships.

- Strength: Clear, principled formulation recasting stitching as LDS with missing data using well-understood inference.

- Strength: Simulations demonstrate recovery of cross-block couplings and good prediction of noise correlations and unobserved activity.

- Strength: Empirical validation on two calcium datasets with fictional splits shows stitched models outperform a naïve baseline and approach fully observed bounds.

- Strength: Practically relevant for 2p imaging where circuits exceed field of view, enabling larger coherent population models.

- Weakness: Validation relies mainly on fictional splits of simultaneous data and does not test real non-simultaneous session issues (drift, nonstationarity, photobleaching, calcium dynamics).

- Weakness: Model misspecification—assumes linear-Gaussian dynamics with diagonal Q and R so all noise correlations are mediated via A, likely violated in real calcium data.

- Weakness: Heavy dependence on overlap; performance degrades with no overlap and only robust at high overlap (c≈60%), with trade-offs not fully characterized.

- Weakness: Limited reproducibility/reporting—no predictive log-likelihood, cross-validation, detailed hyperparameters, or runtime/scalability for large N.

- Weakness: Identifiability and drift issues not formally analyzed; parameters only identifiable up to similarity transforms in unobserved subspaces.

- Quality: Technically sound and simulations well designed, but strongest claims are supported mainly under controlled splits; diagonal Q/R assumption may bias A and evaluation lacks likelihood-based metrics and stronger baselines.

- Clarity: Well written and organized, but some implementation details (initialization, convergence, regularization strength, complexity) are sparse; identifiability discussion would help.

- Originality: Moderately novel—explicitly leveraging neuron identity and overlap across sequential 2p recordings to estimate cross-subset couplings is a useful original contribution.

- Significance: Potentially impactful for systems neuroscience, but practical utility depends on robustness to session variability and realistic observation models.

- Questions for authors: How robust to slow drift in A/B/tuning? Sensitivity to diagonal Q/R assumption or low-rank shared noise? Minimal required overlap as function of N,T? Predictive log-likelihood comparisons with low-rank LDS? Runtimes/memory for N≈200–1000?

- Suggestions: Add stitching experiments on genuinely non-simultaneous recordings; explore sparsity/low-rank regularization and model selection; extend observation model to correlated noise or explicit calcium dynamics; analyze overlap vs coverage trade-offs.

- Pro acceptance: Addresses a timely practical problem with a principled, simple method, shows strong empirical gains, and is likely to stimulate further work and adoption.

- Against acceptance: Evidence limited to fictional splits, strong simplifying assumptions without robustness analysis, and limited discussion of scalability/identifiability.

- Overall recommendation: Borderline accept—idea is sound and promising but needs validation on non-simultaneous recordings and robustness analyses to be convincingly impactful.
