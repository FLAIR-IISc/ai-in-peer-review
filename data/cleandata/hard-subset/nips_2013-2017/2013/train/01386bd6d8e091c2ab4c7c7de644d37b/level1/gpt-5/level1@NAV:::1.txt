This paper proposes “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit by fitting a single latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, thereby inferring cross-population interactions and predicting noise correlations between neurons not imaged simultaneously. The approach uses a linear Gaussian state-space model over calcium signals with EM (plus online-EM) to learn shared dynamics (A, B, Q, R) across sessions with different selection matrices Ck. Validation includes simulations with known ground truth and two in vivo 2-photon datasets from mouse S1, where authors construct fictional splits to emulate non-simultaneous imaging and benchmark stitched LDS against fully observed and “naive” baselines. Results show accurate recovery of couplings in simulation and strong prediction of noise correlations for non-overlapping pairs (r ≈ 0.84 vs fully observed), as well as improved cross-prediction of unobserved activity.

Relation to prior work: The framing builds on LDS/EM and subspace identification [Baum & Petrie; Katayama], and complements latent-population approaches used at NIPS/NeurIPS for partially observed neural data (e.g., GLMs for populations [Pillow et al., Nature 2008], latent factor models/PLDS [Macke et al., NIPS 2012], GPFA [Yu et al., NIPS 2009]). It differs from connectivity inference from calcium [Mishchenko et al., 2011] by directly addressing non-simultaneous observations and from methods assuming monotone noise–stimulus correlation relationships [Wohrer et al., NIPS 2010].

Quality: The method is technically sound for the linear-Gaussian setting, with appropriate EM derivations and sensible benchmarking (upper/lower bounds). However, identifiability under partial observation and the role of overlap are discussed only intuitively; formal conditions and failure modes are not characterized. The reliance on a simplified measurement model (no explicit calcium/spiking or nonlinearities) may bias couplings.

Clarity: Generally clear and well organized; adding details on initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity), computational complexity, and sensitivity to overlap fraction would improve reproducibility. A figure/table summarizing performance vs. overlap would be helpful.

Originality: The key novelty is casting multi-session stitching as a single LDS with known selection matrices, enabling estimation of cross-session couplings. While LDS/EM is standard, this application and evaluation for predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs is fresh.

Significance: High practical impact for systems neuroscience, where sub-sampling is ubiquitous. Predicting cross-session noise correlations could influence experimental design.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, principled formulation with strong empirical gains over naive baselines.
- Addresses an important bottleneck in calcium imaging; likely to be adopted.
- Careful simulation and real-data studies with upper/lower bounds.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited validation on truly independent sessions; relies on fictional splits.
- Linear-Gaussian assumptions and lack of calcium/spike generative model.
- No formal identifiability/consistency guarantees; performance degrades with larger N.
- Missing comparisons to GPFA/PLDS or switching LDS baselines.

Overall: In scope for NeurIPS; a solid, useful contribution with room for theoretical and modeling extensions.
