- Proposes a statistical "stitching" method that fits a latent linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) to partial, non-simultaneous neural recordings to infer joint population dynamics.  
- Demonstrates method on simulated data and real calcium imaging from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.  
- Frames the problem as fitting an LDS with missing observations and uses EM for parameter estimation, providing sufficient math for reproducibility.  
- Relates to prior work on functional connectivity and sub-sampled observations but fills the gap of inferring dynamics across non-simultaneous neurons.  
- Technically sound approach built on well-established LDS framework and validated mathematically and empirically.  
- Experimental validation is thorough: synthetic ground-truth data plus two real experimental conditions (anesthetized and awake).  
- Provides meaningful benchmarks against a fully observed upper bound and a naive lower bound, showing clear performance gains.  
- Paper is well-organized, clearly written, and uses effective visualizations to communicate results.  
- Linear Gaussian assumption may be too simplistic for biological neural dynamics; extension to more realistic models is needed.  
- Does not thoroughly analyze sensitivity to the amount of overlap between recording sessions, despite overlap being critical for stitching.  
- Lacks empirical evidence demonstrating scalability beyond two recording sessions.  
- Comparison to related work is good but could be strengthened by addressing recent NIPS papers on missing-data neural dynamics.  
- Claims are well-supported by simulations and real-data analyses; the paper meets NIPS-quality criteria.  
- The methods and results are clearly presented, enabling replication.  
- Core contribution is original: using latent dynamical systems to stitch non-simultaneous neural populations is novel in this context.  
- Significant contribution for neuroscience: enables characterization of dynamics in much larger circuits and yields noise-correlation predictions approaching fully observed performance.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses an important problem, technically sound, well-validated, and clearly written.  
- Cons for acceptance: simplified model assumptions, limited sensitivity and scalability analyses.
