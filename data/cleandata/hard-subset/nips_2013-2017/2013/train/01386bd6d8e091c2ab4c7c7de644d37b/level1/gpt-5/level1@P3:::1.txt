Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a timely and practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations across neurons that are not recorded simultaneously. The authors cast “stitching” sequential two-photon imaging sessions as parameter estimation in a latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, using EM (with online mini-batch EM for efficiency and batch EM for fine-tuning). The state x_t represents fluorescence activity of N neurons; dynamics are linear Gaussian with stimulus drive (A,B,Q), and observations are masked by session-specific selection matrices C_k with diagonal noise R and per-neuron offset d. The key claim is that combining multiple partial recordings (with some overlap) enables recovery of cross-couplings and, more robustly, prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs.

On synthetic data with known couplings, the stitched model recovers off-diagonal couplings (r≈0.73 for unseen pairs) and predicts noise correlations well (r≈0.92). On two real 2p datasets (mouse S1, anesthetized and awake), they construct a “fictional” two-session scenario by splitting a single simultaneous dataset into two overlapping subsets and hold out a test set. They show the stitched model approaches a “fully observed” LDS fit to all neurons and substantially outperforms a naive baseline that fits each session separately and zeros cross-session couplings. The model also predicts unobserved neurons’ activity using the observed subset.

This work is related to LDS/GLM approaches for neural populations (e.g., Pillow et al., 2008; Macke et al., 2012; Vidne et al., 2011), spike inference for calcium imaging (Vogelstein et al., 2009), and partially observed system ID (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008). The novelty is in leveraging multiple partial recordings with known cell identities and selection matrices to recover cross-session covariance/coupling, and validating that this enables accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs.

Strengths
- Addresses an important experimental bottleneck; provides a conceptually simple and computationally tractable solution that could expand effective population sizes beyond simultaneous capacity.
- Clear modeling framework and fitting procedure; stitching via known C_k matrices is natural.
- Strong synthetic validation including recovery of off-diagonal couplings and activity prediction.
- Real-data evidence that stitched models predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs nearly as well as a fully observed LDS.
- Sensible ablations: upper and lower bounds; analysis across varying population sizes; demonstration that overlap helps.
- Polite and cautious discussion of model limitations and possible extensions (low-rank latent inputs, sparsity priors).

Weaknesses and suggestions

Quality
- Identifiability and necessity of overlap: The paper states that without overlap they “failed to get good performance,” but the conditions under which the cross-session mapping is identifiable are not formalized. Please provide identifiability conditions (e.g., on the patterns of C_k, richness of stimulus u_t, observability/controllability) and an empirical sensitivity analysis vs. overlap fraction, stimulus variance, session lengths, and SNR.
- Evaluation on real data: The stitched model is compared mostly to a fully observed LDS rather than to empirical cross-validated noise correlations computed from the actually simultaneous test set. Since the split is fictional, the ground-truth test-time covariance is available; please report direct comparisons to empirical residual (noise) correlations on held-out data, with confidence intervals and absolute errors, not just correlation with a model.
- Model mismatch: Treating fluorescence as the latent state x_t and ignoring indicator/calcium dynamics and nonlinearity is a strong simplification, especially at 3 Hz. A convolutional observation model or spike-LDS/PLDS observation (or at least deconvolved spike trains) would likely improve fidelity and provide a better test. At minimum, assess robustness to pre-processing (high-pass filtering choices) and include per-session gain as well as offset to handle bleaching/drift.
- Noise structure: Q and R are constrained to be diagonal, which is restrictive; common input and neuropil contamination induce correlated innovations and observation noise. Including a low-rank component in Q (or extra latent dimensions never observed) would be straightforward and likely improve fit; please report results with such extensions or provide ablation on simulated data with correlated noise.
- Session nonstationarity: The current real-data split uses contiguous segments from the same session, with constant A,B,R across “sessions.” Stitching across true separate sessions/days involves changes in tuning and gains. Include an experiment with artificially injected per-session gains/offsets and A/B drift to evaluate robustness, or, ideally, a dataset with two actual non-simultaneous fields-of-view with partial overlap.

Clarity
- Notational/typo issues in Eq. (2) and the EM updates (the measurement model uses “+ t” and σ_kj indexing is unclear). Please clean up these derivations, and specify dimensions and assumptions precisely (e.g., whether x has additional latent dimensions beyond observed neurons).
- State explicitly the computational complexity per EM iteration and report runtimes and convergence behavior; describe initialization (e.g., subspace ID) to avoid poor local optima.

Originality
- The core ingredients (LDS with missing data; multi-view system ID) are known, but the application to stitching multiple 2p imaging sessions with validation on predicting cross-session noise correlations seems novel and relevant. Make clearer how this differs from GPFA/FA with missing entries and from PLDS/latent GLMs in partially observed settings (Pillow & Latham, 2008), and why the proposed approach scales better.

Significance
- If robust, the approach can immediately impact experimental design by enabling larger effective populations. However, practical deployment hinges on overlap requirements, recording stability, and model mismatch. Providing a more thorough sensitivity analysis and, ideally, demonstration on truly non-simultaneous recordings would strengthen significance.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common experimental limitation with a simple, general tool.
- Strong synthetic results and encouraging real-data performance on predicting noise correlations for unseen pairs.
- Clear path to extensions (sparse priors, low-rank latent inputs) and immediate applicability.

Cons for acceptance
- Real-data evaluation leverages a fictional split; limited validation against empirical correlations and no truly separate sessions.
- Over-reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions in calcium space; lack of robustness analyses to session drift, overlap size, and correlated noise.
- Missing theoretical treatment of identifiability with multiple C_k and minimal overlap.

Recommendation
Weak accept, contingent on strengthening the empirical validation (comparison to empirical held-out correlations, sensitivity to overlap and session nonstationarity) and clarifying identifiability and notation. Even in its current form, the paper presents a useful idea with promising results that will interest the NeurIPS community working on population modeling and neural data analysis.
