- Tackles limited field-of-view in 2-photon calcium imaging by “stitching” sequential partial recordings via an LDS with session-specific observation matrices.
- Uses EM (Kalman smoother E-step, closed-form M-step) and online EM to learn parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) with missing observations.
- Key claim: stitching recovers cross-couplings and predicts noise correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously.
- Synthetic results: on a 60-neuron network, stitched model recovers off-diagonal coupling blocks and predicts non-simultaneous pair correlations with r ≈ 0.92, outperforming a naive baseline.
- Real-data results: on split real calcium recordings, stitched models predict pairwise correlations of non-overlapping pairs well (r ≈ 0.84 vs fully observed LDS) and improve cross-prediction over baseline.
- Relates to prior latent-variable and common-noise neural models (PLDS/GLM, latent factor models, Vidne et al.) and system ID with partial observations; novelty is application-driven framing of stitching.
- Formulation is principled and standard; EM with missing observations is appropriate.
- Identifiability intuition (alignment via overlap) is plausible but not formalized; observability/stability conditions are not established.
- Strong modeling assumptions (diagonal Q and R, linear-Gaussian dynamics on high-pass filtered signals) may bias correlation estimates and ignore correlated measurement noise like neuropil.
- Synthetic experiments are convincing and include oracle benchmarking, but real-data validation uses only artificial simultaneous-data splits, not true sequential sessions.
- Baseline comparisons are weak; missing stronger baselines such as low-rank LDS/FA/GPFA with missing data, PLDS with latent factors, subspace ID, or CCA alignment.
- Evaluation metrics are limited to Pearson correlations; held-out likelihood, calibration of correlation magnitudes, and uncertainty are not reported.
- Full N×N A implies O(N^2) parameters with real overfitting risk; no explicit regularization, sparsity, or stability constraints are used or evaluated.
- Computational complexity and scaling beyond ~200 neurons are not analyzed.
- Paper is mostly clear and organized, with useful EM update details, but lacks formal conditions for identifiability and quantitative sensitivity to overlap or drift.
- Preprocessing and stimulus modeling (especially in awake data) are underspecified; handling of motion/arousal confounds and diagonal-Q assumption needs clarification.
- Methodological novelty is limited (uses standard LDS+EM), but the application of stitching sequential recordings to predict cross-session correlations is timely and underexplored.
- If robust, the approach would be practically valuable for calcium imaging studies, enabling inference across larger populations via overlap-based alignment.
- Major cons: only artificial splits for validation, weak baselines, overfitting risk with dense A, no theoretical identifiability guarantees, and limited predictive reporting.
- Suggested improvements: add experiments on true sequential/partially overlapping sessions, include stronger baselines (low-rank LDS/PLDS/subspace ID/CCA), add regularization and stability enforcement, report predictive likelihoods and uncertainty, and analyze sensitivity to overlap and session drift.
- Overall: promising, well-motivated application with strong synthetic validation but currently limited by artificial real-data validation, weak baselines, and lack of theoretical/regularization analysis; would be stronger with added real sequential validation and baseline comparisons.
