**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in systems neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a large, functional circuit. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, sequential recordings of different (but potentially overlapping) subsets of neurons from the same circuit. The core idea is to model the entire neural population as a latent dynamical system where, in any given recording session, only a subset of the state variables are observed. Specifically, they employ a linear dynamical system (LDS) with Gaussian noise to model the joint activity of the full population. The parameters of this comprehensive model are inferred from the partial recordings using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, where the E-step is handled by Kalman smoothing, adapted for the different observation matrices corresponding to each recording session.

The authors validate their approach on both simulated data and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. On simulated data, the method successfully recovers the underlying functional connectivity matrix and accurately predicts noise correlations, even between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. For the real data, they create a "fictional imaging scenario" by splitting a single large, simultaneous recording into two partial, sequential recordings. This clever validation strategy allows them to compare their method's predictions against a "ground truth" provided by a model fit to the complete data. The results show that their "stitching" method accurately predicts pairwise correlations across the entire population, performing nearly as well as the fully-observed upper bound and significantly outperforming a "naive" approach that models each session independently. This work provides a practical computational tool to expand the effective scale of neural population analysis beyond current experimental limits.

The paper builds on a long history of system identification from partial observations but represents a novel and timely application to a pressing problem in modern neuroscience. It distinguishes itself from prior work which either made stronger assumptions (e.g., relating noise and signal correlations), was not scalable, or addressed different problems like static anatomical connectivity.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a critical and widely recognized bottleneck in neuroscience research. The ability to computationally synthesize a larger, coherent view of a neural circuit from experimentally feasible partial recordings is a significant contribution. This method could enable researchers to ask questions about population dynamics at a scale that is currently out of reach for experimental techniques alone, potentially advancing our understanding of neural computation in circuits like cortical columns.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** While the underlying statistical tool (LDS with missing data) is not new, its application to stitch together non-simultaneous neural recordings is highly novel. The conceptual framing of the problem is elegant, and the demonstration that it allows for the prediction of unobserved cross-correlations is a powerful result. This represents a creative and principled solution to a very practical problem.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Rigorous Evaluation:** The methodology is sound and clearly explained. The evaluation is particularly strong.
    *   The use of simulated data with known ground truth provides a clear proof of concept.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is an excellent validation strategy, providing a clear upper bound (the "fully observed" model) and a lower bound (the "naive" model) against which the proposed method is convincingly benchmarked.
    *   The application to data from both anesthetized and awake animals demonstrates the method's robustness across different brain states.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the methods are described with sufficient detail for understanding, and the results are presented logically and compellingly. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Limitations of the Linear-Gaussian Model:** The authors correctly note that the LDS is a simplified statistical description. Neural dynamics are fundamentally nonlinear and spiking activity is point-process-like, not Gaussian. While the model proves effective for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), this simplification could be a limitation for studying more complex dynamic features. It would strengthen the paper to include a brief discussion on which aspects of neural dynamics this model might fail to capture and to speculate on how more complex models (e.g., switching LDS or nonlinear models) could be incorporated into this framework in the future.

2.  **Dependence on Overlapping Neurons:** The authors mention that they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial detail for experimentalists hoping to apply this method. The paper would be more impactful if it included an analysis, even a preliminary one on simulated data, of how stitching performance degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons between sessions decreases. This would provide valuable guidance for experimental design.

3.  **Scalability and Parameter Identifiability:** The experiments are performed on populations of up to 100 neurons, whereas the motivating examples (e.g., cortical barrels) contain thousands. The authors note that the correlation between inferred and "true" coupling parameters drops with population size (Fig 4c). This raises concerns about scalability and potential parameter degeneracy in larger systems. A discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm (which scales cubically with the total number of neurons N) and potential challenges or approximations needed to scale to thousands of neurons would be beneficial.

4.  **Stationarity Assumption:** The method implicitly assumes that the underlying circuit properties (i.e., the model parameters A, B, Q) are stable across the different recording sessions. These sessions could be separated by significant amounts of time, during which plasticity, changes in brain state, or experimental drift could occur. This assumption should be stated explicitly as a potential limitation, especially in the context of experiments on awake, behaving animals.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper presenting a novel, important, and well-executed contribution. It offers a principled and practical solution to a significant problem facing experimental neuroscience. The strengths—high impact, originality, and rigorous validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily related to the limitations of the chosen model and questions of scalability that can be addressed in future work. The paper is a perfect fit for NIPS, lying at the intersection of machine learning methodology and computational neuroscience, and is likely to be highly influential for both communities. I strongly recommend acceptance.
