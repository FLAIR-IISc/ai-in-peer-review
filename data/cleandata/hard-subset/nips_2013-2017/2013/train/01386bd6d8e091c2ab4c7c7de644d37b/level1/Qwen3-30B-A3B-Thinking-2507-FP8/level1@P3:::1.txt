

# Peer Review

This paper presents a statistically grounded method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural populations into a unified dynamical model, addressing a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience. The core insight is that by modeling the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, one can infer correlations between neurons that were never imaged simultaneously. The authors demonstrate this using linear Gaussian dynamical systems (LDS) and apply it to calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing successful prediction of noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The work builds on recent advances in neural population analysis, particularly related to functional connectivity inference from calcium imaging [6,7] and latent dynamical systems [13,15]. The authors properly contextualize their contribution by noting that while prior work has addressed connectivity inference from partial observations [12,13,14,15], none have specifically tackled the problem of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit with the goal of predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously observed pairs. Their approach differs fundamentally from methods that require strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations [12]) or that cannot scale to large population sizes [13].

**Strengths:**
1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper clearly frames the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, a well-established statistical framework that provides a solid theoretical basis for the approach. The authors demonstrate through simulation that the method can recover true coupling matrices and noise correlations, with performance approaching the theoretical upper bound (fully observed model).
2. **Rigorous validation:** The authors provide thorough validation using both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real experimental data from two datasets (anesthetized and awake mice). Their comparison to both a naive baseline (which cannot predict non-simultaneous correlations) and the fully observed model as an upper bound is particularly compelling.
3. **Clear experimental demonstration:** The results in Figures 3-4 effectively show that the method successfully predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, with performance approaching the fully observed model (0.84 correlation) and substantially exceeding the naive approach (0.15 correlation).
4. **Practical significance:** The method directly addresses a major experimental limitation in neuroscience—current imaging technology can only capture a fraction of neurons in a circuit simultaneously—thereby enabling the study of larger neural circuits than previously possible.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Model simplification:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of the complex, nonlinear dynamics of neural circuits. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't thoroughly explore how this might affect performance in real neural data, particularly since calcium dynamics are nonlinear and spiking activity is inherently discrete. More discussion of when this simplification might fail would strengthen the paper.
2. **Overlap requirement:** The paper notes that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work" but doesn't quantitatively explore the minimum required overlap or how performance scales with different overlap percentages. This is a crucial practical consideration for experimental design that needs more detailed analysis.
3. **Stationarity assumption:** The method assumes the neural circuit dynamics are stationary across imaging sessions, which may not hold in awake animals or during longer experiments. The authors don't discuss how this assumption might limit the method's applicability or how to test for violations of stationarity.
4. **Scalability concerns:** While the paper mentions the method can be generalized to more than two sessions, it doesn't address computational complexity for very large populations (thousands of neurons), which is the very problem they're trying to solve. The EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing could become computationally prohibitive for large-scale applications.

**Recommendation:**
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper with significant potential impact on neural data analysis. The method is novel, well-motivated, and rigorously validated. While the linear Gaussian assumption is a limitation, the results demonstrate that even this simplified model can successfully predict noise correlations, which is the key contribution. The authors' acknowledgment of the model's limitations and their discussion of potential extensions (e.g., using sparsifying penalties [6]) shows thoughtful consideration of the method's scope.

The paper makes a strong contribution to NIPS, advancing the state of the art in neural data analysis by providing a practical method to overcome a fundamental experimental constraint. It addresses a significant problem in neuroscience with a statistically sound approach that could be widely adopted by the community.

**Pro-acceptance arguments:**
- Novel solution to a well-recognized experimental limitation
- Rigorous validation with both synthetic and real data
- Clear theoretical foundation with proper comparison to baselines
- Direct relevance to current experimental neuroscience challenges

**Con-acceptance arguments:**
- Simplistic dynamical model (linear Gaussian) may not capture complex neural dynamics
- Stationarity assumption may limit applicability to awake animals
- Limited discussion of computational scalability for very large populations

Overall, this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and meets all NIPS quality criteria. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scalability concerns and provide more detailed discussion of the overlap requirement. The method is likely to be adopted by experimentalists as it directly addresses a practical limitation in neural circuit analysis.
