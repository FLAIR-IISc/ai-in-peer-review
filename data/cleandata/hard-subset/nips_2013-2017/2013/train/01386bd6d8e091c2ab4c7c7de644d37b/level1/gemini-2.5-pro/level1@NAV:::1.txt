This paper presents a novel and valuable statistical method for combining multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of neuronal populations into a single, coherent dynamical model. The core idea is to treat the problem as one of fitting a latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where neurons not present in a given recording session are treated as unobserved latent variables. This approach elegantly builds upon foundational work in dynamical systems theory (e.g., Takens, 1981) but applies it to a critical, modern problem in systems neuroscience, distinguishing it from prior work that either assumes simultaneous recordings or relies on stronger assumptions about correlation structures. The authors demonstrate their "stitching" method on both simulated data and 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper addresses a fundamental, practical limitation in neuroscience: the inability to record from thousands of interacting neurons at once. The proposed solution is original in its application and has the potential for high impact, enabling researchers to build more comprehensive models of large-scale neural circuits than is possible with current experimental technology alone.
*   **Quality:** The technical contribution is sound. The evaluation is particularly rigorous and convincing. The use of simulated data with a known ground truth clearly demonstrates the method's ability to recover couplings and correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons. Furthermore, the creation of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data, benchmarked against appropriate upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds, provides a careful and honest assessment of the method's performance in a realistic setting.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The problem is motivated clearly, the methodology is explained logically, and the results are presented in a compelling manner.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary limitation is the use of a linear Gaussian model, which is a simplification of complex neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of the potential failure modes when strong non-linearities are present.
*   The authors note that some overlap between the recorded neuronal subsets was critical for the method to work. This is a key practical constraint. While a full analysis is likely beyond the scope of this work, a more systematic characterization of how performance degrades with decreasing overlap would make the paper even more useful for experimentalists planning future studies.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** This is a strong paper that presents an elegant, well-validated solution to a significant problem. The originality of the application, the quality of the experimental validation, and the potential significance for the field of computational neuroscience are all high. The identified weaknesses are minor and represent clear avenues for future research rather than fundamental flaws. The work is a complete and important scientific contribution.
