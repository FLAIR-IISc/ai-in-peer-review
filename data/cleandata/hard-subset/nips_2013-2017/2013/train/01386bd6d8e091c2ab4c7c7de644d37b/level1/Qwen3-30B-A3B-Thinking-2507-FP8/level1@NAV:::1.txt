

This paper proposes a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural populations to infer the full population dynamics, addressing a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience where simultaneous imaging of large neural circuits (thousands of neurons) is not feasible with current 2-photon calcium imaging technology. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system for tractability. They demonstrate their approach on both simulated data and real calcium imaging recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing it can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs with high accuracy, substantially outperforming naive baselines that cannot model such correlations.

The paper excels in technical quality and significance. The method is well-motivated by the real experimental challenge and builds on relevant theoretical work on reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21,22,23]. The thorough validation across simulated and real datasets, including careful comparison to meaningful baselines (fully observed and naive approaches), provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The application to real neural data from awake and anesthetized mice demonstrates clear practical utility for neuroscience. The paper is clearly written, with well-organized exposition and sufficient detail for reproducibility.

Strengths include the novel application of latent dynamical systems to this specific neuroscience problem, the strong empirical validation, and the practical significance for expanding population sizes that can be characterized. The authors also thoughtfully discuss limitations, acknowledging the linear model is a simplification and suggesting potential improvements with more complex models.

A few weaknesses include limited exploration of theoretical limits (e.g., minimal overlap between sessions required) and the linear model's potential limitations for highly nonlinear neural dynamics. While the authors note they can extend the model to include more realistic dynamics, a more detailed discussion of when the linear approximation might fail would strengthen the paper. The paper also doesn't fully address how the method might scale to many more than two recording sessions, which could be important for larger-scale experiments.

Overall, this is a high-quality contribution that addresses a significant practical challenge in neuroscience with a technically sound and well-validated method. The work advances the state of the art in neural population analysis and has clear potential to impact experimental neuroscience. The paper meets all four criteria: it's technically sound (quality), clearly written (clarity), provides a novel solution to a real problem (originality), and addresses an important question in neural circuit function (significance). I strongly recommend acceptance.
