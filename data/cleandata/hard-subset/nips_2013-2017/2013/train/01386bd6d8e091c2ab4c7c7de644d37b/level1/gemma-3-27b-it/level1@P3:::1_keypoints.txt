- Problem & approach: Infers neural population dynamics from partial recordings by “stitching” sessions with a latent dynamical system assuming strong local connectivity.
- Relevance: Method is practically relevant for 2-photon calcium imaging where full population recordings are infeasible.
- Core idea: Leverages linear Gaussian state-space models and EM to estimate shared dynamics across partially overlapping neuron subsets.
- Empirical evidence: Demonstrates effectiveness on simulated data and real mouse somatosensory cortex recordings, outperforming naive baselines and approximating fully-observed models.
- Novelty & significance: Addresses an important, previously underexplored problem and can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
- Technical soundness: Statistical framework is well-defined and EM/online-EM choices are appropriate and computationally considered.
- Clarity: Paper is well-written, logically organized, and supported by helpful figures.
- Model simplifications: Reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical model may misrepresent nonlinear and non-Gaussian neural dynamics.
- Connectivity assumption: Method depends on strong local connectivity, which may not hold across all brain areas and should be discussed further.
- Parameter tuning & robustness: Lacks thorough sensitivity analyses for regularization, noise robustness, and imaging condition variability.
- Overlap requirement: Needs substantial session overlap (∼60% in experiments), limiting applicability when overlap is small or absent.
- Biological interpretation: Provides limited discussion of the biological meaning of predicted correlations and circuit implications.
- Arguments for acceptance: Novel, well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically compelling work with strong potential impact.
- Arguments against acceptance: Simplifying model assumptions, connectivity/overlap requirements, and insufficient robustness analyses raise concerns.
- Recommendation & revisions: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address nonlinear model limitations, parameter sensitivity, connectivity assumption implications, and biological interpretation.
