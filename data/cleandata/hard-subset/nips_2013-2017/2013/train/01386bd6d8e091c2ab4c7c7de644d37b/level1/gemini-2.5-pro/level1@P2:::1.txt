**Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: while we want to understand the dynamics of large neural circuits (thousands of neurons), current technologies like 2-photon imaging can only record from a small subset (hundreds) at any given time. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, sequential recordings of different but partially overlapping subsets of neurons from the same circuit. The core idea is to frame this as a problem of fitting a single latent dynamical system model where, for each recording session, the activity of the non-recorded neurons is treated as missing data.

The authors use a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) for its tractability, where the full population's state evolves over time based on a linear coupling matrix (A), stimulus inputs (B), and Gaussian noise (Q). The observed data in each session is a noisy, partial measurement of this full state. They fit the model parameters (A, B, Q, R) using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, with the E-step (Kalman smoothing) naturally handling the missing observations for each session. The key contribution is demonstrating that this framework can infer the full coupling matrix A, including the interactions between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This, in turn, allows for the prediction of statistics like noise correlations across these non-simultaneously observed pairs.

This work builds on classical results in system identification, which suggest that a system's dynamics can be recovered from partial observations. However, it applies this theory to a pressing, modern problem in neuroscience. The authors clearly distinguish their work from prior art. Unlike previous methods for inferring functional connectivity (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011), this method explicitly handles non-simultaneous recordings. It also avoids the strong assumptions of methods like Wohrer et al. (2010) and is presented as a more scalable approach than latent GLM methods (Pillow & Latham, 2008) for the population sizes of interest.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The formulation of the problem as an LDS with missing data is both elegant and statistically sound. The claims are exceptionally well-supported by a combination of simulated and real experimental data.

*   **Strengths:** The validation strategy is a major strength.
    1.  On simulated data, the authors have access to the ground-truth connectivity matrix and dynamics. This allows them to show convincingly that their "stitching" method recovers the true parameters—including the crucial off-diagonal blocks for non-simultaneous pairs—far better than a "naive" approach.
    2.  On real calcium imaging data, they employ a clever "fictional imaging scenario." By taking a fully-observed dataset and pretending it was recorded in two partial, overlapping sessions, they create a scenario with a known ground truth (the model fit to the full data). This allows them to benchmark their stitched model against a well-defined upper bound ("fully observed" model) and a lower bound ("naive" model). The results robustly show their method's performance approaches the upper bound. This is a very compelling and honest evaluation.

*   **Weaknesses:** The primary weakness is the simplicity of the linear Gaussian model. Real neural dynamics involve spiking non-linearities, and the relationship between calcium fluorescence and underlying spikes is also non-linear and non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential failure modes of their approach when these assumptions are strongly violated. Secondly, the authors state that overlap between recording sessions was "critical for stitching to work" but do not systematically analyze how performance depends on the degree of overlap. This is a crucial parameter for guiding future experiments, and even a preliminary analysis would have significantly strengthened the practical contribution.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation is established immediately with a concrete example from neuroscience. The methods are described concisely but with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the approach. The experimental design, particularly the "fictional imaging scenario," is explained perfectly, which is essential for appreciating the strength of the results. The figures are clean, informative, and directly support the paper's claims.

**Originality:**
The core algorithm—EM for an LDS—is not new. However, the originality of this paper lies in its novel application to solve a significant and previously unaddressed problem in the analysis of neural data. The idea of computationally combining separate recording sessions to infer a unified dynamical model of a larger circuit is a new and powerful concept. The paper does an excellent job of citing relevant work and carving out its unique contribution. This is a prime example of how applying existing machine learning techniques to a new problem in a thoughtful way can constitute a significant and original contribution.

**Significance:**
The potential impact of this work is very high. It offers a direct, practical solution to a major bottleneck in systems neuroscience. If this method proves robust, it could enable the construction of functional circuit models on a scale that is currently impossible with simultaneous recording technology alone. This would allow neuroscientists to test theories of computation in much larger circuits, such as cortical columns. The ability to predict noise correlations between neurons that have never been observed together is a powerful demonstration of the model's utility. Researchers and practitioners using 2-photon imaging are very likely to be interested in adopting or building upon this method. It provides a clear proof-of-concept that could inspire a new line of work using more sophisticated non-linear models within the same "stitching" framework.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  Addresses a critical, high-impact problem in neuroscience with a principled statistical approach.
2.  The method is validated thoroughly and convincingly on both simulated and real data, using a clever experimental design with clear upper and lower performance bounds.
3.  The paper is exceptionally well-written, making a sophisticated idea accessible.
4.  The work has the potential to become a widely used tool for analyzing large-scale neural recordings, directly enabling scientific advances.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The linear Gaussian model is a strong simplification of the underlying neural processes.
2.  The critical experimental parameter of neuron overlap between sessions is acknowledged but not systematically investigated.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that presents a novel and significant contribution. The strengths, particularly the importance of the problem, the clarity of the presentation, and the quality of the validation, far outweigh the weaknesses. It bridges the gap between machine learning and experimental neuroscience in a way that is highly valuable to the NIPS community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
