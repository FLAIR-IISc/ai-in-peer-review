**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous, partial recordings of that population. The central idea is to "stitch" together these partial datasets by framing the problem as parameter estimation in a latent dynamical system with missing data. The authors model the joint activity of the entire N-neuron population as a latent state evolving according to a linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is treated as a partial observation of this latent state, where the observation matrix `C_k` simply selects the subset of neurons being recorded. They employ an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, using Kalman smoothing in the E-step, to learn the parameters of the joint LDS model (couplings `A`, stimulus responses `B`, etc.).

The key contribution is demonstrating that this approach can successfully infer functional properties, most notably noise correlations, between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This work builds upon the classic theory of system identification from partial observations (e.g., Takens, 1981; Katayama, 2001) and provides a practical, powerful application to a pressing problem in modern systems neuroscience. Unlike previous work which required strong assumptions to infer cross-session correlations (e.g., Wohrer et al., 2010), this method is more general. The authors validate their approach first on simulated data with a known ground truth and then on 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex using a clever "fictional imaging scenario" that allows for quantitative comparison against an upper-bound model trained on the full, simultaneous data.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is sound, and the application of an LDS with missing observations to this problem is elegant and well-motivated. The experimental validation is a major strength. The use of simulated data with a known ground truth provides a clear proof-of-concept, demonstrating the recovery of both coupling parameters and noise correlations. The analysis of real neural data is particularly compelling. By creating a fictional scenario where a fully observed dataset is split into two partial, overlapping ones, the authors establish a clear upper bound ("fully observed" model) and a lower bound ("naive" model) for performance. This experimental design allows for a rigorous and convincing evaluation of the method's efficacy on real-world data, showing that it performs substantially better than the naive approach and approaches the performance of the ideal, fully observed case.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the methods are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the implementation, and the results are presented logically and supported by clear figures. The intuition provided in section 3.1 for why the method works (using one session to identify the latent space up to a linear transform, and the second to resolve that transform) is particularly helpful for the reader.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the underlying components (LDS, EM algorithm) are standard, their application to the problem of combining non-simultaneous neural recordings is novel and constitutes a significant conceptual advance. It moves beyond analyzing single recording sessions to building a more holistic model of a circuit from piecemeal data. It clearly distinguishes itself from related work by not relying on restrictive assumptions and by directly modeling the joint dynamics of the unobserved population.

**Significance:** The significance of this work is high. The inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously is a fundamental bottleneck in systems neuroscience. This paper offers a principled and practical computational strategy to help overcome this limitation. If this method proves to be robust, it could become a widely used tool, enabling neuroscientists to characterize the dynamics of circuits that are larger than the field of view of their microscopes. This could facilitate the testing of theories of neural computation that depend on network-level interactions that are currently inaccessible.

Despite these significant strengths, there are some weaknesses or areas that could be clarified:

1.  **Linear-Gaussian Assumption:** The choice of an LDS is justified by its tractability, but neural dynamics are inherently nonlinear and non-Gaussian (spiking). The authors acknowledge this limitation, but a deeper discussion would be beneficial. How robust is the method to violations of this assumption? While the model correctly predicts pairwise correlations (a second-order statistic), it is unclear if the inferred linear couplings (`A` matrix) have a meaningful interpretation in the context of an underlying nonlinear system. The fact that correlation prediction was more accurate than parameter recovery (Fig 2) might hint at this issue.

2.  **Requirement for Overlap:** On the experimental data, the authors state that "we failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This contrasts with the simulation, which used non-overlapping sets. This is a critical point for practical application and experimental design. The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic analysis of this requirement. How does performance degrade as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases? A quantitative characterization of this trade-off would be invaluable for experimentalists planning to use this method.

3.  **Identifiability and Scalability:** The paper provides a good intuition for model identifiability but would benefit from a more formal discussion of the conditions under which the full system can be uniquely identified. Regarding scalability, the method is demonstrated on populations of up to 100 neurons. The motivation, however, is to model circuits of "thousands of neurons." The computational complexity of the Kalman smoother in the E-step scales cubically with the number of neurons (N), which would be prohibitive for N > 1000. A discussion of the method's computational limits and potential avenues for scaling it to larger populations (e.g., using approximations or exploiting sparsity) would be a valuable addition.

**Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance**

**Pro:**
*   Addresses a significant, timely, and difficult problem in computational and systems neuroscience.
*   The proposed method is novel, elegant, and technically sound.
*   The validation is thorough and convincing, using both simulated data with ground truth and a clever, well-benchmarked evaluation on real neural recordings.
*   The paper is extremely well-written and clear.
*   The potential impact is high; this could become a standard analysis tool for neuroscientists.

**Con:**
*   The model relies on a linear-Gaussian assumption, which is a strong simplification of neural dynamics.
*   The practical requirement for overlapping neurons in real data is a key detail that is mentioned but not fully explored.
*   Potential scalability issues to the very large populations mentioned in the motivation are not addressed.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel and important contribution. The strengths far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. The work is a perfect fit for NIPS, combining a principled machine learning approach with a high-impact application.
