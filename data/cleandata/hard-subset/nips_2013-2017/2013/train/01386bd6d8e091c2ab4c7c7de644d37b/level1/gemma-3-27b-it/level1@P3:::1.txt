This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring neural population dynamics when recordings are limited to subsets of neurons within a larger circuit. The core idea – leveraging the assumption of strong local connectivity to “stitch” together partial recordings using a latent dynamical system – is both elegant and practically relevant, particularly in the context of 2-photon calcium imaging where complete population recordings are often infeasible. The authors clearly articulate the problem, position their work relative to existing approaches, and demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both simulated and real data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper builds upon a foundation of established techniques in dynamical systems modeling (specifically, linear Gaussian state-space models) but cleverly adapts them to this specific scenario of incomplete observations. The use of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for parameter estimation is standard, and the authors acknowledge and address potential computational challenges through the use of online-EM. The comparison to both an “ideal” fully-observed model and a naive baseline provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating the performance of their stitching method.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:**  The problem of inferring dynamics from partial recordings is important, and the proposed solution is a novel and potentially impactful approach.  Existing methods (cited in the related work) do not directly address this scenario.  The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a particularly strong result.
* **Technical Soundness:** The statistical framework is well-defined and the use of a latent dynamical system is appropriate.  The EM algorithm is a well-established method for parameter estimation, and the authors have considered practical aspects like computational efficiency.
* **Empirical Validation:** The results on both simulated and real data are compelling.  Demonstrating performance on real calcium imaging data from mouse cortex significantly strengthens the claims. The comparison with the fully observed and naive models provides strong evidence for the efficacy of the stitching method.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation for the work is clearly presented, and the methods are explained in sufficient detail. Figures are helpful in illustrating the approach and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplifications:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of the underlying neural dynamics. While acknowledged by the authors, the limitations of this simplification should be further discussed.  Nonlinearities and non-Gaussianity are likely to be present in real neural circuits, and it’s unclear how robust the method is to these factors.  Exploring alternative latent dynamical system models (e.g., those incorporating nonlinearities) would be valuable.
* **Assumptions about Connectivity:** The method relies on the assumption of strong local connectivity. While reasonable in the context of cortical barrels, this assumption might not hold for all brain areas. A discussion of the implications of violating this assumption would be useful.
* **Parameter Tuning & Robustness:** The paper could benefit from a more thorough investigation of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices (e.g., the regularization parameters used in the EM algorithm). A more systematic evaluation of the method’s robustness to noise and variations in imaging conditions would also strengthen the conclusions.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The necessity of some overlap between imaging sessions (c=60% in the experimental data) seems to limit the applicability of the method. While overlap is expected, the implications of a smaller overlap or even no overlap should be discussed.
* **Limited Discussion of Biological Interpretation:** While the paper demonstrates the ability to predict correlations, a deeper exploration of the biological implications of these correlations would be valuable.  What do these predicted correlations tell us about the underlying neural circuitry and computations?

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and previously unaddressed problem in neural data analysis.
* The proposed method is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.
* The work has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of neural population dynamics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a simplified linear Gaussian model may limit the generality and robustness of the method.
* The assumptions about connectivity and overlap require careful consideration.
* Further investigation of parameter sensitivity and robustness is needed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis. While the method relies on some simplifying assumptions, the results are compelling and the approach has the potential to be widely applicable.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should: 

1.  Expand the discussion of the limitations of the linear Gaussian model and potential avenues for incorporating nonlinearities.
2.  Provide a more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices.
3.  Discuss the implications of violating the assumption of strong local connectivity.
4.  Provide a more detailed biological interpretation of the predicted correlations.
