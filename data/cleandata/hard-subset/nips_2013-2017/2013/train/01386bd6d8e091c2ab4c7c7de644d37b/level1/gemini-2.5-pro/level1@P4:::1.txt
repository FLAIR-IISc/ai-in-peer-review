This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential, partial recordings of that population. The central idea is to "stitch" together these partial observations into a single coherent model. The authors frame this problem as one of fitting a latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing data. They use a linear-Gaussian dynamical system to model the activity of the full N-neuron population. Each recording session provides observations of a different subset of these N neurons, which is formalized using a session-specific observation matrix `C_k`. The parameters of this single underlying LDS (connectivity matrix `A`, stimulus response matrix `B`, and noise covariances `Q` and `R`) are estimated from all partial recordings simultaneously using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

This work builds upon a foundation of system identification theory, which suggests that a system's full dynamics can be recovered from partial observations. It directly addresses a pressing issue in modern neuroscience where recording technologies like 2-photon calcium imaging can sample hundreds of neurons, but key circuits may involve thousands. The paper distinguishes its contribution from prior work on inferring functional connectivity, which typically assumes simultaneous recordings. It also contrasts its approach with methods that handle sub-sampled data in different contexts, such as the interleaved sampling on dendritic trees [14] or methods that rely on strong assumptions linking stimulus and noise correlations [12]. The proposed method provides a general and scalable framework for a problem of growing importance.

**Quality:**
The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem within the LDS framework with missing observations is appropriate and elegant. The use of the EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing for inference is a standard and correct approach for this model class. The evaluation is rigorous and a major strength of the paper. On simulated data, where the ground truth connectivity and dynamics are known, the method demonstrates a strong ability to recover the true coupling matrix, including connections between neurons that were never observed simultaneously. The validation on real calcium imaging data is particularly well-designed. By creating a "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is split into two partially overlapping, non-simultaneous sessions, the authors create a testbed with a known performance ceiling (a model trained on the complete data) and a sensible baseline (a "naive" model that combines separately-trained models). The results convincingly show that the proposed "stitching" method performs substantially better than the naive approach and approaches the performance of the fully-observed upper bound in predicting noise correlations.

A potential weakness is the choice of a linear-Gaussian model. Neural activity, particularly spiking, is inherently nonlinear and non-Gaussian. The authors acknowledge this limitation, justifying the model choice by its tractability and its demonstrated success in capturing the correlation structure of the continuous calcium fluorescence signals. While this simplification is reasonable for a first demonstration, it may limit the model's ability to capture more complex dynamic motifs.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem with a concrete example from neuroscience. The methods section concisely presents the model equations and the fitting procedure. The experimental design, especially the "fictional imaging scenario" for evaluating performance on real data, is explained with sufficient detail for the reader to understand and appreciate the results. The figures are clean, informative, and effectively support the paper's main claims. The progression from the intuition behind the method, to its validation on simulated data, and finally to its application on real cortical data provides a clear and compelling narrative.

**Originality:**
The core contribution is the novel application of established statistical techniques to a new and important problem in computational neuroscience. While the LDS model and the EM algorithm are not new, their use to combine non-simultaneous recordings to infer a unified dynamical model of a larger circuit is a novel and creative idea. This work provides a principled statistical solution where previous approaches were either non-existent or relied on restrictive assumptions. It effectively operationalizes theoretical concepts from system identification for a practical challenge in analyzing modern neuroscience data.

**Significance:**
The work is highly significant and has the potential for major impact. As experimental methods for recording neural activity continue to advance, the gap between the number of neurons that can be recorded and the size of the circuits being studied will remain a key challenge. This paper offers a direct, data-driven computational strategy to help bridge that gap. The ability to accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a powerful demonstration of the method's utility, as these correlations are thought to be critical for understanding neural coding and computation. The paper's findings could directly influence how experimentalists design large-scale recording experiments, providing a framework for systematically mapping larger circuits than is possible in a single experiment. This work represents a valuable step toward building comprehensive, data-driven models of entire neural circuits.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a timely, important, and unsolved problem in the analysis of large-scale neural data.
*   The proposed method is principled, technically sound, and based on a clever application of existing statistical tools.
*   The paper is written with exceptional clarity.
*   The experimental validation is thorough and convincing, using both synthetic and real data, with well-chosen upper and lower performance bounds.
*   The results are strong and demonstrate a significant advance over naive approaches.
*   The work has high potential for impact on both computational and experimental neuroscience.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The underlying model (linear-Gaussian LDS) is a simplification of true neural dynamics.
*   The novelty is in the application of existing methods rather than the development of fundamentally new algorithms.

In summary, this is a strong paper that presents an elegant and effective solution to a significant problem. Its strengths in clarity, technical quality, and potential impact far outweigh the limitations of the model's simplicity.
