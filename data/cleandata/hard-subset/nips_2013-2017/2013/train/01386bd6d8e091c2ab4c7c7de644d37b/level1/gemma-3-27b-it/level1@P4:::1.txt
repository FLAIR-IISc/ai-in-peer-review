This paper addresses the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from multiple, sequential recordings of the same neural circuit, a common scenario in 2-photon calcium imaging where it is impossible to record from all neurons simultaneously. The authors propose a statistical method based on fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, effectively “stitching” together information from different imaging sessions. The method is demonstrated using both simulated data and recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The paper builds upon a significant body of work in neural decoding and functional connectivity analysis, referencing relevant studies using both calcium imaging and electrophysiology (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]). It distinguishes itself by specifically tackling the problem of combining *sequential* recordings rather than simultaneous ones, and offering a method for estimating correlations between neurons never observed together in the same recording session. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work addressing partially observed data [12, 13, 14, 15], and clearly explain how their approach differs.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of framing the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations is a clever and potentially powerful way to combine data from different imaging sessions, especially given the limitations of current imaging technology. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a particularly strong result.
* **Technical Soundness:** The method is based on well-established statistical techniques (EM algorithm, Kalman smoothing) applied to a relevant neural modeling framework (linear Gaussian dynamical system). The mathematical formulation is clear and concise. The details of the simulation and experimental data analysis are sufficiently described to allow for reproducibility.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The methods section is detailed but not overly complex. Figures and equations are well-labeled and contribute to understanding the approach.
* **Significance:** Addressing the limitations of simultaneous recordings is crucial for studying large-scale neural circuits. This method has the potential to significantly expand the size of neural populations that can be effectively characterized, facilitating the investigation of more complex neural computations. The successful demonstration on real data from the somatosensory cortex strengthens the practical relevance of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplifications:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of the complex, nonlinear dynamics of neural circuits. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it’s unclear how robust the method is to deviations from these assumptions. Exploring more realistic models would be beneficial.
* **Computational Scalability:** The paper states the method doesn’t scale well to population sizes beyond those tested. This limits the immediate applicability of the method to very large circuits. Addressing this scalability issue is critical for wider adoption.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The necessity of some overlap between recording sessions (60% in the experiments) could limit the flexibility of experimental designs. Exploring methods to reduce this overlap or handle cases with minimal overlap would be valuable.
* **Limited Biological Interpretation:** The paper focuses primarily on the statistical aspects of the method and its ability to predict correlations. A more in-depth discussion of the biological implications of the inferred parameters (e.g., functional connectivity) would be beneficial.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The method addresses a significant and practical problem in the field of neural population dynamics.
* The approach is novel and technically sound, based on established statistical techniques.
* The results demonstrate the method’s ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a key result.
* The paper is well-written and clear, providing sufficient details for reproducibility.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The method relies on strong simplifying assumptions (linearity, Gaussianity) that may limit its applicability to real neural data.
* The computational scalability of the method is a concern.
* The need for substantial overlap between recording sessions could restrict experimental design options.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem in neural data analysis. While the method has limitations, the demonstrated ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a significant achievement. The clarity of the presentation and the technical soundness of the approach make this paper a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a discussion of the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption and potential directions for improving computational scalability.
