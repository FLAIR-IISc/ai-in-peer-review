- Introduces a method to infer whole-population neural dynamics from multiple non-simultaneous partial recordings by "stitching" sessions via a latent linear dynamical system (LDS).

- Models joint activity as an LDS; each session is a partial observation (selection matrix C_k); parameters learned with EM and Kalman smoothing.

- Main contribution: can recover functional properties, notably noise correlations, between neurons never recorded simultaneously.

- Validated on simulated ground-truth data and on 2-photon calcium recordings using a "fictional imaging" benchmark comparing to fully observed and naive baselines.

- High technical quality: sound methodology and elegant application of LDS with missing data.

- Experimental validation is strong and convincing, demonstrating recovery of couplings and correlations in simulations and competitive performance on real data.

- Paper is exceptionally clear and well organized, with useful intuition about identifying the latent space across sessions.

- Original application: novel use of standard tools (LDS, EM) to combine non-simultaneous recordings without restrictive assumptions.

- High significance: addresses a major bottleneck in systems neuroscience and could enable analysis of circuits larger than single fields of view.

- Limitation — Linear-Gaussian assumption: neural dynamics are nonlinear/non-Gaussian; robustness and interpretability of recovered A under model misspecification are unclear.

- Limitation — Overlap requirement: real-data performance required overlapping neurons, unlike some simulations; lack of systematic analysis of performance vs. overlap.

- Limitation — Identifiability and scalability: needs formal identifiability conditions and discussion of computational limits (Kalman smoother scales cubically), unclear how to scale to thousands of neurons.

- Pro: addresses an important problem, is novel and technically sound, thoroughly validated, clearly written, and potentially high impact.

- Con: relies on strong linear-Gaussian simplification, practical overlap needs underexplored, and scalability to very large populations not addressed.

- Overall: excellent paper whose strengths outweigh weaknesses and is a strong fit for NeurIPS.
