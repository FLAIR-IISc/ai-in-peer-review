- Paper tackles stitching sequential two-photon sessions by formulating it as parameter estimation in an LDS with missing observations using EM (online mini-batch + batch fine-tune).  
- Key claim: combining overlapping partial recordings recovers cross-couplings and accurately predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.  
- Synthetic results: recovered off-diagonal couplings (r≈0.73 for unseen pairs) and predicted noise correlations well (r≈0.92).  
- Real-data test: constructed fictional two-session splits from simultaneous data and showed stitched model approaches fully observed LDS and beats a naive per-session baseline.  
- Related work: builds on LDS/GLM, spike inference, and partially observed system ID; novelty is leveraging known cell identities/selection matrices across sessions to stitch recordings.  

Strengths:
- Addresses an important experimental bottleneck with a simple, tractable solution that could expand effective population sizes.  
- Clear modeling and fitting procedure; stitching via known C_k is natural.  
- Strong synthetic validation and sensible ablations (bounds, population size, overlap effects).  
- Real-data evidence that stitched models predict cross-session noise correlations nearly as well as fully observed models.  
- Authors discuss limitations and possible extensions (low-rank inputs, sparsity priors).  

Weaknesses / Suggestions:
- Identifiability and overlap: paper fails to formalize conditions under which cross-session mapping is identifiable; needs theory and sensitivity analysis vs. overlap, stimulus richness, session length, and SNR.  
- Real-data evaluation: relies on fictional split; should compare model predictions to empirical held-out noise correlations with CIs and absolute errors, not just model-to-model correlation.  
- Model mismatch: treating fluorescence as latent state ignores calcium indicator dynamics and nonlinearity; should test convolutional or spike-LDS observations or at least assess preprocessing/gain/offset robustness.  
- Noise structure: diagonal Q and R are restrictive given common input/neuropil; include low-rank noise components or test robustness to correlated noise.  
- Session nonstationarity: need experiments with per-session gains/offsets or A/B drift, or validation on truly separate sessions/days.  

Clarity / Implementation:
- Notational/typo issues (Eq.2, EM updates, indexing); dimensions and assumptions should be specified clearly.  
- Report computational complexity, runtimes, convergence, and initialization strategy to avoid poor local optima.  

Originality / Significance:
- Core methods (LDS with missing data, multi-view system ID) are known, but application to stitching 2p sessions and validating cross-session noise prediction is novel and relevant.  
- Impact depends on overlap requirements, recording stability, and model mismatch; stronger sensitivity analysis or real non-simultaneous data would increase significance.  

Pros for acceptance:
- Practical tool addressing a common limitation, with strong synthetic results and encouraging real-data performance and clear extension paths.  

Cons for acceptance:
- Validation uses fictional splits rather than true separate sessions; over-reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions and missing robustness/identifiability analyses.  

Recommendation:
- Weak accept conditional on strengthening empirical validation (compare to empirical held-out correlations, sensitivity to overlap and nonstationarity) and clarifying identifiability and notation.
