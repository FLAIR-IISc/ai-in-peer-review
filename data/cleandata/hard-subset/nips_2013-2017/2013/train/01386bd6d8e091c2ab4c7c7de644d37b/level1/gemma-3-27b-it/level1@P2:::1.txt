This paper proposes a method to “stitch” together multiple, partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the dynamics and interactions of a larger population of neurons than can be simultaneously imaged. The core idea is to formulate the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, leveraging the assumption that nearby neurons couple strongly. The authors demonstrate their method on both simulated data and recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

This is a valuable contribution with strong potential impact. The problem addressed – extending population recordings beyond the limits of simultaneous imaging – is highly relevant to the field, given the increasing use of 2-photon calcium imaging and the understanding that many computations involve circuits spanning thousands of neurons. The proposed solution, framing the problem as a latent dynamical system, is a natural and well-motivated approach. The results are convincing, particularly the demonstration of predicting noise correlations in real data. This result is significant, as noise correlations are often crucial for understanding neural coding and computation. The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions this work within the existing literature, clearly differentiating it from prior approaches that either focus on connectivity inference *within* a single recording or rely on strong assumptions not made here. The inclusion of both simulated and real data is a strength, providing confidence in the method's generalizability. The authors also thoughtfully address potential limitations and future directions.

However, there are some weaknesses. While the linear Gaussian dynamical system is computationally tractable, the authors acknowledge it's a simplification. A discussion of the potential impact of non-linearities and non-Gaussianity on the results would be beneficial. While the simulation results are strong, the parameters used (e.g., coupling strengths, stimulus drive) are somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of parameter space and sensitivity analysis would strengthen the simulation findings. The details of the stimulus protocol in the experimental data are a bit sparse; a more thorough description would be helpful.  The fictional imaging scenario used to benchmark performance is a clever idea, but it relies on a slightly artificial setup. It’s not entirely clear how robust the method is to deviations from the assumptions underlying this scenario (e.g., the degree of overlap between imaging sessions). The comparison to the "naive" approach feels a little strong; the naive approach is quite basic, and a more sophisticated baseline might be more informative.  Although the authors mention sparsifying penalties in the acknowledgments, including a brief comparison of results with and without such a penalty would add value. The code is not available, which limits reproducibility.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Significance:** Addresses an important problem with broad implications for understanding neural circuit function.
*   **Originality:** Presents a novel approach to combining partial recordings, going beyond existing methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is well-motivated, mathematically sound, and validated with both simulations and real data.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Strong Results:** Demonstrates the ability to predict noise correlations, a key aspect of neural dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Simplifying Assumptions:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification that might limit the method's applicability.
*   **Limited Parameter Exploration:** The simulation parameters are somewhat arbitrary.
*   **Reproducibility:** Lack of code availability hinders reproducibility.
*   **Benchmark limitations:** The artificial imaging scenario, while clever, may not fully capture real-world complexities.



**Overall assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution and should be accepted to NIPS. The strengths of the paper – its significance, originality, technical soundness, and clarity – outweigh the weaknesses. While the simplifying assumptions and limited parameter exploration are valid concerns, they do not fundamentally undermine the validity of the findings. The method presented has the potential to significantly advance our ability to study neural circuits, and the results presented are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above (e.g., a discussion of non-linearities, a more thorough description of the stimulus protocol, and ideally, code availability).
