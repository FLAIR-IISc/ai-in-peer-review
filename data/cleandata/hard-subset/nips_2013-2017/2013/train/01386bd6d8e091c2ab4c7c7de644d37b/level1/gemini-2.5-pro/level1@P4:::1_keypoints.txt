- Problem: infer joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple sequential, partial recordings by "stitching" partial observations into one model.  
- Model: formulate as a latent dynamical system (linear-Gaussian LDS) with session-specific observation matrices C_k to handle missing neurons.  
- Inference: jointly estimate A, B, Q, R across sessions using Expectation-Maximization with Kalman smoothing.  
- Theoretical grounding: builds on system identification and contrasts with prior simultaneous-recording or strongly-assuming sub-sampling methods.  
- Simulation results: method recovers ground-truth coupling, including links between neurons never observed simultaneously.  
- Real-data validation: uses a "fictional imaging scenario" (split fully-observed data) giving a clear upper bound and a sensible naive baseline.  
- Empirical performance: stitching outperforms the naive approach and approaches fully-observed model performance in predicting noise correlations.  
- Technical quality: formulation and inference are appropriate, standard, and correctly applied; evaluation is rigorous.  
- Clarity: paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and figures effectively support the narrative.  
- Originality: novel and creative application of established statistical techniques to a practical neuroscience problem.  
- Significance: high potential impact for scaling circuit inference and guiding experimental design in large-scale recordings.  
- Main weakness: linear-Gaussian assumption may limit capturing nonlinear, non-Gaussian spiking dynamics despite suitability for continuous calcium signals.  
- Pro-acceptance arguments: timely, principled method with strong validation, clarity, and potential high impact.  
- Con-acceptance arguments: limited algorithmic novelty (uses existing LDS/EM tools) and simplifying modeling assumptions.
