This paper presents a novel method for inferring neural population dynamics from multiple, sequential, and partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea is to frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, effectively “stitching” together information from different recording sessions to obtain a more complete picture of the circuit’s activity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were not simultaneously recorded.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in neural decoding and functional connectivity estimation. Existing methods (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) primarily focus on inferring connections within a single, simultaneously recorded population. While some studies address partial observations [12, 13, 14, 15], they either rely on strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations [12]) or are not scalable to the population sizes of interest. The key novelty here lies in the principled statistical framework that allows for inferring dynamics *across* non-simultaneous recordings, opening the door to studying circuits larger than what current imaging techniques can capture in a single session. The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system, while simple, is a reasonable starting point for tractability, and the authors acknowledge the potential benefits of more complex models.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of "stitching" together partial recordings using a latent dynamical system is a significant contribution. It addresses a crucial limitation in current neuroimaging techniques.
*   **Theoretical Soundness:** The method is grounded in a well-established statistical framework (Kalman smoothing and EM algorithm).
*   **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling evidence of the method's performance on both simulated and real data. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly impressive.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the methods section is sufficiently detailed to allow for replication.
*   **Significance:** The work has the potential to significantly impact our ability to study large neural circuits and test theories of neural computation. It extends the scope of what's measurable with existing technology.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Linearity Assumption:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification. While justifiable for initial exploration, it may not accurately capture the complex nonlinear dynamics of real neural circuits. The impact of this assumption on the accuracy of correlation predictions warrants further investigation.
*   **Diagonal Noise Covariance:** The assumption of a diagonal noise covariance matrix (Q) limits the ability to model correlated noise sources. While the authors mention the possibility of extending the framework to allow for correlated noise, this extension is not implemented or demonstrated.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The method appears to require significant overlap between the recorded populations in different sessions, as noted in the results section. The sensitivity of the method to the degree of overlap should be investigated more systematically.
*   **Computational Cost:** The EM algorithm can be computationally expensive, especially for large population sizes. While the authors mention using online EM, further discussion of the computational scaling of the method would be beneficial.
*   **Limited Exploration of Alternatives:** The comparison to “naive” approach is useful, but a more thorough benchmarking against other methods for handling partial observations, even if they are less directly applicable, would strengthen the paper.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and challenging problem in computational neuroscience.
*   The proposed method is novel, theoretically sound, and empirically validated.
*   The results are compelling and demonstrate the potential of the method to advance our understanding of neural circuits.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The method relies on simplifying assumptions (linearity, diagonal noise covariance) that may limit its applicability to real-world data.
*   The requirement for significant overlap between recordings could be a practical limitation.
*   The computational cost may be prohibitive for very large populations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The proposed method offers a promising approach to overcoming the limitations of current neuroimaging techniques and studying large neural circuits. While the simplifying assumptions and potential computational challenges are worth noting, they do not detract significantly from the overall impact of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the limitations outlined in the weaknesses section. The authors should consider discussing the potential impact of the linearity assumption and exploring extensions to handle correlated noise.
