- Presents a statistical method to "stitch" sequentially imaged neuron sets into a single latent dynamical model.  
- Frames the problem as fitting a latent linear Gaussian dynamical system with missing observations using EM.  
- Can predict noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded simultaneously.  
- Builds on prior partial-observation connectivity work but specifically addresses non-simultaneous recordings.  
- Evaluation uses a realistic fictional imaging scenario on real data to benchmark performance.  
- Benchmarks against a fully observed upper bound and a naive lower bound.  
- Method substantially outperforms the naive approach and approaches the fully observed model in predicting correlations.  
- Technical quality is strong: well-motivated model, careful implementation, and appropriate use of EM.  
- Thorough evaluation with both simulated ground-truth data and real experimental data.  
- Authors acknowledge model limitations (linear Gaussian approximation) and note possible improvements with more complex models.  
- Presentation is exceptionally clear with organized math, clear notation, and informative figures.  
- Provides helpful intuition (Section 3.1) that makes the technical approach accessible.  
- Original in its specific application of latent dynamical systems to stitching non-simultaneous recordings.  
- Significance is high: expands usable neural population size without new hardware and shows success on mouse somatosensory data.  
- Weakness: linear Gaussian model simplifies complex neural dynamics and may limit performance on other metrics.  
- Weakness: insufficient discussion of performance under varied conditions (overlap degrees, diverse dynamics).  
- Weakness: some EM/method technical details may be hard for non-specialists; biological plausibility connection is brief.  
- Weakness: core idea (latent dynamics for missing data) is not entirely novel, though the application is.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of scaling to larger circuits and integration into neuroscience workflows; future work discussion is brief.  
- Pro acceptance: novel and significant problem solution, strong technical evaluation, demonstrated real-data success, well-written, potentially high impact.  
- Con acceptance: relies on simplified linear Gaussian model, limited scalability discussion, some technical accessibility issues.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — technically sound, well-evaluated, original application, and likely to impact the field; recommend acceptance with high confidence.
