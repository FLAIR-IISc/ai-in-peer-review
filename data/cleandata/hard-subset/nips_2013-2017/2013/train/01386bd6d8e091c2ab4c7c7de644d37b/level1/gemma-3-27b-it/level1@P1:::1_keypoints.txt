- Core idea: Fit a latent dynamical system to multiple, sequential, partial recordings to "stitch" sessions and infer population dynamics and noise correlations across non-simultaneous recordings.  
- Novelty: Principled framework for inferring dynamics across non-simultaneous recordings, enabling study of larger circuits than single-session imaging allows.  
- Theoretical soundness: Method is grounded in established tools (linear Gaussian LDS, Kalman smoothing, EM).  
- Empirical validation: Demonstrated accurate prediction of noise correlations on simulated data and real mouse calcium imaging.  
- Presentation: Clear writing and sufficiently detailed methods for replication.  
- Significance: Potentially substantial impact on studying large neural circuits and testing neural computation theories.  
- Linearity assumption: Using a linear Gaussian model may miss complex nonlinear neural dynamics; impact on prediction accuracy needs further study.  
- Diagonal noise covariance: Assuming diagonal Q prevents modeling correlated noise sources; extension to correlated noise is not implemented.  
- Overlap requirement: Method seems to need substantial neuron overlap between sessions; sensitivity to overlap is not systematically evaluated.  
- Computational cost: EM (even online) can be expensive for large populations; scaling details are insufficient.  
- Limited benchmarking: Comparison mainly to a “naive” approach; broader comparisons to partial-observation methods are lacking.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses an important problem with a novel, sound, and empirically validated method presented clearly.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Simplifying assumptions (linearity, diagonal noise), overlap requirements, and potential computational limits could restrict real-world applicability.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution warranting acceptance with minor revisions to discuss linearity effects and extensions for correlated noise.
