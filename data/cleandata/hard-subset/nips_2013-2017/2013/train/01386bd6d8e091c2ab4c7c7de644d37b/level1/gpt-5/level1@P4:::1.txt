Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of inferring joint population dynamics from multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same neural circuit, motivated by the gap between the number of neurons that can be imaged simultaneously and the size of cortical microcircuits. The authors propose a simple but principled approach: fit a single linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) to all sessions simultaneously, treating unrecorded neurons at each session as latent variables and using known selection matrices Ck to represent which neurons are observed in each session. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned via EM (with an online-EM warm start, then batch EM). The method is intended to “stitch” sequential recordings into a coherent model, enabling prediction of “noise correlations” between neurons that were not imaged simultaneously, as well as cross-prediction of unobserved activity.

On synthetic data with known ground truth, the stitched model recovers cross-session couplings and noise correlations considerably better than a naive baseline that fits separate models and zeros cross-session couplings. On two real 2-photon datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex (anesthetized and awake), the authors construct a fictional split into two sessions with partial overlap and show that the stitched model predicts pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously “recorded” pairs closely matching those from a fully observed model fit to the full data. They report that some overlap between sessions is important for performance.

The work connects to prior efforts on inferring connectivity from calcium imaging or spikes [6–11], latent-variable GLMs [13], methods for predicting noise correlations from stimulus correlations [12], subsampled connectivity reconstruction [14,15], and latent shared-input models [17,18]. The novelty is enabling inference across non-simultaneous recordings by jointly fitting a single LDS with missing observations, rather than assuming a monotonic relation between stimulus and noise correlations [12] or restricting to single-session inference [6,7,9,13].

Quality
- Technical soundness: The modeling and inference setup is appropriate and standard. The LDS with missing observations and known selection matrices is well-posed, and EM with Kalman smoothing is the correct estimator for ML. The stationarity choice for initial state via the Lyapunov equation is consistent, though constraints ensuring stability of A are not detailed. Assumptions include diagonal Q and R; cross-neuron dependencies are captured via A.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments demonstrate recovery of couplings and noise correlations (with quantitative correlations to ground truth). Real-data experiments are carefully controlled via a fictional split to establish upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) bounds; the stitched model approaches the upper bound for predicting cross-session correlations and cross-predicting activity. The need for overlap is acknowledged and briefly explored.
- Limitations: Validation on truly non-simultaneous sessions (different times/days) is absent, so robustness to session-to-session nonstationarities is not established. The diagonal Q assumption and lack of explicit calcium/spike observation modeling may limit fidelity. Baselines are limited; comparisons to low-rank shared latent models or PLDS-style models spanning sessions would strengthen the case. Computational scaling and regularization/stability details are sparse.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written, with a concise mathematical description of the model and EM updates. The experimental setup is described with enough detail to understand the synthetic and real datasets and the fictional split. A few minor issues: the observation equation has a typographical ambiguity around the noise term; stability constraints on A and initialization are not specified; the exact overlap fraction and its effect are only partially quantified. Nevertheless, the narrative is coherent and the figures (as described) seem to support the claims.

Originality
The core idea—jointly fitting a single LDS across multiple partially overlapping sessions to infer cross-session couplings and correlations—is simple but novel in this context. Prior work on partially observed neural populations [13,17,18] does not directly address stitching multiple sessions with identifiable cross-neuron parameters at this scale. The work extends classical system identification insights to a practically important calcium imaging setting. While the modeling components are standard, their combination and application to this problem is new.

Significance
If robust in practice, the approach materially expands what can be inferred from current imaging technologies by enabling estimates of noise correlations and functional couplings between neurons never recorded simultaneously. This could impact experimental design (e.g., planning overlap vs coverage) and the analysis of large cortical populations. The demonstration on real imaging data is promising, but the lack of validation on truly non-simultaneous sessions and stronger baselines tempers the immediate practical impact. The framework is extensible (e.g., to low-rank latent inputs, sparsity priors, spike/fluorescence models), suggesting potential for further gains.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of stitching as an LDS with missing observations; correct and efficient inference via EM/Kalman smoothing.
- Strong synthetic validation with recovery of cross-session couplings and noise correlations.
- Real-data analysis shows stitched predictions close to a fully observed upper bound and substantially better than a naive baseline.
- Practical insights about the necessity of overlap and the potential to predict activity of unobserved neurons.
- Simple, scalable model that can be extended with priors or richer observation models.

Weaknesses
- Validation limited to fictional splits; no evidence on truly non-simultaneous sessions where drifts, state changes, and registration errors occur.
- Assumes diagonal Q and R; no modeling of low-rank shared variability or correlated measurement noise despite known relevance in cortical populations.
- Baseline comparisons are weak; missing comparisons to alternative latent-variable models that might stitch sessions via shared factors.
- Insufficient analysis of how performance scales with overlap fraction, session length, and mismatch between sessions; computational complexity and stability/regularization details are limited.
- Coupling estimates degrade with population size; causes and remedies (e.g., sparsity, stability constraints) are not systematically investigated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Include experiments across real, truly separate sessions with cell registration to assess robustness to nonstationarities.
- Add baselines using low-rank latent state models (e.g., PLDS/FA with shared latent trajectories aligned via overlapping neurons) and models with correlated innovations.
- Systematically study overlap fraction, session durations, and initialization/regularization (e.g., sparsity, spectral radius constraints) on performance and identifiability.
- Consider more realistic observation models for calcium dynamics or spike deconvolution, and potentially a low-rank Q to capture common inputs.
- Report computational costs and convergence behavior.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and important problem with a simple, principled method.
- Convincing synthetic and controlled real-data results demonstrating prediction of cross-session correlations.
- Method is broadly applicable and extensible; likely to be used by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of validation on truly non-simultaneous datasets and stronger baselines limits confidence in real-world impact.
- Modeling assumptions (diagonal Q/R, linear-Gaussian observation) may be too restrictive for complex cortical dynamics.
- Limited analysis of overlap requirements and parameter identifiability/stability at larger scales.
