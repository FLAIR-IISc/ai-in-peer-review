Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important practical limitation in systems neuroscience: current 2-photon calcium imaging can record a few hundred neurons per session, whereas computations in cortex likely involve thousands. The authors propose a “stitching” approach that combines sequential partial recordings of the same circuit by fitting a single latent linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. For each recording session k, a binary selection matrix Ck picks the simultaneously imaged neurons; activity evolves via x_t = A x_{t−1} + B u_t + η_t with Gaussian innovations; observations y_t = Ck (x_t + d) + ε_t. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned with EM (Kalman smoother E-step; closed-form M-step), including online EM for scalability. The key claim is that combining multiple partial observations enables recovery of cross-couplings and, critically, prediction of noise correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously.

On synthetic data (structured but fairly dense 60-neuron network), the stitched model recovers off-diagonal coupling blocks and predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs (r ≈ 0.92 with ground-truth), outperforming a “naive” baseline that trains separate LDSs per session. On real calcium data (mouse S1, anesthetized and awake), the authors construct a “fictional imaging” scenario by splitting simultaneously acquired data into two overlapping subsets (typically 60% coverage with overlap) and show that stitched models predict the pairwise correlations of non-overlapping pairs well (r ≈ 0.84 with a fully observed LDS fit), and improve cross-prediction of unobserved activity relative to the baseline.

This work relates to: latent variable models for neural populations (e.g., PLDS/GLM and latent factor models; Pillow & Latham 2008; Macke et al. 2012), common-noise models (Vidne et al. 2011), calcium connectivity inference (Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012), and system identification with partial observations (subspace ID). The novelty is applying an LDS with session-varying observation matrices to stitch partial recordings and explicitly target the prediction of across-session noise correlations without assuming a monotonic relation to stimulus correlations (cf. Wohrer et al. 2010).

Quality
- Soundness: The formulation is standard and principled; EM for LDS with missing observations is appropriate. The stated intuition about identifiability across sessions (alignment of the unobserved subspace when different subsets are observed) is reasonable but not formalized; conditions (observability across the union of Ck, stability of A) are not established. Assumptions such as diagonal Q and R and linear-Gaussian dynamics on high-pass filtered calcium signals are strong and may bias correlation estimates (e.g., neuropil-driven correlated measurement noise).
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments are convincing and appropriately benchmarked with an oracle “fully observed” model. Real-data validation relies entirely on splitting simultaneously recorded data into overlapping subsets; this controls stationarity but does not demonstrate robustness to real cross-session nonstationarities (drift, bleaching, behavioral state). The naive baseline is weak; more competitive baselines are missing (e.g., low-rank LDS/FA/GPFA with missing data; PLDS with latent factors; subspace ID with time-varying Ck; CCA alignment via overlap). Predictive metrics (held-out likelihood, calibration of correlation magnitudes) are not reported, only Pearson correlations.
- Scalability/regularization: The full N×N A introduces O(N^2) parameters; overfitting is a real risk, especially with modest T and measurement noise. No explicit regularization or stability constraints are used (sparsity is mentioned only in discussion). Computational complexity and scaling beyond ~200 neurons are not analyzed.

Clarity
The paper is mostly clear and well organized. Useful details are provided on EM updates with varying Ck. However:
- Formal conditions for identifiability and the role of overlap are not stated; the text notes poor performance without overlap in real data but does not quantify sensitivity to overlap fraction or to session-to-session drift.
- Preprocessing and stimulus modeling are underspecified (awake dataset covariates; motion/arousal confounds). Stability constraints on A and handling of diagonal-Q assumption need clarification.
- Evaluation metrics could be described more precisely (e.g., how correlations are computed after removing stimulus drive; whether absolute errors are calibrated).

Originality
Methodologically, the ingredients (LDS+EM, missing observations) are standard; the novelty is framing and validating the “stitching” of sequential partial recordings to recover cross-session noise correlations and cross-couplings. Compared to prior NIPS work on latent neural models and common input, the contribution is an application-driven reconfiguration of known tools rather than a new inference algorithm. Still, the question addressed is timely and underexplored.

Significance
If robust, this approach would be practically valuable for calcium imaging studies, enabling estimation of noise correlations and activity predictions across larger populations than can be imaged at once. The ability to use overlap to align sessions is appealing. However, the absence of validation on truly non-simultaneous recordings (different FOVs/sessions) and of stronger baselines limits the current impact. Claims about “predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons” should be tempered to reflect the fictional split setting.

Pros
- Clear, principled formulation of stitching as an LDS with session-specific observation matrices.
- Strong synthetic validation with ground truth; well-designed upper/lower bounds via oracle/naive models.
- Demonstrates improved prediction of across-session correlations and cross-prediction of activity on real data splits.
- Addresses an important experimental bottleneck; method is simple and potentially adoptable.

Cons
- Real-data validation uses only artificially split simultaneous recordings; no evidence on truly sequential, non-simultaneous sessions with realistic nonstationarities.
- Weak baselines; lacks comparisons to low-rank latent dynamical models (FA/GPFA/PLDS) and subspace ID/CCA approaches.
- Potential overfitting with dense A and no regularization; no stability constraint discussion; diagonal Q/R assumptions are restrictive.
- No theoretical conditions (observability/overlap) ensuring identifiability; empirical sensitivity to overlap not quantified.
- Limited reporting of predictive likelihood, calibration, and uncertainty.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on real sequential recordings of partially overlapping populations (different FOVs or time-separated sessions). Use a small period of true simultaneous imaging for ground-truth cross-pair correlations to assess stitching accuracy.
- Compare against low-rank LDS/FA/GPFA with missing data and PLDS with latent factors; include subspace ID with time-varying Ck and CCA alignment via overlap. These baselines reduce parameter count and may improve robustness.
- Incorporate and evaluate regularization (e.g., sparsity on A, ridge on A/B) and enforce stability of A; report predictive log-likelihood on held-out time and cross-prediction error with confidence intervals.
- Analyze sensitivity to overlap size and session-to-session drift; provide guidance for experimental design (overlap vs coverage trade-offs).
- Clarify preprocessing and stimulus regressors (especially in awake data), consider correlated measurement noise (neuropil), and avoid interpreting A as anatomical connectivity.

Overall assessment
A well-motivated and promising application of LDS with missing observations to a problem of broad interest in neural population analysis. The empirical results are encouraging but, in their current form, rely on convenient fictional splits and lack stronger baselines and theoretical/empirical analysis of identifiability and robustness. With added real sequential validation and baseline comparisons, this would be a strong contribution. As is, it is a solid but somewhat incremental paper with notable practical potential.
