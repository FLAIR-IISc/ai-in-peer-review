Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a practical and important problem in large-scale calcium imaging: how to infer joint population dynamics and, in particular, noise correlations between neurons that are not recorded simultaneously. The core idea is to “stitch” sequential partial recordings into a single latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, leveraging overlap across subsets to resolve identifiability and recover cross-session couplings. The model is a linear-Gaussian state-space model with stimulus drive: x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t, and y_t = C(x_t + d) + ε_t. Parameters are learned via EM (Kalman smoother E-step), using online EM for initialization and full-batch EM for refinement.

The work situates itself among: (i) functional connectivity inference from calcium imaging or spikes (e.g., GLM-based and information-theoretic methods) that typically require simultaneous recording; (ii) latent GLMs and LDS approaches for population activity; and (iii) partially observed/undersampled settings (e.g., Pillow and Latham’s latent GLM; subspace/system ID). The contribution here is to explicitly use multiple partial recordings of the same circuit with known neuron identities and some overlap to estimate joint dynamics and predict noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs—something not addressed by standard approaches, and distinct from methods that assume monotonic relationships between noise- and stimulus-correlations.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation: recasting stitching as an LDS with missing observations is elegant and leverages well-understood inference (Kalman smoothing + EM).
- Demonstrated feasibility: simulations show recovery of cross-block couplings and strong prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs; activity prediction of unobserved units is also quantified.
- Empirical validation on two calcium imaging datasets with a realistic “fictional split” protocol; stitched models substantially outperform a naïve baseline and approach a “fully observed” upper bound for correlation prediction.
- Practical relevance: addresses a key bottleneck in 2p imaging where circuits exceed the field of view; makes a concrete step toward integrating sequential tiles into a coherent population model.

Weaknesses
- Validation primarily uses “fictional split” of truly simultaneous datasets. While appropriate for ground-truth benchmarking, it sidesteps real cross-session issues (drift, nonstationarity, changes in stimulus statistics or brain state, photobleaching, slow calcium indicator dynamics). The paper acknowledges this but does not show stitching across genuinely non-simultaneous sessions/planes.
- Model misspecification: assumes linear-Gaussian dynamics with diagonal Q and R; all noise correlations must be mediated via A. This is strong given shared inputs and measurement correlations in calcium imaging; the authors note extensions but do not evaluate them.
- Dependence on overlap: performance breaks down with no overlap; only c=60% overlap works robustly. Quantitative trade-offs between overlap, total coverage, and estimation error are not fully characterized.
- Limited reporting on reproducibility: no likelihood-based evaluation, cross-validated predictive log-likelihood, or hyperparameter choices; runtime/scalability to hundreds/thousands of neurons not quantified. Regularization (e.g., sparsity in A) is mentioned but not systematically analyzed.
- Identifiability and drift: LDS parameters are only identifiable up to similarity transforms in unobserved subspaces; the paper leverages overlap to fix this but does not provide a formal identifiability analysis under partial overlap and time-varying conditions.

Quality
Technically sound in its use of LDS+EM with missing data and known selection matrices. Simulated experiments are well designed and show both parameter and correlation recovery; real-data analyses use upper/lower bounds for context. However, the strongest claims (predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneous recordings) are supported mainly in a controlled split of simultaneous data; robustness to realistic nonstationarities and correlated observation noise is untested. The assumption of diagonal Q and R is likely violated in calcium data, potentially biasing A. The evaluation focuses on correlation prediction rather than predictive likelihood; adding likelihood-based metrics and baselines (e.g., low-rank latent LDS without neuron-identity constraints) would strengthen the case.

Clarity
Overall clearly written and well organized. The modeling assumptions, EM updates, and experimental protocols are described plainly. Some implementation details are sparse (initialization, convergence criteria, regularization strength, computational complexity). A brief discussion of identifiability with partial overlaps would help readers understand when stitching is expected to succeed.

Originality
Moderately novel. While latent dynamical and GLM approaches for partially observed populations exist, explicitly using neuron identity and overlap across sequential 2p recordings to estimate cross-subset couplings and noise correlations is a useful and, to my knowledge, original contribution in this context.

Significance
Potentially impactful for systems neuroscience, where full simultaneous coverage is infeasible. Accurate prediction of noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs could enable larger-scale models and better hypothesis testing. Significance is tempered by the current scope: success demonstrated under idealized splits; practical utility will depend on robustness to session-to-session variability and more realistic observation models.

Questions for the authors
- How robust is stitching to modest session-to-session drift in tuning or coupling (e.g., A, B changing slowly)? Can you include a stress test with synthetic nonstationarity?
- How sensitive are results to the diagonal Q/R assumption? What happens if you allow low-rank shared noise?
- What is the minimal overlap required as a function of N and T? Any theoretical guidance or empirical scaling law?
- Can you report predictive log-likelihood on held-out data and compare to low-rank LDS baselines that treat all unobserved activity as latent factors?
- What are runtimes and memory usage for N≈200–1000?

Suggestions
- Include a stitching experiment across genuinely non-simultaneous recordings (e.g., different time segments with a gap or adjacent planes) to assess robustness.
- Explore regularization (sparsity in A, low-rank-plus-sparse structures) and model selection via cross-validation.
- Extend to correlated observation noise or a two-stage model incorporating calcium dynamics (AR(1)-like fluorescence) to reduce misspecification.
- Provide an analysis of overlap vs. coverage trade-offs to inform experimental design.

Arguments pro acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem with a principled, simple method.
- Strong empirical gains over a naïve baseline; promising results on real data.
- Method likely to stimulate further work and be adopted in labs doing 2p imaging.

Arguments against acceptance
- Evidence limited to fictional splits; lack of validation on truly non-simultaneous recordings.
- Strong simplifying assumptions (diagonal Q/R, linearity) without robustness analysis.
- Limited discussion of scalability, identifiability, and regularization needed in larger settings.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is sound and useful, and the empirical results are encouraging, but the paper would be notably stronger with validation on genuinely non-simultaneous recordings and analysis of robustness to more realistic noise and drift.
