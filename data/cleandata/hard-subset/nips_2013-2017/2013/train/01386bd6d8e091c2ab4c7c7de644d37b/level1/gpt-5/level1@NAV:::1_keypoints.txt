- Proposes "stitching" multiple partial recordings by fitting a single latent dynamical system (linear-Gaussian LDS) with missing observations to infer cross-population couplings and predict noise correlations across non-simultaneous neurons.
- Uses a linear Gaussian state-space model over calcium signals and learns shared dynamics (A, B, Q, R) across sessions via EM and online-EM with known selection matrices Ck.
- Validated on simulations with ground truth and two in vivo 2-photon mouse S1 datasets using constructed (fictional) splits to emulate non-simultaneous imaging.
- Benchmarked against fully observed and naive baselines; shows accurate recovery of couplings in simulation and strong prediction of noise correlations for non-overlapping pairs (r ≈ 0.84 vs fully observed).
- Demonstrates improved cross-prediction of unobserved activity compared to baselines.
- Builds on classic LDS/EM and subspace identification literature and complements latent-population approaches (GPFA, PLDS, GLMs) used for partially observed neural data.
- Differs from prior calcium-to-connectivity work by explicitly addressing non-simultaneous observations rather than direct connectivity inference.
- Method is technically sound within the linear-Gaussian setting with appropriate EM derivations and sensible upper/lower-bound benchmarking.
- Key weaknesses: identifiability under partial observation and the role of overlap are only discussed intuitively; no formal identifiability/consistency guarantees or failure-mode characterization.
- Simplified measurement model (no explicit calcium-to-spike nonlinearity) may bias inferred couplings and limit biological realism.
- Clarity is good overall, but paper lacks details on initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity), computational complexity, and sensitivity to overlap fraction—add a performance vs. overlap figure/table.
- Originality: framing multi-session stitching as a single LDS with known selection matrices is a novel and useful application, though underlying methods are standard.
- Significance: high practical impact for systems neuroscience where sub-sampling is common; could influence experimental design by predicting cross-session noise correlations.
- Pros for acceptance: principled formulation, strong empirical gains, relevant problem, careful simulation and real-data studies with bounds.
- Cons against acceptance: validation relies on fictional splits rather than independent sessions, linear-Gaussian assumptions, lack of spike/calcium generative model, performance degradation with larger N, and missing comparisons to GPFA/PLDS or switching-LDS baselines.
- Overall assessment: solid, NeurIPS-appropriate contribution with clear utility and room for theoretical and modeling extensions.
