## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses the important problem of inferring the dynamics of large neural circuits when only partial recordings are available, a common limitation in experimental neuroscience. The authors propose a statistical method that “stitches” together sequential recordings of subsets of neurons, framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. This allows them to estimate correlations between neurons never simultaneously recorded, effectively expanding the observable population size beyond what direct simultaneous recording allows. The method is demonstrated with both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing promising results in predicting noise correlations.

This work builds upon a substantial body of research in neural data analysis, particularly approaches to inferring functional connectivity (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]). However, crucially, it extends these methods to handle data where neurons are *never* simultaneously observed, a scenario not addressed by previous work. While related approaches exist for dealing with subsampled data ([12, 13, 14, 15]), these either rely on strong assumptions or are not scalable to the population sizes considered here. The authors appropriately acknowledge and differentiate their work from these related studies.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of stitching together sequential recordings to infer dynamics beyond simultaneous observation is a valuable contribution.
*   **Soundness:** The method is grounded in a well-established statistical framework (latent dynamical systems) and rigorously tested with simulations.
*   **Significance:** The ability to infer dynamics across larger populations has the potential to improve our understanding of neural circuit function and test computational theories.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, method, and results. The descriptions of the simulation and experimental data are thorough.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Simplicity:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system, while facilitating tractability, is a simplification. The authors acknowledge this limitation but a discussion of the potential impact of nonlinearity and non-Gaussianity on the results would be beneficial.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The method appears to require substantial overlap between the recorded neuron subsets, limiting its application in certain experimental scenarios. The authors acknowledge this.
*   **Parameter Identifiability:**  While the simulations demonstrate recovery of ground truth, questions about parameter identifiability in more complex real-world scenarios remain. A more in-depth discussion of the limitations and potential for local optima would be helpful.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in neural data analysis.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The method relies on strong assumptions (linearity, Gaussianity).
*   The overlap requirement may limit practical applicability.



**Overall:**  I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural population dynamics and has the potential to significantly impact how we study large neural circuits. The limitations are acknowledged and do not detract from the overall quality and significance of the research. The paper is well-written and the experimental validation is convincing.
