

This paper proposes a statistical method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural activity to infer the joint dynamics of larger neural populations than can be simultaneously recorded. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) model. They demonstrate their approach on both simulated neural populations and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The paper relates to previous work in neural population analysis, particularly studies that infer functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data [6,7] or electrophysiological measurements [8-11]. However, the authors correctly note that these approaches do not address the specific problem of inferring dynamics across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. They also contrast their method with related work on sub-sampled observations [12-15], highlighting how their approach differs from previous methods that require strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations [12]) or do not scale to large population sizes [13].

Strengths:
1. The problem addressed is highly relevant to neuroscience, where current imaging technology limits simultaneous recording to a few hundred neurons in circuits that may contain thousands.
2. The method is technically sound, using a well-established LDS framework with EM algorithm for parameter estimation. The authors provide sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility.
3. The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real neural data from two different experimental conditions (anesthetized and awake mice).
4. The authors provide meaningful benchmarks by comparing their method to both an upper bound ("fully observed" model) and a lower bound ("naive" approach), demonstrating clear performance gains.
5. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate visualizations that effectively communicate results.

Weaknesses:
1. The linear Gaussian assumption may be too simplistic for neural dynamics, though the authors acknowledge this limitation. They could better discuss how their method might be extended to more biologically plausible models.
2. The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of performance to the amount of overlap between recorded neuron populations, despite noting that some overlap is critical for stitching to work.
3. While the authors mention that their method could be extended to more than two populations, they don't provide empirical evidence on the scalability of their approach to more than two sessions.
4. The comparison to related work is good, but could be strengthened by explicitly addressing recent NIPS papers on neural population dynamics with missing data.

Quality assessment: The paper is technically sound with appropriate theoretical justification and empirical validation. The claims are well-supported by both simulation results (where ground truth is available) and real data analysis. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of their approach.

Clarity assessment: The paper is clearly written with logical organization. The methods section provides sufficient detail for replication, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations that effectively communicate the key findings.

Originality assessment: The core contribution—using a latent dynamical system to stitch together non-simultaneously recorded neural activity—is novel in the context of neural population dynamics. The authors clearly differentiate their approach from previous work on functional connectivity inference and sub-sampled observations, and the method is not present in recent NIPS proceedings.

Significance assessment: The method addresses a fundamental limitation in neural recording technology, potentially enabling researchers to characterize dynamics of much larger neural circuits than currently possible. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons has significant implications for understanding neural computation. The results demonstrate that the method can substantially improve upon naive approaches, with prediction accuracy approaching that of the fully observed model.

Pro/con acceptance arguments:
Pros: Solves an important practical problem in neuroscience; technically sound method with clear empirical validation; provides a meaningful advance over current approaches; well-written and well-organized.
Cons: Relies on a simplified linear model that may not capture complex neural dynamics; doesn't fully explore sensitivity to experimental parameters like overlap between recording sessions; method's scalability to larger numbers of recording sessions isn't demonstrated.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a practical statistical method that substantially expands the population sizes for which neural dynamics can be characterized. The method is technically sound, well-validated, and addresses a clear limitation in current neural recording technology. The paper is well-written and provides sufficient detail for replication. While the linear Gaussian assumption is a simplification, the authors acknowledge this limitation and demonstrate that the method still provides meaningful improvements over naive approaches. The significance of the work for neuroscience is high, as it enables researchers to leverage existing imaging technology to study larger neural circuits than previously possible. The paper meets all NIPS quality criteria and represents a valuable contribution to the field.
