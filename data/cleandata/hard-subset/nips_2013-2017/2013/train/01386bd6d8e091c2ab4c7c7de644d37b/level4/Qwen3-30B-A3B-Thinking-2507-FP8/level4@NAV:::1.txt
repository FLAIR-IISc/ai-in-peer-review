

The paper introduces a statistical method for "stitching" sequentially imaged neuronal populations, enabling characterization of larger neural circuits beyond the constraints of simultaneous imaging. This approach provides a more comprehensive view of circuit dynamics that cannot be captured in a single imaging session due to experimental and technical limitations. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate correlations in variability between neuron populations not imaged simultaneously.  

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian framework. The model defines four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix capturing stimulus-dependent population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a modified EM algorithm.  

**Quality**  
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping neuron populations, but requires a minimum level of population overlap for reasonable results on real data. While the necessity of overlap in real data is intuitive, the absence of this requirement in the simulated example (Figure 2c) warrants clarification. Additionally, the substantial discrepancy in correlation estimates between simulated (Figure 2c) and real data (Figure 3b) is not addressed. The model’s strength lies in recovering pairwise correlations between neurons imaged non-simultaneously.  

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-written, though figure legends lack sufficient detail and the text fails to adequately elaborate on the figures’ key insights.  

**Originality**  
While prior work addresses functional connectivity inference from 2-photon or electrophysiological data, none tackle the specific challenge of inferring connections between neurons not imaged simultaneously.  

**Significance**  
Simultaneous imaging is fundamentally limited in neuron count, restricting circuit-level neural computation studies to small populations. This method directly addresses that limitation, expanding the scope of accessible neural circuit analysis.  

Overall, this is a strong contribution that leverages a simple linear dynamical model to recover critical measures otherwise inaccessible, offering a practical advance for circuit neuroscience.
