This paper presents a statistical method to “stitch” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, enabling characterization of population dynamics beyond the number of neurons that can be imaged simultaneously. This approach offers a more complete view of circuits that cannot be imaged in their entirety due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is validated on both simulated data and mouse calcium imaging data. The authors demonstrate that they can estimate shared variability (correlations) between populations that were not imaged at the same time.

The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model includes four key components: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix that captures stimulus-driven activity, and two covariance matrices representing process (neural) noise and measurement noise. Parameters are learned from data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality
- The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, for real data, the method requires a certain amount of overlap between populations to achieve reasonable performance. While it is intuitive that more overlap improves estimation, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated example. What aspects of the simulation (e.g., signal-to-noise ratio, model correctness, trial structure) made identification possible without overlap?
- The paper does not explain the large discrepancy in inferred correlations between the simulated case (Fig. 2c) and the real data (Fig. 3b). Please discuss the sources of this difference—e.g., deviations from model assumptions in vivo, calcium measurement noise, unmodeled nonstationarities, or differences in stimulus or behavioral variability.
- A particular strength of the approach is the recovery of pairwise correlations between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but the figure legends are not sufficiently descriptive, and the main text does not adequately guide the reader through the figures. Please expand the captions and integrate more explicit references and explanations in the text.

Originality
- Prior work has addressed inference of functional connectivity from two-photon imaging or electrophysiology, but, as the authors note, these methods do not infer relationships between neurons that were not imaged simultaneously. This “stitching” contribution is novel and addresses a practical limitation in current experimental paradigms.

Significance
- Fundamental constraints limit the number of neurons that can be imaged at once, restricting our understanding of circuit-level computation beyond a few hundred cells. This paper proposes a principled method to extend those limits by integrating sequential recordings, which is valuable both methodologically and for experimental design.

Overall
- This is a solid methodological paper. Using a relatively simple linear dynamical model with EM-based estimation, it recovers statistics that are otherwise difficult to obtain, notably correlations across non-overlapping, non-simultaneously imaged populations. Addressing the issues above—particularly the role of overlap in simulations versus real data and the discrepancy between simulated and real correlation estimates—would strengthen the paper and clarify the method’s robustness and practical requirements.
