- Proposes stitching sequential partial recordings into a single linear-Gaussian latent dynamical system (LDS) using EM and Kalman smoothing to recover joint dynamics and predict cross-session noise correlations.  
- Validated on simulations and two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (mouse S1 awake and anesthetized); stitched model predicts noise correlations better than a naïve baseline and approaches a fully observed upper bound.  
- Novelty: practical LDS-based procedure aligning multiple partial observations to infer joint dynamics, distinct from prior latent-GLM and connectivity work.  
- Strength: addresses an important, timely problem of leveraging sequential recordings to approximate population dynamics beyond simultaneous imaging limits.  
- Strength: conceptually simple, computationally tractable, and extensible to other observation types (e.g., point processes).  
- Strength: empirical evaluation includes simulations and real data with strong predictive performance on noise correlations.  
- Strength: potential broad utility for multi-day or multi-field-of-view experiments if generalized and robustified.  
- Concern: method fits linear-Gaussian LDS directly to fluorescence; unclear robustness to realistic deviations (nonlinear calcium dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, stimulus nonlinearities, time-varying dynamics).  
- Concern: identifiability — unclear how much additional information a second non-overlapping subset provides versus simply collecting more data from the first subset; marginal benefit not quantified.  
- Concern: real-data results require overlap between subsets while simulations did not; need intuition/theory and systematic ablation over overlap (including zero overlap).  
- Concern: inferred couplings correlate weakly with fully observed model; shared low-dimensional latent variability may explain covariance more than pairwise couplings.  
- Concern: should compare to models with explicit common latent factors or low-rank latent inputs to test necessity of pairwise couplings.  
- Concern: methods section is terse; M-step updates are presented as key but not derived or explained, reducing clarity about novelty.  
- Concern: missing implementation details — initialization, regularization/prior choices, handling non-identifiability, convergence diagnostics, scaling with N and T, and online-EM specifics.  
- Quality: experiments are suggestive and simulation clear, but gap between correlation prediction and coupling identification needs explicit addressing.  
- Clarity: engaging narrative but estimation procedure needs more space, derivations/references, model variants, and a schematic pipeline.  
- Originality: appealing framing and novel application scale, but if estimation is standard LDS-EM with masks, novelty mainly empirical and should be positioned candidly.  
- Significance: high potential impact if robust to assumption violations and scalable; predicting cross-session correlations is practically valuable.  
- Suggested improvements: add robustness analyses to nonlinearity/non-Gaussianity, report performance vs overlap and session count, and quantify marginal value of additional subsets.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to latent-factor/low-rank models, include full EM derivations and algorithmic details, specify regularization (e.g., sparsity on A), report computational complexity, and consider releasing code.  
- Pros for acceptance: addresses common experimental scenario with a simple framework and strong real-data correlation prediction.  
- Cons for acceptance: limited methodological detail, unclear robustness and reliance on overlap, and weak alignment of inferred couplings with ground-truth estimates without alternative explanations.
