This paper demonstrates a method for inferring the network properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then integrating the resulting sub-models. This approach, when extended to point-process data, has the potential to be applied to numerous datasets where only a subset of channels are typically monitored at any given time. I am unaware of closely related work; Pillow & Latham (2012, “Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons”) appears to be the most relevant. Overall, this seems like a powerful and promising idea.

However, it is unclear how robust this method is to deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper does not provide sufficient intuition regarding how the fitting procedure might break down with such deviations. Specifically, in the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it’s not clear what additional information is gained about the coupling matrix by incorporating additional subsets, as opposed to simply using a larger dataset from one of the initial subsets.

Furthermore, the requirement for overlap between populations in the real-data analysis raises questions. While this suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the complexity of the data, a more detailed explanation of how this overlap constrains the model would be beneficial. 

In the experiments on real data – where the linearity assumption likely does not hold – it is also important to consider the source of the explained noise correlations. While the model fits these correlations well, the estimated couplings themselves do not strongly correlate with true couplings (as shown in Figure 3b). It seems plausible that common input, rather than direct pairwise couplings, is driving these observed correlations, and that latent variables might provide a more accurate representation of noise correlations than pairwise couplings.

The authors dedicate considerable space to the introduction and discussion, but provide relatively few details regarding the estimation procedure. The equation for the M-step (bottom of page 3) is presented as a key contribution, yet is not adequately explained or derived. While this result may be straightforward for experts in latent dynamical systems, its lack of elaboration diminishes the perceived novelty of the paper. 

In summary, the authors have presented a good idea that requires further clarification to fully convey its significance. It's a neat idea tested against real data, but some details remain unclear.
