The paper contributes a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, expanding the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. This approach provides a better image of the circuit, which cannot be imaged as a whole due to experimental and technical limitations. The method is applied to both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data in mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate correlations in variability across two populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a simple linear Gaussian system. Four parameters are defined in the model: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. The parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

The quality of the model is demonstrated through its performance on both simulated and real data. However, it is noted that the model requires a certain level of overlap between populations to produce reasonable results on real data, whereas this was not necessary in the simulated data example. The authors do not address the source of the significant difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b). Nevertheless, the model's ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs is a significant achievement.

The paper is well-written, although some figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text related to the figures in the body of the paper could provide more elaboration. The authors clearly situate their work within the existing literature, highlighting the novelty of their approach in inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons.

The significance of this paper lies in its potential to expand our limited capabilities in revealing the nature of neural computation at the level of the circuit. There are fundamental limits to the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged, and this paper suggests a method for overcoming these limitations. Overall, this is a good model paper that uses a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture measures that are not easy to retrieve otherwise.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel method for inferring population dynamics from sequentially imaged sets of neurons.
* The method is demonstrated to work on both simulated and real data, with significant results.
* The paper is well-written and clearly situates the work within the existing literature.
* The significance of the paper lies in its potential to expand our limited capabilities in revealing the nature of neural computation at the level of the circuit.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The model requires a certain level of overlap between populations to produce reasonable results on real data, which may limit its applicability.
* The authors do not address the source of the significant difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data versus real data.
* Some figure legends and text related to figures could be improved for clarity and elaboration.
