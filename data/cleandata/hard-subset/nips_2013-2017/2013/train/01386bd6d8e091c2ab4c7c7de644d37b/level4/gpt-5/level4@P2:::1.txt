Summary
This paper introduces a statistical method for “stitching” sequentially imaged subsets of neurons into a single population model by casting the problem as inference in a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. The approach aims to recover functional couplings and predict correlations, including noise correlations, between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The model includes a coupling matrix A, stimulus drive B, process noise covariance Q (assumed diagonal), and measurement noise covariance R, with an offset d. Parameters are learned via EM with Kalman smoothing (including an online-EM phase) across sessions that differ in which neurons were observed. The method is validated on simulations and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse S1 (awake and anesthetized). Performance is benchmarked against a “fully observed” upper bound (all neurons imaged simultaneously; possible only in a fictional setting) and a “naive” lower bound (separate models per session combined post hoc). The stitched model accurately predicts pairwise correlations, including for non-simultaneously recorded pairs, and cross-predicts activity of unobserved neurons from observed ones.

Relation to prior work
Previous work on inferring functional connectivity from calcium imaging or electrophysiology (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al., Pillow et al.) does not address inferring interactions between neurons never recorded together. Methods that relate noise to stimulus correlations (e.g., Wohrer et al.) impose strong monotonicity assumptions not required here. Latent GLM and state-space approaches (Pillow & Latham; Vidne et al.; Macke et al.) address partially observed populations but either do not scale or do not handle the specific stitching problem. This paper’s contribution is to operationalize cross-session stitching with an LDS and demonstrate its empirical effectiveness on real calcium data.

Quality
Strengths:
- Clear probabilistic formulation with a tractable inference procedure; appropriate use of EM/Kalman smoothing with per-session observation matrices.
- Strong synthetic experiments showing recovery of cross-session couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-overlapping session halves.
- Real-data analyses (awake/anesthetized) with systematic comparisons against upper/lower bounds; stitched models approach fully observed performance for correlation prediction, and substantially outperform the naive baseline.
- Useful activity cross-prediction analyses, demonstrating practical utility.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Overlap requirement: Real-data stitching required session overlap to perform well, whereas simulations did not. Please analyze why. Likely contributors include model mismatch (calcium nonlinearity, low sampling rate), nonstationarity across sessions, and violations of diagonal Q/R. A controlled study varying overlap fraction and inter-session drift would be valuable, along with reporting performance vs overlap.
- Parameter recovery gap: Coupling recovery is strong in simulations (e.g., ~0.73 off-diagonal) but much weaker against the fully observed model in real data (e.g., Fig. 3b ~0.38). Please discuss reasons (model mismatch, calcium dynamics, unmodeled shared inputs, limited SNR) and whether adding correlated process/observation noise or a low-dimensional latent input improves estimates.
- Identifiability and conditions for success: The paper invokes standard results about reconstructing LDSs up to linear transforms; clarifying the conditions (observability/controllability, stationarity, sufficient excitation) and how overlap resolves alignment would strengthen the argument.
- Assumptions: Both Q and R are treated as diagonal in the main exposition; given known shared sources (neuropil contamination, global fluctuations), allowing low-rank plus diagonal components may improve realism and stitching robustness.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. However:
- Figure legends should be more self-contained (explicitly mark simultaneously vs non-simultaneously observed blocks; indicate overlap fraction; define axes and color scales; show error bars where applicable).
- Precisely define the fictional imaging protocol (c, overlap percentage, train/test split) early in Results, and ensure consistent notation (e.g., Ck, χkj, indices).
- Report initialization, stopping criteria, and runtime/scaling with N, T, and number of sessions.

Originality
Framing cross-session stitching as an LDS with missing observations and demonstrating recovery of correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs is novel and practically important, despite the use of a standard linear-Gaussian model.

Significance
This work addresses a pervasive experimental limitation: sub-sampling in population imaging. Demonstrating that non-simultaneous datasets can be integrated to recover noise correlations and enable cross-prediction meaningfully expands the effective population size. This is likely to be adopted by groups using 2-photon imaging and related modalities, and it sets a foundation for more sophisticated extensions (e.g., nonlinear dynamics, spike-calcium deconvolution, structured priors).

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction and inter-session time to assess sensitivity to nonstationarity.
- Explore richer noise models (low-rank-plus-diagonal Q/R) or a small number of latent inputs to capture common drive.
- Add sparsity or anatomical priors on A; report any gains on real data.
- Include a brief discussion of theoretical identifiability and practical alignment across sessions.
- Provide code and, if possible, the fictional-scenario splits to facilitate reproducibility.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common problem in systems neuroscience with a principled, tractable method.
- Strong empirical results on real data; clear benefits over naive baselines and close to upper-bound performance for key metrics.
- Broad applicability and potential to influence experimental design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited analysis of when stitching fails/succeeds (e.g., overlap dependence, nonstationarity).
- Model simplicity may limit parameter interpretability; weaker coupling recovery on real data requires deeper discussion.
- Some clarity and reproducibility details (figures, implementation specifics) need strengthening.

Overall assessment
A solid and timely contribution that introduces a practical stitching framework with convincing results. With additional analyses on overlap sensitivity and clearer presentation, this paper would make a valuable addition to NIPS. I lean toward acceptance.
