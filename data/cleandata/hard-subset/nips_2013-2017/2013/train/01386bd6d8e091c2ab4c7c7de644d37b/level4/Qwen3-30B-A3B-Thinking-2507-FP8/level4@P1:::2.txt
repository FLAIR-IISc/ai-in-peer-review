

This paper presents a statistically principled method for "stitching" together sequentially recorded neural population subsets to infer joint dynamics of larger circuits, demonstrated on mouse somatosensory cortex data. The core insight—using latent dynamical systems with missing observations to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs—builds upon theoretical work on reconstructing systems from partial observations (e.g., Takens' embedding theorem) while extending it to neural circuit analysis. Unlike prior work on functional connectivity inference from simultaneous recordings [6,7,8,9,10,11], this approach specifically addresses the challenge of combining non-overlapping imaging sessions, avoiding the strong monotonicity assumption required by [12] or scalability limitations of [13,14]. The authors convincingly position their method as filling a critical gap for studying large-scale circuits (e.g., cortical barrels with thousands of neurons) where simultaneous imaging remains experimentally limited.

**Strengths:**  
The method is technically sound and well-validated. The authors demonstrate robust performance on both simulated data (recovering coupling matrices and noise correlations with high accuracy) and real 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from anesthetized and awake mice. Key results show the stitching method predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs with correlation coefficients of 0.84 (vs. 0.15 for the naive baseline), approaching the upper bound of the fully observed model. The comparison to the naive approach (which sets cross-session couplings to zero) clearly establishes the method’s superiority. The application to real neural data—addressing a significant experimental bottleneck—highlights the work’s practical significance for neuroscientists. The theoretical motivation (Section 3.1) effectively explains why stitching works via the linear dynamical system’s identifiability properties.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Model limitations require deeper discussion:** The paper’s reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions is not sufficiently addressed. While the authors acknowledge these simplifications (Section 4), they omit concrete examples of how deviations from linearity or Gaussianity (e.g., spiking nonlinearity, calcium dynamics [16]) might degrade performance. The claim that "even this simple model makes accurate predictions" needs stronger justification—e.g., how might the model fail on highly nonlinear data? The simulated data’s weak stimulus drive (2% variance) also limits generalizability to more stimulus-dependent regimes.  

2. **Overlap requirement lacks theoretical grounding:** The necessity of session overlap (e.g., 60% overlap in real data) is empirically observed but not theoretically explained. The paper states overlap "was critical for stitching to work" (Section 4), yet the intuition for why overlap enables parameter identifiability (beyond avoiding degeneracy) is missing. Why does overlap resolve the ambiguity from the latent transformation mentioned in Section 3.1? A brief discussion of the model’s identifiability conditions would strengthen this point.  

3. **Estimation details lack rigor:** The M-step equations (p. 3) are presented as the method’s contribution but lack derivation or intuition. For non-experts, this obscures why the updates (e.g., for R and d) are valid. While the EM framework is standard, the specific handling of session-specific C^k matrices warrants more detail to assess reproducibility. The claim that "the model can easily be extended" to include common-input models [17,18] feels underdeveloped given the space devoted to the core method.  

4. **Noise correlation interpretation:** The authors attribute explained noise correlations to "pairwise couplings" (Section 3.2), but Figure 3b shows stitching predicts couplings correlating with the fully observed model (r=0.38) while noise correlations correlate highly (r=0.84). This suggests noise correlations may be better captured by latent common-input processes than pairwise couplings—yet the paper does not explore this distinction.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
*Pro:* Novel application to neural circuit analysis with strong real-data validation; addresses a genuine experimental bottleneck; methodologically sound with clear baseline comparisons.  
*Con:* Insufficient discussion of model limitations (linearity/Gaussianity); theoretical justification for overlap requirement is weak; estimation details lack depth; potential misinterpretation of noise correlation sources.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper’s core idea is compelling and well-executed, but the current presentation underemphasizes critical limitations. With clearer articulation of where the method *fails* (e.g., under strong nonlinearity), stronger theoretical grounding for the overlap requirement, and expanded estimation details, the contribution would be significantly strengthened. As-is, it is a solid technical contribution with high significance for neuroscientists, but the discussion of assumptions needs refinement to meet NIPS’s quality bar. I recommend **weak acceptance** pending minor revisions addressing these points. The work is not out of scope (NIPS 2013 covered neural dynamics [5,6,7]), and its significance for expanding experimental capabilities in systems neuroscience is clear.
