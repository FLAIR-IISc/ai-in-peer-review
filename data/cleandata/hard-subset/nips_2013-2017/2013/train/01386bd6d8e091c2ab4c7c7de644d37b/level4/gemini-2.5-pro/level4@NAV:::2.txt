This paper presents a method for inferring the properties of a large network by sequentially observing subnetworks and subsequently integrating the resulting partial models. An extension to point-process data could make this approach applicable to many datasets where recording channels are shifted between sessions, a common experimental paradigm. The proposed method appears novel; to my knowledge, the closest related work is that of J. W. Pillow and P. Latham ("Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons"), and the core concept is compelling.

A primary concern is the method's sensitivity to deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumption. The manuscript does not provide a clear intuition for the failure modes of the fitting procedure when this assumption is violated. Furthermore, in the ideal scenario where a single sub-model is sufficient to identify the full system (up to the stated linear transformation), the paper does not clarify the marginal benefit of incorporating additional subsets compared to simply collecting more data from the initial subset. Specifically, it is unclear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained.

The empirical results on real data raise a critical question: why is an overlap between the observed populations necessary for the model to succeed? This requirement implies that the linear-Gaussian simulations may not fully capture the complexities of the problem. It would be beneficial for the authors to provide some intuition for the role of this overlap in constraining the model fit.

In the real-data experiments, where the linearity assumption is likely violated, further clarification is needed. Given that the model accurately captures noise correlations while the estimated couplings show poor correspondence to the ground truth (as seen in Fig. 3b), it is plausible that these correlations are driven by unobserved common inputs rather than the direct pairwise couplings estimated by the model. A model incorporating latent variables might offer a more parsimonious explanation for these observed noise correlations.

While the introduction and discussion are extensive, the manuscript is notably sparse on the technical details of the model estimation. For instance, the M-step update equation at the bottom of page 3, presented as a central contribution, lacks both a derivation and a clear explanation. If this result is standard for experts in Linear Dynamical Systems, it could diminish the paper's novelty; if it is not, its significance is lost without proper exposition. The paper is based on a strong core idea, but the lack of methodological clarity prevents a full appreciation of the work's significance.

The paper introduces a compelling idea with promising applications to real data, but its impact is hindered by a lack of clarity in key methodological and conceptual areas.
