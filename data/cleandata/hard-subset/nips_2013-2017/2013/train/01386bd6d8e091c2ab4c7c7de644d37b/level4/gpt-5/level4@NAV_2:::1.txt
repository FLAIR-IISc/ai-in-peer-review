This paper presents a statistical approach for “stitching” sequentially imaged neuronal subsets into a single population model. The technique increases the effective population size over which dynamics can be characterized beyond what is simultaneously observable, thereby providing a more complete view of circuits that cannot be imaged in one shot due to experimental constraints. The method is evaluated on simulated data and applied to 2-photon calcium imaging in mice. The authors demonstrate that they can estimate variability correlations between populations that were not recorded concurrently.

The problem is framed as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, instantiated here as a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model includes four key parameter sets: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus-to-neuron receptive field matrix that captures stimulus dependence, and two covariance matrices describing process (neural) and measurement noise. Parameters are learned from data via a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality
The model performs well on simulations with two non-overlapping neuron groups, whereas on real data some overlap between subsets is needed to obtain robust results. While it is intuitive that greater overlap improves performance, it remains unclear why overlap was not required in the simulated setting. In addition, the manuscript does not discuss the source of the large discrepancy in parameter correlation observed between simulation (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b). The standout contribution is the recovery of pairwise correlations for neuron pairs that were never recorded simultaneously.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, but the figure captions are too terse, and the accompanying textual descriptions do not sufficiently unpack the figures.

Originality
Prior work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon or electrophysiology is cited, but those methods do not infer functional relationships between neurons that were not recorded at the same time.

Significance
Because there are fundamental limits on the number of neurons that can be imaged simultaneously, our ability to probe circuit-level computation is constrained. This paper offers a principled way to extend those capabilities.

Overall, this is a solid modeling paper that leverages a relatively simple linear dynamical framework to capture quantities that are otherwise difficult to obtain.
