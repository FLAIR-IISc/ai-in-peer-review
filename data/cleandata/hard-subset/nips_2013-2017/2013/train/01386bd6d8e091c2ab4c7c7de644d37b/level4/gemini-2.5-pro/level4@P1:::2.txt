This paper presents a novel statistical method for inferring the properties of a large neural population by "stitching" together multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, overlapping subnetworks. The core idea is to model the entire population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) where, in each recording session, only a subset of the neurons is observed. The authors demonstrate on both simulated and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex that this approach can successfully recover properties of the full network, such as the noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The proposed method is related to work on latent variable models in partially observed populations (e.g., Pillow & Latham, 2008), but is distinct in its specific goal of combining separate, complete recordings of different subsets to form a unified model.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The problem of integrating data from partial recordings is highly significant for systems neuroscience. Experimental techniques can rarely capture the full dynamics of a neural circuit, making computational methods like this essential. The "stitching" idea is powerful and, if robust, could be widely applied to many existing and future datasets, including multi-day electrophysiology recordings where probe locations are shifted. The approach appears novel for this specific application.

2.  **Empirical Validation:** The authors should be commended for testing their method not only on simulated data but also on two real calcium imaging datasets from both anesthetized and awake animals. The validation strategy, which uses a "fictional imaging scenario" with upper and lower performance bounds, is well-conceived and provides a clear demonstration of the method's efficacy.

3.  **Promising Results:** The method successfully predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons on real data, with performance approaching the upper bound set by a model trained on fully observed data. This is a non-trivial and impressive result.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of Methodological Contribution:** While the core idea is well-motivated, the paper is light on the details of the estimation procedure. The introduction and discussion sections are extensive, but the methods section is comparatively brief. For instance, the M-step update equations are presented at the end of section 2.1 as a key result but without derivation or detailed explanation. If these updates are a straightforward extension of the standard EM algorithm for LDS, this should be stated explicitly. If they represent a more novel contribution, their derivation is necessary to allow the reader to fully appreciate the work's technical novelty.

2.  **Sensitivity to Model Assumptions:** The paper relies on a linear-Gaussian LDS model. A key question is how sensitive the method is to violations of this assumption, which are almost certain to occur in real neural data. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included a more in-depth discussion of the potential failure modes. For example, how might non-linear dynamics or non-Gaussian noise affect the inference? Providing intuition on how the fitting procedure might break down would be very valuable.

3.  **Role of Overlapping Neurons:** The authors state that on the real data, some overlap between the recorded subsets was critical for the method to work, whereas this was not required for the simulated data. This is a crucial finding, as it suggests the simple linear-Gaussian simulation does not capture all the challenges of the real data. The paper would benefit from an analysis or at least a discussion of why this overlap is necessary. It seems likely that the overlapping neurons serve to anchor the coordinate systems of the latent dynamics inferred from each subset, but a more formal explanation would greatly improve the paper.

4.  **Interpretation of Inferred Couplings:** On the real data, the model provides a good fit for the noise correlations, but the inferred coupling parameters themselves show a relatively weak correlation with those from the "fully observed" model (Fig. 3b). This raises the question of what the model is truly capturing. It is possible that the observed noise correlations are driven more by unobserved common input than by direct, pairwise couplings. The authors might consider discussing whether the data could be explained more parsimoniously by a model with a lower-dimensional latent state representing common input, rather than a full-rank coupling matrix.

**Conclusion**

This paper introduces a compelling and potentially high-impact idea for analyzing neural data. The approach is well-motivated, and the validation on real-world data is a significant strength. However, the manuscript's clarity and impact are currently limited by a lack of methodological detail and an insufficient discussion of the model's limitations and assumptions. The work is promising, but I would recommend acceptance only if the authors can substantially improve the clarity of the methods section and provide a more nuanced discussion of the issues raised above, particularly regarding the model assumptions and the necessity of overlapping neuron populations.
