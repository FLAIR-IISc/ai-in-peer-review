This paper introduces a novel statistical method for inferring neural population dynamics from sequentially recorded subsets of neurons within the same circuit. Recognizing the limitations of simultaneously imaging large neuronal populations—despite advances in 2-photon calcium imaging—the authors propose a technique to “stitch” together data from partial recordings, effectively expanding the accessible population size. This is achieved by framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, demonstrated using both simulated and experimental data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The core finding is the successful prediction of noise correlations between neurons never simultaneously recorded, offering a valuable tool for understanding circuit-level computations.

The authors model the problem using a linear Gaussian dynamical system, estimating parameters including a functional coupling matrix, stimulus receptive fields, and noise covariance matrices via an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm. This approach allows them to infer the joint dynamics of a larger population than could be observed at any single time point.

**Quality:**

The method demonstrates strong performance on simulated data, accurately recovering coupling matrices and noise correlations. However, the performance on experimental data requires a substantial overlap between the sequentially imaged populations, a point that warrants further investigation. The discrepancy in correlation recovery between the simulated (Fig. 2c) and experimental (Fig. 3b) data should be addressed; what factors contribute to this difference in performance? Despite this, the ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a significant strength. The paper demonstrates technical soundness, but could benefit from a deeper discussion of the model’s limitations and potential sources of error. 

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. However, figure legends could be more descriptive, providing more context and detail to allow readers to fully grasp the presented results without extensive reference to the main text. Expanding upon the figures within the body of the paper—elaborating on key findings and observations directly tied to the visuals—would further enhance clarity.

**Originality:**

The authors thoroughly acknowledge related work in functional connectivity inference from imaging and electrophysiological data. They convincingly argue that their method distinguishes itself by specifically addressing the challenge of inferring connections *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons, a gap in existing approaches. While latent dynamical systems are not new, the application to this specific problem of stitching together partial population recordings is a novel contribution.

**Significance:**

The work addresses a fundamental limitation in systems neuroscience: the difficulty of simultaneously measuring the activity of entire neural circuits. The proposed method provides a practical approach to overcome this limitation, potentially unlocking the ability to study larger, more biologically relevant networks. The ability to predict noise correlations is particularly valuable, as these correlations are thought to play a crucial role in neural computation. This work has the potential to impact both experimental design and data analysis in the field.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a significant problem in systems neuroscience.
*   Successful demonstration of the method on both simulated and experimental data.
*   Ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
*   Clear and generally well-written presentation.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Performance on real data is sensitive to the degree of overlap between recording sessions.
*   Discrepancy in correlation recovery performance between simulation and experiment requires further explanation.
*   Figure legends and text elaboration on figures could be improved for clarity.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and has the potential to significantly impact our ability to study neural circuits. Addressing the points raised above regarding the performance discrepancy and improving figure clarity would further strengthen the manuscript. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
