The paper contributes a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. This approach enables the expansion of population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons, thereby providing a more comprehensive image of the circuit that cannot be imaged as a whole due to experimental and technical limitations. The method is applied to both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data in mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate correlations in variability across two populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, utilizing a simple linear Gaussian system. Four parameters are defined in the model: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix that models the stimulus dependence of the population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. The parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm.

Quality:
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations of neurons. However, when applied to real data, populations must have a certain level of overlap to achieve reasonable results. It is intuitive that a greater overlap leads to better results, but it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated data example. Furthermore, the authors do not address the source of the significant difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data (Figure 2c) versus real data (Figure 3b). Nevertheless, the model's ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs is a notable strength.

Clarity:
The paper is well-written, although the figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text related to the figures in the body of the paper could provide more elaboration. For instance, the figures could be more clearly explained, and the results could be more thoroughly discussed in the context of the figures.

Originality:
The authors list several works that have addressed the question of inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data or electrophysiological measurements. However, these works do not infer functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons, making the current paper's contribution novel.

Significance:
There are fundamental limits to the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged, which restricts our ability to reveal the nature of neural computation at the level of the circuit (or more than a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method for expanding these limited capabilities, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

Overall, this is a good paper that utilizes a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture measures that are not easily retrievable otherwise. The method's ability to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons makes it a promising approach for understanding neural population dynamics.
