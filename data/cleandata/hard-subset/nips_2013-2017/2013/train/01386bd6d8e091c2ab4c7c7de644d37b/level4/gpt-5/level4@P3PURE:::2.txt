This paper proposes inferring network properties of a large system by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then stitching the resulting submodels. If extended to point-process data, this approach could be applied to many datasets in which a subset of channels is typically moved each day. I am not aware of closely related work beyond J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, “Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons,” which seems most similar. Overall, this is a powerful and promising idea.

However, several aspects are unclear:

- Dependence on linear-Gaussian assumptions: It is not clear how strongly the method relies on (near) linear-Gaussian data, nor how the fitting procedure degrades as assumptions are violated. In the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from one subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it is unclear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by adding other subsets, as opposed to simply collecting more data from a single subset.

- Necessity of overlap in real data: The model appears to work on real data only when there is some overlap between the recorded populations. This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture all relevant complexities. It would help to provide intuition for how overlap better constrains the model and why it is needed in practice.

- Interpreting results on real data: In the real-data experiments—where linearity likely breaks—the method fits noise correlations well, but the inferred couplings hardly correlate with the true couplings (Fig. 3b). How much of the explained noise correlations are due to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings? My expectation is that a latent-variable model might capture noise correlations more effectively than pairwise couplings, and the paper should clarify this distinction.

- Estimation details and novelty: The paper devotes substantial space to the introduction and discussion but is light on the details of the estimation procedure. The M-step equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as the main contribution but is neither explained nor derived. If this step is trivial to LDS experts, that would diminish the novelty; if not, the paper should provide a clear derivation and discussion to convey its significance.

In summary, this is a neat idea with results on real data, but important details remain unclear. The paper would be strengthened by (i) analyzing robustness to deviations from linear-Gaussian assumptions, (ii) explaining why and how overlap between populations constrains the model, (iii) disentangling common-input effects from direct couplings in the real-data results, and (iv) providing thorough derivations and explanations of the estimation steps, especially the M-step.
