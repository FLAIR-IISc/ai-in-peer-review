This paper presents a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially recorded neural populations. This approach allows for the characterization of population dynamics on a scale that exceeds the number of neurons that can be imaged simultaneously, thereby addressing key experimental and technical limitations in studying large-scale neural circuits. The authors validate their method on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, demonstrating its ability to estimate correlations in variability across populations that were not recorded at the same time.

The problem is framed as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, for which the authors employ a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model includes four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix, and covariance matrices for both neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from the data using a variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**

The model performs well on simulated data, even with non-overlapping neural populations. However, when applied to experimental data, the authors note that a degree of overlap between the recorded populations is necessary to achieve reasonable results. While it is intuitive that greater overlap improves performance, the paper does not clarify why this overlap was not a prerequisite for success in the simulated case. This discrepancy between the simulation and real-world application warrants further discussion.

Additionally, the paper does not address the source of the significant difference in the magnitude of correlations recovered from simulated data (Figure 2c) compared to those from the experimental data (Figure 3b).

Despite these questions, a significant strength of the model is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons that were never measured simultaneously, which is a powerful capability.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written. However, its clarity could be improved with more descriptive figure legends and more detailed explanations of the figures in the main text.

**Originality**

The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiological data. The key innovation of this paper is its focus on inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a problem not addressed by the prior work cited.

**Significance**

The number of neurons that can be recorded simultaneously is a fundamental limitation in neuroscience, constraining our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level. This paper proposes a valuable method for overcoming this barrier, potentially expanding the scale at which neural dynamics can be investigated.

**Overall**

In summary, this is a strong paper that introduces a valuable method. By applying a relatively simple linear dynamical model, the authors successfully capture complex statistical relationships that would otherwise be experimentally intractable.
