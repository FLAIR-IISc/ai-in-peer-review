This paper proposes a statistical method to “stitch” together sequentially imaged neuronal populations, enabling characterization of population dynamics beyond the number of neurons recorded simultaneously and providing a more complete view of circuits that cannot be imaged in their entirety due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is demonstrated on simulated data and on experimental calcium imaging data in mice. The authors show they can estimate correlations in variability across two populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The problem is formulated as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model includes four parameter components: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix that captures stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural (process) noise and measurement (observation) noise. Parameters are estimated from data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality
- The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations. However, on real data, a certain level of overlap between populations appears necessary to obtain reasonable results. While it is intuitive that greater overlap improves performance, it remains unclear why overlap was not required in the simulated example. This discrepancy should be addressed.
- The manuscript does not explain the large difference in correlation estimates between the simulated results (Fig. 2c) and the real-data results (Fig. 3b). Clarifying the source of this discrepancy would strengthen the empirical evaluation.
- A key strength is the recovery of pairwise correlations between neuron pairs that were not measured simultaneously.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well written, but the figure legends are not sufficiently descriptive, and the text does not elaborate enough on the figures to guide interpretation.

Originality
- The authors appropriately cite prior work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiology. Importantly, those methods do not infer functional connections among neurons that were not imaged simultaneously, which is the novel contribution here.

Significance
- Practical and physical limits constrain the number of neurons that can be imaged simultaneously, which in turn limits our ability to probe circuit-level computation. This paper offers a method to expand those capabilities by stitching sequential recordings.

Overall
- This is a solid modeling paper that leverages a relatively simple linear dynamical system to recover quantities—particularly cross-population correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons—that are otherwise difficult to measure. Addressing the need for overlap in real versus simulated settings and explaining the discrepancy between simulated and real-data correlation results would improve the paper.
