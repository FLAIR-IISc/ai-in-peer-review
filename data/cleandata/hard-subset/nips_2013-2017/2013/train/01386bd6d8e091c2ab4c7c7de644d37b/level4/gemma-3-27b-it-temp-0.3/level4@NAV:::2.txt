This paper presents a novel method for inferring the properties of large networks by sequentially modeling subnetworks and integrating the resulting sub-models. This approach, particularly when extended to point-process data, holds significant promise for analyzing datasets common in neuroscience and other fields where channel availability changes over time. To the best of my knowledge, this work is unique (with J. W. Pillow and P. Latham’s work on partially observed spiking neuron populations being the closest related), and the core idea is highly impactful.

However, the practical limitations of the method regarding deviations from linear-Gaussian assumptions are not fully explored. The paper lacks a clear discussion of how performance degrades with increasing departures from these assumptions. Specifically, when the full model can be estimated from a single subset of data (allowing for a linear transformation of latent dimensions, as noted by the authors), the added value of incorporating additional subsets – versus simply increasing the data volume from the initial subset – remains unclear. 

Furthermore, the observed requirement for population overlap in the real-data analysis raises questions. While this suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the complexities of real neural data, a more detailed explanation of the role of overlap in constraining the model would be beneficial. 

In the real-data experiments, where the linearity assumption is likely violated, it’s important to consider the source of the explained noise correlations. While the model fits these correlations well, the estimated couplings exhibit limited correspondence to the true couplings (as shown in Figure 3b), prompting the question of whether common input, rather than direct pairwise interactions, is driving these results. A latent variable approach to modeling noise correlations may be more appropriate.

The paper dedicates considerable space to the introduction and discussion, but provides limited detail regarding the estimation procedure. The equation presented on the bottom of page 3, positioned as a key contribution, lacks sufficient explanation or derivation. While this result may be straightforward for experts in Latent Dynamical Systems, its novelty is diminished without a more accessible explanation. Overall, the authors have developed a promising approach, but its significance would be better conveyed with a more thorough and detailed presentation.

A compelling idea validated with real data, but further clarification of certain aspects is needed.
