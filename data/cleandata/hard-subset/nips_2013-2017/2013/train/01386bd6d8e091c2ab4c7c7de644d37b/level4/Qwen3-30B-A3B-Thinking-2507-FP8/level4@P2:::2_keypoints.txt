- Presents a method to "stitch" sequentially recorded neural subpopulations into a unified latent dynamical model to infer population dynamics beyond simultaneously imaged neurons.
- Frames the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations and validates on calcium imaging to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.
- Significant contribution: addresses a key limitation of neural recording technology by effectively expanding analyzable population size for dynamical analysis.
- Experimental validation is thorough, using both simulated data and real mouse somatosensory recordings, with performance compared to upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) bounds.
- Paper quality is high: well-organized, clearly written, and experimentally convincing.
- Strength: method has high potential impact for neural circuit analysis and population studies.
- Main weakness: limited theoretical justification for applicability beyond linear-Gaussian assumptions and unclear robustness to nonlinear dynamics.
- Insufficient distinction from prior work (e.g., Pillow & Latham 2008), especially regarding sequential versus partial observations.
- Unclear why overlap between recorded populations is necessary despite theoretical claims it should work without overlap.
- Ambiguity over whether predicted noise correlations reflect direct couplings or common input from unobserved neurons.
- Clarity issues in estimation details: M-step updates are not well-explained or derived, weakening the perceived novelty and rigor.
- Paper devotes too much space to intro/discussion and insufficient technical detail on the core algorithmic contribution.
- Key questions to address: performance under violated linear-Gaussian assumptions, necessity of population overlap, and interpretation of predicted noise correlations.
- Recommendation: strong, well-validated idea with significant impact potential but needs clearer theoretical foundations and better explanation of algorithmic details before acceptance.
