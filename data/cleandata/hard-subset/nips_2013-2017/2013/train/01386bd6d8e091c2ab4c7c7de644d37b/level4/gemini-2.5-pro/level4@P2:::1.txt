This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to "stitch" together sequentially recorded datasets by modeling the system as a latent dynamical system (specifically, a linear dynamical system) with missing observations. This approach allows the model to characterize population dynamics and functional connectivity for a set of neurons larger than what can be simultaneously recorded with current imaging technology. The authors validate their method on both simulated data and two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. A key result is the successful prediction of noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded at the same time, demonstrating the method's potential to create more complete models of neural circuits from experimentally feasible data. This work is positioned as a novel contribution, distinct from previous methods that either infer connectivity only from simultaneously recorded neurons or require strong assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations.

**Quality**
The paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by experiments on both synthetic and real data. The use of a linear dynamical system (LDS) is a reasonable and computationally tractable choice for a first demonstration of this "stitching" concept. The validation approach is strong, establishing clear upper ('fully observed') and lower ('naive') bounds against which to judge the method's performance.

The model's ability to recover noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs is a significant strength and the central contribution of the paper. This is demonstrated effectively on real cortical data.

However, a few points could be clarified to strengthen the work:
1.  **Requirement for Overlapping Neurons:** The model performs well on simulated data where the two recorded populations are entirely non-overlapping. In contrast, the experiments on real data required a partial overlap between the recorded subsets. The authors note that with no overlap, the method "failed to get good performance." This is a crucial difference. An explanation for why overlap is necessary for real data but not for the idealized simulation would provide valuable insight into the practical limitations of the method. Is this due to the simplified nature of the simulation (e.g., perfect linearity, stationarity, high signal-to-noise ratio) which may not hold in biological data?
2.  **Performance on Coupling Parameter Recovery:** There is a notable drop in performance when recovering the underlying coupling parameters (matrix A) on real data compared to simulated data. For non-simultaneously recorded pairs, the correlation between estimated and true couplings is 0.73 on simulated data (Fig 2c), but the correlation between the stitched model and the fully-observed model is only 0.38 on real data (Fig 3b). While the recovery of population statistics like noise correlations is excellent, the much poorer recovery of individual parameters warrants discussion. Addressing this could be due to model mismatch or identifiability issues and would provide a more complete picture of the method's strengths and weaknesses.

**Clarity**
The paper is well-written and logically organized, with the problem and proposed method explained clearly. However, the presentation could be improved with more descriptive figure legends. The current legends are very minimal (e.g., "Results on simulated data," "Results on anesthetized dataset"), forcing the reader to rely entirely on the main text to understand each panel. Expanding the legends to briefly describe the content of each subplot and its main takeaway would significantly improve the paper's readability and allow the figures to stand more independently.

**Originality**
The paper addresses a well-known problem with a novel and elegant solution. While many methods exist for inferring functional connectivity, they are typically limited to simultaneously observed neurons. The authors correctly point out that prior work addressing non-simultaneous recordings either makes strong assumptions not required here [12] or is not scalable to the population sizes of interest [13]. Framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations from different recording sessions is a novel and powerful approach in this context.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is high. Experimental techniques for recording neural activity are fundamentally limited by a trade-off between the number of neurons recorded and spatiotemporal resolution. This work provides a principled computational tool to help overcome the limitation on the number of simultaneously recorded neurons. If this method proves robust, it could enable the construction of data-driven functional models of entire microcircuits from a series of partial recordings. This would represent a major advance for systems neuroscience, allowing researchers to test theories of neural computation at a more complete circuit level than is currently possible.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical and practical problem at the intersection of experimental neuroscience and machine learning.
*   The proposed method is novel, principled, and effectively demonstrated on both simulated and real neural data.
*   The results, particularly the prediction of noise correlations across non-simultaneous recordings, are impressive and significant.
*   The paper has the potential to have a substantial impact on how large-scale neural recording data is analyzed and interpreted.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The discrepancy between performance on simulated vs. real data (regarding the need for neuron overlap and the accuracy of parameter recovery) is not adequately discussed, leaving open questions about the method's robustness.
*   The clarity could be improved, particularly with respect to the figure legends.

Overall, this is a strong paper presenting a simple yet powerful model that captures measures not easily retrieved otherwise. The weaknesses identified are addressable and do not undermine the core contribution. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the points raised about the discrepancy between simulated and real data performance in the final version.
