### Summary

This paper introduces a novel statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population by combining multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, partially overlapping subpopulations. The core idea is to frame the problem as parameter estimation in a latent dynamical system (specifically, a linear-Gaussian model) with missing data, where neurons not present in a given recording session are treated as unobserved variables. The authors demonstrate this "stitching" method on both simulated data and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main result is that the model can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, a task that is impossible with conventional approaches that analyze each recording session in isolation.

The work addresses a significant practical limitation in neuroscience, where the number of neurons that can be recorded simultaneously is often much smaller than the size of the circuit of interest. While related work has addressed partially observed populations (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008), this paper's direct application to stitching together separate recording sessions to infer cross-session functional connectivity appears to be a new and powerful contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper presents a creative and elegant solution to a pressing and practical problem in systems neuroscience. The ability to build a unified dynamical model from non-simultaneous recordings could substantially expand the scale of circuits that can be characterized experimentally. If the method can be generalized (e.g., to point-process models for spiking data), it could become a widely used tool for analyzing electrophysiology datasets where recording probes are moved between sessions.

2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The method is validated on both synthetic data, where ground truth is known, and on two real calcium imaging datasets. The "fictional imaging scenario" on real data provides a compelling demonstration by using a "fully observed" model as a practical upper bound on performance, showing that the stitching method approaches this bound.

3.  **Clear Potential Impact:** The approach directly enables the investigation of network-level properties, such as noise correlation structures, at a scale that is currently inaccessible to direct measurement. This could be crucial for testing theories of neural computation that depend on the interactions within large assemblies of neurons.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity on Technical Contribution:** The paper could be improved by providing more detail on the estimation procedure. The M-step update equations are presented without derivation or sufficient context. While these may be standard results for experts in linear dynamical systems (LDS), it is not immediately clear to the reader whether this is a straightforward application of existing EM algorithms for LDS with missing data or a novel algorithmic contribution. Clarifying this would help in assessing the paper's technical novelty.

2.  **Robustness to Model Mismatch:** The method's performance is demonstrated using a linear-Gaussian model, which is a simplification of true neural dynamics. A key concern is how the method's performance degrades when these assumptions are violated. This is particularly relevant given the finding that, for real data, overlapping populations were required for the method to work, whereas this was not necessary in the simulation. This discrepancy suggests that the linear-Gaussian assumptions are fragile. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing some intuition or analysis on why this overlap is critical for constraining the model in the face of real-world, non-linear dynamics.

3.  **Interpretation of Inferred Parameters:** The results show that while noise correlations are predicted well, the recovery of individual coupling parameters on real data is relatively poor (Fig. 3b shows a correlation of only 0.38). This raises an important question about what the model is truly capturing. It is possible that the model successfully explains correlations by fitting effective pairwise couplings that are actually proxies for unobserved common input from the wider network. A discussion of this ambiguity would be valuable. The authors might consider whether a model with explicit low-dimensional latent variables could capture these correlations more parsimoniously than a large matrix of weak pairwise couplings.

4.  **Practical Trade-offs:** The authors correctly state that the system dynamics can be identified from a single recording session, up to an invertible linear transformation of the unobserved dimensions. This raises a practical question for experimental design that the paper does not address: what is the relative benefit of recording a second population to resolve this ambiguity versus simply collecting more data from the first population? An analysis of this trade-off would provide valuable guidance for experimentalists seeking to apply this method.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a highly original and significant idea for overcoming experimental limitations in neuroscience. The method is conceptually appealing and its effectiveness is demonstrated on relevant, real-world data. However, the paper's clarity could be improved with more technical detail on the estimation procedure and a deeper discussion of the model's limitations, particularly concerning the assumptions of linearity and the practical necessity of overlapping recordings. Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and potential impact of the work are substantial. I recommend acceptance, with the suggestion that the authors address the points above in the final version.
