This paper presents a novel approach to inferring network properties of large neural populations by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then "stitching" the resulting sub-models together. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this method using both simulated and real data from 2-photon calcium imaging experiments in the mouse somatosensory cortex. The idea of stitching together sub-models to reconstruct the dynamics of a larger network is intriguing and has the potential to be applied to a wide range of datasets where only a subset of the network can be recorded at a time.

The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings and review the relevant literature. They also present a detailed description of their method, which is based on fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The results of the simulated and real data experiments are well-presented and demonstrate the potential of the method to accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

However, there are some limitations and open questions that need to be addressed. Firstly, the method relies on the assumption of linear-Gaussian dynamics, which may not be accurate for all neural populations. It is unclear how the method would perform if the data deviates significantly from this assumption. Additionally, the authors do not provide a clear explanation of how the fitting procedure works, particularly the M-step equation, which is presented as the main contribution of the paper. A more detailed derivation and explanation of this equation would be helpful to understand the novelty and significance of the result.

Another point that raises some questions is the requirement of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments. The authors mention that some overlap is critical for the method to work, but it is unclear what the overlap does to better constrain the model. It would be helpful to have some intuition about this and to understand how the method would perform if there was no overlap between the populations.

In the experiments on real data, the authors demonstrate that the method can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. However, it is unclear how much of these correlations are due to common input and not direct, pair-wise couplings. The authors mention that the noise correlations could be better modeled with latent variables than pairwise couplings, but this is not explored in the paper.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings. However, there are some limitations and open questions that need to be addressed to fully understand the potential and limitations of the method.

Strengths:

* Novel approach to inferring network properties of large neural populations
* Effective demonstration of the method using both simulated and real data
* Clear and concise introduction to the problem and review of the literature

Weaknesses:

* Relies on the assumption of linear-Gaussian dynamics
* Unclear how the fitting procedure works, particularly the M-step equation
* Requirement of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments is not well understood
* Unclear how much of the noise correlations are due to common input and not direct, pair-wise couplings

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and promising approach to inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings.
* The method has the potential to be applied to a wide range of datasets where only a subset of the network can be recorded at a time.
* The authors provide a clear and concise introduction to the problem and review of the literature.

Arguments for rejection:

* The method relies on the assumption of linear-Gaussian dynamics, which may not be accurate for all neural populations.
* The fitting procedure is not well explained, particularly the M-step equation.
* The requirement of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments is not well understood.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a more detailed derivation and explanation of the M-step equation.
* Explore the effect of deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumption on the performance of the method.
* Investigate the requirement of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments and provide some intuition about what the overlap does to better constrain the model.
* Explore the possibility of modeling noise correlations with latent variables rather than pairwise couplings.
