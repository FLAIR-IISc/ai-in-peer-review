This paper presents a novel approach to inferring network properties of large neural populations by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then "stitching" the resulting sub-models together. The idea is to model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, allowing for the estimation of the joint model of the activity of a neural population from partial observations of the population activity. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method using both simulated and real calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper's main contribution is the development of a statistical tool for stitching together measurements from multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit. The authors show that their method can accurately predict the correlated dynamics of large populations of neurons, even if many of the neurons have not been imaged simultaneously. The results on real data are particularly impressive, with the method able to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching that of the upper bound ("fully observed" model trained on all neurons).

The paper's strengths include its clear and concise writing style, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and methodology. The use of simulated data to illustrate the method's properties is also helpful in understanding the intuition behind the approach. The experimental results on real data are well-presented and convincing, demonstrating the method's potential for analyzing large neural populations.

However, there are some limitations and unclear points in the paper. One concern is the assumption of linearity and Gaussianity in the model, which may not hold in practice. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide a clear intuition for how the fitting might break down from various deviations to this assumption. Additionally, the role of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments is not fully explained, and it would be helpful to have a clearer understanding of how this overlap affects the model's performance.

Another point of concern is the lack of detail in the estimation procedure. The equation for the M-step is presented without derivation or explanation, which may make it difficult for non-experts to follow. Furthermore, the authors do not provide a clear comparison of their method to other approaches, such as using more data from one subset or modeling common input with latent variables.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to analyzing large neural populations, but some details need to be clarified and expanded upon to fully appreciate the significance of the result. With some revisions to address these concerns, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.

Arguments pro acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and promising approach to analyzing large neural populations.
* The method is demonstrated to be effective using both simulated and real data.
* The results on real data are impressive, with the method able to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with high accuracy.

Arguments con acceptance:

* The assumption of linearity and Gaussianity in the model may not hold in practice.
* The role of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments is not fully explained.
* The estimation procedure is not fully detailed, making it difficult to follow for non-experts.
* The paper could benefit from a clearer comparison to other approaches.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Provide a clearer intuition for how the fitting might break down from various deviations to the linearity and Gaussianity assumptions.
* Explain the role of overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments and how it affects the model's performance.
* Provide a detailed derivation and explanation of the estimation procedure, including the equation for the M-step.
* Compare the method to other approaches, such as using more data from one subset or modeling common input with latent variables.
