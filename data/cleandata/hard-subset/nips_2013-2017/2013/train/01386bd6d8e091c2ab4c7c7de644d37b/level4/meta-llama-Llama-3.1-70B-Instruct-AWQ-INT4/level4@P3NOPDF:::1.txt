The paper presents a novel statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, thereby expanding the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized. This approach enables researchers to better understand the neural circuit, overcoming experimental and technical limitations that prevent simultaneous imaging of the entire circuit. The method is applied to both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data in mice, demonstrating its ability to estimate correlations in variability across two populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian system. The model is parameterized by four key components: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix that models the stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. The parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

In terms of quality, the model performs well on simulated data, even when dealing with two non-overlapping populations of neurons. However, when applied to real data, the populations must have a certain level of overlap to obtain reasonable results. While it is intuitive that a greater overlap would lead to better results, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated data example. Furthermore, the authors do not address the significant difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b). Nevertheless, the model's ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs is a notable strength.

The paper is well-written, although the figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text related to the figures in the body of the paper could provide more elaboration. For instance, the authors could provide more context about the figures, explaining the key takeaways and how they relate to the main findings.

In terms of originality, the authors acknowledge several works that have addressed the question of inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data or electrophysiological measurements. However, these works do not infer functional connections of non-simultaneously imaged neurons, making the current paper a valuable contribution to the field.

The significance of this paper lies in its potential to overcome the fundamental limits of simultaneous imaging, which restricts our understanding of neural computation at the circuit level. By providing a method to expand these limited capabilities, the paper offers a promising approach for revealing the nature of neural computation in larger populations.

Overall, this is a strong paper that utilizes a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture measures that are not easily retrievable otherwise. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach and provide a valuable contribution to the field of neuroscience.
