

The paper introduces a statistical method to integrate sequentially imaged neuronal populations. This approach expands the effective population size for characterizing neural dynamics beyond the limit of simultaneously imaged neurons, enabling a more comprehensive view of circuits that cannot be fully imaged due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate correlations in variability between two neuron populations not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian framework. The model defines four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix modeling stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

**Quality**  
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping neuron populations. However, application to real data requires a minimum level of population overlap for reasonable results. While the benefit of greater overlap is intuitive, the absence of overlap requirements in the simulated example (Fig. 2c) is unexplained. Additionally, the authors do not address the substantial discrepancy in correlation estimates between the simulated data (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b). A key strength of the model is its recovery of pairwise correlations between neurons not imaged simultaneously.

**Clarity**  
The paper is generally well-written, but several figure legends lack sufficient detail, and the main text does not adequately elaborate on the figures' key insights.

**Originality**  
The authors cite prior work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging or electrophysiological data. However, none of these works address the inference of functional connections between neurons not imaged simultaneously, making this contribution novel.

**Significance**  
Current limitations in simultaneously imaging large neuronal populations constrain our ability to study circuit-level computation beyond a few hundred cells. This work presents a method to overcome this barrier, significantly expanding the scope of accessible neural dynamics.

Overall, this is a strong methodological contribution. The authors employ a relatively simple linear dynamical model to capture non-trivial measures—specifically, correlations between non-simultaneously imaged neurons—that are otherwise inaccessible. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and addresses a meaningful constraint in neural circuit analysis.
