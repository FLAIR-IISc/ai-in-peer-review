

The paper introduces a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged neuronal populations. This approach expands the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons, providing a more complete view of circuits that cannot be imaged entirely due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate variability correlations across two populations not imaged simultaneously.  

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian framework. The model defines four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields matrix modeling stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. Parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.  

**Quality**  
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, when applied to real data, populations must exhibit a minimum level of overlap for reasonable results. While the dependence on overlap is intuitive, it is unclear why overlap was unnecessary in the simulated example. Additionally, the authors do not address the substantial discrepancy in correlation estimates between the simulated data (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b). The model’s strength lies in recovering pairwise correlations between neuron pairs not imaged simultaneously.  

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-written, though figure legends lack sufficient detail, and the text fails to elaborate adequately on the figures in the main body.  

**Originality**  
The authors cite prior work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging or electrophysiology. However, none of these approaches address functional connectivity inference for neurons imaged non-simultaneously.  

**Significance**  
Current limitations in simultaneously imaging large neuronal populations constrain our ability to characterize neural computation at the circuit level (typically limited to a few hundred cells). This work proposes a method to overcome these constraints.  

Overall, this is a strong model paper that employs a relatively simple linear dynamics framework to capture measures otherwise difficult to retrieve.
