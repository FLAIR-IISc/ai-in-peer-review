This paper introduces a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially recorded sets of neurons from the same neural circuit into a single, cohesive model. The central goal is to characterize population dynamics on a scale larger than what is currently achievable with simultaneous imaging techniques, which are often limited to a few hundred neurons. The authors frame this problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically employing a linear Gaussian dynamical system for computational tractability. In this model, the activity of the entire neural population is treated as a latent state, where each recording session provides a partial, noisy observation of this state. The model parameters—including a functional coupling matrix (A), stimulus receptive fields (B), and covariance matrices for neural (Q) and measurement (R) noise—are estimated from the partial recordings using an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm.

The method is validated first on simulated data and then applied to two-photon calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex. A key finding is that the model can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, thereby providing a more complete picture of the circuit's functional connectivity.

**Quality**
The paper presents a technically sound approach to a challenging problem. The application of a latent dynamical system framework is elegant and well-suited for integrating partial observations. The model's strength is clearly demonstrated in its ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neurons, a feat not possible with conventional analysis methods.

However, there are a few points that could be clarified to strengthen the paper's claims:

1.  The model performs well on the simulated data, which consists of two non-overlapping populations. In contrast, the experiments on real data required a degree of overlap between the partially observed populations to achieve good performance. While it is intuitive that more overlap leads to better results, the necessity of overlap for real data but not for the simulation is an important discrepancy. Could the authors elaborate on the potential reasons for this? Is it related to the signal-to-noise ratio, the complexity of real neural dynamics compared to the simulation, or other factors not captured by the linear Gaussian model?

2.  There is a significant difference in the performance of recovering the coupling matrix on simulated data (correlation of 0.73 between true and estimated parameters for non-simultaneous pairs, Fig. 2c) versus real data (correlation of 0.38 compared to the fully observed model, Fig. 3b). A discussion addressing the potential sources of this performance drop would be valuable. This could include the limitations of the linear model in capturing non-linear biological dynamics or the presence of non-Gaussian noise in the experimental recordings.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written and the core idea is communicated effectively. The organization is logical, moving from the model definition to simulations and finally to experimental data.

The main area for improvement is in the presentation of the figures. The figure legends are currently too brief and not sufficiently descriptive, requiring the reader to hunt for details in the main text. Making the legends more self-contained would improve readability. Furthermore, the discussion of the figures within the body of the paper could be expanded to guide the reader more explicitly through the key takeaways of each panel.

**Originality**
The authors provide a good overview of related work on inferring functional connectivity. While many previous studies have focused on inferring connections from simultaneously recorded data, this paper's primary contribution is novel: a practical method for inferring functional connections and correlations between *non-simultaneously* recorded neurons. This work directly addresses a common limitation in experimental neuroscience and offers a new direction for analyzing such datasets.

**Significance**
The significance of this work is high. Experimental techniques for recording neural activity face fundamental physical limits on the number of neurons that can be monitored simultaneously. This bottleneck severely restricts our ability to understand computations at the level of large circuits. The proposed method offers a principled way to computationally expand the scope of these experiments, potentially allowing for the construction of circuit-level models that were previously out of reach. If proven to be robust, this technique could become a valuable tool for neuroscientists seeking to understand the collective dynamics of large neural populations.

**Overall Summary**
This is a strong paper that introduces an elegant and significant computational method to overcome a critical experimental limitation. By using a relatively simple linear dynamics model, the authors demonstrate the ability to capture complex statistical properties of a neural population, such as noise correlations, from partial and non-simultaneous recordings. While there are some questions regarding the model's performance on real versus simulated data and room for improvement in the clarity of the figures, the work represents a valuable contribution with the potential for significant impact on the analysis of large-scale neural recordings.
