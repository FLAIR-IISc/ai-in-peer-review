Summary
This paper presents a statistical method to “stitch” neuronal populations recorded in separate two-photon imaging sessions by fitting a latent linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. The approach enables estimation of correlations (and, to some extent, functional coupling) between neurons that were not imaged simultaneously, thereby expanding the effective population size beyond per-session limits. The model includes a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus-driven receptive-field matrix, and covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. Parameters are learned from data using an EM variant. The method is demonstrated on simulations and on mouse calcium imaging, and the authors report recovery of cross-session variability correlations.

Strengths
- Clear, principled formulation of stitching as inference in an LDS with missing data.
- Demonstrated ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons never recorded together—an important capability given experimental constraints.
- Simple, interpretable model components (coupling, stimulus drive, noise) and a standard inference framework (EM).
- Potentially broad utility for systems neuroscience where session-by-session mosaics are common.

Weaknesses and questions
- Simulations vs. real data overlap: The method succeeds on simulated datasets with two non-overlapping populations, but on real data, reasonable performance appears to require overlap. Please explain the discrepancy. What assumptions in the simulations (e.g., perfectly matched linear-Gaussian dynamics, identical stimulus drive across sessions, stationarity, lack of neuropil/indicator nonlinearities, or longer recordings) made non-overlapping stitching possible? Conversely, what aspects of the real data (e.g., baseline/gain drift, unmodeled nonlinear calcium dynamics, deconvolution errors, behavior-driven state changes, motion/registration differences) break identifiability without overlap?
- Magnitude of recovered correlations: There is a striking difference between the correlation structure in simulations (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b). Please analyze the sources of this discrepancy. Is it due to lower SNR, unmodeled common inputs, nonstationarity across sessions, or mis-specification of the stimulus drive? Including negative controls (e.g., session-shuffled data) would help contextualize observed magnitudes.
- Assumptions and identifiability: A linear-Gaussian LDS with unknown observation masks can be weakly identifiable without constraints. What conditions guarantee identifiability of cross-session correlations (or of the coupling matrix) when there is zero or limited overlap? For example, do you require: (i) a common, time-locked stimulus across sessions with sufficient diversity; (ii) minimal overlap of neurons or anchors; (iii) regularization/prior structure on coupling or noise; or (iv) equal-time binning and drift control? A theoretical statement or empirical characterization would be valuable.
- Validation metrics: Beyond qualitative correlation recovery, please provide quantitative validation:
  - Predictive performance (e.g., cross-validated log-likelihood, held-out time bins/neuron prediction).
  - Recovery error vs. ground truth in simulations across SNR, session length, and degree of overlap (including 0%).
  - Sensitivity to initialization and EM convergence diagnostics; risk of local optima.
  - Uncertainty estimates or confidence intervals for stitched correlations.
- Model adequacy: Calcium imaging introduces nonlinearities and temporal filtering not captured by a simple linear-Gaussian observation model. How sensitive are results to these mismatches? Consider a variant that models calcium dynamics explicitly or uses a spike-latent layer.
- Stimulus term: Please clarify the dimensionality, parameterization, and estimation of the stimulus receptive-field matrix. Are stimulus regressors perfectly aligned across sessions? How is slow drift or time-warping handled?
- Baselines and ablations: It would strengthen the case to compare against:
  - A naïve baseline (correlations from overlapping cells only; extrapolate).
  - Canonical correlation analysis or GPFA/LDS with missing data but no coupling term.
  - Session concatenation with alignment by overlap only.
  - Ablations removing stimulus or coupling terms to assess each component’s contribution.
- Practical guidance: Provide empirical curves of performance vs. percent overlap, SNR, and recording length to guide experimental design. Report computational complexity and run-time for typical datasets.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but figure captions are too terse, and the main text does not sufficiently unpack the figures. Please:
- Expand captions to define all variables, color codes, and axes; state n, SNR, overlap, and dataset details in each panel.
- Add a schematic of the stitching setup (sessions, overlap, latent state, and observation masks).
- Show robustness curves (e.g., correlation recovery vs. overlap/SNR) and uncertainty bands.
- Include negative controls (e.g., shuffled session labels) to calibrate effect sizes.

Originality
Prior work has inferred functional connectivity from two-photon or electrophysiological data, but not for neurons that are never co-recorded. Framing stitching as LDS inference with missing observations is a clean and original contribution in this context.

Significance
Given hard limits on simultaneously imaged neurons, a reliable stitching method would meaningfully extend our ability to study circuit-level computations. If the authors can clarify identifiability conditions, strengthen validation, and establish practical guidelines, the impact will be substantial.

Overall assessment
A promising, well-motivated method with clear potential. The linear-Gaussian framework and EM inference are appropriate and accessible, and the ability to recover cross-session correlations is valuable. The main concerns are (i) the need for and role of overlap in real data versus simulations, (ii) the discrepancy in correlation magnitudes across datasets, and (iii) limited quantitative validation and uncertainty analysis. Addressing these points—with stronger diagnostics, baselines, and clarity around assumptions—would significantly improve the paper and its usefulness to practitioners.
