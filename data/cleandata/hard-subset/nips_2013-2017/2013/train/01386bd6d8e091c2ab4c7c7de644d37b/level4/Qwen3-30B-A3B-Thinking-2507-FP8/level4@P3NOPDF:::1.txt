

This paper introduces a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged neuron populations, enabling the characterization of population dynamics beyond the limit of simultaneously imaged neurons. By modeling the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations (using a linear Gaussian framework), the authors estimate functional relationships between non-simultaneously imaged neurons. The approach is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging datasets from mice, demonstrating the recovery of cross-population correlation structures that would otherwise remain inaccessible due to technical constraints on imaging scale.

The model employs four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix, and neural/measurement noise covariance matrices, estimated via a tailored EM algorithm. The core innovation lies in inferring pairwise correlations between neuron pairs imaged in separate sessions—a capability critical for scaling circuit-level analyses. The method successfully recovers correlations in simulated data with non-overlapping populations, suggesting potential for broader application. However, real-data applications require significant population overlap for reliable results, raising important questions. The simulated example (Fig 2c) achieves high correlation recovery without overlap, while real data (Fig 3b) shows substantially lower correlations even with overlap. The authors do not address this discrepancy, nor explain why overlap is unnecessary in simulations yet essential in practice. This gap warrants clarification, as it impacts the method’s generalizability and practical utility.

**Quality**: The model performs robustly on simulated data but reveals a critical dependency on population overlap in real recordings. The requirement for overlap stems from the need for shared neural dynamics to anchor the latent system, yet the simulation setup (with fully independent populations) bypasses this constraint. The authors should reconcile this disconnect—e.g., by discussing whether simulation parameters (e.g., noise levels, dynamics) artificially decouple populations. Additionally, the large correlation difference between Fig 2c (simulated) and Fig 3b (real) lacks explanation, weakening confidence in the method’s real-world applicability. Despite this, the core contribution—recovery of cross-session correlations—is compelling and well-validated.

**Clarity**: The paper is generally well-written, though figure legends and text lack sufficient detail. Legends often omit key parameters (e.g., "Fig 3b: Correlation estimates") or fail to specify what is being compared (e.g., "true" vs. "recovered" correlations). The main text frequently references figures without elaborating on their implications (e.g., "as shown in Fig 3b" without stating *why* the result matters). Improving these elements would significantly enhance accessibility.

**Originality**: The authors correctly position their work against prior efforts in functional connectivity inference from imaging or electrophysiology data. Crucially, they highlight that existing methods assume simultaneous recordings, whereas this paper uniquely addresses non-simultaneous neuron pairs—a gap with direct relevance to circuit neuroscience. This specificity strengthens the contribution.

**Significance**: The method directly tackles a fundamental bottleneck in neural circuit analysis: the limited number of neurons observable in a single imaging session (typically <100–200 cells). By enabling "stitching" of sequential recordings, it expands the scope of population-level dynamics studies, potentially revealing computational principles obscured by small sample sizes. This addresses a critical need in systems neuroscience and could facilitate larger-scale circuit mapping.

**Overall**: This is a strong, technically sound contribution with clear practical value. The linear Gaussian model is appropriately simple for the task, and the application to real data demonstrates tangible utility. The primary weakness lies in the unresolved tension between simulation and real-data results, which requires explicit discussion. If addressed, this would significantly bolster the paper’s impact. The approach offers a valuable tool for expanding neural population studies without demanding new hardware, making it a noteworthy addition to the field. The method’s simplicity and effectiveness suggest it will be widely adopted, particularly in studies where imaging scale is constrained. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to clarify the simulation-real data discrepancy and improve figure interpretation.
