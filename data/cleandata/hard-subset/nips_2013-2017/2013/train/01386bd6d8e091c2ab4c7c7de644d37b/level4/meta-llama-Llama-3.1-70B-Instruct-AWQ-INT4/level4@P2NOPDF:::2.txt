This paper presents a novel approach to inferring network properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and combining the resulting sub-models. The method, which can be extended to point-process data, has the potential to be applied to numerous datasets where a subset of channels is modified daily. A similar concept is discussed in "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons" by J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, but the current paper appears to offer a distinct perspective.

The paper's idea is powerful, but its practical limitations, particularly with regards to data that deviates from the linear-Gaussian assumption, are unclear. The authors do not provide sufficient intuition on how the fitting process might break down when confronted with various deviations from this assumption. Furthermore, in scenarios where the full model can be estimated from a single subset, it is uncertain how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets, as opposed to using more data from one subset.

Another point of contention is the requirement for overlap between the two populations in the real data experiments. The fact that the model only works with some overlap suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulated data does not accurately represent the full picture. It would be beneficial to have a clearer understanding of how the overlap affects the model's constraints.

In the experiments involving real data, where the linearity assumption likely breaks down, it is essential to consider the possibility that the explained noise correlations may be due to common input rather than direct pairwise couplings. Latent variables might provide a more accurate representation of noise correlations than pairwise couplings.

The authors allocate a significant amount of space to the introduction and discussion but provide limited details on the estimation process. The equation for the M-step, presented as the primary contribution, is not thoroughly explained or derived. Although this may be a straightforward result for experts in linear dynamical systems, the lack of explanation diminishes the novelty of the paper. The authors have a compelling idea, but it requires more detailed explanation to effectively convey the significance of the result.

Overall, the paper presents an innovative concept tested against real data, but certain aspects require clarification.
