Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a statistical method for “stitching” together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. The approach models calcium fluorescence as a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations across sessions. The model includes a functional coupling matrix A, stimulus coupling B, and diagonal covariances for process and measurement noise (Q, R). Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant followed by batch EM), using Kalman smoothing in the E-step and closed-form M-step updates. The key claim is that, by combining partially overlapping recordings, the method can predict noise correlations between neuron pairs never recorded simultaneously.

The authors validate the approach on simulated data where two subsets are recorded in separate sessions with no overlap, and on two in vivo two-photon datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1). For real data, they construct a “fictional” two-session scenario by splitting simultaneously recorded populations into overlapping subsets, compare against a fully observed LDS (upper bound) and a naive baseline (separate fits per subset, lower bound), and show that stitched models recover pairwise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs substantially better than the naive baseline and close to the fully observed model.

This work is related to prior efforts on inferring connectivity from calcium or spikes (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al., Pillow et al.) and to latent variable approaches for partially observed populations (e.g., Pillow & Latham’s latent GLM, Wohrer et al.’s correlation inference under monotonicity assumptions). The novelty here is a simple, scalable LDS framework explicitly designed to align and combine multiple partial recordings to estimate cross-session interactions and noise correlations without requiring monotonic stimulus–noise relationships.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The LDS formulation with session-specific observation matrices Ck is principled and the EM estimation is standard. The identifiability argument (learning the unobserved subspace from one session, then aligning it with a later session to recover cross-couplings) is compelling for linear systems with sufficient data. The benchmarking against an upper-bound “fully observed” model and a naive lower bound is appropriate.
- Empirical validation: On simulated data, the method recovers off-diagonal couplings and accurately predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs. On real data, stitched models predict non-simultaneous correlations much better than the naive baseline and close to the fully observed model. The cross-prediction of unseen neuron activity is another useful metric.
- Open questions and potential issues:
  1) Overlap requirement: In simulation, stitching works with non-overlapping subsets, but on real data the authors report poor performance without overlap. Please discuss why overlap becomes necessary in practice. Likely factors include model mismatch (calcium dynamics and nonlinearity), lower SNR, nonstationarity between sessions, and registration errors. A sensitivity analysis showing performance versus overlap fraction would be valuable.
  2) Parameter recovery vs correlation prediction: Coupling recovery is strong in simulation (Fig. 2c), but the correlation with fully observed couplings is modest in real data (Fig. 3b). Please analyze the sources of this gap (e.g., diagonal Q and R assumptions, stimulus design, temporal resolution, slow drifts) and whether allowing low-rank shared noise or non-diagonal Q improves estimates.
  3) Stability and stationarity: You set Q0 via the Lyapunov equation but do not state how you ensure A is stable (spectral radius < 1). Clarify any constraints or regularization on A during EM.
  4) Model specification: The text implies diagonal R and Q (element-wise updates); please make this explicit and consider reporting results with a low-rank latent noise component to capture common inputs.
  5) Scalability and compute: Provide runtime, memory scaling, and convergence behavior for increasing N and number of sessions. Practical guidance for using online EM (batch sizes, steps) would help adoption.
  6) Real sequential data: The “fictional” split is a clever validation, but a demonstration on truly sequential, partially overlapping fields-of-view would substantially strengthen the claim.
  7) Robustness: Evaluate sensitivity to mis-assignments in Ck (cell identity errors), slow drifts across sessions, and mismatched stimuli.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but some presentation improvements would help:
- Figures: Expand captions to be self-contained (axes, units, subset definitions, overlap fraction c, number of trials/sessions). In the main text, more deeply interpret figures, particularly the differences between simulated and real-data results (e.g., Fig. 2c vs Fig. 3b).
- Notation: Eq. (2) appears to have typographical issues; please rewrite as ykt = Ck(xkt + d) + εt with εt ∼ N(0, R), and define all indices (χkj, σkj) clearly where first used.
- Methods details: Specify initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity on A, if any), hyperparameters, train/test splits, and how stimulus regressors were constructed. Clarify whether calcium traces were deconvolved or only high-pass filtered, and justify the 3 Hz frame rate for the LDS timescale.

Originality
While LDS-based stitching is conceptually simple, applying it to infer cross-session noise correlations and cross-couplings is novel and practically important. The work fills a gap left by prior methods that either require simultaneity, strong monotonicity assumptions between stimulus and noise correlations, or do not scale to larger populations.

Significance
The ability to combine multiple partial recordings to estimate joint dynamics and noise correlations could materially expand what can be learned from current imaging technologies. This is especially relevant for cortical circuits spanning thousands of neurons. The approach is simple, computationally tractable, and likely to be adopted. Its utility for experimental design (e.g., optimizing overlap vs coverage) is noteworthy.

Pros
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem with a simple, principled model.
- Strong validation on simulations and convincing improvements on real data relative to a naive baseline.
- Clear benchmarking with upper and lower bounds; multiple metrics (correlations, cross-prediction).
- Method should generalize to more than two sessions.

Cons
- Real-data validation relies on “fictional” splits; no demonstration on truly sequential recordings.
- Overlap appears necessary in practice; sensitivity to overlap not fully characterized.
- Limited analysis of model mismatch (diagonal noise, linearity, stationarity) and its impact on parameter recovery.
- Scalability and computational details are underreported.
- Some figure captions and notation require clarification.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a systematic study of performance versus overlap fraction and number of sessions.
- Include a small demonstration on genuinely sequential recordings with partial overlap, if available.
- Explore a low-rank shared noise component or non-diagonal Q to capture common inputs; report its effect on coupling and correlation recovery.
- Enforce or report stability constraints on A; provide regularization details (e.g., sparsity penalties).
- Add robustness checks to cell mis-registration and slow session-to-session drifts.
- Expand figure captions and correct notation/typos in Eq. (2) and related definitions.
- Report computational cost, convergence behavior, and practical guidance for EM.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and timely contribution. Despite its simplicity, the proposed stitching framework advances our ability to infer population dynamics from partial recordings and shows promising results on real data. Addressing the overlap dependence, clarifying model assumptions, and strengthening real sequential validation would make the paper even more compelling. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on the clarifications and analyses above.
