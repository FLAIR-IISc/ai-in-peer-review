The paper presents a statistical method for “stitching” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons to expand the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized, addressing a key limitation of current 2-photon calcium imaging techniques. This method, framed as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, enables the estimation of correlations between neurons not simultaneously recorded. The approach is demonstrated using both simulated and experimental calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The authors model the problem using a linear Gaussian dynamical system, estimating parameters including a functional coupling matrix, stimulus receptive fields, and covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise via an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method’s core strength lies in its ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

**Quality:** The method demonstrates strong performance on simulated data. However, the requirement for substantial overlap between imaged populations in the experimental data raises a question – why wasn't this overlap necessary in the simulation? The discrepancy in correlation recovery performance between simulated (Fig. 2c) and real (Fig. 3b) data also warrants further investigation. Despite these points, the successful recovery of pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neurons is a significant technical achievement. The use of a linear Gaussian system, while simplifying, allows for analytical tractability and provides a strong baseline.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. However, figure legends could be more detailed, and the text’s elaboration on the figures could be enhanced to facilitate a more complete understanding of the presented results. Providing more context surrounding the choice of parameters and model assumptions would also strengthen the manuscript.

**Originality:** The authors thoroughly review related work, correctly identifying that existing approaches primarily focus on inferring connectivity from simultaneously observed neurons. This work uniquely addresses the problem of inferring connections and dynamics across non-simultaneous recordings, representing a novel contribution to the field. The framing of the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations is also a clever application of established techniques.

**Significance:** The experimental limitations of simultaneously imaging large neural populations pose a significant barrier to understanding circuit computation. This method offers a valuable approach to overcome these limitations and provides a means to characterize larger, more biologically relevant neural circuits. The potential to reconstruct circuit dynamics beyond the scope of current imaging capabilities is substantial, impacting both research and our understanding of neural computation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant limitation in current neural imaging techniques.
*   Presents a novel and well-motivated statistical method.
*   Demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and experimental data.
*   Provides a promising approach for characterizing larger neural circuits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The requirement for substantial overlap between imaging sessions in the real data scenario raises questions about the practical applicability of the method.
*   The discrepancy in performance between simulated and real data needs further clarification.
*   Figure legends and text-figure integration could be improved for clarity.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a technically sound and potentially impactful method. Addressing the minor concerns regarding overlap requirements and performance discrepancies would further strengthen the manuscript and solidify its contribution to the field.
