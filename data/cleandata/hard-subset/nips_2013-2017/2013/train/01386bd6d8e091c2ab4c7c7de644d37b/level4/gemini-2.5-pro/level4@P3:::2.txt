### Summary

This paper introduces a statistical method to infer the properties of a large neural population by "stitching" together data from multiple, sequential, partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to model the entire population as a latent dynamical system (specifically, a linear-Gaussian dynamical system or LDS) where, in any given recording session, the unobserved neurons are treated as missing data. The model parameters are estimated using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, allowing for the characterization of the full system's dynamics. The authors demonstrate on both simulated and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex that this approach can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This work addresses the significant experimental challenge of recording from large neural circuits, whose scale often exceeds the field of view of current imaging technologies. The approach is related to general system identification from partial observations but appears to be novel in its specific application to stitching together multiple distinct, partially observed neural recordings to build a unified model.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The use of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data—where a fully observed dataset is artificially split into two partially overlapping sessions—is a clever and effective way to validate the method. By comparing the "stitched" model to both a "fully observed" model (an upper bound) and a "naive" model that ignores cross-session interactions (a lower bound), the authors provide a convincing and quantitative evaluation of their method's performance. The experimental results, particularly the accurate prediction of noise correlations on real data, strongly support the central claims of the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the core idea is communicated effectively. However, the methodological details regarding the parameter estimation are sparse. Section 2.1 presents the M-step update equations for parameters `d` and `R` but does so without derivation or sufficient context. While these may be standard extensions for an LDS with missing observations, their presentation as a core part of the methods section suggests they are a key contribution. Clarifying whether this is a standard result (with an appropriate citation) or a novel derivation would improve the paper's clarity and help the reader assess the methodological novelty. A more detailed explanation would make the work more accessible to readers not deeply specialized in LDS models.

**Originality:** The paper addresses an important and practical problem in systems neuroscience with an elegant and novel solution. While methods for handling missing data in dynamical systems exist (e.g., Pillow & Latham, 2008), this paper's specific formulation for combining multiple, non-simultaneous recording sessions to build a single coherent model appears to be a new and significant contribution. The idea has the potential for broad applicability beyond 2-photon imaging, for example, in multi-electrode array recordings where probes might be moved between sessions.

**Significance:** The work is highly significant. It offers a practical computational strategy to overcome fundamental hardware limitations in neuroscience. The ability to build functional models of circuits larger than can be simultaneously observed is a major step forward. This could enable new investigations into population coding and dynamics in larger cortical circuits like barrels or columns. The finding that noise correlations can be inferred between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly impactful, as these correlations are thought to play a crucial role in neural computation.

**Detailed Points for Improvement:**

1.  **The Role of Overlapping Neurons:** A key finding on the experimental data is that the method requires some overlap between the recorded subsets to work well. This contrasts with the idealized simulation, which succeeded with non-overlapping subsets. This is an important discrepancy that warrants more discussion. What is the theoretical role of these "anchor" neurons? An intuitive explanation of how the overlap helps constrain the model and align the latent dynamics inferred from the separate sessions would greatly strengthen the paper.

2.  **Robustness to Model Mismatch:** The linear-Gaussian assumption is a major simplification of true neural dynamics. While the model proves effective at predicting second-order statistics (correlations), it would be valuable to discuss the limitations of this assumption. Under what conditions might the linear approximation break down? For instance, how would performance be affected by strong non-linearities or bursty, non-Gaussian firing statistics? Acknowledging these limitations and speculating on potential extensions would add valuable context.

3.  **Interpretation of Inferred Couplings vs. Correlations:** The results show that noise correlations are recovered much more accurately than the underlying coupling parameters (e.g., Fig 3b vs. 3d). This suggests the model is capturing the correct effective interactions, but not necessarily the veridical monosynaptic connections. It is plausible that the model accounts for the effects of unobserved common input by fitting strong effective pairwise couplings. A discussion on this point—distinguishing the model's ability to predict population statistics versus identifying a "true" circuit diagram—would be beneficial. The authors might also consider discussing how their framework could be extended with low-dimensional latent variables to explicitly model common input.

4.  **Information Gain and Experimental Design:** The paper does not explore the trade-off between recording from new neurons versus collecting more data from an existing population. In the simulation, why is observing the second subset of neurons more informative for inferring cross-population couplings than simply recording for twice as long from the first subset? An analysis of the information gained from the second session would provide deeper insight into the method's mechanics and could inform future experimental design.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to a significant and widespread problem in experimental neuroscience.
*   **Strong Validation:** The evaluation methodology is rigorous, using both synthetic data with ground truth and a well-designed analysis of real neural data with clear upper and lower performance bounds.
*   **Impactful Results:** The demonstration that noise correlations can be accurately predicted between neurons that were never co-recorded is a powerful and surprising result with important implications for studying population codes.
*   **Broad Applicability:** The core idea has the potential to be adapted for other recording modalities and experimental paradigms.

### Arguments against Acceptance:

*   **Methodological Clarity:** The description of the EM algorithm is too brief, making it difficult to fully assess the technical contribution without prior expertise.
*   **Unexplored Limitations:** The paper could do more to explore the boundaries of the method, particularly regarding the need for overlapping populations on real data and the limitations of the linear-Gaussian assumption.

### Conclusion

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution with the potential for high impact. The core idea is innovative, and the results are compelling. The primary weaknesses relate to a lack of clarity in the methodological description and a need for deeper discussion of the model's assumptions and limitations. These are readily addressable. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors improving the manuscript by addressing the points raised above, particularly regarding the methodological details and the role of overlapping neurons.
