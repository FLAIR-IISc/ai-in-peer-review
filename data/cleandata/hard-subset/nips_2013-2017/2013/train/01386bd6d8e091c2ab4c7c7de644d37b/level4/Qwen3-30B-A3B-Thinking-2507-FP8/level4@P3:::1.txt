

This paper addresses a critical bottleneck in neural circuit analysis: the inability to simultaneously image large populations of neurons (thousands) due to technical limitations, despite the need to study such populations to understand circuit computations. The authors propose a statistical "stitching" method to combine sequentially imaged subpopulations using a latent dynamical system (LDS) model with missing observations. By framing the problem as parameter estimation in a linear Gaussian LDS (with coupling matrix *A*, stimulus receptive fields *B*, and noise covariances *Q*, *R*), they demonstrate that noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs can be accurately predicted. This approach significantly extends the effective population size for dynamics characterization beyond the constraints of simultaneous imaging. The method is validated on both synthetic data (showing high accuracy in recovering couplings and noise correlations) and two *in vivo* calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex (anesthetized and awake), where it outperforms a naive baseline and approaches the performance of a fully observed model.

**Quality**  
The technical approach is sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear model formulation, rigorous parameter estimation via an adapted EM algorithm (with Kalman smoothing for missing data), and thorough validation against both synthetic ground-truth and realistic experimental benchmarks. Key strengths include:  
- Accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs (0.92 correlation vs. ground truth in simulation; 0.84 vs. fully observed model in real data), a task impossible for prior methods like [12] (which requires monotonic stimulus correlations).  
- Demonstrated robustness across population sizes (Fig. 4a) and experimental conditions (anesthetized vs. awake).  
Weaknesses require clarification:  
1. **Discrepancy in correlation performance**: The method achieves 0.73 coupling recovery for non-simultaneous pairs in simulation (Fig. 2c) but only 0.38 in real data (Fig. 3b). The paper does not analyze this gap, despite its significance for real-world applicability.  
2. **Overlap dependency**: The real data requires ≥60% overlap between subpopulations for reasonable performance (Fig. 3c), while simulation used 50% overlap without failure (Fig. 2a). The authors should discuss why this difference exists (e.g., biological noise, model simplifications).  
3. **Linear model limitations**: While acknowledged, the paper understates how the linear assumption may constrain predictions of complex dynamics (e.g., nonlinear stimulus responses), though the results suggest it suffices for correlation prediction.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with logical flow from problem statement to methods to results. However, several improvements would enhance accessibility:  
- Figure legends (e.g., Fig. 2a, 3a) lack descriptive detail about key visual elements (e.g., "red-outlined blocks" should specify *which* blocks represent non-simultaneous pairs).  
- The text should explicitly link figures to results (e.g., "As shown in Fig. 3b, stitching predicts couplings for non-simultaneous pairs with correlation 0.38, whereas the naive method yields no such predictions").  
- The model’s scalability to >2 subpopulations is noted but not explored; a brief discussion of computational complexity would strengthen the methods section.

**Originality**  
The core innovation—using latent LDS to stitch non-simultaneous recordings for noise correlation prediction—directly addresses a gap in prior work. The authors correctly contrast their approach with:  
- Connectivity inference from simultaneous data (e.g., [6,7,8,9]), which cannot handle non-simultaneous pairs.  
- Subsampled connectivity methods (e.g., [12,13,14,15]), which either require unrealistic assumptions (e.g., monotonicity in [12]) or fail to scale to large populations (e.g., [13]).  
This work is distinct from recent NIPS contributions on neural dynamics (e.g., [16,17,18]) by focusing on *partial temporal observations* rather than spatial subsampling. The method is novel and well-positioned within the field.

**Significance**  
The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to image entire neural circuits due to technical constraints. By enabling the characterization of dynamics for populations exceeding current imaging limits (e.g., thousands of neurons vs. hundreds), it directly advances the field’s ability to test circuit theories. The method’s practical utility is demonstrated through successful application to *in vivo* data, with results approaching the "fully observed" benchmark. This is particularly valuable for studying circuits like cortical barrels, where thousands of neurons interact. While more complex models (e.g., incorporating calcium dynamics [6]) could improve accuracy, the linear LDS suffices for the key goal of predicting noise correlations—a critical metric for understanding circuit function.

**Summary and Recommendation**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to neural population analysis by solving a pervasive experimental bottleneck. The method is technically sound, validated rigorously, and addresses a clear gap in the literature. While minor clarifications are needed regarding the simulation-real data discrepancy and overlap requirements, these do not undermine the core advance. The work aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on principled statistical methods for complex data. **I recommend acceptance**. The paper’s significance is amplified by its potential to enable new studies of large-scale neural dynamics, making it a strong fit for NIPS.
