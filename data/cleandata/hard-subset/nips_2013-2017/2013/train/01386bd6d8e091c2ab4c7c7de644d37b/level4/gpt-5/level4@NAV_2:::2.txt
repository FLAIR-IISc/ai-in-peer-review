This paper argues that one can recover properties of a large neural circuit by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then “stitching” the resulting sub-models into a single framework. If extended to point-process (spiking) observations, this could be applicable to many datasets in which a subset of channels is moved across days. I am not aware of closely related prior work (Pillow and Latham’s “Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons” is the closest), and the idea appears quite powerful.

In practice, it remains unclear how restricted the approach is to data that are approximately linear-Gaussian, and the paper does not provide much intuition about where fitting might fail under departures from these assumptions. In the limiting case where the full model can be identified from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent subspace, as the authors note), it is not obvious how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by adding further subsets versus simply collecting more data from the same subset.

Another point of concern is why, on real data, the method only works when there is some overlap between the two populations. This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture all relevant complexities; it would help to provide intuition for how overlap helps to constrain the model and why performance degrades without it.

On the real datasets—where linearity likely breaks down—one must also ask how much of the recovered noise correlations (which are well fit, while the couplings show little correspondence to the reference in Fig. 3b) are attributable to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. My expectation is that low-dimensional latent variables would model noise correlations more effectively than pairwise couplings alone.

The manuscript devotes substantial space to the introduction and discussion but is quite brief on estimation details. The M-step equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as a main contribution but is neither explained nor derived. Perhaps this is elementary for LDS experts, but that would lessen the novelty. The core idea is very good, but the exposition needs to be strengthened to convey the significance of the result clearly.

A compelling idea tested on real data, but several methodological details remain unclear.
