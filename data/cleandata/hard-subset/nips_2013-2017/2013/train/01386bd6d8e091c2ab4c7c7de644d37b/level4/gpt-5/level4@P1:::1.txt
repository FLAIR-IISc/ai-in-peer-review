Summary
This paper presents a statistical method to “stitch” together sequentially imaged subsets of neurons into a single latent dynamical system (LDS), enabling inference of population dynamics beyond the number of neurons recorded simultaneously. The authors use a simple linear Gaussian state-space model with missing observations to integrate multiple imaging sessions and estimate noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded at the same time. Parameters include a functional coupling matrix (A), a stimulus receptive field matrix (B), and covariance matrices for process and measurement noise (Q and R). Parameters are learned by EM (with an online variant), and performance is evaluated on simulated data and two mouse somatosensory cortex calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized). The key result is accurate prediction of pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, outperforming a naive baseline and approaching a “fully observed” upper bound in a fictional-splitting evaluation.

Relation to prior work
The paper relates to prior efforts to infer functional connectivity from calcium imaging or electrophysiology, but those approaches rely on simultaneous data and do not infer connections among non-simultaneously observed neurons. The authors also distinguish their setting from methods that require strong assumptions (e.g., monotonicity between noise and stimulus correlations) or that address different subsampling regimes (e.g., interpolable dendritic imaging). The contribution here is a coherent statistical framework that leverages latent dynamics to bridge across sessions and recover cross-population structure.

Quality
- The method performs very well on simulations, successfully recovering cross-couplings and (more robustly) the resulting noise correlations between non-overlapping populations. On real data, however, some overlap between sessions is required to obtain good results. While it is intuitive that more overlap improves identifiability and parameter alignment, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated case but was needed in practice. Please clarify the differences (e.g., amount of data, SNR, model mismatch, or the role of known Ck) that explain this discrepancy.
- The paper highlights a notable discrepancy between simulated and real-data performance: in simulations, couplings for non-simultaneously recorded pairs correlate strongly with ground truth (Fig. 2c), whereas in real data the correlation with the fully observed estimate is much lower (Fig. 3b). Please discuss potential sources for this gap (e.g., nonlinearity of neural/measurement processes, calcium dynamics, unmodeled common inputs, deviations from diagonal R or Q, or finite-sample effects).
- The main strength is the recovery of pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, which is both scientifically valuable and practically important given experimental constraints. The fictional-splitting evaluation with a fully observed upper bound and a naive lower bound is appropriate and persuasive.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. However:
- Figure legends are too terse and do not sufficiently guide the reader through the key comparisons (stitched vs naive vs fully observed), especially in off-diagonal blocks corresponding to non-simultaneous pairs.
- The main text should more explicitly walk the reader through each figure panel, the evaluation metrics, and how to interpret differences across methods.

Originality
While related to prior work on connectivity inference from calcium imaging and spike trains, the focus on stitching non-simultaneous recordings into a unified model of joint dynamics is novel and well motivated. The use of an LDS with missing observations to align and integrate multiple partial measurements is a clear and original contribution in this context.

Significance
Given fundamental limits on simultaneous imaging, this method addresses a critical bottleneck by enabling inference of population-level dependencies across sessions. The ability to estimate noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly impactful and should be of broad interest to experimentalists and theorists working with large-scale imaging.

Pros
- Clear problem formulation with a simple, tractable model.
- Compelling demonstration that pairwise correlations across non-simultaneous recordings can be recovered.
- Sensible benchmarking with upper/lower bounds using fictional splits.
- Practical relevance for current imaging technologies.

Cons / questions for the authors
- Why does the stitching work without overlap in simulations but require overlap in real data? Please analyze the conditions (e.g., SNR, model mismatch, stimulus drive) under which overlap becomes necessary.
- Please discuss the substantial drop in coupling correlation between simulations (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b). What factors most likely drive this difference?
- Improve figure legends and expand the narrative around key figures to better explain what is shown and how to interpret off-diagonal blocks and evaluation metrics.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely contribution. Using a relatively simple linear dynamical model, the authors convincingly demonstrate recovery of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a capability that can materially expand what current imaging experiments can reveal about circuit dynamics. With clarifications regarding overlap requirements, discussion of performance gaps between simulation and real data, and improved figure explanations, this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
