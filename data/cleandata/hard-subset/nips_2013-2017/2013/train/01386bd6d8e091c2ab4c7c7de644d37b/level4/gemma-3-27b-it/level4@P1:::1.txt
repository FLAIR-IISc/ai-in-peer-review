The paper contributes a statistical method to “stitch” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. This method expands the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized, going beyond the limitations of simultaneously imaged neurons and providing a more complete picture of circuits that cannot be imaged as a whole due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is demonstrated using both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, showing that it can estimate correlations in variability across populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically using a simple linear Gaussian system. Four parameters are defined within the model: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix modeling the stimulus dependence of population activity, and covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**

The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations of neurons. However, when applied to real data, populations require a certain degree of overlap to yield reasonable results. The rationale behind this difference – why overlap wasn't necessary in the simulated data – is not fully explained. 

Additionally, the authors do not address the substantial difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b). While the model successfully recovers pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs, the discrepancy in correlation strengths across the datasets warrants further discussion.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written. However, the figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text relating to figures within the main body of the paper does not sufficiently elaborate on the figure content. Providing more detail within the text would enhance understanding and facilitate reproduction of the results.

**Originality**

The authors appropriately cite several works addressing functional connectivity inference from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiological data. However, they correctly point out that these existing approaches do not address the specific challenge of inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons, highlighting the novelty of their contribution.

**Significance**

There are inherent limitations to the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged, restricting our ability to fully understand neural computation at the circuit level. This paper offers a valuable method for overcoming these limitations, potentially enabling the characterization of larger and more complex neural circuits.

Overall, this is a strong paper that leverages a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture information that is otherwise difficult to obtain. The approach provides a promising avenue for advancing our understanding of neural circuit function.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to a significant problem in neural data analysis.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
*   Clear and well-structured presentation of the method and results.
*   Successful application to both simulated and experimental data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The reason for the necessity of overlap in real data but not in simulated data is not explained.
*   The discrepancy in correlation strengths between simulated and real data requires further investigation.
*   Figure legends and text-figure connections could be improved for clarity.
