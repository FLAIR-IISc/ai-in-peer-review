This paper presents a statistical method to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, sequential recordings of smaller, partially overlapping subsets of that population. The core problem addressed is a significant bottleneck in experimental neuroscience, where techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record from a fraction of the neurons in a functional circuit at any one time. The authors frame this "stitching" problem as one of fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. Specifically, they use a linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) where the activity of the entire neural population is the latent state, and each recording session provides a partial, noisy observation of this state. The model parameters are learned via an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The approach is validated on both simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main contribution is demonstrating that this method can successfully predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

This work builds upon a rich literature of inferring functional connectivity from neural data, but it distinguishes itself by tackling the novel and challenging problem of non-simultaneous recordings. While prior work has focused on inferring connectivity from fully observed or subsampled *simultaneous* recordings, this paper provides a principled way to integrate data collected across different experimental sessions into a single, coherent model of circuit dynamics.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper addresses a highly relevant and practical problem in systems neuroscience. The ability to computationally combine partial recordings to model a larger circuit could significantly expand the scope of questions that can be addressed with current imaging technologies. The method could influence future experimental design, for instance, by providing a framework to optimize the trade-off between the number of neurons recorded per session and the total number of unique neurons covered across sessions.
*   **Principled Approach:** The formulation of the problem as a latent variable model with missing data is elegant and statistically sound. Using a standard framework like an LDS makes the approach understandable and extensible.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The demonstration on both simulated and real neural data is convincing. In particular, the ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with high accuracy (approaching an upper bound set by a model trained on fully observed data) is a strong result. The comparison against a "naive" lower-bound model effectively highlights the method's value.
*   **Clarity of Motivation:** The introduction clearly explains the experimental limitations and frames the scientific question in an accessible way.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors:**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in the gap between the performance on simulated and real data, and the lack of a thorough discussion about the reasons for this gap.

1.  **Role of Overlapping Neurons:** The model performs well on simulated data with two completely non-overlapping sets of neurons. However, the authors state that on the experimental data, they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial point that deserves more investigation. Why is overlap necessary in the real-world scenario but not in the idealized simulation? Is this discrepancy due to higher noise levels, shorter recording times, or a fundamental mismatch between the linear-Gaussian model and the true neural dynamics? A more detailed analysis of this point would be invaluable for experimenters seeking to apply this method.
2.  **Accuracy of Parameter Recovery:** There is a significant difference in the quality of the recovered coupling matrix between the simulation (Fig 2c, correlation 0.73) and the real data (Fig 3b, correlation 0.38). The authors briefly suggest this could be due to "near degeneracy of parameters for large systems." However, it is more likely a consequence of model mismatch. The linear-Gaussian assumption is a strong simplification of real neural dynamics. A discussion acknowledging this limitation and speculating on how it might affect parameter identifiability versus the ability to predict second-order statistics (like correlations) would strengthen the paper. The fact that correlation recovery remains high while parameter recovery degrades is an interesting finding in itself and warrants further comment.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   **Quality:** The technical approach is sound. The use of an LDS and EM is standard and appropriate for the problem as formulated. The experiments are well-designed, particularly the "fictional imaging scenario" which allows for quantitative evaluation against an upper bound. The main concern regarding quality relates to the points raised above about the discrepancy between simulation and real-data results, which could be addressed with more discussion.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. However, the presentation could be improved by making the figures more self-contained. The figure legends are sparse and require the reader to hunt through the main text for a full explanation of each panel. For example, in Figure 2a, the legend should explicitly state what the different matrices (top left, top middle, bottom middle) represent. Similarly, in Figure 3, the legend could more clearly describe the "fully observed," "stitch," and "naive" conditions.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While latent dynamical systems are not new, their application to the problem of stitching together non-simultaneous neural recordings to infer population-wide dynamics is a novel and important contribution. The authors do a good job of situating their work with respect to previous studies on functional connectivity and models with missing data.
*   **Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. It offers a practical solution to a common and significant limitation in experimental neuroscience. If proven robust, this method could become a standard tool for analyzing large-scale imaging data, enabling researchers to build more complete models of neural circuits than would otherwise be possible.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pros:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem in neuroscience with an elegant and principled statistical method.
*   Demonstrates strong performance in predicting unobserved noise correlations on real neural data.
*   The approach is novel and represents a significant advance over prior work on functional connectivity.

**Cons:**
*   Insufficient discussion on the discrepancy between results on simulated data (no overlap needed) and real data (overlap is critical).
*   The degradation in parameter recovery on real data is not fully explored, likely pointing to limitations of the model assumptions that should be discussed more openly.
*   Figure legends could be more descriptive to improve readability.

Overall, this is a very good paper presenting a novel and important contribution. The method is well-motivated, the technical approach is sound, and the results are compelling. The weaknesses are primarily in the discussion and could be addressed by adding a more nuanced analysis of the model's limitations and the practical requirements (e.g., neuron overlap) for its successful application. The work is a strong candidate for acceptance at NIPS.
