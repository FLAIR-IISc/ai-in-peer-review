This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the properties of a large neural population by "stitching" together datasets from sequentially recorded, partially overlapping subsets of neurons. The primary goal is to characterize population dynamics on a scale that exceeds the technical limits of simultaneous recording technologies, such as two-photon calcium imaging. The authors model the neural activity as a linear Gaussian state-space model, effectively treating the problem as one of fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The model is defined by four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix, and covariance matrices for both neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from the mouse visual cortex, demonstrating its ability to estimate noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded at the same time.

**Quality**

A key strength of the proposed method is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neurons, a valuable contribution to the analysis of large-scale neural recordings.

The model performs well on simulated data, even when the two recorded populations are completely non-overlapping. However, when applied to real experimental data, the authors note that a significant degree of overlap between the neuronal populations is required to achieve reasonable results. While it is intuitive that performance improves with greater overlap, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why this overlap was not necessary for the simulated data but appears crucial for the experimental data. Clarifying the conditions under which overlap is required would be valuable for potential users of this method.

Furthermore, there is a notable discrepancy in the magnitude of the recovered correlations between the simulated data (Figure 2c) and the real data (Figure 3b). The paper does not address the potential sources of this difference. A discussion on whether this stems from model mismatch, properties of the biological data, or other factors would provide important context for interpreting the results.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written and the core ideas are communicated effectively. However, the clarity could be significantly improved with more detailed figures and legends. The current figure legends are too brief and do not provide sufficient information to fully interpret the results without extensive reference to the main text. Similarly, the descriptions of the figures within the body of the paper could be expanded to more thoroughly guide the reader through the presented data and its implications.

**Originality**

The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on inferring functional connectivity from neural data. While previous studies have addressed this problem for simultaneously recorded neurons using various imaging or electrophysiological techniques, the novelty of this work lies in its specific focus on inferring functional connections between *non-simultaneously* recorded neurons. This is a non-trivial extension that addresses a distinct and important problem in systems neuroscience.

**Significance**

Fundamental experimental and technical constraints limit the number of neurons that can be recorded simultaneously. This limitation poses a significant barrier to understanding neural computation at the circuit level, which often involves the coordinated activity of thousands or millions of cells. The method proposed in this paper offers a principled way to partially overcome this barrier by computationally integrating data from multiple recording sessions. By enabling the characterization of larger effective populations, this work has the potential to advance our ability to study circuit-wide neural dynamics.

**Overall Impression**

This is a solid paper that presents an elegant and useful method. The authors use a relatively simple linear dynamical model to address a challenging problem and successfully capture network properties, such as cross-population correlations, that are otherwise difficult or impossible to measure directly. Despite the minor weaknesses in the evaluation and presentation noted above, the work represents a valuable contribution to the field of computational neuroscience.
