This paper presents a statistical method for integrating activity data from sequentially imaged neuronal populations, effectively expanding the size of the population available for studying circuit dynamics. This is a significant contribution given the experimental and technical limitations that prevent simultaneous imaging of entire neural circuits. The authors demonstrate the utility of their method using both simulated and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, showing that they can estimate correlations in neuronal variability even between populations that were not imaged concurrently.

The authors frame the problem as estimating the parameters of a latent dynamical system with missing data. Specifically, they employ a linear Gaussian system defined by four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix representing neuronal interactions, a stimulus receptive field matrix capturing stimulus-dependent activity, and two covariance matrices representing neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a modified Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality:**

The method performs well on simulated data involving two non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, application to real data requires a substantial degree of overlap between the imaged populations to yield reliable results. While the benefit of increased overlap is intuitive – more shared neurons strengthen parameter estimation – the paper doesn’t adequately explain why overlap wasn’t necessary to achieve good performance in the simulation. This discrepancy warrants further investigation. 

Furthermore, the authors do not address the notable difference in the magnitude of correlations observed in the simulated data (Fig. 2c) compared to the experimental data (Fig. 3b). A discussion of potential reasons for this disparity, such as differences in noise levels or the complexity of real neural activity, would strengthen the analysis. Despite these concerns, the ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons not measured simultaneously remains a key strength of this approach.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and the methodology is reasonably clear. However, the figure legends lack sufficient detail and the text accompanying the figures does not adequately elaborate on the presented results. Providing more descriptive figure legends and expanding the discussion of the figures within the main text would greatly enhance readability and understanding.

**Originality:**

The authors acknowledge existing work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiological data. However, they correctly point out that these methods typically focus on simultaneously recorded neurons. This work distinguishes itself by specifically addressing the challenge of inferring functional connections between *non-simultaneously* imaged populations, representing a novel contribution to the field.

**Significance:**

A fundamental limitation in systems neuroscience is the inability to simultaneously record from a large number of neurons. This constraint hinders our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level. This paper offers a promising method for overcoming this limitation, allowing researchers to analyze larger, more comprehensive datasets and gain deeper insights into neural circuit function. 

**Overall:**

This is a strong methodological paper that effectively leverages a relatively simple linear dynamics model to extract valuable information – particularly pairwise correlations – that would otherwise be difficult to obtain. Addressing the concerns regarding the overlap requirement and the discrepancy in correlation magnitudes would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance.
