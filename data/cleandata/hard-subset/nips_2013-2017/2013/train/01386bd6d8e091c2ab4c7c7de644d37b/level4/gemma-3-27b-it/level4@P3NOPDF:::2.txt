This paper presents a novel method for inferring the network properties of large systems by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then integrating the resulting sub-models. This approach holds particular promise for point-process data, a common scenario in many datasets where only a subset of channels is typically monitored at any given time. While I am unaware of directly comparable work – Pillow & Latham (2013) on neural characterization in partially observed spiking neurons represents a related effort – the core idea appears powerful and potentially broadly applicable.

However, several aspects of the paper warrant further consideration. A significant concern is the method’s sensitivity to deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper does not provide sufficient intuition regarding when and how the fitting procedure might break down as these assumptions are violated. While the authors acknowledge the case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset, it remains unclear what additional information is gained by incorporating further subsets beyond simply increasing the dataset size from the initial selection. A clearer justification for the sequential, subnetwork-based approach would strengthen the argument.

The observation that the model requires overlap between populations when applied to real data is also puzzling. This clearly suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the complexities of real-world data. The authors should offer at least some theoretical or empirical insight into *why* this overlap is necessary to adequately constrain the model. Is it a consequence of unmodeled non-linearities, shared input, or another factor?

Furthermore, when analyzing real data where the linearity assumption is likely compromised, the interpretation of explained noise correlations requires careful consideration. The paper demonstrates good fitting of these correlations, but Figure 3b reveals a weak correlation between the estimated couplings and the true couplings. This raises the question of whether the explained variance in noise correlations is largely attributable to common input rather than direct, pairwise interactions. A model incorporating latent variables to account for shared driving forces might provide a more accurate representation of the underlying network dynamics.

A major weakness lies in the limited detail provided regarding the core estimation procedure. The equation presented on the bottom of page 3, highlighted as a key contribution, lacks adequate explanation or derivation. While it may be a straightforward result for experts in Latent Dirichlet State-Space models (LDS), this assumption undermines the paper’s novelty and accessibility. A more thorough explanation, geared towards a broader audience, is crucial for appreciating the significance of this step. The paper dedicates considerable space to the introduction and discussion, but disproportionately little to the technical details of the estimation algorithm.

In summary, the authors present a promising idea with a compelling potential application. However, the paper falls short in clearly articulating the limitations of the method, explaining the intuition behind key observations, and providing sufficient technical detail regarding the estimation process. Addressing these concerns would significantly strengthen the paper and more effectively convey the significance of the results. 

**Recommendation:** While the idea is strong, I recommend rejection in its current form. Major revisions addressing the points outlined above are necessary before reconsideration.
