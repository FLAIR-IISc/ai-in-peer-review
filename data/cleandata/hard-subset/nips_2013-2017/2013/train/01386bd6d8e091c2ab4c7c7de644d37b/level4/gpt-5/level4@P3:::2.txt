Summary
The paper proposes a statistical “stitching” approach to infer population dynamics from multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea is to fit a single latent linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations, where each session observes a subset of neurons via a known selection matrix. Parameters are learned by EM (with online EM for efficiency), and the model is evaluated on simulated data and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main empirical claim is that one can accurately predict noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded simultaneously, and that stitched models approach the performance of a model trained on fully simultaneous data, while greatly outperforming a naive baseline that fits each session independently.

Relation to prior work
This work is closely related to latent dynamical models for partially observed neural populations (e.g., Pillow and Latham, NIPS 2008) and to connectivity inference from imaging and multi-electrode recordings (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011; Stetter et al., 2012; Pillow et al., 2008). It contrasts with Wohrer et al. (2010), which assumes a specific link between stimulus and noise correlations, and with models that either do not scale to large populations or do not handle non-simultaneous observations. The stitching idea also connects to classical system identification with missing data and subspace methods. Compared to this literature, the main novelty is a practical procedure that uses non-simultaneous, partially overlapping data to recover joint dynamics sufficient to predict cross-session noise correlations.

Quality
- Soundness: Modeling the problem as an LDS with session-specific observation matrices is appropriate and principled. The empirical evaluation is well designed with synthetic data (ground truth known) and real data with “fictional” splitting to establish upper and lower bounds.
- Evidence: The method convincingly predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously observed pairs on both synthetic and real datasets, with large improvements over the naive baseline. However, the method’s ability to recover the actual coupling matrix A is limited on real data (e.g., low correlations with the fully observed estimate for off-diagonal blocks), suggesting that while correlations are well captured, inferred couplings should be interpreted cautiously as “functional.”
- Missing details: The estimation section is too brief. The M-step updates for R and d are presented without derivation or discussion of session-specific effects, initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity), stability constraints, or convergence behavior. This makes it hard to assess robustness.
- Assumptions and failure modes: The paper does not adequately analyze when the linear-Gaussian approximation is sufficient, nor how deviations (nonlinear spike–calcium transformations, non-Gaussian noise, nonstationarity across sessions) affect identifiability and prediction.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, but key algorithmic details are underspecified. Important points that need clearer exposition:
- Provide the full EM derivation for a multi-session LDS with known neuron identities (Ck), including the sufficient statistics aggregated across sessions, and discuss computational complexity.
- Clarify how session-specific baselines and noise levels are handled (d and R): are these per-neuron and shared across sessions, or session-specific?
- Explain identifiability: overlapping neurons anchor the latent coordinate system and resolve the similarity-transform ambiguity across sessions. This gives intuition for why overlap helps on real data.
- Describe initialization, regularization (e.g., shrinkage or sparsity on A), and checks for stability/stationarity.

Originality
The idea of stitching sequential partial recordings into a single dynamical model aimed at predicting cross-session noise correlations is novel and timely. Relative to prior partially observed models, the paper’s contribution is a practical, scalable procedure evaluated on modern calcium imaging data.

Significance
If robust, this approach could substantially expand the effective population sizes accessible with current imaging technologies, enabling estimation of noise correlations and shared variability across thousands of neurons without fully simultaneous recordings. This is particularly valuable for experimental designs with repeated, partially overlapping fields of view. However, the practical impact hinges on clarifying the method’s limits under model mismatch and on providing stronger guidance about overlap requirements and robustness to common input.

Specific suggestions
- Provide EM derivations and algorithmic details (sufficient statistics across sessions, complexity, convergence).
- Analyze identifiability and the role of overlap: simulations varying overlap size to quantify its effect on correlation prediction and coupling recovery; explain how shared neurons align the latent state across sessions.
- Robustness to model mismatch: simulate spiking GLM data with a biophysical spike-to-calcium measurement model and fit the LDS; or include a low-dimensional latent input (common drive) to Q, and compare performance against a model with only pairwise couplings.
- Disentangle stimulus vs. noise: report how much of the predicted correlations comes from stimulus drive (B u) vs. internally generated variability.
- Address session nonstationarities: discuss baseline drift, neuropil contamination, and session-specific scaling; consider session-specific d and R.
- Consider simple priors/regularization (e.g., sparsity on A), which the paper hints can help; report their effect.
- Calibrate claims: emphasize correlation prediction as the primary validated outcome; avoid over-interpreting A as connectivity on real data.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common experimental scenario; potentially high impact.
- Conceptually simple, computationally tractable, and scalable.
- Strong empirical results for predicting cross-session noise correlations on both synthetic and real datasets.
- Clear comparison to upper and lower bounds.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient methodological detail and derivation; reproducibility and robustness are unclear.
- Limited analysis of when and why the method works, especially under model mismatch.
- Dependence on overlap is observed but not analyzed; practical guidance is missing.
- Recovery of couplings is weak on real data; claims about connectivity should be tempered.

Overall
A neat and promising idea with encouraging empirical results on predicting noise correlations from non-simultaneous recordings. I recommend acceptance if the authors substantially strengthen the methods section, clarify identifiability and overlap requirements, and add robustness analyses; otherwise, this is a strong borderline paper.
