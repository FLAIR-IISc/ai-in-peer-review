This paper introduces a novel method for inferring the properties of a large network by sequentially observing smaller, overlapping subnetworks and "stitching" together the resulting sub-models. The core idea is powerful and appears to be novel; the most closely related work seems to be Pillow and Latham's "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons." The proposed framework, if extended to point-process data, could have a significant impact on the analysis of neurophysiological datasets where, for instance, a subset of recording channels is repositioned daily.

Despite the promising direction, several aspects of the paper require clarification. A key concern is the method's reliance on the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper provides limited intuition on how the fitting procedure might degrade as data deviates from this assumption, which is critical for understanding its practical applicability. For example, in cases where the full model is identifiable from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of latent dimensions, as noted by the authors), it is unclear what additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating subsequent subsets, compared to simply using a larger dataset from the initial sub-network. A more thorough analysis of the method's robustness and limitations would significantly strengthen the paper.

Furthermore, the results on real data raise important questions. The empirical finding that the model requires overlap between sub-populations to succeed is particularly telling. This suggests that the idealized linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the complexities of the real-world problem. The paper would benefit from an intuitive explanation of how this overlap helps to constrain the model in practice. Relatedly, in the experiments on real data (where the linearity assumption is likely violated), the model fits noise correlations well, yet the inferred couplings show little correlation with the true couplings (as seen in Figure 3b). This discrepancy raises concerns about model misspecification. It is plausible that the observed noise correlations are driven more by unmodeled common input than by direct, pairwise couplings. In such cases, a model with latent variables might provide a more accurate and parsimonious explanation.

Finally, the manuscript is notably light on the technical details of the estimation procedure. While the introduction and discussion are extensive, the core methodology is not explained in sufficient depth. For example, the M-step update equation, presented as a primary contribution, is stated without derivation or detailed explanation. This makes it difficult for a non-expert to assess the technical novelty; if this is a standard result for Linear Dynamical Systems, the paper's contribution would be significantly diminished. While the core idea is excellent, its presentation needs to be improved to properly convey the significance of the results.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful idea, supported by tests on both simulated and real data. However, the manuscript's clarity is hampered by a lack of technical detail, an insufficient discussion of the model's limitations, and unresolved questions about the interpretation of the empirical results.
