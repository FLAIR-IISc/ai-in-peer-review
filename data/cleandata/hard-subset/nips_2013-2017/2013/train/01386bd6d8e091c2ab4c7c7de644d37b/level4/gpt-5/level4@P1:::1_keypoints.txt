- Paper introduces a statistical method to stitch sequentially imaged neuron subsets into a single latent dynamical system (LDS) using a linear Gaussian state-space model with missing observations.  
- Model parameters include coupling matrix A, stimulus receptive field B, and process/measurement covariances Q and R; parameters learned by EM (including an online variant).  
- Evaluated on simulations and two mouse somatosensory calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized).  
- Key result: accurate prediction of pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, outperforming a naive baseline and approaching a “fully observed” upper bound in fictional-splitting tests.  
- Novelty: unlike prior connectivity inference methods that require simultaneous recordings or strong assumptions, this work stitches non-simultaneous sessions into a unified latent-dynamics framework.  
- Strength: simple, tractable formulation that compellingly recovers cross-population noise correlations important for experiments limited by simultaneous imaging capacity.  
- Simulation performance is strong, recovering cross-couplings and noise correlations robustly.  
- Real-data performance requires some session overlap for good results, unlike simulations—need analysis of why (e.g., data amount, SNR, model mismatch, role of known Ck).  
- Notable discrepancy: couplings correlate strongly with ground truth in simulation (Fig. 2c) but much less so with the fully observed estimate on real data (Fig. 3b); potential causes include nonlinearities, calcium dynamics, unmodeled common inputs, non-diagonal R/Q, and finite-sample effects.  
- Evaluation approach (fictional-splitting with upper/lower bounds) is appropriate and persuasive.  
- Clarity issues: figure legends are too terse and do not sufficiently guide interpretation of stitched vs naive vs fully observed results, especially off-diagonal blocks.  
- Recommend the main text more explicitly walk readers through figure panels, evaluation metrics, and interpretation of method differences.  
- Pros summarized: clear problem formulation, effective recovery of non-simultaneous pairwise correlations, sensible benchmarking, high practical relevance.  
- Cons/questions summarized: explain why overlap is needed in real data but not sims; discuss causes of the simulation vs real-data performance gap; improve figure legends and narrative around key figures.  
- Overall assessment: strong, timely contribution likely to interest experimentalists and theorists; acceptance recommended after clarifications and improved figure explanations.
