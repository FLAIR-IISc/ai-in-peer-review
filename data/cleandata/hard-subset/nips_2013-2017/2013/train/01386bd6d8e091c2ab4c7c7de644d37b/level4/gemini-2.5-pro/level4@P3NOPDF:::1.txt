This paper presents a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially recorded neural populations. This approach aims to overcome experimental limitations on the number of simultaneously recordable neurons, thereby enabling the characterization of population dynamics across a larger, more complete circuit. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental two-photon calcium imaging data from the mouse visual cortex. A key result is the model's ability to estimate pairwise correlations between neurons that were never recorded at the same time.

The authors frame the problem as inference in a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations. The model's parameters—a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix, and covariances for neural and measurement noise—are estimated from the data using a variant of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The primary significance of this work lies in its direct confrontation with a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: the restricted field of view in many recording techniques, such as two-photon imaging. By providing a principled way to infer circuit-wide properties from sequentially recorded subsets of neurons, this method has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of neural computation at the population level.
*   **Originality:** The approach is original. While other methods exist for inferring functional connectivity, this paper is novel in its specific focus on inferring interactions between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a challenging and important problem.
*   **Core Technical Contribution:** A key strength is the model's ability to recover pairwise correlations, a fundamental measure of functional interaction, even between neurons that were never observed together. This is a non-trivial achievement and represents the core value of the proposed model.

**Areas for Improvement**

The paper is promising, but its claims would be substantially strengthened by addressing the following points:

1.  **Discrepancy between Simulated and Real Data Requirements:** The paper demonstrates successful inference on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations. However, for the experimental data, the authors state that a degree of overlap between recorded populations is necessary for reasonable results. This discrepancy requires clarification. Why is overlap not a prerequisite for the simulated data? The authors should discuss the specific conditions or assumptions in the simulation (e.g., stationarity, model correctness) that allow the algorithm to succeed without overlap, and why these conditions might not be fully met in the experimental data.

2.  **Magnitude of Inferred Correlations:** There appears to be a significant difference in the magnitude of the recovered correlations between the simulated results (Figure 2c) and the experimental results (Figure 3b). The correlations in the simulated data are much stronger. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of potential reasons for this difference. Is this due to the noise levels in the real data, model mismatch, or other biological factors not captured by the linear Gaussian model? Addressing this would provide a more realistic picture of the model's performance on real-world data.

3.  **Clarity of Figures and Legends:** While the paper is generally well-written, the presentation could be improved. The figure legends are often too brief and lack the necessary detail for the figures to be understood independently of the main text. Furthermore, the main text itself could do more to walk the reader through the details of each figure, explaining what is being plotted and highlighting the key takeaways. Expanding on these descriptions would significantly improve the paper's clarity and impact.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong contribution that addresses an important and practical problem in the analysis of large-scale neural recordings. The use of a relatively simple and interpretable linear dynamical model to infer difficult-to-measure cross-population statistics is elegant. While there are some points that require clarification, particularly regarding the model's performance on real versus simulated data, the proposed method is a valuable step towards characterizing neural circuits that are too large to be captured in a single experiment. I believe this paper will be of interest to both the computational and experimental neuroscience communities.
