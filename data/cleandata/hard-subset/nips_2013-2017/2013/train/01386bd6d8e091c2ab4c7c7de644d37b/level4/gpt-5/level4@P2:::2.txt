Summary
The paper proposes a method to “stitch” sequential partial recordings of the same neural circuit into a single latent dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Using EM for parameter estimation and Kalman smoothing for inference, the model seeks to recover functional couplings and predict noise correlations between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The approach is validated on simulated data and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse S1 (awake and anesthetized). The stitched model predicts cross-session noise correlations substantially better than a naïve baseline and approaches a “fully observed” upper bound fit to the full simultaneous dataset in a fictional split-session scenario.

Relation to prior work: The problem of modeling partially observed neural populations has been studied in other settings (e.g., Pillow & Latham, 2008, for latent GLMs; work on connectivity from calcium imaging; approaches that rely on stimulus/noise correlation monotonicity assumptions). The novelty here is a practical LDS-based procedure that aligns multiple partial observations of the same circuit to infer joint dynamics and correlations across non-simultaneous recordings.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and important problem: leveraging sequential recordings to approximate population dynamics beyond the limits of simultaneous imaging.
- Conceptually simple and computationally tractable; readily extensible to point-process observations.
- Empirical evaluation includes both simulation and real calcium imaging data; predictive performance on noise correlations is strong.
- If generalized and robustified, the approach could be broadly useful for multi-day or multi-field-of-view experiments where channels/ROIs change across sessions.

Main concerns and questions
- Model assumptions and robustness: The method relies on a linear-Gaussian LDS fit directly to fluorescence. It is not clear how performance degrades under realistic deviations (nonlinear spiking-to-calcium transformations, non-Gaussian noise, stimulus–response nonlinearities, time-varying dynamics). The paper would benefit from experiments or analysis demonstrating failure modes and robustness (e.g., adding nonlinearities to the simulator, varying noise distributions, or introducing slow drifts).
- Identifiability and marginal information: The paper notes that with one subset the joint dynamics are identifiable up to a linear transform of latent dimensions. It remains unclear how much additional information the second subset contributes about the coupling matrix compared with simply collecting more data from the first subset. Please quantify the marginal benefit of stitching an additional non-overlapping subset versus doubling the trials in a single subset.
- Need for overlap on real data: The real-data results require overlap between the subsets to achieve good performance, whereas the simulation works without overlap. Some intuition or theory about why overlap helps (e.g., aligning latent coordinates or managing model mis-specification) would strengthen the paper. An ablation varying overlap systematically (including zero overlap) and reporting the effect on correlation prediction and coupling recovery would be informative.
- Interpreting couplings vs correlations: On real data, the stitched model predicts noise correlations well, but estimated couplings correlate weakly with those from the fully observed model (Fig. 3b). Given likely common inputs and measurement nonlinearity, it seems plausible that low-dimensional latent shared variability, rather than direct pairwise couplings, drives much of the explained covariance. A comparison to models with explicit common latent factors (e.g., low-rank LDS or related population models) would clarify whether pairwise couplings are necessary or whether a shared latent structure suffices (cf. Vidne et al., 2011; Macke et al., 2012).
- Methodological detail: The methods section is terse relative to the Introduction/Discussion. The M-step updates on p. 3 are presented as a key contribution but are not derived or explained. If these are standard consequences of the LDS with missing data, that reduces the novelty; if not, derivations and implementation details (initialization, regularization/priors, handling of potential non-identifiability, convergence diagnostics, scaling with N and T, online-EM specifics) should be included, at least in an appendix. Clarify how you prevent shallow local optima and how parameter uncertainty is handled.

Quality
- The experiments are suggestive and the simulation analyses are clear. Real-data results on correlation prediction are compelling, but the gap between predicting correlations and identifying couplings should be addressed explicitly. More methodological detail and robustness checks are needed.

Clarity
- The narrative is engaging, but the estimation procedure needs more space: derivations or references for the EM updates, model variants (e.g., correlated R, low-rank latent inputs), and precise training setup. A schematic of the stitching pipeline would help.

Originality
- The framing of stitching multiple partial recordings via an LDS with missing observations is appealing and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated at this scale for calcium imaging. However, if the core estimation is standard LDS-EM with session-specific observation masks, the novelty rests mainly in the application and empirical validation; this should be candidly positioned.

Significance
- High potential impact if robust across realistic violations of assumptions and scalable to larger, multi-session datasets. The ability to predict cross-session correlations is practically valuable.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide robustness analyses to nonlinearity and non-Gaussianity; report performance vs degree of overlap and number of sessions.
- Quantify the marginal value of adding a second subset vs more trials in a single subset.
- Compare to latent-factor models capturing common input; consider adding low-rank latent inputs alongside pairwise couplings.
- Include full EM derivations, algorithmic details, regularization choices (e.g., sparsity on A), and computational complexity; consider releasing code.
- Clarify that “true” couplings in real data are proxies from a fully observed fit and discuss the implications for interpreting low coupling correlations.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common experimental scenario.
- Simple, general framework; strong correlation prediction on real data.
- Broad applicability and potential to influence experimental design.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited methodological detail and unclear novelty of the estimation step.
- Unclear robustness to model mis-specification; reliance on overlap in practice.
- Weak alignment of inferred couplings with “ground-truth” estimates on real data without alternative explanations or comparisons.
