This paper presents a novel statistical method for integrating sequentially imaged neural populations, effectively expanding the observable network size beyond the limitations of simultaneous imaging. This is a significant challenge in neuroscience, as experimental and technical constraints often prevent the simultaneous recording of activity from large neuronal ensembles. The authors demonstrate the utility of their approach using both simulated and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, successfully estimating correlations in variability between populations that were not imaged concurrently.

The core of the method lies in formulating the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The authors employ a linear Gaussian system, parameterized by a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix (representing stimulus-dependent activity), and two covariance matrices representing neural and measurement noise. These four parameters are estimated from experimental data using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality:**

The model performs well on simulated data with non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, application to real data reveals a requirement for a substantial degree of overlap between imaged populations to achieve reasonable results. While the intuition behind this – greater overlap provides more shared information for alignment – is clear, the paper does not adequately explain *why* overlap was not necessary for successful reconstruction in the simulated data. This discrepancy deserves further clarification. 

Furthermore, a significant difference in the magnitude of correlations is observed between the simulated data (Fig. 2c) and the experimental data (Fig. 3b). The authors do not address this disparity, leaving open the question of whether this difference reflects a limitation of the model when applied to biological data, or an inherent difference in the underlying dynamics between the simulated and real systems. 

Despite these concerns, the ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons that were not simultaneously measured represents a key strength of this work.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. However, the figure legends are insufficiently descriptive, and the text’s discussion of the figures lacks sufficient elaboration. Expanding on the details presented within the figures would significantly enhance the reader’s understanding of the results. For example, providing more context for the specific choices made in the simulation setup and their impact on the outcomes presented in Fig. 2 would be beneficial.

**Originality:**

The authors appropriately acknowledge existing work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiological data. However, they convincingly argue that these previous approaches do not address the specific problem of inferring functional connections between *non-simultaneously* imaged neurons. This focus on stitching together sequential recordings constitutes a novel contribution.

**Significance:**

A fundamental limitation in systems neuroscience is the restricted number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged. This inherently restricts our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level. This paper offers a promising method to circumvent this limitation, potentially unlocking the ability to study larger and more complex neural networks. By expanding our observational capabilities, this work contributes to a deeper understanding of circuit-level dynamics.

**Overall:**

This is a solid paper that leverages a relatively simple, yet effective, linear dynamics model to address a challenging problem in neural data analysis. The method captures information that would be difficult to obtain otherwise and has the potential to significantly impact the field. Addressing the concerns regarding the necessity of overlap in the real data and the discrepancy in correlation magnitude would strengthen the manuscript further. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon these clarifications.
