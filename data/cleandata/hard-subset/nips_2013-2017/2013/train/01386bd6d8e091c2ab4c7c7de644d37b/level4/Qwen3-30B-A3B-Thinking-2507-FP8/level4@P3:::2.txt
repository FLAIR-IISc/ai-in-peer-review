

This paper introduces a novel statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially recorded subsets of neurons to infer population dynamics of large neural circuits, addressing a fundamental limitation in current experimental neuroscience. The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, enabling them to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. This approach significantly expands the population sizes for which dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons.

The work builds on previous neural population analysis methods, particularly Pillow and Latham's (2008) work on partially observed populations, but makes a significant conceptual advance by demonstrating how to combine multiple partial observations to infer cross-population dynamics. While prior studies have addressed functional connectivity from imaging data (Mishchenko et al., 2011; Stetter et al., 2012), none have specifically tackled the problem of combining non-simultaneous recordings. The authors also properly contextualize their approach relative to other methods for dealing with sub-sampled observations (e.g., Wohrer et al., 2010; Pakman et al., 2013), highlighting how their method differs by focusing on sequential recordings of the same circuit.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a clear theoretical foundation in latent dynamical systems. The method is validated on both simulated data and real calcium imaging recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their stitching approach substantially outperforms a naive baseline that simply averages parameters across sessions. For the real data, the stitched model predicted noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs with correlation 0.84 to the fully observed model, while the naive approach achieved only 0.15.

The significance of this work is substantial. Current imaging technology can only capture hundreds of neurons simultaneously, while cortical circuits often contain thousands. This method allows researchers to effectively work with larger neural populations than current technology permits, potentially expanding the scope of neural circuit studies. The demonstration on real data from both anesthetized and awake mice provides compelling evidence of the method's utility in real experimental settings.

However, the paper has several important weaknesses that require attention. The clarity of the methodological contribution needs improvement. The M-step updates (page 3) are presented without derivation or intuitive explanation, making it difficult to assess the novelty of this specific implementation. While these updates might be trivial to LDS experts, the paper fails to explain why they are novel or how they differ from standard LDS implementations, which weakens the perceived contribution.

The paper also insufficiently addresses the limitations of the linear-Gaussian assumption. The authors note that their model is a statistical description rather than a biologically realistic one, but they do not provide adequate analysis of how the method might fail when this assumption is violated (e.g., in more complex neural systems with nonlinear dynamics). The experimental results on real data suggest the method works well, but without understanding the assumptions' robustness, it's unclear how broadly applicable the approach will be.

Another critical gap is the lack of explanation for why overlap between recording sessions is necessary for the method to work. The paper demonstrates that the stitching approach only works with some overlap between populations (c = 60% in the experiments), but doesn't provide intuition for why this is the case. This is particularly important because the authors claim their theoretical framework suggests that "only measuring the activity of one population of neurons" should allow inference of a second population "up to re-parametrization," yet the experiments require overlap.

The authors also do not sufficiently address whether the noise correlations they predict reflect direct pairwise couplings or common input from unobserved neurons. This is particularly relevant given that the coupling parameters themselves show poor correlation with the fully observed model (Figure 3b), while noise correlations are well-predicted.

Pro: Novel concept addressing a real experimental limitation; Strong empirical validation on real data; Clear demonstration of the method's utility in neuroscience; Well-structured and technically sound.

Con: Insufficient detail on the estimation procedure; Limited discussion of method limitations and assumptions; Unclear explanation of the role of overlap between recording sessions; Inadequate analysis of whether predicted correlations reflect direct couplings or common input.

The paper presents a compelling idea with significant potential to advance neural circuit analysis. While the core concept is strong, the current presentation lacks sufficient detail to fully appreciate the methodological contribution and limitations. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, but need to better explain how their implementation differs from standard LDS methods and under what conditions it will work well.

Given the method's novelty, technical soundness, and clear significance for neuroscience, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should provide more detailed explanation of the estimation procedure, clarify the role of overlap between recording sessions, and better address the limitations of their linear-Gaussian assumption. With these improvements, the paper would make a strong contribution to NIPS.
