This paper proposes inferring properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then stitching the learned submodels into a global model. The extension to point-process observations makes the approach potentially applicable to many neural datasets in which a few channels are moved each day. I am not aware of closely related methods; the closest appears to be Pillow and Latham (“Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons”). Overall, the idea is promising.

However, several issues limit my confidence in the current form. First, it is unclear how dependent the method is on linear-Gaussian assumptions. The manuscript does not provide intuition or diagnostics for how estimation degrades as the data deviate from linearity or Gaussian noise. In the limiting case—where a single subset is sufficient to identify the full model up to a linear transformation of the latent space, as the authors note—it is not obvious how much additional information about the coupling matrix is obtained by incorporating other subsets, as opposed to simply collecting more data from one subset.

Second, on real data the method appears to work only when there is overlap between the recorded populations. This suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture crucial aspects of the problem. Some explanation of how overlap constrains the model and what specific parameters benefit from it would greatly clarify the practical requirements.

Third, in the real-data experiments—where linearity likely breaks down—it is hard to disentangle explained noise correlations arising from shared input versus direct pairwise couplings. Indeed, the couplings show little correlation with ground truth in Fig. 3b, while noise correlations are fit well. I would expect latent-variable structure to capture common input more effectively than pairwise couplings; the paper should clarify how the model separates these effects or acknowledge the limitation.

Finally, the paper allocates substantial space to the introduction and discussion but provides few details on estimation. The M-step equation (bottom of p. 3) is presented as a main contribution but is neither derived nor explained, which undermines the claimed novelty. If this step follows standard LDS results, please state so explicitly; otherwise, include a derivation or clear rationale.

In sum: a neat and potentially impactful idea with encouraging results on real data, but the methodological assumptions, identifiability, and estimation details need clearer exposition.
