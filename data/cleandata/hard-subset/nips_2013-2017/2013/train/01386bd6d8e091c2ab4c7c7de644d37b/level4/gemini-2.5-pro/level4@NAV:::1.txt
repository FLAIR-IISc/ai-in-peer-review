This paper presents a statistical method to computationally "stitch" together neuronal populations that are imaged sequentially. Such an approach allows for the characterization of population dynamics on a scale that surpasses the limits of simultaneous imaging, offering a more comprehensive view of neural circuits that cannot be captured in a single recording due to technical constraints. The method is validated on both simulated datasets and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, where the authors demonstrate its ability to estimate correlations between neurons that were not recorded concurrently.

The authors formulate the problem as an inference task within a latent dynamical system with missing observations, utilizing a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model is defined by four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a receptive field matrix to account for stimulus-dependent activity, and covariance matrices for both neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from the data using a variant of the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**
The model performs well on simulated data featuring completely non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, when applied to experimental data, a degree of overlap between the recorded populations is required to achieve reasonable results. While it is intuitive that increased overlap improves performance, the paper should clarify why overlap was not a prerequisite for success in the simulated case. Furthermore, the authors do not explain the significant discrepancy in the magnitude of inferred correlations between the simulated results (Fig. 2c) and the experimental data (Fig. 3b). A key strength of the model lies in its elegant recovery of pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neurons.

**Clarity**
The manuscript is well-written. However, its clarity could be enhanced by providing more descriptive figure legends. The corresponding descriptions in the main text could also be expanded to more fully elaborate on the information presented in the figures.

**Originality**
The authors appropriately position their work relative to prior studies on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging or electrophysiological data. The primary novelty of this contribution is its specific focus on inferring functional connections between neurons that were not recorded concurrently—a problem not addressed by previous methods.

**Significance**
Fundamental technical constraints limit the number of neurons that can be imaged simultaneously, which in turn restricts our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level. The method proposed in this paper offers a valuable approach to expand our analytical capabilities beyond these experimental limitations.

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents an effective modeling approach. By employing a relatively simple linear dynamical system, the authors succeed in capturing important circuit properties that are otherwise difficult to obtain.
