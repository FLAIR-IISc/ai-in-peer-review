This paper presents a novel and potentially powerful method for inferring the properties of a large network from sequential recordings of smaller, overlapping subnetworks. The core idea is to fit individual sub-models and then "stitch" them together to reconstruct the full network model. This approach has significant practical implications, particularly for neurophysiological datasets where it is common to move a subset of recording channels daily. An extension to point-process data would make this method widely applicable. To my knowledge, this approach is highly original, with the work of Pillow and Latham ("Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons") being the most closely related precedent. The concept of model stitching for network inference is a compelling contribution.

Despite the promise of the core idea, the paper leaves several important questions unanswered, primarily concerning the method's assumptions, limitations, and technical details.

**Clarity of Methodological Contribution:**
The paper devotes considerable space to the introduction and discussion but is surprisingly light on the technical details of the estimation procedure. The M-step update equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as a central contribution but lacks a clear explanation or derivation. If this result is a straightforward extension for experts in Linear Dynamical Systems (LDS), it could diminish the perceived novelty of the paper's methodological contribution. A more detailed derivation, perhaps in an appendix, would greatly strengthen the paper by clarifying the technical novelty and allowing readers to fully appreciate the significance of the proposed method.

**Assumptions and Limitations:**
A key concern is the method's sensitivity to the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper does not provide sufficient intuition about the failure modes of the algorithm when this assumption is violated. How gracefully does the performance degrade with increasing non-linearity or non-Gaussianity in the data? This is particularly relevant given the results on real neural data, which likely deviate from this assumption.

Furthermore, the paper could clarify the information gain from using multiple subsets. In a scenario where a single sub-recording is sufficient to estimate the model (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as noted by the authors), what is the precise benefit of adding subsequent recordings? It would be valuable to compare the improvement gained by stitching a new sub-model versus simply acquiring more data from the initial sub-network.

**Interpretation of Results:**
A particularly intriguing and important result is that the method requires neuronal overlap between sub-networks to succeed on real data. This finding suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture the full complexity of the problem. The authors should provide more intuition as to why this overlap is critical. What specific constraints does it provide that are absent in the non-overlapping case, and what does this imply about the structure of real neural data?

Regarding the experiments on real data, the interpretation of the results warrants further discussion. The model effectively captures noise correlations, yet the estimated pairwise couplings show little correlation with the ground truth couplings (Figure 3b). This discrepancy raises the question of whether the model is fitting shared variability through pairwise couplings that is actually attributable to common, unobserved inputs. It seems plausible that a model with explicit latent variables could capture these noise correlations more directly and perhaps more accurately. This point is crucial for understanding what the learned model parameters truly represent.

**Conclusion:**
In summary, this paper introduces a novel and promising idea for large-scale network inference from partial recordings, supported by experiments on both simulated and real data. However, its significance is currently obscured by a lack of methodological detail and an incomplete discussion of the method's assumptions and the interpretation of its results. Addressing the points raised above—by providing a clearer derivation of the M-step, offering intuition on the model's failure modes, and deepening the analysis of the real-data results—would substantially improve the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
