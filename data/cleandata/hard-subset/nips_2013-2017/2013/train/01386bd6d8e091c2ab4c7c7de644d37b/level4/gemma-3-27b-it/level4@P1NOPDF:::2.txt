This paper demonstrates that network properties of a large network can be inferred by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then stitching together the resulting sub-models. This approach, when extended to point-process data, has the potential to be widely applicable to datasets where only a small number of channels are recorded on any given day. I am unaware of closely related work – Pillow and Latham’s “Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons” seems to be the closest – and the core idea appears quite powerful.

However, it’s unclear how well this method generalizes to data that deviates from the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper doesn’t provide sufficient intuition regarding how the fitting process might break down under various departures from this assumption. Specifically, in the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it’s not obvious what additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets, as opposed to simply using more data from one of the original subsets.

Another point that raises questions is the necessity of overlap between the recorded populations in the real data analysis. While this observation suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations don't fully capture the complexities of the data, it would be helpful to have some intuition as to *how* this overlap helps to better constrain the model.

When analyzing real data – where the linearity assumption likely fails – one must also consider the source of the explained noise correlations (which are well-fit, although the estimated couplings themselves do not strongly correlate with the true couplings, as shown in Figure 3b). It seems plausible that these noise correlations might be better explained by common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. Indeed, a model incorporating latent variables might be more effective at capturing these correlations than pairwise couplings alone.

The authors dedicate considerable space to the introduction and discussion, but provide surprisingly few details regarding the estimation procedure. The equation for the M-step presented on the bottom of page 3 is positioned as a key contribution, yet it lacks sufficient explanation or derivation. While it may be a straightforward result for experts in latent dynamical systems, this lack of clarity undermines the perceived novelty of the paper. I believe the authors have a promising idea, but it requires more thorough explanation to fully convey the significance of their results. 

Overall, this is a neat idea tested on real data, but several details remain unclear.
