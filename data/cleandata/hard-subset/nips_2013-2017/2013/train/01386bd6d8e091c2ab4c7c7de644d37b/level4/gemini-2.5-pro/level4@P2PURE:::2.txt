This paper proposes a powerful method for inferring the properties of a large network by sequentially recording from smaller subnetworks and "stitching" the resulting sub-models together. The proposed extension to point-process data suggests broad applicability, particularly for datasets where recording channels are shifted over time. The approach appears novel, with the closest related work seemingly being that of Pillow and Latham ("Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons").

Despite the promising idea, the paper lacks a clear discussion of the method's limitations, particularly its reliance on the linear-Gaussian assumption. The reader is left without a strong intuition for how the fitting procedure might fail when these assumptions are violated. In the case where a single sub-model can estimate the full model (up to a linear transformation of latent dimensions, as noted by the authors), it is unclear what additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating subsequent subsets, as opposed to simply using more data from the initial subset.

A key question also arises from the experiments on real data: why does the model require an overlap between the observed populations to succeed? This finding suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture the full complexity of the data. The paper would be strengthened by providing some intuition for how this overlap helps constrain the model.

Furthermore, in the experiments on real data where the linearity assumption is likely violated, the interpretation of the results is challenging. While the model accurately fits noise correlations, the estimated couplings show little correlation with the ground truth couplings (Figure 3b). This discrepancy raises the possibility that the well-fit noise correlations are driven by common input rather than the direct, pairwise couplings the model is intended to capture. Indeed, noise correlations might be more effectively modeled by latent variables than by direct pairwise couplings.

The paper dedicates significant space to the introduction and discussion at the expense of methodological detail. The estimation procedure, in particular, is not sufficiently elaborated. The M-step update equation is presented as a core contribution but is provided without sufficient explanation or derivation. If this result is trivial for experts in Linear Dynamical Systems, the novelty of the paper's contribution would be substantially diminished. The authors present a very compelling idea, but a more thorough explanation of the methodology is necessary to fully convey the significance of their results.

In summary, the paper presents a novel and interesting idea tested against real data, but its impact is weakened by a lack of clarity regarding key methodological details and model limitations.
