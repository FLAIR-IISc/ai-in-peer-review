

This paper introduces a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged neuron populations, enabling characterization of larger neural circuits than possible with simultaneous imaging alone. The approach models the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a simple linear Gaussian framework. Key parameters include a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix, and neural/measurement noise covariance matrices, estimated via a modified EM algorithm. The method is validated on simulated data and experimental calcium imaging from mice, demonstrating recovery of pairwise correlations between neurons not imaged simultaneously.

**Quality**  
The model performs well on simulated data with non-overlapping neuron populations, but real-world application requires significant population overlap for reliable results. While the necessity of overlap for real data is intuitive (shared activity patterns facilitate alignment), the authors do not explain why overlap was unnecessary in the simulated example (Figure 2c). Additionally, the substantial difference in correlation estimates between simulated (Figure 2c) and real data (Figure 3b) lacks explanation. This discrepancy warrants clarification, as it impacts confidence in the method’s real-world applicability. The model’s key strength—recovering correlations between non-simultaneously imaged neurons—remains compelling.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-structured and readable, but figure legends lack sufficient detail to fully interpret results. For instance, Figure 3b’s legend omits key methodological specifics (e.g., overlap percentage thresholds), and the main text fails to adequately elaborate on how figures support claims. Enhanced figure descriptions and more explicit connections between figures and text would significantly improve accessibility.

**Originality**  
The authors correctly position their work against prior studies on functional connectivity inference from 2-photon or electrophysiological data. Crucially, none of these approaches address the specific challenge of inferring connections between neurons imaged in separate sessions—a gap this paper directly fills. This focus on non-simultaneous data integration constitutes a meaningful contribution.

**Significance**  
Simultaneous imaging is constrained by technical and biological limits, typically restricting circuit analysis to hundreds of neurons. This method directly addresses that bottleneck, enabling scalable characterization of larger neural populations. By expanding the scope of accessible circuit dynamics, it offers a practical pathway to study computation at more natural circuit scales, making it highly relevant for experimental neuroscience.

**Overall**  
This is a strong technical contribution that effectively leverages a simple linear model to solve a well-motivated problem. The core idea—using latent dynamics to bridge non-overlapping imaging sessions—is elegant and well-executed. While the requirement for population overlap in real data and the simulated vs. real correlation discrepancy need addressing, these do not undermine the paper’s value. The method provides a valuable tool for researchers seeking to extend population-level analyses beyond current imaging constraints. The work is timely, well-motivated, and delivers a usable framework with clear experimental utility. I recommend acceptance.
