This paper presents an innovative approach to inferring network properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then combining the resulting sub-models. The extension of this method to point-process data makes it applicable to various datasets where a subset of channels is typically moved every day. Notably, this idea appears to be novel, with the closest related work being J. W. Pillow and P. Latham's "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons." The proposed method seems powerful, but its limitations, particularly with respect to data that deviates from the linear-Gaussian assumption, are not clearly addressed.

In practice, it is unclear how the method's performance degrades with deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumption, and the paper does not provide sufficient intuition on this matter. Furthermore, in the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as stated by the authors), it is unclear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets, as opposed to using more data from one subset.

Another point of concern is the requirement of overlap between the two populations for the model to work on real data. This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulated data does not capture the full complexity of the problem, and it would be beneficial to have some intuition about how the overlap constrains the model. In the experiments on real data, where the linearity assumption likely breaks, it is also uncertain how much of the explained noise correlations (which are fit well, but the couplings themselves hardly correlate with the true couplings) are due to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. It is possible that the noise correlations could be better modeled using latent variables than pairwise couplings.

The authors devote a significant amount of space to the introduction and discussion but provide limited details on the estimation procedure. The equation for the M-step on the bottom of page 3 is presented as the main contribution but is not thoroughly explained or derived. While this result may be trivial for experts in linear dynamical systems (LDS), the lack of explanation erodes the novelty of the paper. The authors have a promising idea, but it needs to be explained more clearly to convey the significance of the result.

Overall, the paper presents a neat idea tested against real data, but some details are not clearly explained, which detracts from the overall impact of the work.
