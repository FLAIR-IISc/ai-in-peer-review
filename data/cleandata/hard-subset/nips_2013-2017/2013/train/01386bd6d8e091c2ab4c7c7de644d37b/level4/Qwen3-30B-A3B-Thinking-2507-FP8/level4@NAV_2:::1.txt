

The paper presents a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially acquired neural recordings to infer joint dynamics of large populations beyond the simultaneous imaging limit. This addresses a key challenge in neural circuit analysis, where techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging typically capture only hundreds of neurons despite cortical circuits involving thousands. The authors model the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian state-space framework to estimate functional couplings and noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. The approach is validated on simulated data and two experimental datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex (anesthetized and awake), demonstrating accurate prediction of inter-population correlations and activity. This work builds on prior NIPS contributions to neural population modeling (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008; Wohrer et al., NIPS 2010) but uniquely tackles the non-simultaneous observation problem, unlike related work on functional connectivity inference from partial data (e.g., Mishchenko et al., NIPS 2011; Stetter et al., NIPS 2012).

**Quality**  
The method is technically sound and well-supported: it successfully recovers cross-population couplings (correlation 0.73 vs. 0.95 for simultaneously recorded pairs in simulations) and noise correlations (0.92 correlation with ground truth), outperforming a naive baseline by >2× on real data (Fig. 3d). The EM-based parameter estimation is appropriately adapted for missing data. However, the necessity of population overlap (critical for real data but absent in the simulated example with 50% non-overlapping subsets) requires clarification. The authors should address why overlap is dispensable in simulation yet essential in practice—this discrepancy merits discussion. The model’s simplicity (linear Gaussian dynamics) is justified by its predictive success, but extensions to nonlinear dynamics (e.g., as in Vogelstein et al., NIPS 2009) could further strengthen the framework.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, though figure legends lack descriptive detail (e.g., Fig. 2c should specify error metrics). The connection between text and figures (e.g., Section 3.1 explaining Fig. 2b) could be enhanced with explicit reference to statistical significance. The methodological description is sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

**Originality**  
The core contribution—inferring correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons via latent dynamical systems—is novel. Prior NIPS work focuses on *simultaneous* recordings (e.g., Pillow et al., NIPS 2008; Stetter et al., NIPS 2012) or assumes monotonic noise-stimulus correlations (Wohrer et al., NIPS 2010), which this paper explicitly avoids. The stitching framework is distinct from related approaches for sub-sampled connectivity (e.g., Mishchenko & Paninski, NIPS 2012; Fasten et al., NIPS 2013).

**Significance**  
This work significantly advances circuit-scale neuroscience by enabling population dynamics characterization beyond current imaging limits. It directly addresses a fundamental bottleneck (simultaneous neuron count constraints) and provides a practical tool to leverage existing datasets—critical for studying large-scale computations in cortex. The successful prediction of noise correlations (a key metric for circuit function) demonstrates tangible utility for both experimental design and theoretical modeling.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**  
*Pro*: Novel framework solving a well-defined, impactful problem; strong validation on real neural data; clear significance for neuroscience; technically rigorous.  
*Con*: Overlap requirement for real data needs deeper explanation; linear model may limit applicability to complex dynamics; simulation-real data correlation gap requires clarification.  

**Recommendation**  
This paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly presented (Clarity), addresses a gap in prior NIPS work (Originality), and offers a meaningful step toward circuit-scale understanding (Significance). The minor limitations are outweighed by the contribution’s novelty and impact. **Accept**.
