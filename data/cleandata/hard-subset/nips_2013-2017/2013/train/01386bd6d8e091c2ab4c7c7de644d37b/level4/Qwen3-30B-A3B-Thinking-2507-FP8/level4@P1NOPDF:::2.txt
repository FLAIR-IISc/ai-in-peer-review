

This paper proposes a method for inferring global network properties by sequentially sampling subnetworks, estimating local models, and stitching them together. When extended to point-process data, this approach could be valuable for datasets where only a few channels are recorded daily (e.g., in neural recording setups). I am unaware of closely related work—Pillow and Latham (2008) on partially observed spiking populations comes closest—and the core idea appears promising.  

However, it remains unclear how broadly applicable this method is beyond linear-Gaussian assumptions. The paper does not sufficiently address how deviations from these assumptions might degrade performance. Specifically, when the full model can be recovered from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of latent dimensions, as noted by the authors), it is unclear what additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating further subsets versus simply using more data from one subset.  

Another concern arises from the real-data experiments: the model only succeeds when there is overlap between the two recorded populations. This suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture real-world complexities, yet the paper offers no intuition on how overlap constrains the model. Why is overlap necessary? What specific structural property does it enable?  

In the real-data analysis, where linearity likely breaks down, the explanation of noise correlations (well-fit in Fig. 3b) warrants scrutiny. The pairwise couplings themselves show poor correlation with ground-truth couplings. This raises the question: to what extent are the fitted noise correlations driven by common input rather than direct pairwise interactions? Given this, modeling noise correlations via latent variables might be more appropriate than relying on pairwise couplings.  

The paper dedicates significant space to the introduction and discussion but provides minimal detail on the estimation procedure. The M-step equation on page 3 is presented as a key contribution but lacks derivation or intuitive explanation. While this might be trivial for linear dynamical systems (LDS) experts, its omission undermines the paper’s novelty claim. The authors have a strong conceptual idea, but its significance is not adequately conveyed due to insufficient technical exposition.  

In summary, the approach is promising for leveraging partial recordings, but critical details about model limitations, the role of population overlap, the interpretation of noise correlations, and the estimation procedure remain unclear. The method’s real-world utility hinges on resolving these points. The paper would benefit from deeper analysis of non-Gaussian scenarios, clearer justification for the overlap requirement, and a more rigorous derivation of the core algorithm. As presented, several key aspects require elaboration to fully validate the proposed framework.
