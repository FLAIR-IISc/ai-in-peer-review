This paper demonstrates that network properties of a large system can be inferred by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then integrating the resulting sub-models. When extended to point-process data, this approach has potential applications to numerous datasets where only a small fraction of channels are typically recorded at any given time. I am unaware of closely related work – Pillow & Latham (2013) comes the closest – and the proposed method appears to be a powerful concept.

However, it is unclear how strongly the method’s performance relies on data that is approximately linear-Gaussian. The paper does not provide sufficient intuition regarding how deviations from this assumption might affect the fitting process. Specifically, if the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors acknowledge), it is not obvious what additional information is gained by incorporating data from further subsets, versus simply increasing the amount of data from the initial subset. 

Furthermore, the necessity of overlap between populations for successful application to real data raises questions. While this suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the relevant dynamics, more insight into the role of this overlap in constraining the model would be valuable. 

In the real data experiments, where the linearity assumption likely fails, it is important to consider the extent to which the explained noise correlations – which are well-fit – are attributable to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. Indeed, the couplings themselves show limited correlation with true couplings (Figure 3b). I suspect that noise correlations might be more effectively modeled with latent variables than with pairwise couplings.

The paper dedicates considerable space to the introduction and discussion, but provides insufficient detail regarding the estimation process. While the equation for the M-step (page 3) is presented as a key contribution, it lacks sufficient explanation or derivation. Although this result may be straightforward for experts in latent dynamical systems, a clearer presentation is crucial to establish the novelty of the work. Overall, the authors present a promising idea, but the significance of the result would be better conveyed with improved explanation. 

A neat idea validated with real data, but further clarification of certain details is needed.
