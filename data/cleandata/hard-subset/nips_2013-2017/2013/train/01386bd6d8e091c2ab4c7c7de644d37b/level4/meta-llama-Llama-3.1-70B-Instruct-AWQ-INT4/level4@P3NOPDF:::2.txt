This paper proposes an innovative approach to inferring network properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then stitching the resulting sub-models. This method, which can be extended to point-process data, has the potential to be applied to a wide range of datasets where only a few channels are moved every day. The idea appears to be novel, with the closest similar work being J. W. Pillow and P. Latham's "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons." The concept is powerful and demonstrates significant potential.

However, it is unclear how much this method is limited to data that is close to linear-Gaussian, and the paper does not provide a clear intuition for how the fitting might break down under various deviations from this assumption. In extreme cases, where the full model can be estimated from one subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors state), it is uncertain how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained from adding other subsets, as opposed to simply using more data from one subset.

Another point that raises questions is the requirement for overlap between the two populations for the model to work on real data. This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulated data does not capture the full complexity of the problem, and it would be beneficial to have at least some intuition about what the overlap does to better constrain the model.

In the experiments on real data, where the linearity assumption likely breaks down, one must also consider how much of the explained noise correlations (which are fit well, but the couplings themselves hardly correlate to the true couplings at all in Figure 3b) are due to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. It is possible that the noise correlations could be better modeled with latent variables than pairwise couplings.

The authors devote a significant amount of space to the introduction and discussion, but are somewhat light on the details of the estimation. The equation for the M-step on the bottom of page 3 is presented as the main contribution, but it is not thoroughly explained or derived. While this result may be trivial for LDS experts, it erodes some of the novelty of the paper. The authors have a promising idea, but it needs to be explained more clearly to properly convey the significance of the result.

Overall, the paper presents a neat idea tested against real data, but some details are not entirely clear. With further clarification and explanation, the authors can better convey the significance and potential of their approach.
