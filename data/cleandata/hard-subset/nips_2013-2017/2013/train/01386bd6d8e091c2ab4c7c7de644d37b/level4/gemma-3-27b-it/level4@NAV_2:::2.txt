This paper introduces a novel statistical method for inferring the dynamics of large neural populations by “stitching” together sequentially imaged subnetworks. This addresses a critical limitation in current 2-photon calcium imaging, where circuit size is often constrained by the number of simultaneously recorded neurons. The core idea – formulating the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations – is compelling and potentially broadly applicable, particularly given the increasing prevalence of partial recordings. This work builds upon existing approaches for inferring functional connectivity [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11], but importantly extends them to infer connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons, a capability not addressed in prior studies. While related work exists on dealing with subsampled data [12, 13, 14, 15], the authors convincingly argue why these approaches are not directly applicable to their problem.

The strengths of this paper lie in its elegant formulation of a challenging problem, and the demonstration of its effectiveness on both simulated and real calcium imaging data. The synthetic data analysis convincingly shows the method’s ability to recover underlying coupling matrices and predict noise correlations. Applying the method to mouse somatosensory cortex data, the authors demonstrate successful prediction of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, approaching the performance of a fully observed model. This suggests the method’s potential for characterizing larger circuits than currently feasible with standard imaging techniques.

However, there are also weaknesses that warrant consideration. The method’s reliance on a linear-Gaussian dynamical system is a potential limitation. The paper doesn't fully explore the consequences of deviations from these assumptions, and it would benefit from a discussion of how performance degrades with increasing non-linearity or non-Gaussianity in the data. While the authors acknowledge the limitations, further exploration of these points would strengthen the work. The necessity of overlap between observed populations in the experimental data is also not fully explained and deserves more discussion. The paper mentions that the simulated data does not fully capture the complexity of the real data, but does not elaborate. Furthermore, the excellent performance in predicting noise correlations on real data doesn’t necessarily translate to accurate estimation of the underlying coupling parameters (Figure 3b). This raises the question of whether the predicted correlations are driven by common input rather than direct pairwise couplings, and suggests the potential for incorporating latent variables to better model shared noise sources. 

Finally, the presentation of the mathematical details could be improved. While the authors highlight the M-step equation, its derivation and underlying assumptions aren't fully elaborated, potentially limiting accessibility for readers less familiar with latent dynamical system estimation. A more detailed explanation of the fitting procedure would enhance the paper’s clarity and impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and elegant approach to a significant problem in neural data analysis.
*   Strong performance on both simulated and real data.
*   Demonstrated ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
*   Clear connection to existing literature and a convincing argument for the work's novelty.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on simplifying assumptions (linear-Gaussian dynamics).
*   Limited exploration of the method’s robustness to deviations from these assumptions.
*   Lack of detailed explanation of the estimation procedure.
*   Unclear explanation for the necessity of overlap between observed populations.
