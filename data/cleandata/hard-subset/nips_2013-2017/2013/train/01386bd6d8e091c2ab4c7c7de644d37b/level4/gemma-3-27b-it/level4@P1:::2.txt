This paper presents a method for inferring network properties of a large neural population by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then “stitching” the resulting sub-models together. This approach has the potential to be widely applicable to datasets where recording is limited to a subset of neurons at any given time, and could be extended to point-process data. I am not aware of closely related work; Pillow and Latham’s work on neural characterization in partially observed populations (“Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons”) appears to be the most relevant, though it doesn't directly address the sequential stitching problem presented here. The idea seems powerful and offers a compelling approach to expanding the scale of neural circuit analysis.

However, it’s unclear how robust this method is to deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumptions. The paper doesn’t provide sufficient intuition into how fitting performance might degrade with such deviations. In the limiting case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it’s not obvious how much additional information is gained by adding further subsets, as opposed to simply collecting more data from a single, larger subset.

Furthermore, the requirement for overlap between the recorded populations in the experimental data raises questions. While the authors acknowledge this suggests the linear-Gaussian simulation doesn’t fully capture the complexities of the data, a deeper discussion of *why* overlap is necessary would be beneficial to understand the model’s limitations and constraints.

In the experiments with real data, where the linearity assumption is likely violated, the interpretation of the results requires careful consideration. While the model successfully predicts noise correlations, the authors acknowledge that the estimated couplings themselves do not strongly correlate with the “true” couplings (Figure 3b). This raises the question of whether the explained noise correlations are primarily driven by common input to neurons rather than direct, pairwise couplings. Modeling noise correlations with latent variables, rather than relying solely on pairwise couplings, might be a more appropriate approach.

The paper dedicates considerable space to the introduction and discussion, but provides relatively limited detail on the estimation procedure. The equation for the M-step (page 3) is presented as a key contribution, yet lacks sufficient explanation or derivation. While this step may be trivial for experts in latent dynamical systems, a more thorough explanation would enhance the paper’s accessibility and underscore the significance of the result. Overall, the authors present a promising idea, but it requires more detailed explanation to fully convey the significance of the findings.

In summary, this is a neat idea, validated with real data, but some details require clarification.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to addressing a significant problem in neural data analysis (inferring dynamics from partially observed populations).
*   Demonstrated success in predicting noise correlations in both simulated and real data.
*   Addresses a practical limitation of current imaging technology.
*   Potential to scale up analysis of large neural circuits.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of robustness to deviations from linear-Gaussian assumptions.
*   Lack of explanation regarding the necessity of overlap between recording sessions.
*   Insufficient detail on the estimation procedure, particularly the M-step.
*   Potential confounding factors (e.g., common input) not fully addressed in the interpretation of results.
