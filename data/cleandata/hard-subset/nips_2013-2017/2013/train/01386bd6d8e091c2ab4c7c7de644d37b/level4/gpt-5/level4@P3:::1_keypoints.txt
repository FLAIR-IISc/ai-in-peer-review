- Proposes an LDS-based “stitching” method to combine partial neural recordings and infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neurons.  
- Models calcium fluorescence as a linear Gaussian dynamical system with session-specific observation matrices Ck, coupling A, stimulus B, and diagonal Q/R; parameters learned via EM with Kalman smoothing.  
- Validated on simulations (including non-overlapping subsets) and on two in vivo two-photon datasets using “fictional” overlapping splits, benchmarked against a fully observed upper bound and naive lower bound.  
- Stitched models recover pairwise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs much better than the naive baseline and approach the fully observed model; cross-prediction of unseen neuron activity is also promising.  
- Novelty lies in a simple, scalable LDS approach to align and combine partial recordings to estimate cross-session interactions without monotonic stimulus–noise assumptions.  
- Technical soundness: principled LDS formulation, standard EM/Kalman inference, and a compelling identifiability argument for linear systems with sufficient data.  
- Empirical strength: strong parameter and correlation recovery in simulations; convincing improvements on real data relative to naive fits.  
- Practical overlap concern: although simulation allows non-overlap, real data requires overlap; likely causes include model mismatch, low SNR, nonstationarity, and registration errors—needs sensitivity analysis versus overlap fraction.  
- Parameter vs correlation gap: coupling recovery is strong in simulation but correlations with fully observed couplings are modest in real data; investigate diagonal Q/R assumptions, stimulus design, temporal resolution, slow drifts, and consider low-rank shared noise.  
- Stability and regularization: unclear how A’s stability (spectral radius < 1) is ensured or regularized during EM; clarify constraints or regularization used.  
- Model specification ambiguity: make explicit that Q and R are diagonal (if so) and consider reporting results with non-diagonal or low-rank noise to capture common inputs.  
- Scalability and compute details missing: report runtime, memory scaling, convergence behavior, and practical guidance for online EM (batch sizes, steps).  
- Real sequential validation lacking: “fictional” splits are clever but a demonstration on genuinely sequential, partially overlapping recordings is needed to strengthen claims.  
- Robustness tests needed: evaluate sensitivity to cell mis-registration, slow session-to-session drifts, and mismatched stimuli.  
- Clarity issues: expand figure captions (axes, units, overlap fraction, trials), more deeply interpret differences between simulated and real-data results, and fix notation/typos (e.g., Eq. (2)).  
- Methods details missing: specify initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity on A), hyperparameters, train/test splits, stimulus regressor construction, and whether traces were deconvolved or filtered; justify the LDS timescale.  
- Originality/significance: fills an important gap enabling stitching of partial recordings for joint dynamics estimation and has practical value for large-scale imaging and experimental design.  
- Pros: timely problem, principled/simple model, strong simulation validation, clear upper/lower bound benchmarking, and generalizability to multiple sessions.  
- Cons: reliance on fictional splits, practical need for overlap not characterized, limited analysis of model mismatch and noise structure, underreported scalability, and some presentation issues.  
- Suggested improvements: systematic study of performance vs overlap and session count, explore low-rank/shared noise, enforce/report A stability and regularization, robustness checks for mis-registration and drifts, include real sequential demo, and report computational costs.  
- Overall assessment: solid and timely contribution likely acceptable pending clarifications on overlap dependence, model assumptions, and stronger sequential/robustness validation.
