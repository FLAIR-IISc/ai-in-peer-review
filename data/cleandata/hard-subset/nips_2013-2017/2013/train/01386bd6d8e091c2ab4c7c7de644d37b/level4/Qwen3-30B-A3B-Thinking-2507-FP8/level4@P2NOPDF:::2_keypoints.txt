- Proposes a novel method to infer large-network properties by sequentially sampling subnetworks, fitting local models, and stitching them together; extends to point-process data and is promising for sparse-channel datasets (closest prior: Pillow & Latham 2008).

- Major limitation: heavy reliance on the linear-Gaussian assumption with no analysis of how non-linearities or non-Gaussian noise affect performance or cause breakdowns.

- Unclear marginal benefit of adding non-overlapping subnetworks versus simply increasing the size of a single sampled subset; the value of non-overlapping data is not justified.

- Real-data results require overlapping sampled populations to succeed, contradicting simulations; the paper lacks explanation of why overlap is necessary or how it resolves identifiability.

- In real data the model reproduces noise correlations but inferred couplings poorly match ground truth, raising the possibility that common inputs/latents, not pairwise couplings, drive the correlations.

- Insufficient technical detail: the core estimation algorithm—especially the M-step equation—is presented without derivation, explanation, or justification, making it hard to assess novelty or correctness.

- Overall assessment: conceptually compelling but weakened by limited scope analysis, unexplained real-data behavior, ambiguous interpretability of couplings, and inadequate methodological exposition; needs rigorous analysis of failure modes and full derivations.
