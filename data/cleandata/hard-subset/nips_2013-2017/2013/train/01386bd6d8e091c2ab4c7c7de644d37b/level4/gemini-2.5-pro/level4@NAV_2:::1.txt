This paper presents a statistical method for combining sequential, partial recordings of a neural circuit to infer the dynamics of the complete, larger population. The core idea is to model the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, enabling the characterization of population dynamics beyond the scale of simultaneous recordings. The authors frame this "stitching" procedure using a linear dynamical system (LDS) and estimate parameters via an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm. They validate their approach on both simulated data and two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, demonstrating the ability to predict noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded at the same time. This work builds upon a rich literature of inferring functional connectivity from neural data but carves out a novel and important niche by specifically addressing the challenge of non-simultaneous observations, a problem not tackled by most prior methods.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a critical and practical bottleneck in systems neuroscience. As experimental techniques for recording neural activity are fundamentally limited in scope, computational methods that can expand our effective field-of-view are highly valuable. This approach could enable data-driven modeling of larger functional circuits, such as entire cortical columns, which is currently out of reach.
*   **Originality and Approach:** The formulation of the problem as fitting an LDS with missing observations is elegant and powerful. While the components (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to "stitch" neural recordings is novel and effectively addresses the problem. The paper clearly differentiates its contribution from related work.
*   **Thorough Evaluation:** The method is convincingly validated on both synthetic and real neural data. The "fictional imaging scenario" on real data, complete with comparisons to well-defined upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds, provides a clear and rigorous benchmark of the method's performance. The successful recovery of noise correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons is a strong result.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Quality:** The paper reports a discrepancy between the simulated and experimental results regarding the need for overlapping neurons between sessions. While the model performs well on simulated non-overlapping populations, the authors state that overlap was necessary to achieve good performance on the real data. A more detailed discussion of the potential reasons for this—for example, model mismatch, noise structure in real data, or stationarity assumptions—would significantly strengthen the paper and provide crucial guidance for experimentalists planning to use this method.
*   **Quality:** There appears to be a substantial difference in the accuracy of recovering coupling parameters for non-simultaneously recorded pairs between the simulated data (Fig 2c, correlation ~0.73) and the real data (Fig 3b, correlation ~0.38). While correlation recovery is the main goal, a brief discussion on this performance gap would help readers better understand the practical limitations of inferring the underlying functional connectivity matrix itself from real-world datasets.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written, but the presentation could be improved with more descriptive figure legends and text. The current legends are quite concise. Expanding them, and more explicitly walking the reader through the different panels and their implications within the main text, would enhance the manuscript's clarity and impact.

**Summary:**
This paper presents a valuable and well-executed contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. It introduces a novel and significant method for overcoming experimental limitations in population recordings. The approach is technically sound, and its effectiveness is demonstrated through a solid evaluation framework. While there are minor points regarding the discussion of model limitations and the clarity of the figures that could be addressed to improve the manuscript, the overall work is of high quality. The method is likely to be used and built upon by other researchers seeking to understand the dynamics of large-scale neural circuits.
