- Proposes stitching sequential, partially overlapping recordings by treating unobserved neurons as missing data and fitting an LDS via EM to infer a single latent dynamical system and predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons; validated on simulations and two calcium datasets against fully observed and naive baselines.

- Builds on prior partially observed latent-variable work but is novel in explicitly stitching sessions to recover cross-couplings and predict cross-session correlations; potentially extensible to point-process data.

- The LDS + EM modeling choice is natural and standard; empirical results show stitched models predict non-simultaneous pairwise and noise correlations much better than a naive baseline and often close to a fully observed model.

- Unclear how violations of linear-Gaussian assumptions (nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, calcium measurement nonlinearities) affect estimation or cause stitching to fail.

- Identifiability concerns: unclear how much new information additional subsets provide versus simply collecting more data from one subset when latent space is identifiable only up to a linear transform.

- Dependence on overlap: real-data stitching required overlap, suggesting simulations do not capture experimental complexities; need analysis of how performance scales with overlap and behavior with no overlap.

- Gains in predicting noise correlations may reflect shared/common input rather than direct pairwise couplings; models with explicit low-dimensional latent inputs should be considered or compared.

- Methodological details are sparse: M-step derivations, full EM procedure (initialization, online EM, convergence diagnostics), regularization, handling correlated observation noise, and session nonstationarities are insufficiently described.

- Clarity is good at a high level but terse on critical estimation details; recommend an appendix with derivations and a schematic of the stitching workflow.

- Originality: compelling and practically important application to calcium imaging, despite building on known LDS/EM machinery.

- Significance: could expand the utility of imaging/electrophysiology datasets by enabling inference of network-level statistics and cross-session noise correlations; extension to GLMs would broaden reach but has computational/identifiability challenges.

- Strengths: clear problem formulation, simple/scalable model, convincing simulation and real-data demonstrations, practical relevance.

- Weaknesses: heavy reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions, unclear identifiability gains from additional subsets, dependence on overlap, potential confounding of common input and couplings, and sparse methodological exposition.

- Suggestions: supply M-step derivations or citations and full EM details; analyze identifiability and quantify information added by new subsets; empirically probe performance vs overlap; compare to models with explicit latent inputs; run robustness checks for nonlinearities, non-Gaussian and correlated noise, and calcium nonlinearities; discuss scalability and extension to point-process models.

- Overall: promising and potentially powerful idea demonstrated on real data, but needs stronger methodological exposition and robustness/identifiability analyses to be a substantial contribution.
