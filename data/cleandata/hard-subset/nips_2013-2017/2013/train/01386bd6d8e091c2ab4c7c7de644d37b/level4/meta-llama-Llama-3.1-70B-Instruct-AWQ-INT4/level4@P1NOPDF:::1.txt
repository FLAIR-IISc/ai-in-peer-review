The paper contributes a valuable statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, thereby expanding the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. This approach provides a more comprehensive understanding of the circuit, which cannot be imaged in its entirety due to experimental and technical limitations. The method is applied to both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data in mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate correlations in variability across two populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a simple linear Gaussian system. The model is defined by four parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix that models the stimulus dependence of the population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations of neurons, but when applied to real data, it requires a certain level of overlap between populations to achieve reasonable results. While it is clear that a greater overlap leads to better results, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated data example. Furthermore, the authors do not address the source of the significant difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b). Nevertheless, the model's ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs is a notable strength.

Clarity
The paper is well-written, although the figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text related to the figures in the body of the paper could provide more elaboration. This would facilitate a deeper understanding of the figures and their significance.

Originality
The authors acknowledge several works that have addressed the question of inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data or electrophysiological measurements. However, these works do not infer functional connections of non-simultaneously imaged neurons, making the present contribution novel.

Significance
There are fundamental limits to the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged, which restricts our ability to reveal the nature of neural computation at the circuit level (beyond a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method for expanding these limited capabilities, thereby increasing our understanding of neural circuits.

Overall, this is a strong paper that utilizes a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture measures that are not easily retrieved otherwise. The approach is valuable for the neuroscience community, and the results demonstrate its potential for characterizing population dynamics in neural circuits.
