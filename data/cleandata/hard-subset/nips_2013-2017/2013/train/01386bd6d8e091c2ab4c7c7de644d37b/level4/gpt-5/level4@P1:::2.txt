Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes a method for “stitching” together sequential recordings of partially overlapping neural populations to infer a single latent dynamical system (LDS) for the full circuit. The approach treats unobserved neurons as missing data and fits a linear Gaussian state-space model across sessions via EM, allowing prediction of correlations—including noise correlations—between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is demonstrated on simulations and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse S1 (awake and anesthetized), using a “fictional” split to emulate sequential imaging. Performance is benchmarked against a fully observed upper bound and a naive baseline that combines independently fit submodels.

This idea is closely related to prior work on partially observed neural populations and latent-variable models (e.g., Pillow and Latham, NIPS 2008), but differs in explicitly “stitching” across sessions to recover cross-couplings and correlations between non-simultaneously recorded units. If extended to point-process models, it could be valuable for electrophysiology datasets where a subset of channels is moved across days.

Quality
The core modeling choice—an LDS with missing observations—fits naturally with the problem, and the EM implementation appears standard. The empirical results convincingly show that the stitched model predicts pairwise correlations between non-overlapping neurons markedly better than a naive baseline, and often close to a fully observed model. On simulations, the method recovers cross-couplings and noise correlations; on real data, correlation matrices are well predicted even when couplings themselves align only weakly with those from a fully observed fit.

However, several technical aspects remain unclear:
- Scope of linear-Gaussian assumptions: The paper provides limited intuition about how violations (nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, calcium measurement nonlinearities) affect estimation, or when stitching fails.
- Identifiability and value of additional subsets: In the idealized case where one subset suffices to identify the full model up to a linear transform in the latent space, it is unclear how much new information the second subset contributes about the coupling matrix, versus simply collecting more data from the first subset.
- Overlap requirement: On real data, stitching required some overlap between subsets. This suggests the simulation regime does not fully capture experimental complexities. Intuition or analysis is needed to explain how overlap constrains the model, and how performance scales with overlap size.
- Common input vs pairwise couplings: The real-data gains in predicting noise correlations may reflect shared inputs rather than direct pairwise couplings. A model with explicit latent inputs (low-dimensional common drive) might be more appropriate for capturing noise correlations than dense pairwise couplings.
- Estimation details: The M-step updates are presented without derivation or clear reference, and the overall description of the estimation procedure (including online EM, initialization, and convergence diagnostics) is very brief. If these updates are standard, the novelty may rest more in problem formulation and the stitching protocol than in algorithmic contribution; either way, more methodological clarity is needed.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written at a high level, with a motivating application and thorough contextualization. That said, the introduction and discussion occupy substantial space, while critical estimation details are sparse. The notation around session-specific observation matrices is concise but may be terse for non-experts. A short derivation or an appendix citing standard LDS EM updates, along with a schematic of the stitching workflow, would improve accessibility.

Originality
The idea of stitching partial recordings to recover cross-session correlations and couplings is compelling and, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated in this form for calcium imaging. It builds on known LDS/EM machinery and prior work on partially observed populations, but the application to predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is novel and practically important.

Significance
If robust, this approach could substantially expand the utility of current imaging/ephys datasets by enabling inference of network-level statistics beyond the field of view or recording capacity. The ability to predict noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs is particularly impactful. Extending the approach to point-process GLMs would broaden applicability, though computational and identifiability issues may arise.

Strengths
- Clear and important problem formulation; practical relevance to current imaging capabilities.
- Simple, scalable model and inference procedure.
- Convincing demonstrations that stitched models predict non-simultaneous correlations well, outperforming a naive baseline and approaching a fully observed upper bound.
- Application to real datasets, not just simulations.

Weaknesses and questions
- Heavy reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions, with limited analysis of robustness to violations common in calcium imaging.
- Unclear identifiability gains from additional subsets when one subset already constrains the model up to a latent transform.
- Dependence on overlap between sessions on real data; need theory/intuition on why overlap helps and how much is needed.
- Potential conflation of common input and direct couplings; consider explicit latent factors to capture shared drive.
- Sparse methodological detail: lack of derivation for M-step, limited discussion of initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity), correlated observation noise, and session-to-session nonstationarities.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide derivations (or precise citations) for the M-step updates and outline the full EM procedure, including initialization, online EM details, and convergence checks.
- Analyze identifiability: conditions under which cross-session stitching uniquely determines cross-couplings; quantify the added information from a second subset versus longer recordings of one subset.
- Explain and empirically probe the role of overlap: performance as a function of overlap fraction, and behavior with no overlap under realistic deviations from LDS assumptions.
- Incorporate or compare against models with explicit low-dimensional latent inputs to account for common drive; report how this affects coupling estimates and noise-correlation predictions.
- Report robustness checks: synthetic experiments with nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, correlated observation noise, and calcium measurement nonlinearities.
- Discuss scalability and computational complexity; indicate how the approach might extend to point-process models and what limits arise.

Overall evaluation
A neat and potentially powerful idea, demonstrated on real data, but the paper is light on key estimation details and on analyses that would clarify when and why stitching works (or fails). With stronger methodological exposition and robustness analysis, this could make a significant contribution.
