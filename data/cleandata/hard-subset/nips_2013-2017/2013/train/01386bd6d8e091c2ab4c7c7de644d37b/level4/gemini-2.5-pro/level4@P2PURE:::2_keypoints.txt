- Proposes a novel method to infer large-network properties by sequentially recording from subnetworks and stitching sub-models, extended to point-process data for shifting-channel datasets.  
- Method appears original, with closest prior work being Pillow and Latham on partially observed spiking populations.  
- Paper lacks clear discussion of limitations, especially dependence on the linear-Gaussian assumption.  
- No intuition or analysis provided on how fitting fails when model assumptions (linearity/Gaussianity) are violated.  
- Unclear what additional information later subsets provide about the coupling matrix when a single sub-model can already recover the full model up to linear latent transforms.  
- Real-data experiments show the model needs overlap between observed populations to succeed, suggesting simulations don’t capture real-data complexity.  
- Paper does not explain why overlap constrains the model or how overlap aids identifiability.  
- On real data, fitted noise correlations match well but estimated couplings poorly correlate with ground truth (Fig. 3b), challenging interpretation.  
- Raises possibility that noise correlations are driven by common inputs or latent variables rather than direct pairwise couplings the model assumes.  
- Methodological detail is insufficient: key estimation steps (notably the M-step update) lack derivation and explanation.  
- If the M-step result is trivial to LDS experts, the paper’s claimed novelty would be substantially reduced.  
- Overall, interesting and promising idea but weakened by insufficient methodological clarity and unaddressed model limitations.
