Summary
The paper proposes a method for inferring network properties of large neural populations by sequentially recording from overlapping subnetworks, fitting submodels, and “stitching” them into a global model. The approach is presented in a linear-Gaussian framework and extended conceptually to point-process observations. The idea is timely and potentially impactful, particularly in experimental settings where electrodes are moved daily and full simultaneous coverage is infeasible. I am not aware of a close prior that addresses stitching across subsets at this scale; Pillow and Latham’s work on partially observed spiking populations seems the closest conceptual antecedent. Overall, the idea is compelling and well-motivated.

Strengths
- Addresses a practically important problem: reconstructing network structure from partial, sequential recordings.
- Conceptually elegant: leverages overlap across subsets to align and merge submodels.
- Demonstrates empirical utility on simulated data and applies the method to real neural recordings.
- If scalable and robust, could be widely applicable to existing multi-day datasets.

Main concerns and questions
1) Dependence on linear-Gaussian assumptions
- It is unclear how sensitive the stitching procedure and parameter recovery are to deviations from linear-Gaussian dynamics and noise. The paper does not provide a clear intuition or empirical exploration of failure modes under misspecification (e.g., nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, nonstationarities).
- Please characterize when and how the estimation degrades as assumptions are violated. For example, what happens with mildly nonlinear transfer functions, spike count saturation, or temporally correlated noise?

2) Information gain from multiple subsets vs. longer recordings of a single subset
- In the extreme case where a single subset suffices to identify the full model (up to a linear transformation of latent states), what additional information about the coupling matrix is provided by adding more subsets? How does this compare to simply collecting more data from the original subset?
- A concrete analysis (e.g., Fisher information or asymptotic variance) or a controlled simulation would clarify when stitching new subsets is beneficial beyond extending recording time.

3) Role of overlap between subsets
- The real-data results suggest the method works only when there is overlap between recorded populations, whereas the linear-Gaussian simulations suggest broader identifiability. Please provide intuition and, if possible, theory: how does overlap constrain the transformations that align submodels? What minimal overlap is required as a function of noise level and network coupling? How does overlap mitigate rotational/scale ambiguities in latent space?

4) Interpreting couplings vs. correlations on real data
- In real data, noise correlations are fit well, but inferred pairwise couplings correlate poorly with ground truth (Fig. 3b). This raises the possibility that the model’s goodness-of-fit is driven primarily by common input rather than direct synaptic coupling. Latent-variable models might capture these correlations more parsimoniously than dense pairwise couplings.
- Please clarify how you distinguish common-input effects from direct couplings. Comparisons to low-rank latent-variable models (e.g., PLDS/GPFA or GLM+latent factors) and partial correlation/Granger analyses would help interpret what structure the stitching recovers.

5) Estimation details and novelty
- A substantial portion of the paper is devoted to introduction and discussion, whereas the estimation details are sparse. The M-step equation (bottom of p.3) is presented as a key contribution but is not derived or explained.
- If the update reduces to a standard LDS EM step applied to a particular block-structured problem, the novelty should be reframed accordingly; if not, the paper should include a clear derivation, conditions, and algorithmic steps. Please also provide complexity, convergence behavior, and practical guidance (e.g., initialization, regularization, and handling ill-conditioning).

Suggested experiments and analyses
- Robustness to misspecification: Simulations with controlled deviations from linear-Gaussian assumptions (nonlinear dynamics, Poisson/overdispersed observations, temporally correlated noise, and nonstationarities).
- Overlap ablation: Vary the fraction and identity of overlapping neurons across subsets to quantify identifiability and parameter error. Include cases with zero overlap and with indirect overlap via chains of subsets.
- Information trade-off: Compare adding new subsets versus extending recording duration for a fixed subset under equal data budgets.
- Common-input control: Fit latent-variable baselines (e.g., PLDS with low-rank latent factors) and report predictive performance, noise correlation fits, and coupling recovery after accounting for latent structure.
- Metrics beyond correlations: Evaluate predictive log-likelihood on held-out neurons and time, multi-step forecasts, and recovery of directed effects (e.g., Granger or impulse response structure) where applicable.
- Practical considerations: Examine robustness to session-to-session drift and spike sorting variability, and the impact of alignment errors in overlapping units.
- Reproducibility: Provide derivations, pseudocode, and, if possible, code to facilitate adoption.

Clarity and presentation
- Expand the methods section with explicit notation for stitched parameters, the alignment transformation(s) across subsets, and identifiability constraints.
- Derive the M-step (or cite a precise source) and explain how expectations are computed across disjoint and overlapping time windows.
- Clarify the extension to point-process observations: specify the observation model and how EM or variational updates are modified relative to the Gaussian case.

Overall assessment
This is a neat and potentially powerful idea with clear practical relevance. However, the current manuscript under-explains the core estimation procedure and does not sufficiently probe where and why the method succeeds or fails outside the idealized linear-Gaussian regime. Strengthening the theoretical intuition for identifiability (especially the role of overlap), clarifying the true source of performance gains on real data (direct couplings vs. common input), and providing fuller methodological details would significantly increase the paper’s impact and credibility. I encourage the authors to revise with these points in mind.
