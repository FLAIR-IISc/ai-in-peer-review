The paper contributes a statistical method to “stitch” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. This method expands the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized, exceeding the number of neurons simultaneously imaged, and provides a more comprehensive view of the circuit than is possible due to experimental and technical limitations. The method is applied to both simulated and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, and the authors demonstrate their ability to estimate correlations in variability across populations that were not imaged concurrently.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, specifically employing a simple linear Gaussian system. The model is defined by four parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix modeling the stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices representing neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. 

**Quality**

The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations of neurons. However, when applied to real data, reasonable results require a certain degree of overlap between the imaged populations. While the benefit of greater overlap is intuitive, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary to achieve accurate results in the simulated data example. 

Furthermore, the authors do not address the substantial difference in estimated correlation strength observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b). Despite this, the recovery of pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs remains a key strength of the model.

**Clarity**

The paper is well-written, but the figure legends lack sufficient detail, and the accompanying text does not fully elaborate on the information presented in the figures.

**Originality**

The authors acknowledge several existing works addressing functional connectivity inference from 2-photon imaging data and electrophysiological measurements. However, these prior studies do not infer functional connections between neurons that were *not* imaged simultaneously.

**Significance**

Fundamental limitations in the number of simultaneously imageable neurons restrict our ability to reveal the nature of neural computation at the circuit level (or beyond a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method to overcome these limitations, offering a pathway to expand our capabilities. 

Overall, this is a strong methodological paper. It leverages a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture information that is otherwise difficult to obtain.
