

This paper proposes a novel approach for inferring global network properties by sequentially sampling subnetworks, estimating local models, and stitching them together. The extension to point-process data is particularly compelling for applications involving sparse channel recordings (e.g., neuroscience datasets where only a subset of channels is active daily). To my knowledge, this represents a significant departure from existing methods, with Pillow & Latham (2008) being the closest related work. The core idea is powerful and addresses a meaningful practical challenge in large-scale network analysis.

However, several critical gaps in the exposition and technical justification require clarification before the method’s true contribution can be assessed. First, the paper’s reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions is not adequately contextualized. While the method is theoretically grounded under these conditions, the authors provide no intuition about how deviations—such as non-linearities, heavy-tailed noise, or non-Gaussian dynamics—would degrade performance. Specifically, the claim that "the full model can be estimated from one subset (up to linear transformation)" raises a fundamental question: if one subset suffices for a full model, what specific information does adding *additional* subsets provide? The paper fails to quantify the marginal gain of incorporating more subsets versus simply increasing the data volume from a single subset. This ambiguity undermines the necessity of the proposed sequential approach.

A related concern arises from the real-data experiments: the model only succeeds when there is *overlap* between sampled subnetworks. This contradicts the linear-Gaussian simulation results and suggests the model’s behavior in practice is governed by factors not captured by the theoretical framework. The authors should provide a mechanistic explanation for why overlap is essential—it likely relates to resolving ambiguities in latent variable alignment, but the paper offers no intuition. Without this, the practical utility of the method remains unclear, as real-world data may not guarantee such overlap.

Further, the real-data results reveal a troubling disconnect between the model’s fit to noise correlations (which are well-explained) and its couplings (which correlate poorly with ground truth in Figure 3b). This discrepancy strongly suggests that the model may be conflating *common input effects* with direct pairwise couplings. In neuroscience, noise correlations often arise from shared inputs rather than direct connections, so the model’s reliance on pairwise couplings may be misinterpreting the data. The authors should address whether latent-variable models (which explicitly account for common inputs) would better explain the observed correlations, or if their approach inherently confounds these sources.

The paper’s technical presentation also suffers from insufficient detail. The M-step equation (bottom of page 3) is presented as a key contribution but lacks derivation, motivation, or discussion of its novelty relative to existing state-space estimation techniques. While this may be standard for experts in linear dynamical systems (LDS), omitting these details erodes the paper’s credibility for a broader audience. The extensive discussion of background and motivation is disproportionate to the sparse treatment of the core estimation procedure. A concise derivation or comparison to analogous LDS updates would significantly strengthen the contribution.

In summary, the paper introduces a conceptually appealing solution to a genuine problem in network inference, and the real-data validation is a strong point. However, the method’s limitations under non-ideal assumptions, the unexplained necessity of subnetwork overlap, the potential misattribution of noise correlations to direct couplings, and the lack of technical depth in the estimation procedure collectively weaken its impact. The authors have a valuable idea but have not sufficiently demonstrated its robustness, necessity, or distinctiveness from existing approaches. Addressing these gaps—particularly by clarifying the scope of the linear-Gaussian assumption, explaining the role of overlap, and providing a rigorous derivation of the M-step—would substantially elevate the paper. As it stands, the method’s practical applicability remains uncertain, and its novelty is not fully justified.
