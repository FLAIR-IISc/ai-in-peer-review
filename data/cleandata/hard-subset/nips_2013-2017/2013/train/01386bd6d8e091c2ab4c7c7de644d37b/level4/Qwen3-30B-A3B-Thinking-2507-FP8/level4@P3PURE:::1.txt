

The paper introduces a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged neuronal populations, enabling characterization of population dynamics beyond the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged. This approach provides a more complete characterization of neural circuits that cannot be imaged in their entirety due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, demonstrating the ability to estimate correlations in variability between two non-simultaneously imaged neuronal populations.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian framework. The model defines four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields matrix modeling stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

**Quality**  
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, when applied to real data, a minimum level of population overlap is required for reasonable results. While the dependence on overlap is intuitively clear, the authors do not explain why overlap was unnecessary in the simulated example (Fig 2c). Furthermore, they fail to address the large discrepancy in correlation estimates between the simulated data (Fig 2c) and real data (Fig 3b).

The model's key strength lies in recovering pairwise correlations between neuron pairs imaged non-simultaneously.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-written, though figure legends lack sufficient detail, and the text fails to elaborate sufficiently on the figures' key insights.

**Originality**  
The authors cite prior work on inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon or electrophysiological data, but these studies do not address the inference of functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons—this paper’s core contribution.

**Significance**  
Current limitations on the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged fundamentally constrain our ability to characterize neural circuit computation (typically beyond a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method to expand these capabilities, offering a promising path toward studying larger-scale neural dynamics.

Overall, this is a well-executed model paper that employs a relatively simple linear dynamics framework to capture measures otherwise difficult to retrieve.
