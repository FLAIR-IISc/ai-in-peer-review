The paper presents a statistical method to "stitch" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, thereby expanding the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the limitations imposed by simultaneously imaging neurons. This approach provides a more comprehensive understanding of the neural circuit, which cannot be imaged in its entirety due to experimental and technical constraints. The authors apply this method to both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, demonstrating its ability to estimate correlations in variability across two non-simultaneously imaged populations.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian system. The model is defined by four parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix that accounts for the stimulus dependence of population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. The parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations of neurons. However, when applied to real data, the populations must have a certain level of overlap to yield reasonable results. It is intuitive that a greater overlap leads to better results, but it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated data example. Furthermore, the authors do not address the discrepancy in correlation values between the simulated data in Figure 2c and the real data in Figure 3b. A notable strength of the model is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs.

Clarity
The paper is well-written, although the figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text accompanying the figures in the main body could provide more elaboration. This would enhance the reader's understanding of the figures and their significance.

Originality
The authors acknowledge several works that have addressed the question of inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data or electrophysiological measurements. However, these works do not infer functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons, highlighting the novelty of the proposed approach.

Significance
The number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged is limited by fundamental constraints, restricting our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level (i.e., beyond a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method to overcome these limitations, thereby expanding our capabilities to study neural circuits.

Overall, this is a good model paper that utilizes a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture measures that are challenging to obtain otherwise.
