This paper introduces a statistical framework to “stitch” together sequentially imaged neuronal populations. The approach enables characterization of population dynamics beyond the number of neurons recorded simultaneously and provides a more complete view of circuits that cannot be imaged as a whole due to experimental and technical limits. The method is evaluated on simulated data and on mouse calcium imaging, and the authors show they can estimate cross-population variability correlations between groups that were not imaged at the same time.

Model and approach
The problem is formulated as inference in a latent dynamical system with missing observations, instantiated as a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model includes four key components: (1) a functional coupling (state transition) matrix, (2) a stimulus receptive-field matrix capturing stimulus-driven activity, and (3–4) covariance matrices for latent neural noise and measurement noise. Parameters are learned from data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality
- On simulated data comprising two non-overlapping neuronal populations, the method performs well. However, on real data, a certain degree of overlap between populations is required to achieve reliable estimates. While it is intuitive that performance improves with increased overlap, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulations. Clarifying the assumptions in the simulated setting (e.g., model correctness, signal-to-noise levels, identifiability conditions, or shared stimulus structure) that obviate overlap would strengthen the paper.
- The manuscript does not discuss the source of the pronounced difference in correlation estimates observed in simulations (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b). An analysis of factors contributing to this discrepancy—such as model mismatch, noise levels, biological variability, or preprocessing differences—would help interpret the results and set expectations for real-world performance.
- A major strength of the approach is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded concurrently, a quantity that is otherwise difficult to access.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written. However, the figure legends are too terse, and the main text does not sufficiently guide the reader through the figures. More descriptive captions (including definitions of all plotted quantities, axes, and key takeaways) and a tighter integration of figure interpretation into the narrative would improve readability and comprehension.

Originality
Related work has addressed inference of functional connectivity from two-photon imaging or electrophysiology, but has not tackled the challenge of linking neurons that were not imaged simultaneously. This paper fills that gap by offering a principled statistical framework for stitching sequential recordings and inferring cross-population relationships.

Significance
Fundamental constraints limit how many neurons can be imaged simultaneously, which in turn restricts our ability to investigate circuit-level computation beyond a few hundred cells. This work provides a practical method to extend inference beyond these limits, potentially enabling larger-scale analyses of population dynamics and inter-neuronal correlations across sequential sessions.

Overall assessment
This is a strong modeling contribution that uses a relatively simple linear dynamical system to recover statistics that are otherwise hard to obtain, particularly correlations between non-overlapping, non-simultaneously recorded populations. The empirical demonstrations are compelling, but the paper would benefit from:
- A clear explanation for why overlap is unnecessary in the simulated case but essential for real data, including discussion of identifiability and sensitivity to overlap size.
- An analysis of the discrepancy in inferred correlations between simulated and experimental settings.
- More informative figure captions and expanded figure-related discussion in the text.

With these clarifications, the paper would present a more complete and transparent account of the method’s assumptions, limitations, and practical utility.
