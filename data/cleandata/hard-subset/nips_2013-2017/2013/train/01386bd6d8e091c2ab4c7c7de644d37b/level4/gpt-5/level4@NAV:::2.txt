This paper demonstrates that one can infer properties of a large network by sequentially recording from smaller subnetworks and then stitching the resulting submodels together. Extended to point-process data, this could be applicable to many datasets where a few channels are typically moved each day. I am not aware of closely related work (J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons" seems closest), and the idea appears quite compelling.

In practice, it is unclear how constrained this method is to data that are approximately linear-Gaussian, and the paper does not provide much intuition for how the fitting procedure might fail when assumptions are violated. In the limiting case where the full model can be recovered from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it is not obvious how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets, versus simply collecting more data from one subset.

Another issue prompting questions is why the model on real data only works when there is some overlap between the two populations. This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture the full picture, and it would be helpful to provide intuition for how overlap helps better constrain the model.

In the real-data experiments, where the linearity assumption likely breaks down, one must also ask how much of the explained noise correlations (which are fit well, while the couplings themselves show little correlation with the true couplings in 3b) are driven by common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. I would expect that noise correlations could be modeled more effectively with latent variables than with pairwise couplings.

The manuscript spends considerable space on the introduction and discussion but is quite light on the estimation details. The M-step equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as the main contribution, yet it is neither explained nor derived. Perhaps this is straightforward to LDS experts, but that would diminish much of the paper’s novelty. The authors have a very good idea, but it needs clearer exposition to properly convey the significance of the result.

A neat idea tested on real data, but several details remain unclear.
