- Proposes a novel statistical method to “stitch” sequentially imaged subnetworks to infer dynamics of large neural populations.  
- Tackles a key limitation of 2-photon calcium imaging where circuit size is constrained by simultaneously recorded neurons.  
- Core formulation: fit a latent dynamical system with missing observations, potentially broadly applicable to partial recordings.  
- Extends prior functional-connectivity approaches to infer connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons.  
- Argues existing subsampling methods are not directly applicable to this stitching problem.  
- Strength: elegant problem formulation and demonstration on both simulated and real calcium imaging data.  
- Synthetic experiments show recovery of underlying coupling matrices and prediction of noise correlations.  
- Real-data results: successfully predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, nearing fully observed model performance.  
- Major limitation: reliance on linear-Gaussian dynamics; robustness to nonlinearity/non-Gaussianity is not explored.  
- Unclear why overlap between observed populations is necessary; the requirement is not adequately explained.  
- Simulated data may not capture the complexity of real recordings, but this discrepancy is not elaborated.  
- Concern that predicted correlations could be driven by common input rather than direct pairwise couplings; latent shared-noise modeling is suggested.  
- Presentation issue: mathematical details (especially M-step derivation and assumptions) are underexplained, limiting accessibility.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel, elegant approach; strong simulated and real-data performance; ability to predict non-simultaneous correlations; clear literature context.  
- Arguments against acceptance: simplifying linear-Gaussian assumptions, limited robustness analysis, unclear overlap necessity, and insufficient detail on estimation procedure.
