This paper demonstrates that one can infer properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then stitching the resulting submodels. With an extension to point-process data, this could be applied to many datasets where a few channels are typically moved each day. I am not aware of closely related work (J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, “Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons” seems the closest), and the idea appears powerful.

In practice, it is unclear how much the method is constrained to data that are close to linear-Gaussian, and the paper does not provide sufficient intuition for how fitting might fail as assumptions are violated. In the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it is not clear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is obtained by adding other subsets, as opposed to simply collecting more data from one subset.

Another point of concern is why the model on real data only works when there is some overlap between the two populations. This suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture the full complexity, and it would be helpful to provide at least some intuition for how overlap helps to better constrain the model.

In the real-data experiments—where the linearity assumption likely breaks—one must also consider how much of the explained noise correlations (which are fit well, while the estimated couplings show little correlation with the true couplings in Fig. 3b) are due to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. I would expect noise correlations to be better captured by latent variables than by pairwise couplings.

The paper devotes substantial space to the introduction and discussion but is quite light on the details of estimation. The M-step equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as a main contribution but is neither explained nor derived. Perhaps this is trivial to LDS experts, but if so, that diminishes much of the paper’s novelty. The underlying idea is very good, but it needs clearer exposition to convey the significance of the result.

A neat idea tested on real data, but some critical details remain unclear.
