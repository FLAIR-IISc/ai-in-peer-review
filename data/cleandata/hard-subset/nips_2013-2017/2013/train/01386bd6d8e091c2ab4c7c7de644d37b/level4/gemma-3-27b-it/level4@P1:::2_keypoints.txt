- Presents a method to infer network properties by sequentially recording subnetworks and “stitching” sub-models to recover large-population dynamics.  
- Potentially widely applicable to datasets with partial recordings and extendable to point-process data.  
- Few close prior works; Pillow & Latham’s partially observed population work is the most relevant but doesn’t address sequential stitching.  
- The idea is powerful and offers a promising way to scale neural circuit analysis.  
- Unclear robustness to deviations from the linear–Gaussian assumptions; no analysis of how performance degrades under violations.  
- Not obvious how much extra information additional subsets provide versus collecting more data from a single larger subset.  
- Requirement for overlap between recorded populations is not well explained and suggests the simulation model may miss key data complexities.  
- Real-data experiments likely violate linearity; interpretation of results therefore needs caution.  
- Model predicts noise correlations well, but estimated pairwise couplings do not strongly match “true” couplings (Fig. 3b).  
- Raises the possibility that explained noise correlations reflect common inputs or latent factors rather than direct pairwise couplings.  
- Suggests modeling noise correlations with latent variables rather than relying solely on pairwise couplings.  
- Paper lacks sufficient detail on the estimation procedure; the M-step equation is presented without adequate explanation or derivation.  
- Overall assessment: neat and validated on real data, but several methodological and interpretive details require clarification.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel approach, predicts noise correlations in simulated/real data, addresses practical recording limits, and could scale analyses.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited robustness discussion, inadequate explanation of overlap necessity, insufficient M-step details, and potential confounds like common input not fully addressed.
