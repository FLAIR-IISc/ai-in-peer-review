This paper presents a novel statistical approach for integrating data from sequential neuronal imaging, effectively increasing the population size available for studying neural dynamics beyond the constraints of simultaneous imaging. This is particularly valuable given the experimental and technical limitations that prevent whole-circuit imaging. The method is demonstrated using both simulated and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, successfully estimating correlations in variability between neuronal populations not imaged concurrently.

The authors formulate the problem as estimating the parameters of a latent dynamical system with incomplete observations, specifically employing a linear Gaussian system. The model incorporates four key parameter sets: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix representing stimulus-dependent activity, and covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a modified Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

Regarding quality, the model performs well on simulated data with distinct neuronal populations. However, application to real data requires a substantial degree of overlap between imaged populations to yield reliable results. While the benefit of increased overlap is acknowledged, the discrepancy between the simulation results (where overlap wasn’t necessary) and the real data results isn’t fully explained. Furthermore, the significant difference in the magnitude of correlations observed in the simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus the experimental data (Fig. 3b) warrants further discussion. Despite these points, the model’s ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons imaged at different times is a key strength.

The paper is generally well-written, but the figure legends could be more detailed, and the text should provide more in-depth elaboration of the figures’ content.

In terms of originality, the authors appropriately acknowledge existing work on functional connectivity inference from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiology. However, these prior studies do not address the specific challenge of inferring connections between *non-simultaneously* imaged neurons, representing a clear novelty of this work.

The significance of this work lies in its potential to overcome a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: the restricted number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged. By expanding the accessible population size, this method offers a pathway towards a more comprehensive understanding of neural computation at the circuit level. 

Overall, this is a strong methodological contribution that leverages a relatively simple linear dynamics model to extract information – specifically, correlations – that would otherwise be difficult to obtain.
