- Proposes stitching sequentially imaged neuron subsets into a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations to recover couplings and predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
- Model components: coupling A, stimulus drive B, diagonal process noise Q, measurement noise R, offset d; parameters learned via EM with Kalman smoothing and online-EM across sessions.
- Validated on simulations and two 2-photon calcium datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1); benchmarked against fictional fully-observed upper bound and naive per-session baseline.
- Stitched model accurately predicts pairwise/noise correlations for non-overlapping neuron pairs and cross-predicts unobserved activity from observed neurons.
- Novelty: operationalizes cross-session stitching with an LDS and demonstrates empirical effectiveness for calcium imaging datasets.
- Relation to prior work: extends latent-state and connectivity inference literature by addressing interactions between neurons never recorded together, without strong monotonicity assumptions.
- Strength: clear probabilistic formulation and tractable EM/Kalman-smoothing inference with per-session observation matrices.
- Strength: strong synthetic experiments showing recovery of cross-session couplings and accurate non-overlap correlation prediction.
- Strength: real-data analyses show stitched models approach fully-observed performance for correlation metrics and outperform naive baseline.
- Weakness: real-data performance required session overlap whereas simulations did not—needs analysis of causes (model mismatch, calcium nonlinearity, sampling rate, nonstationarity, diagonal Q/R assumption).
- Weakness: parameter/coupling recovery gap between simulations (~0.73) and real data (~0.38) needs explanation (model mismatch, unmodeled shared inputs, limited SNR).
- Weakness: identifiability and conditions for successful stitching (observability/controllability, stationarity, excitation) are not sufficiently clarified.
- Weakness: assuming diagonal Q and R may be unrealistic given shared noise sources; low-rank-plus-diagonal noise models may improve realism and robustness.
- Clarity issues: figures need more self-contained legends (mark simultaneous vs non-simultaneous blocks, overlap fraction, axes, error bars); notation and fictional protocol should be defined earlier.
- Reproducibility gaps: report initialization, stopping criteria, runtime, and scaling with N, T, and number of sessions; provide code and dataset splits if possible.
- Suggestions: quantify performance vs overlap and inter-session time, explore low-rank noise or latent inputs, add sparsity/anatomical priors on A, and discuss identifiability/alignment theory.
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses an important experimental limitation with a principled, tractable method, strong empirical results, and practical applicability to imaging groups.
- Arguments against acceptance: limited analysis of failure modes (overlap, nonstationarity), model simplicity limiting interpretability, and some clarity/reproducibility shortcomings.
- Overall assessment: solid, timely contribution that convincingly demonstrates stitching feasibility; leans toward acceptance pending additional analyses on overlap sensitivity and clearer presentation.
