

This paper presents a method for "stitching" together sequentially recorded neural subpopulations into a unified dynamical model, enabling inference of population dynamics beyond the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, demonstrating their approach using calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. This work extends recent NIPS contributions on neural population analysis (Pillow & Latham, 2008; Wohrer et al., 2010) but addresses a distinct challenge: inferring connectivity between populations recorded in separate sessions rather than single-session partial observations.

The paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a fundamental limitation in current neural recording technology. While 2-photon calcium imaging now allows simultaneous recording of hundreds of neurons, cortical circuits often involve thousands of neurons. The authors' method provides a practical approach to expand the effective population size for dynamical analysis, which is crucial for understanding neural computation. Their experimental validation on both simulated data and real mouse somatosensory cortex recordings demonstrates the method's effectiveness in predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

Strengths: The paper excels in quality, with thorough validation on both synthetic and real datasets. The experimental design effectively demonstrates the method's utility, with clear performance metrics comparing against both upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) bounds. The significance of the work is high, as it directly addresses a major technical bottleneck in neural circuit analysis, with the potential to substantially expand the scope of neural population studies. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method to validation.

Weaknesses: The main weakness lies in the limited theoretical justification for the method's applicability beyond linear-Gaussian assumptions. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore how deviations from these assumptions might affect performance in real neural data, where dynamics are often nonlinear. While the authors reference Pillow & Latham (2008), they don't clearly distinguish their approach from this prior work, particularly regarding the sequential nature of their observations. The necessity of overlap between recorded populations is not adequately explained, which is puzzling given that the theoretical foundation (as mentioned in the paper) suggests the method should work without overlap. The paper also doesn't sufficiently address whether the predicted noise correlations primarily reflect direct couplings or common input from unobserved neurons.

Clarity issues are present in the estimation details, which are crucial to the method's novelty. The M-step updates presented in the paper (bottom of page 3) are not well-explained or derived, making it difficult to assess their contribution. While these updates might be trivial to experts in latent dynamical systems, their lack of explanation weakens the paper's presentation and perceived novelty. The authors devote considerable space to introduction and discussion but provide insufficient technical detail on the core algorithmic contribution.

The paper would benefit from addressing several key questions: (1) How does the method perform when the linear-Gaussian assumptions are violated, and what alternatives might be considered? (2) Why is overlap between recorded populations necessary for the method to work, and how does this align with the theoretical foundation? (3) To what extent are the predicted noise correlations due to direct couplings versus common input from unobserved neurons?

Arguments for acceptance: The paper presents a novel and valuable approach to a significant problem in neural population analysis. The method is technically sound, well-validated, and has clear potential to impact the field by enabling analysis of larger neural circuits than currently possible. The experimental results are convincing, demonstrating that the method can predict noise correlations with accuracy approaching that of a fully observed model.

Arguments against acceptance: The paper's limitations in addressing the method's applicability beyond linear-Gaussian assumptions and the insufficient explanation of the core algorithmic contribution undermine the perceived novelty and robustness of the approach. The lack of clarity about the necessity of population overlap and the interpretation of noise correlations represents important gaps that need addressing.

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant potential impact, but it requires more thorough treatment of the method's theoretical foundations and limitations to fully convey its contribution. The authors have an excellent idea that is well-executed and validated, but the presentation needs to better convey the significance and novelty of their approach. With appropriate revisions addressing the clarity issues and theoretical limitations, this would be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
