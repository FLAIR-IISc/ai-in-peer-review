

This paper introduces a method to "stitch" together sequential partial recordings of neural populations into a unified dynamical model, enabling inference of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The core idea extends prior work on latent dynamical systems for neural data (e.g., Pillow & Latham, NIPS 2008) by addressing the specific challenge of combining non-overlapping imaging sessions. The authors demonstrate that their linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework successfully predicts noise correlations in mouse somatosensory cortex data, significantly expanding the effective population size beyond simultaneous imaging limits. This represents a meaningful advance in leveraging existing 2-photon imaging capabilities for larger circuits.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Originality (High):** The stitching approach is novel for handling non-simultaneous neural recordings, distinct from prior work on functional connectivity (e.g., Mishchenko et al., NIPS 2011) or sub-sampled inference (e.g., Wohrer et al., NIPS 2010). It directly addresses a key experimental limitation in cortical circuit studies.  
2. **Significance (High):** The method enables characterization of population dynamics in circuits with thousands of neurons (e.g., cortical barrels), where simultaneous imaging is infeasible. Real-data validation in both anesthetized and awake mice shows practical utility for predicting noise correlations beyond the naive approach.  
3. **Quality (Moderate-High):** The synthetic and experimental results are well-structured, with clear upper/lower bounds (fully observed vs. naive models). The method consistently outperforms the naive baseline across population sizes and datasets.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Quality (Moderate):** The reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions is inadequately justified. The paper does not sufficiently address how deviations from this model (e.g., non-linear calcium dynamics, spike-rate dependencies) affect performance. The real-data results show poor correlation between estimated couplings and ground-truth (Fig. 3b), yet the method still predicts noise correlations well—this disconnect needs clarification.  
2. **Clarity (Moderate):** The M-step derivation (p. 3, bottom) is presented without derivation or intuition, undermining the technical contribution. The necessity of population overlap (critical for model identifiability) is not explained mechanistically, leaving readers to infer from the results (e.g., "overlap improves stitching performance" without addressing *why*).  
3. **Quality/Significance (Moderate):** The interpretation of noise correlations as arising from direct pairwise couplings (vs. common inputs) is questionable. The paper does not explore whether latent common-input models (e.g., Vidne et al., J Comput Neurosci 2011) could better explain the observed correlations, potentially overestimating direct connectivity.  

**Pro/Con Summary for Acceptance:**  
*Pro:* Novel, well-motivated approach with strong real-data validation; solves a clear experimental bottleneck; extends prior NIPS work on partial observations.  
*Con:* Over-reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions without robustness analysis; insufficient technical detail on model fitting; ambiguous interpretation of noise correlations.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a significant contribution to neural data analysis but requires substantial revisions to clarify assumptions, model limitations, and technical details. The core idea is compelling, yet the current presentation does not fully convey its novelty or robustness. I recommend **weak acceptance** pending major revisions addressing the clarity and quality concerns outlined above.
