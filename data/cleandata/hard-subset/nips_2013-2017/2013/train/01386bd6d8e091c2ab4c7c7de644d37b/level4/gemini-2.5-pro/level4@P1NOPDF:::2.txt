This paper proposes a method for inferring the properties of a large network by sequentially recording from smaller subnetworks and then stitching together the resulting models. An extension of this method to point-process data would make it applicable to many real-world datasets, such as those where a few recording channels are repositioned daily. The approach appears novel; to my knowledge, there is little similar work (Pillow and Latham, "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons," seems the closest). This is a powerful and promising idea.

However, several points require clarification. First, it is unclear to what extent the method's performance is limited to data that adheres closely to the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper does not provide a clear intuition for how the model fitting might degrade when this assumption is violated. In the case where a single sub-model is sufficient to estimate the full model (up to a linear transformation, as the authors note), it is also unclear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets, compared to simply collecting more data from the initial subset alone.

The experiments on real data raise another question: why is an overlap between the recorded populations necessary for the model to work? This requirement suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture all the complexities of the real data. The paper would be strengthened by providing some intuition for how this population overlap helps to constrain the model.

Furthermore, in the experiments on real data where the linearity assumption is likely violated, it is worth considering how much of the well-fit noise correlations are attributable to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings. This is particularly relevant given that the inferred couplings themselves show little correlation with the true couplings (Figure 3b). It seems plausible that these noise correlations could be better modeled by latent variables representing common input, rather than by direct pairwise couplings.

Finally, while the authors devote considerable space to the introduction and discussion, the paper is light on the details of the estimation procedure. For example, the equation for the M-step at the bottom of page 3 is presented as a key contribution but is neither explained nor derived. While this may be a standard result for experts in Linear Dynamical Systems, its presentation without derivation makes it difficult for a non-expert to assess the paper's novelty.

In summary, the authors present a powerful idea and test it against real data. However, the paper needs to explain the methodology more clearly and explore the model's limitations more thoroughly to properly convey the significance of the results. As it stands, some key details remain unclear.
