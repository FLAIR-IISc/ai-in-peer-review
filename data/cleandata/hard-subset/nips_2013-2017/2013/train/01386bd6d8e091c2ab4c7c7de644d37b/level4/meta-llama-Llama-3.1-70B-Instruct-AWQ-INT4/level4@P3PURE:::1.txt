The paper presents a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons, thereby expanding the population sizes for which population dynamics can be characterized beyond the limitations imposed by the number of simultaneously imaged neurons. This approach provides a more comprehensive understanding of the circuit, which cannot be imaged in its entirety due to experimental and technical constraints. The authors apply their method to both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data in mice, demonstrating its ability to estimate correlations in variability across two populations that were not imaged simultaneously.

The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a simple linear Gaussian system. The model is defined by four parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimuli receptive fields' matrix that models the stimulus dependence of the population activity, and two covariance matrices for neural and measurement noise. The parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard EM algorithm.

Quality: 
The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping populations of neurons, but when applied to real data, the populations must have a certain level of overlap to achieve reasonable results. It is clear that the greater the overlap, the better the results are. However, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated data example. Furthermore, the authors do not address the significant difference in correlation observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) and real data (Fig. 3b). The model's strength lies in its ability to recover pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously measured neuron pairs.

Clarity: 
The paper is well-written, although the figure legends could be more descriptive, and the text related to the figures in the body of the paper could provide more elaboration. This would facilitate a better understanding of the results.

Originality: 
The authors acknowledge several works that have addressed the question of inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data or electrophysiological measurements. However, these works do not infer functional connections of non-simultaneously imaged neurons, highlighting the novelty of the proposed approach.

Significance: 
There are fundamental limits to the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged, which restricts our ability to reveal the nature of neural computation at the level of the circuit (or more than a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method for expanding these limited capabilities.

Overall, this is a good model paper that utilizes a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture measures that are not easily retrievable otherwise.
