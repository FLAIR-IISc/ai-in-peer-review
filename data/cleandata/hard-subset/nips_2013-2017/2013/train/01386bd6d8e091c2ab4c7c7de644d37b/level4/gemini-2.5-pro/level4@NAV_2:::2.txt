This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population by "stitching" together multiple, sequential recordings from smaller, partially overlapping subpopulations. The central idea is to formulate the problem as inference in a latent dynamical system—specifically, a linear-Gaussian model—where neurons not present in a given recording session are treated as missing data. This allows for the estimation of a single, unified model describing the entire population. The authors first demonstrate the method on simulated data, showing it can recover the underlying connectivity matrix and predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons. They then apply the technique to real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, again showing successful prediction of correlations across separately recorded neuron pairs. The problem of combining non-simultaneous recordings is highly relevant, and this approach appears novel compared to prior work that has focused on inferring connectivity from single, simultaneous recordings or handling more structured patterns of missing data.

**Strengths / Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper tackles a critical and practical limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously. The proposed "stitching" framework is an elegant and novel solution to this problem. If robust, this method could be widely applicable to many datasets, including multi-day electrophysiology experiments where recording channels are often moved.
*   **Quality:** The method is validated on both synthetic and real in-vivo neural data, lending significant credibility to the approach. The use of "fully observed" and "naive" models provides clear upper and lower bounds on performance, making the evaluation compelling.
*   **Clarity:** The core concept is explained well, and the results are presented clearly, particularly in Figures 2, 3, and 4, which effectively demonstrate the method's superiority over the naive baseline.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

*   **Clarity of Methodological Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the estimation procedure. The M-step update equations are provided without derivation or substantial explanation. It is unclear to the non-expert reader whether this is a standard result for LDS with missing observations or a novel contribution. To properly assess the paper's technical novelty (part of the **Originality** and **Quality** criteria), the authors should either cite the standard derivation or provide one if the result is new.
*   **Robustness and Model Assumptions:** The work relies on a linear-Gaussian model, a strong simplification of true neural dynamics. The manuscript would be strengthened by a discussion of how violations of this assumption might affect performance. This concern is highlighted by the finding that overlapping populations were required for the method to work on real data, but not on the simulated data. Providing some intuition as to why this overlap is critical in the non-ideal, real-world case would help the reader understand the practical limitations of the model.
*   **Interpretation of Inferred Structure:** The results demonstrate strong performance in predicting noise correlations (Fig. 3c), but weaker performance in recovering the underlying coupling parameters themselves (Fig. 3b). This suggests the model might be capturing shared variability via effective pairwise couplings that could, in reality, be due to unobserved common inputs. The paper would be more complete if it discussed this possibility and considered whether a model with explicit low-dimensional latent variables might explain the data more parsimoniously.
*   **Justification for the Stitching Approach:** The authors state that a single recording can identify the system up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions. The paper could provide a clearer intuition for how the second recording session resolves this ambiguity. A brief discussion on the information gained by adding a second recording session versus simply collecting more data from the first subset would help solidify the significance of the proposed technique.
