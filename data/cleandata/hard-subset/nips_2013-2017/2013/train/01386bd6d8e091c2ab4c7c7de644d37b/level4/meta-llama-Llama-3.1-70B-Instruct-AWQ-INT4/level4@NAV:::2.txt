This paper proposes a novel approach to inferring network properties by sequentially recording from subnetworks and combining the resulting sub-models. The extension of this method to point-process data holds promise for its application to various datasets where a subset of channels is modified daily. While the idea appears powerful, its originality is underscored by the lack of similar existing work, with the exception of a study by J. W. Pillow and P. Latham.

However, the practical limitations of this method in handling data that deviates from linear-Gaussian assumptions are unclear, and the paper does not provide sufficient intuition on how the fitting process might break down under such deviations. Furthermore, in scenarios where a full model can be estimated from a single subset, it is uncertain how much additional information is gained from incorporating other subsets, as opposed to using more data from the initial subset.

The requirement of overlap between the two populations for the model to work on real data raises questions, particularly given that this condition is not necessary for the linear-Gaussian simulated data. It would be beneficial to have a clearer understanding of how the overlap affects the model's performance.

In the real-data experiments, where the linearity assumption is likely violated, it is also unclear how much of the explained noise correlations can be attributed to common input versus direct pairwise couplings. Latent variables may provide a better model for noise correlations than pairwise couplings.

While the authors dedicate considerable space to the introduction and discussion, the estimation details are lacking. The equation for the M step on page 3 is presented as a key contribution but is not adequately explained or derived. Although this may be a trivial result for LDS experts, it diminishes the novelty of the paper. The authors' idea is promising, but it requires clearer explanation to convey its significance effectively.

Overall, the paper presents a clever idea tested on real data, but some aspects of the methodology and results require further clarification.
