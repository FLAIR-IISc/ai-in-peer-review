

This paper presents a method for inferring global network properties by sequentially recording from subnetworks and stitching the resulting sub-models. The approach extends to point-process data, making it applicable to datasets where only a few channels change daily. I am unaware of similar work (Pillow & Latham, 2008 comes closest), and the idea appears powerful.  

However, it is unclear how much the method is limited to data close to linear-Gaussian assumptions, and the paper provides little intuition on how deviations from these assumptions might cause fitting to fail. Specifically, if the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of latent dimensions, as stated by the authors), it is unclear what additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets versus simply using more data from one subset.  

Another concern is why the model on real data requires population overlap between subsets. This suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture real-world constraints, yet the paper lacks intuition on how overlap improves model identification.  

In real-data experiments—where the linearity assumption likely breaks—the explained noise correlations (well-fitted, as in Figure 3b) may stem from common input rather than direct pairwise couplings, as the couplings themselves show poor correlation with true couplings. It would be preferable to model noise correlations via latent variables instead of pairwise couplings.  

The paper dedicates excessive space to the introduction and discussion while being overly brief on estimation details. The M-step equation on page 3 is presented as the main contribution but lacks derivation or explanation. If this is trivial for linear dynamical systems experts, it undermines the paper's novelty. The core idea is strong, but the significance is not adequately conveyed due to insufficient technical exposition.  

A promising approach tested on real data, but critical details remain unclear.
