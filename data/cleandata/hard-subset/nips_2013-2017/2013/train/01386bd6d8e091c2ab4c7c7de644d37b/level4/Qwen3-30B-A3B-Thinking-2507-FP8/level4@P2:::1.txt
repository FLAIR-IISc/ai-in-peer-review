

This paper addresses a critical experimental limitation in neural circuit analysis: the inability to simultaneously image thousands of neurons due to technical constraints, despite circuits often involving such population sizes. The authors propose a statistical "stitching" method that combines sequentially recorded partial neural populations into a unified latent dynamical system (LDS) model, enabling prediction of noise correlations between neurons never recorded together. The core innovation lies in modeling the problem as a latent LDS with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian framework to infer functional couplings and noise correlations from non-overlapping imaging sessions. The method is validated on both synthetic data (demonstrating accurate recovery of coupling matrices and noise correlations) and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex (awake/anesthetized), showing significant improvements over naive baselines.

**Quality**  
The method demonstrates strong technical soundness in simulation, accurately recovering cross-couplings (correlation=0.73) and noise correlations (correlation=0.92) between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. However, the real-data application requires a minimum overlap between imaging sessions (e.g., 60% overlap for reliable stitching), while the synthetic example (Fig 2a) used artificial overlap without explicit justification. The authors should clarify why overlap was unnecessary in simulation but critical in practice, as this gap affects reproducibility. Additionally, the correlation performance gap between simulation (Fig 2c: r=0.73) and real data (Fig 3b: r=0.38) for non-simultaneous pairs warrants deeper analysis—e.g., due to biological noise, nonlinearity in calcium dynamics, or model oversimplification. The EM-based parameter estimation is well-executed, and the use of "fully observed" and "naive" baselines provides a rigorous benchmark.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a strong narrative flow from problem statement to solution. However, figure legends lack descriptive detail (e.g., Fig 2a’s "top middle" label is ambiguous), and the text does not sufficiently elaborate on *why* the stitching method succeeds (e.g., the role of overlap in resolving latent variables). Minor improvements to figure captions would enhance reproducibility.

**Originality**  
The work squarely addresses a gap in prior literature. While numerous studies infer functional connectivity from *simultaneous* recordings (e.g., [6,7,8,9,10,11]), none handle *non-simultaneous* neuron pairs. Related work on sub-sampled data ([12,13,14,15]) either assumes monotonic stimulus-correlation relationships (violating neural reality) or scales poorly to large populations. The authors’ LDS framework uniquely enables stitching across sessions without these constraints, making it a significant conceptual advance for neural circuit analysis.

**Significance**  
This paper tackles a fundamental bottleneck in systems neuroscience: the disconnect between circuit-scale neural computations (thousands of neurons) and current imaging capabilities (hundreds of neurons). By enabling population dynamics characterization beyond simultaneous recording limits, the method opens avenues for studying large-scale cortical circuits (e.g., barrel columns) with existing technology. The practical impact is substantial—applications like predicting noise correlations in sensory processing could inform theories of neural coding and computation. The authors’ emphasis on bridging experimental constraints with algorithmic innovation aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on scalable, impactful methods.

**Pro/Con for Acceptance**  
*Pros*:  
- Solves a concrete, high-impact problem in neuroscience with clear experimental relevance.  
- Theoretical grounding (latent LDS with missing data) is elegant and well-executed.  
- Strong empirical validation on both synthetic and real data, with rigorous baselines.  
- Directly advances the field’s ability to characterize neural circuits beyond current imaging limits.  

*Cons*:  
- Overlap requirement for real data lacks theoretical justification (unlike simulation).  
- Correlation gap between synthetic and real data needs explanation to ensure reliability.  
- The linear Gaussian model, while computationally tractable, may oversimplify neural dynamics (though the authors acknowledge this).  

**Conclusion**  
This is a high-quality, impactful contribution that effectively leverages statistical modeling to overcome experimental constraints in neuroscience. The method is technically sound, the validation is thorough, and the significance for understanding neural circuits is compelling. While minor clarifications on overlap requirements and real-data performance gaps would strengthen the work, these do not undermine the core contribution. The paper exemplifies how algorithmic innovation can extend the reach of existing experimental tools—a hallmark of strong NIPS work. **Recommendation: Accept.**
