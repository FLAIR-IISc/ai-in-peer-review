This paper presents a novel approach to inferring network properties of a large network by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then combining the resulting sub-models. This method can be extended to point-process data, making it applicable to various datasets where a subset of channels is modified daily. Although the authors' work appears to be distinct, with the closest related study being "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons" by J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, its originality and potential impact are notable.

However, it remains unclear how the method's performance is affected by deviations from the linear-Gaussian assumption, particularly in practice. The paper does not provide sufficient intuition regarding how the fitting process might break down under various scenarios. In the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset, up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, it is uncertain how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets, as opposed to using more data from a single subset.

Another point of concern is the requirement for overlap between the two populations for the model to work on real data. This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulated data does not fully capture the underlying dynamics, and it would be beneficial to have some intuition about how the overlap affects the model. In the experiments on real data, where the linearity assumption likely breaks, it is also questionable how much of the explained noise correlations are due to common input rather than direct pairwise couplings. It is possible that noise correlations could be better modeled using latent variables rather than pairwise couplings.

The authors dedicate a substantial amount of space to the introduction and discussion but provide limited details about the estimation process. The equation for the M-step on the bottom of page 3 is presented as the primary contribution but lacks a clear explanation or derivation. Although this result may be trivial for experts in linear dynamical systems, it diminishes the novelty of the paper. The authors have a promising idea, but it needs to be better explained to convey the significance of the result.

The paper presents an innovative idea tested on real data, but some details require clarification.
