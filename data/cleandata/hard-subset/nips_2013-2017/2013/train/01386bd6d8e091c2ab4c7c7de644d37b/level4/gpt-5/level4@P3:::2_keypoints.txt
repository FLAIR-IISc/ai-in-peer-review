- Core idea: Fit a single latent linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations using known session-specific neuron selection matrices and learn parameters by EM/online EM.  
- Main empirical claim: The stitched model accurately predicts noise correlations between neuron pairs never recorded simultaneously and approaches fully simultaneous-model performance while outperforming per-session baselines.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on latent dynamical models and connectivity inference literature, differing by practical stitching of non-simultaneous, partially overlapping data to recover joint dynamics.  
- Novelty: Practical, scalable procedure for predicting cross-session noise correlations from partial overlap is the main novel contribution.  
- Soundness: Modeling as an LDS with session-specific observation matrices is appropriate and principled; evaluation uses synthetic and real-data bounds.  
- Evidence strength: Convincing prediction of noise correlations on synthetic and real datasets, large improvement over naive baselines.  
- Limitation in A recovery: Method poorly recovers true coupling matrix A on real data (low off-diagonal block correlations), so inferred A should be interpreted as functional rather than anatomical.  
- Missing methodological details: M-step updates (R and d) lack derivation, treatment of session effects, initialization, regularization, stability constraints, and convergence discussion.  
- Unanalyzed assumptions/failure modes: No adequate analysis of when linear-Gaussian approximation holds or how nonlinear spike–calcium transforms, non-Gaussian noise, or nonstationarity affect identifiability and prediction.  
- Clarity issues: Key algorithmic details are underspecified, especially full EM derivation, aggregated sufficient statistics, and computational complexity.  
- Session-specific parameter ambiguity: Unclear whether baselines and noise levels (d and R) are per-neuron or session-specific and how they are handled.  
- Identifiability explanation needed: Overlapping neurons anchor latent coordinates and resolve similarity-transform ambiguity; this should be clarified.  
- Initialization/regularization/stability: Missing descriptions of initialization strategy, regularizers (e.g., sparsity on A), and stationarity/stability checks.  
- Empirical robustness gaps: No systematic simulations varying overlap size or model-mismatch tests to quantify required overlap and robustness.  
- Stimulus vs noise disentangling: Paper should report how much predicted correlations derive from stimulus drive versus internally generated variability.  
- Session nonstationarity concerns: Needs to address baseline drift, neuropil contamination, session-specific scaling and propose session-specific d and R if needed.  
- Regularization suggestion: Consider and evaluate simple priors (e.g., sparsity on A) as mentioned but not demonstrated.  
- Calibration of claims: Emphasize validated outcome is correlation prediction and avoid over-interpreting inferred A as connectivity on real data.  
- Suggested additional analyses: Provide EM derivations, overlap-sensitivity simulations, robustness tests with spike-to-calcium generative models, and comparisons including low-dimensional latent inputs.  
- Arguments for acceptance: Addresses important experimental scenario, is simple and scalable, and shows strong empirical results for cross-session noise-correlation prediction.  
- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient methodological detail and reproducibility, limited analysis of method limits and model mismatch, unquantified dependence on overlap, and weak coupling recovery on real data.  
- Overall verdict: Promising and timely idea with encouraging results but requires stronger methods exposition, identifiability/overlap analysis, and robustness experiments to warrant acceptance.
