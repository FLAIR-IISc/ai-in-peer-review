This paper presents a statistical method for "stitching" together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. This approach allows for the characterization of population dynamics on a larger scale than is possible with simultaneous imaging, thereby providing a more complete view of neural circuits that cannot be captured in a single experiment due to technical limitations. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice. The authors demonstrate that their model can estimate correlations in activity between two neural populations that were not recorded simultaneously.

The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system to data with missing observations, specifically employing a linear Gaussian state-space model. The model is defined by four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a receptive field matrix that captures stimulus-driven activity, and covariance matrices for both the latent neural dynamics and the measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from the data using a variant of the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**
The model performs well on simulated data with completely non-overlapping neural populations. However, when applied to real data, a certain degree of overlap between populations is required for reasonable performance. While it is intuitive that greater overlap leads to better results, the paper does not explain why this overlap was unnecessary for the simulated data.

Furthermore, the authors do not address the source of the significant discrepancy in the magnitude of the estimated correlations between the simulated results (Fig. 2c) and the experimental results (Fig. 3b).

A key strength of the model is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons that were never recorded at the same time.

**Clarity**
The paper is generally well-written. However, the clarity could be improved, as the figure legends are not sufficiently descriptive, and the corresponding text in the main body does not provide enough detail to fully interpret the figures.

**Originality**
The authors acknowledge prior work on inferring functional connectivity from two-photon imaging and electrophysiological data. However, they correctly point out that these previous methods do not address the challenge of inferring connections between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, which is the primary contribution of this work.

**Significance**
Fundamental technical limitations restrict the number of neurons that can be recorded simultaneously. This, in turn, constrains our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level, beyond populations of a few hundred cells. This paper proposes a valuable method to help overcome this limitation.

**Overall**
Overall, this is a strong paper. It applies a relatively simple linear dynamical model to effectively capture metrics, such as cross-population correlations, that are otherwise difficult or impossible to obtain.
