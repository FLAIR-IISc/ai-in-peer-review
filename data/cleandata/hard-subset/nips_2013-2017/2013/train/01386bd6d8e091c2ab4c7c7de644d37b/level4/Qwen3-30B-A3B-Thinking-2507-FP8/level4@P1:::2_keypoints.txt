- Proposes a principled "stitching" method using latent dynamical systems with missing observations to infer joint dynamics from sequentially recorded neural subsets.  
- Extends prior theoretical work on reconstructing systems from partial observations to the problem of combining non-overlapping imaging sessions, avoiding assumptions or scalability limits of some earlier methods.  
- Strong validation on simulations and real 2-photon data; stitching predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneous pairs with r=0.84 versus 0.15 for a naive baseline, approaching the fully observed model.  
- Method clearly outperforms the naive zero-coupling baseline and addresses an important experimental bottleneck for large-scale circuit analysis.  
- Theoretical motivation via identifiability properties of linear dynamical systems (Section 3.1) is persuasive.  
- Relies on linear-Gaussian assumptions that are not thoroughly examined; lacks concrete examples of failure under strong nonlinearity or realistic calcium dynamics and uses weak stimulus drive in simulations.  
- Empirically observed need for substantial session overlap (e.g., 60%) is not theoretically justified; the paper should discuss identifiability conditions and why overlap removes latent transformation ambiguity.  
- EM/M-step estimation details are presented without derivation or intuition, and treatment of session-specific C^k matrices needs more explanation for reproducibility; claims of easy extension to common-input models are underdeveloped.  
- Interpretation of predicted noise correlations is unclear: high noise-correlation recovery (r=0.84) vs modest coupling recovery (r=0.38) suggests latent common inputs may drive correlations, a distinction the paper does not explore.  
- Pro: novel, practically significant method with solid real-data results and clear baselines. Con: under-discussed model limitations, weak theoretical grounding for overlap, and thin estimation details.  
- Recommendation: weak accept pending minor revisions to clarify failure modes under nonlinearity, provide theoretical grounding for overlap requirements, and expand estimation/derivation details.
