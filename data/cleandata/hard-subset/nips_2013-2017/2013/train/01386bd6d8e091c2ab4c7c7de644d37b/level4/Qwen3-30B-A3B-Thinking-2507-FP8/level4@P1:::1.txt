

This paper presents a novel statistical approach for "stitching" together sequentially recorded neural population data to infer joint dynamics across non-simultaneously imaged neurons. The authors address a critical experimental limitation: while 2-photon calcium imaging enables recording of hundreds of neurons, cortical circuits involve thousands, making simultaneous imaging infeasible. Their method frames the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, allowing prediction of noise correlations between neuron pairs never recorded together. The approach is validated on both simulated neural populations and in vivo calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, demonstrating successful recovery of cross-population correlations. This work builds on prior connectivity inference methods [6-11] but uniquely tackles the challenge of non-simultaneous recordings, distinguishing it from related work on sub-sampled observations [12-15] which either require restrictive assumptions (e.g., monotonic noise-stimulus relationships [12]) or lack scalability [13].

**Quality**  
The paper demonstrates strong technical execution. The latent dynamical system model (LDS) is well-motivated and effectively applied to both synthetic and real data. The method successfully predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons in simulated data (Fig. 2b, correlation=0.92) and real mouse cortex data (Fig. 3d, correlation=0.84), significantly outperforming a naive baseline that cannot model such correlations. A key strength is the clear theoretical justification for why stitching works: the LDS parameters can be uniquely identified when sufficient overlap exists between imaging sessions (Fig. 3a-b). However, the paper does not adequately address a critical discrepancy: why correlation recovery is substantially stronger in simulation (Fig. 2c, r=0.73 for cross-population couplings) than in real data (Fig. 3b, r=0.38), despite similar methodological approaches. Additionally, while the simulation requires no overlap (Fig. 2a), real-data results necessitate overlap (Fig. 4a), and the authors do not explain this difference. The model’s reliance on linear Gaussian assumptions is acknowledged (Section 4), and the authors appropriately note that more complex models [6, 16] could improve accuracy, though this remains for future work.

**Clarity**  
The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to method, validation, and significance. The technical exposition of the LDS model (Section 2.1) is precise and accessible. However, some figure legends lack sufficient detail (e.g., Fig. 2c’s legend does not specify the correlation metric), and the manuscript could better highlight key results in the text (e.g., explicitly stating the 0.84 correlation in Fig. 3d when discussing real-data performance).

**Originality**  
The core contribution—using a latent dynamical system to stitch non-simultaneous recordings—is novel. The authors thoroughly contextualize their work against prior studies on functional connectivity inference [6-11] and sub-sampled observation methods [12-15]. Crucially, they identify a gap: existing methods cannot handle non-simultaneous recordings without restrictive assumptions (e.g., [12]’s monotonicity requirement), whereas their approach avoids such constraints. This represents a meaningful advance over recent NIPS work on neural data analysis [6, 7, 13].

**Significance**  
This work addresses a fundamental bottleneck in systems neuroscience: the inability to image entire neural circuits due to technical limits. By enabling characterization of dynamics beyond the simultaneous imaging capacity (e.g., from hundreds to thousands of neurons), the method expands the scope of circuit-level studies. The demonstration on mouse somatosensory cortex—where large-scale dynamics are thought to underlie whisker processing—highlights direct applicability to key neuroscience questions. The approach could accelerate data-driven modeling of cortical circuits, potentially enabling studies of previously inaccessible scales (e.g., entire barrel columns), as noted in the discussion. While the linear LDS is a simplification, its success in predicting correlations suggests practical utility for experimental design.

**Overall Assessment**  
This is a high-quality, well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to bridging experimental constraints with computational neuroscience. The method is technically sound, validated rigorously, and addresses a genuine need in the field. The primary limitation—requiring sufficient overlap between imaging sessions (Fig. 4a)—is acknowledged and contextualized, though the simulation-real data discrepancy warrants further investigation. The paper aligns perfectly with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful methods for complex data.

**Pros**: Novel approach to non-simultaneous neural recording; strong empirical validation; clear theoretical foundation; high practical significance for neuroscience.  
**Cons**: Unexplained discrepancy in correlation recovery between simulation and real data; linear model may oversimplify neural dynamics; overlap requirement limits applicability to sparse recording scenarios.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The work is technically robust, addresses a critical problem with a clear solution, and offers immediate utility for experimental neuroscientists. While the linear model is a simplification, its effectiveness in predicting correlations demonstrates tangible value, and the authors appropriately suggest extensions for future work. This paper advances the state of the art in neural data analysis and aligns well with NIPS’ mission.
