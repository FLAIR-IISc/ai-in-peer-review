This paper proposes a method to infer the properties of a large network by sequentially recording from smaller subnetworks and "stitching" the resulting sub-models together. An extension of this approach to point-process data could be highly applicable to neurophysiological datasets where a subset of recording channels is typically moved daily. To my knowledge, there is little prior work in this area (J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons," seems the closest), and the core idea appears powerful.

Despite its promise, several aspects of the method are unclear. A key concern is the method's reliance on the linear-Gaussian assumption. The paper does not offer a clear intuition for how the fitting procedure might degrade as the data deviates from this assumption. In the extreme case where the full model could be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions), it is unclear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating subsequent subsets, as opposed to simply collecting more data from the initial subset.

Another question arises from the experiments on real data, where the model requires an overlap between the observed populations to succeed. This requirement suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the complexities of the real data. The paper would be strengthened by an explanation or intuition for why this overlap is necessary and how it helps constrain the model.

Furthermore, in the experiments on real data, where the linearity assumption is likely violated, it is questionable whether the well-fit noise correlations are due to direct pairwise couplings or to unobserved common input. While the model fits the noise correlations well, the inferred couplings themselves show little correlation with the ground-truth couplings (as seen in Figure 3b). It is plausible that a model incorporating latent variables would better capture these noise correlations than one based solely on pairwise couplings.

The presentation also has significant weaknesses. The authors devote considerable space to the introduction and discussion but provide sparse details on the estimation procedure. For instance, the M-step update equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as a key contribution, yet it is neither derived nor explained. If this is a standard result for experts in Linear Dynamical Systems, the novelty of the contribution is diminished; if it is not, a derivation is necessary for the reader to appreciate its significance. The authors present a compelling idea, but the lack of methodological detail prevents a full assessment of its impact.

In summary, the paper presents an interesting idea tested against real data, but its significance is obscured by a lack of clarity on key details and unaddressed questions about its underlying assumptions.
