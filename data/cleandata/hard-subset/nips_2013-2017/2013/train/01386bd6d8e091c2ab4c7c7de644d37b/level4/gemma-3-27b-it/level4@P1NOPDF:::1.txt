The paper presents a statistical method for “stitching” together sequentially imaged sets of neurons. This approach expands the population sizes available for characterizing population dynamics, going beyond the limitations of simultaneously imaged neurons, and provides a more comprehensive view of circuits that cannot be imaged as a whole due to experimental and technical constraints. The method is demonstrated using both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, showing the ability to estimate correlations in variability across populations that were not imaged concurrently.

The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, employing a simple linear Gaussian system. The model incorporates four parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive fields matrix that models stimulus-dependent population activity, and two covariance matrices representing neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data using a variant of the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**

The model performs well on simulated data with two non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, when applied to real data, reasonable results require a certain degree of overlap between the imaged populations. While it’s understandable that greater overlap yields better results, the paper doesn’t explain why overlap wasn’t necessary in the simulation example. 

Furthermore, the authors do not address the substantial difference in the magnitude of correlations observed when applying the model to simulated data (Fig. 2c) versus real data (Fig. 3b).

The strength of the model lies in its ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons that were not measured simultaneously.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written, but the figure legends lack sufficient detail, and the text referencing the figures doesn’t elaborate enough on their content. More descriptive legends and expanded discussion of the figure results would enhance understanding.

**Originality**

The authors acknowledge related work addressing functional connectivity inference from 2-photon imaging data and electrophysiological measurements. However, these existing methods do not infer functional connections between non-simultaneously imaged neurons, highlighting the novelty of this work.

**Significance**

Fundamental limits on the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged restrict our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level (involving more than a few hundred cells). This paper proposes a method for extending these limited capabilities, offering a valuable tool for circuit analysis.

Overall, this is a strong methodological paper that utilizes a relatively simple linear dynamics model to capture information that would otherwise be difficult to obtain.
