

This paper demonstrates that network properties of large networks can be inferred by sequentially recording from subnetworks and stitching the resulting sub-models. When extended to point-process data, this approach could be applied to datasets where only a few channels are observed daily. To the best of my knowledge, no prior work (e.g., J. W. Pillow and P. Latham, "Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons") comes as close to this idea, making it a potentially powerful contribution.  

However, it remains unclear how broadly applicable this method is beyond linear-Gaussian assumptions. The paper does not provide sufficient intuition for how deviations from these assumptions might degrade performance. Specifically, when the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of latent dimensions), it is unclear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating other subsets versus simply using more data from one subset.  

Another concern is why the model only works on real data when there is overlap between the two populations. This suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture real-world constraints, yet the paper lacks intuition on how overlap better constrains the model.  

In the real-data experiments—where linearity likely breaks—the observed fit of noise correlations (Figure 3b) may not reflect true coupling structure, as the fitted couplings show poor correlation with ground-truth couplings. It is plausible that noise correlations arise from common input rather than direct pairwise couplings, which could be better modeled with latent variables.  

The authors devote significant space to the introduction and discussion but provide unusually light detail on the estimation procedure. The M-step equation on page 3 is presented as the main contribution yet lacks derivation or explanation. While this may be trivial to LDS experts, its absence undermines the paper’s novelty.  

Overall, the core idea is promising and tested on real data, but key details remain unclear. The significance of the method requires better exposition to be fully appreciated.
