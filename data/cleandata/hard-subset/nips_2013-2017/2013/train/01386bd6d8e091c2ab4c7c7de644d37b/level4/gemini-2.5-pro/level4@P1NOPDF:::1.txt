This paper presents a statistical method to "stitch" together datasets from sequentially recorded populations of neurons. This approach allows for the characterization of population dynamics on a larger scale than is possible with simultaneous imaging, offering a more complete view of neural circuits that cannot be fully captured due to technical and experimental limitations. The method is validated on both simulated data and experimental calcium imaging data from mice. The authors demonstrate that their model can estimate correlations in neural variability between two populations that were not recorded simultaneously.

The authors frame the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, for which they use a simple linear Gaussian model. The model is defined by four parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a matrix of stimulus receptive fields (modeling the stimulus-dependence of the population activity), and two covariance matrices for the neural and measurement noise, respectively. These parameters are estimated from the data using a variant of the standard Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

**Quality**

The model performs well on simulated data featuring two non-overlapping neural populations. However, when applied to real data, the authors note that a certain degree of overlap between the recorded populations is necessary to achieve reasonable results. While it is intuitive that greater overlap would yield better performance, it is unclear why overlap was not necessary in the simulated data example. The authors should clarify this point.

Additionally, the paper does not address the source of the large discrepancy in the estimated correlation values between the simulated data (Fig. 2c) and the real data (Fig. 3b).

A key strength of the model is its ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously.

**Clarity**

The paper is generally well-written. However, the figure legends are not sufficiently descriptive, and the corresponding explanations in the main text do not always elaborate enough on the figures, which could hinder reader comprehension.

**Originality**

The authors cite several prior works that have addressed the inference of functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging or electrophysiological data. However, a key distinction of the present work is that these prior methods do not infer functional connections between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

**Significance**

Fundamental technical limitations restrict the number of neurons that can be simultaneously imaged. This, in turn, constrains our ability to understand neural computation at the circuit level. This paper proposes a valuable method for helping to overcome these limitations.

**Overall**

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a useful method. By applying a relatively simple linear dynamical model, the authors are able to capture important circuit-level metrics that are otherwise difficult or impossible to obtain.
