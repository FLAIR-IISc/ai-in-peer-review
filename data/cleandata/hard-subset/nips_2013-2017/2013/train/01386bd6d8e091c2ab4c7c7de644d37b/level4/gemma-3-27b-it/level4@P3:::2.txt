This paper presents a novel statistical method for “stitching” together sequential recordings of partially observed neural populations to infer the dynamics and interactions of larger circuits. The core idea – phrasing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations – is compelling and addresses a significant practical limitation in current neural recording technologies. While simultaneous recordings are valuable, they often capture only a small fraction of the neurons comprising a functionally relevant circuit. This work demonstrates the potential to substantially expand the size of populations for which dynamics can be characterized, and successfully predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons in somatosensory cortex. 

The approach builds upon established techniques in latent dynamical systems modeling, but applies them to a unique problem. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first work to directly address inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons in this manner. While Pillow & Latham (2008) explored neural characterization with partial observations, their focus was distinct, and did not address stitching together separate recordings. Related work focusing on functional connectivity inference [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11] generally does not address the scenario of non-simultaneous recordings, and other methods addressing subsampled observations [12, 13, 14, 15] appear less directly applicable due to differing assumptions or scalability limitations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The proposed method addresses a crucial problem in neuroscience – inferring circuit dynamics from incomplete data – and offers a novel solution.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The approach is grounded in a well-established statistical framework (latent dynamical systems), providing a solid theoretical basis.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The method is demonstrated on both simulated and real calcium imaging data, demonstrating its practical applicability. The results on real data, showing accurate prediction of noise correlations, are particularly encouraging.
*   **Potential Impact:** The ability to infer dynamics from larger, stitched populations has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of neural circuit function.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Linear-Gaussian Assumption:** The reliance on a linear-Gaussian dynamical system model is a limitation. While acknowledged by the authors, the paper doesn’t adequately address *how* deviations from this assumption might affect performance and what strategies could be employed to mitigate these effects. A more detailed discussion of model robustness would be valuable.
*   **Information Gain from Additional Subsets:** The paper doesn’t fully clarify the information gained by adding additional, non-overlapping subsets of neurons, particularly in scenarios where the full model could be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation). A more explicit discussion of this point would strengthen the argument.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The necessity of overlap between recorded populations in the experimental data is surprising and unexplained. The authors acknowledge this hints at a limitation of the linear-Gaussian assumption, but a deeper investigation into *why* overlap is required and how it constrains the model would be beneficial.
*   **Interpretation of Noise Correlations:** The authors correctly raise the possibility that observed noise correlations might be driven by common input rather than direct pairwise couplings. The paper could benefit from a discussion of how to distinguish between these possibilities and whether the model could be extended to incorporate latent common inputs.
*   **Limited Estimation Details:** The explanation of the estimation procedure, particularly the M-step equation, is somewhat terse. While it may be standard for experts in the field, a more detailed explanation would increase accessibility and allow for a clearer assessment of the method’s novelty.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and potentially impactful contribution. The method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and demonstrated on relevant data. However, the limitations related to the linear-Gaussian assumption, the overlap requirement, and the lack of detail in the estimation procedure weaken the overall impact. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroscience.
*   Presents a novel and theoretically grounded solution.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.
*   Has the potential to enable research on larger and more complex neural circuits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a linear-Gaussian assumption limits the method's applicability.
*   The unexplained overlap requirement in real data raises concerns about model limitations.
*   The lack of detail regarding the estimation procedure hinders thorough evaluation.



**Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of the limitations of the linear-Gaussian assumption and potential strategies for addressing deviations.
2.  Provide a clearer explanation of the information gained by adding additional, non-overlapping subsets of neurons.
3.  Investigate the reason for the overlap requirement in the experimental data and discuss its implications for model interpretation.
4.  Provide a more detailed explanation of the estimation procedure, particularly the M-step equation.
