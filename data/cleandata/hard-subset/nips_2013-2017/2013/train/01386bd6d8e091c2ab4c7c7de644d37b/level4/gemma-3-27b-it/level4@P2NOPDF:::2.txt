This paper presents a novel method for inferring the properties of large networks by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then integrating the resulting sub-models. This approach holds significant promise for applications involving dynamic, partially observed datasets – particularly those, like many neurophysiological recordings, where only a subset of channels are available at any given time. I am unaware of closely related work; Pillow & Latham (2013) represents the closest parallel, but this paper’s sequential stitching approach feels notably powerful.

However, the practical limitations of this method, particularly its sensitivity to deviations from linear-Gaussian assumptions, are not thoroughly explored. The paper lacks a clear articulation of *how* and *why* the fitting process might degrade under more complex data distributions. Specifically, if the full model can be adequately estimated from a single subset (potentially only up to a linear transformation of the latent space, as the authors acknowledge), the incremental benefit of adding data from additional subsets becomes unclear. It’s not evident why incorporating more subnetworks would yield substantially more information about the coupling matrix than simply collecting more data from an initial, representative subset. 

Furthermore, the observation that successful model fitting on real data requires overlap between recorded populations raises important questions. While the authors rightly point out that this suggests the linear-Gaussian simulations do not fully capture the complexity of real-world data, a more detailed discussion of the role of overlap in constraining the model would be valuable. What specific aspects of network structure or dynamics are better revealed through overlapping recordings?

When applied to real data – where the linearity assumption is likely violated – the interpretation of the fitted noise correlations is also concerning. The paper demonstrates good fits to observed noise correlations, but shows a limited correspondence between fitted couplings and true (or independently estimated) couplings (Figure 3b). This raises the possibility that the explained noise correlations may be primarily driven by common input rather than direct, pairwise interactions. A model incorporating latent variables representing common drive might offer a more parsimonious and biologically plausible explanation for these correlations. 

A significant weakness lies in the paper's treatment of the estimation details. While the equation for the M-step (page 3) is presented as a key contribution, it receives minimal explanation or derivation. Although it may be straightforward for experts in latent dynamical systems, this lack of clarity hinders broader understanding and potentially diminishes the perceived novelty of the work. The paper spends considerable space on the introduction and discussion, but would benefit from a more detailed exposition of the core algorithmic contribution.

In summary, the authors have presented a promising and intuitively appealing idea. However, the lack of clarity regarding the method’s limitations, the role of data overlap, and the details of the estimation procedure – particularly the M-step – prevent a fully positive assessment. A more thorough exploration of these issues, alongside a more rigorous justification of the core contribution, would significantly strengthen the paper.
