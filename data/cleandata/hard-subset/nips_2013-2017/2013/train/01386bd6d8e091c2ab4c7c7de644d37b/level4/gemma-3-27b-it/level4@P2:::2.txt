This paper introduces a method for inferring the dynamics of large neural populations by “stitching” together data from sequentially recorded subnetworks. This is achieved by framing the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The potential impact of this work is significant, particularly for datasets where recording from the entire population simultaneously is impractical – a common scenario in many electrophysiological and calcium imaging experiments. While I am not immediately aware of closely related work (Pillow & Latham, 2008, on neural characterization in partially observed populations comes closest), the core idea of combining sequential recordings to infer larger-scale network properties is novel and promising.

The method’s reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions raises concerns about its robustness to deviations from these assumptions commonly found in neural data. The paper doesn’t fully address how performance degrades with increasing non-linearity or non-Gaussianity, and a more detailed discussion of these limitations would be valuable. Furthermore, when a complete model *can* be estimated from a single subset of neurons (up to a linear transformation, as the authors acknowledge), it's unclear what additional information is gained by incorporating data from other subsets – would simply increasing the data volume from the initial subset be more effective?

The requirement for overlap between the recorded populations in the experimental results is also puzzling. While the authors note this suggests the linear-Gaussian simulation doesn’t fully capture the complexity of real data, a more in-depth explanation of the role of overlap in constraining the model would be beneficial. Additionally, in the analysis of real data, it is difficult to disentangle the contribution of direct pairwise couplings from shared input when interpreting the reconstructed noise correlations; a latent variable model accounting for common input may be a more appropriate framework.

The paper’s strength lies in the core idea, but its weakness is the lack of detailed explanation regarding the method’s implementation. While the equation presented for the M-step is highlighted, it lacks sufficient context or derivation. Although it may be standard for experts in latent dynamical systems, providing a more accessible explanation would enhance the paper’s clarity and strengthen the perceived novelty of the work. The introduction and discussion sections are extensive, but the core methodological details could benefit from greater elaboration.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel approach to combining sequential recordings to infer large-scale network dynamics.
*   Demonstration of successful application to real calcium imaging data.
*   Potential to address a significant limitation in current methods for studying large neural populations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of the method’s sensitivity to deviations from linear-Gaussian assumptions.
*   Lack of clarity regarding the benefits of incorporating multiple subsets beyond what could be achieved with larger single-subset datasets.
*   Insufficient explanation of the role of population overlap in the experimental results.
*   Limited methodological detail, particularly surrounding the M-step calculation.



**Overall assessment:** This is a promising paper with a novel idea that is validated with experimental results. However, several points regarding the method's limitations, justifications and implementation require further clarification. A major revision addressing these points would substantially improve the paper and make a stronger case for its acceptance.



**Reference:**

Pillow, J. W., & Latham, P. E. (2008). Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons. *Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems*, *20*, 1–8.
