This paper introduces a statistical approach for integrating neuronal activity data acquired sequentially, effectively increasing the population size available for studying circuit dynamics beyond the constraints of simultaneous imaging. This addresses a common challenge in neuroscience, where experimental and technical limitations prevent the observation of entire circuits at once. The method is demonstrated using both simulated and experimental calcium imaging data from mice, successfully estimating correlations in variability between neuronal populations that were not imaged concurrently.

The authors frame the problem as estimating the parameters of a latent dynamical system with missing data, utilizing a linear Gaussian system with four key parameters: a functional coupling matrix, a stimulus receptive field matrix, and two covariance matrices representing neural and measurement noise. These parameters are estimated from experimental data via a modified Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

Quality:

The model performs well on simulated data with non-overlapping neuronal populations. However, application to real data necessitates a substantial degree of overlap between imaged populations to achieve reliable results. While the benefit of increased overlap is readily apparent, the reason why overlap wasn’t required in the simulations remains unexplained. Furthermore, the paper doesn’t sufficiently address the substantial discrepancy in correlation magnitudes observed between the simulated data (Fig. 2c) and the experimental data (Fig. 3b). Despite this, the ability to recover pairwise correlations between neurons not measured simultaneously is a significant strength of the model.

Clarity:

The paper is generally well-written, but the figure legends lack sufficient detail, and the accompanying text does not fully elaborate on the information presented in the figures.

Originality:

The authors acknowledge related work in functional connectivity inference from 2-photon imaging and electrophysiological data. However, these existing approaches do not focus on inferring functional connections between neurons imaged at different times – a key distinction of this work.

Significance:

The limited capacity for simultaneous neuronal recording represents a fundamental constraint in circuit neuroscience. This paper offers a promising method to circumvent this limitation, potentially expanding our ability to investigate neural computation at the circuit level, involving more than a few hundred cells. 

Overall, this is a solid methodological paper that leverages a relatively straightforward linear dynamics model to capture information that would otherwise be difficult to obtain.
