This paper proposes inferring network properties of a large system by sequentially recording from subnetworks and then stitching the resulting submodels. If extended to point-process data, this could be applied to many datasets where a few channels are typically moved each day. I am not aware of closely related work beyond Pillow and Latham (“Neural characterization in partially observed populations of spiking neurons”), and the idea seems powerful.

That said, it is unclear how tightly the method depends on data being close to linear-Gaussian. The paper does not provide much intuition for how fitting might fail when assumptions are violated. In the extreme case where the full model can be estimated from a single subset (up to a linear transformation of the latent dimensions, as the authors note), it is not clear how much additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by adding other subsets, as opposed to simply collecting more data from one subset.

Another concern arises from the real-data results: why does the method only work when there is some overlap between the two populations? This suggests that the linear-Gaussian simulations do not capture all relevant structure. It would be helpful to provide at least qualitative intuition for how overlap helps constrain the model.

In the real-data experiments—where the linearity assumption likely breaks—it is also important to disentangle the sources of explained noise correlations. While the noise correlations are fit well, the inferred couplings appear to correlate poorly with the true couplings (Fig. 3b). How much of the explained covariance is due to common input rather than direct, pairwise couplings? My expectation is that latent variables would model shared variability more effectively than pairwise couplings alone.

The exposition is unbalanced: there is substantial space devoted to the introduction and discussion, but too few details on estimation. The M-step equation at the bottom of page 3 is presented as the main contribution, yet it is neither explained nor derived. If this result is trivial to experts in linear dynamical systems (LDS), that would substantially diminish the paper’s novelty; if not, the paper needs to clearly articulate and justify it. In either case, more detail is required to convey the significance and limitations of the approach.

Overall, this is a neat idea tested on real data, but several important details remain unclear.
