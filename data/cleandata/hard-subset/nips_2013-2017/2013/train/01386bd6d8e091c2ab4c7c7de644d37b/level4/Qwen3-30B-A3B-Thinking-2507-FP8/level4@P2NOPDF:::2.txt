

This paper proposes a method for inferring large network properties by sequentially sampling subnetworks, fitting local models, and stitching them together. The approach extends naturally to point-process data, offering potential utility for datasets with sparse channel activity (e.g., a few channels active daily). To the best of my knowledge, this work is novel, with Pillow & Latham (2008) representing the closest prior effort. The core idea is compelling and potentially powerful for large-scale network analysis.

However, several critical concerns regarding the method's scope, interpretation, and technical execution require clarification. First, the paper’s applicability appears heavily constrained by the linear-Gaussian assumption. The analysis offers little intuition about how deviations from this assumption (e.g., non-linearities, non-Gaussian noise) would impact performance or cause model breakdown. In the extreme case where a single subnetwork suffices to estimate the full model (up to latent dimension permutation), it remains unclear what additional information about the coupling matrix is gained by incorporating non-overlapping subsets versus simply increasing the size of one subset. The marginal utility of adding non-overlapping data is not justified.

A second concern arises from the real-data experiments: the model only succeeds when the two sampled populations overlap. This contradicts the simulated linear-Gaussian results, suggesting the synthetic data fails to capture key aspects of real-world dynamics. The paper lacks any discussion of *why* overlap is necessary—what specific constraints does it impose on the model? An intuitive explanation of how overlap resolves identifiability issues would strengthen the argument.

Furthermore, in the real-data analysis (where linearity likely fails), the model explains noise correlations well (Figure 3b), yet the estimated couplings show negligible correlation with ground-truth couplings. This raises a fundamental question: are the explained noise correlations primarily driven by common input (which could be modeled via latent variables) rather than direct pairwise couplings? The paper does not address this ambiguity, potentially overstating the interpretability of the inferred couplings.

The most significant issue is the insufficient technical detail. The authors dedicate substantial space to the introduction and discussion but provide minimal exposition of the core estimation algorithm. The M-step equation on page 3 is presented as a key contribution yet lacks derivation, explanation, or justification. If this result is trivial to experts in linear dynamical systems (LDS), it substantially undermines the paper’s novelty. Conversely, if it is non-trivial, the omission of derivation severely hinders evaluation. The methodological gap here is a major barrier to assessing the paper’s technical contribution.

In summary, the paper presents a promising and novel conceptual framework with compelling real-data motivation. However, the lack of clarity on the method’s limitations beyond linear-Gaussian assumptions, the unexplained necessity of population overlap, the questionable interpretation of noise correlations, and the insufficient technical exposition of the core algorithm significantly weaken the submission. The idea has merit, but the current presentation fails to convincingly establish its significance or robustness. Addressing these gaps—particularly through rigorous analysis of model breakdown, a clear explanation of the M-step, and deeper discussion of real-data constraints—would substantially improve the work. As it stands, the technical contribution remains under-justified.
