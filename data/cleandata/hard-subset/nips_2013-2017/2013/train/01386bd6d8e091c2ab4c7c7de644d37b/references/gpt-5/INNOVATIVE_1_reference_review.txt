Summary
This paper tackles an important and difficult problem: reconstructing the joint dynamics and noise correlations of a large neural population from multiple partial recordings that are not acquired simultaneously. The authors formulate the problem as a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations and session-specific selection matrices, and fit it by EM (with an online variant for efficiency). They demonstrate on simulations and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1) that “stitching” partial sessions can accurately recover cross-session noise correlations and enable prediction of unobserved neurons’ activity, approaching the performance of a model trained on fully observed data while substantially outperforming a naive baseline (separate per-session LDSs merged post hoc). They also discuss identifiability intuitions and practical aspects such as the need for some overlap across sessions.

Relation to prior work
The approach sits at the intersection of system identification with missing data and population latent variable models. Prior work infers functional connectivity from calcium/ephys data or uses latent GLMs/PLDS/FA/GPFA, but typically assumes simultaneous recordings or does not address cross-session coupling. Wohrer et al. predict noise correlations across sessions under strong monotonicity assumptions; other sub-sampling and dendritic connectivity works address different regimes. Conceptually, the paper extends classic observability/identifiability intuitions to the neuroscientific setting and operationalizes them in a practical tool for calcium imaging. Methodologically, the LDS + EM machinery is standard; the main novelty is the multi-session stitching framing and its empirical validation for predicting non-simultaneous correlations.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea with high potential impact: using partially overlapping recordings to infer joint dynamics and noise correlations that were never observed simultaneously.
- Technically sound and simple: LDS with known measurement matrices per session; EM with Kalman smoothing works naturally with missing entries; scalable via online EM.
- Convincing simulated study showing recovery of cross-block couplings and noise correlations, and strong prediction of unobserved neurons.
- Careful benchmarking on real data via a “fictional imaging” split provides an upper bound (“fully observed”) and a lower bound (“naive”), showing the stitched model is close to the attainable upper bound, particularly for noise correlations and cross-prediction in the awake dataset.
- Practical insights: overlap across sessions is helpful/necessary in practice; performance trends with population size; discussion of experimental design tradeoffs.

Weaknesses and questions
- Reliance on overlap: While the simulation shows some recovery without overlap, the real-data analysis reports that no-overlap splits “failed” and that substantial overlap (c=60%) was needed. This is a key practical limitation; please quantify performance vs. overlap and provide theoretical conditions under which zero-overlap stitching is identifiable (e.g., when stimuli, dynamics, and noise are sufficient to align subspaces).
- Model mismatch: Calcium signals are not linear-Gaussian observations of spiking, and Q is assumed diagonal. The authors acknowledge this and suggest extensions, but it would strengthen the case to include comparisons with more expressive latent models (e.g., PLDS, GPFA, linear-Gaussian with low-rank latent common input) in the same stitching framework.
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is very weak. Include stronger baselines such as: (i) shared factor analysis with session-specific loadings and Procrustes/CCA alignment; (ii) PLDS/GPFA with session-specific observation matrices; (iii) subspace system identification approaches that explicitly handle missing data.
- Validation design: The real-data evidence is based on artificially splitting a single simultaneous session. This isolates the stitching algorithm but sidesteps inter-session nonstationarities (behavioral state, adaptation, drift) that would arise across days/fields of view. Please assess robustness to realistic inter-session changes (e.g., additive/linear drifts, stimulus changes, slow dynamics changes).
- Assumptions and hyperparameters: Important details (regularization, handling of R/Q constraints, convergence criteria, sensitivity to T and K) are sparse. Also, the stimulus drive is weak in the simulation; how does stronger stimulus drive or differing stimuli across sessions affect identifiability and performance?
- Cell identity: The stitching relies on known neuron identities in overlap. In many practical scenarios, fields of view are non-overlapping across days. Can the approach be combined with latent-alignment techniques to relax the need for explicit overlap?

Quality
The methodology is correct and implemented appropriately for LDSs with missing observations. Claims are mostly supported by simulations and semi-realistic analyses. The lack of stronger baselines and the reliance on artificial splits limit the strength of the empirical validation, but the key claim—recovering noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs—is demonstrated convincingly within the chosen setup.

Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well organized. The modeling assumptions and EM updates are described concisely. It would help to expand the discussion of identifiability (necessary/sufficient conditions, role of overlap and stimulus), provide algorithmic details (initialization, mini-batch sizes), and add an explicit performance-vs-overlap plot.

Originality
The core idea of stitching non-simultaneous population recordings to infer joint dynamics/noise correlations is novel and important for current experimental constraints. The modeling machinery is standard, but the combination and problem framing are original and valuable.

Significance
High. If robustified, this approach could materially expand the effective population size accessible in 2p imaging and related modalities, enabling network-level analyses that current hardware cannot support. The results already show practical promise and motivate follow-up work on better models and experimental design.

Pros
- Addresses a timely, high-impact problem with a simple, scalable method.
- Strong conceptual contribution with clear neuroscientific utility.
- Solid results on simulations and real data, especially for noise correlations and cross-prediction.
- Thoughtful discussion of overlap and experimental design.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on overlap; zero-overlap performance unclear/weak on real data.
- Limited baselines; no comparison to modern latent models/alignment methods.
- Real-data validation uses artificial splits, not true multi-session recordings with drift/state changes.
- Assumptions (linear-Gaussian, diagonal Q/R) may limit generality.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling and novel direction that could significantly impact large-scale neural data analysis, and the empirical results are promising despite some limitations. I encourage the authors to add stronger baselines and a systematic analysis of overlap and inter-session nonstationarity in the camera-ready.

Confidence
High (4/5).
