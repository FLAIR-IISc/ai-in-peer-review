- Proposes stitching partial neural recordings using a latent linear dynamical system to infer full population dynamics across non-simultaneous sessions.

- Validated on simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets using a "fictional imaging scenario."

- Addresses an important experimental limitation: inability to image all neurons simultaneously.

- Compares against a fully observed upper-bound and a naive session-averaging baseline.

- Demonstrates accurate prediction of correlations between neuron pairs never recorded simultaneously, approaching the fully observed model.

- Clear problem statement, strong motivation, and well-organized presentation.

- Core model is a linear Gaussian LDS, which is an overly simplistic approximation of neural dynamics.

- No comparison to more biologically plausible models (e.g., calcium dynamics or spiking models).

- Lacks sensitivity analysis quantifying how much session overlap is required and how performance degrades with less overlap.

- Limited theoretical justification tying system identification guarantees to properties of neural population dynamics.

- Overstates relevance to the ML community since LDS methods are well-established; main novelty is application to neuroscience.

- Insufficient discussion of limitations: nonlinear dynamics, scalability to thousands of neurons, differing recording states (awake vs anesthetized), and robustness to measurement/identity errors.

- Ambiguous definition of "noise correlations" and how the model captures trial-to-trial variability.

- Suggested improvements: expand theoretical analysis, add overlap sensitivity experiments, compare to realistic neural models, and clarify noise-correlation definition.

- Also suggested: discuss computational complexity, provide results on larger populations, and include a dedicated limitations/failure-cases section.

- Overall assessment: well-executed and valuable for neuroscience but limited ML methodological novelty; borderline recommendation.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions contingent on sensitivity analysis, stronger theoretical grounding, and clearer ML significance.

- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a genuine neuroscience problem, solid synthetic and real-data validation, practical utility, good baselines, and clear presentation.

- Arguments against acceptance: limited methodological novelty, simplistic model, weak theoretical support, and inadequate discussion of boundaries and ML impact.

- Final stance: Accept with minor revisions to strengthen theory, sensitivity analyses, and limitations discussion.
