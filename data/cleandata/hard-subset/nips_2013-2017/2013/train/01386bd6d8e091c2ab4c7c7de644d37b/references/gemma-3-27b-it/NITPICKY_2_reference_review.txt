This paper presents a method for “stitching” together calcium imaging data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics and correlations. The core idea – leveraging overlapping observations to reconstruct a more complete picture of network activity – is intuitively appealing and addresses a practical limitation of current imaging technology. The authors frame this as a latent dynamical system estimation problem, employing a linear Gaussian model for tractability. They demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on both simulated and real data, showing that it can accurately predict noise correlations even between neurons never simultaneously recorded.

While the concept is promising and the results are presented clearly, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor – yet cumulatively significant – issues that detract from its overall impact and raise concerns about the rigor of the analysis. 

**Strengths:**

* **Relevant Problem:** The problem addressed – inferring connectivity and dynamics from incomplete neural recordings – is highly relevant to the field of computational neuroscience.
* **Clear Explanation:** The core method is presented in a reasonably clear and concise manner, making the central idea accessible.
* **Validation on Real Data:** Applying the method to real calcium imaging data adds credibility to the findings.
* **Demonstrated Performance:** The simulation results, and the comparison to a naive approach, demonstrate the potential of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplification:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant limitation. While acknowledged by the authors, the impact of this simplification is not fully explored. The authors state this model “does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade of neural couplings, calcium dynamics, fluorescence measurements and imaging noise” yet proceed as if this simplification doesn’t fundamentally alter the conclusions. A more thorough investigation of the robustness of the method to deviations from these assumptions is needed.
* **Parameter Choices:** The choice of diagonal covariance matrix *Q* in the state-space model is a strong assumption. The paper mentions the possibility of correlated noise but does not explore this option, potentially limiting the model’s ability to capture real neural dynamics. Justification for this simplification is lacking.
* **EM Algorithm Details:** The description of the EM algorithm implementation is brief. Details regarding convergence criteria, initialization strategies, and handling of potential local optima are missing. The use of a “variant of online-EM with randomly selected mini-batches” is mentioned but not sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.
* **Simulated Data:** The simulation setup, while clearly described, feels somewhat contrived. The specific coupling strengths and network structure are not justified in relation to biological plausibility. The truncation of weights at ±0.2 seems arbitrary.
* **Experimental Data Analysis:** The “fictional imaging scenario” created for the experimental data is a questionable approach. Pretending that simultaneously recorded neurons were not simultaneously recorded feels artificial and might introduce biases.  The 60% overlap requirement is also arbitrarily chosen and not well-motivated.  The statement regarding performance failing without any overlapping neurons is concerning – this should be a key discussion point, not a footnote.
* **Overly Strong Claims:** The paper occasionally makes overly strong claims, such as “accurately recover the noise-correlations.” The reported correlations, while good, are not perfect. A more nuanced discussion of the limitations and potential sources of error is warranted.
* **Figure 2 caption:** The caption for Figure 2 refers to “Figure 2a, top left,” then “Figure 2a, top middle,” and then “Figure 2c,” introducing inconsistency.
* **Inconsistent terminology:** "noise-correlations" is used interchangeably with "correlations in variability", which could be more precise.
* **Missing citations:** The discussion of reconstructing anatomical connectivity in the introduction ([15]) would benefit from a more recent citation of methods using sparse deconvolution or similar techniques for this purpose.
* **Typos/Grammar:** Several minor typos and grammatical errors were observed throughout the manuscript (e.g., “lets us identify” should be “allows us to identify”).

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses an important practical problem.
* Demonstrates a potentially useful method for combining incomplete data.
* Provides validation on both simulated and real data.
* Clear and concise writing style.

**Con:**

* Overly simplistic model with strong assumptions.
* Lack of detail in methodological descriptions.
* Questionable experimental validation approach.
* Overly strong claims and limited discussion of limitations.
* Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the core idea, the numerous shortcomings detailed above lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a promising direction for research, the lack of methodological rigor, the overly simplistic model, and the questionable experimental validation raise significant concerns about the reliability and generalizability of the findings. A substantially revised version, addressing these issues with more thorough analysis, detailed methodology, and more conservative claims, would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper, in its current form, falls short of the quality expected for NIPS.
