### **Review**

**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a novel computational method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the full, unobserved neural population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is then treated as a partial, noisy observation of this shared latent state. Using a modified Expectation-Maximization algorithm, the authors demonstrate that they can learn the parameters of the full system, including the functional couplings between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is validated on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, the authors use a clever "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is artificially split to create a ground-truth for comparison. The results show that this "stitching" method can accurately predict pairwise noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, performing vastly better than a naive approach and approaching the performance of a model trained on the complete, simultaneous data.

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this paper is its outstanding novelty and conceptual boldness. The question it addresses—can we characterize the joint dynamics of a circuit we can only see in pieces?—is fundamental to the future of systems neuroscience, where experimental limitations are a major bottleneck. The paper's affirmative answer, and the elegant method proposed to achieve it, represents a significant new direction. The idea that one can infer the statistical relationship between two variables that are never observed together, by leveraging their shared context with other variables observed across different sessions, is powerful and non-obvious. This is precisely the kind of high-impact, paradigm-shifting research that NIPS should champion.

The experimental validation is another major strength. While testing on genuinely separate recordings would be ideal but difficult to validate, the authors' "fictional imaging scenario" is a thoughtful and effective alternative. It allows them to create a well-defined upper bound (the "fully observed" model) on real data, providing a convincing benchmark for their method's performance. The comparison against a sensible lower bound (the "naive" model) further highlights the substantial contribution of the stitching approach.

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written. The motivation is compelling, the method is described with sufficient detail, and the results are presented in a way that is both intuitive and quantitatively rigorous. The authors are also commendably transparent about the limitations of their approach, particularly the simplifying choice of a linear-Gaussian model.

**Weaknesses**

The main weakness, which the authors acknowledge, is the simplicity of the underlying model. A linear dynamical system is a coarse approximation of the complex, non-linear, and non-Gaussian dynamics of spiking neural circuits. While the fact that such a simple model works so well for predicting correlations is itself an interesting finding, it leaves open the question of how the method would perform in regimes with stronger non-linearities. The model effectively captures second-order statistics, but may fail to capture higher-order features of the joint population activity.

The method's performance appears to depend on having some overlapping neurons between sessions, as noted by the failure to achieve good performance on real data with 50% non-overlapping subsets. While not a fatal flaw, this is a crucial practical detail that limits the "free lunch" aspect of the method. The paper could benefit from a more systematic exploration of how performance degrades as a function of overlap size, as this has direct implications for experimental design.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly significant contribution to an important problem at the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience. The conceptual advance of "stitching" non-simultaneous recordings is a powerful one that is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research, both in developing more sophisticated models (e.g., non-linear or spiking models) and in designing new experimental paradigms that leverage this idea.

While the methodology relies on a standard LDS framework and makes simplifying assumptions, this is a perfect example of a paper where the novelty of the problem formulation and the significance of the result should be prioritized over methodological perfection. The authors demonstrate convincingly that their core idea works, opening a new door for data analysis in a field constrained by measurement technology. This work has the potential to change how experimentalists design and analyze large-scale neural recording experiments.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** Addresses a critical, unsolved problem with a creative and powerful new idea.
2.  **Strong Proof of Concept:** The approach is convincingly validated on both synthetic and real neural data.
3.  **Potential for High Impact:** The method could significantly expand the reach of current experimental techniques and is likely to be widely influential.
4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is well-written, and the authors are upfront about the model's limitations.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Model Simplicity:** The linear-Gaussian assumption is a major simplification of true neural dynamics.
2.  **Unexplored Dependencies:** The method's reliance on overlapping neurons for good performance on real data is an important caveat that is not fully characterized.

In my view, the arguments for acceptance decisively outweigh those against. This is an exciting and forward-looking paper.
