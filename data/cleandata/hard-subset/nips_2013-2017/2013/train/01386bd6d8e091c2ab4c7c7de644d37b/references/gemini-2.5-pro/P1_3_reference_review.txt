### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel statistical method for "stitching" together multiple, partial recordings of a neural circuit to infer the dynamics of the entire population. The central problem addressed is a major limitation in modern neuroscience: while technologies like 2-photon calcium imaging can record from hundreds of neurons, this is often a small fraction of a functional circuit. The authors propose to model the full, unobserved circuit as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is then treated as a partial, noisy observation of this underlying system. By applying an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm across all partial recording sessions, the method estimates a single, coherent model of the full circuit's dynamics, including the coupling matrix (functional connectivity) and noise structure.

The key contribution is the ability to infer functional connectivity and noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were *never recorded simultaneously*. The authors validate their approach compellingly on both synthetic and real data. On simulated data with known ground truth, they demonstrate accurate recovery of the underlying coupling matrix and noise correlations. For real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, they employ a clever "fictional imaging scenario": they take a dataset where all neurons were recorded simultaneously, split it into fictional partial and overlapping sessions, and show that their stitching method can recover correlation structures that are very close to those derived from the full, simultaneously-observed data. They benchmark their method against a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound, showing significant improvements over the naive approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

#### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is sound, applying a well-established framework (LDS with missing data, fit via EM) to a novel and important problem. The claims are well-supported by a thoughtful and rigorous set of experiments.

**Strengths:**
*   The experimental validation is a major strength. The "fictional imaging scenario" is an excellent way to create a pseudo-ground-truth for real data, allowing for a quantitative evaluation of the method's performance. The use of both an upper bound (fully observed model) and a lower bound (naive model) provides a clear and convincing context for the results.
*   The analysis is thorough, evaluating not just the recovery of correlation structures but also the underlying coupling parameters and the ability to predict the activity of unobserved neurons.
*   The authors are careful and honest about the limitations of their model, acknowledging that the LDS is a simplified statistical description and that real neural dynamics are more complex.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The paper states that for real data, the method "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" (i.e., with 50% subsets, as in the simulation) and required overlap (60% subsets). This is a critical detail. The performance of the algorithm seems highly dependent on this overlap, but this dependency is not systematically explored. A quantitative analysis of how performance (e.g., correlation recovery) degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases would be extremely valuable, as this has direct implications for experimental design.
*   The model is a linear Gaussian system, which is a significant simplification of spiking neural dynamics and calcium imaging signals. While the authors acknowledge this, a brief discussion of what specific aspects of neural dynamics might be poorly captured (e.g., non-linear interactions, firing rate saturation) and how this might affect the inferred correlations would strengthen the paper.

#### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.

**Strengths:**
*   The motivation and problem statement are laid out clearly in the introduction. The core intuition for why the method should work is explained well.
*   The methods section is concise but provides sufficient detail on the model structure and the fitting procedure.
*   The figures are clear, well-labeled, and effectively support the main claims of the paper. Figure 2, in particular, provides a very clear demonstration of the method's success on synthetic data.
*   The writing is direct and easy to follow, making a potentially complex topic accessible.

**Weaknesses:**
*   Minor point: The details of the online-EM variant and the criteria for switching to full batch EM could be slightly more specific for full reproducibility (e.g., mini-batch size, learning rate schedule, convergence thresholds).

#### Originality

The work is highly original. While system identification from partial observations is a classic topic, and inferring functional connectivity is a common goal in neuroscience, this paper is the first to put these ideas together in a practical framework for combining non-simultaneous large-scale neural recordings.

**Strengths:**
*   The core idea of inferring the dynamics between never-simultaneously-observed neurons is novel and tackles a problem that other contemporary methods (as reviewed in the introduction) do not address.
*   The paper clearly distinguishes its contribution from related work, such as methods that assume a relationship between stimulus and noise correlations or methods that do not scale to large populations.

#### Significance

The potential impact of this work is very high.

**Strengths:**
*   The method directly addresses a fundamental technological bottleneck in systems neuroscience. If this method proves robust, it could dramatically expand the effective scale of neural recordings, allowing researchers to build circuit-level models from data that is currently achievable experimentally.
*   The work opens up new avenues for experimental design. The trade-off between the number of neurons recorded in one session, the degree of overlap between sessions, and the total number of unique neurons covered is a rich problem that this work brings to the forefront.
*   The results are likely to be of broad interest to both computational neuroscientists (as a new modeling tool) and experimental neuroscientists (as a way to get more out of their data). It advances the state of the art by providing a practical path toward modeling neural circuits on a scale larger than that of a single field of view.

### Arguments for Acceptance:

1.  **High Impact Problem:** The paper tackles a critical and practical problem in neuroscience—overcoming the limited field-of-view of current recording technologies.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Solution:** The application of a latent dynamical system to "stitch" partial observations is a novel and powerful idea in this context.
3.  **Strong and Convincing Validation:** The use of both synthetic data and a well-designed "fictional" experiment on real data provides strong evidence for the method's efficacy. The benchmarking against upper and lower bounds is exemplary.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, making the contribution clear and accessible.

### Arguments Against Acceptance / Points for Improvement:

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The crucial role of overlapping neurons is mentioned but not systematically investigated. Quantifying this relationship is important for the practical application of the method.
2.  **Model Simplicity:** While acknowledged, the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption could be discussed in more depth, particularly concerning potential failure modes in circuits with strong non-linearities.

### Recommendation:

This is an excellent paper with a novel contribution to a significant problem. The methodology is sound, and the validation is rigorous and convincing. The potential impact on both computational and experimental neuroscience is substantial. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and can be addressed through further discussion or future work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
