### Summary

This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in each recording session. The core idea is to model the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS), where the unobserved neurons in any given session are treated as latent variables. The observation model for each session is defined by a selection matrix (`Ck`) that maps the full state space to the subset of observed neurons. By fitting this single LDS model jointly across all partial recording sessions using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, the authors demonstrate that it is possible to recover the functional connectivity and, notably, the noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is validated on both simulated data and on two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, a "fictional imaging scenario" is created by splitting a fully-observed dataset into partial sessions, allowing for a quantitative comparison against a "fully-observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound model. The results show that the "stitching" method significantly outperforms the naive approach and approaches the performance of the fully-observed model in predicting cross-correlations.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Practical Relevance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and pervasive problem in experimental neuroscience. The limited field-of-view of techniques like two-photon microscopy is a major bottleneck for understanding circuit-level computation. This work provides a principled and practical computational tool to overcome this limitation, effectively expanding the virtual field-of-view. The potential impact on how experiments are designed and analyzed is substantial.

2.  **Elegant and Principled Methodology:** The application of a latent state-space model to this problem is elegant and well-motivated. Framing the unobserved neurons as latent states is a natural fit, and the use of the `Ck` matrices to handle different observation sessions is a clean implementation. The intuition provided—that the first session constrains the dynamics of the unobserved neurons up to a linear transformation, which is then resolved by the second session—is clear and builds on established results in system identification.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Evaluation:** The validation strategy is a major strength of the paper. Creating a "fictional imaging scenario" from a fully-observed dataset is an excellent way to benchmark performance on real data in the absence of ground truth. The comparison to both a clear upper bound (the "fully observed" model) and a sensible lower bound (the "naive" model) provides a compelling demonstration of the method's efficacy. The results, particularly the accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs (Fig 3c,d), are impressive.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the methods are described succinctly, and the results are presented logically. The authors do a good job of building intuition before presenting the quantitative results.

### Weaknesses and Questions for the Authors

1.  **Reliance on a Linear-Gaussian Model:** The primary limitation is the choice of an LDS. Neural dynamics are inherently nonlinear, and neural activity (spikes) is non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this and show that the model is sufficient for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), this is a strong assumption. It is unclear how well the inferred `A` matrix reflects underlying synaptic connectivity, or how the model would perform in circuits dominated by nonlinear dynamics. How robust are the correlation predictions to violations of linearity? A brief discussion on potential extensions to more realistic models (e.g., switching LDS, or approximations for latent GLMs) would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Importance of Overlap is Under-explored:** The authors mention that with 50% non-overlapping subsets in the real data, the method "failed to get good performance," necessitating a switch to 60% subsets (i.e., 20% overlap). This is a critical point with significant practical implications for experimental design. The performance of the algorithm likely depends strongly on the amount of overlap, the strength of coupling between subsets, and the recording duration. A more systematic analysis of how performance degrades as overlap decreases would be invaluable. Is there a theoretical minimum overlap required for identifiability, perhaps related to the graph properties of the underlying functional connectivity matrix?

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper successfully demonstrates stitching for two sessions. The discussion mentions generalizing to more, but the computational complexity of the Kalman smoother (a core part of the E-step) scales quadratically or cubically with the size of the *total* population `N`, not just the observed subset `Nk`. For ambitions like mapping an entire cortical column (thousands of neurons), this could become computationally prohibitive. A comment on the practical scaling limits of the current implementation and potential approximations for larger `N` would be welcome.

4.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The LDS model assumes that the system parameters (`A`, `B`, `Q`) are stationary across the different recording sessions. These sessions might be separated by significant time, during which brain state changes, plasticity, or photobleaching could occur. How might non-stationarity between recording blocks affect the inference? The awake dataset, in particular, might violate this assumption.

### Detailed Comments

*   The distinction between "functional" and "anatomical" connectivity is made correctly, but it's worth re-emphasizing that the `A` matrix in an LDS can capture both direct and indirect (polysynaptic) interactions, which may be a feature or a bug depending on the scientific question.
*   In the results, it's noted that correlation prediction is more accurate than parameter recovery. This is expected, as many different parameter settings can give rise to similar covariance structures. It correctly focuses the evaluation on the more robust and arguably more useful predictions (the correlations).
*   The finding that prediction accuracy *increased* with population size (Fig 4b) is interesting. The authors' interpretation seems plausible—more observed neurons provide more information to constrain the state of the unobserved ones. This is an encouraging result regarding the method's potential for scaling up.

### Overall Assessment

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel and important contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. It tackles a significant practical problem with an elegant and well-justified approach. The experimental validation is rigorous and convincing. While the underlying model is a simplification of true neural dynamics, the work serves as a powerful proof-of-concept that can be built upon in future research. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations of the current implementation and opportunities for future work, rather than fundamental flaws. The paper is well-suited for NIPS, as it combines a clear application-driven need with a principled machine learning solution. I recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in analyzing large-scale neural recordings.
*   Presents a novel application of a well-understood statistical model.
*   Evaluation is thorough, creative, and convincing.
*   The work is likely to have a significant impact on both experimentalists and theorists.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The linear-Gaussian assumption is a major simplification of neural dynamics.
*   The sensitivity to key experimental parameters like inter-session overlap is not fully characterized.

**Conclusion:** The arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a high-quality, high-impact paper.
