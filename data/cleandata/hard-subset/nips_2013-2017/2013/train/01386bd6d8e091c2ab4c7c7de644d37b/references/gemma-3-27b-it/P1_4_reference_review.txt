## Peer Review for "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sound method for stitching together partial recordings of neural activity to infer the dynamics of a larger population. The approach, based on a latent dynamical system, is well-motivated by experimental realities and demonstrated effectively through both simulations and analysis of real calcium imaging data. The paper is clearly written and makes a significant contribution to the field of neural data analysis. I recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The authors tackle the common problem in neuroscience of incomplete neural recordings. Despite advances in imaging techniques, capturing the activity of entire neural circuits remains challenging. This paper proposes a statistical framework to combine multiple, spatially separated recordings of the *same* circuit to infer the dynamics of the full population, even for neurons not simultaneously observed. The core idea leverages the assumption that nearby neurons are strongly coupled, allowing information to be gleaned from sequential recordings. 

The paper effectively positions itself within the existing literature, acknowledging related work on functional connectivity inference (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) and methods for dealing with subsampled data ([12, 13, 14, 15]). Critically, it highlights how these existing approaches fall short in addressing the specific challenge of inferring connections *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons. The authors correctly point out the limitations of methods requiring monotonic relationships between stimulus and noise correlations [12] or facing scaling issues with large populations [13]. This clear articulation of novelty is a strength.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The approach is innovative.  Inferring correlations between neurons *never* recorded simultaneously is a significant step towards reconstructing the dynamics of larger circuits from limited data. This has clear implications for understanding neural computation and testing circuit-level hypotheses.
* **Technical Soundness:** The method, based on a latent dynamical system and EM algorithm, is well-established and appropriate for the problem. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the justification for key assumptions (e.g., Gaussian noise) is reasonable.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The paper provides strong empirical support for the method. Demonstrating performance on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex lends credibility to the approach. The careful comparison to upper and lower bounds (fully observed model and naive approach) is particularly valuable.
* **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The methods section is detailed and accessible, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner. Figures are informative and well-labeled.
* **Relevance to NIPS:** The work is highly relevant to NIPS, combining statistical machine learning techniques (dynamical systems, EM algorithm) with a biologically relevant problem.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** While justified for tractability, the linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification of real neural dynamics.  The authors acknowledge this, but it's a limitation worth reiterating. Exploring more complex (e.g., non-linear) models would be a valuable future direction.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The method's performance relies on overlap between the recorded populations. The authors demonstrate this, and it's a practical constraint, but it’s a key limitation. The impact of varying degrees of overlap and strategies for dealing with minimal overlap could be explored further.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention using online-EM for efficiency, the computational cost of fitting the model to large populations remains a potential concern. Scaling the method to truly large circuits (thousands of neurons) could be challenging.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** It would be beneficial to briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to different parameter choices (e.g., initial conditions for EM, noise covariance matrix structure).

**4. Detailed Comments & Suggestions:**

* **Section 2.1:** Consider briefly mentioning how the choice of *Q* being diagonal impacts the model's ability to capture correlated noise sources, as touched upon later.
* **Section 2.2:** Clarify the rationale for truncating coupling weights in the simulation.
* **Section 3.1:** The explanation of why the method works (relating to consistent estimation and identifying linear transformations) is insightful but could be slightly more accessible to readers unfamiliar with latent variable modeling.
* **Figure 3:**  Could the color scales be adjusted for better visual clarity, particularly in the coupling matrices?
* **Discussion:** The discussion could benefit from a brief mention of potential applications beyond inferring correlations, such as decoding neural activity or building more accurate circuit models.

**5. Arguments for and against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses an important and challenging problem in neuroscience.
* The proposed method is novel, technically sound, and well-validated.
* The writing is clear and concise.
* The results are significant and have the potential to impact the field.
* The paper is well-positioned within the relevant literature.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a simplification.
* The method requires overlap between recorded populations.
* The computational cost could be a limitation for very large circuits.

**Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis and deserves acceptance to NIPS.**
