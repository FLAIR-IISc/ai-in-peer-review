

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses an important challenge in neuroscience: how to characterize the joint dynamics of large neural populations when experimental limitations prevent simultaneous recording of all neurons. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit, using a latent dynamical system framework to infer correlations between neurons recorded in separate sessions. The core idea is that strong coupling between neurons allows one to infer dynamics of unobserved neurons from observed ones, with the additional constraint of multiple sessions enabling recovery of the true parametrization.

The paper builds on related work in neural data analysis (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) but distinguishes itself by addressing the specific problem of non-simultaneous recordings. The authors correctly identify that existing methods for sub-sampled observations ([12, 13, 14, 15]) are not applicable to their problem setting, where imaging sessions are long compared to neural dynamics timescales.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper effectively articulates a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience and proposes a novel solution. The example of whisker barrel cortex makes the problem concrete and relevant.

2. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, and their intuition about why the method should work (via the invertible linear transformation of unobserved dimensions) is compelling.

3. **Comprehensive experimental validation**: The paper demonstrates the method's efficacy on both synthetic data (with ground truth) and two real calcium imaging datasets. The comparison to both an upper bound ("fully observed" model) and a lower bound ("naive" approach) is particularly valuable.

4. **Practical significance**: The results show the method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with high accuracy (0.84 correlation with the upper bound on real data), which is a significant advance for experimental neuroscience.

5. **Thorough discussion of limitations**: The authors acknowledge the need for some overlap between recorded populations, the limitations of the linear model, and potential extensions to more complex models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Simplistic model**: The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't thoroughly explore how the method might fail when neural dynamics are highly nonlinear or non-Gaussian, which is common in cortical circuits. The paper would be strengthened by testing the method on more complex synthetic data that better mimics real neural dynamics.

2. **Lack of sensitivity analysis**: The paper states that some overlap between recorded populations is critical for stitching to work but doesn't quantify how performance degrades as overlap decreases. A systematic analysis of the relationship between overlap percentage and prediction accuracy would be valuable for experimental design.

3. **Scalability concerns**: The method is demonstrated on populations of 50-100 neurons, but the authors claim applicability to "thousands of neurons" in the discussion. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the method scales well to larger populations, where computational complexity and potential parameter degeneracy could become significant issues.

4. **Limited discussion of failure modes**: The paper doesn't address scenarios where the method might fail, such as when the coupling between recorded and unrecorded populations is weak, or when neural dynamics change substantially between recording sessions.

5. **Overlooking a key neuroscience context**: The paper doesn't discuss how noise correlations in sensory cortex (which they focus on) relate to computational function. While they mention "noise correlations" extensively, they don't connect this to how such correlations might impact information processing or behavioral performance.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important problem with a novel and well-motivated approach, and the experimental validation is strong. The method has clear potential to impact neuroscience practice.

## Specific suggestions for improvement

1. Include a sensitivity analysis showing how performance degrades as the overlap between recorded populations decreases (e.g., from 60% to 20% overlap).

2. Add a subsection discussing limitations and potential failure modes, including examples where the method might not work well (e.g., weakly coupled populations, non-stationary dynamics).

3. Extend the synthetic data experiments to include more realistic neural dynamics (e.g., nonlinearities, spiking models) to better assess the method's robustness.

4. Discuss the relationship between noise correlations and neural computation more explicitly, connecting the method's output to potential functional implications.

5. Clarify the scalability discussion by including a brief analysis of computational complexity as a function of population size.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS.
