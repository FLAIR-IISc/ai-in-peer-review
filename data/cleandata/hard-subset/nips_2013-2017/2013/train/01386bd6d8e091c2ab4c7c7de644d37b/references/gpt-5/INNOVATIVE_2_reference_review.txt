Summary
The paper tackles a timely and important problem: how to infer joint population dynamics and, crucially, noise correlations between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The authors propose a simple but clever “stitching” framework based on a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Multiple imaging sessions are modeled via known, session-specific selection matrices Ck, and parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned by EM with Kalman smoothing. Intuitively, each session constrains a different view of the same underlying dynamical system; overlap across sessions aligns these views, enabling estimation of cross-couplings and noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs.

On synthetic data with known ground truth, the method recovers off-diagonal couplings and noise correlations for non-overlapping populations and predicts unobserved activity. On real 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1), the authors construct a “fictional” split into two partially overlapping sessions and show that stitched models substantially outperform a naive baseline (separately fit per session) and approach a “fully observed” upper bound fitted to all neurons jointly.

Relation to prior work
The manuscript positions itself against two lines of work: (i) inferring functional connectivity from imaging or electrophysiology (e.g., GLMs, penalized state-space, deconvolution-based methods [6–11]) and (ii) handling sub-sampling/partial observations (latent GLMs [13], readout from partial correlation data [12], penalized state-space for dendritic trees [14], anatomical stitching [15]). Unlike these, the present work explicitly infers correlations between neurons never recorded together by leveraging overlapping sessions within a coherent dynamical model. The approach is consistent with system identification ideas (observability/identifiability up to linear transforms [21, 22]) and common-input modeling [17, 18].

Strengths
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: A simple, principled way to “stitch” partial recordings to recover joint dynamics and noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs. This is a bold and practically important direction.
- Practicality: Uses an LDS with EM/Kalman smoothing, making the approach computationally tractable and easy to implement; uses known selection matrices from identified cells, which aligns with 2-photon practice.
- Empirical validation: Clear synthetic experiments with ground truth; real data evaluation with an informative lower bound (naive) and an upper bound (fully observed) shows convincing gains—especially for predicting noise correlations (a key target).
- Utility: Demonstrates cross-prediction of unobserved activity from observed subsets; suggests the method can guide experimental designs that trade off overlap vs. coverage.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Modeling simplicity: Linear-Gaussian assumptions (diagonal Q and R, direct modeling of fluorescence) are strong; real calcium signals involve nonlinearity, nonstationarity, and correlated noise. The paper notes this but does not quantify robustness to these mismatches.
- Identifiability and overlap: The method seems to require overlap across sessions; performance degrades without overlap. There is no theoretical treatment of required conditions (e.g., collective observability, minimal overlap) or sensitivity to overlap size and quality.
- Evaluation design: The real-data evaluation relies on “fictional” splits of simultaneous data; this avoids real-world issues such as drift, registration errors, and nonstationarity across days. A small analysis of robustness to misidentification in Ck or to time-varying dynamics would strengthen claims.
- Scalability: LDS inference scales cubically in state dimension in general; the paper does not discuss computational scaling to thousands of neurons or strategies (sparsity, low-rank structure, block-diagonality) needed for the ambitious use-cases it envisions.
- Comparisons: The baseline is very weak (naive per-session LDS). It would help to compare against stronger alternatives: e.g., factor analysis/PLDS fit jointly across sessions with shared latent factors plus session-specific loadings, or subspace ID approaches that stitch via shared latent spaces, or explicitly low-rank-plus-sparse models capturing common input.
- Uncertainty estimates: Results focus on point estimates and correlations. Confidence intervals or bootstrap variability for predicted cross-session correlations would increase rigor.
- Some methodological details are thin (initialization, regularization, convergence criteria, hyperparameters, handling of stimulus alignment; how d is estimated per neuron; any constraints on A such as stability).

Questions for the authors
- What are the precise identifiability conditions across multiple sessions? Can you characterize minimal overlap or diversity of stimuli required?
- How sensitive is performance to mis-registered cells (errors in Ck) and to slow drift in dynamics across sessions?
- Can you include experiments with correlated process noise Q and/or low-rank latent inputs to mimic unobserved common drive?
- What is the computational complexity per EM iteration and empirical run times for N≈200 vs. N≈1000? Any use of sparsity/low-rank structure?
- How does performance change when using deconvolved spike trains or a simple calcium forward model instead of fluorescence?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an experiment varying overlap size (including zero) and quantify performance vs. overlap and session count, ideally with synthetic and real data.
- Incorporate structural priors (sparsity on A, stability constraints, low-rank latent inputs) to improve identifiability and scalability; report ablations.
- Test robustness to registration errors and cross-session nonstationarity (e.g., allowing session-specific A with a small regularizer towards a shared A).
- Provide stronger baselines using latent factor models shared across sessions and subspace identification methods.
- Report uncertainty on predicted correlations and cross-prediction scores.
- Release code and, if possible, the split protocols, to facilitate adoption.

Assessment
- Quality: Good. Method is sound for LDS; experiments are convincing, though evaluation could be stronger on real, non-simultaneous data and with stronger baselines.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well organized; some implementation details and theoretical conditions could be elaborated.
- Originality: High. The core idea—stitching non-simultaneous partial recordings to recover cross-session noise correlations—is novel and impactful.
- Significance: High. This is a practically important problem for modern imaging; the approach could shape data collection and analysis pipelines.

Recommendation: Accept (leaning strong)
Despite modeling simplifications and missing theoretical/empirical analyses of overlap and scalability, the paper introduces an original and useful idea with clear empirical benefits. Its conceptual contribution outweighs the limitations, and it will stimulate follow-up work on more realistic observation models, identifiability, and experimental design.
