

This paper addresses a critical experimental bottleneck in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in large cortical circuits. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same circuit (e.g., from different imaging sessions) into a unified model that predicts joint dynamics—including noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The core idea leverages a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) model, where the unobserved neurons in each session form latent states. Using expectation-maximization (EM) with Kalman smoothing, they estimate the coupling matrix and predict correlations across the full population. The method is validated on synthetic data (where ground truth is known) and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex (anesthetized and awake).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Significance & Practical Impact**: The problem is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in current neuroscience experiments (e.g., 2-photon imaging capturing only 10–20% of a circuit). The method directly enables building data-driven models of large circuits, advancing the ability to test theories of neural computation.  
2. **Rigorous Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough comparison against meaningful baselines:  
   - *Upper bound*: "Fully observed" model (trained on all neurons, impossible in practice but used for benchmarking).  
   - *Lower bound*: "Naive" approach (averaging parameters across sessions, ignoring cross-couplings).  
   Results show the stitching method approaches the upper bound (e.g., correlation = 0.84 for noise correlations in real data) and significantly outperforms the naive baseline (0.15).  
3. **Clarity & Reproducibility**: The methodology is clearly described, with sufficient technical detail for replication (e.g., EM updates, data simulation setup). Figures (e.g., Fig. 2, 3) effectively illustrate key results.  
4. **Originality**: While LDS models exist in related work (e.g., [13]), this paper uniquely applies them to stitch *non-simultaneous* recordings without restrictive assumptions (unlike [12], which requires monotonic noise-stimulus correlations). The focus on noise correlations—critical for understanding circuit function—distinguishes it from prior connectivity inference work [6,7,8,9,10,11].  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Model Simplicity**: The linear Gaussian LDS is a strong simplification for neural dynamics (which are nonlinear and non-Gaussian). While the authors acknowledge this ([16,6]), they do not explore whether minor nonlinear extensions (e.g., adding a simple nonlinearity to the dynamics) would improve performance on real data. The synthetic data uses linear dynamics, potentially overestimating the method’s applicability to real circuits.  
2. **Scalability Ambiguity**: The method is tested on populations of ≤80 neurons. The discussion notes that performance degrades with larger populations ("correlation drops with increasing population size"), but the paper lacks a concrete analysis of scalability limits (e.g., how the method would perform on 1,000+ neurons, which is the target application).  
3. **Fictional Data Scenario**: The real-data validation uses an artificial "fictional imaging scenario" (splitting simultaneous recordings into two sessions with 60% overlap). While necessary for benchmarking, this assumes experimental design choices (e.g., 60% overlap) that may not reflect typical practice. A discussion of how the method performs with *minimal* overlap (e.g., 20%) would strengthen the practical relevance.  
4. **Limited Comparison to Modern Methods**: The paper cites recent neural decoding work (e.g., [16,6]), but does not compare against state-of-the-art methods for handling partial observations (e.g., deep generative models [25] or graph neural networks [26]). This weakens the significance claim.  

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
1. **Address Model Limitations**: Add a brief analysis comparing the linear LDS to a simple nonlinear variant (e.g., using a ReLU activation in the dynamics) on real data. This would clarify whether the method’s success relies on linearity or generalizes to more complex dynamics.  
2. **Expand Scalability Discussion**: Include a small experiment testing the method on a larger synthetic population (e.g., 500 neurons) or cite existing work on LDS scalability (e.g., [27]). Explicitly state the practical upper limit of the method (e.g., "effective for populations <100 neurons without significant performance loss").  
3. **Refine Real-Data Validation**: Discuss how the method would perform with *no* overlap between sessions (e.g., 0% shared neurons), as this is a more realistic experimental constraint. If performance degrades sharply, note this as a key limitation for future work.  
4. **Update Related Work**: Compare the approach to recent deep learning methods for neural data (e.g., [25,26]) to better position the contribution.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a high-quality, impactful paper that makes a clear contribution to the neuroscience community. The method is technically sound, well-evaluated, and addresses a significant practical problem. The weaknesses—primarily the linear model assumption and scalability gaps—are important but not fundamental flaws. The authors have already acknowledged the model’s simplicity and proposed extensions (e.g., sparsifying priors [6]), so minor revisions would substantially strengthen the paper. The significance is clear: this approach enables researchers to extract more information from existing datasets, accelerating circuit-level understanding.  

**Recommendation:** **Accept** (with minor revisions to address the points above). The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically rigorous (Quality), well-written (Clarity), novel in its application (Originality), and addresses a meaningful problem with broad relevance (Significance). The method’s potential to transform how neural circuits are studied makes it a strong fit for NIPS.
