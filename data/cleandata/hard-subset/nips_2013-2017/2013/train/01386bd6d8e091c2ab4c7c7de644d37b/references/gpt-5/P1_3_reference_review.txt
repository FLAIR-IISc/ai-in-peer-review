Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics, in particular noise correlations and functional couplings, across neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The authors propose “stitching” together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit by fitting a single linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Each session observes a subset of neurons via a known selection matrix Ck; EM with Kalman smoothing is used to fit shared parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) across sessions. The model operates directly on calcium fluorescence traces, includes a stimulus term u with receptive fields B, and assumes diagonal process and observation noise covariances.

On synthetic data, the method recovers the coupling matrix A reasonably well (including off-diagonal blocks corresponding to non-simultaneous pairs) and predicts noise correlations and unobserved activity with high accuracy, outperforming a naive baseline that fits separate LDSs per session and merges parameters. On two real 2-photon datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse barrel cortex), the authors create a “fictional imaging” scenario by splitting simultaneously acquired data into two overlapping subsets and show that the stitched model can predict pairwise correlations between non-simultaneous pairs close to those from a “fully observed” model fit on all neurons, and substantially better than the naive baseline. They note that some overlap in neurons across subsets was important for performance.

This work connects to prior neural connectivity inference from calcium imaging or spikes (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al., Pillow et al.), and to partially observed/latent dynamical systems (Pillow & Latham; Macke et al.). The key differentiator is inferring cross-session correlations between neurons never imaged together, without strong assumptions relating stimulus and noise correlations (cf. Wohrer et al.). Methodologically, the approach leverages standard LDS/EM machinery but applies it in a multi-view, missing-data setting with known selection matrices.

Quality
- Soundness: The modeling and inference are standard and appropriate for the stated goal. The synthetic experiments are carefully constructed and demonstrate recovery of A and noise correlations; the “fictional” real-data splits are a reasonable first validation.
- Limitations: (1) The real-data evaluation does not demonstrate stitching across truly different sessions/days; instead it splits one session, which keeps many confounds (state, drift, alignment) fixed. (2) The method appears to require overlap in neurons across sessions; the authors report poor performance without overlap but do not quantify how much overlap is needed. (3) Identifiability and conditions for successful stitching are only qualitatively discussed; no formal analysis is provided. (4) The model assumes linear dynamics, diagonal Q and R, and stationarity across sessions; robustness to model mismatch (e.g., correlated observation noise, calcium dynamics, state changes) is not evaluated. (5) Computational scaling and runtime are not discussed; the cost of smoothing in an N-dimensional LDS could be prohibitive for thousands of neurons.
- Experimental care: Train/test splits are described for simulations; for real data, more explicit cross-validation details would help. The naive baseline is weak; stronger baselines (e.g., shared-factor models across sessions, GPFA/PLDS with shared loading matrices, Gaussian graphical model with imputation, multi-view subspace ID) would strengthen claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a transparent presentation of the LDS and EM. A few issues: Equation (2) notation is confusing (“ykt = Ck(xkt + d+ t) t ∼ N(0,R)”) and should be clarified as ykt = Ck xkt + Ck d + εkt, εkt ~ N(0,R). Provide details of the stimulus regressors used in the real datasets, constraints to ensure stability of A, online-EM minibatch scheme, and runtime. There is an orphaned footnote and minor typographical issues.

Originality
The core model and inference are not novel, but the problem setting—stitching multiple partial recordings to infer non-simultaneous correlations—and the empirical demonstration on realistic calcium-imaging data are novel and timely. The paper clearly differentiates from prior work that either assumes monotonicity between stimulus and noise correlations or cannot scale.

Significance
Practically significant for systems neuroscience: if validated across real multi-session recordings, this approach could substantially increase the effective coverage of population analyses without simultaneous imaging. For the NeurIPS audience, the contribution is a meaningful application of latent variable models to an important multiview/missing-data setting. Broader impact would be boosted by stronger baselines, robustness analyses, and results on truly separate sessions.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and difficult problem; clear potential utility for neuroscience.
- Conceptually simple, leveraging well-understood LDS/EM machinery; reproducible in principle.
- Strong synthetic results; real-data validations suggest promising performance on predicting cross-pair correlations.
- Honest discussion of overlap importance and potential extensions.

Cons
- Real-data validation uses artificial splits from the same session; no demonstration across days/conditions.
- Overlap across sessions appears necessary; lack of systematic analysis of overlap, T, and K.
- Limited baselines and no theory for identifiability/requirements.
- Modeling assumptions (linear, diagonal Q/R, stationarity) and scalability not stress-tested.
- Some clarity/notation issues.

Questions for authors
1) How much overlap (fraction and which neurons) is needed for reliable stitching? Can you provide performance vs. overlap curves? 2) Can you demonstrate stitching across truly separate imaging sessions with partial overlap and potentially different noise levels or state? 3) How robust is performance to correlated observation noise and to adding low-rank latent inputs (never observed neurons)? 4) What constraints are imposed on A (e.g., spectral radius) to ensure stability? 5) What is the computational complexity and runtime for N≈200 vs. N>1000? 6) Can you compare to a shared-factor model (e.g., FA/GPFA/PLDS with shared loadings across sessions), or to subspace identification methods adapted to multi-view data?

Suggestions
- Add performance analyses as functions of overlap, session length, number of sessions, and observation noise.
- Include stronger baselines (shared low-rank models; multi-view system ID; graphical model imputation).
- Incorporate regularization on A (sparsity, stability penalties, low-rank-plus-sparse) and report its effect; you mention unreported improvements.
- Evaluate robustness to model mismatch (correlated R, calcium dynamics) and to session nonstationarities.
- Clarify the measurement model, stimulus design in real data, and provide pseudo-code and runtime.

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The problem is important and the results are promising, but the empirical validation would be substantially stronger with demonstrations across truly separate sessions and analyses of overlap/robustness, and with stronger baselines. If these additions are feasible, the paper would merit clear acceptance.
