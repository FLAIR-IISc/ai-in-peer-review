- Paper presents a method to stitch multiple partial recordings to infer joint neural population dynamics using a linear Gaussian latent dynamical system.  
- Method enables prediction of correlations, especially noise correlations, between neurons not recorded simultaneously.  
- Demonstrated via simulations and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.  
- Situates the work within prior literature on functional connectivity and subsampled observations, highlighting novelty in inferring across non-simultaneous recordings.  
- Clearly differentiates from prior methods that require strong stimulus–correlation relationships or different data regimes.  
- Strength: Addresses an important experimental limitation by enabling inference of larger circuits.  
- Strength: Latent dynamical system (linear Gaussian LDS) is a natural, tractable modeling choice with EM for estimation.  
- Strength: Rigorous evaluation with controlled simulations, comparisons to naive and fully observed baselines, and a fictional imaging benchmark on real data.  
- Strength: Paper is well-written with clear mathematical notation and organization.  
- Weakness: Linearity and Gaussian assumptions limit applicability to inherently nonlinear neural and calcium dynamics.  
- Weakness: Scalability to very large populations (thousands of neurons) and computational cost are not thoroughly characterized.  
- Weakness: Sensitivity to the amount of overlap between recordings is inadequately explored; optimal overlap is unclear.  
- Weakness: Limited biological analysis of inferred correlations and lack of comparison to known anatomical or functional data.  
- Arguments for acceptance: important problem, novel and sound approach, strong empirical results, clear presentation.  
- Arguments against acceptance: simplifying linearity assumption, uncertain scalability, and limited biological validation.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution; recommended for acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Suggested revisions: discuss impact of linearity and explore nonlinear extensions, provide computational/runtime and scalability details, analyze overlap vs. performance, and investigate biological implications of inferred correlations.
