

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including correlations between neurons not recorded simultaneously. The authors model the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework. They demonstrate the approach on simulated data and two calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

## Strengths
The problem addressed is significant in neuroscience, where experimental limitations prevent simultaneous recording of all neurons in large circuits. The method is conceptually elegant and demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data, with the stitching approach significantly outperforming a naive baseline. The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the method and results.

## Weaknesses

### Methodological inconsistencies
The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that undermine confidence in the methodology. In equation (2), the formulation "ykt = C k(xkt + d+ t) t ∼ N (0, R)" contains a clear typo; it should be "ykt = Ck(xkt + d) + εt, εt ∼ N(0, R)." This is a serious formatting error that could confuse readers.

The description of the initial conditions for the LDS is confusing: "x0 = 0 and Q0 satisfies the Lyapunov equation Q0 = AQ0A> +Q." For a stationary system, the initial state should have covariance Q0, but the mean x0 should be zero (not the covariance). This is a critical technical error that would affect implementation.

The paper states "we assume Q to be diagonal" for neural noise but later uses R as diagonal for measurement noise without clarifying if Q is diagonal in the EM updates. This inconsistency could lead to implementation errors.

### Experimental details
The experimental description lacks crucial details. The paper states "about 100-200 neurons" were imaged but doesn't provide exact numbers for the datasets. The viral expression details ("driven pan-neuronally by the human-synapsin promoter") are insufficient to assess data quality, as expression efficiency varies significantly between animals and could affect calcium signal reliability.

The high-pass filtering with "a time-constant of 1s" is not justified. The choice of this specific value could significantly impact the observed dynamics, yet no rationale or sensitivity analysis is provided.

### Statistical rigor
The paper claims "correlation between predicted and true correlations was 0.92" without providing standard errors or confidence intervals, making it difficult to assess the precision of this estimate. Similarly, the comparison between stitching and naive methods in Figure 3d shows correlation values (0.84 vs. 0.15) without statistical testing to determine if the difference is significant.

The claim that "the naive method, by definition, is unable to infer couplings for non-simultaneously recorded pairs" is not fully justified. If there is overlap between sessions, the naive approach could infer some information about cross-couplings through the overlapping neurons, which the authors don't address.

### Related work
The paper cites [12] as requiring "the strong assumption that noise correlations are monotonically related to stimulus correlations," but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why this assumption is problematic for their application. The claim that [13] "does not scale to the population sizes of interest" lacks supporting evidence.

### Presentation issues
The fictional imaging scenario description is confusingly worded: "The subsets corresponding to different 'sessions' c = 60% of the neurons, meaning that the subsets overlapped..." This should be rephrased as "The subsets for different sessions comprised 60% of the neurons, meaning the subsets overlapped."

The paper contains inconsistent citation formats (some with "pp." and some without) and a minor typo in the author email address.

## Overall assessment

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents promising results, the methodological inconsistencies and lack of statistical rigor undermine the technical contribution. The paper is well-written overall, but the technical details are not sufficiently precise. The authors claim their approach "successfully predicts the magnitude of noise correlations," but the statistical evidence provided is insufficient to support this claim with confidence.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the neuroscience community, but the technical flaws and lack of methodological precision prevent it from meeting NIPS's quality standards. The minor methodological inconsistencies, particularly in the mathematical formulation of the LDS model, could lead to implementation errors and undermine the validity of the results.

The paper's significance is somewhat limited by the simplicity of the linear model, which may not capture the complex dynamics of neural circuits. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide sufficient justification for why this simple model works well for their purpose.

I recommend rejection due to the significant methodological inconsistencies that undermine the technical contribution, despite the paper's interesting problem and promising results. The authors should address these technical issues before the work can be considered for publication at NIPS.
