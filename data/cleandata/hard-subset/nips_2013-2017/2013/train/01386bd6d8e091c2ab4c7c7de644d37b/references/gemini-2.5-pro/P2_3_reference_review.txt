**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a statistical method, termed "stitching," to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous partial recordings of that population. The core problem addressed is a fundamental limitation of current neural recording technologies (e.g., 2-photon calcium imaging), which can only monitor a subset of neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose modeling the entire neural circuit as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is then treated as a partial and noisy observation of this underlying system. The model parameters, including the full coupling matrix that describes interactions between all neurons (even those never observed simultaneously), are estimated from the collection of partial datasets using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is validated on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, using a clever "fictional imaging scenario" to create an effective ground truth for evaluation. The key result is that the method can accurately predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded at the same time, a task impossible with conventional approaches.

**Evaluation according to NIPS Criteria**

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound. The choice of an LDS is a pragmatic and well-justified starting point for modeling neural population dynamics due to its tractability. The application of the EM algorithm to fit an LDS with block-wise missing data (where the missing blocks change per session) is a standard but appropriate approach.

The experimental validation is a major strength.
1.  **Synthetic Data:** The use of simulated data with a known ground-truth connectivity matrix provides a clear and convincing demonstration that the method works in principle. It allows for direct quantification of how well the coupling matrix and noise correlations are recovered.
2.  **Real Data Evaluation:** The "fictional imaging scenario" is an excellent and clever evaluation strategy. By taking a fully-observed dataset and artificially splitting it into partial, non-simultaneous recordings, the authors create a realistic testbed where performance can be compared against an "upper bound" (a model fit to the full, simultaneous data). This provides a strong, data-driven argument for the method's efficacy.
3.  **Benchmarking:** The comparison against both an "upper bound" (fully observed model) and a "lower bound" (naive separate models) provides clear context for the performance and demonstrates significant improvement over the status quo.

The authors are also commendably honest about a key limitation: they note that the method failed to perform well on real data without any overlapping neurons between the two fictional sessions, even though it worked on the simulated data. This is an important finding for guiding future experimental design.

**Clarity: High**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, the method is described concisely in Section 2, and the results are presented logically. The figures are illustrative and support the main claims effectively. Figure 2, in particular, provides a compelling visual demonstration of the method's success on synthetic data. The intuition provided in the first paragraph of Section 3.1—framing the problem in terms of subspace identification and resolving ambiguity with a second observation—is helpful for understanding *why* the method should work. The level of detail provided seems sufficient for an expert reader to understand and likely reproduce the work.

**Originality: High**
While the components (LDS, EM) are standard machine learning tools, their application to the problem of "stitching" non-simultaneous neural recordings appears to be novel. The paper does an excellent job of positioning itself relative to prior work, clearly distinguishing its contribution from methods that infer connectivity from fully-observed data, methods that require strong assumptions (e.g., relating noise and signal correlations), or methods that address different subsampling regimes. This work frames a new and important problem for the computational neuroscience community and provides the first, to my knowledge, effective solution.

**Significance: Very High**
The significance of this work is potentially very high for both experimental and computational neuroscience.
*   **For Experimentalists:** This method provides a direct way to overcome a critical technological bottleneck. It suggests a concrete experimental paradigm: perform multiple recordings at different, overlapping locations within a circuit and then use this method to build a unified model of the whole. This could enable the construction of functional circuit diagrams on a scale previously thought impossible. The discussion of overlap as a critical parameter provides valuable guidance for experimental design.
*   **For Computational Researchers:** This paper opens up a new avenue of research in building large-scale models from limited data. It provides a strong foundation upon which more complex models (e.g., nonlinear dynamics, spiking neuron models) could be developed for the same stitching task. The ability to accurately estimate noise correlations, which are thought to be critical for understanding neural coding and computation, between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a substantial advance.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**
*   **Addresses a critical, real-world problem:** The method directly tackles a major limitation in experimental neuroscience.
*   **Elegant and principled solution:** The formulation as an LDS with missing observations is clean and well-motivated.
*   **Strong and clever validation:** The use of both synthetic data and the "fictional imaging scenario" on real data provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.
*   **High potential impact:** If adopted, this technique could significantly expand the scale at which neural circuits can be functionally characterized.
*   **Clear and well-written paper:** The work is easy to understand, and its contributions are clearly articulated.

**Weaknesses (and suggestions for improvement):**
*   **Limitations of the LDS model:** The linear-Gaussian assumption is a strong simplification of neural dynamics. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of potential failure modes. For instance, how might strong nonlinearities or non-Gaussian drivers (e.g., bursting activity) affect the model's ability to stitch correctly?
*   **Scalability:** The paper demonstrates stitching for two populations. While it mentions generalization is possible, the computational complexity of EM for an N-neuron LDS is significant. A brief discussion on the practical limits of N (the total population size) and the number of sessions would be valuable.
*   **Dependence on Overlap:** The observation that overlap was critical for real data but not simulated data is a key practical finding. The paper would be strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of why this might be the case. Perhaps the higher complexity, non-stationarity, or lower signal-to-noise ratio of real data makes the problem under-constrained without the anchoring provided by overlapping neurons.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper with a novel and highly significant contribution. It presents a method that is both elegant in its formulation and impressive in its empirical performance. The validation is thoughtful and rigorous. The weaknesses identified are minor and represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws. This paper is a perfect fit for NIPS, lying at the intersection of machine learning and a high-impact scientific application. I strongly recommend acceptance.
