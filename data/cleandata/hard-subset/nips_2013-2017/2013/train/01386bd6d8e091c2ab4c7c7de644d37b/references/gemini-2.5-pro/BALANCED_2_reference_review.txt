### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the critical and timely problem of inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings. As experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only capture a fraction of a neural circuit at once, the authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together these partial views into a coherent model of the whole circuit. Their approach models the full population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). The activity of unobserved neurons in any given recording session is treated as a latent variable. By fitting this single LDS model to data from all sessions using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, the method can infer the functional connectivity and joint statistics (e.g., noise correlations) even between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors validate their method first on simulated data with a known ground truth, showing accurate recovery of both coupling parameters and noise correlations. They then apply it to real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex using a clever "fictional imaging scenario" for evaluation, demonstrating that their method can predict correlations nearly as well as a model trained on fully simultaneous data, and significantly better than a naive baseline. This work builds on a rich history of system identification from partial observations (e.g., Takens, 1981; Katayama, 2005) but applies these ideas to a novel and pressing problem in modern systems neuroscience, differentiating itself from prior work that focused on simultaneous recordings or anatomical reconstruction.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The proposed methodology is sound, and the application of an LDS with missing observations is a principled way to approach this problem. The experimental validation is thorough and convincing.
*   **Strength:** The dual validation on both synthetic data (allowing for direct comparison with ground truth) and real experimental data is a major strength. The design of the "fictional imaging scenario" for the real data is particularly clever, providing a way to create upper and lower performance bounds (the "fully observed" and "naive" models) against which the proposed method can be rigorously benchmarked. The results presented are strong, showing that the method approaches the upper bound in predicting correlations.
*   **Weakness:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification of complex neural dynamics. The authors rightly acknowledge this, but the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the potential pitfalls. For instance, how might strong non-linearities, non-Gaussian firing statistics (spiking), or complex stimulus dependencies affect the accuracy of the inferred correlations? While the model performs well on calcium imaging data (which is intrinsically smoother and more Gaussian-like than spikes), its applicability to other data types remains an open question.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow.
*   **Strength:** The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the methods are described with sufficient detail, and the results are presented logically. The figures are clean, informative, and effectively support the main claims of the paper. The intuition provided at the beginning of Section 3.1—that a first recording identifies the system up to a linear transformation and a second recording resolves that ambiguity—is very helpful for the reader.
*   **Weakness:** While the high-level intuition is provided, the paper could benefit from a slightly more detailed explanation of the mechanism by which the ambiguity is resolved. Specifically, clarifying the role of the overlapping neurons in anchoring the latent state space across different sessions would make the paper more self-contained and satisfying for readers unfamiliar with system identification theory.

**Originality:** The work is highly original.
*   **Strength:** While the core statistical tools (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to the problem of inferring joint functional dynamics from non-simultaneous recordings is novel and creative. The paper clearly carves out a new problem space that is highly relevant to current experimental neuroscience. It successfully differentiates its contribution from prior work that handles sub-sampling in different contexts (e.g., anatomical connectivity, intra-session interpolation).
*   **Weakness:** None to note. The problem formulation and application represent a significant conceptual advance.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial.
*   **Strength:** This method provides a direct, practical solution to a major bottleneck in systems neuroscience: the limited field of view of recording technologies. If proven to be robust and generalizable, this "stitching" approach could enable the construction of functional models of entire neural circuits or cortical columns, a goal that is currently out of reach. This could profoundly impact how experiments are designed and analyzed, allowing researchers to trade recording time for spatial coverage. The work opens up a new and exciting direction for data-driven modeling of large-scale neural systems.
*   **Weakness:** The practical utility hinges on a key experimental constraint mentioned in the paper: the need for some overlapping neurons between sessions. The authors state that the method failed without overlap in their experiments. This is a crucial finding, but its implications are not fully explored. The performance of the method is likely a function of the degree of overlap, the number of sessions, and the underlying connectivity structure. A more systematic analysis of these dependencies would be necessary to provide practical guidance to experimentalists.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance:** Addresses a fundamental and timely problem in experimental neuroscience with a solution that could have a major impact on the field.
2.  **Novelty:** Proposes a novel and elegant formulation for combining non-simultaneous functional recordings.
3.  **High Quality:** The methodology is sound, and the validation is rigorous and convincing, using both synthetic and real data with well-designed controls.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Model Simplicity:** The linear-Gaussian assumption is a major simplification, and its limitations could be discussed more deeply.
2.  **Unexplored Dependencies:** The method's performance as a function of key experimental parameters like the degree of neuron overlap between sessions is not characterized, limiting immediate practical guidance.

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

Overall, this is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths and can be addressed to further improve the manuscript.

1.  **Role of Overlap:** It would be highly beneficial to add a more intuitive explanation of *why* overlapping neurons are critical. Do they serve to register or align the latent dynamics inferred from each session? A sentence or two clarifying this mechanism would greatly enhance the paper's clarity. If space permits, a supplementary figure showing performance as a function of overlap percentage would be a fantastic addition and of great practical value.
2.  **Limitations of the LDS:** In the discussion, consider adding a brief paragraph speculating on which specific aspects of neural dynamics the LDS model might fail to capture and how that might manifest. For example, would it tend to underestimate correlations driven by sparse, synchronized spiking events? This would provide a more nuanced view of the model's applicability.
3.  **Experimental Design:** The discussion touches on the trade-off between overlap and coverage. This is a key point. It would be valuable to expand on this and perhaps frame it as a new problem in optimal experimental design that this work opens up for future investigation.

Congratulations to the authors on a fine piece of work.
