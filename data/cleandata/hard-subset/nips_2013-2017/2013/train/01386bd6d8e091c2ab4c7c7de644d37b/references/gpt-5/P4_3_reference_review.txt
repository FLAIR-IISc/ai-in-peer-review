Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of inferring joint population dynamics and, specifically, noise correlations between neurons that are not recorded simultaneously. The authors model population activity with a linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with stimulus inputs and Gaussian innovations, and treat partial recordings as missing observations via known selection matrices per session. Parameters are learned by EM (with mini-batch online EM followed by batch EM), performing Kalman smoothing in the E-step. The key claim is that by “stitching” multiple partial recordings from the same circuit, one can recover cross-session couplings and predict noise correlations between neuron pairs never recorded together.

On simulated data with two non-overlapping 30-neuron subsets (from a 60-neuron network), the method recovers off-diagonal couplings and accurately predicts noise correlations between never co-recorded pairs. On two real 2-photon datasets (mouse S1, GCaMP6s), the authors create fictional splits of simultaneously recorded populations into two sessions with partial overlap, fit the stitched model, and compare to (i) an upper bound given by a “fully observed” model fit to all neurons jointly, and (ii) a lower bound “naive” approach that trains separate models per session and merges. The stitched model strongly outperforms the naive approach and approaches the fully observed model in predicting pairwise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs; it also predicts held-out neural activity better than the naive model.

This work connects to a large literature on inferring functional connectivity and latent-variable models for neural population activity, including LDS/EM with missing data, subspace/system identification, and GPFA-style approaches. The paper cites latent GLMs and related connectivity work but omits closely related LDS/GPFA formulations routinely used with missing data; in particular, Gaussian-process factor analysis (Yu et al., NIPS 2009) and subspace ID with missing observations are highly relevant baselines/comparators. The novelty here is mainly the explicit framing and evaluation of “stitching” non-simultaneous 2p recordings to recover cross-session correlations.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: current imaging cannot cover full circuits; stitching partial views is practically important.
- Methodological simplicity and tractability: standard LDS with missing data; scalable EM implementation.
- Empirical validation: synthetic data with non-overlapping subsets; real datasets with carefully constructed upper/lower bounds, including correlation prediction and activity cross-prediction metrics.
- Practical insight: overlap between sessions improves performance; the approach predicts noise correlations, not just mean responses.

Weaknesses
- Method novelty is limited: the core model and inference (LDS + EM + Kalman smoothing with missing observations) are standard. The main contribution is the application and empirical study in this context.
- Evaluation on real data uses fictional splits of simultaneously recorded neurons; there is no demonstration on truly non-simultaneous recordings across time/fields of view, where drift, nonstationarity, and identity mapping become critical.
- Overlap appears empirically necessary for good performance on real data; the conditions under which non-overlapping sessions suffice are not characterized theoretically or empirically.
- Baselines are weak: “naive” separate fits are expected to fail. Comparisons to GPFA/FA with temporal dynamics, subspace ID, or LDS with low-dimensional latent common input would be more informative.
- Modeling limitations: treating calcium fluorescence directly with a linear measurement model conflates neural, calcium, and measurement dynamics; interpreting A as functional couplings is tenuous at ~3 Hz sampling. Q is assumed diagonal in the main text, though correlated innovations are plausible; R is diagonal.
- Clarity issues: some notation/typos (e.g., Eq. (2) formatting; undefined hk1:T) and missing details (initialization, hyperparameters, computational complexity).

Quality
- The approach is technically sound as an application of well-established LDS/EM machinery to masked observations. The synthetic-study design is appropriate and supports the claims under model match.
- For real data, using fully observed fits as a proxy “upper bound” is reasonable but not ground truth; results indicate good recovery of correlation structure, especially in the awake dataset.
- Potential overfitting is not extensively addressed; regularization (e.g., sparsity) is mentioned but not systematically evaluated. Assumptions of stationarity and session-to-session parameter constancy are strong for real non-simultaneous data.

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized, with an intuitive explanation of identifiability up to transformations. However, the measurement equation needs correction, some symbols are undefined, and additional details on training (initialization, convergence, runtime) would aid reproducibility.

Originality
- Conceptual framing of “stitching” non-simultaneous recordings is useful, but the modeling/inference is standard. Prior LDS/GPFA/subspace ID work appears under-cited and not compared against. Originality is moderate.

Significance
- If robust in real non-simultaneous settings, the approach could be impactful for large-scale imaging, enabling estimation of cross-population noise correlations. As presented, significance is promising but limited by reliance on fictional splits and modest baselines.

Constructive suggestions
- Add stronger baselines: GPFA or LDS with low-dimensional latent common input; subspace ID with missing data; correlation-completion methods using shared stimulus structure.
- Provide a theoretical or empirical study of identifiability/consistency with and without overlap, and sensitivity to overlap fraction, SNR, and nonstationarity.
- Evaluate on truly non-simultaneous sessions (e.g., across time or fields of view) with neuron identity uncertainties and possible drift.
- Consider modeling deconvolved spikes or incorporate a calcium observation model; allow correlated innovations or low-rank latent inputs.
- Report computational complexity, runtime, and scaling with N and T; discuss initialization and convergence behavior.
- Fix notation/typos and include missing references (e.g., GPFA; subspace/system identification).

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a relevant and practical problem with a simple, scalable method.
- Strong empirical results on synthetic and real datasets for predicting cross-session correlations.
- Clear exposition and useful insights about overlap and experimental design.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty beyond standard LDS with missing data.
- Real-data validation relies on fictional splits; no demonstration on truly non-simultaneous recordings.
- Weak baselines and incomplete connection to closely related prior work.
- Insufficient analysis of identifiability, robustness, and modeling assumptions for calcium data.
