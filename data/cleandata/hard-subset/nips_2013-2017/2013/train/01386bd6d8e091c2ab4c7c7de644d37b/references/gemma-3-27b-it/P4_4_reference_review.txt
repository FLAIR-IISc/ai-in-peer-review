This paper addresses the problem of inferring the joint dynamics of a neural population from multiple, partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to leverage the assumption that nearby neurons are strongly coupled, allowing for “stitching” together measurements from different imaging sessions to estimate correlations even between neurons never simultaneously recorded. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, and demonstrate its effectiveness through simulations and analysis of calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper is well-motivated, tackling a significant challenge in neuroscience – the limitations of current imaging technology to capture the full activity of large neural circuits. The proposed approach is clearly presented and logically structured. The authors appropriately relate their work to existing literature, highlighting the novelty of inferring connections between *non-simultaneously* observed neurons, distinguishing it from previous efforts focused on connectivity inference from single sessions or approaches requiring strong stimulus-correlation assumptions. The experimental validation using real calcium imaging data is a significant strength.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The problem addressed—inferring correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons—is novel and relevant to the field. The method offers a potential solution to a practical limitation in neuroscience research.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The use of figures and equations is appropriate and aids understanding.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The use of a latent dynamical system framework is a reasonable choice, and the application of the EM algorithm for parameter estimation is standard. The detailed description of the simulation setup and experimental data analysis enhances reproducibility. The inclusion of both simulated and real data analysis strengthens the findings.
*   **Significance:** The ability to infer correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons could significantly advance our understanding of neural circuit function and allow for the construction of more comprehensive models. The method could be valuable for researchers working with limited imaging data.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Simplification:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of the complex biological reality. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but its potential impact on the accuracy of the inferred correlations should be further discussed. Non-linearities and non-Gaussianity are known to be important in neural dynamics.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the model may be sensitive to the choice of parameters (e.g., noise covariance matrix Q). A more thorough investigation of parameter sensitivity and robustness would be beneficial.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between imaging sessions is “critical” for performance raises a practical concern. The degree of required overlap might limit the applicability of the method in certain experimental scenarios. This could be further explored.
*   **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention online EM for efficiency, the computational cost of fitting the model, especially to large populations, is not thoroughly discussed. Scaling to even larger circuits may be a challenge.
*   **Limited Comparison to Alternatives:** While the authors compare to a "naive" approach and a fully observed model, exploration of other potential methods for stitching data together (e.g., kernel methods, more sophisticated latent variable models) might provide valuable context.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant and novel problem in neuroscience.
*   The proposed method is technically sound and well-supported by both simulations and experimental data.
*   The results demonstrate the feasibility of inferring correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a simplified linear Gaussian model may limit the generalizability of the findings.
*   The potential parameter sensitivity and computational cost could pose practical challenges.
*   The required overlap between imaging sessions may restrict the method's applicability.

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis. While the simplifying assumptions of the model and potential practical limitations are valid concerns, the novelty and potential impact of the proposed method outweigh these drawbacks. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the points raised in the "Weaknesses" section regarding parameter sensitivity, computational cost, and a more comprehensive discussion of the model's limitations. A brief exploration of alternative methods and their comparison could also strengthen the manuscript.
