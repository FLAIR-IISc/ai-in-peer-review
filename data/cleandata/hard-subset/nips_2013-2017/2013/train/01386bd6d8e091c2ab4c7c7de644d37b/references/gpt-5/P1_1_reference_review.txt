Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a practical and important problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations among neurons that are not recorded simultaneously, using multiple partial recordings of the same circuit. The authors cast the problem as learning a single latent linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations across sessions, where each session observes a different subset of the neurons via a known selection matrix Ck. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned via EM (with online-EM followed by batch EM). They evaluate on synthetic data and on two two-photon calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1), using a “fictional” split into two sessions with overlap. Compared to a “fully observed” model (silver-standard upper bound) and a “naive” baseline (separate per-session LDSs stitched post hoc), they show that their stitched LDS recovers cross-session couplings and, more importantly, accurately predicts noise correlations and unobserved activity.

This work connects to prior efforts on inferring functional connectivity from calcium or spike data (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al., Song et al.), latent GLMs and LDSs for partially observed populations (Pillow & Latham), and subspace/system identification and embedding theorems (Baum-Turner, Katayama, Takens). The closest in spirit is Pillow & Latham’s latent GLM with missing observations, and Machens et al. (linear readout with partial correlation data); the present work focuses on a scalable LDS instantiation and the specific stitching use case to infer cross-session noise correlations without assuming a monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations (unlike Wohrer/Romo/Machens).

Strengths
- Problem importance: Stitching partial recordings is a pressing need in neurophysiology; the method could materially expand effective coverage of circuits with current imaging limits.
- Conceptual simplicity and practicality: Standard LDS with missing data and known Ck; EM-based fitting is straightforward to implement and scale to hundreds of neurons.
- Empirical validation: Clear synthetic demonstration (recovery of A and noise correlations, activity prediction) and real-data evaluation with reasonable protocol (silver-standard upper bound, naive lower bound, held-out test).
- Focus on correlations: Emphasizing recovery of noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs is compelling and aligns with many analyses in the field.
- Sensible discussion: The paper transparently notes limitations (linearity, need for overlap, potential for improved priors/latents) and outlines extensions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited theory/identifiability conditions: The paper relies on intuition from system identification but provides no formal conditions for when stitching succeeds (e.g., observability/controllability with missing data, needed overlap size, stimulus richness/persistent excitation). The discrepancy between simulation (no overlap worked) and real data (no overlap failed) deserves analysis.
- Modeling assumptions may be too restrictive: Q is diagonal and R is diagonal and (apparently) shared across sessions; d is global. In real imaging, session-specific baselines, gains, and noise levels vary. Without session-specific Rk and dk (or per-session gain/offset), parameter sharing across sessions may misattribute differences to A/B. Similarly, excluding low-rank process noise (common input) forces correlated variability into A.
- Evaluation targets: For real datasets, the primary metric compares stitched vs fully observed model correlations. Since all neurons were in fact recorded simultaneously, the authors could also directly compare to empirical correlations on held-out time (with and without stimulus regression) to avoid model-to-model comparisons.
- Baselines: The naive baseline is weak. Stronger baselines would include: (i) low-rank factor analysis/GPFA shared across sessions with overlap-based alignment, (ii) LDS with shared low-rank latent plus sparse A (sparse+low-rank), (iii) latent GLM on subsets that scales via variational inference, (iv) simple imputation using overlap-based linear regression. These would clarify where the gain comes from: dynamics vs shared latent factors.
- Nonstationarity across sessions: The approach assumes the same A, B across sessions. Real recordings can drift (state changes, bleaching, behavior). The paper should test robustness to session-specific drifts (e.g., allow A shared but add session-specific affine transformations or additive session latents).
- Scalability: While practical for N≈100–200, complexity of smoothing in full-state LDS scales cubically in N per time step; there is no timing/memory report or discussion of scaling to thousands of neurons or many sessions. Sparse A or low-rank structure, conjugate gradients, or square-root filters could help.
- Clarity issues: A few notational glitches (Eq. 2 formatting, definition of εt), and some implementation details are light (initialization, regularization, convergence criteria, hyperparameters). Reporting confidence intervals or error bars is done for some analyses but could be more consistent.

Quality
- Technically sound as an application of LDS with missing data; the EM updates are standard. Claims are mostly supported by experiments. The evaluation could be strengthened with additional baselines and direct empirical correlation comparisons, and by analyzing identifiability/overlap requirements.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well-organized. Minor notation/typos to fix. Add details on session-specific parameters, preprocessing, initialization, runtime, and the precise noise-correlation computation.

Originality
- The stitching idea via LDS with missing observations is a natural but useful application; novelty lies more in the use case and empirical demonstration than in methodological innovation. Relation to prior partially observed latent models is adequately acknowledged.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for neuroscience workflows; predicting cross-session noise correlations and unobserved activity can influence experimental design and analysis. Broader ML significance is moderate without theoretical advances, but the application is timely and relevant to NeurIPS.

Suggestions and questions for authors
- Provide conditions/diagnostics for when stitching works: effect of overlap fraction, stimulus richness, session length, SNR. Include an ablation varying overlap (0–60%) and stimulus strength on synthetic data.
- Add session-specific measurement parameters (dk, Rk, optional per-session gains) and/or shared low-rank process noise; report whether this improves real-data fits.
- Compare to GPFA/FA baselines and a sparse+low-rank LDS; include runtime and scaling results.
- For real data, report direct correlation recovery against empirical held-out correlations (with stimulus regression), not only vs fully observed model.
- Report robustness to nonstationarity: fit with session-specific offsets/latent states; test on awake data with behavioral covariates.
- Clarify Eq. (2), noise notation, and full EM update details; report regularization (e.g., stability constraints on A, sparsity penalties) and initialization.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important and practical problem with a simple, effective approach.
- Strong empirical results on real datasets; clear gains over naive baselines; useful for experimental design.
- Well-situated within ML-neuroscience; likely to be used and extended.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty and lack of theoretical guarantees/diagnostics.
- Evaluation could be stronger with additional baselines and empirical ground-truth comparisons.
- Modeling assumptions (diagonal Q/R, no session-specific parameters) may be too restrictive.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a valuable and pragmatic contribution to an important problem in neural data analysis; with minor additions/clarifications, it would make a solid NeurIPS paper.
