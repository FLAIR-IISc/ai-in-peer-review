## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the important and challenging problem of reconstructing the dynamics of large neural populations from limited, spatially-separated recordings. The authors propose a statistical method, built upon a latent dynamical system framework, to "stitch" together activity measurements from different imaging sessions, effectively leveraging the spatial correlation inherent in neural circuits. They demonstrate, both through simulations and analysis of real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, that their approach can accurately predict correlations – particularly noise correlations – between neurons that were *not* simultaneously recorded, outperforming a naive baseline approach.  This work builds upon the existing literature in functional connectivity inference and latent variable modeling, specifically addressing a gap in methods capable of inferring relationships between neurons observed in disjointed recordings.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a significant practical challenge:** The limitation of simultaneous recordings is a major bottleneck in understanding circuit function. This work provides a principled method to overcome this limitation, broadening the scope of analysis possible with existing technology.
* **Sound methodology:** The use of a latent dynamical system (LDS) is a well-motivated and flexible approach. The authors clearly explain the model and the EM algorithm used for parameter estimation. The choice of a linear Gaussian LDS for tractability is reasonable and allows for clear demonstration of the core concepts.
* **Strong empirical validation:** The simulations are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in recovering both coupling matrices and noise correlations.  Applying the method to real calcium imaging data and showing improved performance compared to a baseline method significantly strengthens the claims.  The fictional imaging scenario effectively isolates the benefit of the stitching method.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the figures are informative. 
* **Well-aware of related work:** The authors provide a comprehensive review of relevant literature, accurately positioning their contribution within the existing landscape of functional connectivity and latent variable modeling.  They clearly articulate how their work differs from and extends previous approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian assumption:** While justified for tractability, the assumption of a linear Gaussian LDS is a significant simplification of real neural dynamics. The authors acknowledge this but could further discuss the potential limitations it introduces. For example, the impact of nonlinearities on the accuracy of noise correlation estimation, or the potential for the method to miss more complex dynamic relationships.
* **Model identifiability:** While the authors demonstrate successful recovery of parameters in simulations, the issue of identifiability in the real data is less explicitly addressed. The LDS framework, particularly with limited overlap between recording sessions, can be susceptible to identifiability issues. The fact that performance plateaus with increasing population size, as noted in the results, could point to this limitation.  Further exploration of this point would be valuable.
* **Sensitivity to overlap:** The results indicate that a degree of overlap between recording sessions is crucial for good performance.  The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed analysis of the relationship between overlap percentage and prediction accuracy would be helpful.  How does the method behave with minimal or no overlap?
* **Computational cost:** The authors mention using mini-batch EM for efficiency, but the computational demands of the method, particularly for larger populations, are not thoroughly discussed.  A quantification of the computational resources required would be beneficial, particularly for researchers considering applying this method to their own datasets.
* **Limited exploration of stimulus dependence:** While the model includes a stimulus term, the analysis primarily focuses on noise correlations. A more detailed investigation of how the stitching method affects the ability to estimate stimulus-dependent responses across sessions would be valuable.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that tackles an important problem with a principled and well-validated approach. The method is clearly presented and convincingly demonstrated to improve the ability to infer population dynamics from incomplete recordings. While the simplifying assumptions of the model and potential identifiability issues warrant consideration, the demonstrated performance on real data and the potential impact of this work on the field make it a valuable contribution to NIPS.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial practical limitation in studying neural circuits.
* Methodologically sound and well-motivated.
* Strong empirical validation on both simulated and real data.
* Clearly written and well-organized.
* Significant advancement over existing approaches.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on simplifying assumptions (linear Gaussian dynamics) that may limit its applicability.
* Potential identifiability issues could affect the reliability of parameter estimates.
* Sensitivity to the degree of overlap between recording sessions.




**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions.  Specifically, I suggest the authors:

1.  Expand the discussion of the limitations imposed by the linear Gaussian assumption and explore its potential impact on the results.
2.  Address the issue of model identifiability in more detail, perhaps through analysis of the Hessian of the likelihood function or by exploring alternative model constraints.
3.  Provide a more thorough analysis of the relationship between overlap percentage and prediction accuracy.
4.  Provide a more quantitative assessment of the computational cost of the method.
5.  Explore the impact of the stitching method on stimulus-dependent response estimation.
