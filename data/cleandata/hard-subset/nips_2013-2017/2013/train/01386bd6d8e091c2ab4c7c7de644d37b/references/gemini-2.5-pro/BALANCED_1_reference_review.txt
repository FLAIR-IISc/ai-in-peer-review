**Summary**

This paper introduces a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit. The core problem is that experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record from a fraction of the neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose to "stitch" these partial recordings together by modeling the entire population's activity as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each recording session is treated as a partial and noisy observation of this shared latent state. The model parameters, including the coupling matrix that describes interactions between all neurons (even those never recorded together), are learned using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on both simulated data and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex. A key contribution of the evaluation is a "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is artificially split into partial, non-simultaneous recordings. This allows for a quantitative comparison of the stitched model's predictions (e.g., noise correlations) against a ground truth. The proposed "stitchLDS" method is shown to substantially outperform a naive approach and approach the performance of an upper-bound model trained on the complete data, successfully predicting noise correlations between neurons that were never observed simultaneously. This work builds upon a lineage of research using dynamical systems to model neural populations (e.g., Pillow et al., 2008; Macke et al., 2012) but is novel in its specific application to combining non-simultaneous recordings to infer cross-population dynamics, a problem distinct from previous work on subsampled observations (e.g., Pillow & Latham, 2008) or anatomical reconstruction (Mishchenko & Paninski, 2012).

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and highly practical limitation in experimental neuroscience. The ability to computationally synthesize a larger, coherent model of a neural circuit from feasible partial recordings could significantly accelerate our understanding of population coding and circuit function. This method provides a tangible path toward modeling circuits that are currently too large to record from simultaneously, making it a valuable contribution for both computational and experimental neuroscientists.

2.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** The central idea of treating the full population activity as a latent state and individual recording sessions as partial observations is both elegant and powerful. It provides a principled statistical framework for integrating information across datasets that are temporally disconnected. This is a clear and novel extension of LDS models in neuroscience, moving beyond the standard application to single, fully- or partially-observed simultaneous recordings.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Validation (Quality):** The empirical validation is a major strength.
    *   The use of synthetic data with a known ground-truth connectivity matrix clearly demonstrates the method's ability to recover both coupling parameters and noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed neurons.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is a particularly clever and effective evaluation strategy. It enables the authors to create a challenging, realistic test case while retaining access to a ground-truth (the statistics of the original, fully-observed data).
    *   Benchmarking against both a "naive" lower bound and a "fully observed" upper bound provides a clear and compelling context for the performance of the proposed method. The results convincingly show that the method works far better than a simple baseline and approaches the best-case scenario.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly articulated in the introduction, the method is described concisely, and the results are presented in a logical sequence. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Model Simplicity and its Implications:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is justified by its tractability, but it is a significant simplification of the underlying nonlinear, non-Gaussian neural dynamics. The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential limitations.
    *   How might the linear model's performance degrade when faced with strongly nonlinear interactions or saturating dynamics common in neural circuits?
    *   Does the model's success in predicting second-order statistics (correlations) imply that it captures the essential dynamics, or might it fail to predict higher-order statistics that could be computationally relevant? Adding a brief discussion on these points would provide a more nuanced perspective on the model's applicability.

2.  **Dependence on Overlap and Scalability:** The authors state they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" in the real data experiments and that some overlap was "critical". This is a crucial practical finding. The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic investigation of this parameter.
    *   **Suggestion:** A simulation study analyzing performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) as a function of the percentage of overlapping neurons between sessions would be highly valuable for guiding future experimental design.
    *   Furthermore, the paper focuses on stitching two sessions. A discussion of the expected challenges and performance scaling when stitching together many (e.g., >5) partial recordings would enhance the paper's practical significance. Does performance degrade gracefully, and do errors accumulate?

3.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The method implicitly assumes that the circuit's dynamics (i.e., the parameters A, B, Q) are stationary across the different recording sessions. In a real-world scenario, these sessions could be separated by minutes or even hours, during which brain state, attention, or synaptic plasticity could alter the underlying functional connectivity.
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors should explicitly state this assumption and briefly discuss its potential implications. For example, the method might be best suited for recordings taken in close temporal proximity under stable experimental conditions. This would help users understand the conditions under which the model is most likely to succeed.

4.  **Identifiability Conditions:** The paper provides a good intuition for why the model parameters can be identified, referencing classical system identification literature. However, a more formal treatment of identifiability would strengthen the technical contribution. For instance, what are the theoretical conditions on the set of observation matrices {C_k} and the dynamics `A` that guarantee the unique recovery of the off-diagonal coupling blocks? This seems closely related to the empirical finding that overlap is necessary and would provide a deeper theoretical grounding for the method.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant, unsolved, and practical problem in systems neuroscience.
*   Presents a novel and principled statistical approach that is well-motivated and technically sound.
*   Features an exceptionally strong and creative validation on both synthetic and real neural data.
*   The results are clear, convincing, and likely to be of high interest to the NeurIPS community, particularly those at the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The linear Gaussian model is a simplification, and the limits of this approximation are not fully explored.
*   The practical requirements for the method to work (e.g., amount of neuron overlap, stationarity of the preparation) could be characterized more systematically.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution. The strengths, particularly the importance of the problem and the rigor of the validation, far outweigh the weaknesses. The proposed method offers a powerful new tool for neuroscientists, and the paper is a model of clarity and thoughtful experimental design. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations that point toward interesting avenues for future research rather than fundamental flaws in the current work.
