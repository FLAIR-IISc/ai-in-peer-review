- Problem: Infer joint dynamics and noise correlations across large neural populations when neurons cannot be recorded simultaneously by stitching partially overlapping recordings.
- Method: Fit a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations using session-specific selection matrices and EM with Kalman smoothing to learn A, B, Q, R.
- Key claim: Overlap between sessions allows recovery of cross-session couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations for neuron pairs never observed simultaneously.
- Evaluation: Tested on synthetic data with ground truth and on two two-photon calcium datasets using “fictional” splits of simultaneous recordings to emulate non-simultaneous sessions.
- Baselines: Compared to a “fully observed” LDS upper bound and a “naive” baseline that fits separate LDS per subset and combines post hoc.
- Results: Stitched model recovers off-diagonal couplings moderately well and predicts cross-session noise correlations close to the fully observed model, outperforming the naive baseline.
- Novelty/positioning: Uses standard LDS machinery but novel in applying overlap-anchored stitching for non-simultaneous calcium imaging with neuron-identity alignment.
- Strength: Clear, practical formulation with a computationally tractable solution and convincing synthetic demonstrations.
- Strength: Real-data results show stitched correlations approach the fully observed upper bound and are robust across population sizes.
- Strength: Useful discussion of overlap and identifiability with suggested extensions like sparsity and latent inputs.
- Weakness: Validation relies on fictional splits of simultaneous data, leaving external validity for truly sequential recordings uncertain.
- Weakness: Method depends critically on session overlap; paper lacks quantitative characterization of required overlap as function of N, T, SNR.
- Weakness: Strong modeling assumptions (linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q/R, stationarity, perfect identity alignment) may bias couplings and misattribute variability.
- Weakness: Baselines are weak; stronger alternatives (FA/GPFA with Procrustes, subspace ID with missing outputs, low-rank+sparse, multi-view CCA/LDS) should be included.
- Weakness: Scalability concern—state dimension equals number of neurons causing O(N^3) inference per step; paper lacks runtime/complexity or low-rank alternatives.
- Weakness: Notational ambiguities and terse algorithmic/implementation details hinder reproducibility.
- Suggestions: Validate on real sequential recordings with cell registration; quantify overlap/sample-complexity requirements; add stronger baselines.
- Suggestions: Test robustness to session-specific offsets/gains, slow drifts, correlated observation noise, and mismatch in calcium dynamics.
- Suggestions: Consider low-rank LDS or sparse A for scalable inference and report computational complexity and runtimes.
- Suggestions: Clarify notation, expand E/M update details, provide convergence criteria and release code for reproducibility.
- Overall assessment: Technically sound within the LDS framework and promising for neuroscience practice, but limited by validation on truly non-simultaneous data, modeling assumptions, weak baselines, and scalability concerns.
- Recommendation: Weak accept—important problem and promising results, but needs additional validations and stronger comparisons to be a solid contribution.
