### Summary

This paper introduces a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, non-simultaneous recordings from different subsets of neurons within the same neural circuit. The core problem addressed is a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record from a fraction of the neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose to model the entire neural population (both observed and unobserved neurons) as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each partial recording is treated as a noisy, incomplete observation of this larger system's state. The model parameters, including the functional connectivity matrix that governs interactions between all neurons, are inferred using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The key insight, rooted in system identification theory, is that the dynamics of one recorded sub-population contain information about the unobserved neurons they interact with. By observing a second, potentially overlapping sub-population later, ambiguities in the full system's parameters can be resolved. The paper validates this approach first on simulated data, showing it can recover ground-truth connectivity and accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded together. More convincingly, the method is tested on real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex using a clever "fictional imaging scenario." By partitioning a fully-observed dataset into fictional non-simultaneous recordings, the authors demonstrate that their stitching method can predict correlations with an accuracy approaching an upper-bound model trained on the full data, and far exceeding a naive baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a critical and timely problem in systems neuroscience. The ability to create coherent dynamical models of large circuits from partial recordings would be a major leap forward, enabling researchers to test theories of neural computation at a scale that is currently experimentally intractable. The proposed method provides a principled and practical path toward this goal.

2.  **Novelty and Elegance of the Approach:** While LDS models are well-established, their application to this "stitching" problem is novel and insightful. The framing of the problem as inference in a latent state-space model with changing observation matrices (`C_k`) is elegant and provides a solid statistical foundation. This work moves beyond prior efforts that either required simultaneous recordings or made strong assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation strategy is a major strength.
    *   The use of synthetic data with a known ground truth clearly illustrates the principle and demonstrates that the method can, in ideal conditions, recover key parameters like the coupling matrix and noise correlations.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is an excellent validation technique. It allows for quantitative evaluation against a "ground truth" (the fully observed model) that would otherwise be unavailable.
    *   The inclusion of clear upper and lower performance bounds (the "fully observed" and "naive" models, respectively) provides crucial context and convincingly demonstrates the method's efficacy. The results in Figure 3 and 4 are compelling.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clear, and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out plainly, the method is described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand, and the results are presented logically. The intuition behind why the method should work is also explained well in the introduction to the results section.

### Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions

While this is a strong paper, there are a few areas that could be clarified or strengthened.

1.  **Model Simplicity vs. Neural Reality:** The linear-Gaussian assumption is a significant simplification of neural dynamics, which are characterized by spiking, nonlinearities, and non-Gaussian statistics. The authors rightly acknowledge this, and the model's success in predicting correlations is impressive despite this simplification. However, the paper would be stronger with a more explicit discussion of the potential failure modes.
    *   **Suggestion:** Add a brief discussion on how severe nonlinearities or common input from a non-Gaussian source might be misinterpreted by the LDS, potentially leading to incorrect inference of pairwise couplings. This would add nuance and help guide future work.

2.  **Scalability Concerns:** The ultimate goal is to model circuits of thousands of neurons. The EM algorithm for an LDS with N neurons has a computational complexity dominated by the Kalman smoother, which is O(N³T). The paper mentions using online-EM for efficiency but provides no details on computational cost or how the method scales with population size (N). The experiments are limited to N≤100.
    *   **Suggestion:** Briefly report the runtime for the largest models tested and include a short discussion of the computational challenges and potential solutions for scaling to thousands of neurons (e.g., leveraging sparsity, variational approximations).

3.  **The Role of Overlap:** A crucial practical detail seems under-explored. The authors state that on real data, "some overlap was critical for stitching to work," which contrasts with the non-overlapping simulation. This is a vital piece of information for experimental design. The discrepancy between simulation and real data on this point warrants explanation. Is it due to model mismatch, lower signal-to-noise in real data, or a fundamental identifiability issue that is only overcome with overlap in practice?
    *   **Suggestion:** Please clarify this point. If possible, provide a brief analysis (perhaps in an appendix) of how performance degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases. This would provide invaluable practical guidance for experimentalists wanting to use this method.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant, practical problem in modern neuroscience.
*   Presents a novel and statistically principled solution.
*   The validation on both simulated and real data is rigorous, clever, and convincing.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The findings are likely to be influential and built upon by both computational and experimental neuroscientists.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The linear-Gaussian model is a strong simplification, and its limitations are not fully explored.
*   Questions of scalability to larger, more realistic circuit sizes are not addressed.
*   The practical necessity of overlapping recordings is mentioned but not sufficiently analyzed, limiting the immediate experimental guidance.

### Recommendation

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, elegant, and effective solution to an important problem, and the results are validated with a commendable degree of rigor. The weaknesses primarily relate to avenues for future work and do not undermine the core contribution. This is a high-quality paper that would be a definite asset to the NIPS proceedings.

**Decision: Accept**
