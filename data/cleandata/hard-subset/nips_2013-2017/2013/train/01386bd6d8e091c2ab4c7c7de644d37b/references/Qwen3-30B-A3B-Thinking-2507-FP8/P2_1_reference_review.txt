

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record the activity of all neurons in a neural circuit due to experimental limitations. The authors propose a statistical method ("stitching") to combine multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit (recorded in different sessions with non-overlapping neuron subsets) into a coherent model that can predict correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. They model the neural population as a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations and use an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing to estimate the model parameters. The method is validated on both simulated neural population data and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The results demonstrate that their stitching approach can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, substantially outperforming a naive baseline that simply averages parameters across sessions.

This work builds on existing literature in neural population analysis (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) but specifically addresses the novel problem of inferring joint dynamics from non-simultaneous partial observations, which none of the cited approaches solve. The paper clearly distinguishes itself from related work, particularly by identifying the limitations of methods like [12] (which requires strong assumptions about noise correlations) and [13] (which doesn't scale to large populations).

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors present a well-motivated statistical framework using a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) to model the problem. The mathematical formulation is clear and appropriate for the problem, with careful attention to the missing data aspect of the problem. The EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing is correctly applied to the specific problem of stitching together partial observations.

The claims are well-supported by both simulations and real data experiments. In the simulation, the authors show that their method recovers true coupling matrices (including off-diagonal blocks for non-simultaneously recorded neurons) with reasonable accuracy (correlation 0.73 for non-simultaneous pairs vs. 0.95 for simultaneous pairs). For noise correlations, their method achieves a correlation of 0.92 with the true values. In the real data experiments, the stitching method's predicted correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs correlate strongly with the "fully observed" upper bound (0.84 correlation), substantially outperforming the naive approach (0.15 correlation).

The authors are careful to provide appropriate baselines (the "fully observed" model as an upper bound and the naive approach as a lower bound) and discuss limitations, such as the need for some overlap between recording sessions and the potential degradation in performance with larger population sizes. The paper is a complete piece of work that advances the state of the art in analyzing neural population data.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively sets up the problem and its significance, the methods section provides sufficient technical detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results, and the results are presented in a logical and accessible manner. The figures are well-designed and effectively illustrate key findings (e.g., Figure 2 showing recovery of coupling matrices and noise correlations in the simulation, and Figure 3 demonstrating the method's performance on real data).

The authors make good use of visualizations to convey complex results, such as the coupling matrices in Figure 3a and the correlation metrics in Figure 4. The discussion of the method's limitations and future directions is thoughtful and well-integrated.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant contribution in terms of originality. While latent dynamical systems have been used before in neural data analysis (e.g., [13]), the specific application to stitching together non-simultaneous partial recordings is novel. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature: none of the existing methods address the problem of inferring functional connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons.

The paper distinguishes itself clearly from related work. They correctly point out that [12] requires the strong assumption that noise correlations are monotonically related to stimulus correlations (which doesn't hold in their data), [13] doesn't scale to large populations, [14] assumes measurements can be interpolated over time (which isn't the case here), and [15] deals with anatomical connectivity reconstruction rather than dynamics. This careful differentiation from previous work demonstrates a thorough understanding of the field.

## Significance

The significance of this work is high. The ability to extract joint dynamics from multiple partial recordings addresses a major experimental limitation in neuroscience. Current imaging techniques can only record a small fraction of neurons in a neural circuit simultaneously, but the authors show that by recording different subsets in different sessions, they can still infer meaningful correlations across the entire circuit.

This approach could have substantial impact on how neuroscientists analyze existing data and design future experiments. The authors provide evidence that their method works well on real data from mouse somatosensory cortex, demonstrating its practical utility. The paper advances the field by providing a practical tool for extracting more information from existing datasets, potentially leading to better understanding of neural circuit function.

The method's success in predicting noise correlations (which are critical for understanding neural computation) is particularly significant, as these correlations are difficult to measure directly with current technology.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

### Strengths
1. Addresses a significant and timely problem in neuroscience with clear experimental motivation
2. Presents a technically sound statistical framework with appropriate mathematical formulation
3. Provides strong empirical validation on both simulated and real data
4. Carefully benchmarks against meaningful baselines (upper and lower bounds)
5. Clear, well-organized writing with effective visualizations
6. Thoroughly discusses limitations and future directions
7. Demonstrates practical utility for real neuroscience data

### Weaknesses
1. The linear Gaussian model may be too simplistic for complex neural dynamics (though the authors acknowledge this limitation)
2. The requirement for some overlap between recording sessions (though they note this is critical for their method to work) might limit applicability in some experimental designs
3. The performance degradation with increasing population size (as noted in the discussion) could be a concern for very large circuits
4. The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how different degrees of overlap between recording sessions affect performance (though they do mention it as an open problem)

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the analysis of neural population data. The problem is well-motivated, the method is technically sound, and the results are convincing. The paper is clearly written and makes a novel contribution to the field of neural data analysis.
