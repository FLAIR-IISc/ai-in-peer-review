This paper addresses the important problem of inferring neural population dynamics from incomplete recordings, a common limitation in experimental neuroscience. The authors propose a statistical method – essentially a latent dynamical system framework with an EM algorithm for parameter estimation – to “stitch” together observations from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea is sound: leveraging overlapping observations to infer the full population dynamics and correlations, especially noise correlations, which are often difficult to estimate from limited data. The paper is well-motivated, clearly articulates the problem, and presents a reasonable approach. 

The strengths of the paper are several. First, the problem is highly relevant. Current imaging techniques often can’t capture the full activity of a neural circuit, and the ability to combine partial recordings is invaluable. Second, the proposed method is conceptually straightforward and builds on well-established techniques (linear dynamical systems, EM algorithm). This makes it relatively accessible and easier to understand. Third, the authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The simulation results are particularly convincing, showing accurate recovery of coupling matrices and noise correlations.  The application to real data, and the thoughtful comparison to upper and lower bound performance (fully observed model vs. naive approach), further strengthens the argument.  The analysis of the experimental data and comparison with a "naive" method provides valuable insight. The details provided on the experimental setup are also helpful.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. First, the reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the potential impact on the accuracy and applicability of the method is not fully explored. Real neural circuits are highly nonlinear, and calcium dynamics are also inherently nonlinear. While the results suggest the method can still predict correlations even in this simplified model, it’s unclear how well it would perform with more complex, biologically realistic dynamics.  A more thorough discussion of the potential biases introduced by the linearity assumption would be helpful. 

Second, the computational scalability of the method is not clearly addressed. While the authors mention that the algorithm doesn’t scale to the population sizes of interest in some other related work, they don’t provide detailed computational benchmarks or discuss potential strategies for scaling to larger networks (e.g., sparse methods, dimensionality reduction).  Given the goal of modeling large cortical circuits, this is a crucial consideration.

Third, the interpretation of the "functional couplings" (A matrix) is somewhat vague. The authors correctly point out that these couplings don't necessarily represent direct synaptic connections, but a more nuanced discussion of what they *do* represent would be helpful. Are they primarily reflecting correlated noise, indirect connections, or a combination of both? 

Finally, while the comparison to the "fully observed" model is useful, the experimental validation feels somewhat contrived (creating a “fictional imaging scenario”). A more compelling demonstration would involve applying the method to data where the true underlying dynamics are unknown, and comparing the predictions to independent experimental measurements or theoretical predictions.  

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroscience.
* Proposes a reasonable and well-motivated approach.
* Demonstrates the efficacy of the method on both simulated and real data.
* Provides a clear and concise presentation of the method and results.
* Offers valuable insights into the limitations of current experimental techniques.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Relies on a simplifying assumption of linear Gaussian dynamics.
* Computational scalability is not adequately addressed.
* Interpretation of functional couplings is somewhat vague.
* Experimental validation relies on a contrived scenario.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a solid contribution to the field, and the ideas presented are valuable. While the limitations are significant, they are acknowledged by the authors, and the method shows promising results. I would lean towards **accept**, but with a request for minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Expand the discussion of the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption, and consider exploring ways to mitigate its impact (e.g., through data preprocessing or model extensions).
2.  Provide more detail on the computational complexity of the method, and discuss potential strategies for scaling to larger networks.
3.  Clarify the interpretation of the functional couplings, and discuss their relationship to underlying biological mechanisms.
4. Consider a more robust validation using independent experimental data.
