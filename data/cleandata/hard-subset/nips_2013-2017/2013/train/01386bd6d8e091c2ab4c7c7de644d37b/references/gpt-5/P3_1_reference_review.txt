Summary
The paper tackles an important practical problem: inferring joint population dynamics, including noise correlations, across neurons that were not imaged simultaneously. The authors propose a “stitching” approach based on a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS/VAR(1) with stimulus input) in which each session observes a different subset of neurons via known selection matrices. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned by EM with Kalman smoothing under missing observations; Q and R are diagonal, A is unconstrained, and a stationary prior is assumed. On simulations and two 2p calcium datasets (anesthetized and awake mouse S1), the method predicts pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons substantially better than a naive baseline and close to an upper bound obtained by fitting a “fully observed” LDS on the whole dataset (in a fictional split). It also predicts unobserved activity given a subset of observed neurons better than the naive model.

Relation to prior work
- The core methodology is classical: Kalman filtering/smoothing and EM with missing data; LDS for population activity; stimulus terms as exogenous inputs. The key twist is using multiple sessions with different selection matrices and some overlap to align the latent states. This relates to subspace/system identification with incomplete observations, multi-view alignment, and multi-session latent-state models.
- Prior work on connectivity inference from calcium/spikes (e.g., GLMs, PLDS, sparse VARs) typically assumes simultaneous recordings; a few address subsampling but with different assumptions (e.g., [12] linking noise to stimulus correlations; latent GLMs [13] but not scalable). The idea of “stitching” across sessions has appeared in different guises in latent-manifold alignment for multi-session neural data, and matrix completion of covariance structures, though the paper’s framing for calcium imaging is useful.

Strengths
- Addresses a highly relevant experimental constraint and proposes a simple, computationally tractable solution.
- Clear exposition of the model and training procedure; use of EM with session-specific observation matrices is appropriate.
- Evaluation on both synthetic and real data, including a reasonable “fictional split” to mimic non-simultaneous access.
- Predictive tasks beyond parameter recovery (correlations and cross-prediction of activity) are informative and aligned with scientific use-cases.
- The discussion is measured about model limitations and extensions (e.g., latent inputs, sparsity priors).

Weaknesses and questions
Quality/technical
- Identifiability and conditions: The intuition given (recovering an invertible transform that later gets fixed by observing the second half) applies to low-dimensional latent states. Here the state is N-dimensional neural activity, and parameters are learned from partially observed trajectories. A careful statement of identifiability/observability conditions (coverage, overlap size, persistent excitation of u, stability of A) is missing. The discrepancy between simulation (no overlap works) and real data (overlap required) suggests sensitivity to violations (nonstationarity, unmodeled inputs), but this is not analyzed.
- Stationarity and per-session effects: The model shares A, B, Q, R, d across sessions. Real calcium data have session-specific gains, baselines, and noise (photobleaching, motion, arousal). A single global offset d and diagonal R likely underfit these effects. Did the authors try per-session offsets/gains and Rk? How sensitive are results to session-to-session drift?
- Model misspecification: Q and R are constrained diagonal, pushing all correlations to be mediated via A. Instantaneous shared inputs or measurement correlations will be misattributed to dynamics. The authors note possible latent dimensions but do not implement them; this could be important in cortex.
- Stability/regularization: No constraint on the spectral radius of A is described; with EM, unstable A can arise and corrupt inference of Q0 and predictions. Overparameterization (O(N^2) parameters in A) with N≈100–200 risks overfitting; “unreported simulations” suggest sparsity helps, but there’s no principled regularization (sparsity, ridge, stability penalties) in the main experiments.
- Evaluation metrics: On real data, many results compare to a “fully observed” LDS rather than to empirical correlations computed from the simultaneously recorded data on a held-out test segment. This can mask model misspecification if both models share biases. Please report (i) predictive log-likelihood on held-out data and (ii) direct comparison between predicted and empirical noise correlations on held-out time (when both neurons are actually observed). Uncertainty quantification (e.g., via bootstrap) would also be valuable.
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is very weak. Stronger baselines include (i) low-rank LDS/PLDS with shared dynamics across sessions and session-specific observation models, (ii) sparse VAR with matrix completion of covariance, (iii) subspace ID with missing data. Without these, it’s hard to assess relative gains.
- Calcium dynamics: Modeling fluorescence directly with VAR(1) conflates neuronal coupling and calcium/measurement dynamics (3 Hz sampling, slow GCaMP). Explicit observation dynamics (e.g., AR(2) calcium kernel) or spike deconvolution could reduce bias.

Clarity
- Eq. (2) has typesetting issues (ykt = Ck(xkt + d) + εt?); please tidy notation. Define all indices consistently. Some figure references are hard to follow without the figures.
- The fictional split procedure (percentages, overlap c, train/valid/test splits) should be precisely described.

Originality
- The statistical core is standard; the novelty lies in formulating and demonstrating multi-session stitching for partially overlapping calcium imaging. The contribution is more pragmatic than theoretical; that’s acceptable if the empirical advances are solid.

Significance
- If robust, the approach would be useful for current imaging constraints and motivate experimental design (trade-off between overlap and coverage). The lack of theory, strong baselines, and session-robustness analysis tempers impact. Clear takeaways on required overlap, data length, and sensitivity to drift would increase practical value.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add per-session affine calibrations (gain/offset) and noise covariances (Rk), and test robustness to session drift.
- Incorporate stability and sparsity regularization on A; report ablations.
- Include a low-rank latent input (PLDS/LDS with rank-r process) to model common input; compare to diagonal-Q model.
- Evaluate against empirical correlations and predictive likelihood on held-out data; add stronger baselines.
- Provide a study of overlap size vs. performance, and of scaling with N and T; report computation time.
- Consider modeling calcium dynamics explicitly or using deconvolved spikes.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and important problem with a simple method.
- Empirical results on real datasets show promising gains over a naive approach.
- Could influence experimental design and analysis pipelines.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond standard LDS with missing data; no theory for conditions/identifiability.
- Evaluation lacks strong baselines and direct comparisons to empirical test correlations.
- Modeling assumptions (diagonal Q/R, no per-session variation, no stability constraints) are strong and may bias conclusions.

Overall score: Borderline. I would support acceptance if the authors strengthen the evaluation (empirical correlation comparisons, stronger baselines), add regularization/stability, and analyze overlap/session effects; otherwise, this is a useful but incremental application of known tools.
