This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring neural population dynamics from incomplete recordings, a common limitation in experimental neuroscience. The authors propose a method to “stitch” together data from multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit using a latent dynamical system framework. The core idea – leveraging overlapping observations to infer the dynamics of a larger, unobserved population – is conceptually sound and potentially impactful. The paper presents both synthetic and real data experiments demonstrating the efficacy of the approach, particularly in predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

However, despite the novelty of the approach and the potential significance, I find numerous shortcomings, ranging from superficial methodological justifications to inconsistencies in the experimental validation, and ultimately recommend rejection. My concerns stem from a lack of rigorous evaluation, overstatement of results, and a somewhat careless presentation.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of explicitly modeling and inferring correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons is a valuable contribution.
*   **Potential Significance:** Overcoming limitations in experimental data collection is crucial for understanding neural circuit function.
*   **Clear Problem Statement:** The motivation for the work is well-articulated and relevant to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Methodological Justification:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system feels arbitrary and insufficiently justified. While the authors acknowledge its limitations ("does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade…"), they don't adequately explain *why* this simplification is acceptable or how its limitations might affect the results. The consequences of this linearity assumption are not explored.
*   **EM Algorithm Details:** The description of the EM algorithm, while standard, lacks crucial implementation details. For example, the choice of initialization for A, B, Q, and R is not discussed, and the potential for sensitivity to these choices is ignored. The “online-EM with randomly selected mini-batches” is mentioned but lacks details on batch size and update frequency, making reproducibility challenging.
*   **Simulated Data:** The simulation setup, while clear, is somewhat simplistic. The defined cell types and coupling structure, while reasonable, feel artificially constrained. The truncation of coupling weights at ±0.2 is not justified. Crucially, the noise level (std(η) = 0.006) appears extremely low, potentially making the inference task trivial and not representative of real biological data.
*   **Experimental Data - Fictional Scenario:** The "fictional imaging scenario" is a contrived setup. Creating overlap between imaging sessions artificially inflates performance and fails to address the core challenge of reconstructing dynamics from *completely disjoint* observations. The requirement of 60% overlap for adequate performance is a significant limitation not thoroughly discussed. A 50% overlap yields poor results—why?
*   **Benchmarking Issues:** The comparison to the "fully observed" model is unfair, as it represents an unattainable ideal. While a useful upper bound, it doesn’t provide meaningful insight into the practical limitations of the stitching method. The "naive" approach is poorly defined; simply averaging coefficients for overlapping neurons is an arbitrary procedure and likely biased.
*   **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the method can “accurately recover” noise correlations is too strong. While the correlation values in Figure 2b are high, the absolute magnitude of the inferred correlations isn’t assessed, and there's no consideration of potential biases. Similar overstatements appear throughout the results section.
*   **Inconsistencies & Minor Errors:**
    *   In Section 2.3, “T” is defined as the number of time steps *in each recording session*, but then used later in equations as if it represents the total number of time steps across all sessions.
    *   Equation for `dj` in Section 2.1 contains `χkj`, which is defined as 1 if neuron j was imaged in session k, yet used as an index.
    *   Figure 3 caption states correlation of 0.84, yet the plot shows ~0.7.
*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from a somewhat verbose writing style and a lack of conciseness. Figures could be more informative and clearly labeled.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The concept is novel and addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience.
*   The method shows promise for predicting correlations in synthetic data.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The methodological justification is weak.
*   The experimental validation is flawed due to the contrived "fictional" scenario.
*   The benchmarking is inadequate and potentially misleading.
*   The claims are often overstated.
*   Numerous minor errors and inconsistencies detract from the paper’s credibility.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the central idea of this paper is promising, the lack of rigor, the flawed experimental setup, and the numerous minor flaws make it unsuitable for publication at NIPS. The authors need to substantially revise the manuscript, addressing the concerns outlined above, before it can be considered for acceptance. Specifically, a more thorough theoretical justification, a more realistic experimental validation using truly disjoint observations, and a more careful and nuanced discussion of the results are essential. I recommend rejection.
