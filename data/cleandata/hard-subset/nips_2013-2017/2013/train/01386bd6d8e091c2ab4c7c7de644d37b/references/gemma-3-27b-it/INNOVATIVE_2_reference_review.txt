This paper tackles a challenging and practically relevant problem: inferring the dynamics of large neural circuits from limited, spatially-separated recordings. The core idea – leveraging the assumption of strong local connectivity to “stitch” together observations from different imaging sessions – is genuinely novel and exciting. While the methodology relies on a relatively standard linear Gaussian dynamical system, the application to this specific problem, and the demonstration of its feasibility, represents a significant step forward. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the experimental validation (albeit on a limited scale) is encouraging. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of inferring connections and correlations between *non-simultaneously* recorded neurons is a fresh perspective, particularly valuable given the practical constraints of large-scale neural recording. This directly addresses a bottleneck in systems neuroscience.
*   **Potential Significance:** If scalable, this method could unlock the ability to study dynamics in circuits far larger than currently accessible. This has huge implications for understanding neural computation.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is easy to follow. The mathematical formulation is concise, and the explanation of the method's intuition is well done.
*   **Validation:** The results on both simulated and real data are promising, showing the method can recover couplings and predict noise correlations with reasonable accuracy.
*   **Acknowledged Limitations:** The authors transparently discuss the limitations of the linear Gaussian model and acknowledge areas for future improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Simplification:** The linear Gaussian assumption is a strong one, and it's unclear how well the method will generalize to more complex, nonlinear neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact on real-world applicability needs further consideration.
*   **Scalability Concerns:** While the method is demonstrated on populations of ~60-200 neurons, the computational cost of the EM algorithm could become prohibitive for much larger circuits. The authors don't fully address this scalability issue.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The need for significant overlap between imaging sessions (60% in the experimental validation) is a practical constraint. This limits the flexibility of the method in real experimental settings.
*   **Limited Biological Interpretation:** While the method recovers correlations, the biological interpretation of the inferred “functional couplings” remains somewhat vague. More work is needed to relate these couplings to underlying synaptic connections and neural mechanisms.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The comparison to existing methods is adequate but could be more comprehensive. While the authors dismiss previous approaches, a more detailed discussion of why they are unsuitable for this particular problem would strengthen the argument.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The core idea is highly novel and represents a significant conceptual advance in the field of neural circuit analysis.
*   The results, while preliminary, are encouraging and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
*   The clear writing and transparent discussion of limitations make this paper a valuable contribution.
*   The potential impact on our ability to study large-scale neural dynamics is substantial.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a simplified linear Gaussian model raises concerns about generalizability.
*   Scalability and the overlap requirement pose practical challenges.
*   The biological interpretation of the inferred parameters could be strengthened.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, I strongly recommend acceptance. The novelty of this work and its potential impact on the field outweigh the methodological shortcomings. The paper introduces a genuinely new approach to a critical problem in neuroscience and opens up exciting avenues for future research. I believe this work is a significant step forward and fits well within the scope of NIPS, prioritizing bold ideas over methodological perfection. Future work should focus on addressing the scalability issues, exploring more realistic neural models, and investigating the biological relevance of the inferred functional couplings. A minor revision request would be to clearly state the computational complexity of the algorithm and to briefly discuss strategies for improving scalability (e.g., sparse methods, approximations).
