

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper introduces a novel and bold approach to a fundamental problem in neuroscience: how to infer the joint dynamics of large neural populations when experimental limitations prevent simultaneous recording of all neurons. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings from the same neural circuit, enabling prediction of correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This is a significant conceptual leap from prior work that either assumes simultaneous recording (e.g., [6-11]) or makes restrictive assumptions about correlation structures (e.g., [12]).

The key insight is that by treating unobserved neurons as latent variables in a linear dynamical system model, the authors can leverage the strong coupling between nearby neurons to infer the full population dynamics. They demonstrate their method on both simulated data (where ground truth is available) and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. Their approach outperforms naive methods that simply average parameters across sessions, and approaches the performance of a "fully observed" model (which would require simultaneous recording of all neurons - impossible in practice).

The paper's greatest strength lies in its conceptual novelty. While previous work has addressed partial observations of neural activity (e.g., [13,15]), none have tackled the specific challenge of inferring correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons in the way the authors propose. This is a genuinely new problem space with significant implications for how we design and interpret neural recording experiments.

The authors provide convincing evidence through both simulations and real data. Their simulated data demonstrates recovery of coupling matrices and noise correlations with high accuracy (correlation 0.92 for noise correlations), while their application to real data shows that their stitching method predicts correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs with correlation 0.84 to the fully observed model, compared to 0.15 for the naive approach. The method's performance is robust across different population sizes and experimental conditions (anesthetized vs. awake).

The simplicity of the linear Gaussian dynamical system model is a virtue here - the authors acknowledge it's a simplification of the complex neural dynamics (which include nonlinear calcium dynamics, etc.), but they demonstrate it's sufficient to make accurate predictions about correlations. This aligns with my preference for bold, conceptually clear approaches over methodological complexity when the core idea is novel.

I find the paper's discussion of experimental design implications particularly compelling. The authors note that some overlap between recorded neuron populations is critical for stitching to work, and that determining the optimal trade-off between overlap and total coverage is an important open problem. This provides concrete guidance for experimentalists and suggests future directions for the field.

The main weaknesses are that the method requires some overlap between recorded populations (which may not always be feasible), and the linear model, while sufficient for their purpose, is a simplification of the actual neural dynamics. However, the paper acknowledges these limitations and demonstrates that the method works well in practice despite these simplifications. The authors also note that more complex models (e.g., incorporating spiking dynamics or calcium dynamics) could potentially improve performance, but they don't need to for their core claim.

This paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a fundamental experimental limitation in neuroscience. It provides a practical method to extract more information from existing neural recording technology, effectively expanding the capabilities of current imaging techniques. The authors successfully demonstrate that their approach can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching that of a fully observed model - a remarkable achievement.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility. The related work section is thorough and clearly distinguishes this contribution from prior research. The results are presented with appropriate statistical validation, and the authors are honest about the method's limitations.

In terms of NIPS criteria:
- **Quality**: The paper is technically sound, with appropriate statistical validation and clear demonstration of the method's capabilities.
- **Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally clear, with a well-structured argument and effective visualizations.
- **Originality**: The core idea of stitching non-simultaneous recordings is genuinely novel and addresses a problem not previously solved in the literature.
- **Significance**: This work has the potential to significantly impact how neural circuit dynamics are studied, enabling researchers to extract more information from existing data and design more efficient experiments.

The paper's bold conceptual contribution and practical significance outweigh its minor methodological limitations. It represents a significant step toward addressing a fundamental challenge in neuroscience, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings. The method is both conceptually elegant and practically useful, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated its effectiveness.

I recommend acceptance. This paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel approach that NIPS values, and it makes a substantial contribution to the field of neural data analysis.
