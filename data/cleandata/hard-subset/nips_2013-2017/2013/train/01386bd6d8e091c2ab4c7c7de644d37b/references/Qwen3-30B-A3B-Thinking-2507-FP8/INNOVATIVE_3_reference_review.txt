

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a novel computational method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors address a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: current imaging techniques can only record a small fraction of neurons in a circuit (e.g., hundreds out of thousands), but they demonstrate that by recording different subsets of neurons in separate sessions, one can infer the dynamics of the complete circuit.

The core insight is that strong coupling between neurons in a circuit allows a latent dynamical system model to "stitch" together the partial observations. The authors formulate the problem as fitting a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations, where the missing data corresponds to neurons not recorded in a particular session. They use a standard EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing to estimate the model parameters, which include the coupling matrix A (representing functional connectivity), stimulus response matrix B, and noise parameters.

The authors validate their approach through both simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. In simulations, they show that their method accurately recovers the coupling matrix, including off-diagonal blocks corresponding to non-simultaneously recorded neurons, with correlation coefficients of 0.73 for cross-couplings (vs. 0.75 for the naive approach on simultaneously recorded pairs). Crucially, their method successfully predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with correlation 0.92 (vs. 0.0 for the naive approach). In real data, they demonstrate similar performance, with their stitching method predicting correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs with correlation 0.84 compared to 0.15 for the naive approach.

**Strengths:**
- **High novelty**: This is a genuinely novel approach to a fundamental problem in neuroscience. While previous work has addressed neural circuit analysis from partial observations, none have proposed this "stitching" approach to infer dynamics across non-simultaneous recordings without strong assumptions.
- **Significant potential impact**: The method could dramatically expand the scope of neural circuit analysis with current technology, allowing researchers to infer dynamics of much larger populations than currently possible.
- **Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors correctly connect their method to the theoretical possibility of reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations, providing a solid conceptual basis.
- **Clear demonstration**: The paper provides both synthetic and real data validation showing the method works as claimed, with appropriate comparisons to naive baselines.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Simplistic model**: The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this and suggest more complex models could improve performance, the paper doesn't explore these alternatives in depth.
- **Limited real-world validation**: The method is tested on only two datasets, and the paper doesn't address how well it would generalize to different brain regions, species, or experimental paradigms.
- **Scalability concerns**: The paper doesn't thoroughly explore how the method would perform with more than two recording sessions or with larger populations, which would be essential for practical application.
- **Overlooked limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss the critical assumption of strong coupling between neurons across the recorded subsets, which may not hold for all neural circuits.

**Evaluation:**

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound for its core claim. The model is well-formulated, the EM algorithm is appropriately applied, and the validation against appropriate baselines is convincing. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their linear Gaussian model, which shows appropriate scientific honesty.

*Clarity*: The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The main idea is easy to grasp, and the experimental results are well-presented. The mathematical formulation is precise but not overly complex.

*Originality*: This is a highly original contribution. The core idea of "stitching" non-simultaneous recordings to infer joint dynamics represents a fundamentally new approach to a longstanding problem in neuroscience. It's a clear conceptual advance over previous work on partial observations.

*Significance*: The significance of this work is substantial. It addresses a major practical limitation in neuroscience experiments and offers a way to extract more information from existing data. With current imaging technology limited to recording only small fractions of neural circuits, this method could enable researchers to study much larger populations than previously possible.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. While the linear Gaussian model is a simplification, the core idea is so novel and promising that it justifies the methodological choices. The authors have demonstrated the approach works on both simulated and real data, providing a clear path for future work to incorporate more sophisticated neural models. This is precisely the kind of bold, novel approach that advances the field—addressing a real problem with a creative solution, rather than making incremental improvements to existing methods.

The paper is well-positioned to influence both neuroscience and machine learning communities. For neuroscientists, it offers a practical tool to extract more information from current imaging data. For machine learning researchers, it presents a compelling application of latent dynamical systems to a domain with significant real-world impact. The authors have clearly articulated the potential of this approach, and their results provide a solid foundation for further development.

I note that the paper makes a clear distinction between their approach and previous work on partial observations (such as the monotonically related noise correlations assumption in [12]), which is important for establishing novelty. The method also has clear theoretical grounding in system identification literature, which the authors appropriately reference.

This paper represents a significant conceptual advance that could change how neural circuit dynamics are analyzed. I believe it would be a valuable contribution to NIPS, both for its technical novelty and its potential to bridge neuroscience and machine learning.
