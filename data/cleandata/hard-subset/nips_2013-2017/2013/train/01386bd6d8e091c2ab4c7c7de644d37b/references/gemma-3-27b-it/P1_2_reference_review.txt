## Peer Review for "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for “stitching” together data from multiple, partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics and correlations of a larger population than can be observed simultaneously with current technology. The authors propose a latent dynamical system (LDS) approach, specifically a linear Gaussian model, and demonstrate its effectiveness both on simulated data and real calcium imaging recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex. The key finding is that even with incomplete observations, the method accurately predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, approaching the performance of a hypothetical “fully observed” model. This addresses a crucial limitation in studying large neural circuits, particularly in areas like the neocortex where current imaging techniques fall short of capturing entire network activity.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The paper appropriately situates itself within the existing literature on inferring functional connectivity from neural data. It acknowledges related approaches like detailed biophysical models, information-theoretic methods, and latent variable models (GLMs). However, it clearly differentiates itself by focusing on the novel challenge of inferring connections *between* neurons observed in *separate* recording sessions. It correctly identifies and distinguishes itself from works like [12] which relies on strong stimulus-correlation assumptions, and [13] which does not scale to the populations of interest. The authors accurately portray their contribution as filling a gap in the existing methodologies, allowing for a more complete picture of neural circuit dynamics.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important problem:** The inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations is a significant bottleneck in neuroscience. This paper directly tackles this issue with a practical and well-motivated approach.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:** Framing the problem as a latent dynamical system is a natural and mathematically sound approach. The use of the EM algorithm provides a principled method for parameter estimation.
* **Strong empirical validation:** The method demonstrates excellent performance on simulated data, allowing for a rigorous assessment of its ability to recover ground truth parameters and correlations.  Crucially, the application to real calcium imaging data from mouse cortex further validates its applicability and usefulness.
* **Clear comparison to benchmarks:**  The comparison to both an ideal "fully observed" model and a naive approach provides a clear assessment of the method's performance and demonstrates its substantial improvement over simpler alternatives.
* **Well-written and organized:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the methodology and results.
* **Reproducibility:** The detailed description of the methods, including the specific datasets used, enhances the potential for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model simplicity:** The use of a linear Gaussian LDS is a significant simplification of the underlying neural dynamics. While justified for tractability, it may limit the applicability of the method to more complex circuits exhibiting non-linear or non-Gaussian behavior. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but a brief discussion of potential extensions to more complex models would be valuable.
* **Overlap requirement:** The requirement for overlap between recording sessions (60% in the experimental evaluation) is a practical constraint.  The performance with lower overlap (e.g., 50% as mentioned) could be explored in more detail to understand the trade-offs between coverage and accuracy.
* **Parameter tuning:** The paper does not provide a detailed discussion of the parameter tuning process (e.g., choosing the covariance matrix Q, or parameters of the EM algorithm). A more thorough analysis of sensitivity to parameter choices would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
* **Computational cost:** While the authors mention using online-EM, a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the method, particularly for very large populations, would be beneficial.
* **Limited exploration of stimulus dependence:** While the stimulus is included in the model, the analysis focuses primarily on noise correlations. Exploring how the stimulus influences the stitched dynamics and correlations could provide further insights.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and promising paper that addresses a critical challenge in neural circuit analysis. The proposed method is theoretically sound, empirically validated, and well-presented. While the reliance on a simplified model and the overlap requirement are limitations, the demonstrated ability to accurately predict noise correlations in real data is a significant achievement.  

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and challenging problem in neuroscience.
* Presents a novel and principled solution based on a solid theoretical framework.
* Demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real data.
* Provides a clear and comprehensive comparison to relevant benchmarks.
* Is well-written and organized, making it accessible to a broad audience.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on a simplified linear Gaussian model, which may limit its applicability to more complex circuits.
* Requires a significant degree of overlap between recording sessions.
* Lacks a detailed discussion of parameter tuning and computational complexity.



**Rating:**  Strong Accept (8/10)

**Recommendations:**

* The authors should briefly discuss potential avenues for extending the model to incorporate non-linearities and non-Gaussianity.
* A more detailed analysis of the impact of overlap on performance would be valuable.
* Including a discussion of computational cost and scalability would strengthen the paper.
* A short section discussing the potential for applying this method to other neural recording modalities (e.g., electrophysiology) would broaden its appeal.
