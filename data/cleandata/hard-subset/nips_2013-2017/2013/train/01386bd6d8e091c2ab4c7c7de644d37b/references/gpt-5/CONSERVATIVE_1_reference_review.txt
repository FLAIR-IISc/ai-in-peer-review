Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations when experiments can only image subsets of neurons at a time. The authors propose “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same circuit by fitting a single linear-Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with session-specific selection matrices Ck and shared parameters (A, B, Q, R). They learn parameters via EM with Kalman smoothing, and evaluate on (i) simulated linear networks and (ii) two calcium imaging datasets using a “fictional” split of simultaneously recorded neurons into overlapping subsets to emulate multiple sessions. They report that the stitched model recovers cross-session couplings and predicts pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously “recorded” neurons substantially better than a naive baseline and close to a “fully observed” upper bound fitted to the full simultaneous data.

This approach essentially applies established system identification for LDS with missing data, leveraging standard EM/Kalman smoothing and session-specific observation matrices. Related work includes state-space models and subspace identification for partially observed systems (e.g., Katayama; Baum–Petrie EM; NIPS’08 Pillow & Latham on partially observed spiking populations; FA/GPFA variants), as well as connectivity inference from calcium (e.g., Mishchenko et al.). The paper’s contribution is to frame and evaluate this “stitching” use case within LDS for calcium imaging.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and timely experimental limitation; the stitching problem is important for current imaging regimes.
- Clear formulation as an LDS with missing observations; implementation builds on well-understood and tractable methods (EM + Kalman smoothing).
- Sensible evaluation protocol with synthetic data (ground truth) and real data under a constructed split; uses a naive baseline and a “fully observed” model as an upper bound.
- Predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs is a useful target metric; reported improvements over naive baseline are substantial.
- Writing is generally clear, and the authors acknowledge limitations and future extensions.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/rigor
- The core methodology is standard LDS with missing data; no new inference or identifiability results are provided. The intuition that cross-session couplings become identifiable with multiple views and overlap is reasonable, but the paper does not formalize conditions (e.g., observability/controllability, overlap fraction, stimulus richness) or provide guarantees. This weakens claims beyond the demonstrated settings.
- Real-data validation uses a “fictional” split of a single simultaneous recording. This circumvents critical issues that arise in true non-simultaneous sessions: nonstationarity across time, behavioral state/neuromodulatory differences, photobleaching, motion, slow drifts, and stimulus timing inconsistencies. The authors explicitly note they failed without overlap and required c=60% overlap—this undermines the stated motivation of stitching different fields of view where overlap may be minimal.
- Model misspecification is substantial for calcium imaging: diagonal process noise Q, diagonal measurement noise R, linear dynamics, and direct modeling of fluorescence without calcium/spike generative structure. While simplicity has merits, the paper does not quantify robustness to misspecification (e.g., correlated process noise/common input, nonlinear dynamics, slow calcium kinetics).
- The upper bound (“fully observed” model) is itself an LDS fit; comparing the stitched LDS to a fully observed LDS can conflate modeling error with stitching ability. Additionally, correlation metrics reported (e.g., 0.84) are with respect to this surrogate, not ground truth, making the real-data claims less definitive.
- Baselines are weak. The naive approach (fit separate LDS and zero cross-block couplings) is a straw man. Absent are more competitive baselines: (i) low-rank latent LDS/GPFA with shared latent space across sessions anchored by overlap; (ii) subspace identification/CCA-based alignment across views; (iii) sparse+low-rank dynamical models; (iv) factor analysis or probabilistic CCA on concatenated sessions.
- Reproducibility details are thin: no hyperparameter selection, regularization strength, convergence/stability diagnostics, or complexity/scalability analysis. The number of free parameters in dense A for N=100–200 is large; overfitting controls beyond “online EM” are not demonstrated.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but several critical details are underspecified (preprocessing, stimulus construction and alignment, normalization, EM initialization, choice of T, handling of overlapping neurons during training, and criteria for stationarity checks).
- The definition and estimation of noise correlations depend on the accuracy of B and stimulus modeling; this is not probed (e.g., with mis-specified or weak stimuli, as in the simulation).

Originality
- The “stitching” idea applied to calcium data is of interest, but methodologically the paper mainly packages standard LDS-with-missing-observations and EM. The novelty lies more in framing than in algorithmic development. Related prior work on partially observed neural populations and subspace methods should be discussed more deeply to delineate differences.

Significance
- If validated on truly non-simultaneous sessions with realistic nonstationarities and small overlaps, this could be practically valuable. As presented, the empirical evidence is limited to a constructed scenario and one simulation; generalization to real multi-session datasets is unproven. The requirement for substantial overlap and the sensitivity to misspecification are open concerns.

Suggestions/questions for the authors
- Provide experiments on genuinely non-simultaneous sessions (e.g., separate imaging blocks or days) with controlled overlap, and quantify performance degradation with overlap fraction, stimulus mismatch, and state drift.
- Add stronger baselines: low-rank LDS/GPFA with shared latent space, multiset CCA/subspace ID, and sparse+low-rank dynamical models; include L1 or structured sparsity on A (as hinted) in the main results with cross-validated regularization.
- Analyze identifiability and sample complexity: what overlap and stimulus richness are sufficient? How do errors scale with N and T? Provide ablations on Q/R structure (allow correlated process noise, model low-dimensional common input).
- Stress-test robustness to model mismatch (nonlinear dynamics, calcium kinetics) via simulations with realistic fluorescence generative models.
- Report computational complexity, wall-clock times, and scaling behavior; discuss practicality for N>500.
- Clarify all preprocessing and training details for reproducibility; consider releasing code.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important problem with a simple, tractable framework.
- Clear empirical improvements over a naive baseline; promising results on real data splits.
- Could stimulate further work on multi-session population modeling.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond applying standard LDS/EM to a multi-session setting.
- Validation does not use true non-simultaneous recordings; dependence on large overlap is concerning.
- Weak baselines and limited analysis of robustness/identifiability/overfitting.
- Strong claims about predicting noise correlations are not fully substantiated under realistic conditions.

Recommendation
Leaning to reject. The problem is important and the approach is sensible, but the contribution is primarily an application of established methods, and the empirical validation falls short of demonstrating robustness and practical utility in realistic multi-session settings. Strengthening with true multi-session data, stronger baselines, and analysis of conditions under which stitching is reliable would substantially improve the paper.
