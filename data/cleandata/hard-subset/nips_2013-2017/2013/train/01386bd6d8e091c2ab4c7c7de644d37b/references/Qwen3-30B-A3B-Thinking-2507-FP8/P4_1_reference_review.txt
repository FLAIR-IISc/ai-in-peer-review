

This paper presents a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The authors address a significant limitation in current neural recording technology, where imaging techniques can only capture a small fraction of neurons in large circuits (e.g., hundreds of neurons out of thousands in cortical circuits). The core idea is to model the population dynamics as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where each recording session provides partial observations of the same underlying neural population. The method uses an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing to estimate the model parameters, allowing prediction of correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs.

The paper situates its work within the broader context of neural data analysis, correctly noting that previous approaches either focus on inferring connectivity from simultaneous recordings [6,7,8,9,10,11] or address sub-sampled observations in different contexts [12,13,14,15], but none tackle the specific problem of stitching non-simultaneous recordings. The authors acknowledge that [12] requires strong assumptions about noise correlations, [13] doesn't scale to large populations, and [14] deals with dendritic trees where interpolation is possible, which differs from their application.

**Strengths:**
The paper demonstrates strong technical soundness. The method is well-founded in statistical theory (LDS with missing data), and the implementation details are clear. The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The comparison against a meaningful upper bound ("fully observed" model) and a naive baseline provides convincing evidence of the method's superiority. The results show that the stitching method accurately recovers noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons (correlation 0.92 on synthetic data, 0.84 on real data), substantially outperforming the naive approach (correlation 0.15 on real data). The paper also demonstrates the method's ability to predict unobserved neural activity with significant accuracy (average correlation 0.70 vs. upper bound 0.82).

The paper is clearly written with well-organized sections and effective figures that illustrate key findings. The mathematical formulation is precise, and the authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility. The significance of the problem is well-justified, as the method enables more complete characterization of neural circuits with current technology, which could help build better data-driven models of cortical circuits.

**Weaknesses:**
The linear Gaussian model is a simplification of neural dynamics, though the authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation. The paper doesn't fully explore the scalability of the method to very large populations (e.g., thousands of neurons), which is the primary motivation for their approach. While they mention that the method can generalize to more than two populations, they don't demonstrate this empirically. The requirement for some overlap between recording sessions (they found c = 60% overlap worked better than c = 50% with no overlap) is noted but not thoroughly analyzed in terms of minimal overlap requirements. The paper also doesn't explore failure modes in detail (e.g., what happens when the coupling between populations is weak).

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper addresses a significant practical limitation in neuroscience with a novel statistical approach.
- The method is technically sound, with thorough validation on both synthetic and real data.
- The results demonstrate clear improvement over existing approaches, with performance approaching theoretical upper bounds.
- The work has high potential impact for neuroscience, enabling more complete characterization of neural circuits with current imaging technology.
- The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with sufficient detail for reproducibility.

*Con:*
- The linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification of neural dynamics.
- The scalability to very large populations (thousands of neurons) isn't fully demonstrated.
- The requirement for some overlap between recording sessions isn't thoroughly analyzed.
- The method's applicability to very weakly coupled populations isn't explored.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a strong contribution to the intersection of computational neuroscience and machine learning. The problem addressed is important and well-motivated, and the method is technically sound with compelling empirical results. While the linear Gaussian model is a simplification, the authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation and demonstrate that even this simple model makes accurate predictions. The results on real data are particularly convincing, showing the method's practical utility. The paper is well-written and clearly demonstrates the novelty of their approach compared to previous work.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach to stitching non-simultaneous neural recordings (Originality), and addresses a significant problem with practical implications for neuroscience (Significance). The work is directly relevant to NIPS, as it combines statistical modeling with applications in neural data analysis. While there are limitations to the current approach, they are well-acknowledged by the authors and don't undermine the paper's core contribution. The method has the potential to significantly impact how researchers analyze neural population data with current imaging technology.
