This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring neural population dynamics from incomplete recordings, a common limitation in experimental neuroscience. The authors propose a statistical “stitching” method based on a latent dynamical system to combine information from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea – leveraging the strong coupling between nearby neurons – is intuitively appealing and potentially valuable, particularly given the constraints of current imaging technology. The method is demonstrated on both simulated and real calcium imaging data, showing promising results in predicting noise correlations and reconstructing network couplings.

However, while the concept is interesting, the paper suffers from several weaknesses that raise concerns about its overall significance and readiness for NIPS. My primary concern is the reliance on a very simple linear Gaussian dynamical system. While the authors acknowledge this simplification, they don’t fully address how the limitations of this model (e.g., inability to capture non-linearities or non-Gaussian statistics prevalent in neural data) impact the robustness and generalizability of their approach. The claim of predicting “correlated dynamics” feels overstated given this linear framework. It’s not clear if the observed performance is truly attributable to the stitching method, or simply a consequence of the linear model’s ability to capture some underlying linear relationships in the data. 

The presentation of results, while generally clear, lacks sufficient detail and comparative analysis. The simulation results are well presented but the use of a fully observed model as an upper bound, while reasonable, isn’t rigorously justified. A more thorough comparison to established dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., PCA, ICA) or other methods for handling missing data would be beneficial. In the experimental section, the "fictional imaging scenario" feels contrived and the reliance on a 60% overlap between imaging sessions seems arbitrary. It's unclear why this overlap is necessary for reasonable performance, and a sensitivity analysis exploring different overlap percentages would be valuable. The authors state that “without any overlapping neurons” performance failed, yet don't delve into *why* this is the case, suggesting a fundamental limitation that needs to be addressed. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a clear discussion of computational cost and scalability. While the authors mention using online-EM for efficiency, they don’t provide any quantitative assessment of runtime or memory requirements, particularly for larger network sizes. This is crucial given the stated goal of applying this method to circuits containing thousands of neurons.

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, but feels a bit defensive. The authors spend considerable effort dismissing existing approaches, often pointing out limitations that are not necessarily relevant to their specific application. A more nuanced discussion of how their method complements or extends existing techniques would be more constructive.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and practically relevant problem in neuroscience.
* Presents a conceptually simple and intuitive approach.
* Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.
* Potential to leverage existing experimental data more effectively.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Heavy reliance on a simplistic linear Gaussian model.
* Limited comparative analysis against established methods.
* Lack of detailed scalability analysis.
* Contrived experimental setup with an unexplained dependency on overlap.
* Overstated claims regarding the ability to infer “correlated dynamics” given the model limitations.
* Defensive tone in the related work section.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents an interesting idea, but its current form is not sufficiently robust or significant for NIPS. The reliance on a highly simplified model and the lack of thorough comparative analysis raise serious concerns about the generalizability and practical utility of the proposed method. While the initial results are promising, further work is needed to address the limitations outlined above and demonstrate a clear advantage over existing techniques. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to address these concerns in a revised manuscript. The authors should focus on either justifying the use of the linear Gaussian model or exploring more sophisticated, biologically plausible alternatives. A more rigorous evaluation of scalability and computational cost is also essential.
