**Summary of the Paper**

This paper addresses the significant experimental challenge of inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population when only subsets of neurons can be recorded at any given time. The authors propose a "stitching" method that models the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Data from multiple partial recording sessions, which may have some overlapping neurons, are treated as incomplete observations of this unified system. The model parameters are estimated using an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm. The primary claim is that this approach can successfully infer functional couplings and, more robustly, noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is validated on simulated data with known ground truth and on two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex using a "fictional" scenario where a fully-observed recording is artificially split into partial sessions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem in systems neuroscience. The limited field-of-view of current recording technologies is a major bottleneck, and a robust method for combining partial recordings could significantly advance our ability to model large-scale circuit dynamics.

2.  **Principled Approach:** The formulation of the problem within the well-established framework of latent dynamical systems with missing data is elegant and statistically sound. Using an LDS allows for a tractable solution via the EM algorithm and Kalman smoothing, providing a clear and interpretable model of population dynamics.

3.  **Thoughtful Evaluation:** The use of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is a clever way to benchmark the method. By creating upper ("fully-observed") and lower ("naive") performance bounds, the authors provide a clear context for interpreting their results in the absence of ground truth. This demonstrates a commitment to rigorous evaluation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Overly Simplistic Model:** The choice of a linear-Gaussian dynamical system is a major concern. Neural activity, particularly spiking, is fundamentally nonlinear and non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this, they justify the model by its ability to predict correlations. However, calcium imaging signals themselves are a nonlinear function of spike trains. The success of the LDS may be due to the slow, low-pass filtered nature of the GCaMP6s signal, which may appear more Gaussian than the underlying spikes. It is unclear if this approach would generalize to electrophysiological recordings or faster calcium indicators. The paper lacks a discussion of the limitations imposed by this linear-Gaussian assumption and does not explore how the method might perform with more biophysically plausible, albeit less tractable, models (e.g., latent GLMs).

2.  **Unjustified Stationarity Assumption:** The core of the method relies on the assumption that the underlying circuit dynamics (i.e., the coupling matrix A) are stationary across different recording sessions. In a real experimental setting, these sessions could be separated by significant amounts of time (minutes to hours, or even days). It is highly questionable whether the functional connectivity of a circuit, especially in an awake, behaving animal, would remain constant over such periods. The paper's validation, which splits a single continuous recording, completely sidesteps this critical issue. The performance in this idealized "fictional" scenario may be an optimistic overestimation of how the method would perform on genuinely separate recordings, where non-stationarity is a major confound. This assumption needs to be explicitly stated and its potential impact thoroughly discussed, if not tested.

3.  **Dependence on Overlapping Neurons:** The authors state that they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" on the real data. This is a crucial practical limitation. The method's success seems to hinge on having a set of anchor neurons recorded in multiple sessions to align the latent dynamics. The paper does not systematically investigate how performance scales with the degree of overlap. This is presented as an open question for experimental design, but it is a central weakness of the proposed method that limits its immediate applicability. An analysis of the sensitivity to the number of overlapping neurons would have significantly strengthened the paper.

4.  **Parameter Identifiability Issues:** The results show that while noise correlations are recovered well, the estimates of the underlying coupling parameters (the matrix A) are less accurate and degrade with increasing population size (Figure 4, rightmost panel). The authors suggest this may be due to "near degeneracy," which points to potential identifiability or overfitting problems. If the goal is to build data-driven models of circuits, the inability to reliably recover the underlying coupling parameters is a significant drawback. The paper should have delved deeper into why parameter estimation fails while correlation prediction succeeds.

5.  **Limited Novelty of Method:** The technical core of the paper is the application of the standard EM algorithm for an LDS with missing data, a well-known technique in the state-space modeling literature (which the authors appropriately cite). The novelty lies entirely in its application to this specific neuroscience problem. While this is a valuable contribution, the algorithmic novelty is minimal.

**Summary of Review**

The paper presents an interesting and potentially useful application of a standard statistical model to an important problem in neuroscience. The idea of "stitching" partial recordings is appealing, and the results on predicting noise correlations in a controlled, fictional scenario are promising.

However, the work is undermined by its reliance on a simplistic linear-Gaussian model and, more critically, by the untested and likely violated assumption of circuit stationarity between recording sessions. The validation presented is not representative of a real-world use case where recordings are temporally separated. Furthermore, the practical requirement for overlapping neurons and the degradation of parameter estimates with population size suggest significant limitations that are not fully explored.

While the problem is significant, the proposed solution in its current form seems more like a proof-of-concept on idealized data rather than a robust, validated method ready for widespread application. The claims should be tempered by a more thorough discussion of the model's strong assumptions and limitations.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a very important and practical problem in experimental neuroscience.
*   The proposed framework is simple, principled, and interpretable.
*   The validation strategy is sound and provides clear upper and lower bounds on performance.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Relies on a linear-Gaussian model that is a poor match for the underlying neural processes.
*   Makes a critical, unverified assumption of stationarity across recording sessions, which is unlikely to hold in practice. The experimental validation does not test this.
*   The method requires an unspecified amount of neuron overlap between sessions to function, limiting its generality.
*   Fails to robustly recover model parameters, questioning its utility for building mechanistic circuit models.

**Decision:**
Based on the significant and unaddressed methodological weaknesses, particularly the stationarity assumption and the limitations of the validation scenario, I lean towards rejecting this paper. The gap between what has been demonstrated and what would be required for a truly useful tool is too large.
