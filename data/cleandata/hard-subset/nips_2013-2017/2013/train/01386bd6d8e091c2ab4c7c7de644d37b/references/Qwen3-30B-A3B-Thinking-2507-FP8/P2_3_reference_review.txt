

# Review: Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

This paper presents a novel statistical method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including correlations between neurons not recorded simultaneously. The authors address a significant challenge in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record all neurons in large neural circuits due to experimental limitations. Their approach models the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework that can be fit using standard expectation-maximization (EM) with Kalman smoothing.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors provide a clear theoretical foundation for their approach, demonstrating how a latent dynamical system model can recover parameters for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs. Their experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The comparison to a naive baseline (averaging parameters across sessions) and a "fully observed" upper bound model provides a strong benchmark for evaluating performance.

The method's performance is compelling: in simulations, the stitching method recovers noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs with correlation 0.92 with ground truth, significantly outperforming the naive approach (0.15 correlation). In real data, the stitching method predicts correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs with correlation 0.84 with the fully observed model, while the naive approach achieves only 0.15. The authors also demonstrate the method's ability to predict unobserved neural activity with high accuracy (0.70 correlation with ground truth vs. 0.23 for naive approach).

The paper appropriately acknowledges limitations, including the linear Gaussian assumption, the need for some overlap between recorded populations, and the potential for parameter degeneracy in larger systems. This careful evaluation of strengths and weaknesses enhances the paper's credibility.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the methods section provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility, and the results section presents findings in a logical manner. The authors use figures effectively to illustrate key concepts (e.g., Figure 2 showing recovery of coupling matrices in simulations, Figure 3 demonstrating results on real data).

Minor improvements could be made: a brief explanation of why the linear Gaussian assumption is reasonable despite known non-linearities in neural dynamics would strengthen the paper. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could elaborate on why their simplified model still yields accurate predictions.

## Originality

The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by addressing a specific gap in existing methods. Previous approaches either assume simultaneous recordings [6-11] or make strong assumptions about noise correlations [12] or don't scale to large populations [13]. The authors correctly identify that their method differs from prior work by enabling inference of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons without restrictive assumptions.

This is a novel application of latent dynamical systems to a specific problem in neuroscience data analysis, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their approach differs from previous work. The related work section is comprehensive and well-justified.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial for neuroscience. Current imaging technologies can only record a fraction of neurons in a circuit, but this method allows researchers to make more effective use of existing data. The authors demonstrate that their method can predict noise correlations (a key metric in neural circuit function) between neurons never recorded simultaneously, which is impossible with existing approaches.

This work could have immediate impact on how neuroscience experiments are designed and analyzed. It provides a practical tool to expand the effective population size that can be studied with current imaging technology, potentially enabling researchers to study larger neural circuits without requiring new hardware. The authors appropriately note that while simultaneous recording of all neurons would be ideal, there are fundamental physical limits to this, making their approach highly valuable.

## Strengths and Weaknesses Summary

**Strengths:**
- Addresses a significant, practical problem in neuroscience
- Technically sound method with strong experimental validation
- Clear comparison to appropriate baselines
- Well-written and clearly organized
- Demonstrates real-world applicability with mouse somatosensory cortex data

**Weaknesses:**
- The linear Gaussian assumption is a significant simplification of neural dynamics
- Limited exploration of how the method scales to more than two recording sessions
- Insufficient discussion of sensitivity to the amount of overlap between recorded populations
- No exploration of how the method might handle non-Gaussian noise or non-linear dynamics

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches. The problem addressed is important for neuroscience, and the solution has clear practical implications for how researchers can make better use of existing imaging data.

The paper meets the quality bar for NIPS with strong theoretical and experimental validation. While the linear Gaussian assumption is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and demonstrate that their simple model still provides accurate predictions. The significance of the work is high, as it addresses a genuine bottleneck in neuroscience data analysis.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS. The paper's contribution is novel, well-executed, and has the potential to make a meaningful impact on how neural circuit dynamics are studied.
