Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint dynamics and noise correlations of a large neural circuit when only partial subsets of neurons can be imaged at any one time. The authors propose “stitching” multiple partially overlapping recordings by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with session-specific observation masks Ck (known neuron identities), shared dynamics (A,B,Q), and Gaussian measurement noise (R). Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant), and the approach is evaluated on simulated data and two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1).

The work is well grounded in classical system identification, Kalman EM, and latent variable modeling with missing data (e.g., Dempster et al. 1977; subspace methods; identifiability up to invertible transforms). In neuroscience, it connects to PLDS/GPFA-style latent dynamics for populations (e.g., Macke et al. 2012; Pillow et al. 2008) and calcium imaging connectivity inference (Mishchenko et al. 2011). The novelty lies less in the model class and more in the multi-session “stitching” setting and in demonstrating accurate prediction of noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs.

Strengths
- Clear, practical framing of an important problem with immediate relevance to 2-photon imaging.
- Simple, scalable instantiation (LDS with missing observations) and standard inference (EM/Kalman smoothing) make the method accessible.
- Convincing synthetic demonstrations: recovery of cross-session couplings and accurate prediction of non-simultaneous noise correlations; sensible upper/lower bound comparisons.
- Real data results on awake/anesthetized mouse S1: stitched model outperforms a naive baseline and approaches a “fully observed” upper bound for noise correlations and cross-prediction.
- Useful empirical insights on the importance of overlapping cells across sessions and the trade-off with coverage.

Weaknesses
- Methodological originality is modest. The core is a standard LDS with missing data; the contribution is largely application and careful evaluation rather than a new algorithmic framework.
- Identifiability and conditions for successful stitching are only informally discussed. The paper shows empirically that overlap helps and that no-overlap often fails, but does not provide conditions (e.g., observability/controllability, stimulus richness, minimum overlap) under which cross-session alignment becomes unique.
- Strong modeling simplifications: linear Gaussian dynamics on fluorescence with diagonal Q and R, no calcium/spike generative model, no stability constraints on A, and minimal regularization. This can bias estimates and overfit, especially as N grows.
- Validation on “fictional” splits of simultaneously recorded datasets may overstate robustness: same-day stationarity, absence of inter-session drift, and known neuron identities are assumed. The real-world setting (days apart, behavioral nonstationarity, imperfect registration) is not tested.
- Limited baselines. The “naive” model is weak; missing comparisons include low-rank latent dynamical models shared across sessions (e.g., GPFA/PLDS with session-specific Ck), subspace/system-ID methods with missing data, and sparse+low-rank decompositions that are standard in population modeling.
- Clarity/notation: a few typos in the observation model (Eq. 2; “d+ t”); some index notation in the M-step is hard to parse; stability assumptions for A are not stated.

Quality
Technically sound within the LDS framework; inference is standard and implemented carefully. The synthetic experiments are well designed, and the real data analyses are thorough given the fictional split. However, stronger baselines, uncertainty quantification, and stress tests (nonstationarity, lower SNR) would strengthen the empirical case. The assumption of diagonal Q is restrictive; correlated innovations could be confounded with A.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The problem statement, model, and evaluation setup are clear. Please fix typos in Eq. (2) and the M-step, state stability constraints on A, and clarify how Ck is constructed and neuron identities are aligned across sessions.

Originality
Moderate. The key novelty is the stitching application and demonstrating that cross-session noise correlations can be recovered. The underlying statistical machinery and EM with missing observations are well established.

Significance
Potentially impactful for calcium imaging experimental design and analysis, especially in settings where full simultaneous coverage is infeasible. The paper could motivate optimized scanning strategies and multi-session analyses. The current scope is promising but would benefit from stronger theoretical and empirical grounding regarding when stitching is guaranteed to work.

Specific suggestions
- Provide theoretical or at least formal conditions for identifiability/alignment across sessions: role of overlap size, stimulus rank/persistence of excitation, observability/controllability, and stability of A. Even a proposition with sufficient conditions would help.
- Expand baselines: compare to GPFA/PLDS with shared latent dynamics and session-specific loadings, and to subspace ID with missing observations. Include sparse and sparse+low-rank priors on A, and report their effect on couplings and correlation prediction.
- Enforce and report stability constraints on A (spectral radius < 1) during EM; quantify sensitivity to this constraint.
- Test robustness to realistic violations: session-to-session drift in B and A, changes in behavioral state, and imperfect neuron registration. Report sensitivity to SNR, stimulus richness, and calcium time constants.
- Consider incorporating a calcium forward model or deconvolved spikes; at minimum, report whether results change with simple deconvolution.
- Quantify uncertainty: e.g., bootstrap CIs on predicted correlations and cross-prediction accuracy.
- Provide runtime/scaling behavior with N and K and discuss memory/compute requirements.

Questions for authors
- What minimum overlap fraction is typically needed, and how does this scale with N and stimulus dimensionality?
- Do results hold when the two “sessions” are temporally separated by strong state changes (e.g., different anesthesia depth or active vs. quiescent epochs)?
- How sensitive are results to the diagonal Q assumption? Can a low-rank Q capture common input without confounding A?

Pro/Con arguments
Pros:
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem.
- Simple, reproducible method; solid synthetic and real data results.
- Clear gains over naive baselines; offers actionable insights for experimental design.

Cons:
- Limited methodological novelty.
- Missing theoretical guarantees/conditions; reliance on overlap without formal treatment.
- Simplifying assumptions and limited baselines; validation limited to fictional splits.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is a solid and useful application paper with promising results, but it lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical/empirical depth to meet the NeurIPS bar. Addressing the suggested analyses (theory of identifiability/overlap, stronger baselines and constraints, robustness to nonstationarities) would make it a strong submission. Even if not accepted, the work is valuable and likely to be impactful to practitioners.
