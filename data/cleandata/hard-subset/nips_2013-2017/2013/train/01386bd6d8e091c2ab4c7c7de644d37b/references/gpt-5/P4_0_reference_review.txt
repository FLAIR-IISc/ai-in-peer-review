Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper addresses the problem of inferring joint neural population dynamics from multiple partial, non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit. The key idea is to “stitch” together sessions by fitting a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) whose latent state x_t represents the fluorescence activity of all N neurons, with session-specific observation matrices Ck that select the subset recorded in session k. Dynamics are x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t with diagonal Q, and observations y_t^k = Ck(x_t + d) + ε_t with diagonal R. Parameters are learned via EM (online EM for initialization, then batch EM). The model leverages overlap across sessions (shared neurons) to align the latent state and recover cross-couplings among neurons never observed simultaneously.

On simulated data with known ground truth, the stitched model recovers coupling parameters (higher accuracy for simultaneously observed pairs; substantial recovery for non-simultaneous pairs) and predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs (r ≈ 0.92). It also predicts unobserved activity substantially better than a naive baseline that fits separate models and sets cross-session couplings to zero. On two in vivo 2-photon calcium datasets (mouse S1, anesthetized and awake), the authors create a “fictional imaging scenario” by splitting a single simultaneous dataset into two overlapping subsets (typically 60% overlap), hold out 20% for testing, and compare stitched LDS to a “fully observed” LDS (upper bound) and a naive baseline (lower bound). The stitched model matches pairwise correlation structure for non-simultaneous pairs well (e.g., r ≈ 0.84 vs. fully observed), and yields reasonable but weaker agreement for couplings (r ≈ 0.38 for non-simultaneous blocks). The paper contrasts with prior work on connectivity inference from calcium or spikes [6–11], which require simultaneous observations, and with methods for partial observations [12–15], which either rely on restrictive assumptions, do not scale, or address different settings. The approach is related to latent GLMs [13] and common-noise LDS models [17,18], but specifically tackles cross-session stitching with known Ck.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple formulation that reuses well-understood LDS/EM machinery with session-specific observation matrices; computationally tractable and scalable to ~100–200 neurons.
- Addresses a practically important problem in 2-photon imaging: predicting noise correlations and interactions across neurons not recorded simultaneously.
- Systematic evaluations on synthetic and real datasets with upper/lower bounds; strong performance on predicting pairwise correlation structure and cross-predicting activity.
- Transparent discussion of modeling choices and limitations (e.g., linear-Gaussian approximation, need for overlap, potential extensions with additional latent dimensions).

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation on real data uses “fictional” splits within a single recording. Thus, the method is not tested under genuine cross-session variability (drift, arousal/state changes, bleaching, experimental nonstationarity), yet the approach assumes shared A, B across sessions.
- Dependence on overlap: performance without overlap was poor; the paper states “some overlap was critical” but provides no quantitative analysis of how much overlap is required or how performance scales with overlap and coverage, limiting guidance for experimental design.
- Modeling assumptions are restrictive: diagonal Q and R, linear dynamics directly on fluorescence without calcium/spike measurement models. The impact of misspecification on recovered correlations and couplings is not systematically analyzed.
- Coupling recovery on real data is modest (e.g., r ≈ 0.38 for non-simultaneous pairs vs. fully observed), suggesting limited identifiability of A; no regularization is used in reported results, and theoretical identifiability conditions are only qualitatively discussed.
- Comparisons are limited to a naive baseline and a “fully observed” LDS. Alternative baselines (e.g., dynamic FA/GPFA with shared latent factors, probabilistic CCA across sessions, latent GLMs with scalable approximations) are not evaluated.
- No runtime/complexity or robustness analyses (e.g., sensitivity to noise levels, recording rate, stimulus strength).

Quality:
Technically sound use of LDS and EM with missing observations; the implementation details provided for R and d are correct, though full M-step updates are not shown. The empirical claims are supported by the presented metrics; however, external validity to truly separate sessions remains untested. The reliance on overlap is acknowledged but not analyzed in depth.

Clarity:
Generally well-written and organized. The modeling setup and training procedure are clear. Some details are sparse (full updates for A,B,Q, explicit assumptions on diagonality of R, specifics of the stimulus vectors in real data, metric definitions), and the rationale for stationarity is briefly stated. Figures are referenced with informative summaries.

Originality:
The idea of stitching non-simultaneous partial recordings with known selection matrices in a unified LDS is a novel and pragmatic contribution in this domain. Methodologically, it is an application of standard tools rather than a new algorithmic framework.

Significance:
Addresses a common bottleneck in population imaging and could influence experimental design and analysis pipelines by enabling correlation estimates and cross-predictions across larger populations than can be recorded simultaneously. The practical impact depends on robustness under real cross-session conditions and guidance on required overlap.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Evaluate on genuinely separate sessions (e.g., sequential imaging fields) to assess robustness to nonstationarities, and include controls for potential session-specific drifts.
- Quantify how performance scales with overlap fraction and with spatial/functional separation between subsets; provide actionable design recommendations.
- Explore correlated innovations or low-dimensional latent inputs to relax diagonal Q, and report robustness to R/Q misspecification.
- Include regularization (e.g., sparsity on A) in reported results and compare coupling recovery.
- Add baselines from dynamic FA/GPFA or scalable latent GLMs, and report predictive log-likelihood on held-out data.
- Provide complexity/runtime and, if possible, release code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Clear, tractable method addresses a timely and practically important problem.
- Strong empirical gains over a reasonable baseline on both synthetic and real datasets for predicting non-simultaneous correlations.
- Conceptually simple framework that can be extended (e.g., additional latent dimensions, priors).

Arguments against acceptance:
- Real-data validation is limited to artificial splits; no evidence under true cross-session variability.
- Heavy reliance on overlap without quantitative analysis; limited guidance for practice.
- Modeling simplifications and modest recovery of couplings; limited comparison to stronger baselines.

Overall assessment:
A solid and well-executed application of LDS to an important problem, with convincing results on correlation prediction in controlled settings. The main limitation is external validity to real multi-session scenarios and the lack of analysis on overlap requirements. If space permits, additional experiments addressing these points would substantially strengthen the contribution.
