- Proposes a "stitching" method to infer joint neural population dynamics from multiple partial, non-simultaneous recordings using a latent linear Gaussian dynamical system.  
- Demonstrates strong performance on simulations and two real calcium imaging datasets, approaching fully observed-model performance.  
- Method recovers coupling and noise correlations accurately (0.92 simulation, 0.84 real data) and greatly outperforms a naive baseline.  
- Paper is technically sound with clear methodology, well-designed experiments, and multiple evaluation metrics (coupling recovery, noise correlation, activity prediction).  
- Linear Gaussian assumption is a major simplification and may limit applicability to real neural dynamics (nonlinearities, bursting).  
- Authors acknowledge linearity limitation but do not analyze how it affects performance on more complex or nonlinear dynamics.  
- Overlap between imaging sessions is critical for stitching, but the paper lacks quantitative analysis of how performance degrades as overlap decreases.  
- Scalability to many imaging sessions is asserted but not empirically demonstrated or quantified.  
- Clarity is high: well-written, organized, and mathematically detailed with appropriate visualizations and related-work positioning.  
- Computational complexity (especially as session count grows) is not quantified and needs more detail.  
- The stitching idea is novel and meaningful in the context of neural circuit analysis, despite building on standard LDS and EM techniques.  
- High practical significance: could enable analysis of larger populations from existing datasets and aid experimental design.  
- Strengths: clear motivation, comprehensive evaluation on simulated and real data, solid baselines, practical relevance, and good exposition.  
- Critical weaknesses: limited linear model analysis, unquantified sensitivity to session overlap, and untested scaling to many sessions.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions—add discussion of linear-model failure modes and quantify the relationship between session overlap and performance.
