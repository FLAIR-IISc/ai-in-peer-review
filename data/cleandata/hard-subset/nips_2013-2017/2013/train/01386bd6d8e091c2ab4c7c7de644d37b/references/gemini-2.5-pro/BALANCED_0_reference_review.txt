**Summary**

This paper introduces a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) where the observations in each recording session correspond to a different, incomplete view of the full system state. The model parameters, including the coupling matrix that captures functional connectivity, are learned using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The key contribution is the ability to infer couplings and predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, effectively "stitching" together the partial datasets into a coherent whole. The authors validate their approach first on simulated data where ground truth is known, and then on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, they devise a clever "fictional imaging scenario" by splitting a fully-observed dataset, allowing them to benchmark their method against an upper bound (a model trained on all data) and a lower bound (a naive model combining session-specific fits).

This work addresses a critical and practical limitation in experimental neuroscience. While previous work has focused on inferring connectivity from simultaneously recorded data (e.g., Pillow et al., 2008; Mishchenko et al., 2011) or dealing with different types of subsampling (e.g., Wohrer et al., 2010), this paper presents a novel and direct solution to the problem of combining non-simultaneous recordings of interacting neural populations.

**Strengths (Reasons to Accept)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in systems neuroscience: the inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously. A successful method for computationally integrating partial recordings could significantly expand the scale of functional circuit analysis, enabling researchers to build models of larger networks than currently possible. The potential impact for both experimentalists and computational neuroscientists is substantial.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** While the underlying statistical tool (LDS with missing data) is standard, its application to "stitch" non-simultaneous neural recordings is highly novel. The authors clearly articulate the intuition that a second recording session can resolve the unidentifiability of the latent dynamics inferred from a single partial recording. They also do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to prior art, convincingly arguing why existing methods are not suited for this specific problem.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength of this work.
    *   On simulated data, the method successfully recovers the ground-truth coupling matrix and noise correlations, even for never-simultaneously-observed neuron pairs.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is an excellent evaluation strategy. By creating a synthetic stitching problem where the complete data is known, the authors provide a clear quantitative assessment of their method's performance relative to a well-defined upper and lower bound.
    *   The results on real data are impressive. The method accurately predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, with performance approaching the "fully observed" upper bound and vastly outperforming the naive approach.

4.  **Clarity and Quality:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The model is presented concisely, and the experimental design is explained thoroughly. The results are presented clearly, and the figures are informative. The work is technically sound and represents a complete piece of research, not just a proof of concept.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or strengthened. These are intended as constructive suggestions for improvement, as they do not detract significantly from the overall contribution.

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The authors note that for the real data, some overlap between the recorded neuron sets was critical for the method to work (i.e., `c=50%` with no overlap failed, while `c=60%` with 20% neuron overlap succeeded). This is a crucial practical detail. The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic investigation of how performance depends on the degree of overlap. For experimentalists looking to apply this method, guidance on the trade-off between covering more total neurons versus ensuring sufficient overlap would be invaluable. A simple simulation showing performance as a function of overlap percentage would significantly enhance the paper's practical utility.

2.  **Model Simplicity:** The choice of a linear Gaussian model is well-justified for tractability and serves as an excellent proof of principle. However, neural dynamics and calcium imaging are known to be nonlinear and non-Gaussian. The authors rightly acknowledge this limitation. A brief discussion on the potential failure modes of the LDS in the face of strong nonlinearities, or a comment on whether the model's success relies on accurately capturing second-order statistics (which it might do even if the underlying dynamics are more complex), would be a welcome addition to the discussion.

3.  **Scalability:** The experiments are performed on populations of up to 100 neurons. The introduction motivates the work by referring to circuits of thousands of neurons. A short discussion of the computational complexity of the EM algorithm in this context (scaling with N^2 or N^3 depending on implementation) and the potential challenges or necessary approximations for scaling to much larger systems would be helpful for readers interested in applying the method to larger-scale problems.

4.  **Parameter Identifiability:** In Figure 4c, the correlation between the inferred and "fully observed" coupling parameters decreases with population size, even as the correlation predictions (Fig 4a) remain strong. The authors suggest this may be due to parameter degeneracies. This is an interesting point. It suggests that while the exact one-to-one mapping of coupling parameters might be ill-posed, the model is still capturing the effective network dynamics required to predict correlations. Expanding on this observation in the discussion could provide deeper insight into what aspects of the system are robustly identifiable.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and well-validated solution to an important problem. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
