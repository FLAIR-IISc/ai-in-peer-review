This paper presents a statistical method for "stitching" together multiple, partial recordings of a neural circuit to infer the dynamics of the complete population. The authors model the full circuit as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) and treat each partial recording as an incomplete observation of this system. By fitting the LDS parameters across multiple non-simultaneous recording sessions using an Expectation-Maximization algorithm, they aim to recover the functional connectivity and predict noise correlations even between neurons that were never recorded at the same time. The method is evaluated on simulated data and on a "fictional" stitching scenario created by splitting a single, fully-observed calcium imaging dataset into two overlapping, sequential sessions.

**Strengths:**
The problem the paper addresses is of significant practical importance in neuroscience. As experimental techniques for recording neural activity scale up, they still fall short of capturing entire circuits simultaneously. A robust method for combining partial recordings could substantially expand the scope of data-driven circuit modeling. The core idea of using a latent state-space model to represent unobserved neurons is elegant and theoretically grounded in system identification literature. The evaluation strategy, comparing the proposed "stitch" model against a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound, provides a clear framework for assessing performance. The results on predicting pairwise correlations on the prepared experimental data appear promising (Fig. 3d, 4a).

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the appealing high-level concept, the paper suffers from numerous methodological shortcomings, a lack of critical analysis regarding its own limitations, and a general lack of precision and care in its presentation.

1.  **Model Appropriateness and Assumptions:** The choice of a linear-Gaussian dynamical system is a major simplification for modeling calcium imaging data, which reflects a cascade of nonlinear processes (spiking, calcium dynamics, indicator saturation) and non-Gaussian noise. While the authors acknowledge this, they justify it simply by stating it "is able to predict correlations". This is insufficient. Furthermore, several strong assumptions are made without adequate justification or analysis of their potential impact:
    *   The assumption of stationarity over long recording periods (up to 55 minutes in an awake animal) is questionable.
    *   The innovation covariance Q is assumed to be diagonal. The authors state that "our framework also can allow for correlated noise" but never return to this point, leaving the reader to assume this limitation was present in all reported results. This forces all shared variability to be explained via the linear dynamics matrix A, which may be an unreasonable constraint.
    *   The data preprocessing involves a high-pass filter with a 1s time constant on data sampled at ~3Hz. This is an aggressive filter that may remove physiologically relevant slow dynamics, and its selection is not justified.

2.  **Evaluation on Experimental Data:** The validation on real data is based on a "fictional imaging scenario" that is not representative of a real-world application. By splitting a single continuous recording into two "sessions" (first 40% and second 40% of the time), the authors circumvent the most difficult challenge of multi-session analysis: non-stationarity across sessions that could be separated by hours or days. True experiments would involve changes in brain state, electrode drift, or photobleaching, none of which this evaluation can account for. The results, therefore, likely represent a best-case scenario that may not generalize.

3.  **Critical Limitations are Understated:** The paper contains several critical findings that are presented as minor asides but severely undermine the method's claimed utility.
    *   In Section 2.3, the authors state: "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This implies that the method *requires* a subset of anchor neurons to be recorded in all sessions. This is a fundamental constraint on the experimental design and contradicts the more general framing of the problem. The degree of overlap required for successful stitching is a key hyperparameter that is not investigated.
    *   At the end of Section 3.2, the authors note that the correlation between inferred and "true" coupling parameters "drops with increasing population size". This suggests the method's parameter estimates become less reliable precisely in the regime it is intended for (large populations). This is a major scalability concern that is dismissed with a speculative "perhaps due to... near degeneracy".

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The manuscript lacks the polish and precision expected for a top-tier conference.
    *   The notation in Equation (2) is confusing: `ykt = C k(xkt + d+ t) t ∼ N (0, R)`. The variable `t` is used for both the time index and the noise variable. The text clarifies that the noise variable is epsilon, but the equation itself is sloppy.
    *   The formatting of the reference list is highly inconsistent and contains errors (e.g., trailing commas in [1], incomplete entries for [21]).
    *   Capitalization of "Figure" is inconsistent throughout the text (e.g., "Figure 3c" vs. "figure 3d").
    *   A footnote is oddly placed mid-paragraph on page 4, disrupting the flow of the text.

**Summary of pros and cons:**

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem in computational neuroscience.
*   The core idea is intuitive and builds on established theory.
*   The presented results on predicting correlations appear strong within the limited evaluation context.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   The linear-Gaussian model is likely a poor match for the underlying biological processes, and its choice is weakly justified.
*   The evaluation on real data uses a synthetic setup that avoids the key challenge of inter-session non-stationarity.
*   Critical limitations (the requirement for overlapping neurons, performance degradation with population size) are not adequately investigated or discussed.
*   The manuscript contains numerous minor errors in notation, formatting, and presentation, suggesting a lack of careful preparation.

**Recommendation:**
While the problem is important and the direction is interesting, the execution and presentation are not up to NIPS standards. The combination of strong, unjustified assumptions, a non-representative evaluation on real data, and the downplaying of significant limitations makes this work feel premature. The numerous small errors throughout the manuscript further detract from its quality. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should conduct a more rigorous analysis of the model's assumptions, test their method on genuinely separate recording sessions, and thoroughly investigate the impact of overlap and population size before resubmission.
