This paper tackles a compelling and practically relevant problem: inferring the dynamics of large neural circuits from incomplete, multi-session recordings. The core idea – leveraging the principle that nearby neurons are strongly coupled to “stitch” together fragmented observations – is genuinely novel and addresses a significant bottleneck in systems neuroscience. While the method is presented within a relatively simple linear Gaussian framework, the demonstration of successful reconstruction of correlations, even in the face of missing data, is impressive. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and position their work as a meaningful step forward.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of combining multiple partial recordings to infer circuit-level dynamics is a fresh perspective. Previous work largely focuses on connectivity inference *within* a single recording session, or relies on strong assumptions not applicable here.
*   **Significance:** The ability to infer dynamics across non-simultaneously observed neurons has the potential to greatly expand our understanding of large neural circuits, especially in the context of limited imaging capabilities. This directly addresses a major challenge in the field.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clearly stated, and the method is explained in a reasonably accessible manner. The connection to existing dynamical systems literature is helpful.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on both simulated and real data are convincing. The demonstration that the method can predict noise correlations, a notoriously difficult task, is particularly noteworthy. The benchmarking against upper and lower bounds provides valuable context.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Simplicity:** The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification of neural reality. While the authors acknowledge this, it does limit the scope of the method. Nonlinearities and non-Gaussianity are prevalent in neural data and could significantly impact performance.
*   **Scalability:** While the authors mention scalability, the computational cost of the EM algorithm, even with mini-batch optimization, could become prohibitive for extremely large circuits. A discussion of computational complexity and potential bottlenecks would be beneficial.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for performance is important but warrants further investigation. The optimal degree of overlap and its sensitivity to noise levels remain open questions.
*   **Limited Biological Interpretation:** The paper focuses primarily on correlation reconstruction. Exploring the biological implications of the inferred dynamics, such as identifying functionally relevant subnetworks or characterizing stimulus-response relationships, would strengthen the impact of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel approach to a challenging problem in systems neuroscience.
*   The method demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data, particularly in its ability to predict noise correlations.
*   The clear writing and logical organization make the paper accessible to a broad audience.
*   The potential impact of this work is significant, offering a pathway to study larger and more complex neural circuits.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a simplified linear Gaussian model limits the generalizability of the method.
*   The computational scalability of the algorithm could be a concern for very large circuits.
*   The paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the biological implications of the inferred dynamics.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and significant contribution. While the model simplicity and potential scalability issues are legitimate concerns, the authors have clearly demonstrated the feasibility and potential of their approach. The ability to infer correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons is a significant step forward and warrants acceptance. I would recommend acceptance, despite the minor flaws, due to the boldness of the approach and the potential impact on the field. The authors' willingness to acknowledge limitations and benchmark their results is also commendable. I believe this work opens up exciting new avenues for research in systems neuroscience and aligns well with the spirit of NIPS.



**Rating:** Accept (7/10)
