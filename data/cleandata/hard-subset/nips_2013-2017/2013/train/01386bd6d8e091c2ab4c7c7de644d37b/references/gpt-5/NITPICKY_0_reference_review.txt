Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses an important scenario in systems neuroscience: inferring joint population dynamics and noise correlations across neurons that are not recorded simultaneously. The authors propose a “stitching” approach based on a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with shared parameters (A, B, Q) across sessions and session-specific selection matrices Ck that pick observed neurons. Parameters are learned by EM with Kalman smoothing over missing observations. The model is evaluated on simulations and on two calcium imaging datasets by creating “fictional” split sessions from simultaneously recorded data; performance is compared to a “fully observed” upper bound and a naive per-session baseline.

The technical approach (LDS with missing data, EM learning) is standard, with roots in subspace/system identification and dynamic factor models, and related to latent-variable GLMs and common-input models (e.g., Pillow et al., Macke et al.). The novelty is the explicit multi-session stitching for neural data and the focus on predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs. The idea is timely and relevant to NIPS.

Strengths
- Clear, relevant problem with practical importance for 2-photon imaging where full-population simultaneity is infeasible.
- Simple, computationally tractable formulation that leverages well-understood LDS/EM machinery.
- Convincing simulation showing recovery of cross-couplings/noise correlations even for non-overlapping sessions (in simulation).
- On real data, stitching improves prediction of pairwise correlations and activity relative to a naive baseline and approaches a fully observed model.
- Sensible experimental design for benchmarking (upper and lower bounds), and reporting of multiple metrics (correlation of couplings, noise correlations, cross-prediction).

Weaknesses and detailed comments

Quality/technical soundness
- Identifiability and stability are under-specified. The model assumes stationarity (Lyapunov equation for Q0) but no constraints on A (e.g., spectral radius < 1) are stated; EM can produce unstable A. Please clarify any stabilization/regularization (e.g., spectral normalization) and whether results enforce stability.
- Noise model assumptions are restrictive. Q is assumed diagonal and R appears diagonal in practice; calcium measurement noise and neuropil contamination are often correlated. Claims that the framework can handle correlated noise are not empirically validated. Sensitivity to mis-specified R and Q is not studied.
- Overlap is critical in practice. In real data, c=50% with no overlap “failed,” contradicting the strong claim (made in the intro and supported only by simulations) that non-overlapping sessions can be stitched. Quantify failure modes, and characterize how much overlap is needed as a function of N, T, SNR, and network coupling strength.
- Evaluation is limited to fictional splits of a single simultaneously recorded dataset. This setup sidesteps the core difficulty of cross-session variability (photobleaching, behavioral/brain-state drift, ROI re-identification). Demonstrate stitching on truly non-simultaneous recordings (different fields of view or times) and analyze robustness to neuron mis-registration.
- Baselines are weak. The naive baseline is strawman-like. Missing are: (a) dynamic factor models/GPFA fitted per session with alignment via shared neurons (Procrustes/CCA), (b) subspace/system ID with missing data, (c) latent variable GLMs at scale (e.g., low-rank LDS/common-input models), (d) simple correlation-transfer baselines via shared neurons. Without stronger baselines, significance is hard to assess.
- No held-out likelihood or rigorous predictive checks. Pairwise correlation similarity is useful but can be inflated by global fluctuations. Report held-out log-likelihood, multi-step prediction, and posterior predictive checks. Provide confidence intervals beyond a few s.e.m. values.
- Scalability and complexity are not analyzed. Kalman smoothing is O(TN^3) per EM iteration with dense A; even N=1k is challenging. Provide runtimes, memory requirements, and discuss sparsity/low-rank structure or parameter tying to scale.
- Simulation choices seem favorable. Observation noise std=0.006 is unrealistically small; Q diagonal, truncated weights, and relatively long T all favor LDS recovery. Provide sweeps over noise levels, T, SNR, coupling sparsity, degree distributions, and stimulus strength; test with correlated measurement noise.
- Stimulus modeling is simplistic. In awake data, unmodeled behavioral covariates and slow state fluctuations exist; high-pass filtering and constant offset d are ad hoc. Consider incorporating slow drifts, shared modulators, and richer inputs. The term hk1:T appears once and is undefined.
- The claim that “we identify the true parametrization after the second session” remains heuristic. Provide formal conditions for identifiability with missingness (e.g., observability/controllability with block missingness), and sample complexity intuition.
- Regularization is not described. For large N and limited T, A estimation is ill-posed. While sparsity is mentioned in “unreported simulations,” core results should use and report explicit regularizers (L1/group lasso/low-rank + sparse decompositions), with hyperparameter selection.

Clarity
- Numerous notation/typographical errors hinder reproducibility:
  - Measurement equation: ykt = Ck(xkt + d + εt)? The text shows “d+ t” and “t ∼ N(0,R)” with inconsistent symbols; define εt distinctly from ηt.
  - Inconsistent spacing: C k, A>, σkj, std(ii), hk1:T (undefined), “d+ t,” etc.
  - Dimensions of d and the procedure to estimate it are unclear; data were high-pass filtered yet an offset is learned—justify.
  - M-step updates: indices ykt,σkj are awkward; please provide a clean derivation or appendix with full formulas.
  - Figures are heavily referenced but not interpretable without them; ensure captions/axes/units are clear.
- References are incomplete/inconsistent (missing authors/years/pages; e.g., “P.L.”; Katayama entry incomplete; stray punctuation). This is below NIPS standards.

Originality
- Core technical ingredients (LDS + EM + missing data) are well-known. The contribution is the application to multi-session stitching and the empirical demonstration for noise-correlation prediction. Novelty is moderate; it would be strengthened by theoretical guarantees, stronger baselines, and real multi-session results.

Significance
- The application is potentially impactful for calcium imaging. However, without demonstrations on truly non-simultaneous sessions, robustness to overlap/mis-registration, and stronger baselines, the practical significance is not yet established. Claims about scaling to thousands of neurons are not supported.

Pros
- Addresses an important experimental bottleneck.
- Simple method; accessible to practitioners.
- Solid simulation; promising gains on real data vs naive baseline.
- Clear path to extensions (sparsity, latent unobserved inputs).

Cons
- Limited and optimistic evaluation; no truly cross-session results.
- Weak baselines; no likelihood-based metrics; no runtime/scaling analysis.
- Overlap appears necessary in practice; not quantified.
- Technical/notation errors and incomplete references.
- Lack of theoretical conditions for identifiability and stability.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on truly non-simultaneous recordings and quantify overlap requirements; test robustness to neuron-matching errors.
- Include stronger baselines (subspace ID with missing data, factor model alignment via shared neurons, latent common-input models).
- Report held-out log-likelihood, predictive performance, and uncertainty; provide runtime/scalability benchmarks.
- Enforce stability of A; use and report regularization (sparsity/low-rank), hyperparameter selection, and sensitivity analyses.
- Validate with correlated R and non-diagonal Q; sweep SNR and T.
- Clean up notation, equation definitions, and fully fix/complete references.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and relevant, but the paper in its current form has many technical/clarity issues and insufficient empirical evidence on the core use case (true multi-session stitching) and against strong baselines. With the above additions and polishing, it could be a strong submission. Reviewer confidence: high.
