This paper presents a compelling and novel approach to leveraging partially observed neural data, a significant bottleneck in systems neuroscience. The core idea – “stitching” together activity from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings to infer the dynamics of a larger population – is both intuitive and potentially transformative. While the methodological framework relies on a relatively simple linear Gaussian dynamical system, the paper convincingly demonstrates its efficacy on both simulated and real calcium imaging data, predicting correlations between neurons never simultaneously recorded with remarkable accuracy. This is a bold step towards addressing the limitations of current imaging technology and represents a significant departure from existing methods that primarily focus on connectivity within *observed* populations.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear articulation of the problem and the elegance of its solution. The theoretical justification, while building on established latent dynamical system theory, is well-presented and motivates the approach effectively. The empirical validation is also strong, demonstrating performance approaching the theoretical upper bound (full observation) and significantly exceeding a naive baseline. The application to real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex is particularly encouraging, suggesting the method's potential for practical impact.  The authors thoughtfully address the limitations of their approach, acknowledging the simplifying assumptions of the linear Gaussian model and hinting at potential avenues for improvement.

However, the paper isn’t without its limitations. The reliance on the linear Gaussian dynamical system is a key one. While justified by computational tractability, it limits the applicability of the method to scenarios where this assumption holds. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of the potential impact of non-linearities and non-Gaussianity on the results would be beneficial.  The sensitivity analysis on the degree of overlap between recording sessions (requiring 60% overlap for reasonable performance) also suggests a practical limitation – the need for substantial overlap to achieve accurate stitching. The explanation for this dependency – needing to establish a robust mapping between the two subsets – is reasonable, but further exploration of strategies to mitigate this requirement would be valuable. The performance gains from adding more neurons, while noted, could be explored further to reveal scaling behavior.  Also, while comparisons to other related work are made, some feel cursory and don't fully capture the nuances of each technique. For instance, while the authors dismiss latent GLMs due to scaling issues, more recent advancements in variational inference could potentially address these limitations and warrant further discussion.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **High Novelty:** The approach of "stitching" together partial observations to infer population dynamics is genuinely novel and addresses a critical challenge in the field.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The method performs remarkably well on both simulated and real data, exceeding a naive baseline and approaching the theoretical limit.
*   **Potential Impact:** This work has the potential to significantly advance our ability to study large neural circuits, particularly in situations where full population recordings are impossible.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Methodological Simplification:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system may limit the applicability of the method.
*   **Overlap Dependency:** The requirement for substantial overlap between recording sessions could pose a practical limitation.
*   **Limited Discussion of Alternatives:** The dismissal of certain related works feels somewhat perfunctory.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limitations, the novelty and potential impact of this work are substantial. The authors have presented a compelling solution to a significant problem in systems neuroscience. While methodological refinements and further validation are warranted, the paper represents a significant step forward and deserves acceptance.  I am willing to overlook the methodological simplifications given the bold nature of the approach and the strong empirical evidence supporting its efficacy. This paper opens up exciting new avenues for research and has the potential to reshape how we study neural population dynamics.



**Recommendation:** Accept.
