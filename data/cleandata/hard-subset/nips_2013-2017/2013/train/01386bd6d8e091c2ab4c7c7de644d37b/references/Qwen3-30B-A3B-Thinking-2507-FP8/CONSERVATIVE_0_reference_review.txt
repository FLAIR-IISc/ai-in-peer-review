

**Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"**

This paper proposes a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural populations to infer correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously. The authors model neural dynamics using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, applying standard EM algorithms to estimate model parameters. They claim this approach can predict noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons, potentially enabling analysis of large neural circuits where full simultaneous recording is experimentally infeasible.

The paper presents a reasonable simulation study showing that their stitching method can recover coupling matrices and noise correlations for synthetic data, with performance better than a naive baseline that simply averages parameters across sessions. They also apply the method to two calcium imaging datasets (80-200 neurons) from mouse somatosensory cortex, reporting that their method predicts correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching a "fully observed" model (which would be impossible in practice).

The main strengths of the paper include a clear problem statement, reasonable simulation validation, and well-organized presentation of the method. However, as a reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and established approaches, I have significant concerns about the paper's quality, originality, and significance.

The core methodology is a standard application of latent dynamical systems to missing data, which is well-established in the time series literature. The authors acknowledge that their linear Gaussian model "does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade of neural couplings, calcium dynamics, fluorescence measurements and imaging noise" but provide no evidence that this simplification is sufficient for their application. This is a major oversight given the well-documented complexities of neural data. The authors don't compare their method to more sophisticated approaches that explicitly model these nonlinearities (e.g., [6, 16]), which would be necessary to demonstrate the value of their simplified approach.

The experimental validation is insufficiently rigorous. The authors test their method on only two small datasets (80-200 neurons), which is far from the thousands of neurons they claim to address in their introduction. More critically, they don't compare against any existing methods for handling partial neural recordings beyond a simplistic "naive" baseline. The paper fails to demonstrate that their method outperforms or even approaches state-of-the-art approaches for neural data analysis.

The significance claims are substantially overstated. The paper claims their method "can help us build data-driven models of large cortical circuits," but the experimental validation doesn't support this. The authors don't show how their method scales to larger populations or demonstrate that it provides any advantages over existing approaches for neural data analysis. The potential impact on neuroscience is speculative, and the paper doesn't provide compelling evidence that this method is necessary or substantially better than current practices.

The paper's main weaknesses include:
1. Lack of rigorous validation against established baselines (no comparison to methods that model neural nonlinearities)
2. Insufficient experimental validation (only two small datasets)
3. Overstated significance claims
4. Failure to adequately address the limitations of their linear model

The main strengths are:
1. Clear presentation of the problem and method
2. Reasonable simulation study
3. Well-organized technical exposition

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Compare their method to more sophisticated neural modeling approaches
2. Validate on larger datasets with more neurons
3. Provide theoretical justification for the linear Gaussian model's sufficiency
4. Temper their significance claims to match the evidence

As a skeptical reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor, I find the paper's claims unsupported by the evidence provided. While the idea of stitching partial recordings is conceptually interesting, the authors fail to demonstrate that their specific approach offers a meaningful advance over established methods. The paper presents a standard statistical technique applied to a neuroscience problem without sufficient justification for why their simplifying assumptions are valid or why this approach is necessary. Given the high standards of NIPS, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. The authors would need to provide substantially more rigorous validation against appropriate baselines and better justify their modeling choices before this work could be considered for publication.
