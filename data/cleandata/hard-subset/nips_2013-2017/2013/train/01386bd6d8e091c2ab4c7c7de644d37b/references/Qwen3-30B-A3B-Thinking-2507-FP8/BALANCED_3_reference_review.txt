

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience: how to effectively leverage current experimental limitations in neural recording technology to infer population dynamics of large neural circuits. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit (e.g., different subsets of neurons imaged in different sessions), enabling prediction of correlations between neurons not recorded simultaneously. This builds on previous work in neural population modeling (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) and latent dynamical systems (e.g., [13, 17, 18]), but specifically tackles the problem of non-simultaneous observations, which has not been addressed in prior work.

**Strengths**:
1. **Novel Problem and Solution**: The paper identifies a clear gap in the literature—most prior methods assume simultaneous recordings, but experimental constraints prevent this for large circuits. The proposed stitching method using latent dynamical systems is a novel and practical solution that directly addresses this gap.
2. **Strong Experimental Validation**: The authors demonstrate their method on both synthetic data (with ground truth) and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The comparison against a "naive" approach (which cannot predict cross-session correlations) and a "fully observed" upper bound provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.
3. **Technical Soundness**: The method is well-grounded in statistical theory (using EM and Kalman smoothing) and the implementation details are thorough. The results on synthetic data (Figures 2b-2c) show that the method accurately recovers noise correlations (correlation 0.92 with ground truth) and coupling matrices (0.73 correlation for cross-session couplings).
4. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear explanations of the model, its implementation, and the evaluation metrics. The figures effectively illustrate the key results.

**Weaknesses**:
1. **Model Simplicity**: While the linear Gaussian dynamical system provides computational tractability, neural dynamics are known to be highly nonlinear. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't sufficiently explore how more complex models (e.g., nonlinear dynamical systems or spike-based models) might improve performance. The claim that "even this simple model makes accurate predictions" feels under-supported given the known complexities of neural dynamics.
2. **Scalability Concerns**: The method is demonstrated on relatively small populations (up to 200 neurons). For larger circuits (thousands of neurons), the computational complexity of the EM algorithm could become prohibitive. The paper doesn't address how the method would scale to such population sizes.
3. **Limited Discussion of Failure Modes**: The paper shows positive results but doesn't thoroughly discuss cases where the method might fail (e.g., when there's minimal overlap between imaging sessions, or when neural dynamics are highly nonlinear). The authors note that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work," but don't quantify this relationship.
4. **Insufficient Connection to Broader Neuroscience Impact**: While the paper mentions potential applications (e.g., mapping entire circuits), it doesn't clearly articulate how this method advances our understanding of neural computation beyond the technical achievement of stitching data. The significance would be stronger if it connected to specific neuroscience hypotheses or experiments.

**Assessment by Criteria**:

*Quality*: The paper is technically sound, with rigorous evaluation on both synthetic and real data. The method is well-motivated and the results are well-supported. However, the reliance on a linear model for neural dynamics is a notable limitation that isn't fully addressed.

*Clarity*: The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The method is explained in sufficient detail for reproduction. Some technical aspects (e.g., the handling of measurement noise) could be clarified with more mathematical detail.

*Originality*: The core idea of stitching non-simultaneous recordings is novel and well-justified by the literature review. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work on sub-sampled observations (e.g., [12-15]).

*Significance*: The method has clear practical significance for neuroscience, enabling better use of existing imaging data. While the immediate application is in cortical circuit analysis, the approach could potentially be generalized to other fields with similar data limitations. However, the paper could better articulate how this advances the field beyond a useful technical tool.

**Recommendation**: I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by providing a practical solution to a real experimental challenge. The main revisions should address the limitations of the linear model, provide more discussion of scalability and failure modes, and strengthen the connection to broader neuroscience significance.

**Specific Suggestions for Improvement**:
1. Add a brief discussion (or subsection) on how the method could be extended to more complex neural dynamics models (e.g., nonlinear LDS or spike-based models) and how this might affect performance.
2. Include a small section on scalability, discussing computational complexity and potential bottlenecks for larger population sizes.
3. Expand the discussion of limitations to include specific failure cases (e.g., when overlap between sessions is minimal, or when neural dynamics are highly nonlinear).
4. Strengthen the significance section by connecting the method to specific neuroscience questions it could help address (beyond just "building data-driven models").

Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-executed solution. The minor revisions suggested above would make it even more compelling for the NIPS audience.
