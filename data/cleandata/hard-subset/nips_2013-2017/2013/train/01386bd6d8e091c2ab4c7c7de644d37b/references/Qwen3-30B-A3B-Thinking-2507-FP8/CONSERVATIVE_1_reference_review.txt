

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of neural activity to infer the joint dynamics of a complete neural circuit, including correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously. The authors model neural population dynamics using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, applying an EM algorithm to estimate model parameters from non-overlapping subsets of recorded neurons across multiple sessions.

The paper makes a reasonable claim that current imaging technology limits simultaneous recording of large neural populations, and that their method could help overcome this limitation. The problem is well-motivated by the experimental challenges in neuroscience, and the authors correctly identify that existing approaches for inferring functional connectivity (e.g., [6-11]) do not address the problem of non-simultaneous recordings.

**Strengths:** The paper demonstrates reasonable technical execution of a well-established modeling approach (LDS). The simulated data experiments show that the method can recover coupling parameters and noise correlations with reasonable accuracy. The application to real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex is appropriate, and the results suggest the method can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons better than a naive baseline.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the reasonable technical execution, the paper suffers from significant shortcomings that prevent it from being accepted as a strong contribution to NIPS:

1. **Overclaiming results:** The paper claims the method "can successfully predict the magnitude of (so-called) noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons," but the evidence provided is insufficient to support this strong claim. The performance metrics (e.g., correlation 0.84 between predicted and true correlations) are not compared to established baselines that could demonstrate the method's true novelty or advantage. The "fully observed" model they use as an upper bound is not a realistic benchmark since it assumes simultaneous recording of all neurons, which is precisely what their method aims to circumvent.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparison:** The "naive" baseline they use (averaging coefficients for overlapping neurons) is overly simplistic and does not represent state-of-the-art methods for handling partial observations. They do not compare against established methods for inferring functional connectivity from partial data, such as those using latent variable models or Bayesian approaches [13, 17, 18].

3. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why their approach should work. They cite theoretical results about dynamical system reconstruction from partial observations [21-23], but fail to establish how their specific approach differs from or improves upon these existing theoretical results.

4. **Unjustified model assumptions:** The paper relies heavily on the linear Gaussian dynamical system assumption, which is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. They acknowledge this limitation but do not provide evidence that their method would fail without this assumption, nor do they explore more realistic models that could better capture neural dynamics [6, 16].

5. **Limited validation:** The experimental validation is restricted to two small datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The paper does not demonstrate robustness across different neural circuits, different recording technologies, or varying degrees of overlap between recording sessions. The claim that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work" suggests the method may have significant practical limitations.

6. **Insufficient discussion of limitations:** The paper does not adequately address the limitations of their approach, such as the requirement for some overlap between recording sessions, the sensitivity to the quality of the linear model approximation, or the potential for parameter degeneracy in larger systems.

**Comparison to established methods:** The paper does not adequately position itself against existing work on inferring neural dynamics from partial observations. The authors cite [12-15] but fail to demonstrate how their approach fundamentally differs from or improves upon these methods. In particular, [13] also uses a latent dynamical system approach but claims to scale to larger populations, which the authors do not address.

**Recommendation:** This paper presents an interesting application of a well-established modeling framework to a practical neuroscience problem. However, the lack of thorough validation against appropriate baselines, the overclaiming of results without sufficient evidence, and the inadequate theoretical justification prevent it from meeting NIPS's standards for methodological rigor and novelty. The authors would need to substantially strengthen their validation by comparing against more sophisticated baselines, providing stronger theoretical justification, and conducting more comprehensive experiments across diverse datasets before this work could be considered a significant contribution.

The paper would be significantly improved by:
1. Comparing against more sophisticated baselines for partial observation problems
2. Providing a stronger theoretical argument for why their approach works
3. Conducting experiments with more diverse neural datasets
4. More thoroughly discussing the limitations of the linear model assumption
5. Providing a more nuanced interpretation of their results rather than overclaiming their success

As it stands, the paper makes a modest contribution to a niche problem in neuroscience but does not advance the field of computational neuroscience or machine learning in a way that would justify acceptance at NIPS. The method is not sufficiently novel, and the evidence provided for its effectiveness is insufficient.
