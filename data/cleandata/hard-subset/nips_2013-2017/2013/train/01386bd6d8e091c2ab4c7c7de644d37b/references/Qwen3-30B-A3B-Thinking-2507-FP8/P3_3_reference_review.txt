

# Peer Review: Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

## Summary and Context
This paper proposes a novel statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics. The authors address a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: current imaging techniques can only capture a fraction of a neural circuit's neurons simultaneously (e.g., hundreds vs. thousands in a barrel cortex). Their key insight is that by recording different subsets of neurons across multiple sessions, and leveraging strong coupling between neurons, they can infer correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. They model this as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework. The approach is validated on both simulated data and real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper correctly situates its contribution within the literature on neural population dynamics. It distinguishes itself from prior work on functional connectivity inference [6-11] and sub-sampled observation methods [12-15] by specifically addressing the problem of combining non-simultaneous recordings. While the LDS framework is well-established, the application to this specific neuroscience problem represents a novel contribution to the field.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and strong motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the experimental limitation (inability to record all neurons simultaneously) and demonstrates the practical importance of the problem through compelling examples from neuroscience.

2. **Rigorous experimental validation**: The authors provide excellent validation across multiple dimensions: 
   - They demonstrate recovery of coupling matrices and noise correlations in simulated data
   - They establish upper and lower bounds for performance using a "fully observed" model (theoretically optimal) and a "naive" approach (conventional baseline)
   - They apply the method to two distinct real datasets (anesthetized and awake mice), showing consistent performance

3. **Quantitative results**: The paper presents clear, quantitative metrics for performance (correlation coefficients between predicted and true values) that demonstrate the method's effectiveness, particularly for predicting noise correlations (0.84 correlation with the upper bound).

4. **Practical utility**: The approach has immediate applicability to existing datasets and could significantly extend the utility of current imaging techniques without requiring new hardware.

## Weaknesses

1. **Theoretical foundation**: While the paper references theoretical results about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21-23], it lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of why the specific LDS approach works for this problem. The explanation in Section 3.1 is somewhat hand-wavy, and the authors don't establish bounds on the error or conditions for successful stitching.

2. **Model limitations**: The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't adequately address:
   - How the method would perform with more complex, nonlinear dynamics
   - Whether incorporating known calcium dynamics [16, 6] would improve performance
   - The potential impact of the linear model's limitations on real-world applications

3. **Scalability concerns**: The paper demonstrates success on populations of 50-80 neurons, but the main motivation is to scale to thousands of neurons. The authors don't discuss computational complexity or whether the method would remain effective for much larger populations (e.g., >500 neurons), which is a critical concern for the stated application.

4. **Overlap requirements**: The paper shows that some overlap between recording sessions is critical (c=60% worked, c=50% didn't), but doesn't thoroughly explore the relationship between overlap size and performance. A more detailed analysis of this trade-off would be valuable for experimental design.

5. **Comparison to related work**: While the related work section is generally good, it could better distinguish the method from similar approaches like [21] (which also addresses reconstruction from partial observations) and clarify how their specific formulation differs.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed and important contribution to the neuroscience community that addresses a genuine experimental limitation. The method is technically sound, well-validated, and has clear practical utility. The results on real data are particularly compelling, demonstrating that the method can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching the theoretical optimum.

The paper's main weakness is its somewhat limited theoretical foundation and the simplification of the neural dynamics model. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, they don't sufficiently address how these might impact the method's performance in more complex scenarios. The lack of scalability analysis for very large populations (the very problem the method aims to solve) is also concerning.

However, the practical value of this method for neuroscience is significant. The ability to extract meaningful population-level statistics from existing partial recordings could substantially accelerate progress in understanding neural circuit function. The method provides a valuable tool for researchers working with current imaging technologies, even if it's not the theoretically optimal solution.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational neuroscience, addressing a real experimental limitation with a well-validated method. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened and the model limitations acknowledged more thoroughly, the practical utility and strong empirical results outweigh these concerns. The method represents a significant step toward more effective use of existing experimental data in studying neural population dynamics.

The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a compelling case for its method's utility. With minor revisions to better address the theoretical foundations and scalability concerns, this would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.
