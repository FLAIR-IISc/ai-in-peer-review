Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer a single joint model of population dynamics capable of predicting correlations (especially noise correlations) between neurons that were never observed simultaneously. The core technical approach is a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, learned by EM with Kalman smoothing; each session has a known binary observation matrix Ck selecting which neurons are observed. The model includes stimulus drive u via B, assumes diagonal R and Q (Q diagonal, but notes extensions), and sets the initial covariance to the stationary solution. The authors demonstrate on synthetic data that they can recover off-diagonal blocks of the coupling matrix A and predict noise correlations for nonsimultaneously observed pairs; on real 2-photon calcium imaging from mouse S1 (awake and anesthetized) they create a “fictional” two-session scenario by splitting simultaneously imaged neurons into two overlapping subsets and show that the stitched model predicts cross-session correlations close to a “fully observed” LDS (upper bound) and better than a “naive” baseline trained separately on each subset.

This work sits at the intersection of latent state-space modeling for neural data (e.g., LDS/PLDS, GPFA; see [13, 18]) and efforts to infer functional connectivity from calcium imaging [6, 7] and spikes [8–11], as well as sub-sampling/partial observation settings ([12–15]). Unlike prior methods that either assume simultaneous observation or rely on strong assumptions relating noise correlations to stimulus correlations ([12]), the paper explicitly addresses stitching across non-simultaneous sessions using identified cells and a joint LDS fit across sessions.

Strengths
- Clear and appealing problem formulation with practical relevance for modern imaging where full coverage is infeasible.
- Simple, computationally tractable approach (LDS + EM) that many labs could implement; principled handling of missing observations via Ck.
- Convincing synthetic demonstration: recovery of off-diagonal couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations and unobserved activity.
- Real-data experiments in S1; evaluation includes prediction of pairwise correlations and cross-prediction of activity, with upper/lower bounds for context.
- Useful discussion of overlap requirements and experimental design considerations.

Weaknesses
Quality
- Evaluation on real data uses a “fictional” split of simultaneously recorded neurons; there is no demonstration on truly non-simultaneous sessions subject to realistic nonstationarities (e.g., drift, brain state changes, photobleaching), which is the target use case.
- The LDS on raw/high-pass filtered fluorescence ignores calcium kinetics and spike generation; no comparison to more appropriate observation models (e.g., PLDS/Poisson, spike deconvolution + LDS) that are standard for neural population dynamics.
- Identifiability and stability conditions for A are not discussed (e.g., spectral radius constraints; uniqueness with partial overlap). EM local optima and sensitivity to initialization are not characterized.
- Noise models are restrictive (diagonal Q and R); correlated observation noise and shared input are common in calcium imaging.
- Statistical rigor of comparisons is limited. Many metrics compare to a “fully observed” LDS fit on the same data rather than to held-out empirical correlations; while a test split is mentioned, details of cross-validation and uncertainty are sparse.

Clarity
- The presentation of EM and online EM is terse; important implementation details (initialization, regularization, stability constraints, stopping criteria) are missing.
- Figures (as described) are informative, but the text does not quantify runtime or scaling with N, K, T, and overlap size c.

Originality
- The stitching idea is timely and practically important; however, technically it is a straightforward application of LDS with missing data across sessions using known selection matrices. The novelty lies more in the problem instantiation and empirical study than in new methodology.

Significance
- If robust to real non-simultaneous recordings, this could impact large-scale calcium imaging by enabling inference of cross-session noise correlations and activity prediction. As presented, the practical significance is promising but not yet fully substantiated due to the fictional split and modeling simplifications.

Suggestions for improvement
- Validate on genuinely non-simultaneous imaging sessions: e.g., sequential fields-of-view within the same experiment or across days, with some identified overlap. Quantify robustness to inter-session drift and state changes.
- Systematically study the role of overlap: vary c from 0 to, say, 80%, and report performance, failure modes, and identifiability; provide theoretical conditions under which stitching is possible/unique.
- Compare against stronger baselines: factor analysis with dynamics (GPFA), PLDS on deconvolved spikes or direct fluorescence, multi-session latent variable models (low-rank shared latent LDS capturing common input), and subspace identification methods; also include naive correlation-matching baselines using overlapping neurons.
- Incorporate more realistic observation models: either deconvolve calcium to spike rates before LDS, or explicitly model calcium kinetics; allow correlated R; consider low-rank Q to capture shared input.
- Regularization and constraints: impose stability on A, sparsity/structured priors (e.g., anatomical/laminar priors), and report effects on parameter recovery and correlation prediction.
- Provide more details on EM: initialization, minibatch schedule, convergence diagnostics, and runtime; report scaling with N and K; release code if possible.
- Evaluate with predictive log-likelihood on held-out data and held-out neurons; report confidence intervals and statistical tests.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a practically important and common data-collection constraint in neuroscience.
- Method is simple, general, and yields strong results on synthetic and real datasets under a controlled setting.
- Could stimulate broader adoption of principled multi-session modeling.

Arguments for rejection
- Lacks validation on truly non-simultaneous data—key to demonstrating practical viability.
- Technical novelty is modest; largely an application of standard LDS/EM to a multi-session setting.
- Modeling assumptions (linear-Gaussian, diagonal noise) are mismatched to calcium data; no comparisons to stronger baselines.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea and preliminary results are compelling, but the current evidence falls short of NeurIPS standards for significance and rigor. Demonstrating performance on real non-simultaneous sessions, strengthening baselines, and expanding the modeling to more realistic observation/noise structures would likely elevate this to a strong submission.
