Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses a common limitation in two-photon calcium imaging: only a fraction of a neural circuit can be recorded at once. The authors propose a statistical “stitching” method to combine multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same circuit to infer joint dynamics and, critically, predict correlations (especially noise correlations) between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

The approach instantiates a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) over the full population x_t with linear dynamics x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t (η_t ∼ N(0,Q)) and a per-session observation model y_t^k = C^k (x_t + d) + ε_t (ε_t ∼ N(0,R)), where each C^k selects the subset of recorded neurons in session k. Parameters (A,B,Q,R,d) are estimated via EM, with the E-step using Kalman smoothing under session-specific C^k, and the M-step providing closed-form updates (with R and d fit elementwise for observed entries). The model assumes diagonal Q and R but the framework could include additional latent dimensions to capture correlated noise or unobserved input. The authors use online EM for efficiency followed by batch EM.

They validate on simulated data with known connectivity and on two real mouse S1 datasets (awake and anesthetized). For real data, they construct a “fictional” two-session scenario by splitting simultaneously recorded neurons into two partially overlapping subsets; stitched models are compared to (i) a “fully observed” LDS fit to all neurons simultaneously (upper bound proxy) and (ii) a naive baseline that fits separate LDS models per subset and merges parameters (lower bound). The stitched model recovers cross-session couplings on simulations, predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs with high accuracy on simulations, and on real data matches the fully observed model’s predicted correlations substantially better than the naive baseline. The method also predicts activity of unobserved neurons better than the baseline.

This work relates to prior efforts on inferring functional connectivity from calcium imaging or spikes [6–11], latent-variable models for partially observed populations [13], modeling common input with latent LDS [17,18], and subspace/system identification [21]. The novelty is in leveraging known cell identities across multiple partial recordings to recover cross-session correlations and couplings without assuming a monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations (contrast to [12]). The method is a straightforward but effective application of LDS with missing data to a practically important multi-session setting.

Quality (technical soundness)
- Model and inference: The LDS with session-specific selection matrices and EM is standard and appropriate. Using identity-based C^k anchors the latent state to neurons, resolving the usual linear transform ambiguity once all neurons are observed across sessions. Assumptions of diagonal Q and R simplify inference; the authors note extensions via latent factors for correlated noise.
- Identifiability: The intuition that parameters become identifiable once all neurons are observed across sessions is reasonable in this setting (since C^k are known selectors), though a formal statement of conditions (e.g., sufficient overlap, observability, data length) would strengthen the argument.
- Evaluation: Simulations convincingly demonstrate recovery of couplings and noise correlations, and activity prediction. On real data, the “fictional” split is a good design; however, several choices deserve clarification: (i) evaluation of correlations is primarily versus a model-based “fully observed” LDS rather than empirical correlations on held-out simultaneous data; reporting direct comparisons to empirical test-set correlations would avoid model-class bias. (ii) The method requires some overlap (authors report failure at 50% coverage without overlap); quantifying performance as a function of overlap fraction and session length would be valuable. (iii) Potential nonstationarity across long awake sessions (drift in A,B) is not modeled; discussing robustness to drift would be helpful.
- Practical concerns: EM can get stuck in local optima; initialization/regularization are not detailed. The authors mention sparsity penalties help in simulations; a controlled analysis would be informative. Computational scaling (Kalman smoothing O(N^3 T) per EM iteration) may be a bottleneck for thousands of neurons; discussion of scalable approximations (e.g., block/sparse A, subspace methods, parallelization) would strengthen the claims.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a solid methodological description and sensible experiments. A few minor issues:
- Eq. (2) appears to have a typographical error; presumably y_t^k = C^k (x_t + d) + ε_t with ε_t ∼ N(0,R).
- Explicitly state that Q and R are constrained to be diagonal in the reported experiments.
- Define the overlap parameter c and the split procedure earlier and describe how subsets are selected.
- In real-data evaluation, specify whether correlations are computed empirically from held-out simultaneous data or via the fully observed model; include both if possible.

Originality
Moderate. The core technique (LDS with missing observations and EM) is well established; the novelty lies in applying it to stitch multiple partial recordings of identified neurons to recover cross-session correlations and couplings, with an empirical demonstration on real calcium imaging data. Compared to [12–15,17,18], the key distinction is inferring noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded cells without strong monotonicity assumptions and at practical population sizes.

Significance
Practical and relevant. The method addresses a pervasive experimental limitation and could materially impact how multi-session imaging datasets are analyzed, informing both inference of circuit-level correlation structure and experimental design (e.g., overlap vs. coverage trade-offs). While restricted to linear-Gaussian dynamics on fluorescence, the framework is extensible.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, common problem in systems neuroscience with a principled, simple method.
- Strong simulation results with ground truth; real-data results show substantial gains over a reasonable baseline.
- Clear demonstration that partial overlap enables recovery of cross-session correlations.
- Framework is general and could be extended (e.g., latent factors, sparsity priors).

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological novelty is limited; largely an application of standard LDS/EM to a multi-session setting.
- Real-data evaluation relies on comparisons to a model-based upper bound; direct comparisons to empirical correlations on held-out simultaneous data would be stronger.
- Sensitivity to overlap fraction, session lengths, and nonstationarity is not systematically characterized.
- Assumptions (linear-Gaussian fluorescence dynamics, diagonal Q/R) may limit biological interpretability; no comparisons to alternative latent models (e.g., spike deconvolution + Poisson LDS/GLM, GPFA).

Suggestions for improvement
- Report empirical correlation prediction on a held-out simultaneous test segment, not only versus a “fully observed” LDS.
- Analyze performance versus overlap fraction, data length, and degree of cross-population coupling; include failure modes (no overlap).
- Add comparisons to alternative latent models that handle missing data (e.g., GLM/Poisson LDS or GPFA) and to models with low-rank latent inputs (non-diagonal Q via factors).
- Discuss scalability and initialization/regularization; include results with sparsity priors on A.
- Clarify notation and typos; detail assumptions (diagonal Q/R), and address potential session drift.

Scope
The work fits NeurIPS topics in probabilistic modeling, time-series, and computational neuroscience.

Overall assessment
Technically sound and clearly presented, with a practical contribution likely to be useful to the community. Novelty is moderate, but the empirical validation on real data and the focus on non-simultaneous correlation inference are valuable.
