Summary
This paper tackles an important and timely problem: how to infer the joint dynamics (in particular noise correlations) of a neural circuit when experimental constraints only allow partial, non-simultaneous recordings. The authors propose a simple but compelling formulation—an LDS with missing observations—and “stitch” together multiple partial recordings via EM, leveraging known selection matrices Ck and modest overlap between sessions. They show on simulations and on two 2-photon calcium datasets from mouse S1 that the stitched model can predict noise correlations and cross-population activity nearly as well as a “fully observed” upper bound, and far better than a naive baseline that fits sessions independently.

Relation to prior work
The paper is well positioned relative to work on connectivity inference from calcium imaging (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al.), partially observed GLMs (Pillow & Latham), low-dimensional shared-noise models (Vidne et al., Macke et al.), and anatomical stitching (Mishchenko & Paninski). The key difference is the explicit formulation of stitching across non-simultaneous sessions to recover cross-population couplings/noise correlations, without strong monotonicity assumptions between stimulus and noise correlations (cf. Wohrer et al.). The approach itself is classic (LDS + EM with missing data), but applying it to this stitching setting and demonstrating empirical success on real calcium imaging is novel and useful.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea with practical impact: use an EM-trained LDS to align and stitch multiple partial recordings and recover cross-session interactions/noise correlations.
- Strong empirical evidence: convincing simulations with known ground truth and realistic experiments on two datasets, with both per-pair and population-level metrics. The use of “fully observed” as an upper bound and an intuitive “naive” lower bound is sensible.
- The method is simple, computationally tractable for the reported scales, and easy to adopt in labs already using LDS/Kalman methods.
- Good intuition about identifiability: overlap neurons serve to align latent subspaces across sessions; the paper is explicit that some overlap materially improves performance.
- Practical utility: predicting activity of unobserved neurons from observed ones and stimuli, potentially enabling larger effective population analyses than hardware currently permits.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Modeling mismatch: The LDS is placed directly on fluorescence with diagonal innovations (Q). While the authors acknowledge this, it limits interpretability of A as functional coupling and may bias correlation structure given calcium nonlinearity and slow dynamics. A brief comparison to PLDS/GPFA or deconvolved spikes would strengthen the claim.
- Reliance on overlap: On real data, the method “failed” without overlap, and performance improves with overlap. This is practical but narrows the novelty claim; a more systematic analysis of performance vs. overlap size and conditions for identifiability would be valuable.
- Evaluation setting: The “fictional imaging scenario” splits a single continuous recording into two sessions. This does not capture cross-session drift (gains/offsets, bleaching), state changes, or day-to-day variability. The method may be more brittle in true multi-session experiments; a stress test with session-specific offsets/gains or drift would improve credibility.
- Scalability: Smoothing in a full-rank LDS scales poorly with N; experiments top out at ~200 neurons. Claims about stitching thousands of neurons would benefit from a discussion or demonstration using low-rank latent dynamics or block-structured approaches; report runtimes.
- Regularization and stability: A is high-dimensional (O(N^2)). Aside from a note about unreported sparsity penalties, there’s no explicit regularization or stability constraint. Overfitting and unstable A are risks. Including sparsity/low-rank penalties and projecting onto stable A would help.
- Metrics and uncertainty: Most comparisons are via correlation between predicted and target correlations/couplings. Adding absolute error metrics and uncertainty estimates (e.g., via the posterior) would provide a clearer picture of practical accuracy.
- Related work breadth: Consider citing/contrasting with GPFA/PLDS lines of work more explicitly, where shared latent factors can bridge across partially observed units; also recent scalable state-space identification methods.

Quality
Technically sound for the stated model. Claims are supported by appropriate experiments, with honest discussion of limitations (e.g., need for overlap). Some methodological details (initialization, regularization, stability checks, runtime) are light and should be expanded. The interpretation of A as “functional couplings” from fluorescence should be toned down or validated.

Clarity
Overall clear and well organized. The description of EM with session-specific Ck and the bounds (naive vs. fully observed) is easy to follow. I suggest:
- Add a brief section on computational complexity and runtime.
- Explicitly state how stability of A is enforced during learning.
- Summarize sensitivity to overlap, T per session, and noise levels.

Originality
Moderate-to-strong. The modeling machinery is standard, but the problem formulation—stitching across non-simultaneous partial recordings to recover cross-population noise correlations—and the empirical validation are novel and important for current experimental practice.

Significance
High. If robust, this approach could immediately extend the effective reach of calcium imaging and related modalities, enabling labs to characterize larger circuit dynamics without new hardware. The simplicity of the method and strong empirical results on real data make it likely to be adopted and built upon.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the amount of overlap and to session-specific gain/offset drift? Can you include a controlled experiment with synthetic drift and a real-data control (e.g., block-specific re-scaling)?
- Do you enforce stability of A during EM? If so, how?
- What are typical runtimes and memory usage as a function of N, T, and number of sessions?
- Can you report results with a sparsity or low-rank prior on A, and/or a low-dimensional latent augmentation to model unobserved common input (improving scalability)?
- How does stitching perform when stimuli differ across sessions, or in spontaneous activity?

Pros
- Conceptually bold and practically useful idea; immediate impact.
- Strong evidence on real data that noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs can be recovered.
- Simple, general framework extensible to richer observation/dynamics models.

Cons
- Heavy reliance on overlap; limited analysis of true multi-session variability.
- Potential overfitting/scalability concerns without regularization or low-rank structure.
- Modeling on raw fluorescence limits interpretability and may bias couplings.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some limitations, the paper introduces a compelling and novel direction—stitching partial neural recordings to infer joint dynamics—and provides strong empirical support. The idea is likely to stimulate substantial follow-up work (e.g., better priors, low-rank PLDS, robustness to session drift) and has clear significance for neuroscience practice.
