**Summary**
This paper addresses the significant experimental challenge of inferring large-scale neural circuit dynamics from partial recordings. The authors propose a method to "stitch" together multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of different subsets of neurons from the same circuit. The core idea is to model the entire population, observed and unobserved, as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Data from each partial recording session is treated as incomplete observations of this larger system. The model parameters are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors validate their approach on simulated data with a known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, they create a fictional scenario where a fully-observed population is split into overlapping, non-simultaneous subsets. The method's performance is benchmarked against a "naive" approach (lower bound) and a "fully observed" model (upper bound). The results show that the proposed "stitching" method can recover coupling parameters and, most notably, predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, significantly outperforming the naive baseline.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**
1.  **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem in systems neuroscience. The inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously is a major bottleneck, and computational tools that can effectively combine partial data are of great potential significance.
2.  **Elegant Formulation:** The framing of the problem within the well-established LDS framework with missing observations is clear and elegant. It provides a principled statistical approach to a problem that might otherwise be tackled with ad-hoc heuristics.
3.  **Sound Validation Strategy:** The use of both simulated data with ground truth and real experimental data is commendable. Furthermore, the creation of "fully observed" (upper bound) and "naive" (lower bound) models for the real data provides a clear and effective way to contextualize the performance of the proposed method.
4.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The central claim—that the model can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons—is well-supported by the results. The correlation plots in Figures 2b and 3c/d show a dramatic improvement over the baseline and approach the performance of the oracle-like "fully observed" model.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Overly Simplistic Model:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a major simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper's success may be heavily tied to the slow, smooth nature of the calcium imaging data used. Real spiking activity is highly non-linear and non-Gaussian. It is questionable whether the success of this linear model would transfer to electrophysiological recordings or even to calcium imaging data with faster indicators and higher signal-to-noise ratios. The claim that this provides a "statistical description" is valid, but the limitations imposed by the linearity assumption on the interpretation of the learned coupling matrix 'A' should be discussed more critically.
2.  **Validation on "Fictional" Scenarios:** The primary validation on real data relies on taking a simultaneously recorded dataset and pretending it was recorded in two separate sessions. While this is a clever way to create a testbed with a known ground truth for correlations, it sidesteps a critical challenge of real-world application: non-stationarity. The model inherently assumes that the underlying circuit dynamics (the parameters A, Q, etc.) are stable across the different recording sessions. In a real experiment, sessions might be separated by minutes or longer, during which the animal's brain state, attention, or other factors could change, violating this core assumption. The current validation scheme cannot assess the model's robustness to such non-stationarities.
3.  **Unclear Dependence on Overlap:** The paper states that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work," mentioning that they failed to get good performance with 50% subsets (implying zero overlap) on the real data. This is a crucial practical detail that is somewhat downplayed. The theoretical intuition provided in Section 3.1 seems to suggest that as long as the network is connected, the model should work. However, the empirical results indicate a strong reliance on having a common set of neurons to anchor the coordinate systems of the different recordings. The performance of the method as a function of the percentage of overlap is a key characteristic that is missing from the analysis and would be essential for guiding future experimental design.
4.  **Scalability and Identifiability Concerns:** The paper demonstrates results on populations of up to 100 neurons. The authors note that the accuracy of parameter estimation drops with increasing population size, attributing this to "possible near degeneracy." This is a significant concern. The parameter space of the coupling matrix A scales quadratically with the number of neurons (N). For the large circuits mentioned in the discussion (e.g., thousands of neurons), the EM algorithm for this model is likely to face severe identifiability and local minima issues, even if it remains computationally tractable. The strong performance on predicting correlations, despite weaker performance on recovering parameters, suggests the model may be capturing the covariance structure effectively without uniquely identifying the underlying dynamical parameters, which may limit its utility for detailed circuit analysis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Proposes a novel and practical application of a well-understood statistical model to a significant problem in neuroscience.
*   The methodology is clearly explained and the validation, within its constraints, is rigorous.
*   The results are strong and convincingly demonstrate that the method works under the tested conditions, providing a proof of concept.
*   The paper could inspire further work on more sophisticated models for this "stitching" task.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The underlying linear model is a very strong and likely unrealistic assumption for general neural dynamics.
*   The validation does not account for non-stationarities between recording sessions, a key problem in real-world applications.
*   Critical aspects, such as the required degree of overlap between sessions and scalability to larger, more realistic circuit sizes, are not sufficiently explored.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper presents an interesting and potentially useful method. The core idea of using an LDS to combine partial observations is sound. However, the reliance on a simple linear model and the limitations of the validation strategy make it difficult to assess how well this approach would perform in more realistic experimental scenarios. The work feels like a promising first step but lacks the thorough investigation of its own assumptions and limitations that would be expected for a top-tier publication. While the idea is novel, its practical utility hinges on its robustness to factors like non-linearity, non-stationarity, and sparse overlap, none of which are adequately addressed. I am therefore leaning towards rejection, but would reconsider if the authors could provide more evidence regarding these critical points.
