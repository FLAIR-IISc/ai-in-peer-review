Summary
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations from multiple partial recordings of the same circuit when simultaneous imaging of all neurons is infeasible. The authors propose to “stitch” sessions by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where each session’s measurement matrix Ck selects the neurons observed in that session. Parameters are learned via EM (with an online mini-batch variant), using Kalman smoothing in the E step. On synthetic data the method recovers cross-session couplings and noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs. On two 2-photon datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1), the authors create a “fictional” split of a single session into two overlapping subsets and show that stitching recovers pairwise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs substantially better than a naive baseline and close to a “fully observed” upper bound. The method also improves prediction of unobserved activity given the observed subset relative to the naive approach.

Relation to prior work
The approach draws on well-established LDS/system identification with missing data, Kalman EM, and prior work on latent variable models for neural populations (e.g., latent GLM/PLDS). The contribution is not a new model class but an application and empirical demonstration that a single LDS with session-specific observation matrices can meaningfully combine partial population recordings to recover cross-session noise correlations. The paper contrasts with work on inferring connectivity from calcium imaging and methods handling subsampling; it highlights differences from [12,13,14,15] and related latent variable approaches.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria

Quality
- Soundness: The statistical machinery (LDS + EM with missing data) is standard and correctly applied. The evaluation on synthetic data includes ground-truth comparisons; on real data, performance is benchmarked against a “fully observed” model (trained on all neurons) as an upper bound and a naive baseline as a lower bound.
- Evidence: Results convincingly show gains over the naive baseline in predicting non-simultaneous correlations and activity. However, the strongest real-data results rely on a fictional split of a single recording; this avoids session-to-session nonstationarities and leverages stable cell identities, which makes the task easier than true multi-session stitching. The authors acknowledge needing overlap on real data (no-overlap failed), despite synthetic success without overlap.
- Limitations: The model assumes linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q and R, stationarity across sessions, and treats calcium fluorescence as directly observed x_t without explicit calcium/spiking dynamics. There is no theoretical identifiability analysis tailored to the multi-session setting beyond intuition. Baselines are weak; more competitive alternatives (e.g., PLDS/GPFA with missing data, subspace ID with missing observations, multi-view latent models) are absent. Runtimes and scalability are not reported.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written and well organized. The problem, model, and EM updates are described cleanly. Some notation typos in the observation model, but overall the exposition should allow an expert to reproduce results. The experimental protocol (fictional split, overlap fraction, held-out test) is adequately specified, though more detail on cross-validation and hyperparameters would help.

Originality
- The idea of using a global LDS with missing observations to combine sessions is conceptually straightforward, drawing on classic system ID. The novelty lies in framing and demonstrating this approach to predict noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs in calcium imaging. While not a new algorithmic contribution, the application and empirical validation in this context are useful and, to my knowledge, not previously established in this specific form.

Significance
- The problem is important and common in contemporary neuroscience where large circuits cannot be imaged at once. If robust, the approach could impact how labs aggregate multi-field-of-view recordings and plan overlap vs coverage tradeoffs. However, current evidence stops short of true multi-session validation with session-specific drifts and without relying on large overlaps, and comparisons to stronger baselines are missing. Thus, demonstrated significance is moderate but promising.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and practical problem; simple method with broad usability.
- Solid synthetic results with ground-truth evaluation.
- On real data, strong improvement over a naive approach; correlation matrices for non-simultaneous pairs approach a fully observed upper bound.
- Sensible analysis of overlap, population size, and prediction of unobserved activity.
- Clear discussion of limitations and possible extensions.

Weaknesses
- Real-data validation uses a fictional split of a single session; no demonstration on truly non-simultaneous sessions with realistic drift, session-specific noise/gains, or partial cell identity uncertainty.
- Dependence on overlap in practice; no-overlap stitching failed on real data, which materially limits applicability.
- Simplistic observation model for calcium; no explicit calcium/spike measurement model or correlated noise processes.
- Limited baselines; absent comparisons to PLDS/GPFA, subspace ID with missing data, or multi-view latent models (e.g., shared latent dynamics with session-specific loadings).
- No identifiability guarantees or systematic ablations showing when stitching fails (e.g., weak coupling, stimulus structure, overlap fraction).
- No runtime/scalability analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add experiments on truly separate sessions (e.g., different time blocks/days or different fields-of-view) with session-specific drifts, limited overlap, and possibly partial identity matches; include session-specific offsets/gains in the model.
- Compare against stronger baselines: PLDS/GPFA with missing data, subspace identification with missing entries, multi-view latent factor models (shared latent state, session-specific loadings), and LDS with low-rank shared latent inputs plus neuron-specific observation noise.
- Incorporate a simple calcium observation model (e.g., AR(1) fluorescence dynamics) and/or allow correlated innovations Q; report whether results persist.
- Provide an analysis of performance vs overlap fraction, coupling strength, stimulus richness, and mismatch to linear-Gaussian assumptions.
- Report computational complexity and runtimes.

Arguments for acceptance
- Tackles an important and common experimental limitation with a simple, principled tool.
- Empirical results suggest substantial practical benefit in predicting non-simultaneous correlations.
- Clear exposition; method likely to be adopted by practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond applying standard LDS+EM to a multi-session setting.
- Real-data evidence is confined to fictional splits; dependence on overlap and lack of true multi-session validation weakens claims.
- Missing comparisons to competitive latent variable baselines.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a useful and clearly demonstrated idea that could have practical impact, albeit with modest novelty and with empirical validation that should be strengthened as suggested.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
