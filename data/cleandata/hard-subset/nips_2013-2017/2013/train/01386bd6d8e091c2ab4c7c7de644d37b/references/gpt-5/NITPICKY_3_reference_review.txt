Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles a practically important problem in systems neuroscience: inferring joint population dynamics from multiple partial, non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit. The authors propose to “stitch” together sessions by fitting a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with selection matrices Ck indicating which neurons are observed in each session, and learn parameters via EM (with online-EM pre-training). They evaluate on synthetic data and two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (anesthetized and awake mouse S1), focusing on predicting cross-session noise correlations, reconstructing coupling matrices, and cross-predicting activity of unobserved neurons.

The model is a standard LDS with missing data and known selection matrices, including exogenous inputs u via B, Gaussian innovations with diagonal Q, and Gaussian measurement noise R. The key empirical finding is that one can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs; on synthetic data, the method does well even with zero overlap between subsets; on real data, some overlap is needed.

This work is close in spirit to prior latent dynamical models for population activity (e.g., LDS/PLDS/GPFA) fit via EM with missing observations, and to approaches for inferring functional connectivity from imaging and spikes. It improves upon [12] by not assuming monotonicity between noise and stimulus correlations, and is related to [13,17,18] in using latent dynamical structure to capture common input. However, the core technical mechanism (LDS with missing observations and selection matrices) is standard, and the paper does not provide new inference or identifiability results.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practically relevant problem with a simple, scalable tool that many labs could use.
- Clear empirical objective: predict cross-session correlations and cross-predict unobserved activity.
- Synthetic experiments demonstrate recovery of cross-block couplings and noise correlations; real datasets show strong improvements over a naive baseline.
- Sensible benchmarking against a “fully observed” upper bound within a fictional scenario; multiple random subsets reported.

Weaknesses and technical concerns
- Limited novelty: Fitting a single LDS across sessions with selection matrices is standard missing-data EM; the contribution is primarily an application and empirical observation. No new inference algorithm, regularization, or identifiability analysis is provided.
- Identifiability claims are not substantiated. The paper states that after recording the second half “we can identify this linear transformation, and thus identify all model parameters uniquely,” citing [21]. This requires specific observability/controllability/persistence conditions and, in practice, some overlap or additional constraints. Indeed, later the paper concedes that without overlap performance degrades on real data. Please clarify necessary/sufficient conditions and whether overlap is required.
- Equation (2) is confusing/mis-specified: y_k,t = C^k(x_k,t + d + ε_t), ε_t ~ N(0,R)? As written “ykt = Ck(xkt + d + t) t ∼ N(0,R)” is ambiguous. If R is diagonal (as suggested by element-wise updates), state this explicitly. If not, give full M-step update.
- Stationarity and stability: You set Q0 via the Lyapunov equation to enforce stationarity, but do not state how you ensure spectral radius(A) < 1 during EM. Unstable A invalidates Q0 and smoothing. Were constraints or reparameterizations used?
- Noise model: Assuming diagonal Q while attributing correlated variability to A conflates dynamics and correlated innovations. Many neural datasets exhibit substantial shared input unaccounted for by instantaneous R. Please analyze sensitivity to misspecification (e.g., with correlated Q or a low-rank latent).
- Calcium preprocessing: You model x as fluorescence but high-pass filter the traces (1 s time constant). This changes the effective observation/dynamics model; B and A estimates can be biased. Please justify or incorporate filtering in the generative model.
- External inputs: The anesthetized dataset has a defined whisker stimulus; the awake dataset does not clearly specify u_k,t. If u is absent or unmeasured, how is stimulus-locked variability handled? Are movement/behavioral covariates included?
- Overlap inconsistency: Synthetic experiments succeed with zero overlap; on real data, “we failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons.” This is a central practical limitation. Please quantify how overlap fraction affects performance, and discuss when no-overlap is possible.
- Evaluation: 
  - Correlation between estimated and “fully observed” couplings is reported, but without calibration. Negative log-likelihood on held-out data and predictive correlation for unobserved neurons are better metrics; provide these consistently across settings.
  - Selecting the “50 most correlated neurons” for the example likely inflates results; although later you do random subsets, please avoid biased selection in main figures.
  - In synthetic data, measurement noise is set “very small” (std 0.006); this is unrealistically favorable and may exaggerate recovery of A. Provide results across noise levels and stimulus strengths.
  - Baselines are weak. Compare to latent factor models across sessions (e.g., FA/GPFA/PLDS with shared latent dynamics) and to fitting separate models with shared priors or parameter tying. A “naive zero cross-block” baseline is too easy to beat.
- Practical applicability: The approach assumes cell identities across sessions are known and registered into a common index; in real multi-FOV experiments this is a significant challenge. Please discuss robustness to mismatches and potential probabilistic matching.
- Computational aspects: No complexity analysis, runtimes, or convergence diagnostics are given. EM can be slow and get stuck; what initializations and safeguards were used? Any regularization on A (e.g., sparsity, stability penalties)? You mention unreported sparsity penalties—please include.

Questions for authors
- Precisely define the observation model (eq. 2): is R diagonal? Is d per neuron and per session, or shared? How are session-specific baselines handled?
- How do you constrain A to be stable? Do you rescale after M-steps or parameterize via a stable basis?
- What is u_k,t in the awake dataset? If absent, how do you compute “noise correlations” vs total correlations?
- Can you provide results showing performance vs overlap fraction, vs population size, and vs SNR?
- What are the necessary observability/identifiability conditions under no overlap? Can you provide at least a proposition and a counterexample?

Minor issues and editorial
- Eq. (2) notation appears malformed; cleanly separate mean and noise terms.
- Clarify whether R is diagonal; M-step update given only for Rjj suggests so.
- Define all symbols at first use (e.g., 〈·〉, χ_kj, σ_kj).
- Typos/formatting: “simulatenous,” “emphasise,” inconsistent spacing around references, units (µm) formatting, and footnote marker “1” appears in the main text without formatting.
- Some references are incomplete or mismatched; ensure full bibliographic info and correct numbering.

Quality: Sound overall, but several methodological assumptions are underspecified, and key claims (identifiability, need for overlap) are not rigorously supported. Evaluation lacks stronger baselines and ablations.

Clarity: Generally readable, but equations and assumptions need precise specification; figures (not provided here) are heavily relied upon.

Originality: Modest. The core technique is a straightforward application of LDS with missing data. The contribution is empirical.

Significance: Practical relevance is high, but current form lacks rigor and breadth of comparison to influence adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common experimental limitation.
- Simple, potentially useful tool with encouraging empirical results.
- Clear message on predicting cross-session correlations.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty; standard model/inference without new theory.
- Unclear identifiability and reliance on overlap not analyzed.
- Ambiguities in model specification and stability; evaluation under favorable conditions; weak baselines.

Recommendation
Reject in current form. With clarified modeling, stronger baselines, theoretical/empirical analysis of overlap and stability, and more rigorous evaluation, this could become a solid contribution.
