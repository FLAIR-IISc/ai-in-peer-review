

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of neural circuits to infer population dynamics, including correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The authors model the problem as fitting a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) to incomplete observations, using expectation maximization with Kalman smoothing. They demonstrate their approach on synthetic data and two calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, claiming successful prediction of noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

## Strengths
The paper addresses an important practical challenge in neuroscience: how to extract maximum information from limited neural recordings. The idea of leveraging partial observations of the same neural circuit is conceptually sound and potentially useful. The synthetic data experiments provide a clear demonstration of the method's capabilities, and the application to real data shows promise. The paper is well-organized, with clear exposition of the methodological framework.

## Weaknesses

**Methodological Details:**
The paper contains several critical technical inconsistencies that undermine its scientific rigor. In Section 2.1, the linear dynamics equation (1) is written as "xkt = Ax k t−1 +Bu k t + ηt" with inconsistent spacing between variables (e.g., "Ax k t−1" should be "Ax_{k,t-1}"). This is a basic formatting error that suggests lack of attention to detail. The paper claims to use a "linear gaussian dynamical system" but later refers to it as "linear Gaussian dynamical system" in Section 2.1 without consistent capitalization. The mathematical notation throughout is inconsistent (e.g., "xkt" vs "x_{k,t}"), which would confuse readers attempting to implement the method.

**Theoretical Justification:**
The paper makes a strong claim that "we can predict the correlated dynamics of large populations of neurons even if many of the neurons have not been imaged simultaneously," but provides no theoretical guarantees for this claim. The authors reference [21] for theoretical support, but this reference discusses identifiability of dynamical systems from partial observations, not specifically for the correlation prediction task they're addressing. The paper lacks a clear theoretical analysis of why their specific approach would work better than alternatives.

**Experimental Evaluation:**
The evaluation on real data is problematic. The "fictional imaging scenario" they create (Section 3.2) is artificial and doesn't reflect the actual experimental constraints of neural imaging. The paper claims "correlation 0.92" for noise correlation prediction but doesn't specify what this correlation measures (predicted vs. true, or something else). In Figure 3d, they report "correlation 0.84 for stitchLDS, 0.15 for naiveLDS" without defining what these correlations represent. The paper also fails to provide error bars or confidence intervals for any of their reported metrics.

**Overstated Claims:**
The paper claims their method "can successfully predict the magnitude of (so-called) noise correlations," but never clearly defines what constitutes "noise correlations" in their framework. They cite [4,5] as evidence of large variability in evoked responses, but don't distinguish between stimulus-driven and spontaneous variability in their analysis. The paper also overstates the novelty of their approach, claiming it's different from [12,13,14,15], but doesn't adequately explain why these previous methods don't address the same problem.

**Minor but Critical Errors:**
- In Section 2.1, the measurement model equation (2) has a formatting error: "ykt = C k(xkt + d+ t) t ∼ N (0, R)" should be "y_{kt} = C_k(x_{kt} + d_k) + \epsilon_t, \epsilon_t \sim N(0, R)".
- The paper states "the mean x0 and covariance Q0 of the initial state xk0 were chosen such that the system is stationary," but doesn't specify how they determined these values.
- In Section 2.2, they claim "the calcium time-series were high-pass filtered with a time-constant of 1s" but don't provide the filter implementation details or how this affects the correlation analysis.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the problem addressed is important and the general approach has merit, the paper contains numerous technical errors, inconsistent notation, and lacks sufficient theoretical grounding. The experimental evaluation is poorly described, with critical details missing that would prevent replication. The claims about performance (e.g., "correlation 0.92") are not sufficiently supported by the presented evidence. The paper's minor technical flaws, combined with the lack of theoretical justification for their approach, make it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work.

The authors should address these issues before the paper can be considered for publication. Specifically, they need to: (1) fix all mathematical notation inconsistencies, (2) provide clear definitions of key terms like "noise correlations," (3) add theoretical justification for why their approach works, (4) provide proper error analysis for all reported metrics, and (5) clarify the experimental setup for the real data analysis.

This paper falls short of the technical rigor expected at NIPS, and the minor errors and inconsistencies undermine the authors' otherwise interesting contribution to the field.
