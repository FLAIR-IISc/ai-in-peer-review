This paper proposes a method to infer the joint dynamics of a neural population from multiple partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to leverage the strong coupling between nearby neurons, recording from different locations, and “stitch” together these observations using a latent dynamical system, specifically a linear Gaussian model. The authors demonstrate the method’s effectiveness on both simulated and real calcium imaging data, showing that it can accurately predict correlations between neurons not simultaneously recorded.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a practical limitation in current neuroimaging techniques – the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations. The problem is clearly stated, and the proposed solution is intuitively appealing. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, contrasting it with studies focusing on connectivity inference from simultaneous recordings, or methods applicable to different data characteristics (e.g., higher sampling rates allowing interpolation). The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system simplifies the problem and allows for tractable inference via the EM algorithm, which is a reasonable starting point. The experimental validation with real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex adds significant credibility.

The strengths of the paper include: (1) **Addressing a relevant problem:** The ability to combine partial recordings is crucial for studying large neural circuits. (2) **Clear methodology:** The model and inference procedure are well-explained. (3) **Strong validation:** The method performs well on both simulated and real data, demonstrating its practical utility. (4) **Benchmarking:** The comparison against both an upper bound (fully observed model) and a lower bound (naive approach) provides a clear assessment of the method's performance. (5) **Detailed experimental setup:** The description of the experimental data (acquisition parameters, preprocessing steps) is thorough.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses: (1) **Model simplicity:** The linear Gaussian assumption is a strong one. Neural dynamics are inherently non-linear, and calcium signals are not Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's unclear how much the results rely on this simplification. Exploring more complex models (e.g., incorporating non-linearities) would strengthen the work. (2) **Scalability:** The EM algorithm, while tractable, may not scale well to very large populations (thousands of neurons). Addressing scalability concerns is important for broader applicability. (3) **Parameter tuning:** The paper doesn’t provide extensive details on parameter tuning (e.g., regularization strength). A more systematic exploration of parameter sensitivity would be valuable. (4) **Overlap requirement:** The findings suggest significant overlap between imaging sessions is necessary for good performance. This could limit the flexibility of the method in experimental designs where complete overlap isn’t feasible. (5) **Limited novelty of the LDS framework:** While applying an LDS to this specific problem of stitching partial recordings is a reasonable approach, the LDS itself isn't novel. The core contribution lies in the application and demonstration of its effectiveness in this context.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant practical challenge in systems neuroscience.
* The proposed method is well-motivated and clearly explained.
* The results on both simulated and real data are compelling.
* The benchmarking against appropriate baselines provides a solid evaluation.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The linear Gaussian assumption is a strong simplification.
* Scalability of the method may be a concern for very large populations.
* The need for substantial overlap between recordings could limit applicability.
* The novelty is incremental, building upon existing LDS frameworks.



**Overall assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis. While the method relies on simplifying assumptions, it demonstrates a practical approach to leveraging limited experimental data for understanding the dynamics of large neural circuits. The strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I believe it warrants acceptance to NIPS, albeit potentially with a lower confidence score reflecting the incremental nature of the novelty. I recommend acceptance contingent on the authors briefly addressing the scalability concerns in the camera-ready version.
