- Paper proposes a statistical "stitching" method to infer joint neural population dynamics from multiple partial (non-simultaneous) recordings using a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) fit with EM and Kalman smoothing.

- Distinguishes from prior functional connectivity and sub-sampling work by explicitly handling non-simultaneous recordings without relying on assumptions like monotonic stimulus-noise correlation relationships.

- Quality: Method is technically sound with clear mathematical formulation, appropriate baselines, and validation on simulated and real calcium imaging datasets.

- Clarity: Paper is well-organized and clearly written, with sufficient mathematical detail and effective figures illustrating key results.

- Originality: The stitching idea for combining partial recordings to infer joint dynamics is novel in this context.

- Significance: Addresses an important neuroscience challenge, enabling prediction of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons and extracting more value from existing data.

- Experimental validation: Thorough tests across multiple population sizes and two experimental conditions (anesthetized vs. awake), showing predictions approaching a fully observed model.

- Model limitation: Uses a simple linear Gaussian LDS which may not capture complex nonlinear neural dynamics; no evidence shown that more complex models would improve stitching performance.

- Overlap requirement: Method needs session overlap (e.g., 60% overlap works, 50% fails), which could limit practical applicability and experimental design flexibility.

- Real data scale: Demonstrations use 80–200 neurons, leaving unclear how the method scales to the thousands of neurons that constitute "large cortical circuits."

- For acceptance: Tackles a significant problem with practical implications, is novel, rigorously validated, reproducible, and outperforms baselines.

- Against acceptance: Concerns over model simplicity, overlap dependency, and insufficient evidence of scalability to very large populations.

- Recommendation: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify limitations on model complexity and scalability.

- Requested revisions: More clearly articulate limitations regarding model complexity and scalability, discuss adaptations for larger populations, and give concrete examples of integration into experimental workflows.

- Overall assessment: High-quality, original, and significant work likely to impact computational neuroscience; recommended for acceptance.
