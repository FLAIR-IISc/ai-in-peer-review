

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of neural circuits to infer joint dynamics of the entire population, claiming the ability to predict correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors use a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) model with missing observations, framed as a standard expectation-maximization problem with Kalman smoothing.

The paper addresses an important challenge in neuroscience: the limitation of current imaging technology to capture only a small fraction of neurons in a circuit. While the problem is well-motivated, the method builds on established techniques (LDS, EM algorithm) rather than introducing fundamentally novel computational approaches. The theoretical foundation draws from system identification literature (e.g., [21, 22, 23]), which suggests that a dynamical system can be reconstructed from partial observations.

**Strengths:**
1. The paper clearly articulates a meaningful problem in neuroscience.
2. The mathematical formulation is technically sound and properly grounded in established system identification principles.
3. The simulation results demonstrate the method's ability to recover coupling matrices and noise correlations in synthetic data.
4. The experimental validation on real calcium imaging data shows promising results compared to a naive baseline.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Overstated claims and limited real-world validation:** The paper's strongest claim - that the method can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons - is validated only through a "fictional imaging scenario" where neurons that were actually recorded simultaneously are artificially split into two sessions. This does not constitute true validation of the method's ability to handle genuinely non-simultaneous recordings. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not adequately address how this fictional scenario differs from the real-world challenge they claim to solve.

2. **Inadequate comparison to established baselines:** The paper compares only to a naive baseline that simply averages coefficients across sessions and sets non-overlapping pairs to zero. This is an unreasonably weak baseline that fails to acknowledge existing methods for handling partial observations. For example, the authors cite [12] which addresses noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, but they do not compare their method to this or other established approaches. A proper comparison would include state-of-the-art methods for handling partial neural recordings.

3. **Unjustified simplifications:** The paper models calcium fluorescence directly as the state variable, ignoring the nonlinear cascade of neural couplings, calcium dynamics, fluorescence measurements, and imaging noise [16, 6]. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they do not demonstrate that this simplification is sufficient for their claims. In fact, their results on real data show only modest correlation (0.84) between predicted and fully observed correlations, suggesting the model may not capture the complexity of real neural dynamics.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification for key assumptions:** The paper claims that "with enough data, the maximum likelihood estimate of the model parameters lets us identify the true joint dynamics of the whole population up to an invertible linear transformation." However, the paper does not provide a rigorous analysis of the identifiability conditions or how the required amount of data scales with population size. This is particularly concerning given the authors' own observation that correlation between stitched and fully observed couplings drops with increasing population size.

5. **Questionable significance:** While the problem is important, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that their method provides a substantial improvement over existing approaches. The results show the method outperforms a naive baseline but fall short of the theoretical upper bound. Given that the fully observed model is unattainable in practice, it's unclear how much practical benefit the method provides.

**Recommendation:**

This paper presents an interesting application of established system identification techniques to a neuroscience problem. However, the claims are overstated, the validation is limited to a fictional scenario, and the comparison to baselines is inadequate. The method's reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification that may not capture the complexity of neural dynamics. While the approach has potential, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that it constitutes a meaningful advance over existing methods for handling partial neural recordings.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Using truly non-simultaneous recordings (not a fictional split of simultaneously recorded data)
2. Comparing to relevant existing methods for handling partial neural observations
3. Providing a more rigorous analysis of the model's limitations and identifiability conditions
4. Demonstrating a clear advantage over existing approaches in real-world scenarios

As it stands, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The authors have made a promising start, but the current work lacks the rigor and thorough validation required to demonstrate a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend rejection on the grounds of insufficient validation and overstated claims.
