- Summary: Proposes an LDS-based method to "stitch" multiple partial recordings to infer joint neural dynamics and predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons; validated on synthetic and calcium-imaging data, outperforming naive averaging.

- Strength 1: Clearly motivated problem with a tangible experimental example (whisker barrel).

- Strength 2: Strong methodological foundation grounded in latent dynamical system theory with theoretical justification.

- Strength 3: Rigorous validation on synthetic and real data, including comparisons to naive baseline and a fully observed upper bound.

- Strength 4: Significant practical impact by enabling combination of partial recordings to study larger effective populations.

- Strength 5: Clear presentation with effective figures and thoughtful experimental design.

- Weakness 1: Uses a simplistic linear Gaussian model and lacks exploration of extensions to spiking dynamics or nonlinear models.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient quantitative analysis of failure modes and how much session overlap is required for reliable stitching.

- Weakness 3: Validation using simulated separate sessions from simultaneous recordings is somewhat artificial and realism is not discussed.

- Weakness 4: Limited engagement with recent related NIPS work on neural decoding and analysis (e.g., 2022–23 papers).

- Weakness 5: Scalability concerns—computational complexity of EM and applicability to thousands of neurons are not addressed.

- Overall assessment: Well-executed, important contribution with sound results, though bounded by model simplicity and limited discussion of limitations.

- Recommendation: Accept.

- Final thoughts: Good fit for NIPS, likely to impact neuroscience practice; authors should more fully address model limitations, overlap requirements, and scalability in revision.
