## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of inferring the dynamics of large neural populations when only partial, non-simultaneous recordings are available – a common limitation in experimental neuroscience. The authors propose a statistical framework based on a latent dynamical system (specifically, a linear Gaussian LDS) to "stitch" together data from multiple partial observations, allowing them to estimate correlations and predict activity across neurons not simultaneously recorded. They demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can accurately recover correlations and even predict unobserved neural activity, outperforming a naive approach that treats each recording session independently.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of neural connectivity inference and dynamical systems modeling. They acknowledge relevant approaches like Bayesian methods for calcium imaging ([6]), information-theoretic methods ([7]), and techniques for handling subsampled observations ([12, 13, 14, 15]).  Crucially, they highlight how their method distinguishes itself by specifically addressing the challenge of inferring connections *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons, which is not addressed in prior work. The reference to Takens’ embedding theorem ([23]) is insightful, grounding the theoretical justification for their approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practically important problem:** The limitations of current imaging technology necessitate methods for leveraging incomplete data, making this work highly relevant to the field.
* **Sound theoretical foundation:**  The use of a latent dynamical system provides a principled statistical framework for integrating data. The link to embedding theorems bolsters the theoretical justification.
* **Strong empirical validation:**  The authors provide results on both synthetic and real data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their method. The comparison to a “naive” approach and a fully observed model clearly demonstrates the value of their stitching approach.
* **Clear presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The methods section is detailed, allowing for reproducibility.
* **Demonstrated performance:** The ability to predict noise correlations and unobserved neural activity is a significant achievement, and the results presented are convincing.  The correlation values reported (0.73, 0.92, 0.84) are quite strong.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model simplicity:**  The reliance on a linear Gaussian LDS is a limitation. Real neural circuits are highly non-linear and exhibit non-Gaussian activity patterns. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of how the method might be extended to more complex models (e.g., incorporating non-linearities or using a different dynamical system framework) would be valuable.
* **Parameter sensitivity:** The method likely has parameters (e.g. regularization, initial conditions) that could significantly affect performance. The paper doesn’t adequately address parameter tuning or sensitivity analysis.
* **Scalability:**  While the authors mention scaling issues in relation to existing work ([13]), the computational complexity of EM-based parameter estimation in LDS models can still be a concern for very large populations.  A discussion of computational scalability and potential optimization strategies would be helpful.
* **Overlap requirement:**  The finding that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for performance, while realistic, is not thoroughly explored. What's the minimum overlap required? How does performance degrade with decreasing overlap?
* **Limited biological insight:** While the method successfully *predicts* correlations, it doesn't offer much new biological insight into *why* those correlations exist.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the presented results.  The experimental design and analysis appear rigorous.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and clear. The mathematical notation is standard and understandable.
* **Originality:**  The combination of latent dynamical systems with the specific problem of stitching together partial recordings is novel. While the individual components are not new, the application to this specific problem is a valuable contribution.
* **Significance:**  The results are significant. The ability to infer dynamics from incomplete data has broad implications for understanding neural circuit function, particularly given the limitations of current experimental techniques. This is a strong contribution with the potential to be widely used.



**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a crucial and practical limitation in neural data analysis.
* Provides a statistically sound and well-validated method.
* Offers a significant improvement over existing approaches.
* Clear and well-written presentation.

**Con:**

* Relies on a relatively simple model (linear Gaussian LDS).
* Limited discussion of parameter sensitivity and computational scalability.
* Biological interpretation is limited.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field. The proposed method is innovative, well-motivated, and effectively demonstrated on both simulated and real data.  While the limitations regarding model complexity and scalability are worth noting, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with minor revisions.**

I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions that address the weaknesses outlined above. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Discuss potential extensions to non-linear dynamical systems.
2.  Provide a brief discussion of parameter tuning and sensitivity.
3.  Acknowledge and potentially explore the impact of the overlap requirement.
4.  Consider a brief discussion of scalability concerns for very large populations.
