This paper presents a method for inferring the joint dynamics of neural populations from multiple, partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to leverage the assumption that nearby neurons are strongly coupled and, therefore, combining recordings from different locations can provide a more complete picture of the circuit’s activity. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, and demonstrate its ability to predict correlations between neurons not simultaneously recorded, both in simulated and experimental data from the mouse somatosensory cortex. This builds on previous work aiming to infer functional connectivity from neural data [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11], but crucially extends it to scenarios with non-simultaneous observations, a limitation of existing methods. The approach is compared to a naive method and a theoretical upper bound (fully observed data) to demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Quality:** The technical foundation of the paper is sound. The use of a latent dynamical system is well-motivated and the application of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is standard. The derivation of the updates for the model parameters is clear, and the authors acknowledge limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption. The experimental validation using both simulated and real data strengthens the claims. The method’s performance relative to upper and lower bounds is convincingly demonstrated. However, the choice of a linear Gaussian model, while simplifying computation, might limit its applicability to more complex neural dynamics. A more thorough discussion of the limitations imposed by this simplification would be beneficial. The statistical significance of improvements over the naive approach in the experimental data could also be strengthened with more rigorous statistical testing (e.g., significance testing on the correlation values).

**Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly articulates the problem and motivation. The methods section provides sufficient detail for reproduction, though the mathematical notation could benefit from more consistent formatting. The results are presented clearly, with informative figures and tables. The discussion section effectively summarizes the findings and their implications. However, the description of the “fictional imaging scenario” in Section 2.3 feels slightly awkward and could be streamlined. Furthermore, while the simulation details are described, a more precise specification of the coupling parameters and stimulus properties would improve reproducibility.

**Originality:** The problem of inferring connectivity from partial observations is not entirely new [12, 13, 14, 15], but the specific focus on non-simultaneous recordings and the application of a latent dynamical system for stitching together data represent a novel contribution. The authors successfully address a gap in existing literature by providing a method for combining information from multiple recordings to estimate the full circuit dynamics. The application to real calcium imaging data further enhances the originality and impact of the work. The paper effectively differentiates itself from existing methods and provides a clear statement of its novelty.

**Significance:** The results are significant. The ability to infer correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons has important implications for understanding neural circuit function. This is particularly relevant in the context of large-scale brain mapping, where complete simultaneous recordings are often impossible. The demonstrated success on real data suggests that this method could be a valuable tool for neuroscientists studying complex neural circuits. The technique could be instrumental in constructing data-driven models and testing theories of circuit computation. The potential to reconstruct circuit dynamics from incomplete data is a substantial advance and could lead to new insights into brain function.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and challenging problem in neuroscience.
*   Presents a technically sound and novel method.
*   Demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real data.
*   Provides clear and well-supported results.
*   Has the potential to impact the field of neural circuit analysis.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a simplified linear Gaussian model may limit its generalizability.
*   Statistical significance of experimental results could be strengthened.
*   The description of the “fictional imaging scenario” is slightly convoluted.
*   More detailed specification of simulation parameters would improve reproducibility.
