### **Summary**

This paper presents a computational method for "stitching" together multiple, partial recordings of a neural circuit to infer the dynamics of the full population. The authors model the joint activity of all neurons (both observed and unobserved) as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Data from different recording sessions, which capture non-identical subsets of neurons, are treated as incomplete observations of this shared latent state. Using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, the model parameters—including the functional coupling matrix—are estimated. The key contribution is demonstrating that this framework can predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is validated on simulated data and then on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, where a "fictional" scenario of partial recordings is created from a fully observed dataset.

### **Strengths**

The problem addressed by this paper is of high significance to experimental neuroscience. As the scale of neural recordings grows, the ability to record all relevant neurons in a circuit simultaneously remains a major bottleneck. A robust method for combining partial, non-simultaneous recordings would be a powerful tool for building more complete models of circuit dynamics. The core idea of using a shared latent dynamical system to link the different recording sessions is elegant and well-motivated by classical results in system identification. The paper is clearly written, and the experiments, particularly the use of "fully observed" and "naive" models as upper and lower performance bounds, provide a clear context for evaluating the proposed method's efficacy. The demonstration that the model can predict cross-correlations with reasonable accuracy in the fictionalized experimental data (Fig. 3d, 4a) is compelling.

### **Weaknesses**

Despite the novelty of the problem and the elegance of the approach, the paper suffers from a substantial number of methodological oversimplifications, underdeveloped analyses of key limitations, and a lack of technical detail that, in aggregate, undermine the confidence in its conclusions.

1.  **Inappropriate Model Choice:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is motivated solely by "computational tractability." This model is a poor match for the underlying biophysics. Neural activity is characterized by discrete, non-negative spiking events (point processes), and the resulting calcium dynamics are highly nonlinear. The authors acknowledge this mismatch but dismiss it by stating their model "is able to predict correlations." However, this predictive power may be limited to the specific datasets and preprocessing choices used here. Modeling the raw fluorescence signal directly, after a simple high-pass filter, confounds the slow dynamics of the GCaMP6s indicator with the much faster neural dynamics the model purports to capture. More principled approaches that explicitly model the cascade from spikes to calcium to fluorescence exist (e.g., [6, 16], which the authors cite but do not follow) and would have made the inferred parameters more interpretable.

2.  **Artificial Evaluation Scenario:** The primary validation on real data relies on a "fictional imaging scenario" where a single, simultaneous recording is split into two pseudo-sessions. This setup conveniently bypasses the most difficult challenge of real-world multi-session analysis: non-stationarity. The underlying circuit properties (captured by matrix A) are unlikely to remain perfectly constant across experimental sessions separated by hours or days, due to factors like plasticity, attention, or arousal state. The current evaluation provides no evidence that the method would be robust to such session-to-session variability.

3.  **Critical Limitations are Under-analyzed:** The authors make two concerning admissions that are not adequately explored.
    *   First, they state: "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This implies the method is critically dependent on a subset of "anchor" neurons being present in all recordings. This is a major practical limitation. A thorough analysis of how performance degrades as the size of this overlap decreases is essential for understanding the method's practical utility but is completely absent.
    *   Second, regarding parameter recovery on real data: "Interestingly, this correlation [between stitched and fully observed couplings] drops with increasing population size, perhaps due to possible near degeneracy of parameters for large systems." This is a significant red flag. It suggests that as the model is scaled to larger, more biologically interesting circuits, the parameter estimates become less reliable. The authors' brief speculative explanation is insufficient; this finding questions the scalability of the approach and warrants a much more detailed investigation.

4.  **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper omits several details crucial for reproducibility.
    *   The fitting procedure is vaguely described as "a variant of online-EM...followed by full batch EM." The specific variant of online EM, the minibatch size, the learning rate schedule, and the criterion for switching to full-batch EM are not specified.
    *   The model assumes diagonal innovation noise (Q), a strong simplification implying that all instantaneous correlation in the innovations is zero. The authors mention this can be relaxed but do not do so or discuss the potential impact of this assumption.
    *   In the discussion, a "sparsifying penalty" is mentioned in passing, but this is not described in the Methods section. Was regularization used to obtain the presented results? If so, what form did it take and how were hyperparameters chosen?

5.  **Minor Formatting and Clarity Issues:** The reference list contains several formatting inconsistencies (e.g., extra commas in [1], [2], [3], etc.). Footnote 1 contains an important result about the naive model that should be integrated into the main text.

### **Decision**

I recommend **rejection** for this paper.

While the problem is important and the high-level idea is sound, the execution and evaluation are not up to the standard expected for NIPS. The reliance on a simplistic linear model for highly nonlinear neural data is not sufficiently justified. More critically, the evaluation is based on an artificial scenario that ignores the key challenge of non-stationarity, and the paper fails to adequately analyze or discuss major limitations that were discovered during the authors' own experiments (the need for neuron overlap and the degradation of parameter estimates with scale). The accumulation of these methodological weaknesses and overlooked details casts significant doubt on the robustness and practical applicability of the proposed method. The work is a promising proof-of-concept but requires a more realistic model, a more rigorous evaluation on true multi-session data, and a more transparent analysis of its limitations before it is ready for publication.
