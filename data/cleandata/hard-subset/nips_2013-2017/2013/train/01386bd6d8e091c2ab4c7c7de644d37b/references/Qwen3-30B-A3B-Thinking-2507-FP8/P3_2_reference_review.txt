

# Peer Review: "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary of the Paper and Relation to Previous Work

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record all neurons in large neural circuits due to technical constraints. The authors propose a "stitching" method that leverages the strong coupling between neurons to infer the joint dynamics of an entire neural population from multiple partial recordings. The core idea is to model the neural population dynamics using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS), where each recording session observes a subset of neurons, and the model parameters are estimated using an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing.

The authors demonstrate that their method can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, outperforming a naive approach that simply averages parameters from separate sessions. They validate their method on both simulated data (with known ground truth) and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

This work builds upon theoretical results in system identification (references [21, 22, 23]) that show dynamical systems can be reconstructed from partial observations. It relates to several recent NIPS papers on neural population analysis, particularly those addressing connectivity inference from partial data (e.g., [6, 7, 13]). The authors correctly identify the gap in existing literature: while many methods exist for inferring connectivity from simultaneous recordings, none address the problem of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings from the same circuit.

## Strengths

1. **Well-motivated problem with clear significance**: The paper addresses a genuine limitation in current neuroscience methodology. The authors convincingly argue that their approach could help build data-driven models of large cortical circuits, which is a major goal in systems neuroscience.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper correctly situates its work within the broader theoretical framework of system identification from partial observations, citing relevant literature (e.g., [21, 22, 23]). The linear LDS model, while simple, is a well-established tool in the field.

3. **Rigorous empirical validation**: The authors provide thorough validation on both simulated data (where ground truth is known) and real experimental data. The comparison against the "fully observed" model (theoretical upper bound) and the naive approach (lower bound) is particularly strong.

4. **Clear methodology**: The paper's technical presentation is clear and well-organized. The description of the LDS model, the EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing, and the evaluation metrics are all well-explained.

5. **Practical relevance**: The authors demonstrate the method on real calcium imaging data, showing it works in the context of real experimental constraints (e.g., slow calcium dynamics, limited recording time).

## Weaknesses

1. **Over-reliance on a simple LDS model**: While the authors acknowledge that the LDS is a simplified model, they don't adequately address how this limitation affects their results. The neural dynamics they're modeling are inherently nonlinear (spiking activity, calcium transients, fluorescence), and the linear model might miss important aspects of the true dynamics. The authors cite [16, 6] as more realistic models, but they don't explore how incorporating these would affect their stitching method's performance. A more thorough comparison with nonlinear models would strengthen the paper.

2. **Inadequate comparison to related work**: The authors mention [12] as a related method that "requires the strong assumption that noise correlations are monotonically related to stimulus correlations," but they don't provide a direct comparison of their method with [12] on the same datasets. This is a significant omission, as [12] specifically addresses the problem of inferring noise correlations from non-simultaneous recordings. The authors should have benchmarked against this method to better establish the novelty and advantages of their approach.

3. **Insufficient analysis of overlap requirements**: The paper mentions that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for stitching to work, but it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how much overlap is needed or how performance degrades as overlap decreases. The authors state they "found that some overlap was critical" but don't quantify this. A systematic analysis of the trade-off between overlap and coverage would be valuable for experimental design.

4. **Limited discussion of scalability**: The authors apply their method to two sessions, but the paper doesn't address how the method scales to more than two sessions or larger populations. Given that the goal is to stitch together data from multiple partial recordings of a large circuit, the scalability of the method is crucial. The authors mention this as an open problem but don't provide any analysis of the computational complexity or performance as the number of sessions increases.

5. **Superficial discussion of implications for neural computation**: While the paper claims to help "build data-driven models of large cortical circuits and help test theories of circuit function," the discussion of these implications is quite brief and lacks specific examples of how their method could be used to test particular theories.

## Evaluation of Criteria

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound and well-executed on the problem it addresses. The methodology is clearly presented and the experiments are well-designed. However, the use of a simple LDS model without adequate discussion of its limitations weakens the quality of the claims. The authors are careful to evaluate their method against appropriate baselines, which is commendable.

**Clarity**: The paper is very well-written and clearly organized. The problem statement, methodology, and results are all presented in a logical flow. The figures are informative and well-labeled. The paper provides sufficient detail for experts to reproduce the results, though some additional details about the hyperparameter choices and computational resources would be helpful.

**Originality**: The core idea of "stitching" together non-simultaneous recordings is novel in the context of neural circuit analysis. While the theoretical underpinnings draw from system identification literature, the application to neural population dynamics from partial recordings is new. However, the paper would be stronger if it more clearly distinguished its approach from [12] and other related work.

**Significance**: The significance is high for the neuroscience community, as it addresses a fundamental limitation in current experimental practice. The method could potentially enable researchers to make better use of existing data and reduce the need for longer recording sessions. However, the significance for machine learning and NIPS would be stronger if the authors had made more explicit connections to broader machine learning challenges in handling partial observations or missing data.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Addresses a genuine and important problem in neuroscience with clear potential impact.
- The method is theoretically sound and well-validated on both simulated and real data.
- The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
- The results demonstrate clear advantages over a naive baseline.

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The reliance on a simple LDS model without addressing how it might limit the method's applicability to more complex neural dynamics.
- Inadequate comparison to the closest related work [12].
- Insufficient analysis of the critical parameter of overlap between recording sessions.
- Limited discussion of how the method would scale to larger populations and more recording sessions.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with significant potential impact for neuroscience, particularly for researchers working with calcium imaging data. The core idea of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings is novel and well-executed. However, the paper would be significantly stronger with more thorough comparison to the related work of Wohrer et al. [12], a more detailed analysis of the overlap requirements, and a better discussion of how the method would scale to larger neural circuits.

While the linear LDS model is a reasonable starting point, the authors should acknowledge more explicitly the limitations of this model and discuss how incorporating more biophysically realistic models (as they mention in the discussion) would affect their results. The paper would also benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational requirements for the method.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address these limitations, particularly the comparison to [12] and the analysis of overlap requirements.

## Specific Suggestions for Revision

1. Add a direct comparison between the proposed method and the method by Wohrer et al. [12] on the same datasets, highlighting the advantages of the proposed approach.

2. Include a systematic analysis of how the performance of the stitching method depends on the degree of overlap between recording sessions.

3. Expand the discussion of the LDS model limitations and how more realistic models might be incorporated into the stitching framework.

4. Add a brief analysis of the computational complexity of the method and how it scales with the number of recording sessions.

5. Provide more specific examples of how the stitching method could be used to test particular theories of neural circuit function.

With these revisions, this paper would be a strong contribution to NIPS, bridging the gap between machine learning and neuroscience with a practical and theoretically grounded method.
