**Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"**

**Summary**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a large circuit. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, partial recordings of the same circuit, taken at different times, to infer the dynamics of the entire population. The core idea is to model the full neural population's activity as a latent state in a linear dynamical system (LDS). Each partial recording session is then treated as an incomplete, noisy observation of this shared latent state. Using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, the model parameters (functional connectivity, stimulus response, noise covariance) are estimated from all partial recordings collectively. The authors validate their method on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, they cleverly create a "fictional imaging scenario" where a full recording is split into two overlapping partial recordings, allowing them to benchmark their stitched model's predictions against a "fully observed" model trained on the complete data. The main finding is that this method can successfully recover functional connectivity and, remarkably, predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, performing vastly better than a naive approach and approaching the accuracy of a model with access to the full, simultaneous data.

The work relates to previous research on inferring functional connectivity and modeling population dynamics, but distinguishes itself by specifically tackling the problem of non-simultaneous recordings. Unlike prior work [12] which makes strong assumptions about the relationship between signal and noise correlations, this method infers the latent dynamics directly. It presents a practical and scalable approach compared to others like latent GLMs [13] for the population sizes considered here.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The problem addressed is of immense practical importance. Experimentalists are constantly pushing the boundaries of imaging/recording technology, but fundamental physical and biological constraints remain. A computational tool that can virtually expand the experimental field-of-view by combining non-simultaneous recordings would be a game-changer. It enables the study of larger circuit dynamics than is currently possible and provides a clear methodology for designing experiments to maximize circuit coverage over time. The potential for this work to influence future experimental design and data analysis in neuroscience is very high.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Problem Formulation:** While the use of Linear Dynamical Systems and the EM algorithm is standard, their application to this "stitching" problem is highly novel and elegant. Framing the entire population as a single latent state and the separate recordings as partial observations with different measurement matrices (`C_k`) is a powerful and intuitive formulation. This represents a significant conceptual contribution.

3.  **Rigorous and Convincing Validation:** The evaluation of the method is thorough and compelling.
    *   The use of simulated data with a known ground truth connectivity matrix clearly demonstrates that the method can, in principle, recover the correct parameters, including the crucial off-diagonal blocks corresponding to non-simultaneously observed pairs.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is an excellent methodological choice. It allows the authors to create a situation with a known "best-case" performance (the fully observed model) on messy, real-world data. This provides a much more convincing demonstration of the method's utility than simulation alone.
    *   The comparison against both an upper bound ("fully observed") and a lower bound ("naive") model provides clear context for interpreting the performance and demonstrates the substantial gains achieved by the stitching approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, the methods are described concisely, and the results are presented in a logical progression from simulation to real data. The authors are clear about the model's assumptions and limitations.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The paper notes that for the real data, some overlap between the recording sessions was "critical for stitching to work," whereas the simulation was performed with non-overlapping sets. This is a crucial practical detail that is not fully explored. The performance of the algorithm is likely highly dependent on the number of "anchor" neurons shared between sessions. A more systematic analysis of how performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) degrades as the percentage of overlap decreases would significantly strengthen the paper and provide vital guidance for experimental design. Without this, it is unclear how much overlap is "enough."

2.  **Limitations of the Linear Gaussian Model:** The authors correctly acknowledge that the LDS is a simplified statistical description. However, neural dynamics are inherently nonlinear and spiking activity is non-Gaussian. While the model is shown to work well for predicting second-order statistics (correlations) in slow calcium data, its limitations should be discussed more deeply. For instance, would the model fail in a circuit dominated by strong, nonlinear inhibitory interactions? Could it misattribute complex dynamics to simple linear couplings? While beyond the scope of this paper to implement a full nonlinear model, a more thorough discussion of the potential failure modes of the linear approximation would be valuable.

3.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper suggests the method could be used to map entire circuits like a whisker barrel (thousands of neurons). However, the experiments are performed on populations up to 100 neurons. The M-step of the EM algorithm for a standard LDS involves matrix operations that scale cubically with the number of state dimensions (`N`). The authors should briefly address the computational scalability of their approach to the much larger populations mentioned in the introduction and discussion, and whether approximations (e.g., assuming sparse or low-rank connectivity) would be necessary.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, clever, and highly significant solution to a pressing problem in systems neuroscience. The strengths—in particular the importance of the problem, the elegance of the solution, and the rigor of the validation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily avenues for future work and refinement. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and the results are likely to be of great interest to both computational and experimental neuroscientists. It advances the state of the art by providing a practical tool to overcome a major experimental bottleneck.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
