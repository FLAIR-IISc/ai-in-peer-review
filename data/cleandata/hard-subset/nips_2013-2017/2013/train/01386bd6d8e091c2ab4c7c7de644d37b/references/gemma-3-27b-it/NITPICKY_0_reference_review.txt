This paper presents a method for stitching together calcium imaging data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics and correlations. The core idea – leveraging overlapping observations and a latent dynamical system framework – is intuitively appealing, given the limitations of current imaging technology. The authors position their work relative to existing methods for connectivity inference and sub-sampled observations, highlighting the novelty of addressing correlations between *non-simultaneously* observed neurons.

While the motivation is strong and the proposed approach conceptually sound, the paper suffers from a number of issues, ranging from minor formatting inconsistencies to concerns about the rigor of the experimental validation. The presentation, while generally clear, could be significantly improved with greater attention to detail.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** The problem of inferring dynamics from incomplete neural recordings is highly relevant to current neuroscience research.
*   **Sound Conceptual Framework:** The application of a latent dynamical system is a reasonable approach for tackling this problem.
*   **Demonstration on Simulated Data:** The simulations provide a useful proof-of-concept and demonstrate the potential of the method to recover ground truth parameters.
*   **Application to Real Data:** Applying the method to real calcium imaging data is a positive step towards demonstrating practical utility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Formatting and Style:** Numerous minor formatting issues detract from the professionalism of the manuscript. For instance, inconsistent use of equation numbering, several instances of dangling spaces within equations, and the lack of a concluding sentence in several paragraphs. The citation style appears inconsistent.
*   **Insufficient Detail in Model Specification:** While the authors state they use a linear Gaussian dynamical system, crucial details are glossed over. The choice of diagonal covariance matrix *Q* is mentioned as a simplification, but the implications of this assumption for capturing correlated noise are not fully discussed. Similarly, the justification for the initial state selection (x0 = 0, Q0 satisfying the Lyapunov equation) feels perfunctory.
*   **Limited Explanation of EM Implementation:** The EM algorithm is mentioned but the practical implementation details – especially concerning the online-EM variant and mini-batch selection – lack sufficient explanation. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness and efficiency of the fitting procedure.
*   **Experimental Validation Concerns:** The “fictional imaging scenario” created for evaluating performance on real data is problematic. The c=60% overlap requirement, and the observation that performance dropped without overlap, isn’t well motivated and feels *ad hoc*. The definition of “fully observed” and “naive” approaches as upper and lower bounds is questionable; a more thorough justification is needed.
*   **Overstatement of Results:** The claim of “accurately recovering” noise correlations seems strong, especially given the correlation of 0.38 between the stitched and fully observed coupling matrices (Figure 3b). The phrasing throughout the Results section occasionally leans towards over-interpretation.
*   **Missing Error Bars:** Figures 2b, 2c, 3c, and 3d lack error bars, hindering the assessment of statistical significance. The standard error mentioned in the simulation section for predicting unobserved activity is welcomed, but this level of rigor should be applied consistently.
*   **Related Work Discussion:** The discussion of related work is superficial. Several cited papers [12, 13, 14, 15] are dismissed with brief justifications that do not fully address their potential applicability or limitations in the context of the authors' method. A more nuanced discussion is warranted.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a relevant and challenging problem in neuroscience.
*   The proposed method shows promise for leveraging incomplete neural recordings.
*   The simulated data results are encouraging.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The paper suffers from numerous minor flaws in formatting, style, and explanation.
*   The experimental validation is not entirely convincing and relies on questionable methodological choices.
*   The results are sometimes overstated and lack sufficient statistical support.
*   The discussion of related work is superficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential of the presented ideas, the paper, in its current form, falls short of NIPS standards. The numerous minor flaws, coupled with concerns regarding the rigor of the experimental validation and the overstatement of results, outweigh the strengths of the work. I recommend **rejection**. The authors should address the aforementioned weaknesses – particularly regarding model specification, experimental validation, and clarity of presentation – before resubmitting. A more thorough and nuanced discussion of related work is also crucial.
