### NIPS Review

**Paper:** Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper addresses a fundamental and pressing challenge in systems neuroscience: how to model the dynamics of a large neural circuit when it is only possible to record from small subsets of neurons at any given time. The authors propose a statistical "stitching" method to combine multiple, non-simultaneous partial recordings into a single, coherent model of the entire population's dynamics. The core idea is to model the full N-neuron circuit as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each partial recording is then treated as an incomplete, noisy observation of this underlying latent state. The model parameters, including the functional coupling matrix that describes interactions between all neurons (even those never recorded together), are inferred using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The method's efficacy is demonstrated on both simulated and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. A key contribution is the clever evaluation strategy on real data: the authors create a "fictional imaging scenario" by splitting a fully-observed dataset into two partial, non-simultaneous sessions. This allows them to benchmark their "stitched" model against a "fully observed" upper bound (trained on all data simultaneously) and a "naive" lower bound (models trained on each session separately). The results convincingly show that the proposed method can accurately recover functional couplings and, notably, predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never simultaneously recorded, performing far better than the naive approach and approaching the performance of the ideal, fully-observed model.

**Evaluation:**

This is a strong, well-executed paper that presents a novel and highly significant solution to a real-world problem in the analysis of neural data. The method is simple, elegant, and shown to be effective.

**Quality:**

The paper is of high technical quality.

*   **Soundness:** The choice of a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations is a principled and appropriate framework for this problem. The use of the standard EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing for inference is technically sound and well-established.
*   **Evaluation:** The experimental design for validation is a major strength. The simulation provides a clear proof-of-concept with known ground truth. The "fictional imaging scenario" for the real data is an excellent way to quantitatively assess performance in a setting where ground truth is unavailable. The use of well-defined upper and lower bounds ("fully observed" and "naive" models) provides a clear and convincing context for the results.
*   **Completeness:** The work is presented as a complete story, from theoretical motivation and model formulation to thorough validation on both synthetic and complex, real-world biological data. The authors are careful to evaluate not just parameter recovery (the coupling matrix) but also the recovery of emergent network properties like noise correlations, which are of greater interest to neuroscientists.
*   **Honesty:** The authors are upfront about the limitations of the linear Gaussian model, noting that it is a simplified statistical description. They also candidly report that their method "failed to get good performance" without any overlapping neurons between sessions, which is an important and honest finding for practitioners.

**Clarity:**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

*   **Organization:** The manuscript is logically structured. The introduction perfectly frames the problem with a concrete experimental example. The methods section is concise and provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand the implementation. The results are presented in a logical progression, moving from the intuition behind the method to simulation and then to real-world data.
*   **Readability:** The language is precise and accessible. The core intuition—that shared dynamics allow one partial observation to constrain the latent state of the other, which is resolved by the second observation—is communicated effectively. The figures, as described, seem well-designed to illustrate the main points (e.g., comparing the recovered coupling matrices).

**Originality:**

The paper is highly original in its problem formulation and application.

*   **Novelty of Problem:** While methods for handling missing data in dynamical systems exist, this paper is, to my knowledge, the first to frame the problem of combining non-simultaneous neural recordings in this way and provide a practical, scalable solution.
*   **Relation to Prior Work:** The authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to existing literature. They clearly distinguish their approach from those that rely on stimulus correlations (Wohrer et al., 2010), those that do not scale to large populations (Pillow & Latham, 2008), and those that address different problems like anatomical reconstruction (Mishchenko & Paninski, 2012). The contribution is not in inventing a new statistical model from scratch, but in the novel and impactful application of an existing framework to solve a new and important problem.

**Significance:**

The significance of this work is very high, with potential for major impact in both computational and experimental neuroscience.

*   **Impact:** This method directly addresses a critical technological limitation in neuroscience. It provides a concrete pathway to move beyond the constraints of the microscope's field-of-view, enabling the construction of functional models of circuits that are orders of magnitude larger than what can be recorded simultaneously. This could be transformative for understanding computation in circuits like cortical columns or the olfactory bulb.
*   **Practical Usefulness:** The proposed LDS-based method is computationally tractable and relatively straightforward to implement. It is likely that other researchers and experimentalists will be able to adopt and use these ideas.
*   **Future Directions:** The paper opens up several interesting avenues for future research, which the authors acknowledge in their discussion. These include developing principled experimental design strategies (e.g., optimizing the trade-off between overlap and total coverage) and extending the framework to more biophysically realistic non-linear models. The success of this simpler model provides a strong foundation and motivation for such extensions.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The paper is very strong, but could be improved slightly by addressing the following points:

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The authors state that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work." This is a crucial practical parameter. The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic analysis of how performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases. A plot showing performance vs. overlap percentage would be highly informative for experimentalists planning their recordings.
2.  **Limitations of Linearity:** The authors acknowledge that the LDS model is a simplification. While it clearly works well for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), a brief discussion of what specific aspects of neural dynamics it might fail to capture (e.g., higher-order correlations, non-linear interactions) would add valuable context.
3.  **Identifiability:** The intuition in Section 3.1 regarding identifiability up to a linear transformation is correct but could be stated more formally with a citation to the relevant system identification literature (e.g., Ljung, 1999 or Katayama, 2005) for interested readers. The current explanation is good, but this would add technical rigor.

**Summary of Strengths/Weaknesses:**

*   **Pros:**
    *   Addresses a highly significant and unsolved problem in neuroscience.
    *   Proposes an elegant, principled, and effective method.
    *   Excellent and convincing validation on both simulated and real data.
    *   Exceptionally clear and well-written.
    *   High potential for real-world impact on experimental science.

*   **Cons:**
    *   The crucial role of overlapping neurons is mentioned but not systematically analyzed.
    *   The discussion on the limitations imposed by the linear-Gaussian assumption could be slightly expanded.

**Recommendation:**

This is a clear-cut accept. The paper presents a novel, important, and well-validated contribution that will be of great interest to the NIPS community, particularly those at the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience. It is a prime example of how machine learning techniques can be thoughtfully applied to break through existing barriers in scientific discovery.
