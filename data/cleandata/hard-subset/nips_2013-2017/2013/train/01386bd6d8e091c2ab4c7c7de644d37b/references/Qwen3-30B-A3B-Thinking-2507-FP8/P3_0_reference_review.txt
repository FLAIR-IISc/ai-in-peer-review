

# Peer Review: "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary

This paper presents a novel statistical approach for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, even for neuron pairs that were never recorded simultaneously. The core insight is that by modeling the neural population dynamics as a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations, one can leverage the strong coupling between neurons to predict correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The authors demonstrate their method on both synthetic data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their approach outperforms a naive baseline and approaches the performance of a "fully observed" model that would require simultaneous recording of all neurons.

This work builds on previous efforts in neural population analysis (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) but addresses a critical gap: inferring functional connections between neurons recorded in separate sessions. Unlike previous methods that require strong assumptions (e.g., [12] assumes monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations), the authors' approach makes minimal assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Problem Addressed**: The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in neural recording technology - the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations. This is a critical bottleneck in systems neuroscience, and the authors provide a practical computational solution that leverages existing experimental capabilities.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The authors ground their approach in a solid theoretical framework of latent dynamical systems with missing observations. Their explanation of how the model can recover parameters for unobserved neurons (via the invertible linear transformation argument) is intuitive and well-justified.

3. **Rigorous Validation**: The paper provides comprehensive validation through both synthetic data (with ground truth available) and real experimental data. The use of upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) bounds for performance comparison is particularly strong.

4. **Practical Impact**: The results demonstrate that the method works on real neural data with meaningful accuracy (e.g., correlation 0.84 between stitched model predictions and fully observed model for real data). This suggests the method could be immediately useful to experimental neuroscientists.

5. **Clear Presentation of Methodology**: The technical details of the LDS model and EM fitting procedure are well-explained and should be reproducible. The use of a simple linear model as a proof of concept is appropriate given the goal of demonstrating the core principle.

## Weaknesses and Critical Analysis

1. **Model Assumptions and Limitations**:
   - The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't sufficiently address how violations of these assumptions might impact performance. Neural circuits often exhibit nonlinear dynamics, and calcium imaging data is inherently nonlinear and non-Gaussian (as noted in their own references [16, 6]).
   - The paper assumes that the same neural circuit is being imaged across sessions, but doesn't address potential sources of non-stationarity between sessions (e.g., changes in animal state, adaptation, or neural plasticity). This could be a major limitation in real applications.

2. **Experimental Design and Overlap Requirement**:
   - The method requires some overlap between recording sessions (they found 60% overlap was necessary for good performance), but the paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how the amount of overlap affects performance. A systematic exploration of the minimum required overlap would strengthen the argument.
   - The fictional imaging scenario used for benchmarking (splitting simultaneously recorded neurons into two "sessions") is somewhat artificial. In real experiments, the overlap might be more limited, and the authors don't demonstrate performance with lower overlap (e.g., 30% or less).

3. **Comparison to Alternative Methods**:
   - While the paper contrasts with [12], [13], [14], and [15], it doesn't engage with other recent work on handling partial observations in neural data. For example, the work by [24] on inferring functional connectivity with priors on network topology could be relevant.
   - The paper doesn't explore whether more sophisticated models (e.g., nonlinear LDS, state-space models with sparsity priors [6]) would improve performance, despite noting in the discussion that "more realistic models can help improve the accuracy."

4. **Statistical Rigor**:
   - The paper reports correlations between predicted and true values but doesn't provide statistical tests for whether the improvements over the naive baseline are significant (e.g., p-values for the correlation differences).
   - The performance metrics for the real data (e.g., correlation 0.84) are impressive, but the paper doesn't discuss the absolute magnitude of the noise correlations being predicted. How large are these correlations in the real data? Are they biologically meaningful?

5. **Theoretical Limitations**:
   - The paper claims that "with the right amount of overlap" the method can work, but doesn't provide a theoretical bound on how much overlap is required or how the performance scales with overlap. This is important for experimental design.
   - The paper doesn't address the potential for degeneracy in the parameter estimation for large systems, which they mention in the discussion ("this correlation drops with increasing population size, perhaps due to possible near degeneracy of parameters for large systems").

## Specific Technical Comments

1. **Model Formulation**: The measurement model (equation 2) assumes that the measurement matrix C^k is known and that the recording sites correspond to identified cells. While this is often true for calcium imaging, the paper could better address cases where the exact neuron identities are not known (e.g., if imaging is done without cell identification).

2. **EM Algorithm Details**: The paper mentions using "online-EM with randomly selected mini-batches" but doesn't provide details on how this variant differs from standard EM. A brief description would help with reproducibility.

3. **Real Data Analysis**: The paper states that "the anesthetized dataset was collected during an experiment in which the C2 whisker of an anesthetized mouse was repeatedly flicked randomly in one of three different directions," but doesn't specify how the stimulus was structured or whether the stimulus was the same across sessions. This could impact the validity of the stitching approach.

4. **Noise Correlation Definition**: The paper defines noise correlations as "correlations in variability of neural activity after subtracting contributions due to the stimulus," but doesn't explain how this subtraction is performed. This is critical for reproducibility.

5. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper claims computational tractability, but doesn't provide runtime comparisons with other methods (e.g., how long does it take to fit the model to a dataset of 100 neurons vs. 200 neurons?).

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that addresses a significant practical problem in neuroscience with a well-motivated and rigorously validated solution. The core idea is novel and important, and the results demonstrate clear utility for real neural data.

**Quality**: The paper is technically sound with appropriate validation. The methodology is well-explained and the experiments are comprehensive. However, the assumptions of the linear Gaussian model are significant simplifications, and the paper could better address their potential limitations.

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and well-organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The figures are informative and help convey the key findings.

**Originality**: The core idea of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings is novel. While related to theoretical results on system identification from partial observations [21, 22, 23], the authors make a significant contribution by applying this to neural data in a practical way.

**Significance**: This work has the potential to significantly impact experimental neuroscience by enabling more efficient use of existing imaging technology. It provides a practical method that can be immediately applied to existing datasets, and the approach could be extended to other modalities and neural systems.

## Recommendations for Revision

1. Address the limitations of the linear Gaussian model more thoroughly, including how non-Gaussianity and nonlinearity might affect performance.

2. Provide a more systematic analysis of the overlap requirement (minimum overlap needed, how performance scales with overlap).

3. Include statistical significance tests for the performance improvements over the naive baseline.

4. Discuss the potential impact of session-to-session non-stationarity on the method's performance.

5. Consider adding a brief discussion of how this approach might integrate with more sophisticated neural models (e.g., incorporating spiking dynamics or calcium dynamics as in [6]).

## Pro and Con Arguments for Acceptance

**Pro:**
- Addresses a fundamental limitation in neural recording technology with a practical computational solution
- Demonstrated on real neural data with strong performance
- Well-grounded in theoretical system identification literature
- Clear methodology and comprehensive validation
- High potential for immediate impact in neuroscience

**Con:**
- Relies on significant simplifying assumptions (linear, Gaussian)
- Limited discussion of model limitations and potential failure modes
- The fictional imaging scenario for benchmarking is somewhat artificial
- Doesn't fully explore how the method might scale to larger populations or different neural systems

## Final Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While there are some limitations to the model assumptions, the core contribution is significant, well-validated, and addresses a critical problem in neuroscience. The authors have demonstrated that the method works on real neural data with meaningful accuracy, and the approach could become a valuable tool for experimental neuroscientists. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides a clear contribution to the field. With the suggested revisions (particularly regarding model limitations and overlap requirements), this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The work represents a meaningful step toward overcoming the physical limitations of current imaging technology, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated that their approach can successfully predict correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons in real neural data. This is a significant advance over previous methods that cannot handle non-simultaneous recordings, and it opens the door to more efficient use of existing experimental data.
