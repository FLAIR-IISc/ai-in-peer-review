### Summary

This paper introduces a novel and powerful computational method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous partial recordings. The core problem addressed is a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: experimental techniques like 2-photon imaging can only record from a fraction of the neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose to "stitch" together these partial datasets by modeling the entire neural population as a single latent dynamical system (here, a linear dynamical system, or LDS). Each recording session is treated as a partial, noisy observation of this underlying system. The parameters of the full system, including the functional couplings between neurons that were never observed together, are learned using an Expectation-Maximization algorithm. The authors validate their approach on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. In the latter case, they create a "fictional" scenario from a fully observed dataset to provide a ground truth for evaluation. The key result is that their method can successfully predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a feat impossible with standard analysis techniques.

### Strengths and Arguments for Acceptance

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper's primary strength lies in its bold and highly original approach to a critical, long-standing problem. The idea of computationally overcoming physical limitations in neural recording by stitching together partial observations is transformative. If this approach proves generalizable, it could fundamentally change how large-scale neuroscience experiments are designed and interpreted, enabling the construction of circuit-level models on a scale that is currently inaccessible. This is not an incremental improvement but a potential paradigm shift in data analysis for systems neuroscience. The conceptual contribution is significant and aligns perfectly with the kind of high-impact, forward-looking research that NIPS should showcase.

2.  **Elegant Conceptual Framework:** The formulation of the problem within a latent variable framework is both elegant and intuitive. Treating the full population dynamics as the latent state and each recording session as a different partial observation (via the `C_k` matrices) is a clean and powerful way to formalize the problem. The paper provides a clear intuition for why the model is identifiable: unobserved neurons in one session act as latent variables whose structure can be constrained and ultimately resolved by observing them in another session.

3.  **Compelling Proof-of-Concept:** Despite the use of a simple linear model, the results are surprisingly strong. The validation on both synthetic and real data is well-designed, and the use of "fully observed" and "naive" models as upper and lower performance bounds, respectively, provides a clear and convincing demonstration of the method's efficacy. The ability to recover the coupling matrix structure and, more importantly, to accurately predict noise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs (Fig 3c,d) is a striking result that clearly demonstrates the value of the approach.

4.  **Clarity and Honesty:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors clearly motivate the problem, explain their methodology, and are transparent about the model's limitations, such as its linear-Gaussian assumption and the empirical need for some neuronal overlap between sessions on their real dataset. This candor strengthens the paper's credibility.

### Weaknesses and Arguments against Acceptance

1.  **Model Simplicity:** The choice of a linear dynamical system is a major simplification. Neural dynamics are known to be highly non-linear, and the relationship between calcium fluorescence and underlying spiking is also complex and non-linear. The LDS model cannot capture these features, which limits the direct biological interpretation of the learned coupling parameters (`A` matrix). While sufficient for a proof-of-concept, the performance of this approach on circuits with strongly non-linear dynamics remains an open question.

2.  **Incomplete Validation of Requirements:** The authors note that for the real data, some overlap between the recorded populations was necessary for the method to work well (they failed with 50% subsets with no overlap but succeeded with 60% subsets with overlap). This is a crucial practical detail, but the paper does not systematically explore the conditions required for successful stitching (e.g., the trade-off between degree of overlap, strength of coupling, recording duration, and signal-to-noise ratio). This leaves the practitioner with limited guidance on how to design an experiment to leverage this technique.

### Recommendation: Accept

I strongly recommend acceptance. The weaknesses identified are minor in the context of the paper's overall contribution. The use of a simple LDS is a reasonable starting point for introducing a fundamentally new idea; its success here is encouraging and opens the door for future work using more sophisticated models. Similarly, the incomplete characterization of the method's boundary conditions is a natural limitation of an initial study and points toward clear avenues for follow-up research.

This paper is a prime example of high-risk, high-reward research. It tackles a grand challenge with a creative, bold, and conceptually beautiful idea. The potential impact on a major scientific field is immense. I am willing to overlook the methodological simplifications and incomplete validations because the novelty and significance of the core contribution are outstanding. This work introduces a new way of thinking about experimental data and is likely to inspire a great deal of follow-up research, both in developing more advanced stitching algorithms and in designing new experimental paradigms. It is precisely the kind of innovative and thought-provoking paper that NIPS should champion.
