**Summary**

This paper proposes a computational method for "stitching" together multiple, partial, non-simultaneous recordings of a neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population. The core idea is to model the full population activity using a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). For each partial recording session, the observed neurons are treated as noisy measurements of a subset of the full system's state, while the unobserved neurons are treated as latent variables. The model parameters are estimated across all sessions using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors validate their approach on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, where they create a "fictional" multi-session scenario by splitting a single large recording. The method is shown to successfully predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, significantly outperforming a "naive" baseline that models each session independently.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Significant Problem:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem in systems neuroscience. The inability to record simultaneously from all neurons in a circuit is a major experimental bottleneck. A method to computationally overcome this limitation would be of great value to the field.

2.  **Principled Approach:** The formulation of the problem within the well-established framework of latent dynamical systems is clear and logical. Grounding the intuition in system identification theory (e.g., Takens' theorem) provides a solid theoretical underpinning for why such a reconstruction might be possible in the first place.

3.  **Rigorous Evaluation Framework:** The use of "fully observed" and "naive" models as upper and lower performance bounds, respectively, is commendable. This provides a clear and quantitative context for evaluating the performance of the proposed "stitching" method, allowing the reader to judge how much of the achievable performance is being captured.

4.  **Clear Empirical Results:** On both simulated and real data, the method demonstrates a clear improvement over the naive baseline, particularly in the crucial task of predicting correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons. The visualizations in Figures 2 and 3 effectively illustrate the method's ability to recover underlying structure.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Oversimplified Model Assumptions:** The choice of a Linear Dynamical System (LDS) is a major point of concern. Neural dynamics are fundamentally nonlinear, and neural activity (spikes) is non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge that the LDS is a statistical description rather than a mechanistic model, the paper's central claims rest entirely on the performance of this highly simplified model. It is difficult to assess whether the success reported is specific to the datasets chosen, which may have correlation structures simple enough to be captured by a linear model, or if it would generalize to circuits with more complex, nonlinear dynamics. The manuscript would be substantially stronger if it included a discussion of the potential failure modes of applying an LDS to neural data and the conditions under which it is expected to work.

2.  **Limitations of the Validation on Real Data:** The validation on experimental data relies on artificially splitting a single, simultaneously recorded dataset into multiple "fictional" sessions. While a clever approach for creating a test case with a known ground truth (the "fully observed" model), this procedure sidesteps a critical challenge in real-world applications: non-stationarity across recording sessions. Experiments conducted on different days or even hours apart may involve changes in animal state (e.g., attention, arousal), recording quality, or underlying synaptic efficacies. The current validation protocol implicitly assumes perfect stationarity, which is unlikely to hold in practice. The paper's claims about applicability to real experimental data are therefore not fully supported.

3.  **Understated Requirement for Overlapping Neurons:** In Section 2.3, the authors mention that the method "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial finding that significantly constrains the method's applicability and should be highlighted and analyzed, not relegated to a brief note in the methods section. The problem of aligning two datasets with a subset of known "anchor" neurons is fundamentally less challenging than inferring relationships between two completely disjoint sets. The paper does not systematically investigate how performance depends on the size or properties of this overlapping population, which is a key parameter for practical experimental design.

4.  **Scalability Concerns:** The experiments are performed on populations of up to 100 neurons, yet the motivation is to model entire cortical circuits spanning thousands of neurons. The results in Figure 4 (right panel) show that the accuracy of inferring coupling parameters *decreases* with population size. This trend is worrying and suggests that the method may not scale well to the very problem domains it is motivated by. The potential for overfitting and the computational demands of the EM algorithm in much larger systems are not sufficiently discussed.

**Recommendation**

This paper presents an interesting and well-motivated application of a standard machine learning model to a challenging problem in neuroscience. The core idea is elegant, and the evaluation is carefully structured. However, my enthusiasm is tempered by several significant concerns. The reliance on a simple linear model for a complex nonlinear system, combined with a validation protocol that assumes perfect stationarity, raises doubts about the method's robustness and real-world utility. Furthermore, the unstated but critical dependence on having overlapping neurons between sessions limits the scope of the problem being solved.

While the work is a valuable proof-of-concept, it feels preliminary. The claims of being able to reconstruct the dynamics of large cortical circuits are not yet fully substantiated by the evidence provided. For the paper to be acceptable, it would need to more directly address the limitations of the LDS model, provide evidence of robustness to at least minor non-stationarities, and more thoroughly characterize the performance as a function of neuron overlap. As it stands, the work presents an established method applied to a novel but highly idealized version of a real-world problem.

**Decision: Weak Reject**
