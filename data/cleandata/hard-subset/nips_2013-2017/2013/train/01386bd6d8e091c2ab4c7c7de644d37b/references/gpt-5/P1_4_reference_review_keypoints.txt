- Proposes fitting a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with session-specific observation matrices Ck to stitch partial, non-simultaneous neural recordings and predict noise correlations for neuron pairs never recorded together.  
- Parameters (A,B,Q,R,d) are learned by EM (online/batch variants) and inference uses Kalman smoothing; known inputs u can be incorporated.  
- Synthetic experiments show recovery of cross-session couplings, noise correlations, and prediction of unobserved neurons from observed ones and stimuli.  
- Real-data validation uses controlled “fictional” splits of simultaneous two-photon recordings (with overlap) and compares to a naive baseline and a fully observed upper bound.  
- Conceptually simple, computationally tractable framework leveraging standard LDS/EM tools; easy to extend and implement.  
- Strong synthetic validation with parameter recovery metrics, correlation prediction, and cross-prediction performance.  
- Real-data controlled split provides interpretable upper/lower bounds and reduces confounds.  
- Thoughtful discussion of assumptions, potential extensions (latent inputs, sparsity), and experimental design trade-offs (overlap vs. coverage).  
- Main gap: no demonstration on truly non-simultaneous sessions with potential nonstationarities (cell drift, photobleaching, behavioral changes).  
- Overlap is claimed critical but not quantified; need performance vs. overlap fraction (including no-overlap) and explanation of discrepancy between synthetic no-overlap success and real-data claims.  
- Strong modeling assumptions (linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q) may be unrealistic for calcium fluorescence; robustness to misspecification should be tested (e.g., spiking→calcium simulation, correlated process noise, deconvolution).  
- Identifiability/observability conditions are discussed informally; paper would benefit from formal propositions or clearer theoretical conditions for unique cross-session coupling recovery.  
- Baselines are weak; recommend additional comparisons (shared-factor/LDS with alignment, CCA alignment, Wohrer-style stimulus-based predictions, multi-session GPFA).  
- Notation/clarity issues: fix Equation (2) formatting, state whether R is diagonal, clarify definition of overlap fraction and fictional split, specify time step and stimulus design, and define how residual correlations are computed.  
- Scalability concerns: provide computational complexity and runtime scaling with N, T, K and discuss strategies for large N (sparse/block A, subspace ID).  
- Statistical reporting: add absolute-error metrics (RMSE, slope/intercept), confidence intervals, and calibration assessments in addition to correlation coefficients.  
- Overall technical quality is sound for the LDS/EM approach, but real-world applicability is tempered by lack of true multi-session validation and limited baselines.  
- Originality: stitching partial recordings with session-specific observation matrices in this calcium imaging context appears novel and practically useful.  
- Significance: potentially high if validated on real multi-session data; currently promising but preliminary for broader adoption.  
- Pros: addresses an important problem with a usable method, strong synthetic results, and sensible controlled real-data bounds.  
- Cons: missing true non-simultaneous session demos, limited baselines, unformalized theoretical conditions, and restrictive modeling assumptions.  
- Key questions for authors: can you stitch truly non-simultaneous sessions (with registration/drift), how does performance scale with overlap/session length/stimulus richness, are Q/R diagonal in experiments, what if process noise is correlated, and how does runtime scale to N~1000?  
- Recommendation: borderline accept (leaning weak accept) contingent on adding at least one real multi-session experiment, broader baselines, and clarifications/theory/robustness analyses.
