**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a large circuit. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, non-simultaneous, partial recordings from the same neural circuit to infer a single, coherent model of the entire population's dynamics. The core of their method is to model the full population activity as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is treated as a partial, noisy observation of this full latent state. The model parameters, including the functional connectivity matrix, are learned from the collection of partial datasets using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The authors validate their approach on both simulated and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex. A key contribution of their validation strategy is a "fictional imaging scenario" where a simultaneously recorded dataset is artificially split into non-simultaneous, overlapping subsets. This allows for direct comparison of the stitched model's predictions against an upper bound (a model trained on the full, simultaneous data) and a lower bound (a "naive" approach of combining models trained on each subset independently). The main result is that the proposed method can accurately predict statistics of the joint dynamics, such as noise correlations, even between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This work connects the established theory of system identification from partial observations to a pressing, practical problem in the analysis of large-scale neural data.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The application of a latent state-space model is a principled and well-suited framework for the problem of integrating partial observations. The use of the EM algorithm for parameter estimation is standard and appropriate for this model class. The claims are well-supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The validation on simulated data with known ground truth demonstrates that the method can, in principle, recover the correct parameters and correlations. The analysis on real experimental data is particularly strong due to the well-designed "fictional imaging scenario," which provides clear upper and lower performance bounds and allows for a quantitative assessment of the method's efficacy. The results robustly show that the stitching method approaches the performance of the "fully observed" model, far surpassing the naive baseline.

A potential weakness lies in the simplicity of the linear Gaussian model. Real neural dynamics are nonlinear and non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how these simplifying assumptions might impact performance in more complex scenarios. For instance, would severe nonlinearities break the ability to identify the correct linear transformation between latent spaces inferred from different sessions? Despite this simplification, the model's success on real calcium imaging data suggests it is a powerful statistical descriptor, even if it is not a mechanistic one.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction provides excellent motivation by framing the problem within a concrete experimental context. The methods are described with sufficient detail for an expert to understand the implementation. The progression from the intuition behind the method, to its demonstration on synthetic data, and finally to its application on real data, creates a clear and compelling narrative. The figures are well-designed, informative, and effectively support the main claims of the paper.

**Originality:** While the technical components—LDS models and the EM algorithm—are well-established, their application to solve the problem of combining non-simultaneous neural recordings is highly novel. The authors have correctly identified a significant gap in the current toolkit for computational neuroscience. They do an excellent job of citing related work and clearly articulating how their approach differs from previous methods for handling subsampled data, which either make stronger assumptions (e.g., [12]) or address different data structures (e.g., anatomical connectivity [15] or dense temporal subsampling [14]). The originality lies in the formulation of the problem and the demonstration of a simple, effective solution.

**Significance:** The potential impact of this work is substantial. The limited field-of-view of current recording technologies is a major bottleneck for understanding circuit-level computations in the brain. This paper presents a practical and computationally tractable method to overcome this limitation, effectively creating a "virtual" microscope with a wider field of view. If this method proves to be broadly applicable, it could enable researchers to build and test models of much larger neural circuits than previously possible, using existing experimental equipment. This would represent a significant advance for systems neuroscience. The problem is important, the proposed solution is elegant, and the results suggest it is a viable approach for real-world data analysis.

One area for future exploration, noted by the authors, is the systematic characterization of how experimental design choices, such as the degree of overlap between recording sessions, affect performance. The current validation also does not account for potential non-stationarities that might exist between genuinely separate recording sessions (e.g., due to changes in brain state). Addressing how the model handles such challenges would further increase its practical significance.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant and unsolved problem of high relevance to both the machine learning and computational neuroscience communities.
*   Presents a novel and principled application of established machine learning techniques (LDS, EM) to solve this problem.
*   The validation is methodologically sound and rigorous, using both simulated and real data with well-defined performance bounds.
*   The results are strong and clearly demonstrate the method's ability to infer correlations between non-simultaneously observed variables.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The core algorithmic components (LDS, EM) are not new; the novelty is in the application.
*   The linear Gaussian model is a simplification of neural dynamics, and the limitations imposed by this assumption are not fully explored.
*   The dependence on key parameters like the amount of overlap between sessions is mentioned but not systematically investigated.
