Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics when only partial, overlapping subsets of neurons can be recorded at a time (e.g., across multiple 2-photon imaging sessions). The authors propose a “stitching” approach based on a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with session-specific observation matrices that select the recorded neurons. Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant for efficiency). The method aims to recover cross-population couplings and, critically, to predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. They demonstrate on simulations and on two calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1) by creating a fictional two-session split from a simultaneously recorded dataset to obtain an “upper bound” (fully observed model) and compare against a “naive” baseline (separate models per session with no cross-session couplings).

The work is related to prior LDS/PLDS/GPFA–style latent variable models for population activity (e.g., Macke et al. NIPS 2012; Yu et al. NIPS 2009) and to GLM-based network inference in electrophysiology (e.g., Pillow et al. 2008). It differs by addressing the partially observed, multi-session setting and explicitly estimating cross-session couplings and noise correlations. It also connects to system identification with missing data and to recent attempts to infer functional connectivity from calcium imaging (Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012). The “stitching” problem has been less explored in these literatures, making the application novel for neuroscience.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Highly practical for current imaging technologies that cannot capture entire cortical microcircuits at once.
- Conceptual clarity: A simple, transparent LDS with known selection matrices per session suffices to demonstrate feasibility; the EM learning procedure is standard and reproducible in principle.
- Empirical demonstration: On simulations, the method recovers off-diagonal coupling structure and predicts noise correlations between non-simultaneously observed neuron pairs markedly better than a naive baseline; on real data, predicted correlation matrices approach the fully observed upper bound.
- Insight on overlap: The paper identifies overlap across sessions as important, and reports failure without overlap—useful guidance for experimental design.
- Quantitative evaluation: Compares to both an optimistic upper bound (fully observed) and a naive lower bound; provides cross-prediction of held-out neural activity.

Weaknesses
- Evidence limited to “fictional” splits: The real-data experiments simulate multi-session splits from a single simultaneous recording. This avoids core challenges of true multi-session data (nonstationarity across days, changes in stimulus statistics or behavior, slow drifts, bleaching, ROI instability). As a result, the central claim—effective stitching across actual sessions—remains only partially validated.
- Model mismatch and restricted noise structure: The LDS is fit directly to fluorescence, assumes diagonal innovation and measurement noise, and omits calcium/spiking nonlinearity. While the authors discuss extensions, they do not test non-diagonal Q or latent common-input dimensions, which could be crucial for correctly attributing noise correlations and avoiding biased couplings.
- Identifiability and necessity of overlap: The intuition section implies latent-space alignment can be recovered after a second session; in practice, the paper reports poor performance without overlapping neurons. The conditions under which stitching is identifiable (amount of overlap, shared stimulus drive, rank conditions) are not formalized or empirically characterized.
- Limited baselines: The comparison is to a naive LDS baseline. There is no comparison to established latent variable approaches (e.g., GPFA/PLDS/SLDS) adapted to multi-session missing data, subspace identification with missing observations, or to models with low-rank latent common input. This makes it hard to assess how much the gain is due to “stitching” per se vs. simply sharing parameters across sessions.
- Evaluation scope: Main metrics are correlations between inferred and ground-truth (sim) or fully observed (real) correlation matrices and activity cross-prediction. There is no predictive log-likelihood on held-out data, no calibration of uncertainty, and limited analysis of sensitivity to session length, population size, overlap fraction, stimulus drive, or sampling rate.
- Selection bias: Some results use the 50 most correlated neurons, which may overstate performance; although later analyses use random subsets, the main figures emphasize the favorable subset.
- Practical details: Limited discussion of computation/runtime scaling to larger populations and many sessions, initialization/regularization choices, and hyperparameter selection.

Quality
Technically sound within the LDS framework and the EM implementation. Results are consistent and well presented. However, the empirical support is weakened by the absence of true multi-session experiments and by limited ablations (noise structure, overlap, initialization), making it difficult to judge robustness.

Clarity
Clearly written and organized, with a good exposition of the model and training. Some implementation details (initialization, regularization, computational complexity) and precise experimental settings (stimulus in the awake dataset, preprocessing impact) could be expanded. The role of overlap should be highlighted earlier and more quantitatively.

Originality
Moderate. The LDS and EM are standard; the novelty is in posing and empirically validating the stitching problem for partially overlapping neural recordings. This application-driven contribution is useful, though methodologically incremental.

Significance
Potentially significant for neuroscience practice if validated on real multi-session experiments. As presented, the impact is promising but not yet definitive due to the simulated split setting and limited baseline comparisons.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and important experimental limitation with a simple, usable approach.
- Solid improvements over a naive baseline; close to fully observed upper bound on some metrics.
- Clear path to influencing experimental design (overlap considerations).

Arguments against acceptance
- No demonstration on truly separate sessions; core challenges (nonstationarity, ROI tracking) untested.
- Limited methodological novelty and lack of comparisons to stronger latent-variable baselines.
- Assumptions on noise and linearity may bias coupling estimates; robustness not established.

Suggestions for improvement
- Validate on genuine multi-session datasets (different days/fields of view) with neuron identity tracked; report robustness to nonstationarity and drift.
- Systematically quantify the required overlap: sweep overlap fraction, shared stimulus, and session lengths; provide identifiability conditions or empirical phase diagrams.
- Compare against stronger baselines: GPFA/PLDS/SLDS with missing data, LDS with low-rank latent input (non-diagonal Q or explicit latent factors), subspace ID methods with partial observations.
- Report predictive log-likelihood on held-out time segments and uncertainty estimates for couplings/correlations.
- Explore more realistic observation models: spike deconvolution or calcium dynamics, non-diagonal Q, heteroscedastic R; include sparsity or structural priors on A.
- Provide computational complexity, runtime vs. N and number of sessions, and release code.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is compelling and the preliminary evidence is encouraging, but the absence of validation on real multi-session data and limited baseline/robustness analyses make the contribution not yet mature for NeurIPS. With the suggested extensions, this could become a strong submission.
