

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a novel computational approach to address a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record activity from all neurons in large neural circuits. The authors propose a "stitching" method that combines multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics, including noise correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously. This is a bold and highly significant contribution to the field, addressing a real experimental constraint that has limited our understanding of neural computation.

The core insight is that because neurons in a local circuit are strongly coupled, recording different subsets of neurons in separate sessions allows us to infer the joint dynamics of the entire circuit. The authors model this as a latent dynamical system with missing observations and demonstrate that a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) can effectively "stitch" together these partial recordings. Their method is conceptually simple yet powerful, and they validate it on both simulated data and real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The novelty of this work is substantial. Previous approaches either require simultaneous recordings of all neurons [6,7,8,9,10,11] or make strong assumptions about the relationship between noise correlations and stimulus correlations [12]. The authors correctly identify these limitations in their related work section and demonstrate that their method outperforms these alternatives significantly. Their theoretical justification—drawing on dynamical system reconstruction from partial observations [21,22,23]—is elegant and well-grounded.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, the problem addressed is critically important in neuroscience, where current imaging technology cannot capture the full activity of large neural circuits (thousands of neurons). Second, the method is computationally efficient and demonstrates clear practical utility. Third, the experimental validation on real data from both anesthetized and awake mice is convincing, showing that the stitching method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching the theoretical optimum. The results in Figure 3c and 3d are particularly compelling, showing the stitched model's superior performance over the naive baseline.

The paper also makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of neural population dynamics. By demonstrating that the coupling structure of a neural circuit can be reconstructed from non-simultaneous observations, the authors provide a powerful new tool for analyzing neural circuits. This has the potential to dramatically expand the scope of neural circuit analysis, allowing researchers to study larger circuits than currently feasible with imaging technology.

While the paper is strong overall, there are some limitations worth noting. The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification of complex neural dynamics, though the authors acknowledge this and demonstrate that the method works well in practice. The paper also doesn't fully explore the limits of the approach—how much overlap between recording sessions is required, or how many sessions can be stitched together. Additionally, the computational details of the online-EM implementation could be more thoroughly explained.

However, these are minor concerns in the context of the paper's significant contribution. The authors' approach is innovative, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear practical value. The core idea of "stitching" together non-simultaneous recordings is both elegant and potentially transformative for neuroscience research.

In terms of significance, this work addresses a major bottleneck in neural circuit analysis and provides a practical solution that could be immediately adopted by experimentalists. It has the potential to enable new discoveries about neural computation by allowing researchers to study larger neural circuits than currently possible. The paper's clear demonstration of this capability on real data makes it particularly compelling.

Compared to other NIPS papers, this work stands out for its bold conceptual contribution to a domain outside of traditional machine learning. It bridges neuroscience and machine learning in a way that could have significant impact on both fields. The authors successfully demonstrate that a relatively simple statistical model can solve a complex experimental problem, which aligns well with the NIPS tradition of innovative applications of statistical methods.

I believe this paper makes a strong contribution to the field and would be an excellent addition to NIPS. The novelty of the approach, the significance of the problem addressed, and the convincing experimental results all support acceptance. While the methodological details could be expanded, the paper's core contribution is sufficiently novel and impactful to overcome these minor limitations.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for its approach. It provides a clear path forward for experimentalists to leverage their existing data more effectively, which is a significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience.

I recommend acceptance of this paper, as it presents a bold and novel approach to a fundamental problem in neuroscience with clear potential for impact. The paper's strengths in novelty and significance substantially outweigh its minor methodological limitations.
