This paper tackles a compelling and practically relevant problem: inferring the dynamics of large neural populations from limited, spatially fragmented recordings. The core idea – leveraging the inherent connectivity within a neural circuit to "stitch" together information from partial observations – is both clever and, frankly, quite bold. It directly addresses a major bottleneck in systems neuroscience, where complete neural recordings remain elusive. While the work relies on a relatively simple linear Gaussian dynamical system, the authors convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness on both simulated and real calcium imaging data, and importantly, show its superiority over naive approaches. The ability to predict correlations between neurons *never* simultaneously observed is a particularly strong result.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The motivation is well established, and the mathematical formulation is accessible. The inclusion of both simulated and experimental validation strengthens the claims. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations and related work, and the experimental details are sufficient for reproducibility.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of stitching together partial recordings is genuinely novel and addresses a significant practical problem. While latent dynamical systems are not new, applying them in this specific context—inferring connections *between* non-simultaneously recorded neurons—is a significant contribution.
*   **Significance:** The method has the potential to dramatically improve our ability to study large neural circuits, especially in situations where complete recordings are impossible.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not intimately familiar with dynamical systems.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The results on both simulated and real data are compelling and support the claims. The demonstration on real calcium imaging data is particularly strong.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to a “naive” approach effectively highlights the advantage of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Model Simplicity:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the extent to which the results generalize to more complex, nonlinear neural dynamics remains unclear.
*   **Parameter Tuning/Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of parameter sensitivity. How robust are the results to different choices of model parameters (e.g., regularization strength, noise covariance)?
*   **Scalability:** While the method is demonstrated on populations of ~100 neurons, its scalability to truly large circuits (thousands of neurons) is not thoroughly addressed. The computational cost of the EM algorithm could become prohibitive.
*   **Overlap Requirement:** The dependence on sufficient overlap between recording sessions is a practical constraint. The authors mention this, but quantifying the minimum required overlap for reliable inference would be valuable.
*   **Limited Biological Interpretation:** While the method successfully predicts correlations, the biological interpretation of the inferred connections remains somewhat limited. The authors refer to "functional connectivity," but a more detailed discussion of how these functional connections relate to underlying synaptic mechanisms would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a genuinely novel and significant contribution to the field of neural data analysis.
*   The core idea is well-motivated and addresses a critical challenge in systems neuroscience.
*   The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
*   The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest avenues for future research.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The reliance on a simple linear Gaussian model may limit the generalizability of the results.
*   The scalability of the method to truly large circuits is not fully demonstrated.
*   A more detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity would strengthen the claims.



**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend acceptance. Despite the limitations, the novelty and potential impact of this work are substantial. The paper presents a bold and promising approach to a difficult problem, and the results are convincing. The methodological imperfections are outweighed by the significance of the idea and the clear demonstration of its effectiveness. While further work is needed to address the limitations, this paper represents a significant step forward in our ability to study large neural circuits. I believe this work will inspire further research in this area and has the potential to become a standard tool for analyzing neural data.
