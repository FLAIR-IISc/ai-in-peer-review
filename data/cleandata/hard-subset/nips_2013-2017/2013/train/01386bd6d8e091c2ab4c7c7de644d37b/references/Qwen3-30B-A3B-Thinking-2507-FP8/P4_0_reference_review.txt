

This paper presents a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics and correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework. The key insight is that strong coupling between neural populations allows inference of correlations across non-simultaneously observed neurons, which previous methods cannot address. The authors position their work against related approaches: studies inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon data (e.g., [6,7]), latent GLMs [13], and methods for reconstructing anatomical connectivity [15], noting their limitations for this specific problem.

The paper demonstrates their approach on both synthetic data and real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. For synthetic data, they show that their stitching method accurately recovers coupling matrices and noise correlations (correlation 0.92 with ground truth), outperforming a naive approach that cannot estimate correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. On real data, they demonstrate that the method predicts correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs with accuracy approaching the "fully observed" model (correlation 0.84 vs. 0.15 for naive approach), with consistent performance across population sizes (10-80 neurons).

Strengths:
1. The problem is well-motivated by a genuine experimental limitation in neuroscience (inability to simultaneously record all neurons in large circuits).
2. The technical approach is sound and well-executed, using a standard LDS framework with EM algorithm for parameter estimation. The authors provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.
3. The evaluation is comprehensive, including synthetic data with ground truth, real data applications, and appropriate baselines (fully observed as upper bound, naive as lower bound).
4. The paper correctly identifies the novelty gap in the literature and provides a clear theoretical justification for why the approach should work.
5. The discussion of limitations (linear model simplification, need for some overlap between recordings) is honest and well-considered.

Weaknesses:
1. The linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification of neural dynamics, which may limit applicability to more complex neural circuits. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't explore how the method might be extended to more biologically realistic models (e.g., incorporating spiking dynamics or nonlinearities).
2. The method requires some overlap between recorded neuron populations (60% in experiments), which may not always be feasible in experimental designs. The paper doesn't sufficiently explore the trade-offs between overlap and coverage.
3. The scalability to very large populations (thousands of neurons) is not addressed, though the authors mention it as a potential limitation.
4. The paper doesn't discuss how the method might handle non-stationary neural dynamics, which are common in awake animals.

Originality: The core contribution of stitching together non-simultaneously recorded neural populations is novel in the context of neural circuit analysis. Previous work has addressed inferring connectivity from partial observations but not specifically from non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit. The paper effectively differentiates itself from related work (e.g., [12] requires strong assumptions about noise correlations, [13] doesn't scale to large populations).

Significance: The method directly addresses a major experimental constraint in neuroscience (limited simultaneous recording capacity), with potential to significantly expand the scope of neural circuit studies. The successful application to real data demonstrates practical utility, and the approach could be generalized to other neural recording modalities. The authors correctly note that this could enable mapping population dynamics of entire neural circuits previously out of reach.

Quality: The technical execution is sound, with appropriate statistical modeling and experimental validation. The synthetic data experiments provide clear evidence of the method's theoretical underpinnings. The real data application demonstrates practical value. The paper is well-written, with clear organization and sufficient detail for replication.

Pro-acceptance arguments: The problem is important, the method is novel, the evaluation is thorough, the approach is technically sound, and the results demonstrate significant practical utility for neuroscience.

Con-acceptance arguments: The linear model is a simplification that may limit biological relevance, the requirement for population overlap could restrict applicability, and scalability to very large populations isn't addressed.

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that addresses a meaningful problem in neuroscience with a novel and well-executed method. While the linear model is a simplification, the authors provide sufficient justification for using this tractable framework to achieve their goal, and the results on real data demonstrate clear utility. The paper meets NIPS standards for technical soundness, clarity, and significance. The novelty is well-established in relation to prior work, and the significance is demonstrated through both synthetic and real data experiments. The paper would be a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for experimental neuroscientists seeking to maximize the utility of their partial recordings.
