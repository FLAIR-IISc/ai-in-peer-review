**Summary:**
This paper proposes a computational method to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings. The core idea is to model the entire population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) and treat the different recording sessions as incomplete observations of this system's state. The authors demonstrate that by fitting this model using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, it is possible to "stitch" together the partial recordings to estimate the full functional connectivity matrix and predict noise correlations, even between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is validated on synthetic data and then applied to two-photon calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex, where a "fictional imaging scenario" is used to create non-simultaneous recordings from an initially complete dataset for evaluation.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Important Problem:** The paper addresses a significant and practical limitation in experimental neuroscience. The ability to combine partial recordings to model a larger, more complete circuit would be a powerful tool for the field.
2.  **Principled Approach:** The formulation of the problem within the well-established framework of latent dynamical systems with missing data is elegant and statistically sound.
3.  **Clever Validation Strategy:** The "fictional imaging scenario" for experimental data, complete with "fully observed" (upper bound) and "naive" (lower bound) models, provides a convincing and rigorous way to quantify the performance of the proposed stitching method in the absence of ground truth.
4.  **Demonstration on Real Data:** The application to both anesthetized and awake mouse calcium imaging data demonstrates the potential utility of the method on real-world, noisy biological signals.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper, while presenting a promising idea, suffers from a number of methodological simplifications, under-analyzed results, and minor inconsistencies that collectively undermine its contribution.

**Major Weaknesses:**
1.  **Oversimplification of the Model:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is primarily for computational tractability, but it is a poor match for the underlying biology of spiking neurons and calcium dynamics. The authors acknowledge this ("does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade...") but justify it by showing it can predict pairwise correlations. However, this is a low bar. It is unclear if the model captures any higher-order statistics or more complex dynamic features. This simplification limits the potential insights and the direct interpretability of the learned parameters (e.g., the coupling matrix A) in a biological context.
2.  **Unaddressed Scalability:** The introduction motivates the work by mentioning circuits of "thousands of neurons," but the experiments are conducted on populations of at most N=100. The computational cost of Kalman smoothing in the E-step scales poorly with N. The authors mention using online-EM but provide no analysis of computational complexity or how the method would scale to the population sizes that are the primary motivation for this work. This represents a significant gap between the claims and the evidence provided.
3.  **Critical Dependence on Overlap:** A crucial detail is mentioned almost as an aside in Section 2.3: "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a vital finding. The method's success seems to hinge on having a set of "anchor" neurons recorded in both sessions. The paper fails to characterize this dependence. How much overlap is required? How does performance degrade as overlap decreases? By framing this as an "intriguing open problem in experimental design" rather than a core part of their analysis, the authors avoid addressing a fundamental limitation of their approach.
4.  **Deteriorating Parameter Recovery:** The authors report in Section 3.2 that the correlation between the stitched coupling parameters and the fully-observed ones "drops with increasing population size." This is a very worrying result. It suggests that as the model scales to more interesting problem sizes, its ability to identify the correct parameters degrades, potentially due to identifiability or degeneracy issues. The authors' speculative explanation ("perhaps due to... near degeneracy") is insufficient and does not inspire confidence in the robustness of the fitting procedure for larger systems.

**Minor Weaknesses and Inconsistencies:**
1.  **Assumption on Innovation Noise (Q):** The methods state that the innovation covariance Q is assumed to be diagonal. Immediately following this, the authors write "(see below for how our framework also can allow for correlated noise)." This "see below" is never followed up on anywhere in the manuscript. This is a confusing and unfulfilled promise to the reader. Assuming diagonal Q is a strong restriction, as it forces all shared variability to be explained through the explicit couplings in A.
2.  **Simulation Parameters:** The simulated data seems to be configured for success. The observation noise is "very small," and the stimulus drive is "very weak." The performance of the method under more realistic levels of noise or stronger stimulus-driven correlations (which could mask network effects) is not explored.
3.  **Data Preprocessing:** The calcium time-series were high-pass filtered with a 1s time-constant. This parameter choice is not justified, and the sensitivity of the results to this preprocessing step is not analyzed.
4.  **Formatting and Typos:** The bibliography is riddled with formatting errors. For instance, reference [21] is just an author's name, [24] ends in "9, ,", and the style is inconsistent across entries. A footnote in Section 3.2 is improperly formatted as an indented paragraph. While minor, such a lack of attention to detail detracts from the overall quality of the manuscript.

**Recommendation: Reject**

While the problem is important and the high-level idea is appealing, this paper feels like a preliminary proof-of-concept rather than a complete and rigorous piece of work. The core methodological contributions are incremental (applying a standard LDS with missing data to a new problem). The evaluation, while cleverly designed, reveals significant weaknesses—namely, a critical and un-investigated dependence on neuronal overlap and poor parameter recovery for larger systems—that are glossed over. Combined with the oversimplified model and numerous minor flaws in presentation, the paper does not meet the high standards of quality and completeness expected for NIPS. The authors should perform a thorough analysis of the overlap-dependence and scalability issues before this work is ready for publication.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a novel and significant problem at the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience.
*   The proposed "stitching" idea is intuitive and the validation on real data is a strength.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   Uses an overly simplistic linear model for a complex nonlinear biological system.
*   Fails to address or analyze scalability to the large populations that motivate the work.
*   Critical dependence on overlapping neurons is mentioned but not properly investigated.
*   Evidence suggests parameter recovery degrades with population size, a major flaw that is not adequately explained.
*   Numerous minor issues with clarity, unsubstantiated claims, and formatting detract from the paper's quality.
