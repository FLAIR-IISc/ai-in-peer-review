Summary
The paper proposes “stitching” multiple partially overlapping recordings of the same neural circuit by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Each session observes a subset of neurons; a shared state-transition A and stimulus drive B are learned via EM, with session-specific selection matrices Ck that pick out observed neurons. The goal is to recover cross-couplings and, more importantly, noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. On synthetic data with known ground truth, the approach recovers off-block couplings and noise correlations; on two 2-photon calcium datasets from mouse S1, the stitched model predicts pairwise correlations for non-simultaneously measured pairs substantially better than a naive baseline and close to a “fully observed” LDS fitted to all neurons simultaneously (in a fictional split).

Relation to prior work: The method builds directly on classical LDS/Kalman smoothing/EM and missing-data system identification, with connections to subspace ID and multi-view latent variable modeling. It complements work on connectivity inference from calcium imaging and GLM-based latent models, and on handling subsampling (e.g., Pillow et al. 2008; Mishchenko et al. 2011; GPFA and related latent factor approaches), but specifically targets the non-simultaneous recording problem while leveraging known neuron identities across sessions.

Strengths
- Clear, tractable formulation: a single LDS with known selection matrices across sessions; straightforward EM-based learning and inference.
- Convincing synthetic validation showing recovery of off-diagonal A blocks and noise correlations, and cross-prediction of unobserved neural activity.
- Real-data demonstration on two datasets; stitching improves correlation prediction over a naive baseline and approaches a strong model-based upper bound; analysis across population sizes.
- Practical relevance: directly addresses a common experimental constraint in imaging; method is easy to implement and extend.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Novelty: Methodologically, the approach is largely an application of standard LDS with missing data and shared parameters across sessions. The main contribution is empirical and domain-specific.
- Identifiability/overlap: The paper states that overlap is “critical” and that without overlap performance is poor, but does not analyze identifiability conditions. A more careful discussion (observability/controllability; role of overlapping cells versus sufficiently rich shared stimulus u; when the latent-space linear transform can be fixed) would strengthen the claims. A systematic study of overlap fraction (0%, 10%, 30%, …) and stimulus richness would be valuable.
- Model misspecification: The fluorescence signal is modeled as the latent state; calcium/spiking nonlinearity and AR kinetics are ignored. Q and R are assumed diagonal, pushing all shared variability into A. This can bias inferred couplings/noise correlations, especially in awake data with common inputs and neuropil contamination. The authors mention extensions (correlated noise via extra latent dims; sparsity priors), but do not implement them in the main results.
- Evaluation choices: On real data, the “fully observed LDS” is used as an upper bound. Since the original data are simultaneous, it would be more compelling to compare predicted noise correlations directly to empirical noise correlations computed from held-out simultaneous data (after subtracting stimulus drive), not to another fitted model. Similarly, the cross-prediction metric should be defined against actually recorded activity in the held-out intervals where those neurons were “fictionally” unobserved.
- Baselines: The naive baseline is weak. Stronger alternatives include:
  - Static FA/GPFA or low-rank LDS with shared latent across sessions (missing data handled naturally), to predict correlations.
  - Subspace/system identification methods with missing outputs and shared dynamics.
  - A static Gaussian graphical model of residuals with EM over missing entries.
- Scalability/regularization: A full N×N A is heavy in parameters and may overfit at N≈100–200; no regularization or stability constraints are reported in the main experiments, and there is no held-out likelihood. Claims about scaling to thousands of neurons are not supported empirically; complexity and runtime should be discussed.
- Nonstationarity and experimental details: The fictional split uses sequential halves of a session; slow drifts/arousal could violate the shared-parameter assumption. Clarify how the stimulus u is defined in the awake dataset, and whether behavioral covariates (running, licking) were included.

Quality
Technically sound as an application of LDS/EM; results support the main empirical claim (predicting cross-session correlations). However, the evaluation could be stronger (ground-truth correlation comparisons; stronger baselines), and assumptions (diagonal Q/R; linearity in fluorescence) are restrictive.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. A few notation issues (Eq. 2 appears malformed; please fix ykt = Ck xkt + d + εt with εt∼N(0,R)). Provide details on EM schedule, stability constraints, and stimulus definition in both datasets.

Originality
Conceptual contribution is moderate: leveraging a standard LDS with missing observations to stitch multi-session data with known cell identities. The application to 2-photon imaging and the focus on predicting non-simultaneous noise correlations are useful but not a major methodological advance.

Significance
High practical relevance for systems neuroscience; moderate for NeurIPS methodology. If strengthened with identifiability analysis, stronger baselines, and ground-truth evaluations, the impact would increase.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical or empirical analysis of identifiability and the role of overlap and stimulus richness; ablation over overlap fraction.
- Compare predicted noise correlations to empirical ones on held-out simultaneous data; report confidence intervals.
- Add stronger baselines (FA/GPFA, low-rank LDS, subspace ID, static covariance EM).
- Include regularization (e.g., sparsity on A, stability constraints) and/or a low-rank latent component to capture common inputs; report effects.
- Consider incorporating calcium dynamics or spike deconvolution to reduce mismatch.
- Report computational complexity and runtimes; discuss scaling strategies.

Pros
- Simple, practical method; easy to adopt.
- Demonstrated on real datasets; promising gains over naive approach.
- Addresses a problem that many labs face.

Cons
- Limited methodological novelty.
- Incomplete analysis of identifiability/overlap.
- Evaluation relies on a model-based “upper bound” rather than empirical ground truth; weak baselines.
- Strong modeling assumptions (diagonal Q/R, linearity in fluorescence).

Recommendation
Borderline accept. The idea is timely and useful, and the empirical results are encouraging, but the paper would benefit from stronger evaluation and analysis of identifiability and overlap. If improved along these lines, it would make a solid contribution. Reviewer confidence: high.
