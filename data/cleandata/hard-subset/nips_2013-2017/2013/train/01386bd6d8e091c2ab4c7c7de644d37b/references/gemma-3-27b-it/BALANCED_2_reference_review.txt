This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to leverage the assumption that nearby neurons are strongly coupled and that sequential recordings capture overlapping subsets of the population. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, to "stitch" together these partial observations and estimate the full population dynamics, including correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly presenting the experimental limitations that necessitate this type of inference. It appropriately frames the problem within the existing literature on functional connectivity and dynamical systems modeling, carefully distinguishing their approach from related work such as methods requiring monotonic stimulus correlations or those limited by scaling issues. The proposed method is relatively straightforward to understand and implement, building on established techniques like the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and Kalman smoothing. The validation through both simulated and real calcium imaging data is a significant strength, and the comparison against both an upper bound (“fully observed”) and a naive approach provides a valuable benchmark. The results demonstrate that the method can effectively predict correlations and activity even in the absence of simultaneous recordings. 

However, the paper also has several weaknesses. First, the reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a significant limitation. While justified by computational tractability, it’s acknowledged that this model doesn't capture the non-linear and non-Gaussian complexities of neural data. The extent to which the results generalize to more realistic scenarios is therefore unclear. While the authors mention potential extensions to more complex models, a more thorough discussion of the implications of this linearity assumption would be beneficial.

Second, the explanation of *why* the method works, beyond stating that it's related to reconstructing latent variables, could be more insightful. The discussion hints at identifiability issues and the role of overlapping neurons, but a deeper theoretical exploration of the conditions under which accurate reconstruction is possible would strengthen the paper. The mention of re-parametrization is tantalizing but left unexplored.

Third, the experimental validation, while commendable, could be more rigorous. The fictional imaging scenario, while clever, introduces potential biases. Specifically, the comparison to the “fully observed” model relies on the assumption that this model is ground truth in the fictional setting. The sensitivity of the results to the degree of overlap between the imaging sessions (c = 60%) should be further investigated. Additionally, the discussion of the awake data mentions increased prediction accuracy with population size, but doesn't fully address the potential for overfitting. A more systematic analysis of model complexity and its impact on performance would be valuable.

Fourth, the figures could be improved. Figure 2, displaying the simulation setup, is somewhat cluttered. Similarly, Figure 3, showing the coupling matrices, would benefit from a more clear visual representation of the non-simultaneously recorded pairs and a better color scale.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and practically relevant problem in neuroscience.
*   Proposes a conceptually sound and computationally feasible approach.
*   Provides compelling validation on both simulated and real data.
*   Clearly articulates the limitations of the method and potential future directions.
*   Comparison to reasonable baselines (upper and lower bounds) strengthens the claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Strong reliance on a simplifying linear Gaussian assumption.
*   Limited theoretical justification for the method’s success.
*   Experimental validation could be more rigorous and less susceptible to bias.
*   Figures could be improved for clarity and visual impact.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the limitations of the linear Gaussian model and the need for a more robust experimental validation are concerns, the method's potential to leverage existing experimental technology and infer population dynamics from partial recordings is significant. The clear writing, thoughtful analysis, and constructive discussion of future directions make this a worthwhile paper for NIPS.

**Recommendation:**

Weak Accept. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  Provide a more detailed discussion of the limitations imposed by the linear Gaussian assumption and potential avenues for addressing them.
2.  Expand the theoretical discussion to provide a more intuitive understanding of the method’s underlying principles and conditions for successful reconstruction.
3.  Perform a more systematic analysis of the experimental validation, addressing the potential biases introduced by the fictional imaging scenario and investigating the effects of model complexity on performance.
4.  Improve the clarity and visual impact of the figures.
