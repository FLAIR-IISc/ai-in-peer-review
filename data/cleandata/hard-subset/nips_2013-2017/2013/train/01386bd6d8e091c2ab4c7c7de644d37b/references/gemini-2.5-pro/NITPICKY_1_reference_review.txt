### Review

**Summary:**
This paper proposes a computational method to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings. The core idea is to model the entire neural circuit as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) and treat the different recording sessions as incomplete observations of this system's state. The model parameters are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors demonstrate their "stitching" method on both simulated data and two real 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The key result is that the method can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, a task impossible for conventional analysis methods. The evaluation is structured cleverly, using a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data to benchmark the proposed method against a lower bound (a "naive" approach) and an upper bound (a model trained on the full, simultaneous data).

**Strengths:**
1.  **Significance and Originality:** The problem addressed is of high practical importance in systems neuroscience, where experimental limitations on the field of view are a major bottleneck. The proposed application of a latent variable model to stitch together non-simultaneous recordings is a novel and elegant solution to this problem. It represents a significant conceptual advance over methods that require simultaneous recordings or make strong assumptions about the relationship between signal and noise correlations.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The choice of an LDS is a reasonable starting point, providing a tractable and interpretable model. The use of the EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing is the standard and correct approach for parameter estimation in this model class.
3.  **Thorough Evaluation:** The evaluation strategy is a major strength. Using simulated data with a known ground truth provides a clear demonstration of the method's potential. The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is a particularly clever way to create a controlled testbed, and the inclusion of well-justified upper and lower performance bounds provides crucial context for interpreting the results. The successful prediction of cross-session noise correlations is a compelling demonstration.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor flaws, technical omissions, and a general lack of polish that, in aggregate, undermine confidence in the work.

1.  **Methodological Clarity and Reproducibility:**
    *   The model assumes a simple linear-Gaussian form, which is a strong simplification of both non-linear neural dynamics and the complex, non-Gaussian process of calcium imaging. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the potential pitfalls of this simplification more critically.
    *   The update equations for `d` and `R` are provided, but the derivation is omitted. More importantly, crucial details of the fitting procedure are missing. The authors mention using "online-EM with randomly selected mini-batches" followed by "full batch EM", but provide no information on hyperparameters (e.g., learning rates, batch sizes), initialization strategies, or convergence criteria. This makes the work difficult to reproduce.
    *   The paper mentions in the discussion that a "sparsifying penalty... improves parameter estimates slightly" but that these simulations were "unreported". If this improves performance, it should have been included in the main methodology and results section. As it stands, it feels like an incomplete analysis.

2.  **Critical Limitations Understated:**
    *   A critical finding is buried in Section 2.3: "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This implies that having a subset of neurons common to both recordings is essential for the method to work on real data. This is a very strong limitation with major implications for experimental design and should be a central part of the results and discussion, not a passing comment. A systematic analysis of how performance scales with the degree of overlap is missing but necessary.
    *   The identifiability of the model is treated somewhat superficially. The authors provide an intuition but do not discuss the practical conditions required for the unobserved linear transformation to be identifiable. The observation that parameter estimation quality drops for larger populations (end of Section 3.2), attributed to "possible near degeneracy", points directly to this issue but it is not investigated further.

3.  **Formatting and Presentation:**
    *   The manuscript suffers from numerous formatting and typographical errors that detract from its professionalism.
    *   **Citations:** The reference list is inconsistently formatted. For example, reference [21] is incomplete ("T. Katayama, , ,"). The author's email is presented as a footnote attached to the title, which is unconventional.
    *   **Notation:** The notation in Equation (1) `xk t-1` is poorly typeset and should be `x_{k, t-1}` for clarity. In the description of the simulated data, the observation noise is given as "std( ii) = 0.006"; the variable `ii` is undefined. A typo exists in Section 2.3: "simulatenous".
    *   **Figure References:** The text refers to figure panels conversationally (e.g., "Figure 2a, top middle") rather than using standard panel labels (e.g., Fig 2a, 2b). Descriptions rely on color (e.g., "red-outlined blocks"), which is poor practice for accessibility and clarity.
    *   **Terminology:** Footnote 1 uses the term "view", which has not been previously defined in the text.

**Decision:**
This paper presents a creative and important idea for advancing neural data analysis. The core concept is strong, and the evaluation strategy is well-conceived. However, the execution presented in the manuscript is insufficiently rigorous for a NIPS publication. The work feels preliminary due to the lack of detail regarding the fitting procedure, the glossing over of the critical requirement for overlapping neurons, the superficial treatment of model identifiability, and the numerous formatting errors. While the core contribution is significant, the sheer volume of minor flaws suggests a lack of care that prevents me from confidently accepting the paper in its current state.

**Recommendation: Reject.**

I would be open to reconsidering a substantially revised version of this work in the future. A revised manuscript would need to: (1) provide complete, reproducible details of the fitting algorithm; (2) systematically investigate and prominently feature the impact of neuron overlap on performance; (3) more thoroughly discuss the practical issues of model identifiability; and (4) be meticulously proofread and formatted to the standards of the conference.
