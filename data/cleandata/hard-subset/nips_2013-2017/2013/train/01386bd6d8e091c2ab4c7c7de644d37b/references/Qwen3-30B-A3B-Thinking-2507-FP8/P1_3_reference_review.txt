

# Peer Review: "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary and Context

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in neuroscience: inferring the joint dynamics of large neural populations when simultaneous recording of all neurons is experimentally infeasible. The authors propose a statistical "stitching" method that combines multiple partial recordings (from different imaging sessions) to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. Their approach models the neural population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, which can be fit using an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing.

The paper distinguishes itself from prior work on functional connectivity inference [6-11] by addressing the specific challenge of non-simultaneously recorded neurons, which previous methods do not handle. It also contrasts with related work on sub-sampled observations [12-15] by noting that those approaches make assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations) that don't hold in their application domain.

## Strengths

**Quality:** The paper presents a technically sound method with solid theoretical grounding. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem, demonstrate its application on both simulated data and real calcium imaging datasets, and include appropriate baselines (naive approach and "fully observed" model). The experimental validation is thorough, with multiple population sizes tested across two different experimental conditions (anesthetized vs. awake mice). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations, such as the need for some overlap between recording sessions and the simplicity of the LDS model.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The methodology is presented in a logical sequence, with sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility. The figures effectively illustrate the key concepts (e.g., Figure 2 shows the recovery of coupling matrices in simulated data, Figure 3 demonstrates real data results). The authors make an effort to explain the intuition behind their method, which aids comprehension.

**Originality:** The core idea of "stitching" together multiple partial recordings to infer joint dynamics is novel. While latent dynamical systems have been used in neuroscience before [13, 17, 18], the specific application to this problem of non-simultaneous recordings is new. The paper clearly differentiates their approach from prior work, which strengthens the novelty claim.

**Significance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to neuroscience, where simultaneous recording of large neural populations remains technically challenging. The method enables researchers to extract more information from existing experimental data without requiring new hardware. The results demonstrate that their approach can predict noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching that of a "fully observed" model, which is a significant advance over naive approaches. This could help build better data-driven models of cortical circuits and test theories of neural computation.

## Weaknesses

**Model Limitations:** The authors use a simple linear Gaussian dynamical system, which may not fully capture the complex nonlinear dynamics of neural activity. While they acknowledge this limitation and suggest more realistic models could improve performance, the paper doesn't provide evidence that these more complex models would actually yield better results with their stitching approach. The claim that "even this simple model makes accurate predictions" might be overstated given the complexity of neural dynamics.

**Overlap Requirement:** The method requires some overlap between recording sessions (the paper shows c=60% overlap works, while c=50% without overlap fails). While they acknowledge this limitation, they don't explore the practical implications for experimental design. For neuroscience experiments where overlap is difficult to achieve, this could limit the method's applicability.

**Real Data Analysis:** The paper demonstrates the method on two datasets, but the sample sizes (80-200 neurons) are still relatively small for the "large neural circuits" they claim to address (which they note can span thousands of neurons). The paper doesn't clearly establish how the method would scale to much larger populations, which is a critical concern for the stated significance.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Addresses a significant problem in neuroscience with practical implications
- Novel application of latent dynamical systems to a specific experimental challenge
- Rigorous validation with both simulated and real data
- Clear methodology that is reproducible
- Demonstrates clear performance gains over baselines

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The linear Gaussian model may be too simplistic for complex neural dynamics
- The requirement for session overlap limits the method's applicability
- The paper doesn't sufficiently address scalability to very large neural populations
- The significance claim of "large cortical circuits" (thousands of neurons) is not fully supported by the experimental validation (80-200 neurons)

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of computational neuroscience and machine learning. The method addresses a genuine experimental challenge with clear practical value, and the results are well-supported by both simulations and real data. While the linear Gaussian model has limitations, the authors acknowledge these and suggest potential improvements. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a novel contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the following:
1. More clearly articulate the limitations regarding model complexity and scalability
2. Expand the discussion on how the method might be adapted for larger neural populations
3. Provide more concrete examples of how this method could be integrated into existing experimental workflows

This work has the potential to significantly impact how neuroscience data is analyzed, allowing researchers to extract more information from existing datasets without requiring new hardware. It represents a thoughtful application of statistical learning to a challenging problem in neuroscience, which aligns well with NIPS' focus on machine learning and its applications.

**Overall Assessment:** This paper is of high quality, original, and significant. It makes a compelling case for a method that could become an important tool in computational neuroscience. I recommend acceptance.
