Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a statistical method to “stitch” together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics, in particular the cross-couplings and noise correlations between neurons that were not measured simultaneously. The core model is a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with known, session-specific selection matrices Ck that indicate which neurons are observed in each session. Parameters (A,B,Q,R,d) are learned by EM with Kalman smoothing across all sessions; the hidden state is the full activity vector, and missing entries correspond to unobserved neurons. The authors evaluate on simulations and on two calcium imaging datasets (mouse S1, awake and anesthetized), using a “fictional split” of simultaneously measured populations to create two partially overlapping sessions. They compare to (i) a “fully observed” upper bound (LDS fit to all neurons simultaneously; not feasible in practice) and (ii) a naive baseline that independently fits separate LDS models to each subset and zeros cross-block couplings.

This work relates to prior NIPS literature on population latent variable models (e.g., PLDS/GLM models for correlated spiking, Pillow et al., 2008; latent GLMs, Pillow & Latham, 2008), GPFA/PLDS-type approaches to shared variability, system identification and subspace methods, and recent work on inferring connectivity from calcium imaging (Mishchenko et al., 2011; Stetter et al., 2012). The novelty here is explicitly addressing multi-session partial observations of the same identified neurons and demonstrating that joint dynamics—including noise correlations—can be inferred despite non-simultaneous acquisition, using a conceptually simple LDS with missing data. The authors discuss identifiability (up to invertible transforms of unobserved dimensions) and how subsequent sessions can resolve this ambiguity.

Quality
- Modeling: The LDS on fluorescence traces is a reasonable first step for computational tractability, though it abstracts away spiking, calcium nonlinearities, and neuropil contamination. The model allows for per-neuron offsets and diagonal observation noise; Q is assumed diagonal (with a discussion of extensions via additional latent dimensions).
- Inference: EM with Kalman smoothing across sessions is appropriate; mini-batch online EM for initialization is sensible. However, the paper does not discuss constraints to ensure stability of A (spectral radius < 1) or regularization beyond a brief note that sparsity helps in unreported simulations.
- Evaluation: Simulations show recovery of cross-block couplings (r≈0.73) and noise correlations (r≈0.92) for non-simultaneously recorded pairs; prediction of unobserved activity is strong. On real data, the fictional split plus hold-out test shows that stitched models predict cross-session correlations well (e.g., r≈0.84 vs. 0.15 for naive), and activity prediction approaches the fully observed upper bound in the awake data. The repeated subsampling across population sizes is a plus. A limitation is that the real-data evaluation uses splits of the same recording, not truly different sessions, so session-to-session nonstationarities are not tested.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized, with a concise description of the model, learning, and evaluation. A few notational issues should be fixed (e.g., Eq. (2) seems to have a typo: ykt = Ck(xkt + d) + εt, εt ~ N(0,R) is presumably intended; clarify indices σkj and χkj earlier). More detail on initialization, convergence criteria, and computational complexity would help reproducibility.

Originality
The idea of using a single joint LDS with missing observations to “stitch” partial recordings is simple but, to my knowledge, not previously demonstrated for predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The paper usefully connects classical system identification/latent state estimation ideas to a timely neuroscience problem. Related latent models are well cited; it may help to discuss connections to PLDS/GPFA-style models and subspace identification for missing data more explicitly.

Significance
Inferring cross-session correlations and couplings is important for modern imaging experiments that cannot observe full circuits simultaneously. If robust in practice, the method could influence experimental design (overlap vs coverage trade-offs) and data analysis pipelines. The demonstration on real calcium imaging data (albeit with fictional splits) suggests practical promise. Extensions to more realistic observation models and explicit common-input latent factors could broaden impact.

Strengths
- Clear formulation of a multi-session LDS with known selection matrices and missing data.
- Conceptual insight: identifiability across sessions and recovery of cross-block couplings/noise correlations.
- Solid simulation study and carefully controlled real-data evaluation with upper/lower bounds.
- Quantitative metrics: correlations of couplings, noise correlations, and activity prediction on held-out data; robustness across population sizes.
- Practical considerations (online EM) and discussion of overlap.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Real-data evaluation uses a split of a single simultaneous recording; the method is not tested on truly separate sessions where nonstationarities (baseline/gain changes, indicator bleaching, behavioral state) and cell-tracking errors occur. This is a key gap for the intended use case.
- Model mismatch: LDS on high-pass filtered fluorescence with diagonal R and Q is simplistic; neuropil-driven correlated observation noise is common, and deconvolution/spike-LDS/PLDS comparisons are absent.
- Identifiability vs overlap: Simulations succeed with no overlap, but real-data experiments “failed” without overlap. This inconsistency deserves analysis (data requirements, SNR, initialization, local optima).
- No discussion of computational scaling with N, T, and number of sessions; A is dense, so smoothing can be cubic in N unless structure is exploited.
- Limited baselines: the naive model is weak. Comparisons to multi-session factor models (e.g., shared latent factors with per-session loadings), subspace ID, or PLDS with shared dynamics across sessions would strengthen claims.
- Lack of regularization/priors and hyperparameter selection; potential instability of A not addressed.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Provide experiments on truly separate sessions (e.g., different time blocks or days) with overlapping cells, including per-session offsets/gains and possibly per-session drift terms. How sensitive is stitching to mild nonstationarity?
- Analyze sample complexity and the role of overlap: performance vs overlap fraction, T per session, SNR, and number of sessions. Can you formalize conditions under which no-overlap stitching is identifiable/tractable?
- Consider per-session affine calibration (offset and gain) and/or per-session R to accommodate imaging conditions; discuss how you handle bleaching and neuropil.
- Explore correlated observation noise via either non-diagonal R or explicit low-dimensional “common input” latent factors (as you suggest), and report the impact on correlation prediction.
- Report whether you constrain A to be stable; if so, how? If not, how often do you observe instability?
- Add comparisons to a low-rank latent factor dynamical model (e.g., PLDS/GPFA variants) that shares latent dynamics across sessions, and to subspace ID with missing data.
- Release code and include more implementation details (initialization strategy, stopping criteria, runtime on the largest datasets).

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practical problem with a simple, principled method.
- Strong empirical gains over a baseline and close to a fully observed upper bound in real data.
- Clear exposition and potential impact on experimental design and analysis.

Cons for acceptance
- Validation on real multi-session data (with genuine session differences) is missing.
- Modeling assumptions are simplistic for calcium imaging; stronger baselines and robustness analyses are needed.
- Limited theoretical guidance on identifiability/sample complexity and on the necessity/sufficiency of overlap.

Overall recommendation
Weak accept. The paper delivers a clear and potentially impactful idea with encouraging results, but would benefit from evaluation on truly separate sessions, stronger baselines, and analysis of overlap and robustness. With these additions, it could be a solid NIPS contribution.
