## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a statistical method for “stitching” together partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of a larger population than can be observed simultaneously with current technology. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, and demonstrate its ability to predict correlated dynamics and noise correlations between neurons recorded in separate sessions. They validate their method using both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can recover true correlations and predict unobserved neural activity with accuracy approaching an ideal “fully observed” model. The core idea leverages the assumption of strong coupling between nearby neurons, making it plausible to infer the behavior of unobserved neurons from those that *are* observed.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the broader landscape of neural data analysis. They acknowledge related work on inferring functional connectivity from calcium imaging and electrophysiology (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011; Stetter et al., 2012), but emphasize that these approaches typically don’t address inferring connections between *non-simultaneously* observed neurons.  They also appropriately differentiate their approach from methods dealing with sub-sampled observations that rely on strong monotonicity assumptions (Wohrer et al., 2010) or are not scalable to population sizes of interest (Linderman & Adams, 2013). The reference to Takens’ embedding theorem (Takens, 1981) is a nice touch, providing a theoretical grounding for the plausibility of reconstructing dynamics from partial observations.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an important practical problem:** The limited field of view of current imaging technologies is a major bottleneck in systems neuroscience. This paper tackles this limitation directly with a novel and potentially impactful approach.
* **Sound methodology:** The use of a latent dynamical system framework is well-motivated and provides a principled approach to combining data from different recordings. The authors provide sufficient methodological detail, including the EM algorithm implementation and considerations for computational efficiency.
* **Strong empirical validation:**  The results on both simulated and real data are compelling. The ability to recover noise correlations, a notoriously difficult task, is particularly impressive. The fictional imaging scenario on the experimental data effectively demonstrates the performance gain compared to naive approaches.
* **Clear writing and organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the results section is well-structured and easy to follow.
* **Acknowledges limitations:** The authors are upfront about the assumptions of their model (linearity, Gaussianity) and the potential for improvement with more complex models.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian assumption:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a significant limitation.  While it allows for tractable analysis, it may not capture the rich, nonlinear dynamics of real neural circuits.  The authors acknowledge this but could further discuss the potential impact of this simplification on their results.
* **Parameter identifiability:** While the authors demonstrate good recovery of parameters on simulated data, the real data analysis relies on a fictional imaging scenario for benchmarking.  Without ground truth, it’s difficult to definitively assess the identifiability of the model parameters and the extent to which the results reflect true neural dynamics versus model bias.
* **Overlap requirement:** The method seems to require a substantial degree of overlap between the observed neurons in different sessions (c=60% in the experiments).  The authors note that performance degrades without overlap, which limits the applicability of the method in scenarios where overlap is minimal. Further investigation into the minimum overlap needed for reliable inference would be valuable.
* **Scalability:** While the authors mention that their method scales, they don’t provide detailed computational benchmarks for very large populations (e.g., thousands of neurons). The computational cost of the EM algorithm could become a bottleneck for large-scale applications.



**Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a practically important problem with a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* The empirical validation is strong, demonstrating the method's ability to recover key statistics of neural dynamics.
* The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.
* The method has the potential to significantly expand our ability to study large neural circuits.

**Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumptions of linearity and Gaussianity may limit the applicability of the method to real neural data.
* The reliance on a fictional imaging scenario for benchmarking the real data analysis makes it difficult to assess the robustness and accuracy of the results.
* The overlap requirement between recording sessions could limit the method's applicability in certain experimental settings.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of neural data analysis. While the linear Gaussian assumption is a limitation, the method is well-motivated, rigorously implemented, and validated on both simulated and real data. The potential to infer the dynamics of unobserved neurons from partial recordings is significant, and could open up new avenues for studying large neural circuits. 

**Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper, with minor revisions.  I would suggest the authors:

*   Discuss the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption more thoroughly and explore potential avenues for incorporating nonlinearities.
*   Provide more information on the computational cost of the method and its scalability to larger populations.
*   Investigate the minimum overlap requirement for reliable inference and discuss potential strategies for dealing with scenarios where overlap is limited.
