This paper presents a method for “stitching” together calcium imaging data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics and correlations. The core idea – leveraging overlapping observations across sessions to reconstruct a more complete picture of population activity – is intuitively appealing, particularly given the limitations of current imaging technology. The authors frame this as a latent dynamical system estimation problem, utilizing a linear Gaussian model for tractability. They demonstrate the method's effectiveness on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The mathematical formulation is concise and reasonably accessible. The authors correctly position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related approaches for inferring connectivity and dealing with incomplete data, and differentiating their method by focusing on *non-simultaneous* observations. The inclusion of both simulated and experimental results strengthens the paper.

However, I have significant reservations about the novelty and overall significance of this work, and find the validation insufficient to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The central claim – that one can infer correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons – relies heavily on the assumption of a linear Gaussian dynamical system. While the authors acknowledge this simplification, they do not convincingly demonstrate that the method generalizes beyond this specific model. Given the known non-linearities and non-Gaussianity inherent in neural data (acknowledged in the introduction itself, citing [6]), the applicability of this method to real biological circuits remains questionable.  The performance gains over a naive approach, while demonstrable, are not transformative.

The simulation setup, while helpful, feels somewhat contrived. The strong coupling and relatively low noise levels may overestimate the method’s performance in more realistic scenarios. Crucially, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to more sophisticated state-space models or non-linear approaches that *could* potentially address the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption. Existing methods for latent GLMs ([13] acknowledged but dismissed for scalability) are undergoing rapid development, and the authors do not convincingly argue why these cannot be adapted or scaled to address the problem at hand. Simply stating that the current methods don’t scale is insufficient; a practical demonstration of the scaling limitations or a comparative analysis would be more persuasive.

The experimental validation, while presented, also lacks rigor. The "fictional imaging scenario" is a clever way to evaluate performance, but the chosen overlap (60%) seems somewhat arbitrary. The authors state that performance “failed” at 50% overlap but provide no quantitative details. More importantly, the performance metrics are largely framed relative to an idealized “fully observed” model and a simplistic “naive” approach. A comparison to more established methods for estimating functional connectivity from partial data (e.g., techniques based on maximum entropy or sparse regression) is missing. The assertion that their method performs "nearly as good as" the fully observed model is a high bar, and a more detailed analysis of the discrepancies is needed. Finally, the discussion of the coupling matrices (Figure 3a) is somewhat superficial; a more quantitative assessment of the agreement between the stitched and fully observed models would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The problem addressed is relevant to a growing field – understanding neural circuit function through large-scale recordings.
*   The method is conceptually simple and potentially useful for leveraging existing experimental data.
*   The simulation results demonstrate the feasibility of inferring correlations under specific assumptions.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The reliance on a strong linear Gaussian assumption limits the generalizability of the method.
*   The validation lacks a comparison to more sophisticated and relevant alternative approaches.
*   The performance gains over simpler methods are not substantial enough to justify a dedicated NIPS publication.
*   The paper doesn't sufficiently address potential issues related to model identifiability and parameter estimation in complex neural systems.



**Overall Assessment:** I rate this paper as **weak reject**. While the ideas are interesting, the technical contribution is incremental, the assumptions are strong, and the validation is insufficient. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the standards for novelty and significance expected at NIPS. The authors should consider significantly expanding the comparative analysis and exploring the robustness of their method to more realistic data conditions before resubmitting.
