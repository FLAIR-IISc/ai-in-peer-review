Summary
The paper proposes a statistical framework to “stitch” together multiple partial, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics, cross-couplings, and especially noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded together. The core model is a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where session-specific observation matrices encode which neurons were imaged in each session. Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant for efficiency). The key claim is that, by leveraging overlap across sessions and known inputs, one can recover the joint dynamics sufficiently well to predict correlations and even reconstruct unobserved activity.

On synthetic data with known ground truth, the stitched model recovers cross-session couplings and noise correlations substantially better than a naive baseline that fits each session separately. On two real 2-photon datasets (anesthetized and awake mouse barrel cortex), the method recovers pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons that closely match those obtained by a “fully observed” model fit to the complete, simultaneous dataset (upper bound in the fictional split), and much better than the naive baseline.

Relation to prior work
The work is related to: (i) inference of functional connectivity from calcium imaging (e.g., Mishchenko et al.; Stetter et al.) and electrophysiology (e.g., Pillow et al.), which typically assume simultaneous recordings; (ii) latent variable models/latent GLMs for partially observed populations (Pillow & Latham), though the cited latent GLM approach does not scale here; (iii) methods that attempt to infer correlations from partial data under particular assumptions (Wohrer et al.) or reconstruct anatomical connectivity from subsampled data (Mishchenko & Paninski). The paper’s distinctive contribution is the explicit stitching of sessions to reconstruct cross-session correlations without requiring the strong monotonic assumption between stimulus- and noise-correlations, and with a tractable LDS-based implementation.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: A clear and bold proposal to reconstruct joint dynamics across non-simultaneous recordings. This is timely and important given physical limits of imaging.
- Practicality: Uses a simple LDS with EM and session-wise observation matrices; straightforward to implement and extend. Demonstrates that even a linear-Gaussian model suffices to predict non-simultaneous noise correlations.
- Validation breadth: Synthetic data with ground truth plus application to two real datasets; multiple metrics including couplings, noise correlations, and cross-prediction of unobserved activity.
- Empirical insight: Shows that some overlap across sessions is important in practice, and quantifies benefits across population sizes.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Theory/identifiability: The intuition for identifiability is appealing, but there is no formal statement of conditions (e.g., observability/controllability, role of known inputs, required overlap, session alignment) under which cross-session couplings are recoverable, especially with measurement noise and model mismatch. Given the centrality of stitching, even a partial theorem or precise assumptions would strengthen the work.
- Dependence on overlap: The method “failed to get good performance” at 50% coverage with no overlap on real data. This is a key practical limitation; it would be valuable to show performance as a function of overlap and to discuss minimal overlap needed under realistic SNR.
- Model mismatch: The LDS directly models fluorescence, assumes linear interactions and diagonal process noise Q, and ignores calcium/spike nonlinearity and correlated inputs that are ubiquitous in cortex. While the paper argues these can be added, the current results blur whether accurate noise-correlation prediction comes from A, Q, or both. Some ablations (e.g., allowing low-rank common input, AR(1) calcium dynamics) would clarify robustness.
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is weak. Stronger alternatives include: factor analysis/GPFA with missing data, subspace system identification across sessions, multi-session LDS with shared latent factors and session-specific loadings, or covariance completion methods using the overlap. A comparison to at least one stronger baseline would better contextualize gains.
- Assumption of stationary dynamics across sessions: Real experiments often involve drift, arousal changes, bleaching. The method assumes time-invariant A, B across sessions. A brief analysis of robustness to session-specific offsets or mild A changes would be helpful.
- Evaluation on real data: The upper bound uses a fully observed model fit to simultaneous data. That is appropriate for a fictional split, but it would be useful to provide an external validation of predicted cross-session noise correlations (e.g., by holding out a truly simultaneous subset) or to assess generalization across different stimuli/behavioral epochs.

Quality
Technically solid within the LDS framework and missing-data EM; experiments are well executed and metrics appropriate. The lack of formal identifiability guarantees and limited baselines are the main quality gaps.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The Methods specify the model and fitting well. A figure summarizing the stitching pipeline and the role of overlap would help. The paper should clarify whether Q is enforced diagonal in all experiments and how stationarity is ensured in practice.

Originality
High. While LDS and EM are standard, using them to stitch non-simultaneous partial recordings to recover cross-session noise correlations is a novel and impactful application that differs from prior partial-data and connectivity works.

Significance
High. If adopted, this approach could materially expand what neuroscientists infer from limited imaging fields of view and guide experimental design (e.g., optimal overlap vs. coverage). The idea is likely to spur follow-ups with richer models and theory.

Questions for authors
- Can you formalize identifiability and the role of overlap/inputs? Under what conditions can zero-overlap stitching succeed?
- How sensitive are results to session-to-session drift or nonstationarity? Have you tried session-specific biases or affine transformations?
- What happens if you allow low-rank correlated process noise or an explicit calcium dynamics layer?
- Could you include a stronger baseline (e.g., FA/GPFA with missing blocks)?

Suggestions
- Add experiments varying overlap fraction and SNR; report failure modes.
- Include a low-rank Q or common input latent to separate network vs. shared drive contributions.
- Consider a simple subspace-ID or FA baseline with missing data.
- Release code to encourage adoption.

Recommendation
Accept (leaning strong). The paper introduces a compelling and timely idea with clear practical utility, and demonstrates it convincingly despite some theoretical and baseline limitations. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the imperfections.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel “stitching” concept addressing a pervasive practical limitation.
- Strong empirical performance on synthetic and real data for predicting non-simultaneous correlations.
- Simple, extensible methodology likely to see use and spur further work.

Arguments against acceptance
- No formal guarantees; dependence on overlap not fully characterized.
- Limited baselines; model mismatch to calcium/spiking biophysics.
- Assumes stationary dynamics across sessions without stress-testing.
