

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary

This paper presents a novel computational method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors address a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience: current imaging technologies can only capture a fraction of neurons in a neural circuit (e.g., hundreds out of thousands), but they demonstrate that by recording different subsets of neurons in separate sessions, one can infer the full correlation structure using a latent dynamical system model.

The authors model the neural population dynamics as a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where the unobserved neurons are treated as latent variables. They use an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing to estimate the model parameters from multiple partial recordings. The approach is validated on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing significant improvements over a naive baseline that simply averages parameters across sessions.

This work builds on related research in neural population dynamics, particularly the literature on inferring functional connectivity from calcium imaging [6, 7] and electrophysiology [8-11], but makes a distinct contribution by addressing the specific problem of non-simultaneous recordings. The authors correctly distinguish their approach from previous work, particularly noting the limitations of [12] (which requires strong assumptions about noise correlations) and [13] (which doesn't scale to large populations).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-executed. The authors present a clear mathematical formulation of the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations. The method is properly validated through both synthetic data experiments and real neural data. The authors provide appropriate baselines for comparison (a "fully observed" model as an upper bound and a naive approach as a lower bound), which is crucial for establishing the value of their method.

The results are well-supported: in the simulated data, the stitching method recovers 73% correlation with true off-diagonal couplings (for non-simultaneously recorded pairs) compared to only 0% for the naive approach. In the real data, the stitching method predicts correlations with 0.84 correlation to the fully observed model, compared to 0.15 for the naive approach. The authors also provide evidence for the robustness of their method across different population sizes.

The paper acknowledges limitations honestly, particularly the need for some overlap between recorded subsets (critical for stitching to work) and the simplicity of the linear model. They also note that more complex models could improve performance, which demonstrates appropriate scientific rigor.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly establishes the problem and the authors' approach. The methods section provides sufficient detail for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the work, including the mathematical formulation, fitting procedure, and data processing details. The results are presented with clear visualizations (Figures 2-4) that effectively illustrate the key findings.

The paper follows a logical structure that builds from problem statement to method to validation. The authors use clear explanations of their intuition for why the approach should work (e.g., "After the second imaging session, where we image the second (and previously unobserved) half of the population, we can identify this linear transformation, and thus identify all model parameters uniquely"). The writing is precise without being overly technical, making it accessible to readers with a background in machine learning or computational neuroscience.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution to the field. The problem of inferring correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons has not been addressed in prior work with the approach presented here. While previous work has considered inferring functional connectivity from partial observations [12-15], none have addressed the specific challenge of stitching together multiple partial recordings of the same circuit where the recordings are temporally separated.

The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from related approaches in the "Related recent research" section, highlighting the limitations of existing methods. Their approach is not merely a combination of existing techniques but a novel application of latent dynamical systems to a specific experimental constraint in neuroscience.

The paper's contribution is distinct from recent NIPS papers on neural population dynamics (e.g., [6-11]), which focus on inferring connectivity from simultaneous recordings rather than stitching together non-simultaneous data. The authors also cite the relevant literature comprehensively, demonstrating awareness of the field's state of the art.

## Significance

This work has significant potential impact for neuroscience. The ability to infer correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons directly addresses a major experimental limitation in the field, allowing researchers to make better use of current imaging technology without waiting for technological advances. This could enable more comprehensive studies of neural circuits with existing equipment.

The paper demonstrates that the method works on real neural data from both anesthetized and awake mice, which significantly increases its practical relevance. The authors show that their approach can predict noise correlations (a key measure of neural population dynamics) with accuracy approaching that of a "fully observed" model.

The significance is further enhanced by the potential for generalization to more than two populations and the implications for mapping larger neural circuits (e.g., "the zebrafish larval olfactory bulb, or layers 2 & 3 of a whisker barrel"). This represents a practical step toward a more complete understanding of neural circuit dynamics.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Addresses a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience with a technically sound solution
2. Well-validated on both simulated and real neural data
3. Clear, well-organized, and well-written paper
4. Properly compares against meaningful baselines (fully observed and naive approaches)
5. Demonstrates significant practical impact for current neuroscience research
6. Acknowledges limitations honestly and suggests future directions

**Weaknesses:**
1. The linear Gaussian model is a simplification of neural dynamics; while acknowledged, it's unclear how much this affects performance on real data
2. The requirement for some overlap between recorded subsets (critical for stitching to work) may limit applicability in some experimental designs
3. Limited exploration of how the method scales to very large populations (e.g., >1000 neurons)
4. The sensitivity to model parameters (e.g., the number of latent dimensions) isn't thoroughly explored

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field that meets all the key criteria for NIPS. It is technically sound, clearly written, substantially novel, and has significant potential impact. The authors address an important problem in neuroscience with a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically useful.

The weaknesses noted are minor in comparison to the strengths and are appropriately acknowledged by the authors. The paper represents a clear advance over previous work and provides a valuable tool for neuroscientists working with current imaging technology.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**

This paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, providing both theoretical insights and practical tools for analyzing neural population data. It exemplifies the kind of work that bridges computational neuroscience and machine learning, which is at the heart of NIPS's mission.
