

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations. The authors propose a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit (from different imaging sessions) to infer the joint dynamics and correlations across the entire population, including neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This is a significant conceptual leap beyond existing methods that either require simultaneous recordings or make strong assumptions about noise correlations.

The key insight is that neural circuits exhibit strong functional coupling, meaning that observing different subsets of neurons across sessions allows inference of the full coupling structure through a latent dynamical system model. The authors demonstrate this using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS), which they fit to multiple partial imaging sessions using an EM algorithm. Crucially, they show that their method can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, which is impossible with conventional approaches.

The paper's strengths are numerous. First, the core idea is highly novel and addresses a real bottleneck in neuroscience. While there's substantial work on inferring connectivity from partial observations, none of the previous approaches (cited in the paper) can handle the specific challenge of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit. The authors clearly articulate this gap in the literature and demonstrate how their method uniquely solves it.

Second, the method is both theoretically grounded and empirically validated. The authors provide a clear explanation of why the approach should work (using the theoretical foundation that a dynamical system can be reconstructed from partial observations), and they thoroughly test it on both simulated data (where ground truth is available) and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. Their results are compelling: the stitching method accurately recovers coupling matrices, predicts noise correlations, and even predicts unobserved neural activity with accuracy approaching the "fully observed" model (which would be impossible in practice).

Third, the paper is exceptionally well-written and clear. The authors effectively communicate a complex methodological idea through careful explanation and illustrative figures. The comparison to the naive baseline (which simply averages parameters for overlapping neurons) is particularly effective in highlighting the novelty of their approach.

The paper's significance is substantial. Current imaging technology cannot capture thousands of neurons simultaneously in cortical circuits, but this method allows researchers to effectively "expand" their recording capabilities by leveraging multiple partial sessions. This could significantly accelerate progress in understanding neural circuit function, potentially enabling the construction of complete data-driven models of cortical circuits that would otherwise be impossible.

Minor weaknesses include the use of a linear Gaussian model (which is a simplification of neural dynamics), the requirement for some overlap between recording sessions (though the authors acknowledge this as a practical constraint), and limited exploration of failure cases. However, these are minor limitations given the paper's bold conceptual contribution. The authors appropriately acknowledge these limitations in the discussion.

This paper stands out as a significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience, with clear potential to impact experimental design and data analysis. While the methodological details are sound, the true value lies in the novel conceptual approach - the idea that we can effectively "stitch" together non-simultaneous recordings to infer the full circuit dynamics. This is the kind of bold, paradigm-shifting idea that NIPS should prioritize.

In terms of novelty, the paper is clearly distinct from previous NIPS work. While there are papers on inferring connectivity from partial observations, none address the specific challenge of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit. The authors properly contextualize their work within the literature and clearly articulate their contribution.

The significance of this work extends beyond the immediate neuroscience community. The idea of "stitching" together partial observations could inspire new approaches in other fields where simultaneous observation of all components is limited. The paper's demonstration on real neural data makes the contribution concrete and compelling.

Given my emphasis on novelty and bold conceptual contributions, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. The core idea is transformative, the method is sound, and the results are compelling. While the linear model is a simplification, the authors demonstrate it's sufficient for their key goal of predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons - a task that previous methods could not accomplish. The paper provides a clear path forward for expanding the capabilities of current imaging technology, which is a significant contribution to the field.

I believe this paper will be highly influential in neuroscience and could inspire similar approaches in other domains where complete observation is limited. The authors have made a strong case for the practical utility of their method, and their results on real data provide convincing evidence of its value. This is exactly the kind of innovative work that NIPS should celebrate.
