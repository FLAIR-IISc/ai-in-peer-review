## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a statistical method to “stitch” together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics and correlations of a larger population than can be observed in a single recording session. The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system, specifically a linear Gaussian dynamical system, to the combined data. They demonstrate, through simulations and analysis of real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, that their method can accurately predict correlations – particularly noise correlations – between neurons that were not simultaneously recorded. This is a significant contribution as it offers a pathway to study larger neural circuits than currently feasible with existing imaging technology.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The introduction does a good job situating the work within the existing literature. It correctly identifies several lines of related research: inferring functional connectivity from calcium imaging and electrophysiology (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]), methods for dealing with subsampled observations [12, 13, 14, 15]. Crucially, the authors highlight the novelty of their approach – inferring connections between *non-simultaneously* observed neurons – which is not addressed by existing work. The paper clearly differentiates itself from existing methods like [12], which relies on a strong (and often unrealistic) monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations, and [13, 14, 15] which are applicable to different data characteristics or scales.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The problem addressed is fundamentally important. Current experimental limitations constrain our ability to record from large neural populations, hindering our understanding of circuit function. This work provides a promising computational solution to partially overcome this limitation.  The demonstrated ability to predict noise correlations is particularly valuable, as these correlations are often crucial for understanding neural coding and computation.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of a latent dynamical system is a natural and well-motivated framework for this problem. The choice of a linear Gaussian LDS, while simplifying, allows for tractable analysis and provides a clear baseline for evaluating more complex models in future work. The use of EM for parameter estimation is standard and appropriate.
* **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through both simulations and real data analysis. The simulation study carefully controls the ground truth and allows for a quantitative evaluation of the method's performance.  The comparison to a “naive” approach and a “fully observed” model provides valuable context for interpreting the results on real data. The fictional imaging scenario created on the experimental data is a clever way to benchmark performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the explanations are concise.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linearity Assumption:** The assumption of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant limitation. While acknowledged by the authors, the impact of this simplification on the accuracy of the results is not fully explored. Real neural circuits are inherently nonlinear, and calcium dynamics are also non-Gaussian. This limits the generalizability of the method to more complex scenarios.
* **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors mention that the method scales to the population sizes of interest, the computational cost of fitting the LDS, particularly for larger populations, is not thoroughly discussed. The online-EM approach is a good start, but the scaling behavior for truly large circuits (thousands of neurons) should be investigated.
* **Sensitivity to Overlap:** The results suggest that some degree of overlap between the observed populations is critical for successful stitching. The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate the relationship between the amount of overlap and the accuracy of the inferred correlations. The optimal overlap for a given experimental setup and population size is an important practical consideration.
* **Limited Exploration of Real Data:** The analysis of the real data is somewhat limited. While the authors demonstrate that the method works, a deeper investigation of the biological implications of the inferred correlations would be valuable. For example, do the inferred correlations align with known anatomical connections or functional roles of the neurons?



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial limitation in current neuroimaging technology.
* Provides a novel and theoretically sound approach to inferring population dynamics.
* Demonstrates strong performance in both simulations and real data.
* Well-written and clearly presented.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on a simplifying linearity assumption that may limit its generalizability.
* Scalability to very large neural populations is not fully established.
* Limited exploration of the biological implications of the inferred correlations.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in computational neuroscience. While the linearity assumption is a limitation, the method provides a valuable tool for leveraging existing data and inferring the dynamics of larger neural circuits. The rigorous evaluation and clear presentation further strengthen the paper.  I believe this work is a significant contribution to the field and would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:** **Accept** with minor revisions. 

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Discuss the impact of the linearity assumption more thoroughly.** Consider exploring extensions to nonlinear models, even if only briefly.
* **Provide more details on the computational cost and scalability of the method.** Include runtime information for different population sizes.
* **Investigate the relationship between overlap and performance in more detail.** 
* **Explore the biological implications of the inferred correlations in the real data.** Compare the inferred connections to known anatomical data or functional roles of the neurons.
