Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses an important experimental limitation: current two-photon imaging captures only subsets of neurons within a circuit, often in separate sessions. The authors propose a “stitching” approach that fits a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) to multiple partial recordings with known cell identities, treating unobserved neurons as latent dimensions. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned via EM (with an online variant), using session-specific observation matrices Ck that select which neurons are observed. The method aims to recover functional couplings and, more importantly, predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The approach is validated on simulations and on two datasets from mouse S1, using a fictional split of simultaneously recorded data into two sessions to emulate the partial-recording scenario. Performance is benchmarked against an upper bound (LDS trained on full simultaneous data) and a naive lower bound (separately fit LDSs combined ad hoc).

Relative to prior NIPS and related work, the formulation leverages well-established LDS modeling and EM with missing data [Dempster et al. 1977; standard system identification texts], and is conceptually related to latent GLMs for partially observed populations [Pillow & Latham 2008], inference of functional connectivity from calcium imaging [Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012], and to subspace/system identification [Katayama]. It differs from [Wohrer et al. 2010] by directly modeling dynamics to infer noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs without assuming a monotonic link to stimulus correlations. The main novelty is the explicit use of known observation masks across sessions to align partial views and empirically demonstrate accurate recovery of cross-session pairwise correlations.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and practical relevance: stitching partial 2p recordings is a pressing challenge. The proposed LDS-with-missing-data framework is a natural, computationally tractable solution.
- Solid empirical demonstration: On synthetic data, the method recovers cross-block couplings (r≈0.73) and noise correlations (r≈0.92). On real data, predicted correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs approach the fully observed model (r≈0.84) and markedly outperform a naive baseline.
- Sensible evaluation setup: upper/lower bounds provide context; activity prediction of unobserved neurons adds a useful metric; robustness evaluated across population sizes and two datasets.
- Transparent discussion of limitations (need for overlap, linear-Gaussian simplification) and potential extensions (sparsity, unobserved common inputs).

Weaknesses
Quality
- Assumptions and identifiability: The method assumes a single stationary LDS shared across sessions (A, B, Q, R constant), diagonal process noise Q and observation noise R, and accurate neuron identity across sessions. In practice, session-to-session nonstationarities (behavioral state, photobleaching/gain changes), correlated observation noise (neuropil contamination), and imperfect cell registration can bias inferred couplings and inflate apparent shared variability. These issues are acknowledged but not tested.
- Overlap is critical: Although the introduction suggests identification may be possible without overlap, the experiments report failure without it. The paper lacks a theoretical or empirical characterization of how much overlap is needed for reliable stitching (as a function of SNR, stimulus drive, network structure).
- Limited baselines: The “naive” baseline is weak. More informative comparators include: (i) shared-latent FA/GPFA with alignment via overlapping neurons; (ii) multiview LDS/subspace identification sharing A but allowing session-specific observation biases; (iii) LDS with low-dimensional latent inputs capturing common drive [e.g., Macke et al. 2012; Vidne et al. 2011]. This would help disentangle how much of the gain comes from dynamics vs shared latent structure vs overlap-based alignment.
- Calcium measurement model: The analysis works directly on high-pass filtered fluorescence, ignoring calcium/spike dynamics and nonlinearities. While the authors cite more realistic models, no experiments assess whether deconvolution or a calcium-aware LDS materially changes stitching performance.
- Reporting: No runtime/complexity analysis or scaling experiments beyond N≈100–200; no cross-validated likelihood or calibration metrics for correlation prediction.

Clarity
- Generally well written, but some details are underspecified: the exact stimulus used in the awake dataset; whether session-specific offsets/gains were allowed beyond d; how cell identity across sessions is established in practical non-overlapping fields of view; hyperparameters and stopping criteria for online EM; and the exact handling of stationarity (e.g., checks for drift).
- The role of process noise vs coupling in shaping noise correlations could be clarified, especially given diagonal Q.

Originality
- Methodologically modest: EM for LDS with missing observations is standard; the contribution is largely an application/instantiation to the partial-recording setting with empirical validation. There is limited algorithmic novelty.

Significance
- For systems neuroscience, the ability to predict cross-session noise correlations could be impactful, guiding experimental design when full-field imaging is infeasible. For the NIPS audience, significance is moderated by the incremental methodology and limited theoretical analysis.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, realistic problem; simple approach that works well on real data.
- Careful empirical comparisons to an upper bound; multiple datasets; activity prediction results are encouraging.
- Clear path for extensions (sparsity, latent common inputs) and experimental design implications.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty relative to standard LDS/EM with missing data.
- Heavy reliance on strong assumptions (stationarity, diagonal noise, accurate registration) not stress-tested.
- Overlap requirement not quantified; no theory on identifiability/necessary conditions.
- Baselines do not include stronger multiview latent variable models; no explicit calcium measurement model.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger baselines (shared-latent FA/GPFA, multiview LDS with session-specific observation biases, LDS with low-dim latent inputs) and report performance on correlation prediction and activity reconstruction.
- Quantify the overlap–coverage trade-off via simulations: how many overlapping neurons are needed as a function of SNR, population size, and stimulus informativeness? Provide guidance for experimentalists.
- Incorporate session-specific gains/offsets and possibly slow drift terms; test robustness to modest nonstationarity across sessions.
- Model correlated observation noise (e.g., per-field-of-view neuropil components) and/or include latent inputs to absorb common drive; compare to diagonal R/Q.
- Explore deconvolution or an explicit calcium dynamics observation model to assess sensitivity of stitching performance.
- Report computational complexity, convergence behavior, and scaling to larger N; consider sparsity-promoting priors on A and show their effect.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The problem is important and the empirical results are promising, but the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation lacks stronger baselines, robustness analyses, and guidance on overlap requirements. With these additions, the work could be competitive.

Confidence: 0.7
