Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations among neurons that were not recorded simultaneously, by “stitching” together multiple partial recordings of the same circuit. The authors model the underlying fluorescence activity with a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) and treat unobserved neurons as latent states. Different sessions correspond to different observation matrices Ck that select recorded neurons; parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned by EM (with an online-EM variant for efficiency), and inference is via Kalman smoothing. The key claim is that given partial, overlapping measurements across sessions, one can recover cross-couplings and predict noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs.

This is conceptually related to classic system identification and LDS with missing data (e.g., subspace identification, EM for state-space models), and to latent GLM/LDS approaches used in neural data analysis (e.g., Pillow et al. 2008; Macke et al. 2012; Mishchenko et al. 2011). It differs from prior “functional connectivity from calcium” work by explicitly addressing non-simultaneous recordings and aligning multiple “views” of the same circuit through shared dynamics. Compared to approaches that infer noise correlations from stimulus correlations (e.g., Wohrer et al. 2010), this method does not rely on monotonicity assumptions and instead uses a generative dynamical model.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The modeling framework (LDS + EM) is standard and appropriate for the proposed stitching aim. The rationale for identifiability (recovering the unobserved subspace up to a linear transform in the first session, then aligning with the second) is sound at an intuitive level. However, the paper does not formalize conditions (e.g., observability/controllability, overlap size, excitation by inputs) under which the parameters are identifiable and the alignment succeeds; this omission limits theoretical strength.
- Assumptions: The model assumes linear-Gaussian dynamics, stationary noise, and diagonal Q and R (with a note that correlated noise could be modeled via additional latent dimensions). Given calcium imaging’s nonlinearity and slow dynamics, this is a simplification; the authors acknowledge this and position their goal as predicting correlations rather than biophysical fidelity.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments with known ground truth show convincing recovery of couplings (r=0.95 observed pairs; r=0.73 unobserved pairs) and noise correlations (r=0.92), and activity prediction improvements. On real data, the validation uses a “fictional” split of simultaneously recorded datasets into two overlapping sessions; the stitched model predicts cross-session correlations approaching a “fully observed” upper bound and greatly outperforms a naive baseline. This is a reasonable first validation, but ecological validity would be stronger with truly non-simultaneous recordings.
- Baselines and metrics: The “naive” and “fully observed” comparisons are informative, but additional baselines (e.g., shared-latent FA/GPFA across sessions with session-specific loadings, Poisson/LDS variants, multi-view CCA) would better contextualize gains. Also, some metrics are reported relative to the fully observed LDS rather than empirical correlations on held-out data; including the latter would strengthen claims. Regularization is mentioned but not systematically explored; overfitting risks (A has O(N^2) parameters) are not thoroughly analyzed.
- Reproducibility: The model and EM updates are mostly clear, but there are notational typos in the measurement equation and limited details on initialization, convergence, and computational complexity; scalability to thousands of neurons is asserted but not demonstrated.

Clarity:
The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear statement of the problem and method. The intuition behind stitching is explained accessibly. Some notation errors (e.g., ykt = Ck(xkt + d + t), placement of noise term) should be fixed. The experimental setup, especially the fictional split and overlap fraction, is explained clearly, and the figures (as described) appear to support the claims.

Originality:
Algorithmically, the work leverages standard LDS/EM machinery with session-specific observation matrices; the main novelty is the framing and empirical demonstration that multi-session stitching can recover cross-pair correlations in practice. As such, originality is moderate. The explicit focus on non-simultaneous calcium imaging and the analysis of overlap requirements are useful contributions to experimental methodology.

Significance:
Within computational and systems neuroscience, the ability to infer cross-session noise correlations and joint dynamics is practically valuable and can influence experimental design (trade-off between overlap and coverage). The approach is simple, scalable to medium-sized populations, and could be extended with richer observation models. For the NeurIPS audience, the contribution is of moderate significance: it brings a known ML tool to a timely neuroscience problem and provides evidence that it works, but lacks strong theoretical advances or extensive real-world deployment.

Pros:
- Addresses an important and practical problem for modern imaging experiments.
- Simple, principled model; leverages well-understood inference with missing data.
- Convincing synthetic validation with ground truth.
- Real-data results show substantial gains over a naive baseline; clear analysis of overlap necessity.
- Potential to inform experimental design and to be extended with richer models/priors.

Cons:
- Limited novelty in methodology; largely an application of standard LDS/EM.
- Validation on real data is “fictional” (split sessions) rather than truly non-simultaneous recordings.
- Missing theoretical conditions for identifiability and required overlap; no analysis of failure modes.
- Sparse set of baselines; lacks comparisons to multi-view latent variable methods.
- Scalability and regularization are not rigorously addressed; some notation/clarity issues.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide theoretical conditions (or empirical phase diagrams) relating overlap fraction, session lengths, and stimulus richness to successful stitching.
- Evaluate on genuinely non-simultaneous sessions (e.g., repeated imaging across days) to test alignment under drift.
- Compare against shared-latent FA/GPFA/Poisson-LDS with session-specific loadings and to multi-view CCA.
- Report performance versus empirical held-out noise correlations, not only versus the fully observed LDS.
- Explore sparsity/structure in A and quantify effects on recovery and scalability; provide timing/complexity.
- Fix notation typos and detail initialization and convergence criteria.

Recommendation: Weak accept (6/10). The problem is important, the approach is sound and practical, and the results are promising, though methodological novelty and validation depth are limited.

Reviewer confidence: 3/5.
