

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics, including noise correlations between neurons not recorded simultaneously. The authors frame this as a latent dynamical system (LDS) problem with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian model and EM algorithm for parameter estimation. They validate on simulated data (with ground truth) and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, demonstrating that their approach outperforms a naive baseline (which assumes no cross-correlations between non-simultaneous recordings) and approaches the performance of a "fully observed" model (trained on all neurons, which is experimentally infeasible).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Clear problem framing and significance:** The paper effectively highlights a critical experimental bottleneck (imaging only subsets of large circuits) and proposes a method with immediate practical value for neuroscience. The synthetic and real-data experiments convincingly demonstrate that noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons can be predicted, addressing a gap in prior work (e.g., [12] requires strong monotonicity assumptions).  
2. **Strong technical validation:** The synthetic experiments provide ground-truth comparisons (e.g., correlation between predicted and true noise correlations = 0.92), while the real-data analysis uses a rigorous fictional imaging scenario to benchmark against an upper bound ("fully observed" model). The results show consistent superiority over the naive method (e.g., correlation accuracy for non-simultaneous pairs: 0.84 vs. 0.15).  
3. **Novel application of LDS:** While LDS is standard, applying it to *non-simultaneous recordings* is novel. The theoretical intuition (reconstruction from partial observations [21, 22]) is well-motivated, and the method scales to realistic population sizes (80–200 neurons), unlike prior latent GLM approaches [13].  
4. **Clarity and reproducibility:** The methods are well-described, with clear equations, pseudocode for the EM steps, and sufficient detail for replication. Figures (e.g., Fig. 2, 3) effectively illustrate key results.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Model limitations and lack of robustness testing:** The linear Gaussian LDS is a significant simplification for neural data (calcium dynamics are nonlinear, noise is often non-Gaussian [6, 16]). While the authors acknowledge this, they do not test whether more biophysically plausible models (e.g., incorporating calcium dynamics [6]) would improve performance. Crucially, they do not explore how performance degrades with *reduced overlap* between recording sessions (e.g., c=40% instead of c=60%). The paper states that "some overlap was critical" but provides no quantitative analysis of this dependency, which is essential for experimental design.  
2. **Naive method clarity:** The naive approach (Section 3.2) is poorly defined. How exactly are couplings averaged for overlapping neurons? The description ("set coefficients to 0 for non-simultaneous pairs, average for overlapping") is ambiguous. Without a precise definition, the comparison is hard to evaluate (e.g., is averaging the right baseline?).  
3. **Scalability and generalization:** The synthetic data is small (N=60 neurons), and real data is limited to 80–200 neurons. The method’s performance with larger populations (e.g., >500 neurons) or more than two sessions is untested. The paper claims generalization to "more than two populations" but lacks empirical evidence.  
4. **Overstated significance:** While the method is valuable, the claim that it "expands our limited capabilities" risks overstatement. Current imaging *can* capture hundreds of neurons [1, 2], and the method requires substantial overlap (c=60%), which may not be feasible in all experiments. The paper should temper this by discussing practical constraints (e.g., trade-offs between coverage and overlap).

**Constructive Suggestions:**  
- **Quantify overlap dependency:** Test performance across a range of overlap levels (e.g., c=30–70%) and provide a clear recommendation for minimum overlap.  
- **Clarify the naive baseline:** Define the naive method mathematically (e.g., via equations) and compare it to other baselines (e.g., [12] if applicable).  
- **Extend model evaluation:** Add a brief experiment with a nonlinear LDS or a calcium-deconvolution step [6] to assess if more realistic models improve predictions.  
- **Discuss limitations:** Explicitly state the method’s constraints (e.g., "Requires >50% overlap; performance degrades with sparse connectivity") and how they impact experimental design.  

**Decision:** Weak Accept. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a technically sound, well-validated method. Its significance for neuroscience is clear, and the core idea is original. However, the lack of robustness analysis (overlap dependency), ambiguous naive baseline, and model simplifications prevent a stronger recommendation. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors have a solid foundation; with minor revisions, this work could be a valuable contribution to NIPS.
