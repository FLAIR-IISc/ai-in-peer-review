This paper presents a novel and important computational method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, non-simultaneous recordings. The core idea is to model the entire unobserved population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS) and treat each experimental recording session as a different partial observation of this underlying system. The parameters of the full system, including couplings between neurons that were never recorded together, are then inferred using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. This "stitching" approach is validated on both simulated data and on two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

The problem addressed is of immense practical significance in systems neuroscience. Experimental limitations are a primary bottleneck in understanding circuit-level computation, and methods that can computationally extend the reach of recording technologies are highly valuable. This work is well-situated within the existing literature on functional connectivity inference, correctly distinguishing itself from methods that require simultaneous recordings (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011; Pillow et al., 2008) or those that make strong assumptions linking stimulus and noise correlations (Wohrer et al., 2010). While the theoretical underpinnings that a dynamical system can be identified from partial observations are not new (as the authors cite), the application of this principle to the practical problem of stitching modern neuroscience recordings is novel and impactful.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The primary strength of this paper is its direct and elegant solution to a critical, unsolved problem in experimental neuroscience. The ability to infer joint dynamics and noise correlations across non-simultaneous recordings could fundamentally change how large-scale circuit mapping experiments are designed and analyzed. The proposed "stitching" framework is a significant conceptual and practical advance.

2.  **Methodological Soundness and Clarity:** The choice of an LDS with missing observations is a principled and well-justified starting point. The paper is exceptionally clear in its exposition of the model, the EM fitting procedure, and the experimental setup. The framing of the problem is intuitive and the proposed solution is elegant.

3.  **Rigorous and Creative Evaluation:** The validation strategy is a major strength. The use of simulated data with a known ground truth provides a clear proof-of-concept. More impressively, the "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is a clever and powerful way to quantitatively assess performance in the absence of ground truth. By defining a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound, the authors provide a compelling demonstration of their method's efficacy, showing it performs much better than the naive approach and often approaches the theoretical best-case performance.

4.  **Impressive Results:** The results are strong and convincing. The method not only recovers coupling matrix structure (Fig. 2a, 3a) but, more importantly, accurately predicts pairwise noise correlations—a key quantity of interest in neuroscience—for neuron pairs that were never observed together (Fig. 2b, 3c). The summary results in Figure 4 demonstrate robustness across population sizes and datasets.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Model Simplicity vs. Neural Reality:** The choice of a linear-Gaussian model is pragmatic for tractability but is a significant simplification of neural dynamics and calcium imaging. Real neural activity is characterized by discrete spiking (non-Gaussian), and the transformation from spikes to calcium fluorescence is nonlinear. The authors acknowledge this limitation but could discuss its implications more deeply. For example, is the model's success dependent on the slow, low-pass-filtered nature of calcium signals, which might appear more linear-Gaussian than the underlying spikes? How might the method fare on deconvolved spike data or direct electrophysiological recordings?

2.  **The Role of Overlapping Neurons:** A critical detail that warrants more investigation is the necessity of overlapping neurons between recording sessions. In the simulated data, the method works with two completely disjoint sets of neurons. However, the authors note that for real data, they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" and used a 60% overlap. This is a crucial finding with major implications for experimental design. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included a systematic analysis of how performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases. This would provide invaluable practical guidance to experimentalists.

3.  **Identifiability and Scalability:** The intuition that a second recording can resolve the linear ambiguity of the latent state is key, but this is explained somewhat informally. A more formal discussion of the conditions for identifiability would strengthen the paper. Furthermore, while the method is presented for two sessions, the discussion suggests generalization to many. However, the performance and computational cost of stitching together, for example, ten different small recordings are not explored. As the number of parameters in the matrix `A` grows quadratically with the total neuron count `N`, potential issues of overfitting and computational scaling could become significant and should be discussed.

4.  **Comparison to a Simpler Baseline:** While the "naive" model serves as a reasonable lower bound, another useful baseline would be a factor analysis-style model (e.g., PCA or FA) fit to the concatenated data, where latent factors are shared across sessions. This would model shared variance but without the explicit temporal dynamics of the LDS. Comparing against such a model could help disentangle the contributions of modeling shared variance versus modeling explicit temporal dependencies.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, elegant, and highly significant contribution. It addresses a fundamental problem in modern neuroscience with a principled approach and validates it thoroughly and creatively. The strengths—in particular, the importance of the problem, the clarity of the method, and the rigor of the evaluation—far outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily relate to the limitations of the linear model and opportunities for further analysis. The work is likely to be highly influential for both computational and experimental neuroscientists.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   High significance: Addresses a major bottleneck in experimental neuroscience.
*   Novel and impactful application of established machine learning techniques.
*   Clear, well-written, and methodologically sound.
*   Excellent and convincing validation on both synthetic and real-world data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The linear-Gaussian assumption is a strong simplification.
*   The critical dependence on overlapping neurons for real data is not systematically explored.

Overall, this is a strong submission that clearly meets the standards for NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
