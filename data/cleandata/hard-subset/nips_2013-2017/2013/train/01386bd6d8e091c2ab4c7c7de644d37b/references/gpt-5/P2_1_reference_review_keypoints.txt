- Summary: Proposes stitching partial neural recordings by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with session-specific observation matrices and EM/Kalman smoothing to recover cross-session dynamics and noise correlations.

- Main empirical claim: Method recovers non-simultaneous pairwise correlations on synthetic data and on a fictional split of real 2-photon recordings, outperforming a naive baseline and approaching a fully observed upper bound.

- Method novelty: Not a new model class—uses standard LDS/EM with missing data—but frames and empirically evaluates this approach for multi-session stitching in calcium imaging.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on established latent-variable models (PLDS/GPFA), system identification, and missing-data Kalman EM; contrasts with connectivity inference and subsampling literature but lacks comparisons to several relevant baselines.

- Soundness: Statistical machinery is standard and correctly applied; synthetic ground-truth evaluation is appropriate.

- Evidence strength: Convincing gains over naive baseline, but strongest real-data results rely on a fictional split that avoids true multi-session nonstationarities.

- Key limitation: Requires overlap in practice—no-overlap stitching failed on real data despite synthetic success.

- Modeling assumptions: Linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q and R, stationarity across sessions, and treating fluorescence as direct observations without explicit calcium/spike dynamics.

- Identifiability: No theoretical identifiability analysis specific to the multi-session setting beyond intuition.

- Baselines: Comparisons are weak; missing baselines include PLDS/GPFA with missing data, subspace ID with missing entries, and multi-view/shared-latent models.

- Scalability: Runtimes and computational complexity are not reported.

- Clarity: Paper is well written and reproducible in principle; minor notation typos and some missing hyperparameter/cross-validation detail.

- Originality judgement: Conceptually straightforward application of classic methods, but useful and not previously established in this specific practical framing.

- Potential significance: Could impact how labs aggregate multi-field recordings and plan overlap vs coverage, but current evidence falls short of robust multi-session validation.

- Strength: Addresses a practical problem with a simple, broadly usable method and solid synthetic results.

- Strength: Real-data fictional-split experiments show strong improvement over naive baseline and good recovery of non-simultaneous correlations.

- Weakness: No demonstration on truly separate sessions with realistic drift, session-specific noise/gains, or cell identity uncertainty.

- Weakness: Simplistic observation model for calcium; ignores AR calcium dynamics and correlated noise processes.

- Weakness: Lack of systematic ablations (e.g., overlap fraction, coupling strength) and no identifiability/rules for failure modes.

- Suggestion: Test on truly separate sessions with session-specific drifts and limited overlap; add session-specific offsets/gains.

- Suggestion: Compare to stronger baselines (PLDS/GPFA, subspace ID, multi-view latent models).

- Suggestion: Incorporate a simple calcium observation model and allow correlated innovations; test robustness to model mismatch.

- Suggestion: Provide performance vs overlap, coupling strength, stimulus richness, and report runtimes/scalability.

- Acceptance argument: Tackles an important experimental limitation with a simple principled tool and promising empirical benefits.

- Rejection argument: Limited novelty, reliance on fictional-split real-data validation, dependence on overlap, and missing comparisons to competitive baselines.

- Recommendation: Weak accept (medium reviewer confidence).
