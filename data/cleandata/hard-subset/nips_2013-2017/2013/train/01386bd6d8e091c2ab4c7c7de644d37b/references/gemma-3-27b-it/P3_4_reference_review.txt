## NIPS Review: Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and technically sound method for stitching together partial recordings of neural activity to infer the dynamics of a larger population. The approach, leveraging a latent dynamical system framework, is well-motivated by experimental limitations in large-scale neural recording and demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data. While the method relies on a relatively simple linear Gaussian model, the paper convincingly argues that even this simplification allows for accurate prediction of crucial statistics like noise correlations. I believe this work represents a significant contribution with clear potential impact on the field, and recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles a fundamental problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer the activity of large neural circuits when recording is limited to a subset of neurons at a time.  The authors propose a method to combine data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings, motivated by the experimental reality of 2-photon calcium imaging in cortical circuits. They formulate the problem as inferring the parameters of a latent dynamical system (LDS), where the observed data represents incomplete observations of the full population activity.  

The authors thoroughly contextualize their work within the existing literature. They correctly identify and differentiate themselves from studies focusing on connectivity inference *within* a single recording session ([6,7,8,9,10,11]), and from previous attempts to address partial observations, highlighting the crucial distinction that their method specifically targets inferring correlations *between* neurons never observed simultaneously ([12,13,14,15]). The acknowledgment of the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption is also appreciated, recognizing the ongoing need for more complex models.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates a pressing challenge in systems neuroscience and presents a practical solution tailored to current experimental capabilities.
* **Sound Methodology:** The choice of a latent dynamical system as a framework is appropriate and well-justified. The use of the EM algorithm for parameter estimation is standard and the authors highlight practical considerations like online-EM for efficiency.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The results on both simulated and real data are convincing. The ability to accurately recover coupling parameters and, crucially, noise correlations in the simulated data provides a strong validation of the method.  The application to experimental data from mouse somatosensory cortex demonstrates the applicability of the method to real-world datasets. The thoughtful fictional imaging scenario for evaluating performance on real data, coupled with clear comparisons to upper and lower bounds, strengthens the conclusions.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and organized. The equations are presented clearly, and the figures effectively illustrate the key concepts and results.
* **Significance:**  This work opens up exciting possibilities for analyzing large-scale neural circuits. By enabling the integration of data from multiple recordings, it paves the way for building more comprehensive models of neural computation and testing theoretical predictions.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:**  While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a potential drawback. Real neural circuits are inherently non-linear, and calcium dynamics are demonstrably non-Gaussian. This simplification may limit the accuracy of the inferred dynamics in certain scenarios. However, the paper convincingly demonstrates that even within this constraint, the method provides valuable insights.
* **Computational Scalability:**  Although the authors mention using online-EM, the computational cost of fitting the LDS model to large populations could still be a limitation.  Further discussion of scalability challenges and potential solutions (e.g., sparse modeling, dimensionality reduction) would be beneficial.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for performance raises a practical concern. The paper could benefit from a more in-depth investigation of the optimal level of overlap and its impact on the accuracy of the inferred dynamics.



**4.  Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Section 2.2 (Experimental Data):**  Could the authors provide more detail on the pre-processing steps applied to the calcium imaging data (e.g., denoising, baseline correction)?
* **Section 3.2 (Experimental Data):**  The choice of 50 most correlated neurons seems somewhat arbitrary. How sensitive are the results to this choice?
* **Figure 3:**  The visualization of the coupling matrices is helpful. It would be beneficial to also show the corresponding correlation matrices directly, to provide a more intuitive understanding of the relationship between couplings and correlations.
* **Discussion:**  The discussion section briefly mentions priors on network topology.  Could the authors elaborate on how such priors might be incorporated into the model and what benefits they might offer?




**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and challenging problem in systems neuroscience.
* Presents a technically sound and well-validated method.
* Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.
* Clearly written and organized.
* Potential for significant impact on the field.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on a simplifying linear Gaussian assumption.
* Computational scalability may be a concern.
* Requires some overlap between recording sessions.



**6. Final Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The method presented is a valuable contribution to the field, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated its effectiveness. While the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption and computational scalability should be acknowledged, they do not detract from the overall significance of the work.  The potential to unlock deeper insights into the dynamics of large neural circuits makes this paper a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
