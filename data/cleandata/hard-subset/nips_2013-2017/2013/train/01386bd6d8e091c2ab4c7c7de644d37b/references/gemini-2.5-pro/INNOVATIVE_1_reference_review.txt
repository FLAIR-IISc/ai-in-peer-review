**Summary**
This paper addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a large, functional circuit. The authors propose a statistical method, which they term "stitching," to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings. The core idea is to model the entire population as a single latent dynamical system (here, a Linear Dynamical System, or LDS) and treat each experimental session as a partial observation of this larger system's state. Using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, they fit a single, unified model of the full circuit's dynamics. The main and most surprising claim is that this method can accurately estimate the functional connectivity and noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded at the same time. The authors validate this bold claim first on simulated data with known ground truth and then with a clever "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Ambition:** The central contribution of this paper is not an incremental improvement but a genuinely new and bold conceptual proposal. The idea of inferring interactions between non-simultaneously observed units is counter-intuitive and tackles a major, practical bottleneck in systems neuroscience. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that can open up new avenues for both computational and experimental work. The paper shifts the perspective from analyzing single datasets to coherently integrating multiple partial views of a system into a unified whole.

2.  **Significant Potential Impact:** If this method proves robust, its significance for neuroscience would be immense. It suggests a concrete computational path to move beyond the physical limitations of current recording technologies. Researchers could design experiments to systematically tile a large neural circuit across multiple sessions with the confidence that the resulting data could be combined into a single, comprehensive dynamical model. This could enable the construction of circuit-scale functional models that are currently out of reach, profoundly impacting our ability to test theories of neural computation.

3.  **Elegant and Convincing Validation:** The authors' approach to validation on real data is particularly strong. Lacking ground-truth connectivity, they create a "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is artificially split into partial recordings. This allows them to benchmark their stitching method against a clear upper bound (a model fit to all simultaneous data) and a lower bound (a naive combination of models fit to each partial dataset). The results, showing that the stitched model's performance in predicting cross-correlations approaches the upper bound and far exceeds the lower bound, provide compelling evidence for the method's efficacy.

4.  **Clarity and Simplicity of the Approach:** The authors wisely opt for a simple Linear Dynamical System (LDS) to demonstrate their core idea. While this model is an obvious simplification of the underlying nonlinear, spiking neural dynamics, it serves as a powerful and interpretable proof-of-concept. By keeping the model simple, the paper's central message—that stitching is possible—is made exceptionally clear and is not obscured by complex, non-essential modeling machinery.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Simplified Model of Neural Dynamics:** The choice of an LDS, while a strength for clarity, is also the primary weakness. Real neural activity is not linear or Gaussian. The model does not explicitly account for the non-linear transformation from spikes to calcium fluorescence. The authors acknowledge this, but the performance of the stitching concept with more realistic (and harder to fit) models, like switching linear dynamical systems or nonlinear models, remains an open question. However, for a paper introducing a new paradigm, this simplification is acceptable as the goal is to demonstrate feasibility.

2.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The method implicitly assumes that the underlying circuit dynamics (i.e., the coupling matrix A) are stationary across the different recording sessions. In biological reality, synaptic weights can change, and the overall state of the animal (e.g., attention, arousal) can modulate functional connectivity. The paper does not explore the method's robustness to violations of this assumption, which could be a significant practical challenge for experiments spanning multiple days.

3.  **Dependence on Overlapping Neurons:** The authors note in their experiments on real data that having an overlapping set of neurons recorded across sessions was critical for good performance. This is an important practical finding, but its implications are not fully explored. It raises questions about experimental design: what is the optimal trade-off between maximizing the total number of unique neurons recorded versus ensuring sufficient overlap between sessions? While the paper introduces the concept, it leaves these practical guidelines for future work.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper presents a bold, novel, and highly significant idea. It directly confronts a critical limitation in a key scientific field and offers an elegant and computationally plausible solution. The core contribution is conceptual: demonstrating that it is possible to infer latent interactions across non-simultaneously observed parts of a system.

While the methodological implementation relies on a simplified linear-Gaussian model, this is a reasonable choice for a proof-of-concept paper that aims to establish a new principle. The weaknesses, such as the stationarity assumption and reliance on a simplified model, are not fatal flaws but rather obvious and exciting directions for future research that will build upon this foundational work. The paper's validation is thorough and clever, providing convincing evidence that the "stitching" idea works in practice on both simulated and real neural data.

The potential impact of this work is substantial. It could change how neuroscientists design, analyze, and interpret experiments involving large neural populations. This is not an incremental paper; it introduces a new way of thinking about data integration. For its novelty, ambition, and potential significance, I strongly recommend acceptance.
