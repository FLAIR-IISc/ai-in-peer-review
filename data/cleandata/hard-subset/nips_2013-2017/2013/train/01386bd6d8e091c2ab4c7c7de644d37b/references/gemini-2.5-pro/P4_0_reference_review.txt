### Summary

This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous, partial recordings of that population. The core idea is to model the entire neural circuit as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session, which captures only a subset of the neurons, is treated as a partial and noisy observation of the full latent state. The authors propose using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to fit the parameters of this single LDS (i.e., the coupling matrix, stimulus response, and noise statistics) using data from all partial recording sessions combined. The key contribution is demonstrating that this "stitching" approach can successfully recover statistics, such as noise correlations, between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

This work addresses a significant practical limitation in experimental neuroscience, where techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only access a fraction of a circuit at any given time. While previous work has focused on inferring connectivity from fully observed populations (e.g., Pillow et al., 2008; Mishchenko et al., 2011) or dealing with subsampled data under different assumptions (e.g., Wohrer et al., 2010, which requires a link between stimulus and noise correlations), this paper offers a direct and principled approach for combining non-simultaneous recordings. The method is grounded in the well-established theory of system identification from partial observations (e.g., Katayama, 2005), but its application to this specific problem in neuroscience appears novel.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and important problem. The ability to computationally combine partial recordings to model a larger, unobserved circuit could significantly extend the reach of current experimental technologies. If effective, this method could enable data-driven modeling of circuit dynamics at a scale that is currently inaccessible, advancing our ability to test theories of neural computation.

2.  **Originality and Novelty:** The proposed approach of using a single latent dynamical system to stitch together non-simultaneous recordings is a novel and clever solution to a practical problem. While the components (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to this "stitching" problem is new and not an obvious extension of prior work. It provides a clear conceptual framework for integrating disparate datasets from the same underlying system.

3.  **Technical Quality and Evaluation:** The methodology is technically sound. The use of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real experimental data is a strong point of the evaluation. By splitting a fully-observed dataset into non-simultaneous partial sets, the authors can compare their method's output against an upper bound (a model trained on the full, simultaneous data) and a lower bound (a "naive" model that treats sessions independently). This provides a rigorous and quantitative assessment of the method's performance. The validation on both simulated data with known ground truth and two real calcium imaging datasets (anesthetized and awake) demonstrates the method's effectiveness and robustness.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly articulated with a concrete experimental example. The methods are described concisely, and the results are presented logically with clear figures that effectively support the main claims. The distinction between the proposed method, the upper bound, and the lower bound is made very clear, which strengthens the interpretation of the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Model Simplicity:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of the underlying biophysical reality of neural circuits, which involve nonlinear dynamics and spiking activity. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper would be stronger if it included a more detailed discussion of the potential limitations this imposes. For instance, can this model capture higher-order correlations or non-Gaussian features of the population activity? While the model proves effective for predicting pairwise correlations, its limitations for other dynamic properties are not explored.

2.  **Identifiability and Dependence on Overlap:** The paper provides an intuitive argument for why the method works but lacks a formal discussion of the conditions required for model identifiability. A key practical question for experimental design is how much overlap (i.e., neurons recorded in multiple sessions) is necessary for the stitching to succeed. The authors state that for their simulation, "some overlap was critical" when they switched from c=50% (no overlap) to c=60% (overlap). This critical dependency is not systematically investigated. An analysis of how performance degrades as a function of overlap size, population size, and the number of recording sessions would significantly increase the paper's practical value.

3.  **Scalability:** The demonstrations are limited to stitching two partial recordings. While the framework can conceptually be extended to more sessions, its practical and computational scalability is not addressed. The EM algorithm for LDS can be computationally demanding, and performance might degrade as more sessions are added, especially with sparse or complex overlap patterns.

4.  **Validation Scenario:** While the "fictional imaging scenario" is a clever validation strategy, it relies on temporally contiguous data from a single long recording. A more stringent test would involve genuinely separate recordings, potentially taken on different days. This would introduce additional challenges like non-stationarities in neural activity or recording quality, which the current model does not account for but would be present in a real-world application.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   Addresses a significant and practical problem at the intersection of machine learning and experimental neuroscience.
*   Presents a novel and elegant application of a well-understood statistical framework (LDS) to a new problem domain.
*   The validation is rigorous, using both synthetic and real data, and includes well-chosen upper and lower performance bounds.
*   The results are convincing, demonstrating accurate prediction of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.
*   The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow.

### Arguments against Acceptance

*   The linear Gaussian model is a major simplification, and the limitations of this choice are not fully explored.
*   The paper lacks a systematic analysis of key parameters for practical application, such as the required amount of overlap between sessions and scalability to many sessions.
*   A more formal discussion of model identifiability conditions would strengthen the theoretical underpinnings.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its high significance, novelty, and strong empirical validation, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The work introduces a valuable computational tool that could have a considerable impact on the analysis of large-scale neural recordings. The weaknesses identified are primarily limitations that could be addressed in future work and do not undermine the core contribution presented here.
