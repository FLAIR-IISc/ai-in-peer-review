- Proposes a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) method to "stitch" multiple partial recordings and infer joint neural population dynamics, including correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.  
- Demonstrates method on simulations and two real calcium imaging datasets, showing stitching outperforms a naive per-session approach and approaches a (fictional) fully observed upper bound.  
- Method is technically sound, theoretically grounded, and implemented with EM and Kalman smoothing for missing observations.  
- Experimental validation is clear and comprehensive, comparing stitching to naive and fully observed baselines on simulated and real data.  
- The approach has strong practical significance by enabling better use of existing recording tech to study large neural circuits.  
- Authors situate the work well within prior literature and clearly differentiate from related methods.  
- Paper is well-structured and clearly written, with informative figures and reproducible mathematical detail.  
- The linear Gaussian LDS assumption is simplistic relative to real neural dynamics and limits discussion of nonlinear/spiking extensions.  
- Sensitivity to session overlap is underexplored; no systematic analysis of how performance varies with different overlap levels.  
- The "fully observed" baseline is a fictional upper bound and not a realistic practical comparator.  
- Scalability beyond two populations is asserted but not empirically demonstrated (e.g., stitching three+ populations).  
- Lacks comparison to alternative missing-data approaches (e.g., matrix completion) to contextualize advantages.  
- Overall quality is high: complete, empirically supported work rather than a position piece.  
- Clarity is good, though readers unfamiliar with LDS might benefit from a brief glossary.  
- The stitching idea is original in this neural population context and not seen in recent proceedings.  
- Significance is high: could materially improve analyses of large cortical circuits without new hardware.  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, novel, and practically important despite limitations.  
- Suggested improvements: discuss extensions to nonlinear/spiking models, provide performance vs. overlap plot, compare to matrix completion, justify/clarify diagonal noise covariance choice, and detail how the fictional imaging scenario was generated.
