Summary
The paper tackles a practically important problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations from multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same neural circuit when simultaneous imaging of all neurons is infeasible. The authors propose a “stitching” framework based on a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with known selection matrices per session (Ck), shared latent dynamics x_t+1 = A x_t + B u_t + η_t and measurement model y_t = Ck (x_t + d) + ε_t. Parameters are learned with EM (online mini-batch followed by batch EM). The key claim is that by jointly fitting all sessions—even with limited overlap in recorded neurons—one can reconstruct cross-couplings and, more importantly, predict noise correlations between neurons never observed simultaneously.

They demonstrate the approach on simulations (N=60, structured A, weak stimulus, K=10 trials) and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (mouse S1; anesthetized and awake; 80–200 ROIs at ~3 Hz). For the real data, they create a fictional split into two sessions with 60% overlap and withhold 20% for testing, benchmarking against an upper bound (LDS fit to all neurons jointly) and a lower bound (naive: independent LDS per session, merged parameters). Results show accurate prediction of noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs (sim: r≈0.92; anesthetized: r≈0.84 wrt fully observed model) and improved activity cross-prediction over the naive baseline. Off-diagonal couplings are partially recovered (sim: r≈0.73; real: lower but positive). They note performance degrades without overlap.

Relation to prior work
The work connects to: (i) inference of functional connectivity from calcium data (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al.); (ii) latent dynamical models for population activity (e.g., Pillow & Latham latent GLMs; GPFA/PLDS literature); (iii) system identification/subspace methods and LDS with missing data; (iv) multi-session alignment/“stitching” of population recordings (less developed at the time). The novelty here is applying a joint LDS with known selection matrices across sessions to infer cross-session couplings and predict noise correlations for never co-recorded pairs, with empirical validation on real imaging data.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely, impactful problem with clear relevance to both ML for neuroscience and experimental design.
- Conceptually simple, computationally tractable framework; uses standard EM/Kalman smoothing and scales to 100–200 neurons.
- Solid empirical evaluation: simulations with known ground truth and real 2p data; comparison to upper and lower bounds; multiple metrics (correlations, couplings, cross-prediction).
- Demonstrates practical insights (need for overlap; prediction improves with population size).
- Transparent about assumptions and limitations; discusses extensions (latent low-rank noise, sparsity priors).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Model mismatch: LDS on fluorescence ignores known nonlinearities (spiking→calcium→fluorescence, indicator kinetics). While authors acknowledge this, adding an experiment with a more realistic observation model (e.g., deconvolved spikes, PLDS) or showing robustness to synthetic nonlinearity would strengthen claims.
- Identifiability and theory: The intuition section is helpful, but formal conditions for identifiability with missing observations and limited overlap are not established. Citing and briefly stating conditions from LDS identification with missing data (e.g., Ghahramani & Hinton 1996; Roweis & Ghahramani; subspace ID with missing outputs) would clarify when stitching can succeed and why it fails without overlap.
- Evaluation on real data uses the “fully observed” LDS as the reference for correlation matrices. A stronger test would compare model-predicted noise correlations to empirical residual correlations computed directly from simultaneously recorded held-out data (after regressing out stimulus), with confidence intervals accounting for sampling noise.
- Overlap dependence and data requirements: The paper notes poor performance without overlap. Please quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction, session length T, and number of sessions; include ablations to guide experimental design.
- Regularization and stability: Large LDS can be ill-conditioned. Details on enforcing stability (spectral radius of A), priors (e.g., sparsity, smoothness), and initialization matter. Provide practical guidance and runtime/complexity, and report whether A is constrained stable or regularized.
- Noise model: Q and R are taken diagonal; correlated innovations are suggested via unobserved latent dimensions but not evaluated. Demonstrate the benefit of a low-rank plus diagonal Q or factor-analytic observation noise on real data.
- Cell identity across sessions is assumed known. In practice, cross-day/session cell matching can be imperfect. A robustness analysis to mismatches or a probabilistic assignment model would increase applicability.

Quality
Technically sound overall; the EM formulation with session-specific Ck is correct and well-motivated. Claims are supported by simulations and real data, though the key real-data correlation evaluation should also be made against empirical held-out correlations. The paper is cautious about limitations.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Some notation is dense (e.g., ykt,σkj), and a compact algorithmic summary (pseudo-code), initialization details, and computational costs would help reproducibility. Clarify how noise correlations are computed on data (trial structure, residualization). Minor typos/formatting.

Originality
The stitching idea via a joint LDS with missing observations is a natural but useful combination of known techniques. The contribution is primarily in demonstrating its efficacy for multi-session calcium imaging to predict never-co-recorded noise correlations, which, to my knowledge, is not shown in prior work.

Significance
High practical significance for large-scale imaging and experimental design. The approach is likely to be adopted or extended (e.g., to PLDS/GPLDS, LFADS-like models, low-rank noise). The empirical results are compelling.

Questions for authors
- How does performance vary with overlap fraction, T per session, and number of sessions? Any guidance for optimal overlap under fixed imaging time?
- Do results hold if you compute empirical noise correlations on held-out simultaneously recorded data rather than comparing to a fully observed LDS?
- How do you ensure stability of A? Any regularization used in reported experiments?
- Runtime and scaling: what are N, T, K limits in practice? Any use of sparsity/exploitation of structure?
- Can you quantify robustness to mis-registered cells across sessions?

Pros
- Addresses an important, realistic problem with a simple and effective method.
- Strong empirical results on both synthetic and real data.
- Clear improvement over a naive baseline; approaches an upper bound.
- Provides actionable insights for experimental design (need for overlap).

Cons
- Limited theoretical guarantees and conditions for success with partial overlap.
- Real-data evaluation relies on model-to-model comparisons for correlations.
- Simplified observation/noise models; limited robustness analyses.
- Scalability and regularization details are sparse.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The paper offers a practical, impactful approach with solid empirical support. Addressing the evaluation against empirical held-out correlations, adding overlap/length ablations, and clarifying stability/regularization would further strengthen it.
