**Summary**

This paper presents a method for inferring the dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population, where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The authors frame this as a missing data problem and propose using a Linear Dynamical System (LDS) to model the entire population's activity. The parameters of this global LDS are estimated from the partial observations using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is tested on simulated data generated from an LDS and on two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, a "fictional imaging scenario" is created by splitting a single, fully-observed recording into two partial, overlapping "sessions". The main claim is that this "stitching" method can accurately predict statistics, such as noise correlations, between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

The primary strength of this paper is that it addresses a significant and practical problem in experimental neuroscience. The inability to record simultaneously from all neurons in a circuit is a fundamental limitation, and computational tools to overcome this would be of great value. The proposed idea of "stitching" together partial recordings is conceptually appealing, and the paper is clearly written and well-organized. The validation strategy on real data, using "fully observed" and "naive" models to establish upper and lower performance bounds, is sound and provides a clear context for the results.

However, the paper has several substantial weaknesses that undermine its conclusions and potential impact.

**1. Methodological Soundness and Model Choice:**
The core of the proposed method relies on a Linear Dynamical System (LDS) with Gaussian noise. While computationally tractable, this is a highly simplified and questionable model for neural population dynamics. Neural activity is characterized by nonlinear interactions and non-Gaussian, point-process spiking activity. The calcium fluorescence signals analyzed here are themselves a nonlinear and slow representation of the underlying spikes. The authors acknowledge this limitation but proceed to claim that the model can still predict correlations accurately. This raises a critical question: are the inferred parameters (e.g., the coupling matrix A) a meaningful representation of the underlying circuit's functional connectivity, or is the LDS simply a flexible enough statistical tool to fit second-order moments in the data? The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the former, more impactful, interpretation. The reliance on such a demonstrably simple model for a complex biological system requires much more justification and analysis of its limitations than is provided.

**2. Inadequate Validation:**
The validation presented is not sufficiently rigorous to support the broad claims made.

*   **Simulated Data:** The synthetic data is generated from an LDS, the very model being used for inference. While this serves as a useful sanity check that the EM implementation is correct, it offers no evidence that the method will work on data that violates the model's core assumptions (i.e., any real neural data). The strong performance on this simulation is expected and does not validate the approach for its intended application.

*   **Real Data and the "Fictional" Scenario:** The "fictional imaging scenario" is a clever way to generate a test case with a known ground truth (the fully observed model). However, it critically sidesteps the most challenging aspect of applying this method in practice: non-stationarity between recording sessions. In a real experiment, different subsets of neurons would be recorded on different days or at least in separate experimental blocks. The underlying state of the animal (e.g., arousal, attention) and the circuit itself could change significantly between these sessions. The current model assumes a single, stationary LDS governs the dynamics across all sessions, an assumption that is unlikely to hold. By drawing both "sessions" from a single contiguous recording, the authors test their method under the most ideal and unrealistic conditions, where stationarity is guaranteed.

*   **Requirement of Overlapping Neurons:** A crucial detail mentioned in Section 2.3 is that the method "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" on the real dataset. This is a significant practical limitation that contradicts the more general framing of the problem. It suggests that the model relies on these "anchor" neurons to align the dynamics across sessions. This is in contrast to the simulated experiment, which successfully used non-overlapping populations. This discrepancy is not adequately explained and substantially weakens the claim of being able to infer dynamics between entirely separate, non-simultaneously recorded populations. The paper should have explored the performance as a function of overlap percentage, as this appears to be a critical parameter for success.

**3. Originality and Significance:**
From a machine learning perspective, the technical novelty is limited. The paper applies the standard EM algorithm for an LDS to a specific missing data pattern. While the application is novel, the underlying algorithmic machinery is well-established. The significance of the contribution, therefore, rests entirely on its demonstrated utility for real-world neuroscience problems. As argued above, the current validation is insufficient to establish this utility. Without demonstrating robustness to model mismatch (non-linearity, non-Gaussianity) and experimental non-stationarities, the proposed method remains an interesting proof-of-concept rather than a readily applicable tool. The claim that this can help "map population dynamics of all the neurons in an entire neural circuit" seems premature given the demonstrated limitations.

**In summary:**

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a well-motivated and important problem in neuroscience.
*   The core idea is intuitive and the paper is clearly presented.
*   The use of upper and lower bounds for evaluation on real data is a good methodological choice.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The method relies on a linear Gaussian model, which is a poor match for the known properties of neural circuits.
*   Validation on simulated data is circular, as the data is generated from the same model class used for inference.
*   The validation on real data uses an idealized scenario that ignores the crucial problem of non-stationarity between experimental sessions.
*   The method's apparent reliance on overlapping neuron populations in real data is a major practical limitation that is not fully explored or emphasized.

The paper presents an interesting idea but fails to provide the rigorous evidence needed to convince a skeptical reader of its practical value. The gap between the idealized conditions under which the method is shown to work and the complexities of real experimental data is too large.
