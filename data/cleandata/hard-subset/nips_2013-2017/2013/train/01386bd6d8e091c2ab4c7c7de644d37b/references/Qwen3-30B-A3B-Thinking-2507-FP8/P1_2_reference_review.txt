

# Peer Review

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper presents a novel computational approach to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, even for neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The authors address a critical limitation in current neural imaging technology: while 2-photon calcium imaging can record hundreds of neurons simultaneously, cortical circuits often contain thousands of neurons, making complete population recordings infeasible. Their key insight is that because neurons in a circuit are strongly coupled, recording different subsets at different times can be combined to infer correlations and dynamics across the entire population.

The authors model neural population dynamics using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. They use an expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm with Kalman smoothing to fit the model parameters from partial observations. The method is validated on both synthetic data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The main claim is that their "stitching" method can successfully predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, which is a significant advance over existing methods that cannot handle this scenario.

The paper properly situates itself within the literature, noting that while previous work has addressed inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon data or dealing with sub-sampled observations, none have specifically addressed the problem of stitching together non-simultaneously recorded populations. They contrast their approach with [12] which requires strong assumptions about monotonicity between noise and stimulus correlations, and with [13] which doesn't scale to large population sizes.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper effectively articulates a significant challenge in neuroscience (the gap between circuit size and imaging capabilities) and proposes a compelling solution. The whisker barrel example makes the problem concrete and relevant.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The authors build on established theoretical results about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21, 22, 23], providing a solid theoretical basis for their approach.

3. **Thorough validation**: The paper includes comprehensive validation on both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real experimental data. The comparison to both an upper bound ("fully observed" model) and lower bound ("naive" approach) is particularly valuable for establishing the method's performance.

4. **Appropriate technical approach**: The use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system with EM for parameter estimation is well-motivated for the problem and computationally tractable. The implementation details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility.

5. **Clear presentation of results**: The results are well-organized and presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 2-4) that effectively communicate the key findings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overly simplistic model**: The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't sufficiently address how this simplification might limit the method's applicability to more complex neural dynamics. The paper states "While this model does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade of neural couplings, calcium dynamics, fluorescence measurements and imaging noise [16, 6], we will show that this model nevertheless is able to predict correlations across non-simultaneously observed pairs of neurons." However, they don't provide a thorough analysis of when the linear approximation fails or how to determine if it's appropriate for a given dataset.

2. **Limited exploration of parameter requirements**: The paper doesn't thoroughly explore the sensitivity of the method to key parameters, such as the degree of overlap between imaging sessions (which the authors acknowledge is critical for stitching to work). The analysis in Section 3.2 mentions "some overlap was critical" but doesn't provide a systematic exploration of how much overlap is required and how performance degrades as overlap decreases.

3. **Overstated significance**: While the method is valuable, the paper overstates its significance in the abstract and discussion. The phrase "Our method can help us build data-driven models of large cortical circuits and help test theories of circuit function" is somewhat overstated given the current limitations of the linear model. The method is a useful tool but doesn't represent a paradigm shift in neural circuit analysis.

4. **Limited discussion of practical implementation**: The paper doesn't adequately address how practitioners would apply this method in real-world scenarios. For example, how many sessions are needed? How to determine the optimal overlap? What are the computational requirements for large populations?

5. **Insufficient comparison to alternative approaches**: While the paper compares to a naive approach, it doesn't compare to other potential methods that might address the same problem, such as more sophisticated neural network models or alternative dynamical system formulations.

## Qualitative Evaluation

### Quality
The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis (in the simulation) and experimental results (in the real data). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations and provide appropriate baselines for comparison. However, the reliance on a linear Gaussian model for neural dynamics is a significant limitation that isn't fully addressed. The paper would be stronger with more detailed analysis of the model's limitations and conditions under which it works well.

### Clarity
The paper is generally well-written and well-organized. The mathematical formulation is clear, and the figures effectively communicate the key results. The methods section is sufficiently detailed for replication. However, some technical details (e.g., the specific implementation of the EM algorithm with different Ck matrices) could be clarified for readers less familiar with Kalman filtering.

### Originality
The core idea of "stitching" together non-simultaneously recorded neural populations is novel and represents a meaningful contribution to the field. While the use of LDS for neural data isn't new, the specific application to this problem is original. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly [12] which requires strong assumptions about noise correlations. The method represents a novel combination of established techniques applied to a new problem.

### Significance
The problem addressed is important for neuroscience, as it allows researchers to extract more information from existing imaging data without requiring new hardware. The method has the potential to significantly impact how neural population data is analyzed. However, the significance is somewhat overstated in the paper. The method is valuable but limited to cases where the neural dynamics can be approximated by a linear model and where sufficient overlap exists between recording sessions. The paper would benefit from more realistic discussion of the method's practical utility.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel, well-motivated approach to a significant problem in neural data analysis. The method is technically sound and validated on both synthetic and real data. While the model simplifications are a limitation, they are acknowledged by the authors and the approach still yields valuable results.

I would recommend that the authors address the following points in their revision:

1. Provide a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the linear Gaussian model and when it might fail to capture neural dynamics.
2. Include a systematic analysis of how the method's performance depends on the degree of overlap between imaging sessions.
3. Moderate the claims about the method's significance to better reflect its practical limitations.
4. Add a brief discussion of practical implementation considerations for neuroscience researchers.

These revisions would strengthen the paper without requiring major changes to the core contribution.

## Pro and Con Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel approach to a significant problem in neuroscience
- Well-motivated and technically sound method
- Comprehensive validation on both synthetic and real data
- Clear comparison to appropriate baselines
- Addresses an important gap in the literature

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Overly simplistic model that may limit applicability
- Some overstated claims about the method's significance
- Limited exploration of key parameters (e.g., overlap between sessions)
- Insufficient discussion of practical implementation

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses, and it makes a valuable contribution to the field that would be of interest to the NIPS community.
