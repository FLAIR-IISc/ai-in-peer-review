## NIPS Review: Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a compelling and theoretically sound method for stitching together partial recordings of neural activity to infer the dynamics of larger populations. The approach, leveraging a latent dynamical system, is well-motivated by experimental constraints in neuroscience and demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real data. While the linear Gaussian assumption is a simplification, the results are surprisingly robust and demonstrate the potential for significant impact in the field. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the fundamental challenge of limited recording scope in neural circuit analysis. Current 2-photon imaging techniques, while powerful, are often unable to simultaneously record from the thousands of neurons comprising a cortical circuit. The authors propose a method to combine information from multiple, spatially offset recordings, effectively increasing the observable population size. They frame this as a latent dynamical system problem, where the full population activity is hidden and partially observed across multiple sessions. This is a clever and practical approach.

The authors do a good job positioning their work within the relevant literature. They correctly identify prior work on functional connectivity inference (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) as typically focusing on simultaneously recorded neurons.  They also accurately highlight the limitations of existing methods for handling sub-sampled data ([12, 13, 14, 15]) – specifically, the strong assumptions of monotonic relationships between stimulus and noise correlations [12], scalability issues [13], or applicability to different sampling regimes [14, 15]. The distinction between their approach and these prior works is clearly articulated.  It would be beneficial to briefly mention the broader literature on state-space models and Kalman filtering in signal processing, although this isn’t critical.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The formulation as a latent dynamical system provides a principled statistical framework for combining data. The use of Kalman smoothing and EM for parameter estimation is well-established and appropriate.
* **Robust Performance:**  The results on simulated data are convincing, demonstrating the ability to accurately recover both coupling parameters *and*, crucially, noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.  The performance approaching the "fully observed" upper bound on real data is particularly impressive.
* **Biological Relevance:** The motivation is deeply rooted in the limitations of current experimental techniques. The application to somatosensory cortex data is a strong demonstration of real-world applicability.  
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The explanation of the method and results is easy to follow. The figures are informative and well-designed.
* **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors provide a thoughtful comparison to both an ideal ("fully observed") and a naive baseline, clearly demonstrating the benefits of their approach. 

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** The simplification to a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant limitation. Real neural circuits are undoubtedly nonlinear, and calcium signals are not perfectly Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of this assumption on the accuracy of the inferred dynamics, particularly for more complex circuits, is not fully explored.  
* **Overlap Requirement:** The necessity for overlap between recording sessions is mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the method to the amount of overlap would be valuable. How does performance degrade as overlap decreases?
* **Scalability:** While the paper demonstrates performance on populations of ~100 neurons, the computational cost of the EM algorithm could become prohibitive for even larger circuits.  A discussion of scalability and potential algorithmic optimizations would be beneficial.
* **Parameter Identification:** The authors acknowledge potential near-degeneracy of parameters for large systems. Further exploration of parameter identifiability and potential regularization techniques could strengthen the results.



**4. Significance & Originality:**

This work is highly significant. It offers a practical solution to a major bottleneck in neural circuit analysis – the inability to simultaneously record from entire populations. The potential to infer dynamics and connectivity from partial recordings opens up exciting new avenues for studying circuit function and testing hypotheses about neural computation. The approach is original in its specific application to stitching together *multiple* partial recordings, and in its demonstration of the ability to infer noise correlations in this context. It’s a clever combination of established statistical techniques applied to a novel and important problem.

**5. Arguments For & Against Acceptance:**

**For:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and robust empirical results.
* Addresses a critical limitation in current neuroscience research.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Thorough evaluation and comparison to baselines.
* Potential for significant impact in the field.

**Against:**

* The linear Gaussian assumption is a simplification that could limit the accuracy of the inferred dynamics.
* The overlap requirement and potential scalability issues need further investigation.



**6. Questions for the Authors:**

* How sensitive is the method to the amount of overlap between recording sessions? Could you provide results demonstrating performance with varying levels of overlap?
* What is the computational complexity of the EM algorithm, and what are the practical limits on the size of the population that can be analyzed?
* Have you explored the use of more complex dynamical system models (e.g., nonlinear or non-Gaussian) within this framework?
* How might this method be adapted to incorporate prior knowledge about circuit connectivity or neural dynamics?




**Recommendation:**  **Accept** with minor revisions.  I recommend that the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more detailed discussion of the linear Gaussian assumption, the overlap requirement, and scalability considerations.  Addressing these points will further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
