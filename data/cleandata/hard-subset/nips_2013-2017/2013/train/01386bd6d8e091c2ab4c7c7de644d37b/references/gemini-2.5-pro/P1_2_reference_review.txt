**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel statistical method to "stitch" together multiple, partial recordings of a neural circuit to infer the dynamics of the entire population. The core problem is that experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record a subset of neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose modeling the entire neural population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording is then treated as a partial, noisy observation of this underlying latent state. By fitting a single LDS model across multiple non-simultaneous recording sessions (which may have some overlapping neurons), they aim to recover the full dynamics matrix and, consequently, predict statistics like noise correlations even between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

The method is validated on two fronts. First, on a simulated dataset with a known ground-truth connectivity matrix, the authors show that their "stitching" method accurately recovers both the coupling parameters and the noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs. Second, they apply the method to real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. Here, they create a "fictional imaging scenario" by taking a single, large simultaneous recording and splitting it into two artificial, overlapping "sessions." This clever approach allows them to benchmark their method against an upper bound (a model fit to the full, simultaneous data) and a lower bound (a "naive" approach that models each session independently). Their results demonstrate that the stitching method's predictions of pairwise correlations are far superior to the naive approach and closely approach the performance of the fully-observed model.

This work relates to a large body of literature on inferring functional connectivity from neural data. However, it distinguishes itself by specifically addressing the challenge of non-simultaneous recordings, a problem largely unaddressed by previous methods, which typically assume all neurons of interest are recorded in one session.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly practical limitation in experimental neuroscience. The ability to computationally combine partial recordings to build a model of a larger circuit could dramatically expand the effective scale of current recording technologies, enabling new kinds of systems-level analyses that are currently out of reach. This work has the potential to be highly influential for both computational and experimental neuroscientists.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The core idea of using a shared latent dynamical system to bridge non-simultaneous recordings is both intuitive and powerful. The paper provides a clear intuition for why this should work: the dynamics of one observed population contain statistical traces of their unobserved partners, and a subsequent recording of those partners can resolve the remaining ambiguities. This is a novel application of established statistical methods to a new and important problem.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Validation:** The experimental evaluation is a major strength. The use of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is an excellent methodological choice. It allows for a rigorous quantitative assessment with clear upper and lower performance bounds, making the results compelling and easy to interpret. The demonstration that the method works on both anesthetized and awake behaving animal data further strengthens its claims of general applicability.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated, the method is described with sufficient detail, and the results are presented logically and persuasively. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Limitations of the Linear Gaussian Model:** The LDS model is a significant simplification of real neural dynamics, which are nonlinear, non-Gaussian (spiking), and further complicated by the dynamics of the calcium indicator. The authors acknowledge this but could provide a more in-depth discussion of the potential limitations. For instance, under what conditions might this linear approximation fail catastrophically? Is the success here partly due to the slow, low-pass filtering nature of calcium imaging, which might make the dynamics appear more linear and Gaussian than the underlying spikes?

2.  **Importance of Overlapping Neurons:** The paper notes that with non-overlapping subsets (`c=50%`), the method "failed to get good performance" on real data, requiring an overlap (`c=60%`) to work. This is a critical practical detail. The performance of the algorithm likely depends heavily on the size and statistical properties of this "anchor" population of overlapping neurons. A more systematic analysis of how performance degrades as the percentage of overlap decreases would significantly enhance the paper's practical value for experimental design. A brief discussion of the theoretical necessity of overlap for model identifiability would also be welcome.

3.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The method implicitly assumes that the underlying circuit dynamics (the `A` matrix) are stationary across the different recording sessions. In the "fictional" scenario, this is true by construction. However, real experiments might involve sessions separated by hours or days, during which the circuit could undergo changes due to learning, adaptation, or shifts in the animal's behavioral state. The authors should discuss this assumption and its potential as a failure point in real-world applications.

4.  **Scalability:** The paper critiques a previous method for not scaling to large populations. However, it does not discuss the computational complexity of its own approach. Standard Kalman smoothing for an LDS with `N` latent dimensions scales as O(N³). While feasible for the N=100 used here, this could become prohibitive for the "thousands of neurons" mentioned in the introduction. A short discussion on computational cost and potential avenues for scaling (e.g., structured `A` matrices, approximation methods) would be beneficial.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, unsolved problem at the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience.
*   The proposed method is novel, elegant, and well-motivated.
*   The experimental validation is rigorous and convincing, using a clever benchmarking strategy on real neural data.
*   The paper is written with outstanding clarity.
*   The potential impact on the field of systems neuroscience is very high.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The linear Gaussian model is a strong simplification, and its limitations are not fully explored.
*   The crucial dependence on overlapping neuron populations is mentioned but not systematically investigated, leaving open practical questions for experimentalists.
*   Potential issues of non-stationarity in real experimental protocols and computational scalability are not addressed.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a creative and impactful solution to an important problem. The strengths—in particular, the significance of the problem, the novelty of the approach, and the quality of the validation—far outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses are primarily areas for future work and further discussion rather than fatal flaws. The paper is technically sound, clearly presented, and its ideas are likely to be built upon by many other researchers. I strongly recommend acceptance.
