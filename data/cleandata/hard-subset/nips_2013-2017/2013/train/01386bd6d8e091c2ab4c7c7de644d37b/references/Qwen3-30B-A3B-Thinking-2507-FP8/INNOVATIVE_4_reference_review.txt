

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper presents a novel approach to address a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a large neural circuit. The authors propose a "stitching" method that leverages multiple partial recordings (from different imaging sessions) of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics and correlations across the entire population, including between neurons never recorded simultaneously. The core insight is that by modeling the neural population as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, they can recover correlations and dynamics that would otherwise be inaccessible.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it introduces a computational framework that directly addresses the problem of combining non-simultaneous recordings, a gap in the literature where existing methods either require simultaneous recording or make strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations [12]). Second, the authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness on both simulated neural populations and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their stitching approach can accurately predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. Third, they establish clear performance bounds using a "fully observed" model (impossible in practice but feasible in their fictional imaging scenario) and a naive baseline, demonstrating that their method approaches the theoretical optimum.

The paper's greatest strength lies in its bold conceptual contribution: the idea that we can "stitch" together partial observations of a neural circuit to infer the full dynamics. This is not just an incremental improvement to existing methods but a fundamentally new way of thinking about how to extract information from current experimental limitations. The authors successfully demonstrate that their simple linear Gaussian dynamical system model can make accurate predictions about correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously, which is a remarkable finding given the complexity of neural dynamics. Their results on real data (Figure 3-4) are particularly compelling, showing that the stitching method outperforms the naive approach by a factor of 2 in predicting correlations, and approaches the performance of the "fully observed" model.

While the use of a linear Gaussian model is a simplification of the actual neural dynamics (which involve nonlinear calcium transients, spiking activity, etc.), the authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation and note that more complex models could further improve performance. Their demonstration that even this simple model can make accurate predictions is itself a significant finding, suggesting that the core idea of "stitching" is robust to model complexity.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and thoroughly evaluated. The method is clearly described, the experiments are well-designed, and the comparisons to baselines are appropriate. The authors effectively address the key question: can we characterize joint dynamics from multiple partial observations? Their answer is a resounding yes.

The main limitation is that the method requires some overlap between imaging sessions (the paper shows that 60% overlap works well, while 50% with no overlap fails). This is a practical constraint that would need to be considered in experimental design, but it's a reasonable trade-off given the significant gains in information. The paper also doesn't explore the method's performance on more complex neural dynamics or in other brain regions, but these are natural extensions rather than fundamental flaws.

In terms of significance, this work has the potential to transform how we analyze neural population data. Current imaging technology limits us to recording from small subsets of neurons, but this method allows us to extract information about the full circuit from these partial observations. This could enable more comprehensive models of neural circuits and help test theories of neural computation that have been out of reach due to experimental limitations.

Compared to previous NIPS work, this paper stands out for its bold approach to a real-world neuroscience problem. While many papers focus on improving existing methods for simultaneous recordings, this work addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. It's a clear step toward the ambitious goal of mapping population dynamics of entire neural circuits.

In summary, this paper presents a novel, conceptually bold approach to a significant problem in neuroscience. The method is well-grounded, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches. While the linear model is a simplification, the authors appropriately acknowledge this and show that their core insight holds even with this simple framework. The ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a significant contribution with clear implications for how we analyze neural population data.

I strongly recommend acceptance. This paper introduces a new direction in neural data analysis that could have broad impact, and the authors provide convincing evidence for their method's effectiveness. The novelty of the approach, combined with its practical significance for neuroscience, makes it a compelling contribution to NIPS.
