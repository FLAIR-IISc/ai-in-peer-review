Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer the joint dynamics and noise correlations of large neural populations when only partial subsets can be recorded at a time. The authors propose “stitching” multiple partial recordings by fitting a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where each session uses a known selection matrix that picks out the recorded neurons. Parameters are learned via EM (with an online-EM variant), and the method is evaluated on simulated data and on two in vivo two-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse S1.

Conceptually, this is a natural application of system identification with missing data and of multi-session state-space modeling. It relates to prior work on functional connectivity and latent dynamical modeling from calcium and spikes (e.g., Mishchenko et al. 2011; Pillow et al. 2008; Macke et al. 2012; latent GLMs), subspace/system ID (Katayama), and partial observation/readout work (Wohrer et al. 2010). The novelty is not a new estimator per se but a careful framing and demonstration that a simple LDS can combine non-simultaneous recordings to recover cross-population statistics—especially noise correlations—which prior approaches either did not attempt or required strong assumptions (e.g., monotone relation between stimulus and noise correlations).

Strengths
- Clear problem statement and compelling motivation. Stitching partial recordings is directly relevant given the limits of current imaging.
- Methodologically simple, scalable, and easy to reproduce (Kalman smoothing + EM with known selection matrices).
- Good experimental design for benchmarking: simulated ground truth; “fully observed” upper bound vs “naive” lower bound; correlation and prediction metrics, not just parameter error.
- Results are encouraging: on simulations, high accuracy for noise correlations and reasonable cross-couplings; on real data, predicted correlations for non-simultaneous pairs approach the fully observed model and outperform the naive baseline; activity prediction also improves substantially.
- The paper is clearly written and places work in context; limitations are acknowledged (e.g., role of overlap, linear-Gaussian simplification).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Validation on real data uses a “fictional split” of simultaneously recorded data rather than truly independent sessions/fields of view. This setup sidesteps practical challenges (drift, day-to-day nonstationarity, stimulus/behavior differences, cell registration) and may overestimate performance.
- Dependence on overlap is critical in practice, but is not analyzed systematically. The method fails on real data without overlap; this contradicts the stronger claim in the intro use case (moving to nearby locations with no shared cells). A quantitative study of performance vs fraction of overlap and conditions under which zero-overlap can work is needed.
- Modeling assumptions are strong: LDS on calcium traces, diagonal innovation noise, and Gaussian measurement noise. Real data likely include low-dimensional correlated inputs and nonlinear calcium dynamics. The current model attributes all shared variability to A, which can bias inferred couplings and correlations.
- Identifiability and observability are only argued heuristically. Clear conditions (e.g., what patterns of Ck, stimulus richness, overlap, and data length suffice) would strengthen the contribution and explain failure modes as N grows.
- Parameter recovery of couplings is modest on real data (correlations drop with population size) and not well analyzed. Regularization (sparsity/low-rank) or low-dimensional latent inputs are mentioned but not included in core results.
- Some notational/typo issues (e.g., measurement equation suggests noise is multiplied by C; clarify that noise is additive after selection). Runtime and computational scaling are not reported.

Quality
- The method is technically sound for LDS with missing data and the experiments are competently executed. Claims about predicting noise correlations from partial recordings are reasonably supported within the constraints of the fictional split. However, broader claims (stitching disjoint fields without overlap; robustness to nonstationarity) are not demonstrated. The authors are generally honest about limitations.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and readable. Provide a corrected measurement equation and be explicit about pre-processing (filtering), sampling rate vs model time step, and stationarity assumptions. Include more details on hyperparameters, convergence criteria, and computation time.

Originality
- The main novelty is in framing and empirical demonstration rather than algorithmic innovation. Using an LDS with known selection matrices and EM is standard; applying it to stitch non-simultaneous calcium recordings and focusing on noise correlations is a useful contribution, but incremental.

Significance
- If robustly validated across real multi-session recordings, the idea is valuable for neuroscience practice. As presented, significance is moderate: the method is simple and seems to work on split datasets, but practical readiness is limited by the need for overlap and by modeling mismatches.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide experiments on truly separate recordings (different FOVs, small overlap, or across days) to validate stitching under realistic variability.
- Systematically study performance vs overlap fraction, data length, and stimulus richness; probe zero-overlap conditions on simulations with realistic calcium noise and model mismatch.
- Incorporate more realistic observation models (spike-to-calcium and measurement noise) or, at minimum, latent low-dimensional inputs (correlated innovations) and regularization (sparse A, low-rank plus sparse) to stabilize parameter estimates as N increases.
- Add identifiability/observability analysis tailored to this selection-matrix setting (what Ck patterns guarantee recovery?).
- Report runtime/memory and release code to aid adoption.

Pros
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem.
- Simple, scalable approach with strong results on simulations and promising real-data benchmarks.
- Clear writing and reasonable evaluation framework with upper/lower bounds.

Cons
- Real-data validation is constrained to a fictional split; lack of true multi-session demonstrations.
- Strong dependence on overlap without a principled analysis.
- Simplifying assumptions (diagonal Q, linear-Gaussian calcium dynamics) may bias conclusions.
- Limited theoretical grounding of identifiability in this setting; parameter recovery degrades with N.

Overall
The paper is clear, technically solid within LDS, and shows promising empirical results for predicting cross-session correlations. Novelty is incremental and the validation is not yet fully convincing for the intended use case (stitching distinct fields/sessions). With additional real multi-session experiments, analysis of overlap/identifiability, and modest modeling extensions or regularization, this could be a strong contribution. In its current form, it meets clarity and basic quality criteria well, has moderate originality, and moderate practical significance.
