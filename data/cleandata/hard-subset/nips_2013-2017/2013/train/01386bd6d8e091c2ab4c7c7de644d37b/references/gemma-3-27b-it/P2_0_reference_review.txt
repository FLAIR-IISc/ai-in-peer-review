## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for “stitching” together data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of a neural population to infer the dynamics of the entire circuit. The core idea is to model the population activity as a latent dynamical system and leverage the fact that nearby neurons are strongly coupled. By sequentially recording subsets of the population, the authors demonstrate—both on simulated and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex—that they can accurately estimate correlations between neurons, even those never recorded simultaneously.  The method effectively bridges the gap between the limited field of view of current imaging techniques and the need to study large-scale neural circuits.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The introduction appropriately situates the work within the broader field of neural connectivity inference from imaging and electrophysiology.  The authors acknowledge related work on functional connectivity mapping (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]) and sub-sampled observations ([12, 13, 14, 15]), clearly differentiating their approach which focuses specifically on inferring correlations *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons.  The comparison to [12] is particularly relevant and well-addressed, as the proposed method doesn't rely on the restrictive monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem addressed – inferring dynamics *across* partially observed recordings – is a crucial one for advancing large-scale neural circuit analysis, and the proposed approach provides a practical solution. While the underlying dynamical systems framework is not new, its application to this specific problem and the demonstrated ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons *is* novel.
* **Soundness:**  The mathematical framework (latent dynamical system with Gaussian assumptions) is well-defined and the use of the Expectation-Maximization algorithm is appropriate. The justifications for model choices (e.g., Gaussian noise, linear dynamics for tractability) are clearly stated.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The paper presents strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The simulations are well-designed and the application to real calcium imaging data from mouse cortex provides a compelling demonstration.  The careful comparison to “fully observed” and “naive” approaches is particularly valuable, effectively establishing upper and lower bounds on performance.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The methods section is detailed enough for an expert reader to understand and potentially reproduce the work.  The presentation of results is clear and the figures are informative.
* **Significance:** This work has the potential to significantly impact the field by allowing researchers to leverage existing data more effectively and to construct more comprehensive models of neural circuits. The ability to infer correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a key step towards understanding the large-scale dynamics of brain activity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplifications:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification of real neural dynamics. While acknowledged by the authors, the potential impact of these simplifications on the accuracy of the inferred correlations isn't fully explored. More realistic models (e.g., incorporating non-linearities or non-Gaussian noise) might yield more accurate results, though at the cost of computational complexity.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The results suggest that some overlap between recording sessions is crucial for performance. The paper doesn't extensively explore the sensitivity of the method to the degree of overlap, or provide guidance on how to optimally design imaging experiments to maximize performance.
* **Scalability:** While the authors mention scaling limitations of related work ([13]), the scalability of their EM-based approach to *very* large populations (thousands of neurons) isn’t explicitly addressed.  A discussion of computational costs and potential optimizations would be valuable.
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't detail the specifics of parameter tuning (e.g., regularization parameters) used in the EM algorithm. Providing more information about these choices would enhance reproducibility.



**Arguments For/Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem in neural data analysis.
* Presents a novel and theoretically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on both simulated and real data.
* Is well-written and clearly explains the methodology.

**Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on simplifying assumptions that may limit the accuracy of the inferred dynamics.
* The impact of overlap between recordings isn't fully explored.
* Scalability to very large populations isn’t fully addressed.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field. The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, and supported by convincing evidence. While the model simplifications and potential scalability limitations are weaknesses, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not fundamentally undermine the significance of the work.  I believe this paper meets the NIPS acceptance criteria and recommend acceptance.

**Recommendation:** **Accept**
