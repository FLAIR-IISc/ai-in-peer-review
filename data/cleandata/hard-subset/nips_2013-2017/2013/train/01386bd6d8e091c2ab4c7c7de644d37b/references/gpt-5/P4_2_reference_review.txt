Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses the problem of inferring joint population dynamics from multiple, partially overlapping recordings of the same neural circuit. The core contribution is a “stitching” approach: fit a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with shared parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) across sessions, while each session has its own observation matrix Ck that selects the subset of recorded neurons. The model incorporates stimulus inputs u via B, assumes diagonal process and observation noise covariances, and is fit by EM (with an online-EM variant for efficiency). With this unified LDS, the authors aim to recover cross-couplings and, more practically, predict noise correlations between neuron pairs that were never recorded simultaneously. They evaluate on (i) simulated data with known ground truth, and (ii) two 2P calcium imaging datasets (anesthetized and awake mouse S1); for real data, they create “fictional” subsampling scenarios and compare the stitched LDS to a “fully observed” upper bound (fit to all neurons jointly) and to a naïve baseline that trains separate models per subset and merges parameters.

The work is related to: connectivity inference from calcium data (e.g., Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012), latent/partially observed GLMs (Pillow & Latham 2008), and handling sub-sampled observations (e.g., Wohrer et al. 2010) though with different assumptions. Methodologically, it uses standard LDS/EM machinery (Dempster et al. 1977; Kalman smoothing; subspace/system identification [Katayama]) but applies it to the multi-session partial-observation setting with known cell identities. The novelty lies in demonstrating that this suffices to infer noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs and in quantifying performance relative to upper/lower bounds.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: a single LDS with session-specific selection matrices Ck naturally unifies partially overlapping recordings; the E/M steps are standard and reproducible.
- Empirical validation: synthetic results show accurate recovery of off-diagonal couplings and noise correlations; real-data analyses show strong agreement of stitched-model correlations with the fully observed upper bound and substantial improvement over the naïve baseline.
- Practical utility: the approach predicts unobserved neural activity and noise correlations, addressing a key limitation of 2P imaging when full populations cannot be imaged simultaneously.
- Sensitivity analyses: performance vs population size is explored; the importance of overlap is acknowledged and qualitatively examined.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Model mismatch and identifiability: The LDS operates directly on fluorescence without explicit calcium/spiking dynamics; this can blur stimulus/noise separation and bias “noise correlation” estimates. The paper notes this but does not quantify sensitivity to mismatch. Identifiability of A in large, weakly constrained LDSs is delicate; overlap appears empirically necessary on real data, but the required amount of overlap is not quantified.
- Stationarity and session effects: All parameters are shared across sessions, yet real experiments often exhibit session-specific gains/baselines or slow non-stationarities. The model includes a single per-neuron offset d and diagonal R, but no per-session offsets/gains or session-specific B. This could confound stitching; an ablation evaluating per-session offsets/gains would strengthen the claim.
- Regularization and stability: Fitting a dense N×N A with limited data risks overfitting. No explicit sparsity or stability constraints on A are used during EM (the paper mentions sparsity only in discussion). It is unclear how stability (spectral radius of A) is enforced; details would help assess robustness.
- Baselines: The naïve baseline is weak. Comparisons to alternative latent variable models that handle partial observations (e.g., PLDS/GPFA-like models; low-rank common-latent LDS with neuron-specific loadings) would better contextualize gains. A baseline with shared low-rank latent dynamics plus per-neuron loadings could be competitive for correlation prediction.
- Evaluation design: Real-data validation uses a split of contiguous time within the same recording session. This avoids between-session non-stationarities and may overestimate real-world performance. A test on truly separate sessions (e.g., different days or fields of view) with matched identities would be more convincing.
- Reporting details: Equation (2) appears to have a typographical error; presumably ykt = Ck(xkt + d) + εt, εt ~ N(0,R). Details on stimulus encoding (especially for awake data), hyperparameters, runtime/memory scaling, and uncertainty estimates (CIs for correlation metrics) are limited.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The approach is a correct application of LDS/EM with missing data and known Ck. The empirical results, especially correlation prediction, are consistent with the model’s strengths. However, the lack of regularization/stability control, session-specific effects, and stronger baselines leaves open questions about robustness and generality.
- Empirical support: Synthetic experiments are well controlled; real-data results are encouraging but constrained by the “fictional” split within the same session.

Clarity
- Generally well organized and readable. Suggested improvements: fix Eq. (2); specify how stability of A is ensured; detail stimulus representations and the computation of “noise correlations” after subtracting Bukt; report key hyperparameters and initialization; clarify whether R and d are per-neuron and whether any per-session parameters were used.

Originality
- Methodologically incremental (standard LDS with partial observations), but the framing and demonstration of stitching to infer non-simultaneous noise correlations in practical 2P imaging scenarios is novel and valuable.

Significance
- Moderate. The ability to predict correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons is practically important for population imaging. The work could inform experimental design (overlap vs coverage) and motivate richer models.

Arguments for acceptance
- Simple, principled method with clear practical relevance.
- Strong improvements over a conventional baseline; near-upper-bound performance on correlation prediction in real data.
- Careful synthetic experiments and multiple population sizes; actionable insights about overlap.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited novelty in modeling; relies on standard LDS/EM without strong new technical contributions.
- Evaluation on real data uses intra-session splits; generalization to truly separate sessions not demonstrated.
- Missing regularization/stability controls and stronger baselines; potential overfitting and identifiability concerns not fully addressed.
- Session-specific variability and stimulus-model mismatch not thoroughly treated.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add per-session offsets/gains (and optionally per-session R) and evaluate robustness to session-specific changes.
- Include stability constraints or regularization on A (e.g., L1/L2 or low-rank-plus-sparse) and report effects; provide runtime scaling.
- Compare to low-rank latent variable baselines (e.g., PLDS/GPFA-like shared latent dynamics with neuron-specific loadings) under partial observations.
- Quantify required overlap vs. performance, and test on truly separate sessions.
- Provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., bootstrap CIs) for correlation prediction metrics and clearer stimulus modeling details.

Overall, the paper presents a useful application of LDSs to a timely problem with promising empirical results, though stronger baselines, controls, and session realism would solidify the contribution.
