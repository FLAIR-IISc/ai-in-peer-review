Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses an important practical constraint in systems neuroscience: large circuits cannot be imaged simultaneously, but nearby fields-of-view are functionally coupled. The authors propose “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit by fitting a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with session-specific observation matrices Ck and shared parameters (A, B, Q, R). Parameters are learned via EM (with an online variant), and the model is used to predict cross-session noise correlations and to impute unobserved neural activity. They evaluate on simulations and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets by creating “fictional” splits of simultaneously recorded populations into overlapping subsets.

This builds on a large literature on latent dynamical models for population activity (e.g., LDS/PLDS/GPFA) and Kalman smoothing with missing observations, as well as subspace/system identification. Relative to prior work on inferring connectivity from imaging or spikes, the novelty is not in the model class per se, but in the framing and empirical demonstration that shared dynamical parameters can stitch together non-simultaneous partial observations to predict cross-session correlations.

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Stitching partial imaging fields is a real bottleneck; a tool to leverage multiple partial recordings would be impactful in practice.
- Simplicity and scalability: A standard LDS with missing data is computationally tractable, easy to implement, and amenable to mini-batch EM. The approach naturally accommodates multiple sessions via distinct Ck.
- Empirical performance: On simulations, the stitched model recovers cross-block couplings and noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs, and imputes unobserved activity well. On real data, stitched models predict cross-session correlations with high agreement to a “fully observed” upper bound and outperform a naive baseline.
- Clear framing about noise correlations vs stimulus correlations and careful benchmarking with upper and lower bounds.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Identifiability and conditions: The core claim hinges on identifiability of A, B, Q from multiple partial sequences. The paper gives an intuition but no formal conditions. In particular:
  - Without simultaneous overlap, it is not obvious that off-diagonal blocks of A are identifiable from marginal auto-covariances of disjoint blocks across different sessions, absent strong assumptions (e.g., diagonal Q, known B, sufficient excitation). The simulation succeeds without overlap but real data “failed to get good performance” without overlap. This discrepancy needs explanation and a clearer statement of when stitching is guaranteed (or likely) to work.
  - The LDS linear transformation ambiguity argument is more standard for latent factor models; here the state is the full neuronal vector. Clarify what exactly is identifiable as a function of overlap c, controllability/observability, and excitation by u.
- Evaluation limitations on real data:
  - The main demonstration uses fictional splits from a single simultaneous recording. This overestimates the practical utility in real scenarios where fields are recorded at different times/locations, with drift and nonstationarities (e.g., changes in A, B, Q, R). A stronger validation would stitch truly non-simultaneous fields acquired sequentially by moving the microscope, ideally with a known shared subset.
  - The “upper bound” is a model fit to the same data; better would be direct comparison to held-out empirical noise correlations computed from the untouched simultaneous test segment (the authors mention a withheld 20% test set; specify whether reported correlation metrics are to fully observed model predictions or to empirical test correlations).
- Modeling assumptions:
  - Linear-Gaussian dynamics on raw calcium signals at ~3 Hz are a coarse approximation; the paper notes this but does not quantify the impact. No exploration of PLDS/GPFA or simple low-rank LDS with additional latent dimensions for common input (suggested but not demonstrated).
  - Q and R are constrained to be diagonal; this pushes correlated noise into A. An ablation allowing low-rank Q (or extra latent dimensions) would test robustness to model mismatch.
  - Stationarity across sessions is assumed; awake behavior violates this (movement, arousal). No checks for parameter drift or session-specific biases.
- Methodological details:
  - Initialization and regularization are crucial for high-dimensional LDS EM; details are sparse. Authors note sparsity penalties improve estimates, but do not report them.
  - Sample complexity and computational cost are not discussed; no runtime or convergence diagnostics.
- Related work: The paper should better situate itself within LDS/PLDS/GPFA and subspace identification with missing data, as well as multi-view factor analysis and system ID literature. Some important references (e.g., GPFA) are missing; several references are incomplete.

Questions for the authors
- What are the precise identifiability conditions when there is zero overlap? Can you provide a theorem or counterexample, or at least an empirical phase diagram over overlap c, T, SNR, and stimulus richness?
- How were B and u specified in the real datasets, particularly for the awake task? How sensitive are results to misspecification of u?
- Do the reported correlation metrics compare to empirical correlations on held-out simultaneous data, or only to the fully observed model’s predictions?
- How were EM initialized and regularized? Any constraints on spectral radius of A? Any stability checks?
- How does performance change with low-rank Q or with additional latent dimensions capturing common input?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a theoretical or empirical analysis of identifiability and performance as a function of overlap, stimulus richness, SNR, and T; include failure cases.
- Demonstrate stitching across two truly non-simultaneous fields from the same animal with known overlap.
- Compare against richer latent models (e.g., LDS with extra latent factors; PLDS/GPFA) and against subspace ID baselines; include an ablation with low-rank Q.
- Report direct accuracy on empirical held-out noise correlations and activity imputation, with confidence intervals across multiple random splits; provide runtime and convergence statistics.
- Clarify preprocessing, u/B construction, and add missing references; improve reference formatting.

Overall evaluation
- Quality: Solid empirical demonstration, but theoretical conditions and real-world validation are incomplete.
- Clarity: Generally clear, but some methodological details and evaluation choices need sharpening.
- Originality: The stitching application is novel and useful; the modeling machinery is standard.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for population imaging, contingent on robustness in truly non-simultaneous settings.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and important experimental limitation with a simple, scalable tool.
- Strong simulated and promising real-data results on predicting cross-session correlations.
- Clear path to practical utility and extensibility.

Cons for acceptance
- Lack of formal identifiability analysis; dependence on overlap not characterized.
- Main real-data validation is on fictional splits; no demonstration on truly separate recordings.
- Limited exploration of model misspecification and alternative latent models.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on clarifying identifiability, strengthening real-data validation, and expanding comparisons.
