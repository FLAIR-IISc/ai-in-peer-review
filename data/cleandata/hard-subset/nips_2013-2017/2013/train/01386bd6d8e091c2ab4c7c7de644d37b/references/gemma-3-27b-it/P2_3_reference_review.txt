## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a statistical method for “stitching” together multiple, partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics and correlations of a larger population of neurons than can be observed simultaneously with current imaging technology. The authors frame this as a latent dynamical system problem and demonstrate, both on simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, that they can accurately recover cross-couplings and, crucially, noise correlations between neurons that were *not* recorded simultaneously. The method outperforms a naive approach that treats each recording session independently.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The introduction does a good job of situating the work within the broader field of neural connectivity inference, citing relevant papers on calcium imaging analysis, information-theoretic approaches, and methods for handling subsampled data. The authors correctly point out that prior work hasn’t focused on inferring connections *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons.  The paper acknowledges related work using latent GLMs and anatomical reconstruction, and clearly differentiates its approach based on the longer timescales of neural dynamics relative to those settings.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a practically important problem:** The limitations of current imaging techniques are a significant bottleneck in neuroscience. This work directly tackles that bottleneck by providing a method to leverage existing data more effectively.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of a latent dynamical system framework is well-motivated and appropriate for the problem.  The explanation of the EM algorithm and its adaptation to this scenario is clear.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide compelling evidence from both simulated and real data. The simulations are well-designed to demonstrate the method's ability to recover ground truth parameters. The results on real data show that the method performs significantly better than a naive baseline and approaches the performance of a fully observed model (an appropriate upper bound).
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, making the core ideas accessible. The figures are helpful and well-labelled.
* **Novelty:** While the underlying techniques (LDS, EM) are established, the application to this specific problem of stitching together partial recordings, and the demonstration of its success in inferring noise correlations, is novel and significant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** The method relies on a simplifying assumption of a linear Gaussian dynamical system. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, its potential impact on the applicability of the method to more complex neural systems isn't fully explored.  A discussion of how the method might be extended to incorporate non-linearities or non-Gaussianity would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices (e.g., initialization of the EM algorithm, regularization parameters). A more detailed analysis of robustness would strengthen the claims.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for success, and that performance improves with more overlap, is important, but could be discussed further.  How does the *amount* of overlap influence performance? Is there a minimum required overlap? What are the practical implications for experimental design?
* **Limited Discussion of Scalability:** The authors mention scaling limitations in the introduction when referencing other works. While they demonstrate the method on populations of ~60-200 neurons, a more in-depth discussion of the computational complexity and scalability to even larger populations would be beneficial.



**Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant practical challenge in neuroscience.
* Technically sound methodology with strong empirical validation.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Demonstrates a novel and valuable approach to inferring neural dynamics.

**Con:**

* Relies on simplifying assumptions that may limit its applicability.
* Limited exploration of parameter sensitivity and scalability.
* The dependence on overlap between recording sessions is acknowledged but not thoroughly investigated.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a technically sound and well-validated approach. The limitations are acknowledged, but addressing them more thoroughly would further strengthen the work. The originality and significance of the contribution are high. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept** -  I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. It meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. While there are areas for improvement, the work is a valuable contribution to the field and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience.  I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above in future work.
