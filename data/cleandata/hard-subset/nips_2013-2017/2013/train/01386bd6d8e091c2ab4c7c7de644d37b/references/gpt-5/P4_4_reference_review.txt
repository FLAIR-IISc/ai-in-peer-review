Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a method for “stitching” together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics, with a focus on predicting noise correlations between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The approach instantiates a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) at the level of fluorescence signals: x_t evolves linearly with additive Gaussian innovations, and observations y_t are noisy, partial (via known selection matrices C^k for each session), and include a constant offset. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned with EM (with an online-EM variant), using the Kalman smoother in the E-step. The model leverages that, across sessions, the identity of recorded neurons is known, so parameters are shared across sessions and the missing activity is treated as latent. The authors evaluate on synthetic data and on two 2-photon datasets from mouse S1 (awake and anesthetized). They create a “fictional” split of an actually simultaneous recording into two partially overlapping sessions and compare: (i) a stitched LDS trained jointly on the two partial sessions, (ii) a “naive” baseline that fits separate models to each session and combines parameters, and (iii) a “fully observed” model fit to the true simultaneous data (upper bound). The stitched model recovers off-block couplings on simulations, predicts noise correlations for nonsimultaneously observed pairs on both simulated and real data (approaching the fully observed upper bound), and predicts unobserved activity substantially better than the naive baseline.

The work builds on a large literature on latent state-space models and neural system identification, including EM for LDS [Dempster et al. 1977], subspace identification [Katayama], and neural latent-variable models (e.g., PLDS/FA/GPFA and related work; cf. Macke et al. NIPS 2012; Pillow & Latham NIPS 2008). It is distinct from prior calcium-connectivity inference focused on single-session data [Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012], from information-theoretic or GLM-based approaches for simultaneously recorded spiking [Pillow et al. 2008], and from methods relying on monotonic stimulus–noise correlation relationships [Wohrer et al. 2010]. Its main contribution is to formulate and empirically validate a multi-session LDS with missing observations to infer cross-session correlations, an area not directly addressed by the cited connectivity-from-subsampling works [Pakman et al. 2013; Mishchenko & Paninski 2012].

Quality (technical soundness)
- Modeling: The LDS with shared parameters across sessions and known selection matrices is standard and appropriate as a tractable baseline for stitching partial observations. The EM implementation appears correct, and the paper provides explicit update formulas for d and R and notes stationarity constraints for x0. The assumption of diagonal Q and R simplifies inference but imposes that shared variability beyond stimulus is explained via A; this may bias A if there is fast shared input or measurement correlations. The authors note that correlated noise can be incorporated via additional latent dimensions, but this is not evaluated.
- Identifiability and overlap: The intuitive argument for identifiability across disjoint sessions is plausible when neuron identities are consistent across sessions and dynamics are sufficiently excited. However, the empirical results indicate that, on real data, some overlap is important (c = 60% worked; c = 50% with no overlap did not). Conditions under which zero-overlap stitching succeeds (given known neuron identities) are not characterized theoretically, and the method’s sensitivity to overlap is only qualitatively discussed.
- Evaluation: On simulations, ground truth supports parameter and correlation recovery, with appropriate held-out prediction of unobserved activity. On real data, using a held-out test set to assess predicted noise correlations between nonsimultaneous pairs is compelling and goes beyond model-to-model comparisons. The naive baseline is a reasonable lower bound. Additional baselines (e.g., low-rank latent models with shared subspace across sessions, PLDS/GPFA with alignment) would further contextualize gains. Runtime/scaling characteristics are not reported.
- Practical considerations: The continuous fluorescence LDS abstracts away spike–calcium–fluorescence nonlinearities and temporal filtering, neuropil contamination, and motion/registration issues. While acceptable as a first step, these choices should be discussed as potential sources of bias; the paper mentions this and suggests more realistic models.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The core model and training procedure are described concisely. Minor issues:
- The observation model equation appears to have a typographical error (placement of noise term and parentheses around C^k(x_t + d) + ε_t).
- More detail on initialization, online-EM minibatch scheme, and any regularization (e.g., stability constraints on A, sparsity penalties) would aid reproducibility.
- The fictional split protocol and the exact computation of “noise correlations” (stimulus regression procedure) should be specified precisely.

Originality
The mathematical machinery (LDS with EM and missing data) is standard. The novelty lies in the problem framing—stitching multiple partial recordings to infer cross-session correlations and couplings—and in demonstrating that this can work in practice on real calcium imaging data. This is a meaningful but incremental methodological contribution.

Significance
Accurately predicting noise correlations and cross-couplings between nonsimultaneously recorded neurons is valuable for scaling population analyses beyond imaging field-of-view limits. The demonstrated performance on real data suggests practical utility. Current scope is modest (tens to ~100 neurons, two sessions), with dependence on overlap and simplified noise structure; nonetheless, the approach could spur broader adoption and extensions (e.g., correlated innovations, spike-aware observation models, multi-session designs).

Pros (arguments for acceptance)
- Clear, principled formulation of multi-session stitching as an LDS with missing observations and shared parameters.
- Strong simulation results with ground truth; convincing real-data evaluation using held-out simultaneous data to assess predictions for nonsimultaneous pairs.
- Practical relevance for current 2-photon imaging constraints.
- Transparent discussion of limitations and possible extensions.

Cons (arguments against acceptance)
- Core method is a straightforward application of standard LDS/EM; methodological novelty is limited.
- Reliance on linear-Gaussian assumptions and diagonal Q/R may misattribute shared variability to A; no exploration of correlated innovations or spike/calcium observation models.
- Empirical necessity of session overlap on real data contradicts the most ambitious zero-overlap scenario; identifiability conditions are not formalized.
- Limited baseline comparisons and no analysis of computational scaling or robustness to model misspecification.

Suggestions for improvement
- Quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction, session count, and trial length; provide guidance for experimental design.
- Add a low-rank-plus-diagonal Q or an explicit latent common-input model and compare.
- Compare to alternative latent approaches (e.g., PLDS/GPFA with cross-session subspace alignment).
- Report runtime and scaling with N and T; describe regularization and stability constraints on A.
- Clarify the observation model equation and the exact procedure for computing noise correlations; consider modeling calcium dynamics explicitly.

Overall evaluation
A solid and carefully evaluated application of LDS with missing observations to an important practical problem. Novelty is moderate but the empirical validation is strong. I view this as a borderline-to-positive submission, with acceptance justified if the committee values well-executed, practically useful methods even when mathematically straightforward.
