

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of neural circuits to infer joint dynamics across the entire population. The authors model neural activity as a latent linear dynamical system with missing observations, where each recording session observes a subset of neurons. Their key claim is that they can predict correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, which has significant potential for neural data analysis given current imaging limitations.

## Strengths

The paper makes a clear and important contribution to the field of neural data analysis. The problem addressed is well-motivated by current experimental limitations in imaging large neural populations. The authors provide a theoretically grounded approach with both synthetic and real data validation. Their method is conceptually simple yet effective, and they properly contextualize their work against related literature. The paper is well-structured and generally clearly written, with appropriate technical details in the methods section.

## Weaknesses

While the paper presents a promising approach, several methodological and technical issues undermine its technical soundness and clarity:

1. **Incomplete model specification**: The paper states "we assume the latter to be diagonal" (referring to covariance matrix Q) but never explains why this is a reasonable assumption or how it might affect results. The authors claim "see below for how our framework also can allow for correlated noise" but never actually demonstrate this extension, leaving a critical gap in their model's capabilities.

2. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses "noise correlations" without clear definition, which is problematic in neuroscience where this term has a specific meaning (correlations in trial-to-trial variability). The authors should explicitly connect their model parameters to this standard definition.

3. **Insufficient experimental justification**: The paper states "we experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" but provides no explanation for this failure or evidence of why overlapping neurons are necessary. This is a critical detail for practitioners.

4. **Methodological details missing**: The authors reference "a variant of online-EM" but don't specify how it differs from standard online-EM or how batch sizes were chosen. This lack of detail makes it difficult to assess their implementation or reproduce their results.

5. **Formatting inconsistencies**: Mathematical equations are inconsistently numbered, and the paper uses both "LDS" and "latent dynamical system" without consistently defining the acronym. References have inconsistent formatting (e.g., "vol. 9" vs. "volume 24").

6. **Missing statistical validation**: The paper claims their method "successfully predicts" noise correlations but doesn't provide statistical tests showing this is significantly better than chance or other methods. The correlation coefficients presented (e.g., 0.92) are descriptive but don't establish statistical significance.

7. **Overstated claims**: The paper states "our model can successfully predict the magnitude of (so-called) noise correlations" but the experiments only show correlation between predicted and true values, not whether the predictions are statistically significant or practically useful.

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses an important problem with a potentially valuable approach. However, the technical gaps, inconsistent terminology, and lack of sufficient methodological detail significantly undermine the paper's quality. The authors make strong claims about their method's performance without providing the necessary evidence to support them. The minor methodological flaws and unclear explanations accumulate to a point where the paper's contribution appears less significant than it might be with proper technical execution.

The paper is well-written and well-motivated, but the technical shortcomings prevent it from being a strong contribution to NIPS. The method is based on a standard linear dynamical system, which is a reasonable choice for a proof-of-concept, but the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of this model or demonstrate how it could be extended to more complex neural dynamics.

While the core idea of stitching partial recordings is novel and potentially impactful, the paper does not provide sufficient technical depth to convince the NIPS community of its value. The minor methodological issues identified above are not trivial—they are fundamental to the validity of the claims made in the paper. Without addressing these issues, the paper's claims about predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons cannot be properly evaluated.

Given the stringent standards of NIPS, I recommend rejection. The paper's contribution is interesting but the execution is insufficiently rigorous. The authors would need to provide more thorough methodological details, statistical validation, and clarification of key concepts to meet NIPS standards.
