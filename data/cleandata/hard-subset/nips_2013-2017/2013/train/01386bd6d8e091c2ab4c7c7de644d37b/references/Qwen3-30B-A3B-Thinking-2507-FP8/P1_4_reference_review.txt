

# Peer Review for "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary

This paper addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience: how to infer the joint dynamics of large neural populations when only partial recordings are available due to technical limitations. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit, allowing them to predict correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The core idea builds on latent dynamical system models, specifically using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework. The authors demonstrate their approach on both synthetic data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their method significantly outperforms naive approaches that simply average parameters across sessions.

The work relates to previous NIPS research on neural decoding (e.g., Pillow et al., 2008; Vogelstein et al., 2009) and latent variable models (e.g., Song et al., 2013), but makes a novel contribution by addressing the specific problem of non-simultaneous recordings. Unlike previous work on functional connectivity inference (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011; Stetter et al., 2012), this paper focuses on the statistical challenge of combining data from different imaging sessions.

## Strengths

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the practical limitations of current imaging technology and the importance of understanding neural circuit dynamics. The whisker barrel example makes the problem tangible and relevant to neuroscientists.

2. **Strong Methodological Foundation**: The authors ground their approach in a well-established latent dynamical system framework. The theoretical justification that the method should work (based on the ability to reconstruct a system from partial observations) is compelling.

3. **Rigorous Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough validation through both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real neural data. The comparison to both a naive baseline and a theoretical upper bound ("fully observed" model) is particularly strong.

4. **Significant Practical Impact**: The method addresses a real bottleneck in neuroscience research. For many experimental setups, the ability to combine multiple partial recordings could substantially expand the effective population size that can be studied.

5. **Clear Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with effective figures that illustrate key concepts and results. The experimental design for validation is thoughtful and directly addresses the core question.

## Weaknesses

1. **Simplistic Model Choice**: While the linear Gaussian model provides a good starting point, it's a significant simplification of neural dynamics. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't thoroughly explore how more complex models (e.g., incorporating spiking dynamics, nonlinearities) might improve performance. The paper would be stronger with a brief discussion of how the method could be extended to more realistic models.

2. **Limited Discussion of Limitations**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address the conditions under which the method might fail. For instance, how robust is the method to different levels of overlap between recording sessions? The paper states that "some overlap was critical," but doesn't provide quantitative analysis of how much overlap is needed for different population sizes.

3. **Artificial Validation Setup**: The fictional imaging scenario used for evaluation on real data (pretending simultaneous recordings were from separate sessions) is somewhat artificial. While it's a good proof-of-concept, the authors don't discuss whether this setup accurately reflects the challenges of real-world experimental designs.

4. **Limited Connection to Recent NIPS Work**: The related work section could better contextualize the paper within recent NIPS contributions to neural data analysis. For example, the paper doesn't engage with recent NIPS papers on neural decoding (e.g., from the 2022-2023 proceedings) that might have similar approaches.

5. **Scalability Concerns**: While the paper demonstrates success with populations of 50-100 neurons, it doesn't thoroughly address how the method would scale to the thousands of neurons in a full neural circuit. The computational complexity of the EM algorithm for large N is not discussed.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of computational neuroscience and machine learning. The problem is important, the method is sound, and the results demonstrate significant improvements over naive approaches. The paper provides a valuable tool for neuroscientists to more effectively leverage existing imaging data.

The main limitations relate to the simplicity of the underlying model and insufficient discussion of the method's boundaries. However, these are common challenges in methodological papers, and the authors acknowledge them appropriately. The paper represents a significant step toward more efficient use of neural imaging data.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work is technically sound, addresses an important problem, and provides a method that is both novel and practically useful. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field that aligns well with NIPS's focus on machine learning and its applications.

The paper's significance is particularly strong because it provides a practical solution to a real experimental bottleneck in neuroscience. The results demonstrate that the method can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching the theoretical optimum, which is a substantial advance over existing approaches.

The authors should consider addressing the weaknesses mentioned above in a revision, particularly by more thoroughly discussing the limitations of their model and the conditions under which the method works best. However, these are not critical flaws that would prevent acceptance.

## Final Thoughts

This paper exemplifies the kind of work NIPS values: addressing a significant practical problem with a clear, well-justified methodological contribution that advances the state of the art. The authors have done a thorough job of validating their approach and demonstrating its utility. While the model is simplified, this is a common and reasonable starting point for such methods, and the authors appropriately acknowledge this limitation.

The paper has clear potential to impact neuroscience practice by enabling more effective use of existing imaging data. I believe this work will be of interest to both machine learning researchers and neuroscientists.
