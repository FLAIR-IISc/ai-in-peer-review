

# Peer Review for NIPS

## Summary

This paper presents a novel statistical method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, even for neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors address a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience where current imaging techniques cannot capture all neurons in a circuit (e.g., thousands of neurons in a whisker barrel), but can only image small subsets (hundreds of neurons). Their approach models the neural population as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system framework. They validate their method on both simulated data (where ground truth is available) and real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex (both anesthetized and awake states).

The key insight is that by leveraging strong coupling between nearby neurons (as documented in the literature), they can infer correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The authors demonstrate that their method can accurately predict noise correlations and other statistics of the joint dynamics, approaching the performance of a "fully observed" model (which would require recording all neurons simultaneously) and substantially outperforming a naive approach that simply averages parameters across sessions.

This work builds on previous research in neural dynamics modeling (e.g., [6, 7, 13]) but makes a significant conceptual leap by addressing the specific challenge of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings, which previous methods do not handle. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work, noting that existing methods either require strong assumptions ([12]), don't scale to large populations ([13]), or are designed for different experimental contexts ([14]).

## Quality

The paper is technically sound and well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental validation. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and the solution, with appropriate details on the model fitting procedure using an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing. The validation strategy is particularly strong: they use simulated data with ground truth to establish the method's theoretical capability, and then apply it to real data with a clever benchmarking approach (creating a "fictional" imaging scenario to compare against an upper bound "fully observed" model and a lower bound "naive" model).

The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of their approach, particularly the simplifying assumption of a linear Gaussian dynamical system, and note that more realistic models could potentially improve performance. They also discuss how their method might generalize to more than two populations and address experimental design considerations.

## Clarity

The paper is well-written and well-organized, with a clear narrative flow from problem statement to method to validation. The introduction effectively establishes the significance of the problem and the novelty of the approach. The methods section provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and potentially reproduce the work, though some aspects of the fictional imaging scenario could be clarified (e.g., the exact procedure for creating the "two sessions" from a single simultaneous recording).

The figures (particularly Figures 2-4) are well-designed and effectively communicate the key results. The presentation of results is logical, moving from simulated data to real data and then to more comprehensive analyses across multiple populations. The paper would benefit from a slightly more explicit definition of "noise correlations" in the introduction, as this is a key concept but not defined.

## Originality

This paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field. The core idea of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings to infer joint dynamics is novel and addresses a specific, important problem in neuroscience that previous methods have not solved. The authors carefully contextualize their work against related approaches and clearly articulate how their method differs from and improves upon existing techniques.

The problem itself is well-motivated by the current limitations of imaging technology in neuroscience. The authors demonstrate that their method can be applied to real data with meaningful results, which is an important step beyond theoretical novelty. While the use of linear dynamical systems is not new, the specific application to stitching partial recordings is novel.

## Significance

The significance of this work is substantial. It directly addresses a major bottleneck in neuroscience research—limited imaging capabilities that prevent simultaneous recording of large neural populations. The method allows researchers to make better use of existing imaging technology without waiting for faster imaging modalities. The results demonstrate that the method can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching that of a fully observed model, which is a significant practical advance.

The paper also provides a promising framework for future work, with potential applications to mapping population dynamics in larger neural circuits. The authors correctly note that this could help researchers "map population dynamics of all the neurons in an entire neural circuit such as the zebrafish larval olfactory bulb, or layers 2 & 3 of a whisker barrel— an ambitious goal which has until now been out of reach."

## Strengths

1. **Novel problem formulation**: Addresses a specific, practical challenge in neuroscience that previous methods have not solved.
2. **Strong validation**: Uses both simulated data (with ground truth) and real data with a well-designed benchmarking strategy.
3. **Clear theoretical foundation**: Builds on established latent dynamical system theory while making a specific application to the stitching problem.
4. **Practical relevance**: Directly addresses limitations of current experimental technology.
5. **Well-organized presentation**: Clear narrative flow from problem to solution to validation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Simplified model**: The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't thoroughly explore how much this limitation affects the results in real-world scenarios.
2. **Artificial benchmarking**: The fictional imaging scenario for real data (pretending simultaneous recordings were from two separate sessions) is somewhat artificial and may not fully capture the complexities of real experimental constraints.
3. **Scalability concerns**: The paper doesn't address how the method would scale to very large neural populations (thousands of neurons), despite the authors noting that "in neocortex where circuits or subnetworks can span thousands of neurons, current imaging techniques are still inadequate."
4. **Limited discussion of overlap requirements**: While the authors note that "some overlap was critical for stitching to work," they don't quantify the minimum overlap required or discuss how to optimize this in experimental design given limited imaging time.
5. **Insufficient comparison with alternative methods**: While they compare with a naive approach and a theoretical upper bound, they don't compare with other potential methods that might handle partial observations differently.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It presents a novel, well-validated approach to an important problem in neuroscience with clear practical significance. While the linear Gaussian model is a simplification, the authors are transparent about this limitation and demonstrate that their method works effectively with real data. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

The weaknesses noted are important but do not undermine the core contribution. The authors could address some of these concerns in a revision (particularly regarding scalability and the artificiality of the benchmarking approach), but these are not critical flaws that would prevent acceptance.

This paper represents a significant step toward better utilizing existing experimental data in neuroscience, which aligns well with NIPS's focus on statistical and computational methods for understanding complex systems.

## Final Assessment

**Quality**: 9/10 - The paper is technically sound with strong experimental validation.
**Clarity**: 8/10 - Well-written but could clarify some technical aspects.
**Originality**: 8.5/10 - Novel approach to a well-defined problem.
**Significance**: 9/10 - High practical relevance to neuroscience with potential to impact experimental design.

**Overall**: Strong accept. This is a high-quality, significant contribution that advances our ability to analyze neural circuit dynamics with current experimental constraints.
