This paper tackles a crucial problem in modern neuroscience: leveraging limited, spatially-separated recordings to infer the dynamics of large neural populations. The core idea – “stitching” together observations via a latent dynamical system – is elegant and surprisingly effective. While the authors employ a relatively simple linear Gaussian model, the demonstration of its ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons, particularly in the face of noisy calcium imaging data, is a significant achievement. This work differentiates itself from existing methods by explicitly addressing the challenge of incomplete spatial sampling and offering a principled framework for combining data across sessions. It nicely contextualizes its approach within existing literature on functional connectivity and latent variable modeling, clearly articulating its novel contribution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The approach of inferring correlations *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons is a genuinely novel contribution. Most existing work focuses on inferring connectivity *within* a single, fully observed (or nearly so) recording.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The formulation within a latent dynamical system framework provides a solid theoretical foundation. The connection to established results in state-space modeling is well-explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** Demonstrating the method's effectiveness on both simulated and real calcium imaging data is compelling. The results on the mouse somatosensory cortex data are particularly encouraging.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The description of the experimental setup and data analysis pipeline is thorough.
* **Significance:** The ability to reconstruct population dynamics from fragmented recordings has substantial implications for the field. It offers a pathway to studying circuits that are currently too large to image in their entirety.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplicity:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact of this simplification on the accuracy of their inferences is not fully explored. Real neural circuits are undoubtedly more complex, and deviations from linearity and Gaussianity could affect performance.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the EM algorithm could be sensitive to initialization and local optima. While the authors employ online-EM, a more rigorous analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The dependence on overlap between imaging sessions (c = 60% in the experimental setup) feels somewhat restrictive. The authors mention the need for overlap, but the impact of varying the degree of overlap on performance isn't fully investigated.
* **Limited Exploration of Prior Information:** While the authors mention the possibility of incorporating priors on network topology, they don’t demonstrate it in practice. Leveraging prior knowledge about circuit structure could potentially improve reconstruction accuracy.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The core idea is genuinely novel and represents a significant step forward in our ability to analyze large-scale neural data.
* The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
* The method is theoretically sound and well-motivated.
* The potential impact of this work on the field is substantial.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The reliance on a relatively simple model could limit the accuracy and generalizability of the method.
* The requirement for overlap between imaging sessions could be a practical constraint in some experimental settings.
* Further analysis of parameter sensitivity and the impact of prior information would strengthen the paper.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a highly novel and promising approach. While the model simplicity and overlap requirement are legitimate concerns, they are outweighed by the significant contribution of the work. The ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons is a remarkable achievement, and the method has the potential to unlock new insights into the dynamics of large neural circuits. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program. I am willing to overlook the minor flaws in validation given the boldness and originality of the idea.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
