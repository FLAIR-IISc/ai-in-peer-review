## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable and technically sound method for addressing a significant challenge in neuroscience – inferring the dynamics of large neural populations from limited, spatially fragmented recordings. The approach of “stitching” together partial observations using a latent dynamical system is well-motivated, clearly explained, and demonstrated to work effectively in both simulated and real data. While not groundbreaking in terms of the underlying mathematical framework (building on established LDS methods), the application to this specific problem, coupled with the convincing empirical results, constitutes a significant contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**1. Summary and Related Work:**

The paper addresses the limitation of current 2-photon imaging techniques in capturing the activity of entire neural circuits. Recognizing the strong coupling between nearby neurons, the authors propose a method to combine information from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of overlapping sub-populations.  They formulate this as a latent dynamical system (LDS) estimation problem, using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for parameter fitting. 

The authors correctly position their work within the broader context of neural connectivity inference, citing relevant studies focusing on functional connectivity estimation from imaging and electrophysiological data [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]. They also effectively differentiate their approach from existing methods dealing with sub-sampled observations [12, 13, 14, 15], highlighting how their problem differs due to the longer timescale of neural dynamics compared to the sampling rate, and the need to infer correlations *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons. The acknowledgement of related work is thorough and demonstrates a good understanding of the field.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Formulation:** The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the problem of combining partial recordings is well-defined. The example of the whisker barrel is particularly effective in illustrating the challenge.
* **Solid Methodology:** The use of an LDS framework, while not novel, is appropriate for the problem. The choice of a linear Gaussian model simplifies the computation without sacrificing essential features for this initial investigation. The EM algorithm is a standard approach for parameter estimation in LDS models.
* **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The results on both simulated and real data are compelling.  The demonstration that the method can accurately recover noise correlations, a crucial aspect of neural population dynamics, is particularly impressive.
* **Rigorous Comparison:** The authors thoughtfully compare their method to both an upper bound (fully observed model) and a lower bound (naive approach), providing a clear benchmark for performance.  This highlights the benefits of their stitching approach.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methods section is detailed enough to allow for replication.
* **Relevance to Neuroscience:** This method has the potential to significantly impact our ability to study large neural circuits, overcoming a major limitation in current experimental techniques.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** While justifiable for initial exploration, the linearity and Gaussianity assumptions are limiting. Real neural data is inherently non-linear and often exhibits non-Gaussian distributions. The authors acknowledge this, but future work should explore more sophisticated models that address these limitations.
* **Scalability:** The computational cost of the EM algorithm, even with mini-batch optimization, might become prohibitive for extremely large populations (thousands of neurons). Investigating more scalable inference techniques would be beneficial.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The performance of the method appears to depend on sufficient overlap between the recorded populations. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed exploration of the impact of overlap on performance, and potential strategies for dealing with minimal overlap, would be valuable.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the EM algorithm can be sensitive to initialization. The paper could benefit from a discussion of strategies for robust initialization and assessing the stability of the estimated parameters.
* **Limited Exploration of Biological Plausibility:** While the method works well, further investigation into the biological plausibility of the inferred connections and dynamics would strengthen the findings. For example, examining whether the inferred connectivity patterns align with known anatomical connections.



**4. Arguments Pro and Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* Addresses a significant and practical problem in neuroscience.
* Presents a technically sound and well-motivated method.
* Demonstrates strong performance in both simulated and real data.
* Rigorous comparison to alternative approaches.
* Potential to advance our understanding of neural circuit function.

**Con:**

* Relies on simplifying assumptions (linearity and Gaussianity).
* Scalability might be a concern for very large populations.
* Dependence on overlap between recorded regions.




**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the amount of overlap between recordings? Could you provide a more detailed analysis of this relationship?
*  Have you investigated alternative optimization strategies to address potential scalability issues, such as variational inference or stochastic gradient descent?
*  Could you elaborate on the strategies used for parameter initialization, and discuss the potential for instability or convergence to local optima?
*  Do the inferred connections align with known anatomical connections in the somatosensory cortex?
*  How would the approach need to be adapted to handle more than two partial recordings?



**Overall Recommendation:**

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions.  The authors have presented a significant contribution to the field of neural data analysis. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, and responding to the questions posed, will further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. The work is well-executed, clearly written, and has the potential to be widely used by neuroscientists seeking to unravel the complexities of neural population dynamics.
