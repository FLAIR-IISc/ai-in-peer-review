Summary
The paper proposes “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit by fitting a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations. Each imaging session observes a subset of neurons via a known selection matrix Ck; the joint dynamics x_t follow x_t = A x_{t-1} + B u_t + η_t with diagonal Q, and observations y_t = Ck (x_t + d) + ε_t with diagonal R. Parameters (A,B,Q,R,d) are learned by EM (with an online-EM variant), performing Kalman smoothing in the E-step. The goal is to estimate cross-couplings and, more practically, predict pairwise “noise correlations” among neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. On synthetic data the method recovers off-diagonal coupling blocks and noise correlations; on real 2-photon calcium data, a “fictional” split into two sessions with partial overlap is used to evaluate against a “fully observed” upper bound and a “naive” baseline (separate LDS per session). The stitched LDS approaches the fully observed model in predicting nonsimultaneous correlations and improves activity prediction over the naive baseline.

Relation to prior work
The approach builds directly on standard LDS/system identification via EM/Kalman smoothing, and on prior latent-variable approaches for neural data (e.g., partially observed models, GLM/LDS hybrids, GPFA). The stitching idea leverages observability/identifiability intuitions from system identification and the fact that a second session can align latent spaces. Related works on inferring connectivity from imaging/ephys and on handling subsampling are cited; however, there is substantial prior literature on subspace identification with missing outputs, multi-session latent factor alignment, and non-Gaussian/spiking models that is not fully engaged with in baselines or analysis.

Strengths
- Addresses an important practical problem: combining multiple partial recordings to infer joint dynamics and correlations.
- Methodologically simple and computationally tractable; uses well-understood LDS/EM machinery.
- Clear demonstration that some overlap across sessions can anchor a joint model; empirical gains over a weak baseline.
- Evaluation includes both synthetic data with known ground truth and real 2-photon data; metrics include correlation prediction and cross-prediction of held-out activity.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is modest: the contribution largely repurposes a standard LDS with missing data; no new algorithmic or theoretical development beyond straightforward EM with different Ck.
- Baseline comparisons are weak. The “naive” baseline is not competitive. Missing are baselines like:
  • Joint factor models (FA/GPFA) stitched via shared neurons,
  • Subspace identification (e.g., N4SID) adapted to missing outputs,
  • Latent GLM/Poisson LDS or low-rank latent LDS with shared/global factors,
  • Simple correlation-alignment baselines using overlap to impute cross-block covariances.
- Evaluation design hinges on a “fictional” split of simultaneously recorded data. While pragmatic, it does not emulate true multi-session variability (e.g., drift, bleaching, behavioral state changes, alignment errors). The “upper bound” fully observed model is not ground truth and may itself be biased.
- The method appears to require nontrivial overlap to work; the authors state performance degrades without overlap, which undercuts the claim that nonsimultaneous recordings can be stitched in general. No analysis of the minimum overlap required or identifiability conditions (observability/controllability, persistent excitation).
- Strong modeling assumptions: linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal Q and R, stationarity, and operating directly on calcium traces. These are likely violated (calcium nonlinearity, neuropil contamination, temporally correlated measurement noise, behavior/task-dependent nonstationarities). No robustness analysis to misspecification.
- Limited metrics and uncertainty quantification: correlations between estimated and “upper bound” quantities are reported, but no predictive log-likelihoods, confidence intervals, calibration, or ablations (e.g., effect of Q/R structure, sparsity penalties, dimensionality).
- Practical issues for real deployment are unaddressed: neuron identity matching across sessions, differing stimuli across sessions, heteroscedastic noise across cells/sessions, runtime/complexity and scaling (to thousands of neurons/sessions).

Quality
Technically sound as an application of LDS/EM with missing observations; the synthetic experiments support feasibility. However, the empirical validation is limited by weak baselines, reliance on a fictional split, and lack of robustness/stress tests. Claims about predicting nonsimultaneous correlations would benefit from stronger, more realistic experiments and clearer statistical evaluation.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-written. The core model and EM updates are described cleanly. Missing details include initialization, convergence criteria, computational complexity, choice of latent dimensionality (the model seems full-dimensional at N, which is unusual), and handling of potential correlated measurement noise. Figures referenced but not provided here appear to support main claims.

Originality
Conceptual framing (“stitching” sessions via LDS with missing data) is interesting but methodologically close to standard system ID. The paper does not provide new theory for identifiability without overlap or new algorithms for alignment.

Significance
The problem is significant for neuroscience practice. Given the modest novelty and limitations in validation, the current contribution seems incremental. With stronger baselines and more realistic multi-session tests, impact could be higher.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add competitive baselines (GPFA/FA stitched via overlap, subspace ID with missing outputs, Poisson/latent GLM variants).
- Analyze required overlap and provide theoretical/empirical identifiability conditions; test varying overlap, SNR, coupling strength, and session-to-session drift.
- Include predictive log-likelihood on held-out data, uncertainty estimates, and ablations (Q/R structure, sparsity on A, inclusion of unobserved latent factors).
- Evaluate on genuinely different sessions (not only fictional splits), ideally with some simultaneous ground truth (e.g., volumetric imaging or ephys) to quantify cross-session correlation recovery under realistic shifts.
- Address neuron identity uncertainty across sessions and stimulus mismatch; discuss practical pipelines.

Pros
- Clear, tractable method addressing a relevant problem.
- Positive results on synthetic and real data for correlation prediction.
- Insightful observation that small overlap can anchor joint dynamics.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond standard LDS/EM.
- Weak baselines; evaluation relies on fictional splits.
- Strong assumptions, limited robustness analysis, and unclear scalability/identifiability without overlap.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). Promising direction, but the current version lacks rigorous validation against strong baselines and under realistic multi-session variability, and offers limited methodological innovation. Strengthening the empirical study and situating the approach among stronger alternatives would substantially improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
High.
