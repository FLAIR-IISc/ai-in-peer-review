- Problem: Infers joint population dynamics and noise correlations across neurons not recorded simultaneously by "stitching" partial recordings.  
- Method: Fit a single linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations using EM and Kalman smoothing, with session-specific selection matrices Ck.  
- Data/model details: Operates on calcium fluorescence, includes stimulus regressors B, assumes diagonal process and observation noise (Q, R) and stationarity across sessions.  
- Synthetic results: Recovers coupling matrix A well, predicts noise correlations and unobserved activity accurately, outperforming a naive per-session baseline.  
- Real-data validation: Uses “fictional imaging” splits of single sessions to show stitched model predicts non-simultaneous pair correlations close to a fully observed fit and better than the baseline.  
- Overlap requirement: Some overlap across sessions is important for performance; method performs poorly without overlap.  
- Relation to prior work: Connects to neural connectivity inference and partially observed dynamical systems, but novel in inferring cross-session correlations without strong stimulus/noise assumptions.  
- Soundness: Modeling/inference are standard and appropriate; synthetic experiments are carefully constructed.  
- Limitation — session realism: Real-data tests split a single session (not separate days), so confounds like state/drift are not evaluated.  
- Limitation — overlap quantification: No quantitative analysis of how much overlap is required (fraction or which neurons).  
- Limitation — identifiability: No formal analysis of identifiability or conditions for successful stitching, only qualitative discussion.  
- Limitation — model mismatch: Assumes linear dynamics and diagonal Q/R; robustness to correlated noise, calcium dynamics, or nonstationarity is not tested.  
- Limitation — scalability: Computational cost and runtime for large N (thousands) and smoothing cost are not discussed.  
- Experimental care: Train/test splits OK in simulations; real-data cross-validation details and stronger baselines are needed.  
- Baselines: Naive baseline is weak; recommend comparing to shared-factor models (FA/GPFA/PLDS), multi-view subspace ID, and graphical-model imputation.  
- Clarity issues: Notation in Eq.2 is confusing, stimulus/regressor details, stability constraints on A, online-EM scheme, and runtime need clarification; minor typos/footnote issues.  
- Originality: Core LDS/EM machinery is not novel, but the multiview/missing-data “stitching” application and empirical demonstration are timely and original.  
- Significance: Potentially high practical impact for neuroscience if validated across real multi-session recordings; relevant to NeurIPS audience.  
- Pros: Addresses an important problem, conceptually simple and reproducible, strong synthetic results, honest discussion of limitations and extensions.  
- Cons: Lacks validation across separate sessions, requires overlap, limited baselines, no theory on identifiability, and untested modeling/scalability assumptions.  
- Key questions for authors: Required overlap fraction/type, demonstration on separate sessions, robustness to correlated noise/latent inputs, constraints on A for stability, computational complexity for N≈200 vs N>1000, and comparisons to shared-factor/subspace-ID methods.  
- Suggestions: Add performance vs overlap/session length/number analyses, stronger baselines, regularization on A (sparsity/stability/low-rank), robustness tests to model mismatch, and clearer measurement/stimulus/runtime details.  
- Recommendation: Borderline accept pending stronger empirical validation across separate sessions, robustness/overlap analyses, and improved baselines.
