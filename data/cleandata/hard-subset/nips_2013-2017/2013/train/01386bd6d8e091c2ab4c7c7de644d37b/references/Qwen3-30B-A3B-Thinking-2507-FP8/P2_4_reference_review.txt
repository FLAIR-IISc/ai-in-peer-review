

# Review: Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
This paper addresses a critical experimental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record the activity of large neural populations due to technical constraints. The authors propose a statistical method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit (from different imaging sessions with non-overlapping subsets of neurons) to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously. They frame this as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) model that can be fit via expectation maximization with Kalman smoothing.

The work builds on theoretical results about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21-23], but extends them to the neuroscience context. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from prior work on inferring connectivity from partial data [12-15], noting that their method does not require the strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations) made by previous methods. This represents a significant step beyond the "naive" approach of separately analyzing each recording session and combining parameters.

## Quality
The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The method is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and implemented using standard techniques (EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing). The experimental validation is thorough, with both synthetic data (where ground truth is available) and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. The authors provide appropriate baselines (fully observed model and naive approach) to benchmark performance, which is crucial for evaluating the method's effectiveness.

The results are well-supported: the stitching method accurately recovers coupling matrices (correlation 0.73 for non-simultaneously recorded pairs vs. 0.95 for simultaneously recorded), predicts noise correlations (correlation 0.92 with ground truth on synthetic data), and outperforms the naive baseline by a significant margin on real data (correlation 0.84 vs. 0.15 for predicted correlations). The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of their linear model and the need for more sophisticated approaches in future work.

## Clarity
The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The problem statement, method, and results are logically presented. The figures are informative, with Figure 2 providing a particularly clear explanation of the synthetic data generation and results. The authors effectively use the synthetic data to demonstrate the core intuition behind their method before moving to the more complex real data.

The technical details in Section 2.1 are appropriately detailed for the audience, and the experimental setup in Section 2.2 is clearly described. The only minor clarity issue is that the paper sometimes refers to "noise correlations" without explicitly defining them as correlations after subtracting stimulus contributions (a standard definition in neuroscience, but worth clarifying for broader audiences).

## Originality
This work makes a significant contribution to the field. While the theoretical foundation (reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations) is established, applying it to the specific neuroscience problem of stitching partial neural recordings is novel. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method works where previous approaches fail (e.g., they explicitly state that the method of [12] would be impossible to apply due to weak stimulus drive in their simulation).

The paper is sufficiently distinct from recent NIPS papers on neural data analysis [6-11, 13-15]. While there are papers on inferring connectivity from partial data, none address the specific problem of inferring correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons without strong assumptions. This represents a meaningful advancement in the field.

## Significance
The significance of this work is high. The ability to infer population-level dynamics from multiple partial recordings could substantially expand the scope of neural circuit studies, allowing researchers to work with existing imaging technology rather than waiting for technological advances. The paper demonstrates this by successfully applying their method to real datasets, showing that correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons can be predicted with high accuracy.

The work addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience with immediate practical implications. As the authors note, while faster imaging modalities are developing, there are physical limits to simultaneous recording of large populations. Their method provides a computational approach to overcome this limitation, potentially enabling studies of much larger circuits than currently possible.

## Strengths and Weaknesses
**Strengths:**
- Addresses a critical experimental bottleneck in neuroscience
- Theoretical foundation is sound and well-explained
- Comprehensive validation on both synthetic and real data
- Clear comparison to appropriate baselines
- Demonstrates practical utility with real neural data
- Provides a clear explanation of why the method works

**Weaknesses:**
- The linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification of neural dynamics (e.g., it doesn't account for calcium dynamics, spiking, or nonlinearities)
- Limited exploration of how the method's performance varies with different experimental parameters (e.g., degree of overlap between recording sessions, recording duration)
- The paper doesn't fully explore the limitations of the method when neural dynamics are highly non-stationary or complex
- The real data validation is limited to two datasets (anesthetized and awake mice), which may not capture the full range of neural dynamics

## Recommendation
This paper makes a strong contribution to the field of computational neuroscience and has significant potential impact. While the linear Gaussian model is a simplification, it's a reasonable starting point that demonstrates the core idea effectively. The method is both theoretically sound and practically useful, and the results convincingly show it outperforms naive alternatives.

The paper meets all four NIPS review criteria well. The quality is high, the clarity is excellent, the originality is clear, and the significance is substantial. The minor weaknesses noted do not detract from the overall contribution.

I recommend acceptance. This work provides a valuable tool for neuroscientists, and the method could inspire extensions to more complex models that better capture neural dynamics. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses an important problem in a novel way.

**Pro for Acceptance:** The paper solves a real, significant problem in neuroscience with a method that is both theoretically grounded and practically useful, demonstrated on real data with strong results.

**Con for Acceptance:** The model is relatively simple, but the authors acknowledge this and provide clear paths for future improvement. The simplicity actually helps demonstrate the core idea clearly.
