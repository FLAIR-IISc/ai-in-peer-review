- Summary: Presents an EM-fitted latent linear-Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) to “stitch” multiple partial neural recordings and infer cross-session couplings, noise correlations, and unobserved activity.

- Relation to prior work: Builds on latent GLMs/LDSs, system identification and embedding results, closest to Pillow & Latham’s latent GLM with missing observations.

- Strength — problem importance: Tackles a practical, high-impact problem for neurophysiology—stitching partial recordings to expand circuit coverage.

- Strength — simplicity/practicality: Uses a standard LDS with known selection matrices and EM; method is straightforward to implement and scales to hundreds of neurons.

- Strength — empirical validation: Synthetic recovery of A and noise correlations and real-data tests with silver-standard and naive baselines support claims.

- Strength — focus: Emphasizes recovery of noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs, aligning with common neuroscientific analyses.

- Strength — transparency: Authors discuss limitations and outline plausible extensions.

- Weakness — theory/identifiability: Lacks formal conditions/diagnostics for when stitching succeeds (observability, required overlap, stimulus richness); simulation/real-data discrepancies unexplained.

- Weakness — restrictive modeling assumptions: Assumes diagonal Q and R and shared session parameters (d), which can misattribute session differences to A or miss shared process noise.

- Weakness — missing session-specific parameters: No per-session baselines, gains, or noise levels; could reduce fit quality on real heterogeneous sessions.

- Weakness — ignoring low-rank process noise: Forcing correlated variability into A may be inappropriate when common inputs exist.

- Weakness — evaluation targets: Relies on model-to-model comparisons; should directly compare reconstructed correlations to empirical held-out correlations (with stimulus regression).

- Weakness — weak baselines: Naive baseline is insufficient; missing comparisons to GPFA/FA alignment, sparse+low-rank LDS, variational latent GLMs, and simple imputation.

- Weakness — nonstationarity: Assumes same A/B across sessions; no tests for robustness to session drift, behavioral state changes, or bleaching.

- Weakness — scalability: No runtime or memory analysis; smoothing scales poorly with N and many sessions; no discussion of sparse/low-rank or iterative solvers.

- Weakness — clarity/implementation details: Notational glitches, sparse EM implementation details (initialization, regularization, convergence) and consistent error bars are lacking.

- Quality: Technically sound application of LDS with missing data, but experimental evaluation could be strengthened.

- Clarity: Generally well-organized, but needs fixes to notation and more implementation/preprocessing detail.

- Originality: Useful application of existing ideas; novelty is in the stitching use case and empirical demonstration rather than methodological innovation.

- Significance: Potentially impactful for neuroscience workflows; moderate ML novelty without theoretical advances.

- Suggestions — diagnostics/ablations: Provide overlap, stimulus strength, session length, and SNR ablations to characterize when stitching works.

- Suggestions — richer modeling: Add session-specific measurement parameters (dk, Rk, gains) and allow low-rank process noise or session latents.

- Suggestions — stronger baselines and scaling: Compare to GPFA/FA, sparse+low-rank LDS, variational latent GLMs, and report runtime/scaling.

- Suggestions — empirical evaluation: Directly compare recovered correlations to empirical held-out correlations (with stimulus regression) and test robustness to nonstationarity.

- Suggestions — clarity/details: Clarify Eq. (2), noise notation, full EM updates, regularization/stability constraints, and initialization.

- Arguments for acceptance: Practical, well-validated approach to an important problem with clear utility for neuroscience.

- Arguments against acceptance: Limited novelty, insufficient theoretical guarantees, restrictive modeling choices, and incomplete evaluation.

- Recommendation: Weak accept.
