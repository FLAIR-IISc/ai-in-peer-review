This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring the dynamics of a neural population from partial, non-simultaneous recordings. The core idea – leveraging the assumption of strong local connectivity to “stitch” together information from multiple imaging sessions – is conceptually sound and practically relevant, given the limitations of current imaging technology. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, to achieve this, and demonstrate its effectiveness both on simulated data and real calcium imaging recordings from mouse somatosensory cortex.

**Strengths:**

* **Important Problem:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to the field of neural computation. Being able to infer dynamics from incomplete data is crucial for studying larger, more complex circuits.
* **Clear Motivation & Framing:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of current imaging techniques and motivates the need for a method like the one proposed. The example of the whisker barrel is particularly effective in illustrating the scenario.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** Framing the problem as a latent dynamical system is a principled approach, and the authors correctly connect their work to existing literature in this area. The choice of a linear Gaussian model, while simplifying, allows for tractable analysis and provides a clear baseline.
* **Demonstration on Real Data:** Applying the method to experimental calcium imaging data is a significant strength. The results showing successful reconstruction of correlations are compelling.
* **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The inclusion of both an “upper bound” (fully observed model) and a “lower bound” (naive approach) provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed method.
* **Detailed Methods:** The methods section is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproducing the results, including parameter choices and algorithmic details.
* **Good visualization:** The figure showing the coupling matrix in the anesthetized data is a very compelling illustration of the method’s performance.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** While understandable for tractability, the linear Gaussian assumption is a significant limitation. Neural dynamics are inherently nonlinear and non-Gaussian. This is acknowledged by the authors, but the implications for the generalizability of the method are not fully explored.  It is unclear how much of the success stems from the linear assumption itself.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between recording sessions is *critical* for performance is concerning. This suggests the method may be sensitive to the specific experimental design and may not be applicable in scenarios where overlapping recordings are not feasible. The paper doesn't explore how the *amount* of overlap influences performance.
* **Limited Exploration of Model Complexity:** The authors focus on a relatively simple linear Gaussian model. Exploring more sophisticated latent dynamical systems (e.g., incorporating nonlinearities or state-dependent noise) could potentially improve performance, but would add computational complexity. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion.
* **Parameter Tuning and Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of parameter tuning and the sensitivity of the results to different parameter choices. Are the results robust to changes in the regularization strength, or the initial conditions for the EM algorithm?
* **Interpretation of “Functional” Connectivity:** The authors appropriately caution against interpreting the estimated ‘A’ matrix as representing direct synaptic connections. However, further discussion of the biological interpretation of these functional couplings would be valuable.
* **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** While figures visually demonstrate the performance, statistical tests (e.g., comparing the correlation coefficients between different methods) are missing. This would strengthen the conclusions.

**Originality:**

The idea of “stitching” together partial observations is novel, although it builds on established techniques in latent dynamical systems and state-space modeling. The application of this framework to the specific problem of inferring neural population dynamics from incomplete calcium imaging data is a significant contribution. The thorough comparison to alternative methods strengthens the originality of the work.

**Significance:**

The proposed method has the potential to significantly advance our ability to study neural circuits. By enabling the inference of dynamics from incomplete data, it opens up new possibilities for investigating the functional organization and computational properties of larger brain regions. The results on real data are promising and suggest that this method could be a valuable tool for neuroscientists.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses an important and challenging problem in computational neuroscience.
* Proposes a principled and well-motivated method based on established theoretical frameworks.
* Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.
* Provides a thorough comparison to relevant baseline methods.
* Offers a clear and detailed description of the methods and results.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on a potentially limiting linear Gaussian assumption.
* Requires a significant degree of overlap between recording sessions.
* Lacks a detailed exploration of parameter sensitivity and robustness.
* Could benefit from more rigorous statistical analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field. While the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption and the overlap requirement are significant, the novelty of the approach, the compelling results on real data, and the thorough comparison to baseline methods make this a strong submission. I recommend **acceptance**, but with a request for minor revisions. Specifically, I would encourage the authors to:

1.  Briefly discuss the limitations of the linear Gaussian assumption and suggest potential directions for future work that address these limitations.
2.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between the amount of overlap between recording sessions and the performance of the method.
3.  Include statistical tests to support the visual comparisons presented in the figures.
4.  Discuss parameter sensitivity and robustness in the supplemental materials.
