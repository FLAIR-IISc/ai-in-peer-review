

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit using a latent dynamical system model. The authors argue that by leveraging the strong couplings between nearby neurons, one can infer correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. They demonstrate their approach on simulated data and two calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

**Strengths:**
The paper addresses a meaningful experimental limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations. The idea of using multiple partial recordings to infer population dynamics is novel and potentially valuable for experimental design. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and demonstrate reasonable performance on simulated data. The comparison to a naive baseline is appropriate and highlights the value of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological flaws:** The linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) model appears fundamentally inadequate for neural data. The authors acknowledge this limitation ("While this model does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade of neural couplings, calcium dynamics, fluorescence measurements and imaging noise"), yet they don't provide sufficient justification for why this simple model works well in practice. The assumption of diagonal noise covariance matrix (Q) is particularly problematic for neural data where noise correlations are a key feature of interest. The paper should have included a comparison to more sophisticated models that account for neural dynamics.

2. **Inconsistent notation and terminology:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout (e.g., "xkt" for population activity in equations but referring to "the activity of N neurons" as if it's a single value). The term "noise correlations" is used without clear definition or calculation method. In neuroscience, noise correlations are specifically defined as correlations in trial-to-trial variability after accounting for stimulus-driven responses, but the paper doesn't clarify how this is computed.

3. **Technical details missing:** The paper claims their method "successfully predict[s] the magnitude of noise correlations," but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these predictions. The correlation values they report (e.g., "correlation 0.84 for stitchLDS") lack confidence intervals or statistical tests to determine if these differences are meaningful.

4. **Experimental design flaws:** The "fictional imaging scenario" used to evaluate performance on real data is problematic. By pretending that simultaneously imaged neurons were recorded in two separate sessions with 60% overlap, they create an artificial scenario that may not reflect real experimental constraints. The paper doesn't address how their method would perform with less overlap or more realistic experimental constraints.

5. **Insufficient validation:** The authors claim their method "can predict the correlated dynamics of large populations of neurons," but they only demonstrate this on relatively small populations (up to 100 neurons). The paper doesn't provide evidence of scalability to larger neural populations, which is a critical concern for the stated goal.

6. **Formatting and presentation issues:** The abstract contains an oddly placed email address ("large ∗sturaga@gatsby.ucl.ac.uk populations"). The reference list has inconsistent formatting (some entries use "vol." while others use "volume"). The paper contains multiple grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "stitching" sometimes hyphenated, sometimes not).

7. **Insufficient comparison to related work:** The authors dismiss [12] as requiring "the strong assumption that noise correlations are monotonically related to stimulus correlations," but don't explain how their method avoids this assumption. They cite [13] as not scaling to large populations, but don't provide evidence that their method scales better.

**Overall assessment:**
While the paper addresses a meaningful problem in neuroscience and presents a potentially useful method, the technical flaws are substantial. The fundamental mismatch between the linear Gaussian model and the complex, nonlinear nature of neural dynamics undermines the paper's main claim. The lack of statistical validation for key claims and the artificial experimental design for evaluation significantly weaken the contribution. The paper's claims about predicting noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neurons are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented.

The paper would benefit from addressing these methodological concerns, providing more rigorous statistical validation, and demonstrating scalability to larger neural populations. As currently written, the paper does not provide a sufficiently strong foundation for its claims, and the technical shortcomings outweigh the potential significance of the problem being addressed.

**Recommendation: Reject**
