### Summary

This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population. The core problem is that experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record from a subset of neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose to "stitch" together these non-simultaneous recordings by modeling the entire population's activity as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each recording session is treated as a partial observation of this underlying system, specified by a session-specific observation matrix. The model parameters, including the full coupling matrix that describes interactions between all neurons (even those never recorded together), are learned using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The authors validate their approach on both simulated data and two real 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. A key strength of their evaluation is the creation of a "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is artificially split into partial, overlapping recordings. This allows for direct comparison against a "ground truth" model trained on the complete data (an upper bound on performance) and a "naive" model that treats each recording session independently (a lower bound). The results demonstrate that the proposed "stitching" method can successfully recover functional couplings and, crucially, predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never observed simultaneously, performing far better than the naive approach and approaching the performance of the fully-observed model.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and highly practical limitation in experimental neuroscience. The ability to build a coherent model of a large neural circuit from partial recordings could dramatically expand the effective scale of current imaging technologies. This work provides a principled and effective method for a problem many experimentalists face, making it highly likely to be used and built upon by others in the computational neuroscience community.

2.  **Methodological Clarity and Elegance:** The core idea of modeling the problem as an LDS with missing data, where the missingness pattern changes across sessions, is both elegant and powerful. The choice of an LDS is pragmatic, allowing for tractable inference via Kalman smoothing within a standard EM framework. The paper is exceptionally well-written and the method is explained clearly, making it easy for the reader to understand the approach.

3.  **Rigorous and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation strategy is a major strength. The "fictional imaging scenario" is a clever way to benchmark the method on real data where the true joint dynamics are otherwise unknown. Establishing clear upper and lower bounds for performance (the "fully observed" and "naive" models, respectively) provides a compelling and easy-to-interpret demonstration of the method's efficacy. The validation on both simulated and real data (from both anesthetized and awake animals) further strengthens the conclusions.

4.  **Strong and Clear Results:** The results are impressive. The key finding—that noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons can be accurately predicted (Fig 3c,d)—is a non-trivial and important achievement. The visualizations are effective, and the quantitative comparisons to the upper and lower bounds robustly support the paper's claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Model Simplicity vs. Neural Reality:** The choice of a linear-Gaussian model is justified by its tractability, but it is a significant simplification of the underlying biophysics. Neural activity is characterized by discrete spikes (non-Gaussian) and interacts non-linearly. The paper acknowledges this limitation in the discussion, but it would be beneficial to discuss its potential consequences more explicitly. For instance, could the linear model misattribute shared non-linear dynamics to direct linear coupling? While the model works well for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), its limitations for capturing other aspects of neural computation should be more prominent.

2.  **The Critical Role of Overlap:** The paper states, "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial finding with major implications for experimental design. The success of the method seems to depend on having a set of "anchor" neurons that are common across sessions to link the different recordings. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more systematic analysis of this dependency. A plot showing performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) as a function of the percentage of overlapping neurons would be invaluable for experimentalists looking to apply this method.

3.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The paper uses an LDS, for which standard inference (Kalman smoothing) scales cubically with the size of the state space (the total number of neurons `N`). While the authors mention using online EM, there is no discussion of the computational complexity or how the method scales to the very large circuits mentioned in the introduction (e.g., "thousands of neurons"). For a NIPS audience, some analysis of the computational runtime and potential bottlenecks for scaling to larger `N` would be a valuable addition.

4.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The model assumes that the system dynamics (i.e., the `A` matrix) are stationary throughout all recording sessions. In biological systems, particularly in awake animals, network properties can change over time due to learning, adaptation, or shifts in behavioral state. The current framework does not account for this. This is a reasonable simplifying assumption for a first paper, but it should be mentioned as a limitation in the discussion.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Addresses a significant, practical problem in a large and active field (systems/computational neuroscience).
*   Proposes a simple, elegant, and technically sound solution.
*   The experimental validation is exceptionally well-designed and convincing.
*   Results are strong and clearly demonstrate the method's value over naive approaches.
*   The paper is very well-written and organized.

### Arguments against Acceptance:
*   The linear-Gaussian model is a strong simplification of neural dynamics.
*   The critical dependence on overlapping neurons is mentioned but not systematically analyzed.
*   Lack of discussion on computational scalability to truly large populations.

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that presents an elegant solution to an important problem. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is rigorous, and the results are compelling. The weaknesses are primarily related to the simplifying assumptions of the model and a lack of analysis on some practical parameters (overlap, scalability), but these are acceptable limitations for a conference paper and represent clear avenues for future work. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem and the quality of the evaluation, far outweigh these weaknesses. The work is a perfect fit for NIPS, bridging machine learning methods with a key application in scientific discovery. I strongly recommend acceptance.
