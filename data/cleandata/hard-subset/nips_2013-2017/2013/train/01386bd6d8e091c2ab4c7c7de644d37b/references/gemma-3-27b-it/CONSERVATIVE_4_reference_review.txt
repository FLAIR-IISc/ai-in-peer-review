This paper addresses the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from incomplete recordings, a common challenge in modern neuroscience. The core idea—to “stitch” together information from multiple partial observations using a latent dynamical system—is intuitively appealing and addresses a practical limitation of current imaging technology. The authors frame this as an EM-based estimation problem within a linear Gaussian state-space model, providing a clear mathematical formulation. The application to both simulated and real calcium imaging data is a positive aspect.

However, while the idea is promising, the paper falls short of convincing me of its substantial novelty and significance. The use of a latent dynamical system for combining data from multiple observations is not a novel concept in signal processing or state estimation (see, e.g., Kalman filtering and smoothing, and related work in control theory). The authors acknowledge this lineage but don't adequately contextualize their contribution within this broader field. The paper positions itself as addressing a gap in *neural* data analysis, specifically for non-simultaneous recordings, but the implementation feels somewhat standard, relying heavily on established methods.

A primary weakness lies in the limited complexity of the model. While the authors acknowledge the simplifying assumptions (linear dynamics, Gaussian noise), they don’t adequately justify why these are sufficient for capturing the essential dynamics of neural circuits. Neural circuits are inherently nonlinear, and calcium imaging data is demonstrably non-Gaussian. The authors claim predictive power on correlations *despite* these simplifications, which requires stronger justification. The simulation, while informative, is rather simplistic and doesn't fully capture the richness of biological neural networks.

The comparison to existing methods is also insufficient. The “naive” approach provides a lower bound, and the “fully observed” model an upper bound, but the space of alternative methods is broader. For instance, recent advances in variational autoencoders (VAEs) and generative adversarial networks (GANs) offer potentially more flexible frameworks for modeling complex, non-Gaussian data. A more thorough comparison to these techniques would strengthen the argument for the proposed approach. The authors dismiss related work [12, 13, 14, 15] with brief explanations, but these may be applicable with modifications, and a more detailed discussion of their limitations within the context of *this* specific problem would be valuable.

The results on the real data are encouraging, demonstrating that the method can predict correlations reasonably well. However, the fictional imaging scenario used for evaluation feels contrived. The choice of 60% overlap between "imaging sessions" seems arbitrary and may significantly impact performance. Further, the reliance on the fully observed model as a benchmark is problematic, as this represents an idealized scenario that is rarely achievable in practice. The fact that performance improves with population size, while not surprising, also suggests potential overfitting concerns.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a practically relevant problem in neuroscience.
*   Provides a clear mathematical formulation of the stitching problem.
*   Demonstrates reasonable performance on both simulated and real data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks substantial novelty; relies heavily on established methods.
*   Simplifying assumptions (linearity, Gaussianity) are not adequately justified.
*   Insufficient comparison to alternative approaches (VAEs, GANs, etc.).
*   Evaluation relies on a somewhat contrived experimental setup.
*   The significance of the contribution is not fully established.



**Overall Assessment:**

I find this paper to be a technically sound, but ultimately incremental contribution. The idea of stitching together partial observations is reasonable, but the implementation lacks the novelty and rigor required for acceptance at NIPS. The authors need to either significantly expand the model complexity, provide a more compelling comparison to alternative methods, or demonstrate a more substantial improvement over existing approaches to justify publication. I recommend **rejection** at this time. A revised version addressing these concerns could be reconsidered.
