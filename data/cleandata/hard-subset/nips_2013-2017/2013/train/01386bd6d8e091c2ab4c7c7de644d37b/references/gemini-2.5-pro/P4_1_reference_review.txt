This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the entire population's activity as a latent state in a linear dynamical system (LDS). Each recording session is treated as a noisy, partial observation of this latent state, specified by a known measurement matrix that selects the neurons recorded in that session. The model parameters, including the functional coupling matrix that describes interactions between all neurons, are estimated from the collection of partial recordings using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

This work addresses a significant practical limitation in experimental neuroscience, where technologies like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record from a fraction of a neural circuit at a time. The proposed "stitching" approach is novel in its application to inferring functional dynamics across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. This differentiates it from prior work that infers connectivity from single, fully-observed (albeit smaller) populations [6, 8] or reconstructs anatomical connectivity from subsampled data [15]. It also builds upon the theoretical foundation of system identification from partial observations [21, 23], providing a practical demonstration in a challenging real-world domain.

**Quality**

The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem within the LDS framework is appropriate and principled. The use of the EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing for parameter inference is a standard and correct approach for this model class. The evaluation methodology is a key strength. On synthetic data, the authors demonstrate that the method can recover ground-truth coupling parameters and noise correlations for neuron pairs that were never observed together. For the real calcium imaging data, the creation of a "fictional imaging scenario" is an effective strategy for validation. By splitting a fully-observed dataset into partial sessions, the authors can compare their method's performance against a well-defined upper bound (a model trained on the complete, simultaneous data) and a lower bound (a "naive" model that cannot infer cross-session couplings). The results clearly show the proposed method substantially outperforming the lower bound and approaching the performance of the upper bound, lending strong support to the authors' claims. The work is presented as a complete piece of research, with a clear method and thorough validation.

**Clarity**

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem with a concrete experimental example. The methods section provides a concise and complete description of the model and the fitting procedure, sufficient for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work. The results are presented logically, starting with an intuitive explanation and a demonstration on synthetic data before moving to the more complex real-world datasets. The figures are clear, well-labeled, and effectively support the main claims of the paper. Figure 2, in particular, provides a very clear illustration of the method's ability to recover the underlying connectivity structure in the simulated case.

**Originality**

The primary contribution is the novel application of a known statistical model (LDS) to solve a pressing problem in systems neuroscience. While the underlying components (LDS, EM algorithm) are not new, their combination to "stitch" non-simultaneous functional recordings is an original and creative idea. The paper does an excellent job of situating this contribution within the context of related literature, clearly distinguishing it from previous methods for inferring connectivity or handling subsampled data. The problem itself—of combining partial functional recordings—has not been extensively addressed, and this paper presents a compelling first solution.

**Significance**

The potential impact of this work is high. If experimentalists can reliably combine partial recordings to model larger circuits, it could fundamentally change how population dynamics are studied. This method could enable the construction of functional models of circuits that are an order of magnitude larger than what is currently possible to record simultaneously. This would allow researchers to test theories of neural computation at a more appropriate scale. The demonstration that the method works well on real calcium imaging data from both anesthetized and awake animals suggests it is not just a theoretical curiosity but a potentially practical tool for the neuroscience community. The ideas presented are likely to be built upon by others, for example by incorporating more biophysically realistic nonlinear models or developing principled approaches to experimental design (e.g., optimizing the degree of overlap between sessions).

---

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Addresses a significant and timely problem at the intersection of machine learning and experimental neuroscience.
*   Presents a novel and elegant solution by framing the problem in a latent variable modeling context.
*   The technical approach is sound, and the experimental validation is rigorous and convincing, employing both synthetic data and a well-designed analysis of real-world data.
*   The paper is written with exceptional clarity, making a potentially complex topic accessible.
*   The results are strong and demonstrate a clear advantage over naive approaches, with performance approaching a theoretical upper bound.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The model relies on a linear Gaussian assumption, which is a simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this and show it works for slow calcium signals, the limitations and applicability to other data types (e.g., spikes) are not fully explored.
*   The method assumes the underlying circuit properties (i.e., the coupling matrix A) are stationary across recording sessions. This may not hold in practice, as sessions can be separated by minutes or hours, during which learning or changes in brain state could occur. This critical assumption is not discussed.
*   The analysis is limited to stitching two sessions. While the framework generalizes, its performance and computational scalability as the number of sessions increases are not investigated.
*   The paper notes that some overlap between recorded populations was "critical" but does not systematically investigate how performance depends on the amount of overlap. This is a key parameter for anyone wishing to apply this method experimentally.

Overall, the strengths of this paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses. It introduces a valuable and original computational technique to address a fundamental experimental limitation. The identified weaknesses are primarily avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws in the current contribution. The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance.
