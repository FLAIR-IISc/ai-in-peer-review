This paper presents a method for “stitching” together calcium imaging data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit to infer correlated dynamics. The core idea – leveraging the strong coupling between nearby neurons to estimate activity beyond the scope of a single imaging session – is conceptually sound and addresses a practical limitation of current neuroimaging techniques. The approach utilizes a linear Gaussian dynamical system, fitted via Expectation-Maximization, to model population activity and predict correlations. The paper demonstrates the method's efficacy on both simulated and real data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

While the premise is compelling and the results appear promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet persistent, issues that collectively detract from its overall quality. These range from imprecise language and lacking rigor in justification of modeling choices to formatting inconsistencies and a somewhat inflated sense of novelty. 

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses a relevant problem:** The limitation of imaging only a fraction of a neural circuit is a significant bottleneck in systems neuroscience.
* **Conceptually sound approach:** The idea of leveraging overlapping observations and strong local connectivity is logical.
* **Demonstration on real data:** Application to experimental data, even with a fictional imaging scenario, adds credibility.
* **Clear presentation of the method:** The mathematical formulation is relatively straightforward and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Lack of Rigor in Model Choice:** The justification for choosing a linear Gaussian dynamical system is weak. The authors state it's for “computational tractability,” but don’t adequately discuss the potential limitations of this simplification given the known non-linearities and non-Gaussianity inherent in neural data (acknowledged in the introduction but dismissed too readily). A more thorough discussion of how these simplifications might affect the results is needed.
* **Imprecise Language:** The term “stitching” feels overly colloquial for a rigorous scientific paper. While evocative, it doesn’t clearly convey the statistical nature of the method. Similarly, statements like “Surprisingly, the answer is yes” in the introduction are unnecessary and unprofessional.
* **Insufficient Detail on Data Preprocessing:** The description of calcium imaging data preprocessing (Section 2.2) is surprisingly sparse. Specifically, the method of ROI definition is not detailed. Were ROIs manually curated? If so, what criteria were used? This information is crucial for reproducibility. The filtering details (high-pass with 1s time constant) are provided, but justification for this choice is absent.
* **Overstated Novelty:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the novelty of inferring connections between *non-simultaneously* observed neurons. However, previous work (e.g., [12]) *does* address this problem, albeit with different assumptions. The authors’ dismissal of these works feels dismissive and doesn’t fully acknowledge the contributions of the field. The claim that [12] requires a “strong assumption” is a subjective assessment.
* **Inconsistent Notation:**  The notation is occasionally inconsistent. For example, *Q* is described as the covariance of ηt, but is later used in the Lyapunov equation as a diagonal matrix. This lack of consistency can be confusing.
* **Formatting Issues:** Several minor formatting errors exist, including inconsistent spacing and numbering.  For instance, equations (1) and (2) are not aligned consistently with the surrounding text.
* **Limited Discussion of Limitations:** While acknowledging some limitations, the paper doesn't adequately address potential failure modes of the method. For example, what happens if the overlap between imaging sessions is minimal? What is the sensitivity of the method to noise in the measurements?
* **Poor Figure Labeling**: Figure 2b's y-axis label is cut off. Figure 3a’s labels are small and difficult to read.

**Arguments for/against acceptance:**

**Pro:**

* The method addresses a real and important problem in systems neuroscience.
* The approach is conceptually sound and demonstrates promising results.
* The code is available (as stated on the authors’ website), which facilitates reproducibility.

**Con:**

* The technical justification for the modeling choices is weak.
* The presentation contains numerous minor flaws in language, notation, and formatting.
* The authors overstate the novelty of their approach.
* The discussion of limitations is insufficient.

**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potentially significant contribution of this work, the numerous minor flaws detract from its overall quality. While the method itself appears promising, the lack of rigor in justification, imprecise language, and formatting inconsistencies raise concerns about the authors’ attention to detail.  I believe the paper, in its current form, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NIPS. Significant revisions, addressing the points outlined above, are necessary. I recommend **rejection**.  A revised version, with a more thorough justification of the modeling choices, more precise language, improved formatting, and a more nuanced discussion of related work, would be worth reconsidering.
