This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) and treat each recording session as a partial observation of this underlying system, defined by a session-specific observation matrix. The authors demonstrate that by fitting this single encompassing model using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm across all partial recordings, it is possible to "stitch" the data together. The key contribution is showing that this allows for the accurate prediction of statistics, such as noise correlations, between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The approach is validated on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex using a clever "fictional imaging" evaluation paradigm.

This work addresses a significant and practical problem in systems neuroscience. While many methods exist for inferring functional connectivity from simultaneously recorded neural activity (e.g., Pillow et al., NIPS 2008; Mishchenko et al., AoAS 2011), the problem of combining non-simultaneous recordings has been largely unaddressed. The paper correctly distinguishes its contribution from related work, such as methods that assume a relationship between stimulus and noise correlations (Wohrer et al., 2010) or those focused on different sub-sampling regimes (Pakman et al., 2013). The proposed solution is elegant and builds upon a well-established theoretical foundation regarding the observability of dynamical systems.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience. The ability to computationally combine partial recordings to build a model of a larger, more complete circuit could be transformative for the field. It provides a clear path to move beyond the physical constraints of current recording technologies, potentially enabling data-driven models of entire microcircuits. The experimental design implications mentioned in the discussion (e.g., trading off overlap vs. coverage) are highly relevant.

2.  **Principled and Elegant Method:** The formulation of the problem within a latent LDS framework is clean, intuitive, and statistically sound. Using a different, known observation matrix `C_k` for each session is a natural way to model the experimental setup. The use of a standard, tractable model (LDS) and fitting procedure (EM with Kalman smoothing) makes the approach accessible and demonstrates the core principle effectively without unnecessary complexity.

3.  **Rigorous and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation strategy is a major strength. On simulated data, the authors show successful recovery of both coupling parameters and, more importantly, noise correlations. The "fictional imaging scenario" for the real experimental data is particularly clever. By taking a fully-observed dataset and artificially splitting it, they create a scenario with a well-defined ground truth (the model fit to the full data), allowing for a quantitative assessment of performance. The inclusion of clear upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds provides essential context and convincingly demonstrates the method's efficacy.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the methods are described with sufficient detail for understanding, and the results are presented logically and supported by clear figures. The intuition behind why the method should work is also explained well in the results section.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** A critical detail mentioned in Section 2.3 is that the method "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" when applied to real data. This is a crucial finding that has significant practical implications, as it suggests that some "anchor" neurons recorded across sessions are necessary to link the different recordings into a common reference frame. This point is somewhat understated in the main text and deserves a more prominent discussion. An analysis of how performance degrades as the percentage of overlap decreases would be a valuable addition and would provide direct guidance for experimental design.

2.  **Model Simplicity and its Limitations:** The choice of a linear Gaussian model is justified for its tractability and serves well for a proof-of-concept. However, neural dynamics are known to be non-linear and non-Gaussian (e.g., spiking activity). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, which is good. The paper could be strengthened by briefly discussing the conditions under which this linear approximation is likely to succeed or fail. For instance, is performance dependent on the overall strength of correlations in the data? The fact that performance was better on the more correlated "awake" dataset suggests this might be the case.

3.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The model implicitly assumes that the underlying circuit dynamics (i.e., the parameters A, B, Q) are stationary across the different recording sessions. These sessions could be separated by significant amounts of time, during which plasticity, changes in animal arousal/attentional state, or other non-stationarities could alter the functional connectivity. This is a strong assumption that should be explicitly stated and discussed as a potential limitation.

4.  **Scalability:** The computational complexity of the E-step (Kalman smoothing) scales as O(T*N^3) with the total number of neurons N. While the authors mention using online EM to improve efficiency, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the practical computational limits of the approach. How would it scale to stitching, for instance, 10 sessions of 100 neurons each, resulting in a total population of N~1000?

**Overall Assessment:**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, important, and well-executed contribution. The strengths—addressing a critical problem with an elegant method and validating it with a rigorous and clever evaluation scheme—far outweigh the weaknesses. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, highly original in its application, and significant for both the machine learning and computational neuroscience communities. It opens up new avenues for analyzing large-scale neural data and designing future experiments. I strongly recommend acceptance.
