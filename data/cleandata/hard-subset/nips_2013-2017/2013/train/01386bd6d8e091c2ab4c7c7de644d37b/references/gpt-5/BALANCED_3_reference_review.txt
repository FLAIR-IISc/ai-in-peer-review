Summary
The paper addresses an important practical problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations from multiple partial recordings that do not cover all neurons simultaneously. The authors model the full neural population with a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with inputs, treat unobserved neurons as latent variables, and fit parameters via EM while accommodating session-specific selection matrices Ck that specify which neurons were recorded in each session. They argue that with two (or more) partially overlapping recordings, the model can “stitch” views and recover cross-couplings between neurons never recorded simultaneously. On synthetic data with known ground truth, the approach recovers coupling structure and predicts noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs. On two calcium imaging datasets (anesthetized and awake mouse S1), they create “fictional sessions” by splitting a single simultaneous recording into two overlapping subsets and show that the stitched LDS predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs substantially better than a naive baseline and close to a “fully observed” upper bound. They also report cross-prediction of activity for unobserved subsets.

Relation to prior work
The work is positioned relative to: inference of functional connectivity from calcium imaging and spikes (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al., Pillow et al.), latent GLMs for partially observed populations (Pillow & Latham), and methods relying on stimulus-noise monotonicity (Wohrer et al.). The central technique—fitting an LDS with missing observations via EM/Kalman smoothing—is standard in system identification and time series (Dempster et al.; Katayama) and has been widely used for neural population modeling (e.g., GPFA/FA/LDS variants). The novelty lies primarily in the application to stitching across partial recordings and in the evaluation on 2p imaging data, rather than in algorithmic advances.

Strengths
- Important and timely problem with clear relevance to current imaging constraints.
- Simple, principled model and inference; well-motivated use of LDS with session-specific observation masks.
- Convincing synthetic demonstrations showing recovery of cross-couplings and noise correlations; better correlation prediction than parameter recovery is a useful observation.
- Real-data analyses across population sizes; demonstrating robustness and the benefit of overlap.
- Discussion acknowledges limitations (overlap, linearity, potential for better priors/regularization, unobserved inputs).

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is limited: this is essentially a standard LDS with missing data and EM. No new inference, identifiability analysis, or theoretical conditions are provided.
- Real-data evaluation uses “fictional sessions” carved from simultaneous recordings. This likely inflates stitching performance relative to truly non-simultaneous measurements (shared recording context, stable preparation, identical stimulus drive). The authors note poor performance without overlap but do not quantify performance under realistic cross-session shifts (e.g., recording separate fields-of-view or time-separated sessions).
- Baselines are weak. The “naive” model is a strawman; stronger and more appropriate comparators include: (i) low-rank latent factor models (FA/GPFA) shared across sessions with session-specific loadings; (ii) LDS/GPFA with multi-view alignment (CCA/Procrustes) across sessions; (iii) LDS with low-rank latent common input plus sparse local couplings; and (iv) subspace ID methods that explicitly handle missing data. This is crucial for establishing significance.
- Model assumptions are strong and under-explored: linear-Gaussian dynamics on calcium fluorescence, diagonal Q and R, a single offset d, stationarity across sessions, and accurate cell identity mapping in Ck. No analysis of robustness to registration errors, session-wise gain/drift differences, or correlated measurement noise/neuropil contamination.
- Limited guidance on how much overlap is needed and why. The text mentions that overlap is “critical,” but lacks systematic trade-off analysis (overlap fraction vs. reconstruction error), nor identifiability/observability conditions.
- Scalability/regularization: estimating a full N×N A risks overfitting, especially at larger N; sparsity/low-rank priors are suggested but not integrated or validated. Implementation details (initialization, convergence, complexity) are sparse. Minor clarity issues (e.g., undefined hk in inference section).

Quality
- The approach is technically sound for LDS/EM and the synthetic experiments are appropriate. However, claims about predicting non-simultaneous correlations on real data are weakened by the fictional-session design and limited baselines. The paper would benefit from more rigorous robustness analyses and better controls.

Clarity
- Generally clear and well organized. Some details are missing (initialization, computational cost, parameter constraints, explicit handling of changing Ck per time step). Consider clarifying the evaluation protocol (train/test split) and fixing notation issues.

Originality
- Conceptual framing (stitching partial recordings) is appealing, but the core method is a standard application of LDS with missing data. The paper currently lacks theoretical or algorithmic novelty.

Significance
- Potentially high practical impact if validated on truly non-simultaneous recordings and with stronger baselines/robustness. As is, significance is moderate due to evaluation design and limited novelty.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical analysis (or at least explicit conditions) for identifiability/observability with partial views: required overlap, controllability/observability conditions, and ambiguity up to linear transforms; quantify how overlap resolves these.
- Evaluate on truly non-simultaneous datasets (different fields-of-view or sessions/days) with known overlapping cells, to better emulate the intended use case. Quantify sensitivity to session-wise gain shifts and drift; include per-session affine observation parameters.
- Add stronger baselines: FA/GPFA with session-specific loadings; LDS with low-rank latent common input; multi-view latent alignment (CCA/Procrustes) approaches; subspace ID with missing data.
- Explore regularization (sparse or low-rank structure in A, group priors) with cross-validated hyperparameters; report effects on coupling and correlation prediction.
- Relax noise assumptions: allow correlated R and/or low-dimensional latent inputs to capture common drive; test robustness to neuropil contamination.
- Clarify the train/test protocol for correlation prediction and activity cross-prediction; ensure all metrics are computed on held-out data.
- Consider spike deconvolution or a more realistic observation model (calcium convolution) to disentangle biophysical sources of variability.

Arguments for acceptance
- Addresses an important bottleneck in population imaging.
- Simple, general framework that many labs could adopt.
- Strong synthetic validation; promising real-data results within the fictional-session setup.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty beyond standard LDS with missing data.
- Real-data evaluation does not reflect truly non-simultaneous recordings; weak baselines.
- Lack of theoretical conditions, robustness analyses, and regularization for scalability.

Recommendation: Weak reject
This is a well-motivated and promising direction with clear practical relevance. However, the current submission lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to meet the NeurIPS bar. With stronger baselines, realistic cross-session evaluations, theoretical/empirical analysis of overlap requirements, and regularized modeling, the work could become a compelling contribution.
