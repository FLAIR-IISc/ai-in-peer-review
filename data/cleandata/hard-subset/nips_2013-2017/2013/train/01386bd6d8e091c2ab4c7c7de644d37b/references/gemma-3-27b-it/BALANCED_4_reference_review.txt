This paper presents a method for inferring the dynamics of a neural population from multiple partial recordings of the same circuit. The core idea is to model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system, leveraging the assumption that nearby neurons are strongly coupled. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from the mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

This is a well-motivated and technically sound paper addressing an important problem in neuroscience. The increasing availability of 2-photon imaging data, coupled with the limitations in imaging volume, makes the problem of stitching together partial recordings highly relevant. The authors correctly identify existing work that addresses related problems (connectivity inference, subsampled observations) but convincingly argues why those approaches are not directly applicable to their setting. The linear Gaussian dynamical system chosen as a model is a reasonable starting point, offering computational tractability while still capturing essential features of neural dynamics. The use of the EM algorithm for parameter estimation is standard and well-explained. 

The strengths of the paper are numerous. The theoretical justification is clear, and the method is well-described. The simulation results are convincing, showing that the method can accurately recover both the coupling matrix and noise correlations. Crucially, the application to real data demonstrates that the method is not just theoretically appealing but also practical and effective. The comparisons to the "naive" and "fully observed" models provide a good benchmark for evaluating performance, and the results are consistently favorable for the proposed stitching method. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as the linearity assumption and the need for some overlap between imaging sessions.

However, there are also some weaknesses that could be addressed. While the choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is justified for tractability, it would be valuable to discuss the potential impact of this simplification on the results.  The paper doesn’t fully explore the robustness of the method to violations of the Gaussian assumption, or to non-linear dynamics. A brief discussion of these limitations would strengthen the paper. 

The description of the experimental data could be more detailed. For example, providing more information about the imaging setup (e.g., laser power, objective magnification) and the data preprocessing steps (e.g., motion correction, artifact rejection) would improve reproducibility. While the authors mention the manual ROI definition, a quantitative assessment of ROI quality (e.g., signal-to-noise ratio) would be beneficial.

The discussion section is strong, but could be extended to address potential applications of the method beyond correlation inference. Could this approach be used to predict the response of the full population to novel stimuli? Or to identify key neurons that drive population dynamics? Exploring these possibilities would further highlight the significance of the work. 

Finally, the figures, while generally clear, could be improved. For example, Figure 3a would benefit from a more informative color scale and clearer labeling of the different methods. Figure 4a could benefit from error bars.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses an important and timely problem in neuroscience.
*   Technically sound and well-motivated approach.
*   Convincing results on both simulated and real data.
*   Clear and well-written paper.
*   Demonstrates a practical method for leveraging incomplete experimental data.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Relies on a simplifying linear Gaussian assumption.
*   Limited exploration of the method's robustness to model violations.
*   Experimental details could be more comprehensive.
*   Discussion could be expanded to explore broader applications.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. The method presented is novel, well-justified, and demonstrably effective. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically a discussion of the impact of the linearity assumption, and expansion of the experimental detail and discussion.
