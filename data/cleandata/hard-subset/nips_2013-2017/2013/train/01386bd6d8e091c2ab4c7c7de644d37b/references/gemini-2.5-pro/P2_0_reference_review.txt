**Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"**

**Summary**
This paper presents a computational method to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings. The core problem is that experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record a subset of neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose to "stitch" together these partial observations by modeling the entire population's activity as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each recording session is treated as a noisy observation of a known subset of the latent state variables. The model parameters are learned using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors validate their approach on both simulated data, where they successfully recover ground-truth coupling parameters and noise correlations, and on real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, they create a "fictional imaging scenario" by splitting a fully-observed dataset, allowing them to benchmark their method against a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound. The results show that their stitching method can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, performing vastly better than the naive approach and approaching the performance of the ideal, fully-observed model.

**Evaluation**

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound and the claims are well-supported by the experiments.
*   **Strengths:** The experimental design is a major strength. The use of both simulated data (with known ground truth) and real neural data provides a comprehensive validation. The "fictional imaging scenario" is a particularly clever and effective way to quantify performance on real data, where the ground truth is inaccessible. The inclusion of clear upper and lower bounds (the "fully observed" and "naive" models) provides crucial context and convincingly demonstrates the method's efficacy. The analysis is thorough, examining not just the recovery of noise correlations but also the underlying coupling matrix and the ability to predict the activity of unobserved neurons.
*   **Weaknesses:** The primary technical weakness is the choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system. The authors acknowledge that this model does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian nature of neural activity. While they demonstrate that it works well for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), the paper would be stronger with a more detailed discussion of the potential pitfalls of this simplification. For instance, are the recovered "functional couplings" simply reflecting slow, shared modulations common in calcium imaging data, rather than faster, spike-level interactions? While extending the model is future work, a more critical assessment of what the current linear model is likely capturing would be valuable.

**Clarity: Very High**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized.
*   The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, making the problem immediately accessible.
*   The model (Equations 1 and 2) is described precisely and concisely. The connection between the abstract model and the experimental reality (the structure of the measurement matrix `Ck`) is made explicit.
*   The experimental setup, for both simulated and real data, is explained with sufficient detail to understand the results. The figures are clear, well-labeled, and effectively support the main claims of the paper. Figure 2, in particular, provides a compelling visual summary of the method's success on synthetic data.

**Originality: High**
While the underlying machinery (LDS with missing data, EM algorithm) is standard in machine learning, its application to this specific problem in neuroscience is novel and creative.
*   The paper clearly situates its contribution with respect to related work. It correctly points out that previous methods for inferring functional connectivity do not address the problem of non-simultaneous recordings, or make strong, often untenable assumptions (e.g., relating noise correlations to stimulus correlations).
*   The core original idea is the framing of the problem: treating non-simultaneous recordings of a single circuit as a unified missing data problem within a shared latent dynamical model. This conceptual leap is the paper's main contribution and distinguishes it from prior work which would typically analyze each recording session in isolation.

**Significance: Very High**
The potential impact of this work is substantial.
*   It addresses a fundamental and practical limitation in experimental neuroscience. The inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously is a major bottleneck for understanding neural computation. This paper provides a principled and validated tool to help overcome this barrier, effectively expanding the reach of current experimental technologies.
*   The method could enable researchers to build more complete functional maps of neural circuits than was previously possible. The ability to estimate noise correlations—a key feature of population coding—across the entire circuit, even between non-simultaneously observed neurons, is a powerful capability.
*   The work raises interesting questions for experimental design, such as optimizing the trade-off between the number of unique neurons recorded and the degree of overlap between sessions. This could guide future experiments to maximize the information gained.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **High Impact:** The paper addresses a critical, real-world problem in neuroscience with an elegant and effective computational solution. It has the potential to be widely used by experimentalists.
2.  **Novel Formulation:** The conceptual framing of stitching non-simultaneous recordings via a latent dynamical system is novel and powerful.
3.  **Strong Validation:** The evaluation is rigorous, employing both simulated and real data, and using a clever "fictional scenario" with well-justified upper and lower performance bounds.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, making a sophisticated idea easy to understand.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Model Simplicity:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While effective for the stated goal, it limits the interpretability of the learned model parameters as direct representations of neural circuitry.
2.  **Limited Algorithmic Novelty:** The core machine learning method (EM for LDS) is not new. The contribution is in the application and problem formulation, not in developing a new algorithm.

**Conclusion:**
This is an excellent paper that presents a valuable and well-validated method for a significant problem in computational neuroscience. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem and the rigor of the evaluation, far outweigh the weaknesses. While the model itself is simple, its application is novel and the results are convincing. This work represents a clear advance that will likely be of great interest to both neuroscientists and machine learning practitioners working on neural data. I strongly recommend acceptance.
