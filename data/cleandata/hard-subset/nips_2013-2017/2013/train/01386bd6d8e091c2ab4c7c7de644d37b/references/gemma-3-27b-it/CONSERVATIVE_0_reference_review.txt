This paper addresses the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea – leveraging overlapping recordings to estimate the dynamics of a larger, unobserved population – is intuitively appealing and relevant to the limitations of current calcium imaging technology. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, and demonstrate its ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons in both simulated and experimental data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-established, and the mathematical formulation is presented concisely. The inclusion of both simulation results and experimental validation is a strength. The authors acknowledge limitations – specifically the simplifying assumption of a linear Gaussian model – and appropriately frame their work as a first step towards more sophisticated approaches. 

However, despite these positives, I remain unconvinced of the novelty and significance of this work as presented. The core concept of inferring latent variables from incomplete observations is hardly new, and the application of latent dynamical systems to neural data, while valuable, is also well-established (as the authors acknowledge in the related work section). The contribution here appears to be more of an application of existing techniques to a specific, albeit practical, problem – combining partially overlapping calcium imaging datasets. While this is useful, it doesn’t represent a substantial advancement over existing methodologies. 

My primary concern lies with the limited validation against stronger baselines. The comparison to a "naive" approach, which simply averages parameters from separate session fits, is a weak benchmark. A more rigorous evaluation would involve comparing the performance of the proposed method against state-of-the-art techniques for dimensionality reduction and dynamical systems inference, particularly methods specifically designed for handling missing data (beyond simply fitting separate models). The “fully observed” model serves as a useful upper bound, but doesn't represent an achievable alternative method.

Furthermore, the reported performance gains, while statistically significant in some cases, appear modest. The correlation of 0.73 for recovered couplings in the simulation, and the similar figures reported for the real data, suggests that a significant amount of information is still lost during the “stitching” process. The authors briefly mention potential improvements through more sophisticated models and priors, but these are not explored in detail. The claim that the method accurately *predicts* correlations is also overstated; the method infers a model *that explains* observed correlations. True predictive power would require demonstrating the ability to accurately forecast future correlations based on past observations.

The justification for choosing a linear Gaussian model, beyond its computational tractability, is also weak. The authors acknowledge its limitations in capturing the complex nonlinearities of neural data, but fail to demonstrate how sensitive the results are to this assumption. A brief exploration of nonlinear dynamical systems, even with simplified parameterizations, would strengthen the argument for the robustness of the findings.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

* Addresses a practically relevant problem in neuroscience – combining limited calcium imaging data.
* Clearly written and well-organized.
* Provides both simulation and experimental validation.
* Demonstrates a proof-of-concept for inferring correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* Limited novelty – the core concept is not new.
* Insufficient comparison to strong baselines.
* Modest performance gains.
* Limited exploration of model assumptions (linearity, Gaussianity).
* Overstated claims regarding “prediction” of correlations.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak reject**. While the work is technically sound and clearly presented, it lacks the novelty and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. The authors demonstrate a useful application of existing techniques, but fall short of presenting a substantial advancement in the field. Addressing the concerns regarding baseline comparisons, model assumptions, and performance evaluation would significantly strengthen the paper, but as it stands, it does not meet the high standards of NIPS.
