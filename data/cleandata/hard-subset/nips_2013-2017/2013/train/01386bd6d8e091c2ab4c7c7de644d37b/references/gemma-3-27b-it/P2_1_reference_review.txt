## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for “stitching” together partial recordings of neural activity to infer the dynamics of a larger neural population than can be observed at any single time. The authors frame this as a latent dynamical system estimation problem, leveraging a linear-Gaussian model and Expectation-Maximization (EM) for parameter learning. They demonstrate the efficacy of their approach, both on simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing successful recovery of coupling matrices and, importantly, accurate prediction of noise correlations between neurons never simultaneously recorded.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the existing literature on neural connectivity inference from calcium imaging and electrophysiology. It distinguishes itself from previous work by explicitly addressing the problem of inferring connections *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons – a crucial step towards reconstructing activity patterns in larger circuits.  The authors acknowledge relevant prior work (e.g., [12, 13, 14, 15]) and explain why these approaches are not directly applicable to their setting.  The paper builds upon the well-established framework of latent dynamical systems and EM algorithms, but applies it to a novel problem with significant practical implications.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty & Significance:** The problem addressed is a significant bottleneck in neuroscience research.  Current imaging technology limits our ability to observe entire neural circuits, and this paper offers a compelling method to overcome this limitation. The ability to predict noise correlations is particularly valuable, as these correlations are often crucial for understanding neural computation.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The authors provide results on both simulated and real data, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. The comparison to both an “ideal” fully observed model and a naive baseline provides a strong benchmark for evaluating performance. The results on real data are particularly encouraging.
* **Clear Methodology:** The methods section is well-written and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The use of a linear-Gaussian model is a reasonable starting point for tractability, and the authors acknowledge its limitations.
* **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** Framing the problem within a latent dynamical system provides a principled statistical framework. The connection to existing theory on system identification (referenced in the discussion) strengthens the work.
* **Well-written and organized:** The paper is generally easy to follow, and the figures are informative.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplifications:** The use of a linear-Gaussian model is a major simplification of neural activity, which is inherently nonlinear and non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this, it’s unclear how robust the method is to deviations from these assumptions. The paper could benefit from a discussion of how more complex models could be incorporated (though computational cost is likely a barrier).
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices (e.g., regularization strength, initial conditions for EM).  This is particularly important for the EM algorithm, which can be prone to getting stuck in local optima.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The finding that some overlap between imaging sessions is *critical* for performance is a limitation. The paper could explore methods for mitigating this requirement, or for dealing with scenarios where overlap is minimal. The requirement of 60% overlap seems somewhat high.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention using mini-batch EM for efficiency, the computational cost of the method is not explicitly discussed. Scaling to truly large populations (thousands of neurons) could be a significant challenge.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a crucial limitation in current neuroscience research.
* Presents a novel and statistically sound approach.
* Demonstrates strong performance on both simulated and real data.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.
* Offers a significant advance in our ability to infer neural circuit dynamics.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on strong simplifying assumptions (linear-Gaussian model).
* Sensitivity to parameter choices and potential for local optima in EM.
* Requires substantial overlap between imaging sessions.
* Computational cost may limit scalability to large populations.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in neuroscience. While the method relies on simplifying assumptions, the authors demonstrate its effectiveness and provide a solid theoretical justification.  The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is clearly written.  The weaknesses are not fatal, and could be addressed in future work.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the following points:

1.  **Discussion of Model Limitations:** Expand the discussion of the limitations of the linear-Gaussian model and potential strategies for incorporating more complex models.
2.  **Parameter Sensitivity Analysis:**  Include a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the results to key parameter choices.
3.  **Overlap Investigation:** Explore the relationship between overlap percentage and performance more thoroughly.  Consider discussing strategies for handling low-overlap scenarios.
4. **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational complexity of the method, and potential strategies for scaling to larger populations.



**Confidence:** 8/10.  The paper is generally well-executed, but the potential impact hinges on the robustness of the method to deviations from its simplifying assumptions.
