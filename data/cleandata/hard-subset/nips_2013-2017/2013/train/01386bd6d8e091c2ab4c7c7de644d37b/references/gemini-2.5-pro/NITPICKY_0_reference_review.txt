**Summary:**
This paper introduces a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous, partial recordings of that population. The core idea is to model the entire circuit as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is treated as a partial observation of the latent state of this complete system. The authors use the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to fit the model parameters (e.g., the functional connectivity matrix) from the collection of partial datasets. The method is validated on both simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. For the real data, they create a "fictional imaging scenario" by splitting a fully-observed recording into two non-simultaneous, overlapping subsets. The main result is that this "stitching" method can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, significantly outperforming a naive approach and approaching the performance of a model trained on the complete, simultaneous data.

**Strengths:**
*   **Significance & Originality:** The paper addresses a crucial and highly practical problem in systems neuroscience. Overcoming the field-of-view limitation of current recording technologies is a major challenge. The proposed "stitching" framework is a novel and potentially high-impact approach to computationally extend the effective scale of neural recordings.
*   **Methodological Clarity:** The problem is well-motivated, and the proposed solution is described clearly. Using an LDS as a base model is sensible for a proof-of-concept due to its tractability.
*   **Validation Strategy:** The use of a "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is a clever way to benchmark the method in the absence of ground truth. Comparing against both a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound provides a clear and convincing demonstration of the method's efficacy in predicting correlations.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the promising direction, the paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological oversimplifications, troubling empirical findings that are not adequately addressed, and a general lack of care in its presentation.

1.  **Methodological Concerns & Oversimplifications:**
    *   **Model Mismatch:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a severe simplification of true neural dynamics. The authors acknowledge that the model doesn't capture the "nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade" of neural processes but proceed nonetheless. While it may capture pairwise correlations, this fundamental mismatch casts doubt on the interpretability of the learned parameters (e.g., the coupling matrix `A`) and the model's generalizability. Applying this model directly to filtered fluorescence signals, rather than attempting to deconvolve spikes, further conflates neural dynamics with calcium indicator dynamics.
    *   **Assumption of Diagonal Noise:** The model assumes the innovation noise covariance `Q` is diagonal. This is a strong and biologically questionable assumption, as it ignores the effects of common input from unobserved sources, a key driver of correlated variability. The authors mention in passing that the model *could* be extended to handle this, but they do not do so, which is a notable omission.
    *   **Critical Dependence on Overlap:** A crucial detail is buried in the methods section: "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a major finding that severely weakens the paper's central claim of stitching together non-simultaneous populations. It suggests the method relies critically on having "anchor" neurons to align the dynamics across sessions, and is not a general solution for stitching disjoint sets. The degree of performance as a function of overlap is a critical parameter that is not explored. The title and introduction, which suggest the method works for entirely separate populations, are therefore misleading.

2.  **Troubling Empirical Results:**
    *   **Poor Parameter Recovery Scaling:** At the end of Section 3.2, the authors report that the correlation between the stitched couplings and the fully observed couplings "drops with increasing population size." This is an alarming result for a method intended to scale up to large circuits. The authors offer a brief, speculative explanation ("possible near degeneracy of parameters") but do not investigate this critical scaling failure. A method whose parameter estimates degrade as the problem size increases is of limited practical use.
    *   **Data Selection for Figures:** Figure 3, a main result figure, displays results from the "50 most correlated neurons." This constitutes cherry-picking and may not be representative of the method's performance on a typical, more sparsely correlated population. While summary results are shown later, the primary illustrative example should be on a more representative dataset (e.g., a random sample).

3.  **Lack of Meticulousness and Clarity:**
    *   **Formatting and Citations:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of professional polish. The reference list is poorly formatted, with missing information (e.g., [21] Katayama, [24] Linderman), inconsistent author formatting, and stray punctuation.
    *   **Confusing Notation:** The notation in the M-step update equations is cumbersome (e.g., `σkj`). The formulation of the observation model in Eq. (2), `ykt = Ck(xkt + d + εt)`, is non-standard. Typically, observation noise is additive *after* the projection (`ykt = Ckxkt + εt`). While possibly equivalent here since `Ck` is a selection matrix, it is written confusingly.
    *   **Footnote Clarity:** Footnote 1 on page 4 is confusing. It makes a claim about the naive approach over-estimating correlations "within each view" and attributes this to "biases resulting from averaging couplings," but this is not clearly explained or substantiated by the figures.

**Decision:**
I recommend **rejection**.

While the paper addresses an important problem with a novel idea, the execution and presentation are not up to the standards of NIPS. The reliance on a simplistic linear-Gaussian model is a significant limitation, but more importantly, the work is undermined by its own empirical results. The admission that the method fails without overlapping neurons and the finding that parameter estimation quality degrades with population size are critical flaws that contradict the paper's primary motivation of scaling to large, potentially disjointly-recorded, circuits. These issues, combined with numerous minor flaws in presentation and a general lack of thoroughness, lead me to conclude that the paper is not ready for publication. The core idea is valuable, but the authors need to use more realistic models, be more transparent and thorough in their analysis of the method's limitations (especially regarding overlap and scaling), and significantly polish the manuscript.
