**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces a statistical method to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, non-simultaneous recordings. The core problem addressed is a fundamental limitation in neuroscience: experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging can only record from a fraction of the neurons in a circuit at any given time. The authors propose an elegant solution by modeling the entire neural population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). For each partial recording session, the observed neurons are treated as a noisy measurement of a subset of the full system's state variables. By fitting a single LDS model across all recording sessions—each with its own "measurement matrix" selecting the observed neurons—the method can "stitch" together the information to infer the full coupling matrix and predict joint statistics, such as noise correlations, even between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors validate this approach on simulated data with known ground truth and, more compellingly, on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex by creating a "fictional imaging scenario" where they pretend a fully observed dataset was recorded in two overlapping partial sessions. The results show that the stitching method accurately predicts noise correlations, significantly outperforming a naive approach and approaching the performance of a model trained on the complete, simultaneous data.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a highly significant and practical problem in experimental and computational neuroscience. The idea of computationally overcoming physical recording limitations is powerful. While the underlying mathematical principle (system identification from partial observations) has theoretical roots (e.g., Takens' theorem, subspace identification), its application in this context, particularly for stitching non-simultaneous neural recordings to recover noise correlations, is novel, timely, and of high interest to the NIPS community and neuroscientists.

2.  **Methodological Elegance:** The framing of the problem within the LDS framework is simple, intuitive, and effective. Treating the unobserved neurons in any given session as latent states and using session-specific observation matrices is a clean and principled way to combine the disparate datasets. This provides a strong foundation that can be extended with more complex models in the future.

3.  **Convincing and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design for validation is a major strength. The "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is an excellent way to benchmark the method's performance. The use of clear upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds provides a compelling quantitative demonstration of the method's efficacy. The ability to recover noise correlations (Fig 3c,d), a critical quantity for understanding population coding, is particularly impressive.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained concisely, and the results are presented logically. The intuition behind how the method works—inferring the influence of latent neurons from their "shadows" on the observed dynamics—is conveyed effectively.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The paper states, "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial finding with significant practical implications, yet it is mentioned almost as an aside. The theoretical intuition provided suggests that overlap might not be strictly necessary to resolve the linear ambiguity. The empirical failure without overlap suggests that for noisy, real-world data, "anchor" neurons present across sessions are critical for aligning the inferred latent dynamics into a common reference frame. This point deserves a more thorough investigation and discussion. A quantitative analysis (e.g., performance as a function of overlap percentage) would be invaluable for guiding future experimental design.

2.  **Limitations of the Linear-Gaussian Model:** The authors rightly acknowledge that the LDS is a simplification of complex neural dynamics. However, the discussion could be deepened. Real neural activity is characterized by non-linearities, discrete spiking events, and non-Gaussian statistics. It would be beneficial to discuss potential failure modes. For instance, how would the model cope with circuits exhibiting bistability or strong oscillations, where a linear model is a poor approximation? While the current model works well for the presented data, understanding its breaking points is important for its general application.

3.  **Scalability and Identifiability Concerns:** The paper notes a drop in the accuracy of recovered coupling parameters with increasing population size (Fig. 4c), attributing it to "possible near degeneracy." This is a key issue. As the size of the total population (N) grows while the size of the observed subset (Nk) remains fixed, the fraction of latent dimensions increases. This can lead to identifiability problems and a flat likelihood landscape, making optimization difficult. A more detailed discussion of the scaling properties and potential regularization strategies (e.g., sparsity priors on the coupling matrix A, as briefly mentioned in the discussion) would strengthen the paper.

4.  **Assumption of Stationarity:** The method implicitly assumes that the circuit's dynamics (i.e., the parameters A, B, Q) are stationary across the different recording sessions. In a real experimental setting, these sessions might be separated by hours or even days. The paper should discuss the potential impact of non-stationarity (e.g., due to plasticity, learning, or changes in brain state) on the model's performance.

**Detailed Comments**

*   **Clarity of the Naive Model:** The description of the naive model's handling of overlapping neurons ("averaged coefficients") is a bit brief. Clarifying the exact averaging method would be helpful.
*   **Experimental Design Implications:** The discussion rightly points out the trade-off between overlap and total coverage. This is a fantastic point. The paper could be made even more impactful by expanding this into a more formal discussion of optimal experimental design, perhaps with preliminary simulations showing how to best allocate a fixed "imaging time budget."

**Overall Assessment**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The methodology is sound, the experiments are well-designed, and the claims are well-supported by the results. The authors are honest about the limitations of their model choice.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is extremely clear, well-organized, and a pleasure to read. It provides sufficient detail for an expert to understand and likely reproduce the work.
*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. While it leverages a standard modeling framework (LDS), its application to the problem of stitching non-simultaneous recordings is novel and constitutes a significant conceptual advance. It is clearly distinguished from prior work.
*   **Significance:** The significance is very high. This work provides a practical tool that could fundamentally change how large-scale circuit dynamics are studied. It directly addresses a major experimental bottleneck and opens up new possibilities for constructing data-driven models of entire neural circuits from achievable partial recordings. Researchers in both computational and experimental neuroscience are very likely to use and build upon these ideas.

In conclusion, this is an excellent paper that presents an elegant, powerful, and practical solution to an important problem. Its strengths far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a perfect fit for NIPS, bridging machine learning theory with impactful application in a key scientific domain.
