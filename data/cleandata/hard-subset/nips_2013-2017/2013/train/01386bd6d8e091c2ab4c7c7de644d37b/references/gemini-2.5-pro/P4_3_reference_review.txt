This paper presents a novel statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial, and non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea is to model the entire population's activity as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is treated as a partial observation of this shared latent state, where the observation matrix for a given session simply selects the subset of neurons being recorded. The model parameters, including the functional connectivity matrix that describes interactions between all neurons, are estimated from the collection of partial recordings using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.

The problem of inferring functional connectivity from neural data is well-established, with previous work focusing on data where all neurons are recorded simultaneously (e.g., Pillow et al., Nature 2008; Mishchenko et al., Ann. Appl. Stat. 2011). The key contribution of this paper is to extend this inference to the challenging and experimentally relevant scenario where recordings are not simultaneous. The authors correctly position their work as distinct from methods that assume a monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations (Wohrer et al., NIPS 2010) or those focused on reconstructing anatomical connectivity from subsampled data (Mishchenko & Paninski, J Comp Neurosci 2012). This work provides a general, data-driven framework for combining temporally distinct recordings into a unified dynamical model.

### Quality

The paper is technically sound. The formulation of the problem as an LDS with missing data is appropriate and principled. The application of the EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing for parameter estimation is a standard and correct approach for this model class.

The evaluation methodology is a major strength. The authors test their method on both simulated and real data. On simulated data, where the ground truth connectivity is known, they demonstrate that the "stitching" method successfully recovers the coupling matrix and noise correlations, even between neurons that were never observed together. This provides a clear proof of concept.

For the real experimental data, the authors employ a clever validation strategy. By taking a fully-observed dataset and creating a "fictional imaging scenario" with two partial, overlapping sessions, they create a testbed where performance can be compared against meaningful benchmarks. The use of a "fully observed" model (trained on all data simultaneously) as an upper bound and a "naive" model (combining separately trained models) as a lower bound provides a clear and compelling demonstration of the method's efficacy. The results robustly show that the proposed stitching method's performance in predicting cross-correlations approaches the upper bound and significantly outperforms the lower bound.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly motivates the problem from an experimental perspective. The methods section provides a concise and complete description of the model and the fitting procedure, sufficient for an expert to understand the implementation. The results are presented logically, starting with the intuition and a demonstration on synthetic data before moving to the more complex real-world datasets. The figures are clear, well-labeled, and effectively support the main claims of the paper.

### Originality

The central idea of using a shared latent dynamical system to combine non-simultaneous neural recordings is novel and constitutes a significant conceptual contribution. While the components of the model (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to this specific problem of "stitching" is new and not an incremental extension of previous work. The paper addresses a gap in the existing literature on functional connectivity analysis, moving beyond the single-session paradigm to a more flexible and powerful framework that better leverages modern experimental capabilities.

### Significance

The potential impact of this work is high. Experimental neuroscience is often constrained by a trade-off between the number of neurons recorded and the recording duration or quality. This method provides a principled way to computationally overcome some of these limitations, allowing researchers to build models of larger circuits than can be observed in a single experiment. The ability to estimate "noise correlations"—a key feature of population coding—between neurons that were not co-recorded is particularly powerful. This could enable new investigations into the structure of neural variability across large populations and help test theories of cortical computation that rely on specific correlation structures. Practitioners could readily apply this method to existing datasets from sequential recordings in the same brain area to generate new hypotheses about circuit-wide dynamics.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **High Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a critical, unsolved problem in systems neuroscience with a novel and principled statistical approach.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Evaluation:** The use of both simulated data with ground truth and a well-designed "fictional scenario" on real data provides robust evidence for the method's effectiveness. The comparison to clear upper and lower bounds is particularly compelling.
3.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-organized, and accessible, making the contribution easy to understand and appreciate.
4.  **Practical Relevance:** The method directly addresses a common experimental limitation and offers a tool that could be widely adopted by neuroscientists to get more out of their data.

**Arguments for rejection (and suggestions for improvement):**
1.  **Model Simplicity:** The LDS model is a linear-Gaussian approximation of highly non-linear and non-Gaussian neural activity. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the potential failure modes of this approximation or a demonstration on simulated data with more realistic, non-linear dynamics.
2.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The authors note that some overlap between recording sessions was "critical for stitching to work" on real data. This is a key practical constraint. A more systematic analysis of how performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) degrades as a function of the percentage of overlapping neurons would be a valuable addition for guiding experimental design.
3.  **Scalability:** The method is demonstrated on populations of up to 100 neurons. The introduction motivates the work by citing circuits of "thousands of neurons." A discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm and its practical scalability to such large systems would be beneficial.
4.  **Identifiability Concerns:** The authors briefly mention that the accuracy of parameter estimation (as opposed to correlation estimation) drops for larger populations, possibly due to "near degeneracy." This hints at practical identifiability or optimization issues that merit a more thorough discussion, as it may limit the interpretability of the learned connectivity matrix `A` itself in large-scale applications.

In summary, this paper presents a high-quality, novel, and significant contribution. The strengths, particularly the novelty of the problem formulation and the thoroughness of the evaluation, far outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily represent avenues for future work. The proposed method is a valuable new tool for the analysis of large-scale neural recordings.
