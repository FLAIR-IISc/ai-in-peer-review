Summary
The paper addresses an important practical scenario in two-photon calcium imaging: when the same circuit is imaged in multiple sessions with partial, possibly overlapping subsets of neurons. The authors propose to “stitch” these observations by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with session-specific observation matrices Ck and shared dynamics A, stimulus filter B, and noise covariance Q, via EM with Kalman smoothing. The aim is to recover cross-couplings and, more importantly, predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. Results on a synthetic network and two real S1 datasets (awake/anesthetized) suggest that the stitched model can predict pairwise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs substantially better than a naive baseline and close to a “fully observed” upper bound in a fictional split-session setting.

Relation to prior work
The work sits at the intersection of state-space modeling (EM for LDS; Kalman smoothing; subspace/system identification) and neural population modeling (PLDS/GPFA/common-input latent models; GLMs with latent structure). The novelty is not in the model class or inference (standard LDS with missing data), but in framing and empirically validating its use for stitching multiple partial population recordings. The paper cites related work on connectivity inference and latent GLMs, but does not thoroughly position itself with respect to modern latent population models (e.g., GPFA/PLDS) or system identification with missing/structured observations.

Strengths
- Important and timely problem: how to leverage non-simultaneous, partially overlapping recordings to infer joint population statistics.
- Simple, computationally tractable approach that many labs could try with existing toolchains.
- Sensible evaluation protocol using a fictional split to create a test bed where ground-truth simultaneous data exist.
- Clear empirical takeaways: (i) some overlap is crucial; (ii) stitchLDS improves correlation prediction over a naive baseline; (iii) cross-prediction of unobserved activity is feasible.
- Writing is generally clear and accessible.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited novelty: the technical core is a standard LDS with missing data and EM; “stitching” is essentially fitting a single model with varying Ck. There is no algorithmic or theoretical contribution beyond this reframing.
- Assumptions vs. reality: stationarity across sessions is assumed; only a global offset d is modeled, with no session- or neuron-specific gains/offsets or slow drifts. Real non-simultaneous sessions differ in baseline, gain, neuropil contamination, and brain state. All real-data results use a fictional split of a single session, sidestepping these issues.
- Reliance on overlap: authors report failure without overlap and do not quantify performance vs. overlap fraction. This is central to feasibility and experimental design but is not rigorously analyzed.
- Identifiability and conditions: the paper gives intuition but no formal conditions on when cross-couplings are identifiable (observability/controllability given the union of Ck, how much overlap is needed, sensitivity to noise, stimulus richness). Degradation with increasing N is noted but not analyzed.
- Model mismatch: fitting directly to high-pass filtered fluorescence with a linear-Gaussian LDS, diagonal Q and R, ignores calcium/spike nonlinearity and correlated process noise. If Q is diagonal, all shared variability must be routed through A, which may bias coupling estimates. Authors mention extensions but do not test them.
- Baselines are weak: “naive” baseline is too easy to beat. Missing comparisons to (i) PLDS/GPFA/common-input low-rank latent models trained jointly on the two views; (ii) subspace ID approaches with time-varying C; (iii) deconvolved spikes + PLDS; (iv) methods that explicitly model session effects.
- Metrics: many results are reported as correlations between estimated and “fully observed” model outputs, not against empirical held-out correlations computed from real simultaneous data (which is available in the fictional setup). Predictive log-likelihood on held-out data and absolute error metrics would be more informative.
- Overfitting/regularization: For N up to 80–100, estimating dense A (~N^2 parameters) without strong priors risks overfitting. The paper mentions sparsity helps in “unreported” simulations but does not use or validate it.
- Scalability claims: No demonstration beyond ~100–200 neurons; computational and statistical scaling to thousands (as motivated) is not addressed.

Questions for the authors
1. Can you report performance versus overlap fraction (including 0%) and versus mismatch between sessions (e.g., simulated per-session gains/offsets/drifts)?
2. On the real datasets, can you compare predicted correlations directly to empirical pairwise correlations computed from held-out simultaneous data, not only to a “fully observed” model?
3. How sensitive are results to the diagonal Q assumption? What happens with correlated process noise or a low-rank plus diagonal Q?
4. Can you include stronger baselines (PLDS/GPFA with shared latent factors across sessions; subspace ID with time-varying C)?
5. Do you model session-specific observation noise Rk and offsets dk? If not, does adding them help on truly non-simultaneous sessions?

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide formal identifiability conditions (or at least empirical phase diagrams) as a function of overlap, T, SNR, stimulus richness, and network sparsity.
- Incorporate session-specific calibration (per-session d, gains, and possibly slow drift) and evaluate on genuinely non-simultaneous datasets.
- Use regularization (e.g., sparsity, stability constraints) with cross-validation; report predictive log-likelihood and held-out correlation errors.
- Compare against PLDS/GPFA/common-input models trained jointly on both views, and against subspace ID methods with missing data.
- Quantify computational complexity and provide scaling experiments.

Assessment
- Quality: Moderate. The method is sound but standard; validation is limited by use of fictional splits and weak baselines; key assumptions (stationarity, diagonal Q) are strong.
- Clarity: Good; the exposition is generally clear, though some notation (measurement equation) could be tightened and modeling choices justified more thoroughly.
- Originality: Low-to-moderate. The contribution is mainly an application of established LDS/EM machinery to a relevant stitching setting, with limited methodological novelty.
- Significance: Potentially high if the approach proves robust to real session variability and scales, but current evidence is not yet convincing.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important and common experimental constraint.
- Simple, accessible approach with promising preliminary results.
- Could stimulate more rigorous work on stitching partial population recordings.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited novelty beyond applying standard LDS with missing observations.
- Validation avoids the hardest aspects (true session variability), relies on overlap, and lacks strong baselines and metrics.
- No theoretical guarantees or clear guidance on required overlap/data for success.

Recommendation
Lean reject. The idea is interesting and practically relevant, but stronger methodological grounding, more realistic validation, and comparisons to established latent population models are needed to justify publication at NeurIPS.
