

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural activity from the same circuit to infer joint dynamics and noise correlations across neurons not recorded simultaneously. The authors model the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework with EM optimization. They demonstrate their approach on simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The core idea of combining partial observations to infer unobserved dynamics has some theoretical grounding in system identification literature (e.g., [21, 22, 23]), but the authors' specific application to neuroscience with the LDS framework represents a novel adaptation. Previous work on inferring connectivity from partial observations [12, 13, 14, 15] does not address the same problem, as they either require strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations [12]) or don't scale to large populations [13].

**Strengths:**
1. The method is technically sound and implemented using well-established techniques (LDS, EM, Kalman smoothing).
2. The experimental validation is comprehensive, comparing against a "naive" baseline and a "fully observed" upper bound.
3. The results on real data show meaningful improvements over the naive approach (e.g., correlation of 0.84 for stitchLDS vs. 0.15 for naiveLDS in Figure 3d).
4. The paper acknowledges limitations of their linear model and discusses potential extensions.

**Weaknesses:**
1. **Overstated novelty:** The core approach is a straightforward application of established system identification techniques to a neuroscience problem. The authors don't clearly distinguish how their approach differs from standard LDS methods applied to missing data problems.
2. **Insufficient validation:** The simulated data uses a simple linear model with Gaussian noise, which doesn't reflect the complex, nonlinear dynamics of real neural systems. The authors don't demonstrate how their method would perform with more realistic neural dynamics (e.g., spiking, nonlinear calcium dynamics).
3. **Weak baseline comparison:** The "naive" baseline is overly simplistic (setting couplings between non-simultaneously recorded pairs to zero). A more meaningful comparison would be against established methods for inferring connectivity from partial data, such as [7] or [6], which could provide a stronger baseline.
4. **Lack of failure analysis:** The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where stitching would fail (e.g., when there's minimal overlap between recording sessions, or when neural dynamics are highly nonlinear).
5. **Theoretical justification gap:** While they cite theoretical results about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations, they don't rigorously establish why their specific approach would work better than others for neural data, particularly given the known challenges of neural dynamics.

**Significance and contribution:**
The paper addresses a genuine challenge in neuroscience: the difficulty of simultaneously recording large neural populations. If validated with more realistic data, this could be a useful tool. However, the method's reliance on a simple linear model significantly limits its potential applicability to real neural data, where dynamics are known to be highly nonlinear. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that their method provides substantial advantages over simpler approaches that could be used with the same data.

**Overall assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper that applies established methods to a neuroscience problem. However, the novelty is limited, and the validation is insufficient for the claims made. The authors overstate the significance of their method while underestimating the challenges of applying linear models to neural data. The paper would be significantly stronger with:
1. More rigorous comparison against established neuroscience methods for connectivity inference
2. Validation on data with more realistic neural dynamics
3. A clearer theoretical justification for why their approach outperforms simpler alternatives

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- Well-executed application of established methods to a relevant neuroscience problem
- Comprehensive experimental validation on real data
- Clear presentation of results with appropriate metrics

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Overstated novelty without sufficient theoretical or empirical justification
- Insufficient validation against realistic neural dynamics
- Weak comparison to relevant baselines
- Limited discussion of method limitations and failure modes

**Recommendation:**
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem in neuroscience, it fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over established approaches. The reliance on a simple linear model without addressing its limitations for neural data, combined with insufficient validation, means the claims are not well-supported. The paper would require substantial additional experiments and analysis to meet NIPS standards for methodological rigor and significance.
