This paper presents a method for inferring the joint dynamics of neural populations observed across multiple, non-simultaneous recordings. The core idea – stitching together partial observations using a latent dynamical system – is well-motivated by the limitations of current imaging technology and the strong coupling observed in neural circuits. The authors convincingly argue that this approach can predict correlations between neurons that were not simultaneously recorded, a capability lacking in existing methods.

**Summary of Strengths:**

* **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of limited observational capacity in neural imaging and positions their method as a practical solution. The example of the whisker barrel is particularly effective.
* **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of a latent dynamical system (specifically, a linear Gaussian model) provides a principled statistical framework for combining data from different recording sessions. Leveraging existing theory on state-space models is a strong point.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method on both simulated and real calcium imaging data. The comparison to both a fully observed model (upper bound) and a naive approach (lower bound) provides a clear benchmark for performance.
* **Demonstrated Ability to Predict Correlations:** The key contribution – accurately predicting noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons – is convincingly demonstrated.
* **Well-Written & Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The methods section is detailed and the results are presented in a logical manner.

**Summary of Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplicity:** While the linear Gaussian model is computationally tractable, it is a significant simplification of the underlying neural dynamics. The authors acknowledge this, but the limitations of this simplification on the generalizability of their findings are not fully explored. Could non-linearities significantly impact performance?
* **Parameter Tuning & Sensitivity:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the method to parameter choices (e.g., regularization strength, initial conditions for the EM algorithm). Are the results robust to different parameter settings?
* **Computational Cost:** Although tractability is a benefit, the computational cost of the EM algorithm, even with the online variant, could be a limiting factor for very large populations or long recording sessions. A discussion of scalability would be valuable.
* **Overlap Requirement:** The requirement for some overlap between recording sessions (c=60% in the experimental data) is a practical constraint.  The impact of varying overlap on performance isn't fully explored beyond the mentioned 50% and 60% scenarios.
* **Limited Exploration of Alternative Models:** While the authors briefly mention more sophisticated models (e.g., non-Gaussian models), they do not present any results comparing their performance to the linear Gaussian model.

**Originality:**

The core idea of stitching together partial observations is not entirely novel, as acknowledged in the related work section. However, the application to neural population dynamics, particularly the focus on inferring *correlations* between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, appears to be a significant contribution.  The rigorous validation on real calcium imaging data strengthens the originality of the work.  The approach cleverly leverages established theory in a new context.

**Significance:**

The potential impact of this work is significant.  Being able to infer the dynamics of larger neural circuits from limited observational data has the potential to dramatically accelerate our understanding of neural computation.  The method could be a valuable tool for neuroscientists studying a wide range of brain regions and functions. The successful demonstration on real data further enhances the significance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* Addresses a well-defined and important problem in computational neuroscience.
* Presents a technically sound and principled approach.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results on both simulated and real data.
* Offers a practical solution to a limitation of current experimental techniques.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

* Relies on a relatively simple model that may not capture the full complexity of neural dynamics.
* Lacks a detailed analysis of parameter sensitivity and computational cost.
* The required overlap between recordings may limit its applicability in some scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The method is well-motivated, technically sound, and empirically validated. While the simplicity of the model and the lack of detailed parameter sensitivity analysis are weaknesses, they are not fatal.  The potential impact of this work is significant, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings.

**Recommendation:**

Accept.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the following points:

1. **Discuss parameter sensitivity:** Include a brief analysis of how the performance of the method is affected by different parameter settings (e.g., regularization strength).
2. **Address computational cost:** Briefly discuss the computational cost of the method and its scalability to larger datasets.
3. **Expand on model limitations:**  Elaborate on the potential impact of the linear Gaussian assumption on the accuracy of the results, and suggest directions for future work to address these limitations.
4. **Explore overlap variations:** Briefly comment on the expected performance with lower overlap percentages.
