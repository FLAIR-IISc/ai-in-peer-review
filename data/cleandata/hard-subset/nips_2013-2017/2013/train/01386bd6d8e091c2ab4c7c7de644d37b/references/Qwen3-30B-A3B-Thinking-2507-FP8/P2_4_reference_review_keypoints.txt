- Addresses inability to record large neural populations by "stitching" multiple partial recordings to infer joint population dynamics.  
- Frames problem as a latent linear Gaussian dynamical system with missing observations, fit via EM and Kalman smoothing.  
- Builds on theoretical results for reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations and adapts them to neuroscience.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior partial-data connectivity methods by not requiring strong assumptions about stimulus–noise relationships.  
- Strong technical quality: well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and implemented with standard, appropriate algorithms.  
- Thorough experimental validation on synthetic and real calcium imaging data with suitable baselines (fully observed and naive).  
- Quantitative results show good recovery: coupling correlations 0.73 (non-simultaneous) vs. 0.95 (simultaneous); noise-correlation correlation 0.92 on synthetic data.  
- On real data, stitching outperforms naive baseline (predicted correlations 0.84 vs. 0.15).  
- Paper is clearly written and well-organized; figures (especially Fig.2) effectively convey the method and intuition.  
- Minor clarity issue: "noise correlations" not explicitly defined as correlations after subtracting stimulus contributions.  
- Original and novel: applies established theory to a new, practical neuroscience problem of inferring correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons.  
- High significance: enables larger-scale neural population studies without requiring new imaging hardware.  
- Strength: addresses a critical experimental bottleneck with a sound theoretical foundation and practical demonstration.  
- Strength: comprehensive validation and clear comparison to baselines; explains why the method works.  
- Weakness: linear Gaussian model oversimplifies neural dynamics (ignores spiking, calcium dynamics, nonlinearities).  
- Weakness: limited exploration of performance dependence on experimental parameters (overlap, duration) and non-stationary dynamics.  
- Weakness: real-data validation limited to two datasets, possibly not covering full neural dynamics diversity.  
- Recommendation: accept — method is theoretically sound, practically useful, and well-evaluated.  
- Pro: solves a significant, practical neuroscience problem with strong empirical support.  
- Con: model simplicity limits realism, though authors acknowledge this and suggest future extensions.
