Summary
The paper tackles an important practical problem: inferring joint population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same circuit, where simultaneous imaging of all neurons is infeasible. The authors propose to “stitch” datasets by fitting a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations across sessions, using known selection matrices per session (Ck). Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are estimated via EM (with an online mini-batch phase followed by full-batch EM). The main claims are that this approach can: (i) recover cross-couplings between neurons not imaged simultaneously, (ii) predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs, and (iii) predict unobserved activity given observed subset activity. Validation includes one synthetic experiment (two non-overlapping sessions) and two real 2p datasets (awake/anesthetized) using a fictional split into two sessions with some overlap. Performance is bracketed by a “fully observed” LDS and a “naive” baseline (two separate LDS, cross-couplings zeroed).

Relation to prior work: The approach is essentially an LDS with missing observations; related to subspace/system ID, Kalman smoothing, and multi-session latent models. The paper cites GLM/latent GLM work and factor-analytic ideas, and notes the possibility of adding latent dimensions for common input.

Strengths
- Practical problem of high interest in systems neuroscience; potential to extend utility of current imaging tech.
- Conceptually simple and computationally tractable method leveraging standard LDS+EM; easy to reproduce in principle.
- On synthetic data with known ground-truth couplings, stitched LDS recovers off-diagonal couplings and noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs; cross-prediction of hidden activity is good.
- On real data, stitched model predicts pairwise correlations of non-simultaneous pairs substantially better than the naive baseline and close to a fully observed LDS.
- Sensible discussion of overlap vs. coverage tradeoffs and potential extensions (priors, sparsity, latent common input).

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical
- Measurement model is written unusually: ykt = Ck(xkt + d + t), t ~ N(0,R). This implies noise is multiplied by Ck. Standard is ykt = Ck xkt + d_obs + εkt with εkt ~ N(0,Robs). The current notation is confusing; the M-step updates (element-wise Rjj) suggest a diagonal Robs at the observed coordinates. Please clarify dimensions of d and R, and whether noise enters pre- or post-selection.
- Q is assumed diagonal. This precludes instantaneous correlated process noise, forcing A to absorb shared variability. While the text mentions possible extensions with latent unobserved dimensions for common input, the presented results use diagonal Q. This limits realism and can bias A, especially in cortex where shared variability is substantial.
- Stability/identifiability: No constraints on spectral radius of A are described; EM for LDS can yield unstable dynamics. The stationarity assumption for Q0 does not enforce stability during learning. No regularization (e.g., spectral penalties, sparsity) is reported, despite large parameter counts (A is N×N). Coupling estimates degrade with N, which is unsurprising without priors.
- Overlap required: Synthetic experiments used two non-overlapping sessions and report success; but on real data, the method “failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons.” This is a critical caveat and contradicts the earlier intuition about identifiability up to linear transforms that can be resolved by a second session. The paper needs a precise characterization (theory or controlled experiments) of identifiability and overlap requirements.
- Evaluation of correlations: On real data, the reference for “ground-truth” correlations is a “fully observed” LDS, not empirical correlations on held-out data. Since simultaneous full recordings exist in the fictional split, the authors could compute empirical noise correlations from held-out time segments (after removing stimulus effects) and compare directly. Using a model as the upper bound conflates model misspecification with evaluation.
- Baselines are weak. The naive model is a strawman. Missing comparisons include: multi-session factor analysis/GPFA with subspace alignment; LDS with shared low-rank latent processes across sessions; subspace/system identification baselines; simple shrinkage estimators using overlap only; methods like [12] when its assumptions approximately hold. Also, a version with correlated Q or with a low-rank latent block would be a strong internal baseline.
- Practicalities omitted: scaling and runtime (O(TN^3) Kalman costs), convergence diagnostics, initialization strategies, sensitivity to filtering (signals are high-pass filtered), per-session gains/offsets (GCaMP amplitudes vary across sessions). The current model shares a single d across sessions, which is unrealistic; session-specific biases/gains should be included.
- Ambiguities about trials/time: Synthetic uses K trials of length T; the anesthetized dataset had repeated whisker flicks; the awake dataset is continuous. How are trial boundaries and initial conditions handled? Are inputs uk standardized? How are noise correlations defined in the awake dataset with variable behavior?
- Some claims are qualitative without error bars or statistical testing (e.g., “nearly as good”). Figures are referenced but not described in enough detail to assess reproducibility.

Clarity
- Notation has inconsistencies (e.g., ykt,σkj indexing, footnote marker dangling “1”, mismatched parentheses in Eq. (2)). The text often uses unusual spacing/punctuation. The presentation of EM updates is partial (only R and d are shown).
- The write-up should cleanly separate the generative model, inference, and the multi-session stitching mechanics (how Ck changes across sessions; how overlapping neurons are used to align).
- References contain multiple incomplete entries (e.g., [21], [22] formatting; missing venues/pages; inconsistent capitalization). Some important related work (e.g., GPFA and multi-session alignment methods) is not discussed.

Originality
- The core technique is a straightforward application of LDS with missing data across sessions. The novelty is in the specific multi-session “stitching” framing for 2p data. Without theoretical guarantees or substantial model extensions, methodological novelty is modest.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could be impactful for calcium imaging analysis. However, reliance on overlap for real data, lack of stronger baselines, and model misspecification (diagonal Q, no per-session calibration) limit immediate adoption.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely and practically important problem with a simple, accessible method.
- Promising empirical results on synthetic and real datasets for predicting noise correlations of non-simultaneously recorded pairs.
- Potentially useful as a component in larger pipelines; extensible with priors/latent common input.

Cons for acceptance
- Methodological simplicity with several unrealistic assumptions (diagonal Q, shared d, no stability/regularization) and limited analysis of identifiability/overlap requirements.
- Weak baselines and evaluation choices (model-as-upper-bound rather than empirical held-out ground truth).
- Missing implementation details and scalability discussion; unclear reproducibility.
- Numerous clarity/formatting/reference issues.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and correct the observation model; include session-specific gains/offsets; consider correlated Robs and/or low-rank latent processes for common input; enforce stability and add sparsity priors on A.
- Provide theoretical or empirical characterization of when stitching works without overlap; quantify the minimum overlap needed and how performance scales with overlap fraction and recording duration.
- Evaluate correlations against empirical held-out correlations (with repeated stimuli) and include stronger baselines (multi-session FA/GPFA with alignment, subspace ID, low-rank plus sparse models).
- Report runtimes, initialization, convergence behavior, and sensitivity analyses; release code.
- Clean up notation, figures, and references.

Recommendation: Reject (borderline). The problem is important and the empirical signal is promising, but the current submission lacks rigor in modeling, evaluation, and clarity needed for NeurIPS. With the above improvements, this could become a strong contribution. Confidence: high.
