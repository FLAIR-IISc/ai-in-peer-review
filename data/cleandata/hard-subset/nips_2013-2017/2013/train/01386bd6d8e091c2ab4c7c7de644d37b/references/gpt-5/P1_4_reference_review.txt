Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a statistically principled method to “stitch” together multiple partial, non-simultaneous recordings from the same neural circuit to infer a joint model of population dynamics and, crucially, predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded together. The approach is to fit a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) across sessions with session-specific observation matrices Ck that select the subset of neurons imaged in each session. Parameters (A,B,Q,R,d) are learned by EM (with an online/batch variant), and inference is performed via Kalman smoothing. The model can incorporate known inputs u (stimuli) and assumes diagonal process noise Q in the main experiments, with extensions discussed.

The authors demonstrate on synthetic data that the method recovers cross-session couplings and noise correlations, and predict activity of unobserved neurons from observed ones and the stimulus. On two real two-photon datasets (awake/anesthetized mouse S1), they construct a controlled “fictional” split of simultaneously recorded data (with some overlap between subsets), showing that stitching recovers pairwise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs far better than a naive baseline (separate models combined post hoc) and close to a “fully observed” upper bound fit to all neurons simultaneously. They also explore robustness across population sizes and show benefits for cross-prediction.

This connects to prior work on inferring neural couplings from calcium imaging (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al.), latent GLM/state-space models for populations (Pillow et al., Macke et al.), methods dealing with subsampling (Pillow & Latham latent GLM; Wohrer et al. correlation prediction with strong assumptions), and system identification/observability results. The novelty here is explicit stitching across sessions with identified cells and missing observations to estimate cross-session couplings and noise correlations.

Strengths
- Addresses an important and practical problem: how to combine multiple partial recordings to characterize larger circuits than current imaging permits.
- Conceptually simple, computationally tractable framework leveraging standard LDS/EM machinery; easy to extend and implement.
- Clear empirical evidence on synthetic data that cross-session couplings and noise correlations can be recovered; performance metrics include parameter recovery, correlation prediction, and cross-prediction of activity.
- Real-data validation via a controlled split with upper/lower bounds helps interpret performance and reduces confounds.
- Thoughtful discussion of assumptions, possible extensions (latent inputs, sparsity priors), and experimental design trade-offs (overlap vs. coverage).

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Validation uses “fictional” splits of simultaneous data rather than truly non-simultaneous sessions with potential nonstationarities (cell drift, network state changes, photobleaching, behavioral state changes). This is a substantial gap given the paper’s aim. Suggest adding at least one experiment that stitches two real sessions separated in time, including registration of common cells, and analyzing robustness to session-to-session drift (e.g., different mean, gain, or A across sessions).
- Overlap is asserted to be “critical” for real data; quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction, including the no-overlap case, and characterize what minimal overlap is needed for stable estimation. The synthetic case succeeds with no overlap; the discrepancy and underlying identifiability conditions deserve clearer treatment (theory and/or empirical).
- Assumptions: Q diagonal and linear-Gaussian dynamics on calcium fluorescence are strong. Robustness to model misspecification should be evaluated: simulate spiking data with realistic calcium dynamics and non-Gaussian noise, then apply stitching on fluorescence; or perform deconvolution first. Also test correlated process noise and inclusion of low-dimensional latent inputs.
- Identifiability and conditions for success are discussed only informally. It would strengthen the paper to formalize conditions (observability/controllability, persistent excitation of inputs, role of overlap in fixing similarity transforms) or at least provide a proposition outlining when cross-session couplings are uniquely determined.
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is quite weak. Consider additional alternatives: (i) fit factor analysis/LDS with shared latent structure across sessions aligned via overlapping cells; (ii) CCA-based alignment of low-dimensional dynamics; (iii) correlation prediction from stimulus correlations (Wohrer et al.) as a diagnostic, even if expected to perform poorly in some settings; (iv) multi-session GPFA. This would situate the contribution more convincingly.
- Clarity/notation: Equation (2) seems to have formatting issues (“ykt = Ck(xkt + d+ t) t ~ N(0,R)”). Please fix and state whether R is diagonal. Clarify the definition of c and actual overlap fraction in the fictional split. Specify time step, stimulus design in each dataset, and how residual (noise) correlations are computed (e.g., subtract Bukt via filtering?).
- Scalability and computation: Provide complexity and runtime scaling with N, T, K (sessions). In Kalman smoothing the state dimension is N, so practical limits matter for thousands of neurons; discuss sparse A, block structure, or subspace-ID alternatives.
- Statistical reporting: In addition to correlations between predicted and true/upper-bound correlations, report absolute errors or calibration (e.g., RMSE, slope/intercept) to assess magnitude bias. Include CIs across resamples.

Quality
Technically sound in the LDS/EM framework, with solid synthetic experiments and careful upper/lower bounds on real data. However, the absence of true multi-session validation and limited baselines temper the strength of the claims about real-world applicability.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Some notation glitches and ambiguities around the fictional split and assumptions should be corrected. More implementation details would aid reproducibility.

Originality
Stitching partial recordings via a shared LDS with session-specific observation matrices is, to my knowledge, novel in this specific calcium imaging context and addresses a gap not covered by previous subsampling/latent GLM works. The conceptual advance is a practical synthesis rather than a new model class.

Significance
Potentially high: if validated on truly non-simultaneous sessions, this enables leveraging existing imaging setups to infer larger-scale circuit statistics, which many labs could adopt. As is, the impact is promising but preliminary for real multi-session use.

Pros and cons for acceptance
Pros:
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a simple, usable method.
- Strong synthetic validation and sensible bounds on real data.
- Clear evidence that correlations between non-simultaneously observed pairs can be recovered.

Cons:
- No demonstration on genuinely non-simultaneous sessions; dependence on overlap not fully quantified.
- Limited baselines; theoretical conditions not formalized.
- Assumption set (linear-Gaussian, diagonal Q, stationarity) may be restrictive; robustness not fully explored.

Questions for authors
- Can you provide results stitching two real sessions measured at different times, including registration errors and potential changes in dynamics?
- How does performance vary with overlap fraction, session length, and stimulus richness?
- Is Q and/or R assumed diagonal in all experiments? What happens when process noise is correlated?
- How does runtime scale to N ~ 1000? Any use of sparsity or structured A to accelerate EM/smoothing?

Recommendation
Borderline accept (leaning weak accept). The problem is important and the proposed solution is elegant and effective on synthetic and controlled real-data splits. To strengthen the paper, adding at least one truly multi-session experiment and broader baselines, plus clarifications and minor theory/robustness analyses, would substantially increase confidence in real-world applicability.
