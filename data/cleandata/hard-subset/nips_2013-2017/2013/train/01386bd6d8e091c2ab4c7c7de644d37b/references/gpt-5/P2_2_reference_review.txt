Summary
The paper addresses an important practical limitation in systems neuroscience: we often can only image subsets of a neural circuit at a time. The authors propose “stitching” multiple partial recordings into a single joint model by fitting a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations across sessions, using EM (with mini-batch online EM for speed). Assuming cell identities are known and there is some overlap across sessions, they estimate a single coupling matrix A and stimulus filters B that jointly explain all sessions. They show on simulations that they can recover cross-couplings and noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, and on two calcium imaging datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1) they show that their stitched model predicts pairwise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs well, approaching a “fully observed” upper bound (trained on the whole dataset simultaneously in a fictional scenario) and outperforming a naive baseline that fits each session independently.

Relation to prior work
This work situates itself among methods for inferring population dynamics and functional connectivity from calcium imaging or spikes (e.g., GLMs, PLDS, factor models, and deconvolution-based approaches). It is closest in spirit to standard LDS with missing data and system identification, as well as multi-session latent alignment methods. The authors argue that prior methods for subsampled populations either do not address non-simultaneous pairs or require restrictive assumptions (e.g., monotonic relation of noise and stimulus correlations). The novelty is less in the model class (a standard LDS) and more in the problem setup and demonstration that cross-session stitching can recover noise correlations across non-overlapping subsets under realistic imaging conditions.

Strengths
- Timely and important problem with clear relevance for 2p imaging practice.
- Simple, scalable tool (LDS+EM with known selection matrices) likely to be adopted.
- Well-motivated evaluation protocol with upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) baselines.
- Convincing simulations and real-data results on predicting non-simultaneous correlations; activity cross-prediction shows practical value.
- The paper acknowledges limitations and suggests sensible extensions (e.g., low-rank latent inputs, sparsity priors).

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Identifiability and conditions for success: The core claim hinges on aligning latent representations across sessions to recover cross-couplings. The paper provides intuition but no formal conditions (e.g., observability/controllability, required overlap, stimulus richness). Empirically, stitching failed without overlap at c=50%, suggesting overlap is often necessary; a systematic analysis of performance vs overlap fraction is missing.
- Model misspecification: The LDS assumes diagonal process and measurement noise, assigning shared variability to A. This can inflate apparent performance on correlation prediction and bias A. The paper mentions correlated noise can be modeled but does not test it. A low-rank+diagonal Q or a PLDS/GPFA baseline would strengthen claims.
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is weak. Comparisons to factor models (FA/GPFA), PLDS, or LDS with low-dimensional latent shared across sessions (with alignment) would be more informative. Also compare to subspace identification or pCCA for correlation prediction.
- Real-data validation uses a fictional split of simultaneous data, thus avoiding nonstationarities (drift, bleaching, state changes) inherent to truly separate sessions. This likely overestimates real-world performance. Ideally include at least different fields of view or time-separated recordings from the same animal.
- Practicalities: No discussion of ensuring stability of A (spectral radius), initialization, regularization (e.g., sparsity, shrinkage), or runtime/complexity. Recovery of A degrades with N; regularization could help. How robust is performance to sampling rate, measurement noise, and HP filtering?
- Cell identity across sessions is assumed known. In practice, cross-session registration is imperfect. Robustness to mislabeling is not studied.

Quality
Technically sound for the LDS-with-missing-data setup; EM and smoothing are standard. Claims about correlation prediction are supported by experiments, but reliance on a fictional split, a weak baseline, and simplifying noise assumptions limit how strong the conclusions are about general applicability. The authors are reasonably candid about limitations.

Clarity
Generally clear and well organized. The stitching setup, EM details for R and d with partial observations, and evaluation are described understandably. Missing are some implementation details (stability constraints, initialization, regularization, convergence criteria), and more explicit description of train/test splits and hyperparameters.

Originality
Moderate. The model is standard; the novelty is in applying LDS to stitch partially observed populations across sessions and demonstrating that non-simultaneous noise correlations can be recovered. The positioning relative to prior multi-session and latent alignment work is reasonable, but additional comparisons would help.

Significance
High potential impact for experimental neuroscience, as this is a common data collection scenario. Even if the method is simple, demonstrating feasibility and providing a clear recipe is valuable. However, practical constraints (need for overlap, stationarity, identity alignment) may limit direct applicability without further development.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a quantitative analysis of performance vs overlap fraction and vs degree of stimulus drive.
- Add baselines: FA/GPFA, PLDS, LDS with low-rank Q, and/or pCCA for correlation prediction.
- Test robustness to correlated process noise and to modest mislabeling of cell identity across sessions.
- Include results on truly non-simultaneous recordings (different time blocks or fields of view) to assess nonstationarities.
- Describe constraints ensuring stable A, regularization choices (e.g., sparsity on A), and runtime/complexity. Report sensitivity to initialization.
- Clarify whether R and Q are shared across sessions; consider session-specific measurement noise and offsets to handle drift.

Pros
- Addresses an important and practical problem.
- Simple, scalable approach with clear recipe.
- Strong empirical results on predicting non-simultaneous correlations.
- Careful upper/lower bound framing and cross-prediction metrics.

Cons
- Limited theoretical grounding of identifiability/overlap requirements.
- Weak baselines and simplified noise assumptions.
- Validation uses fictional splits that may overestimate real-world robustness.
- Degradation of A recovery with N; lack of regularization/stability discussion.

Recommendation: Weak accept
Justification: The problem is important, the approach is simple and likely to be useful, and the empirical results on real data are promising. The paper would be stronger with additional baselines, robustness tests, and clearer guidance on overlap and identifiability, but the contribution is sufficiently novel and practically relevant for NeurIPS.

Confidence: 3/5
