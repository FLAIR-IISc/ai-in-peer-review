

# Peer Review for NeurIPS

## Summary

This paper presents a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the full population, even when different subsets of neurons are imaged in separate sessions. The authors propose using a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) model to combine data from non-simultaneous imaging sessions, with the goal of predicting correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. They validate their approach on simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the neuroscience community by addressing a practical limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to image all neurons in a circuit simultaneously. The authors properly distinguish their work from previous methods that either require simultaneous recordings, assume monotonic relationships between stimulus and noise correlations, or don't scale to large population sizes.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation**: The paper effectively articulates the experimental limitations in neural circuit recording and presents a compelling case for why stitching together partial recordings would be valuable for understanding neural computation.

2. **Strong validation with both synthetic and real data**: The authors demonstrate their method's efficacy on both simulated data (where ground truth is available) and real experimental data (where they create a "fictional imaging scenario" to validate their approach).

3. **Appropriate baselines**: They compare their method against both an "upper bound" fully observed model (which would be impossible in practice) and a "naive" approach that simply averages parameters across sessions, providing meaningful context for their results.

4. **Clear demonstration of the method's capabilities**: The results clearly show that their stitching method can accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, with performance approaching that of the fully observed model.

5. **Well-organized presentation**: The paper is logically structured, with a clear progression from problem statement to methods, validation, and discussion.

## Weaknesses and Critiques

1. **Overly simplistic model**: The core method relies on a linear Gaussian LDS, which is a significant simplification of neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't thoroughly explore how this simplification affects the accuracy of their predictions or how more realistic models (like those accounting for calcium dynamics, spiking, etc.) might improve their results. The paper would be stronger if they explicitly compared their LDS approach to more biologically plausible models.

2. **Lack of sensitivity analysis**: The paper doesn't explore how sensitive their method is to key parameters, particularly the overlap between imaging sessions. The authors note that some overlap is critical, but don't quantify how much overlap is needed for reliable stitching, or how performance degrades with decreasing overlap. This is a significant limitation for experimental design.

3. **Limited theoretical justification**: While the paper cites theoretical work on system identification, it doesn't fully develop the theoretical foundation for why their approach should work for neural data. The connection between the theoretical guarantees of system identification and the specific properties of neural population dynamics could be strengthened.

4. **Overstated significance for ML community**: The paper focuses heavily on the neuroscience application, but doesn't clearly articulate how this advances machine learning methodology. The LDS approach is well-established in the ML community, and the main contribution is its application to neural data. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of how this contributes to ML theory or methodology.

5. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The authors briefly acknowledge limitations but don't thoroughly explore the method's boundaries. For example:
   - How does the method perform when the underlying dynamics are nonlinear?
   - How does the method scale to very large populations (thousands of neurons)?
   - What happens when the two recording sessions have very different dynamics (e.g., awake vs. anesthetized states)?
   - How robust is the method to errors in the measurement matrices (e.g., if the cell identities are misassigned)?

6. **Ambiguous definition of "noise correlations"**: The paper uses the term "noise correlations" without clearly defining it in the context of their model. In neuroscience, noise correlations typically refer to trial-to-trial variability in neural responses that's not explained by the stimulus. The paper doesn't clarify how their model specifically captures this concept.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Expand the theoretical discussion**: Include a more detailed theoretical analysis of why the stitching approach works for neural data, particularly addressing how the coupling structure and the overlap between sessions enables parameter recovery.

2. **Add sensitivity analysis**: Conduct experiments varying the amount of overlap between sessions and report how performance changes. This would provide crucial guidance for experimental design.

3. **Compare to more realistic neural models**: Add a comparison to a more biologically plausible model (e.g., incorporating calcium dynamics) to show how much the linear LDS approximation affects the results.

4. **Clarify the definition of noise correlations**: Provide a clear mathematical definition of "noise correlations" in the context of the paper and explain how the model captures this concept.

5. **Address scalability**: Discuss the computational complexity of the method and provide results for larger population sizes to better understand practical limitations.

6. **Expand the discussion of limitations**: Include a dedicated section on the method's limitations, including failure cases and conditions under which the method might not work well.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that addresses a genuine challenge in neuroscience with a practical solution. The method shows promising results on both synthetic and real data, and the paper is clearly written. However, the methodological contribution is limited to the application of an established technique (LDS) to a new problem domain, rather than a significant advancement in the technique itself.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to neuroscience, but its significance for the NeurIPS community is limited. While the problem is important for neuroscience, the methodological approach is not novel in the ML community. The paper would be stronger if it more clearly articulated how this work advances ML methodology, or if it included more rigorous theoretical analysis of the stitching procedure.

## Recommendation

**Borderline** - I would lean toward acceptance but with the following conditions:

1. Address the limitations mentioned above, particularly by adding a sensitivity analysis to overlap parameters and a more thorough discussion of the method's boundaries.

2. Clarify the theoretical foundation and the connection between system identification theory and neural dynamics.

3. Provide a more nuanced discussion of the significance for the ML community, acknowledging that the core method is an application of existing techniques.

This paper is an important contribution to neuroscience methodology, and with the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for NeurIPS. The method could have significant impact on how researchers analyze existing calcium imaging data, and the paper demonstrates this impact convincingly on real datasets.

## Arguments for Acceptance
- Addresses a genuine and important problem in neuroscience
- Clear, well-executed validation on both synthetic and real data
- Provides a practical tool for neuroscientists to make better use of existing data
- Good comparison to meaningful baselines
- Clear presentation of results

## Arguments Against Acceptance
- Methodological contribution is limited to application of established techniques
- Overly simplistic model for neural dynamics
- Insufficient theoretical justification for why the approach works
- Limited discussion of method's boundaries and limitations
- Significance for ML community not clearly articulated

## Final Recommendation

Accept with minor revisions (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the key weaknesses identified above). The paper has strong experimental validation and addresses a meaningful problem, but needs to strengthen its theoretical foundation and more thoroughly discuss the method's limitations and boundaries.
