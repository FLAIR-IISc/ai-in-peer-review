

# Peer Review: "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations due to imaging constraints. The authors propose a "stitching" method that combines multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer joint dynamics, including correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously. The approach models the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system for computational tractability. The authors demonstrate their method on both simulated neural populations and two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing it significantly outperforms a naive baseline and approaches the performance of a "fully observed" model.

## Quality
The paper is technically sound with a clear methodology and well-designed experiments. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their latent dynamical system approach and demonstrate its effectiveness through multiple evaluation metrics (coupling matrix recovery, noise correlation prediction, and activity prediction). The results are convincing, with the stitching method achieving 0.92 correlation with true noise correlations in simulations and 0.84 in real data, compared to near-zero performance for the naive baseline.

However, the linear Gaussian assumption, while computationally convenient, represents a significant simplification of neural dynamics. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how it might constrain the method's applicability. For instance, the paper doesn't explore how the method performs on more complex neural dynamics (e.g., nonlinear responses or bursting behavior) that are common in cortical circuits. Additionally, while the authors note that some overlap between imaging sessions is critical for stitching to work, they don't provide quantitative analysis of how performance degrades as overlap decreases.

## Clarity
The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The methodology is explained with sufficient mathematical detail, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations. The authors effectively distinguish their approach from related work, particularly in Section 1 where they contrast their method with previous approaches to inferring functional connectivity from partial observations.

One minor improvement could be to provide more detail about the computational complexity of the method, especially as the number of imaging sessions increases. The paper mentions the method scales to population sizes of interest, but doesn't quantify the computational requirements for larger datasets.

## Originality
The core idea of "stitching" together non-simultaneous recordings to infer correlations between neurons is novel in the context of neural circuit analysis. While the authors build on standard techniques (latent dynamical systems, EM algorithm), their application to this specific problem of inferring correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons represents a significant contribution. The related work section is thorough and well-motivated, showing the authors have a strong understanding of the field and clearly distinguish their approach from previous methods.

## Significance
The significance of this work is high. The ability to infer joint dynamics from partial recordings could dramatically expand the scope of neural circuit studies, allowing researchers to effectively study larger populations than current imaging technology permits. The authors demonstrate the method's utility on real datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, a standard model system in neuroscience, making the work immediately relevant to experimentalists.

The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in neural recording technology and provides a practical solution that leverages existing experimental data. This could enable researchers to extract more information from their current datasets, potentially saving time and resources in neuroscience research.

## Critical Weaknesses
1. **Linear model limitations:** The authors acknowledge the linear Gaussian assumption is a simplification but don't provide evidence of how this affects performance on real data. A brief analysis of where the method might fail due to nonlinearity would strengthen the paper.

2. **Parameter sensitivity:** The paper states that some overlap between imaging sessions is critical but doesn't quantify how performance changes with varying overlap. This is particularly important for experimental design.

3. **Scalability to more sessions:** While the authors note their framework generalizes to more than two sessions, they don't provide evidence of how performance scales with additional sessions.

## Strengths
1. Clear problem statement with strong motivation from experimental limitations.
2. Comprehensive evaluation on both simulated and real data.
3. Thorough comparison with appropriate baselines.
4. Practical relevance to current neuroscience challenges.
5. Well-written with clear explanations of methodology.

## Recommendation
This is a strong paper with significant potential impact on neuroscience. The method is novel, well-executed, and addresses an important practical problem. While the linear Gaussian assumption represents a limitation, it's a reasonable starting point for a method that demonstrates impressive performance in practice.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The authors should address the limitations of the linear model by adding a brief discussion of potential failure modes and how they might be addressed in future work. They should also quantify the relationship between session overlap and performance, which would be valuable for experimental design.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to bridging the gap between experimental constraints and the need to study large neural circuits, and it advances the state of the art in analyzing population neural data. It would be a valuable addition to the NeurIPS proceedings.
