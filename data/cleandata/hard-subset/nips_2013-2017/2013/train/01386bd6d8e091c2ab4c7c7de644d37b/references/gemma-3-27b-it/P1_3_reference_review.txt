## NIPS Review: Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a method to stitch together partial recordings of neural population activity, addressing the challenge of limited observation windows in large-scale neural recordings (e.g., 2-photon calcium imaging). The core idea is to model the underlying neural dynamics as a latent linear Gaussian dynamical system and leverage the expectation-maximization (EM) algorithm for parameter estimation. The authors demonstrate, both through simulation and analysis of real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, that their method can accurately infer correlations, even between neurons *never* simultaneously recorded, outperforming a naive approach that treats each recording session independently.  The work builds on existing research in latent dynamical systems and neural connectivity inference but uniquely focuses on combining *non-simultaneous* observations.

**Relation to Prior Work:**

The paper correctly positions itself within the landscape of neural data analysis. It acknowledges prior work on inferring functional connectivity from single-session data (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]), as well as methods dealing with incomplete data [12, 13, 14, 15].  Crucially, it highlights that these existing methods don’t tackle the specific problem of combining data from non-simultaneous recordings. The comparison to [12] is particularly pertinent as that work makes a strong assumption about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations that this paper avoids. The authors effectively differentiate their contribution by focusing on a fundamentally different data acquisition scenario and providing a novel solution.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The problem addressed – stitching together non-simultaneous recordings – is a practical and important one for advancing our understanding of large neural circuits.  The proposed solution is a clever application of existing statistical tools (latent dynamical systems and EM) to a novel scenario.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The framework is grounded in a statistically sound model (linear Gaussian dynamical system), providing a clear basis for analysis and inference.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The authors provide compelling evidence of the method’s efficacy through both simulated data *and* real calcium imaging data.  The comparison to the “naive” approach and the “fully observed” benchmark provides a clear demonstration of the benefits of their stitching method. The fictional imaging scenario on experimental data is clever and insightful.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The method is explained clearly, and the results are presented in a logical manner.
* **Practical Relevance:** The method has the potential to significantly improve our ability to analyze data from large-scale neural recordings, especially in situations where simultaneous recording of all neurons is not feasible.
* **Thoroughness:** The authors thoughtfully consider and address limitations, such as the simplicity of the linear Gaussian model and the need for some overlap in neuron coverage across sessions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Model Simplifications:** The linear Gaussian dynamical system, while computationally tractable, is a simplification of the complex non-linear and non-Gaussian dynamics of real neural circuits. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of how these simplifications might affect the accuracy of the results would be valuable.
* **Overlap Requirement:**  The necessity for overlap in neuron coverage between sessions is a practical limitation. While acknowledged, the paper doesn't fully explore the sensitivity of the method to the *amount* of overlap.  How much overlap is *sufficient* for reliable inference? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
* **Scalability:** The EM algorithm, while a standard approach, can be computationally expensive for very large populations.  The authors mention using mini-batch EM to improve efficiency, but further discussion of scalability limitations would be beneficial.  Could stochastic gradient descent-based methods be explored for improved scalability?
* **Parameter Tuning:** The paper doesn't delve into the sensitivity of the results to the choice of model parameters (e.g., the diagonal assumption for the noise covariance matrix Q). 
* **Limited Exploration of Biological Interpretation:** While the paper demonstrates the ability to predict correlations, it doesn’t deeply explore the *biological* significance of these predicted connections. Linking the inferred correlations to known circuit motifs or functional roles would strengthen the impact of the work.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant and practically relevant problem in large-scale neural data analysis.
* Presents a novel and statistically sound solution.
* Demonstrates strong empirical results, both in simulation and with real data.
* Well-written and clearly explained.
* Builds on existing literature but makes a clear and valuable contribution.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on simplifying assumptions that may limit the accuracy of the results.
* Has practical limitations related to the need for overlap in neuron coverage and potential scalability issues.
* Could benefit from further exploration of parameter sensitivity and biological interpretation of results.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to make a significant impact on the field of neural data analysis. While the method relies on simplifying assumptions and has some practical limitations, the authors convincingly demonstrate its efficacy and relevance. The originality of the problem addressed and the soundness of the proposed solution outweigh the weaknesses.

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should:

1.  **Expand the discussion on model limitations:** Briefly discuss how the simplifying assumptions (linearity, Gaussianity) might affect the results and suggest potential avenues for addressing these limitations in future work.
2.  **Investigate the impact of overlap:** Include a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the method to the amount of overlap between recording sessions.
3.  **Address scalability concerns:**  Provide more details on the computational cost of the method and potential strategies for improving scalability.
4. **Briefly comment on the choice of parameters:** Why was a diagonal Q chosen? Are there advantages and disadvantages to not doing this?

These revisions would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field.
