Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit into a single model that can predict correlations between neurons that were not simultaneously imaged. The method instantiates a standard linear Gaussian state space model (LDS) with session-specific observation matrices Ck indicating which neurons were observed in each session, and fits parameters via EM using Kalman smoothing. The main empirical claims are: (i) on simulations, the method recovers cross-session couplings and predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs; (ii) on two 2p calcium datasets (awake/anesthetized mouse S1), in a “fictional” split of a single session into two overlapping subsets, the stitched model predicts pairwise correlations across non-overlapping pairs nearly as well as a “fully observed” upper bound and substantially better than a naive baseline.

This connects to extensive prior work on LDS/PLDS and EM/Kalman smoothing with missing data (e.g., Ghahramani & Hinton 1996; Shumway & Stoffer 1982), partially observed neural population models (Pillow & Latham 2008; Macke et al. 2012), and latent low-dimensional approaches such as GPFA (Yu et al., NIPS 2009). The specific neuroscientific problem of inferring correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons has been discussed (e.g., Wohrer et al. 2010), though with different assumptions. The contribution here is to show empirically that a simple LDS with session-dependent Ck and some overlap can “align” sessions and recover cross-session correlation structure.

Strengths
- Clear formulation: a straightforward LDS with missing observations and session-specific observation matrices; EM/Kalman implementation is standard and reproducible.
- Useful problem: predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneous recordings is practically important for current 2p imaging regimes.
- Sensible benchmarks: “fully observed” as an upper bound and a naive two-model baseline as a lower bound; simulation with ground-truth.
- Results: good correlation prediction for held-out simulated non-overlapping pairs; in real data, stitched correlation matrices for non-simultaneously recorded pairs align well with the fully observed model.

Weaknesses and concerns
Quality/technical soundness
- Model mismatch and identifiability: The LDS assumes linear-Gaussian dynamics, diagonal process noise Q, and stationarity; fluorescence at ~3 Hz with calcium dynamics is nonlinearly related to spikes. These choices push shared variability into A, conflating recurrent coupling with correlated inputs. No analysis of identifiability or conditions (e.g., observability/controllability, overlap fraction) under which cross-session alignment is guaranteed, beyond informal intuition.
- Overlap is essential: Authors report failure without overlap and require c=60% with shared neurons for success. There is no systematic study of performance vs overlap fraction, and this substantially weakens claims about “non-simultaneously recorded” populations.
- Validation is largely internal: On real data, the “upper bound” is a model fit on the same simultaneously recorded dataset, and the stitched model’s success is measured against it, not against independent ground truth or truly separate sessions. The fictional split may favor stitching (same preparation, drift-free). The intended use case (different fields/times/days) is not demonstrated.
- Baselines are weak: The naive baseline is expected to fail by construction. Missing are stronger baselines such as:
  - Static Gaussian model (EM for Gaussian with missing data) to estimate the full covariance without temporal dynamics.
  - Low-dimensional latent models across sessions (FA/GPFA/PLDS) with shared latent structure and session-specific loadings.
  - Covariance completion/matrix completion approaches with block-missing entries.
Without these, it’s unclear that the LDS dynamics are necessary to recover correlations.
- Statistical rigor: No held-out predictive likelihoods; limited sensitivity analyses (regularization, diagonal vs full Q/R, stimulus mis-specification, sampling rate). The parameter count for A (O(N^2)) is large relative to data; overfitting risks are not quantified; sparsity priors are only mentioned informally.

Clarity
- Overall readable, but Eq. (2) is unclear/mis-typed (y = C(x + d + ε), ε ~ N(0,R)?), and some notation is inconsistent. More detail on initialization, convergence criteria, and run-time/scaling would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The implementation is a relatively standard LDS with missing data; the novelty is the application to “stitching” multiple partial population recordings via overlapping neurons. This is a modest conceptual contribution that would benefit from theoretical conditions and stronger empirical validation.

Significance
- If robust, the approach could be impactful for large-scale imaging. However, the reliance on overlap, the lack of validation on truly non-simultaneous sessions, and missing comparisons to stronger baselines limit confidence in practical utility.

Questions for authors
1. How does performance vary with overlap fraction and with the number/identity of overlapping neurons? Is there a minimal overlap needed?
2. Can you compare to a static Gaussian covariance EM baseline and to GPFA/FA/PLDS with shared latent structure across sessions?
3. How robust are results to relaxing diagonal Q, and to including never-observed latent dimensions (shared inputs)?
4. Do results hold when stitching truly separate sessions (different times/fields/animals) with realistic drift? Any evidence beyond the fictional split?
5. What are run-time and memory scaling with N and T? Can this handle thousands of neurons as suggested?
6. How sensitive are results to calcium nonlinearity and sampling rate? Any benefit from an observation model that accounts for calcium dynamics?

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and timely problem.
- Simple, transparent method; code would be easy to reproduce.
- Promising empirical results on simulations and internal consistency on real data.

Cons
- Limited novelty beyond standard LDS-with-missing-data.
- Validation confined to splits of a single session; no real cross-session demonstration.
- Critical dependence on overlap, not characterized.
- Missing strong baselines; unclear that temporal modeling is needed.
- Potential overfitting/identifiability not analyzed; assumptions (diag Q, stationarity) are strong.

Recommendation
Reject (borderline). The idea is interesting and practically motivated, but the current evidence is not sufficient: stronger baselines, systematic overlap studies, and validation on truly non-simultaneous recordings are needed to support the claims and establish significance beyond a standard LDS application.

Reviewer confidence: High.
