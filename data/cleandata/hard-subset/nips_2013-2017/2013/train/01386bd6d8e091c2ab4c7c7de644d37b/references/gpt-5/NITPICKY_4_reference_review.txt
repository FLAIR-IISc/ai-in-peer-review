Summary and relation to prior work
This paper proposes “stitching” multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations across neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The approach is to fit a linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with session-specific selection matrices Ck that pick out the imaged neurons per session, and shared dynamical parameters (A, B, Q) and measurement parameters (R, d). Parameters are learned by EM with Kalman smoothing, using an online-EM variant for efficiency. The authors validate on simulated data and on two calcium imaging datasets from mouse S1, using a “fictional imaging scenario” in which simultaneously recorded neurons are split into two overlapping subsets to mimic non-simultaneous imaging. They show that the stitched model predicts pairwise noise correlations and unobserved neuron activity better than a “naive” baseline (separate LDS per subset), and close to a “fully observed” upper bound (single LDS on all neurons).

The core technical ingredients (LDS, EM/Kalman smoothing with missing data, selection matrices) are standard and well-studied in system identification and state-space modeling. The contribution is largely an application of these tools to a practically important neuroscience setting (combining multiple partially overlapping two-photon recordings) and an empirical demonstration that this can recover cross-subset correlations. Prior work on partially observed neural populations (e.g., LDS/GLM/GPFA variants, common noise models, subspace ID with missing data) is discussed but not comprehensively compared.

Strengths
- Addresses a timely and practically relevant problem in systems neuroscience: inferring joint statistics across non-simultaneously imaged populations.
- Simple, scalable framework (LDS + EM with known selection matrices) that leverages existing tools; likely easy to adopt.
- Clear empirical focus on predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneously measured pairs; synthetic and real data analyses.
- Sensible benchmarking with naive lower bound and a “fully observed” upper bound; some robustness analyses over population size.

Weaknesses and concerns
Technical/methodological:
- Identifiability and overlap: The paper informally argues identifiability of cross-block couplings after a second session, but then reports failure without overlap. Necessary/sufficient conditions (amount of overlap, stimulus richness, stability of A) are not formalized. A systematic study of performance vs % overlap (including zero) and theoretical or empirical guidance is needed for experimental design.
- Evaluation targets: On real data, correlation recovery is evaluated against the “fully observed” model, not against empirical correlations computed from the withheld simultaneous test period. This risks circularity if the “upper bound” is imperfect. Please report prediction against ground-truth empirical correlations on held-out test data, with uncertainty.
- Model specification: Q is assumed diagonal (briefly) and R seems treated as diagonal in the M-step, but text earlier suggests allowing correlated noise. The actual constraints on Q and R (diagonal vs full) should be stated consistently, with rationale. If R is diagonal and calcium noise is temporally/neurally correlated, bias is likely.
- Stability/regularization: Stationarity is assumed by solving Q0 = AQ0Aᵀ + Q, but no constraint on spectral radius(A) or regularization is described. EM for LDS often drifts toward unstable A without constraints. Please specify stability enforcement, regularization (e.g., ridge/lasso on A), model selection, and whether the “unreported” sparsity penalty was used in the presented results.
- Stimulus modeling: It is unclear what ukt is in the real datasets (especially the awake one). For the anesthetized data, directions are discrete; how were regressors constructed? For the awake session, were task/behavioral covariates included? Since noise correlations are defined after subtracting stimulus contributions, this matters.
- Metrics beyond correlations: Predictive log-likelihood on held-out data (and per-neuron R²) would provide a more principled model comparison than correlation matrices alone. Also compare predicted unobserved activity to actual held-out traces in the real dataset (possible in your fictional split).
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is weak. Stronger baselines include: (i) LDS with shared parameters but trained jointly without using cross-block off-diagonals (ablating stitch), (ii) low-rank latent models (GPFA/FA) aligned across sessions via shared neurons or Procrustes/CCA, (iii) subspace ID methods with missing data. Without these, novelty and significance are difficult to assess.
- Calcium preprocessing: High-pass filtering with a 1s time constant materially changes temporal structure; please justify compatibility with the LDS assumptions, and report whether the time constants of calcium dynamics and sampling rate (3 Hz) are appropriately captured.

Clarity and presentation:
- Equation (2) seems incorrect: ykt = Ck(xkt + d + t), t ∼ N(0, R). Likely intended ykt = Ck xkt + d + εkt, εkt ∼ N(0, R). As written, t is both time and noise. Similar notation issues recur (e.g., ykt,σkj; undefined hk1:T in Sec. 3.1; “std( ii)”). Please fix all notation.
- The definition of c in the fictional split is unclear (“c = 60% of the neurons, meaning that the subsets overlapped and a few neurons in common”). Please provide an unambiguous definition and actual overlap counts.
- Many references are incomplete (missing years/pages), misformatted, or placeholders; some important related work seems missing (e.g., GPFA across trials; cross-session alignment methods).
- Several typographical/formatting issues: stray footnote marker in the introduction; inconsistent spacing in indices/superscripts; repeated commas; inconsistent symbols for noise.

Originality and significance
The methodological novelty is limited: LDS with missing observations and known selection matrices is classical. The application to stitching non-simultaneous two-photon recordings is useful, but the lack of theoretical conditions for identifiability/performance, limited baselines, and evaluation against a fitted “upper bound” rather than empirical ground truth weaken the significance. The empirical message—that with some overlap one can recover cross-subset correlations reasonably well—seems plausible but needs more rigorous substantiation.

Questions for the authors
- What overlap fraction is minimally sufficient in practice? Please provide performance vs overlap curves (including zero overlap) on both synthetic and real data.
- How do you ensure stability/regularization of A? Were sparsity penalties used in reported results?
- Are Q and R diagonal or full in the implemented model?
- What stimulus covariates were used in the awake dataset?
- Can you report predictive log-likelihood and direct comparisons to empirical noise correlations on held-out test segments?

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a real and growing need in neuroscience.
- Simple, practical method that many labs can adopt.
- Promising empirical results on real data.

Cons for acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty; missing theoretical conditions.
- Incomplete and sometimes incorrect notation; numerous presentation/formatting issues.
- Weak baselines and evaluation choices; unclear comparisons to empirical ground truth.
- Important implementation details (stability, regularization, noise structure) are underspecified.

Recommendation
Reject. The idea is useful and the direction is promising, but the paper, as written, has too many technical, evaluation, and presentation shortcomings. With clearer modeling/specification, stronger baselines and evaluation against empirical ground truth, and a more rigorous treatment of overlap/identifiability, this work could be impactful in a future submission.
