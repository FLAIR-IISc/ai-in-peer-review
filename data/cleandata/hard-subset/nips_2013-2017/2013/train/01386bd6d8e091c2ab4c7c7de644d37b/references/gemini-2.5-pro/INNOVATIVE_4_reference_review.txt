**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel and ambitious computational method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous, partial recordings of that population. The core problem addresses a fundamental limitation in current experimental neuroscience: the inability to record from all neurons in a circuit at once. The authors propose an elegant solution by modeling the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each partial recording is treated as a noisy, incomplete observation of this single underlying system. Using an expectation-maximization (EM) framework, they "stitch" these partial views together to learn a unified model of the full circuit. The key contribution is the ability to infer functional couplings and predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors validate this approach on simulated data with known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex by creating a "fictional" stitching scenario.

**Strengths**

This paper is an excellent example of a bold, conceptually-driven contribution that has the potential to open up a new research direction. Its primary strengths are its novelty and significance.

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness:** The paper tackles a problem of immense practical importance that has, until now, seemed largely intractable. The idea of using a shared latent dynamical model as a computational bridge to unify disparate recordings is a significant conceptual leap. It moves beyond the well-trodden ground of inferring connectivity from a single, fully-observed (or partially-observed but simultaneous) dataset. This is not an incremental improvement on an existing method; it is a proposal for a new paradigm in analyzing large-scale neural data.

2.  **Major Significance and Potential Impact:** If this method proves robust, it could fundamentally change how experimentalists approach the study of large neural circuits. It provides a concrete path to building circuit-wide dynamical models that are larger than what can be captured in a single field of view, effectively expanding the reach of current recording technologies. The ability to estimate noise correlations across the entire circuit, as demonstrated, is crucial for testing many theories of neural computation. This work could inspire a new class of algorithms and experimental designs focused on large-scale circuit mapping.

3.  **Convincing Proof of Concept:** Despite the simplicity of the chosen model (a linear dynamical system), the results are surprisingly strong and compelling. The validation on both simulated and real data convincingly demonstrates that the core idea works. The ability to recover the coupling matrix structure and accurately predict noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs (Fig 2b, 3c) is a powerful demonstration. The use of "fully observed" and "naive" models as upper and lower performance bounds is a methodologically sound and highly effective way to frame the results.

**Weaknesses**

While the core idea is outstanding, the current work has some limitations. However, in my view, these are acceptable for a foundational paper introducing a new concept and do not detract from the overall contribution.

1.  **Model Simplicity:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a clear simplification of the nonlinear, non-Gaussian dynamics of spiking neurons and calcium indicators. The authors rightly acknowledge this. While a more complex model might provide a more accurate fit, the success of the simple LDS is in itself a powerful statement about the feasibility of the core stitching idea. This paper should be viewed as a proof of principle, laying the groundwork for future extensions with more sophisticated models (e.g., switching linear models, GLMs, or nonlinear deep learning approaches).

2.  **Validation Scenario:** The evaluation on real data relies on splitting a single simultaneous recording into fictional, temporally distinct sessions. While this is a clever and necessary strategy for quantitative benchmarking (as it provides a "ground truth" from the fully observed model), it does not address potential confounds that could arise from stitching truly separate recordings performed on different days, such as non-stationarities in brain state or recording quality. A demonstration on genuinely separate recordings, even if only qualitative, would have further strengthened the claims.

3.  **Dependence on Overlap:** The authors state that some overlap between the recorded neuron sets was "critical" for good performance. This is an important practical finding. The paper would be strengthened by a more systematic exploration of how performance degrades as a function of overlap size. This is a key parameter for experimental design, and providing guidance on the trade-off between coverage and overlap would be highly valuable for practitioners.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It presents a bold, novel, and highly significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. The authors address a critical bottleneck in systems neuroscience with an elegant and powerful idea. While the methodology has room for future refinement (e.g., using more complex models), the paper serves as a fantastic proof of concept that opens up a promising new avenue of research. The strengths in novelty and potential impact far outweigh the weaknesses, which are primarily limitations of scope rather than fundamental flaws. This is precisely the type of forward-looking, high-impact research that NIPS should be highlighting.
