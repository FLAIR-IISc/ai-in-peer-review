

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper addresses a significant challenge in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record activity from large neural populations due to experimental limitations. The authors propose a statistical method ("stitching") to combine multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics and correlations across the entire population, even for neuron pairs never recorded simultaneously. The core idea builds on theoretical results about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations, but extends this to practical neural data analysis using a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) model. The authors demonstrate their approach on both synthetic data and real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors correctly cite relevant literature on inferring neural connectivity from partial observations [12,13,14,15] and identify the key gap: existing methods either require simultaneous recordings or make strong assumptions (e.g., monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations [12]). The authors properly contrast their approach with prior work on latent GLMs [13] and methods for reconstructing anatomical connectivity [15], noting their inapplicability to the current problem.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The authors present a clear mathematical formulation of the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, with detailed derivation of the model (equations 1-2) and parameter estimation procedure (using EM with Kalman smoothing). The evaluation framework is well-designed, including synthetic data with ground truth for validation, and a realistic fictional imaging scenario on experimental data with appropriate upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) bounds for comparison. The results demonstrate that the stitching method successfully recovers coupling matrices and noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neuron pairs, with performance approaching the upper bound and substantially outperforming the naive baseline. The authors are careful to acknowledge limitations of their linear Gaussian model and the need for some overlap between recording sessions.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the methods section provides sufficient technical detail for reproducibility, and the results are presented with appropriate visualizations (Figures 2-4) that clearly illustrate key findings. The explanation of the theoretical intuition for why stitching works (Section 3.1) is particularly helpful for readers unfamiliar with dynamical systems. The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed discussion of the experimental data collection process (e.g., exact imaging parameters, stimulus details), but the current level of detail is sufficient for a machine learning audience.

**Originality:** The core idea of "stitching" together non-simultaneous recordings to infer correlations across the entire population is novel in the context of neural population dynamics. While theoretical results exist about reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21,22,23], the authors successfully translate these into a practical method for neural data analysis. The paper clearly articulates how this work differs from previous approaches, particularly those requiring simultaneous recordings or making strong assumptions about the relationship between noise and stimulus correlations. The authors' literature review is thorough and properly contextualizes their contribution.

**Significance:** The method addresses a real and important limitation in experimental neuroscience (inability to record all neurons simultaneously in large circuits), with potential to significantly advance the field by enabling more complete models of neural circuits. The results demonstrate substantial improvements over the naive approach, with the stitching method approaching the performance of a "fully observed" model (which would be impossible in practice). The paper also opens up new possibilities for experimental design, such as determining optimal trade-offs between overlap and total coverage when planning imaging sessions. The authors appropriately acknowledge that more complex models could further improve performance, but the current linear Gaussian approach already provides a valuable practical tool.

**Strengths:**
- Clear articulation of a meaningful problem in neuroscience
- Sound theoretical foundation combined with practical implementation
- Comprehensive evaluation with both synthetic and real data
- Appropriate comparison to relevant baselines (naive approach and fully observed model)
- Well-organized presentation with clear visualizations
- Practical implications for experimental design in neuroscience

**Weaknesses:**
- The linear Gaussian model is a significant simplification of neural dynamics; while the authors acknowledge this, the paper could more thoroughly discuss how the method might be extended to more complex models (e.g., incorporating spike dynamics)
- Limited analysis of robustness to different overlap levels between recording sessions; the paper states some overlap is critical but doesn't quantify how much overlap is needed for reliable stitching
- The method's scalability to more than two recording sessions or very large neural populations (thousands of neurons) is not thoroughly explored
- The real data analysis is limited to two datasets (anesthetized and awake), which may not fully capture the diversity of neural dynamics across different conditions

**Conclusion:** This paper presents a novel and technically sound method for addressing a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience. The approach is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and demonstrates clear practical utility. While the linear Gaussian model is a simplification of neural dynamics, the results show that this simple model can still make accurate predictions of correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The method has the potential to significantly advance the field by enabling more complete models of neural circuits from currently available experimental data. The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the intersection of machine learning and neuroscience.

Pro: Strong theoretical foundation, clear experimental validation, practical significance for neuroscience, well-organized presentation
Con: Model simplification may limit applicability to more complex neural dynamics, limited analysis of scalability and robustness to overlap variations

The paper is clearly within scope for NIPS, addressing a significant challenge in neural data analysis with a novel computational approach. I recommend acceptance.
