

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

## Summary
This paper proposes a method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of neural circuits to infer joint dynamics, including noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors model the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) to estimate parameters from non-overlapping imaging sessions. They demonstrate the method on simulated data and two in vivo calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The core contribution is showing that noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons can be accurately predicted using this stitching approach, outperforming naive baselines.

## Strengths
The paper addresses a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience (the inability to simultaneously image large neural populations) with a novel computational approach. The theoretical motivation is sound, the method is well-justified, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate the method's utility. The comparison to a "fully observed" model (as an upper bound) and a naive approach (as a lower bound) provides a strong evaluation framework. The paper also makes clear connections to related work in neural data analysis and dynamical systems theory.

## Weaknesses and Technical Issues

1. **Inconsistent Notation and Typos (Critical):** 
   - Equation (2) contains a severe typo: "ykt = C k(xkt + d+ t) t ∼ N (0, R)". The term "d+ t" is nonsensical. It should either be "d + ηt" (where ηt is the noise) or "d + t" (with t being the noise term, but then the notation conflicts with the time index). This error makes the equation ambiguous and confusing.
   - In the parameter update for dj, the expression "ykt,σkj" appears to be a typo (should be y_kt,σ_kj or similar). The inconsistent use of subscripts and notation throughout the paper creates unnecessary confusion.

2. **Methodological Inconsistencies:**
   - The paper states that "the mean x0 and covariance Q0 of the initial state xk0 were chosen such that the system is stationary (apart from the stimulus contribution Bukt), i.e. x0 = 0 and Q0 satisfies the Lyapunov equation Q0 = AQ0A> +Q." However, for a stationary process, the initial state distribution should match the stationary distribution, not necessarily have mean 0. The authors should clarify why they chose x0 = 0 specifically, as this could introduce bias in the estimation of the stationary distribution.

3. **Unjustified Model Assumptions:**
   - The paper uses a linear Gaussian dynamical system as the core model, despite acknowledging in the introduction that "this model does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade of neural couplings, calcium dynamics, fluorescence measurements and imaging noise [16, 6]." The authors claim that "this model nevertheless is able to predict correlations across non-simultaneously observed pairs of neurons," but they provide no theoretical justification for why a linear model would be sufficient for this prediction task. This is a significant gap in the paper's reasoning.

4. **Presentation of Results:**
   - In Section 3.1, the paper states "recovery was better for couplings across observed pairs (correlation between true and estimated parameters 0.95, excluding self-couplings) than for non-simultaneously recorded pairs (Figure 2c; correlation 0.73)." However, Figure 2c caption states "correlation between true and estimated coupling matrix entries for non-simultaneously observed pairs," which is ambiguous. Is this the correlation between true and estimated values for those specific entries, or the correlation across all entries? The presentation lacks clarity.

5. **Formatting and Reference Issues:**
   - The references show inconsistent formatting (e.g., some use "pp." while others don't, and some have inconsistent capitalization).
   - The acknowledgements section incorrectly lists "Bernstein Center Tübingen" as a funding source, which is not a funding agency but a research center.
   - The paper uses "2-photon" in the text but "2-photon" in the title, creating inconsistency in terminology.

6. **Minor but Notable Inconsistency:**
   - The paper states "On average, 24% of the variance of each neuron was noise, 2% driven by the stimulus, 25% by self-couplings and a further 49% by network-interactions." While the percentages add up to 100%, the authors don't clarify how they partition the variance between these components, which is important for understanding the simulation setup.

## Recommendation

While the paper presents a novel and potentially valuable method for addressing a real experimental limitation in neuroscience, the technical inconsistencies and methodological gaps are significant enough to warrant rejection. The typos and notation errors in the equations (particularly Equation 2) are serious enough to undermine the paper's technical credibility. The unjustified use of a linear Gaussian model for neural dynamics, despite acknowledging its limitations, is a critical flaw. The paper also fails to provide sufficient theoretical justification for why a linear model would be sufficient for predicting noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons.

The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmitting. For instance, they should:
1. Correct the typos and notation inconsistencies in the equations
2. Justify the use of the linear Gaussian model for their prediction task
3. Clarify the variance partitioning in their simulation
4. Fix the reference formatting and acknowledgements

These issues are not merely superficial; they affect the paper's ability to communicate its technical contribution clearly and accurately. Given the high standards of NIPS, these flaws are substantial enough to recommend rejection. The paper's core idea is promising, but the current version does not meet the technical rigor required for publication.
