**Summary:**
This paper proposes a method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The authors frame this problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations. They employ a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) model, where the unobserved neurons in any given session are treated as latent variables. The model parameters are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is validated first on simulated data, where it successfully recovers coupling parameters and noise correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons. It is then applied to 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex by creating a "fictional" scenario: a fully-observed dataset is split into two overlapping, partial datasets. The proposed "stitching" method is shown to predict noise correlations between the artificially non-simultaneously recorded neurons with high accuracy, substantially outperforming a naive baseline and approaching an upper bound set by a model trained on the full data.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Problem Significance:** The paper addresses a fundamental and practical limitation in experimental neuroscience—the inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously. A robust method for combining partial recordings would be a significant contribution to the field.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The approach of using a latent variable model (specifically, an LDS) is a well-established and principled way to handle missing data. The application of this standard tool to the novel problem of "stitching" neural recordings is elegant and intuitive. The use of EM for parameter estimation is appropriate for this model class.
3.  **Rigorous Evaluation Framework:** The authors establish a clear and sensible evaluation strategy. By creating fictional recording sessions from a fully observed dataset, they can compare their method's performance against a "fully observed" upper bound and a "naive" lower bound. This provides a quantitative and interpretable measure of success.
4.  **Strong Correlation Prediction:** The central result—that the method can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were never observed together—is compelling. The high correlation between predicted and "ground truth" correlations (Fig 3d, Fig 4a) is the paper's strongest piece of evidence.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Overly Simplistic Model:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is primarily motivated by tractability, but it is a poor match for the known properties of neural dynamics and calcium imaging signals. The authors acknowledge this, but the implications are significant. While the model may successfully capture second-order statistics (correlations), its ability to infer the underlying functional connectivity matrix (`A`) is questionable. The reported correlation of 0.38 between the stitched and fully-observed coupling matrices on real data is quite low and suggests the model is not capturing the true circuit structure well. The paper's success might be more about fitting a covariance matrix than about inferring dynamics.
2.  **Artificial Validation Scenario:** The validation relies on splitting a single, continuous recording into fictional sessions. This controlled experiment is a useful first step, but it avoids the most significant challenges of combining real, separate experiments. True separate recordings would be subject to non-stationarities in the animal's state (e.g., attention, arousal), day-to-day changes in the circuit, and slight shifts in the imaging plane. The current validation provides no evidence that the method would be robust to these real-world confounds. As such, the practical utility of the method remains unproven.
3.  **Unclear Reliance on Overlapping Neurons:** There is a critical discrepancy between the simulation and the real data experiments. The simulation demonstrates success with two completely non-overlapping sets of neurons. However, the authors state in the methods: "We also experimented with c = 50% as in our simulation above, but failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial finding that is understated. It suggests that the theoretical ability to identify the system from latent dynamics does not hold in practice with real data, and that the method relies heavily on "anchor" neurons recorded in multiple sessions. This fragility significantly limits the method's generality and should be a central point of discussion, not a brief note in the methods section.
4.  **Identifiability and Scaling Issues:** The paper reports that the accuracy of the estimated coupling parameters *decreases* with population size. This is a concerning result for a method intended to scale up to large circuits. It points towards potential problems with model identifiability or overfitting as the number of parameters grows. While the prediction of correlations remains strong, the inability to reliably estimate the underlying parameters for larger systems is a significant weakness.

**Overall Assessment:**
The paper presents an interesting and elegant application of a classic statistical model to an important problem in neuroscience. The core idea is clever, and the demonstration that pairwise correlations can be inferred between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is a promising result.

However, the work is let down by the mismatch between the simple model and the complex reality of neural data, and by a validation scheme that, while internally rigorous, sidesteps the most difficult real-world challenges. The reliance on overlapping neurons in the experimental data (despite not needing them in the idealized simulation) and the declining parameter accuracy with scale raise serious questions about the method's robustness and scalability.

While the idea is novel and the results on correlation prediction are noteworthy, the claims about inferring network dynamics are not fully supported, and the practical applicability of the method is not yet established. The paper presents a proof-of-concept on a highly idealized version of the problem rather than a tool ready for experimental application.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Addresses a significant, unsolved problem in experimental neuroscience.
*   The proposed solution is simple, elegant, and based on established statistical principles.
*   The results on predicting noise correlations are surprisingly strong and constitute a novel finding.

**Arguments for rejection:**
*   The underlying linear-Gaussian model is a gross simplification of neural dynamics.
*   The validation is performed on artificial "fictional" data splits, which ignores key challenges of real multi-session experiments.
*   The method appears to fail on real data without substantial neuron overlap between sessions, a critical limitation that is not adequately addressed.
*   Evidence suggests the model parameters are not well-identified, especially for larger populations.

I lean towards rejection in its current form. The core idea has merit, but the evidence presented is not extensive enough to support the claims of its utility for modeling large-scale neural circuits. A more thorough investigation of the method's limitations, particularly regarding the need for overlap and its performance under more realistic non-stationarities, would be required to make a compelling case.
