Summary
The paper proposes “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit into a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) with missing observations, leveraging Kalman smoothing and EM. Ck encodes which neurons are observed in each session; parameters (A,B,Q,R,d) are shared across sessions. The aim is to recover cross-couplings and noise correlations between neurons that were never simultaneously observed. On synthetic data the method recovers cross-block entries of A and noise correlations; on two 2p datasets from mouse S1, the stitched model’s predicted pairwise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons match those of a “fully observed” model much better than a “naive” baseline.

Relation to prior work: Modeling neural population activity with LDS/Kalman EM and missing data is classical (e.g., Shumway & Stoffer 1982; Ghahramani & Hinton 1996). The authors cite EM [19] but omit these canonical LDS-EM references. The idea of combining non-overlapping recordings via shared latent dynamics or state-space models has appeared in various forms (e.g., GPFA/FA and cross-session alignment, shared latent LDS with partial observations, demixed models, and multi-session system ID). The paper’s specific contribution is an application of standard LDS-with-missing-observations to multi-session calcium imaging with identified neurons and some overlap.

Quality
- Modeling assumptions: Q is assumed diagonal; R is treated as diagonal in the M-step but not stated explicitly upfront. Correlated innovations and per-session common inputs are only mentioned as future extensions, yet they are important for realistic calcium imaging. The strong claim that A shapes noise correlations is contingent on Q’s diagonal structure; this can bias the interpretation of A.
- Identifiability and overlap: The intuitive argument in 3.1 about resolving the “linear transformation” by recording the second subset lacks rigor. Empirically, stitching “failed” without overlap on real data (c=50%)—a key limitation that should be formalized (what are the identifiability conditions as a function of overlap, excitation, and observation patterns?). A theoretical statement (or counterexample) is needed.
- Session effects: The model assumes a single (A,B,Q,R,d) across sessions. In practice, session-specific offsets/gains, bleaching, behavioral state, and nonstationarity are substantial. A global d is unlikely to suffice. No per-session bias/gain or drift terms are included.
- Stability and initialization: A stationary prior Q0 via Lyapunov requires spectral radius(A)<1. The paper does not enforce stability or describe projections/regularization to ensure EM does not yield unstable A (common in LDS EM).
- Evaluation: In the real-data “fictional” split, performance is measured primarily versus a “fully observed” model of the same class rather than empirical test-set correlations. A direct comparison to empirical pairwise correlations computed on held-out simultaneous data (the 20% test) would more convincingly validate the method. Selecting the “50 most correlated neurons” may inflate structure and performance. No comparison to stronger baselines (e.g., FA/GPFA with missing data, low-rank plus sparse models, latent LDS with shared low-dimensional inputs) is given. Runtime and scaling are not reported.
- Calcium-specific issues: Modeling x as fluorescence and ignoring the calcium/spike convolution is a simplification; for GCaMP6s at ~3 Hz, A will conflate cellular/network dynamics with calcium decay and sampling. The awake dataset’s stimulus design is unclear (if any); yet the model includes B,u. The “noise correlations” are defined after subtracting stimulus contributions, but u and B for awake data are not specified.
- Notation/derivations: The measurement equation is misstated: ykt = Ck(xkt + d + εt), εt ~ N(0,R) seems intended, but the text reads ykt = Ck(xkt + d+ t) t ~ N(0, R). The symbol hk in 3.1 appears without definition (likely u). Assumptions (R diagonal) are used but not clearly stated. Multiple uses of K (sessions vs trials). Several typos.

Clarity
The paper is readable but contains numerous notation errors/typos and missing technical details (assumptions on R, stability constraints, initialization, convergence criteria, computational complexity). Figures are central to claims but not sufficiently described in text to be self-contained. Referencing is inconsistent; several key system ID/LDS-EM citations are missing.

Originality
Methodologically modest: this is essentially an LDS with block-missing observations across sessions and known cell identities, fit by standard (online) EM. The novelty lies in the application to multi-session 2p data and the explicit evaluation of cross-session noise-correlation prediction. Claims about inferring cross-couplings across non-overlapping sessions are weakened by the empirical need for overlap and lack of identifiability analysis.

Significance
The problem is important for neuroscience where simultaneous imaging is limited. A robust, well-validated stitching framework would be valuable. However, given the strong simplifying assumptions, limited baselines, and evaluation primarily versus a model of the same family, it is hard to conclude state-of-the-art significance. The practical constraint that overlap is “critical” also limits experimental utility; quantifying the overlap/coverage trade-off experimentally or theoretically would significantly strengthen impact.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and timely problem (multi-session population modeling).
- Simple, scalable approach implementable with standard tools.
- Synthetic and real-data analyses suggest substantial gains over a naive split-fit baseline.
- Useful insight that some overlap materially improves performance.

Cons
- Methodologically incremental; missing key references to standard LDS EM with missing data.
- Lack of identifiability analysis; contradictory claim vs empirical need for overlap.
- Strong assumptions (diagonal Q/R, shared parameters across sessions) with limited justification; no per-session effects.
- Evaluation relies on similarity to a fully observed LDS rather than empirical test-set correlations; limited baselines.
- Clarity issues (notation errors, typos), missing stability/initialization/complexity details, and selection bias (most-correlated neurons).

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical conditions for identifiability as a function of overlap and observation patterns; quantify the overlap/coverage trade-off (and include experiments at varying overlap).
- Include per-session offsets/gains and allow correlated innovations via a low-rank latent component; compare to FA/GPFA/low-rank LDS baselines and sparsity-regularized A.
- Evaluate against empirical correlations on held-out simultaneous test data; report predictive log-likelihood and runtime/scaling.
- Enforce stability of A during EM; detail initialization and convergence.
- Clarify stimulus modeling for awake data, calcium-specific considerations, and neuropil correction.
- Fix notation/typos and cite foundational LDS EM/system ID work.

Recommendation: Reject (promising application, but method is standard, evaluation and theoretical grounding are insufficient, and the manuscript needs substantive clarification and experimental strengthening).
