

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a method to "stitch" together multiple partial recordings of neural populations to infer correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors model neural activity as a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, using expectation maximization to estimate parameters from multiple non-overlapping imaging sessions. The core claim is that their method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, which would be impossible with conventional approaches.

The paper addresses a genuine experimental limitation in neuroscience: current imaging technology cannot capture all neurons in large neural circuits simultaneously. The authors cite relevant prior work on neural population modeling [6, 7, 13] but fail to adequately distinguish their contribution from established methods for inferring connectivity from partial observations. While the problem is compelling, the proposed solution relies on a highly simplified model that may not capture the complexity of neural dynamics.

**Strengths:** The paper clearly articulates a meaningful problem in neuroscience. The simulation results are promising, demonstrating that the method can recover coupling matrices and noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neurons with reasonable accuracy (correlation 0.73 for off-diagonal couplings, 0.92 for noise correlations). The application to real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex provides some evidence of practical utility, with the method performing substantially better than a naive baseline.

**Weaknesses:** The primary weakness is methodological unsoundness. The authors rely on a linear Gaussian dynamical system model for neural activity, which is known to be a severe simplification of actual neural dynamics. As acknowledged in their own references [6, 16], calcium imaging data involves nonlinear dynamics, non-Gaussian noise, and complex relationships between fluorescence, calcium transients, and spiking activity. The paper provides no evidence that this simplified model is sufficient for the task, nor does it compare against more sophisticated methods that explicitly account for these complexities.

The validation strategy is particularly concerning. The authors compare their method only against a "naive" approach (which sets couplings between non-simultaneously recorded neurons to zero) and a theoretical "fully observed" model (which would be impossible in practice). They fail to compare against established methods for neural population analysis, such as the Bayesian approaches [6] or information-theoretic methods [7] they cite. This is a critical omission, as the paper claims to address a problem already tackled by previous work.

The paper also lacks thorough validation. The simulation is limited to a small population of 60 neurons with highly artificial coupling structures. The real data analysis uses only two datasets with modest population sizes (50-200 neurons), which may not reflect the challenges of larger-scale neural circuits. The paper does not investigate how the method performs under varying conditions (e.g., different levels of noise, different degrees of overlap between recording sessions, or more complex neural dynamics).

Most troublingly, the paper makes an unsupported leap from "our simple model works on these specific datasets" to "this method will enable us to map population dynamics of entire neural circuits." The authors acknowledge the limitations of their model but do not provide evidence that these limitations would be overcome with more sophisticated modeling approaches. The claim that this method "can help us build data-driven models of large cortical circuits" is not substantiated by the evidence provided.

**Originality and Significance:** The core idea of using latent dynamical systems to handle missing data is not novel in machine learning [19, 21]. The application to neural data is somewhat novel, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that this approach offers significant advantages over existing methods for neural population analysis. The significance is overstated given the limited validation.

**Quality and Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with adequate technical details for reproducibility. However, the quality of the scientific contribution is undermined by the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines and the use of an overly simplistic model.

**Recommendation:** I recommend rejection. While the problem is important, the authors have not provided sufficient evidence that their method is scientifically sound or superior to existing approaches. The paper presents a new idea without extensive validation against established baselines, which is contrary to NIPS' emphasis on methodological rigor. The claims about predicting noise correlations for non-simultaneously recorded neurons are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. A more compelling contribution would require comparison against established neural population modeling methods, validation across a wider range of datasets, and a more realistic model that accounts for the known complexities of neural dynamics.
