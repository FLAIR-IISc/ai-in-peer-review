**Summary**

This paper introduces a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the entire population as a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) and treat each recording session as a partial observation of this underlying system. The model parameters, including the coupling matrix that captures interactions between all neurons (even those never recorded simultaneously), are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors demonstrate their "stitching" method on both simulated data and two real 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. By creating a fictional scenario where simultaneously recorded data is split into two overlapping, non-simultaneous sessions, they show that their method can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were not "observed" together, performing significantly better than a naive approach and approaching the performance of a model fit to the fully observed data.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: the trade-off between the number of neurons recorded and the spatial extent of the recording. The ability to computationally combine partial recordings to build a model of a larger, functionally connected circuit is a significant contribution. If effective, this method could greatly expand the utility of existing recording technologies like 2-photon microscopy, enabling the study of circuit dynamics at a scale that is currently inaccessible.

2.  **Novel and Elegant Application of a Known Method:** While LDS models and EM for missing data are standard tools, their application to this "stitching" problem is novel, clever, and highly relevant to the NIPS community. The paper correctly distinguishes its approach from prior work, noting that it does not rely on strong assumptions (like a monotonic relationship between stimulus and noise correlations) and is focused on inferring dynamics between non-simultaneously observed neurons, a problem largely unaddressed by previous literature.

3.  **Strong and Convincing Validation:** The evaluation of the method is a major strength.
    *   The use of synthetic data with a known ground-truth connectivity matrix provides a clear proof-of-concept, demonstrating that the model can, in principle, recover couplings and correlations between non-simultaneously observed units.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is particularly compelling. By creating upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") performance bounds, the authors provide a rigorous and interpretable benchmark for their method's performance in a real-world setting.
    *   The results are consistent across two different experimental datasets (anesthetized and awake), suggesting the method's robustness.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is described concisely, and the results are presented logically. The intuition behind why the method works (resolving the linear transformation ambiguity) is explained well. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Limitations of the Linear Gaussian Model:** The primary weakness is the choice of a simple LDS. Neural dynamics and calcium imaging signals are inherently non-linear and non-Gaussian. The authors acknowledge this but could discuss the potential implications more deeply. While the model successfully captures second-order statistics (correlations), it's unclear how well it captures the true underlying dynamics. The success of the linear model might be due to the slow timescales of calcium imaging and specific properties of the analyzed data. It would be valuable to discuss conditions under which this approximation might fail (e.g., in circuits with strongly bursting activity or state-switching dynamics).

2.  **The Critical Role of Overlap:** In Section 2.3, the authors mention that they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" on the real data. This is a crucial finding with significant practical implications for experimental design, yet it is mentioned only briefly. The theoretical intuition provided in Section 3.1 seems to suggest that overlap might not be strictly necessary, but the empirical results contradict this. A more thorough analysis and discussion of this point are needed. How does performance scale with the percentage of overlapping neurons? A characterization of this trade-off would be invaluable for experimentalists looking to apply this method.

3.  **Scalability and Identifiability:** The authors show results for up to 100 neurons but also note that the accuracy of estimated coupling parameters decreases with population size, possibly due to "near degeneracy of parameters." This points to potential scalability and identifiability issues. The number of parameters in the coupling matrix `A` scales with N², which can quickly lead to an under-determined problem with limited data. The paper mentions that sparsity priors help "slightly" but provides no data. Given the ambitious goal of mapping entire circuits, a more in-depth discussion of these challenges and potential solutions (e.g., structured priors on `A`, low-rank assumptions) is warranted. The current results do not fully support the idea that this can be readily scaled to the "thousands of neurons" mentioned in the introduction.

**Overall Assessment**

This is an excellent paper that presents a novel and highly significant contribution to the field of computational neuroscience. It addresses a critical problem with an elegant and well-validated approach. The strengths—in particular, the importance of the problem and the quality of the empirical validation—far outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses primarily concern the limitations of the specific model chosen (LDS) and questions of scalability, which are reasonable for an initial paper and open up clear avenues for future work. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and its ideas are likely to be used and built upon by other researchers. It is a perfect fit for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
