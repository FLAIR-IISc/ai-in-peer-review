**Summary:**
This paper addresses a fundamental and pressing problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer the dynamics of a large neural circuit when it can only be observed in parts. The authors propose a method to "stitch" together multiple, non-simultaneous partial recordings of a neural population by modeling the entire system as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each recording session is treated as a partial observation of the full latent state. By fitting this model using an EM algorithm across all sessions, the authors show that it is possible to infer the functional connectivity and, remarkably, predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded at the same time. The core idea builds on classical results in system identification, but its application to this crucial problem in neuroscience is novel and timely. The method is validated on both simulated data and two-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, where a "fictional" stitching scenario is created to benchmark performance against upper and lower bounds.

**Strengths:**
This is an excellent paper that exemplifies the kind of bold, conceptually-driven research that NIPS should champion.

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The central contribution is not an incremental improvement but a significant new idea. The prospect of computationally overcoming the physical limitations of current recording technologies (i.e., the limited field of view) is a potential game-changer for neuroscience. If this method proves generalizable, it could enable the construction of large-scale functional circuit models that are currently far out of reach, profoundly impacting how experiments are designed and analyzed.

2.  **Bold and Elegant Approach:** The authors frame a complex problem in a simple and elegant way. Modeling the entire system as a single LDS and treating different recordings as different observation matrices (`C_k`) is a powerful and intuitive formulation. While the underlying statistical machinery (LDS, EM) is standard, its application here is highly creative and effective.

3.  **Convincing Proof of Concept:** The validation is well-designed and compelling. On synthetic data, the method successfully recovers the underlying coupling matrix and noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed units. More importantly, the application to real cortical data, using a clever "fictional imaging scenario" with well-defined upper and lower performance bounds, demonstrates that the principle works in practice. The results, particularly in Figure 3 and 4, show that the "stitched" model's predictions of correlations approach the performance of a model with full access to the data, and vastly outperform a naive approach.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is clear, the method is explained concisely, and the results are presented logically. The intuition behind why the method should work is provided early on, helping the reader grasp the core concept.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's weaknesses are primarily related to the simplicity of the chosen model and the scope of the validation, which are acceptable trade-offs given the novelty of the core idea.

1.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The choice of a linear Gaussian dynamical system is, as the authors acknowledge, a major simplification of true neural dynamics, which are nonlinear, non-Gaussian, and operate on spikes. The model is applied directly to calcium fluorescence signals, bypassing the difficult step of spike deconvolution. While a more methodologically rigorous reviewer might see this as a major flaw, I view it as a strength. The fact that such a simple model can successfully demonstrate the "stitching" principle is a powerful testament to the soundness of the underlying idea. It establishes a strong baseline and opens the door for future work using more sophisticated models (e.g., switching LDS, GLMs, nonlinear models).

2.  **Limited Scope of Validation:** The validation is confined to stitching just two datasets and relies on a "fictional" splitting of a single larger recording. While this is a necessary and clever way to create a ground truth for benchmarking, it leaves open questions about performance in real-world scenarios with multiple recording sessions, potential non-stationarities between sessions (e.g., changes in brain state, photobleaching), and imperfect cell re-identification. Furthermore, the authors note that overlap between recorded populations was critical for success. While they are transparent about this, the practical limits on how much overlap is needed and the trade-offs with total population coverage remain open questions for future work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a highly novel and significant idea that addresses a major bottleneck in experimental neuroscience.
*   **Pro:** The proposed method is elegant, intuitive, and shown to be effective in a strong proof-of-concept demonstration.
*   **Pro:** The work has the potential to open up a new direction of research in both computational methods and experimental design.
*   **Pro:** It prioritizes a bold conceptual contribution over incremental methodological refinement, making it a perfect fit for NIPS.
*   **Con:** The LDS model is a simplification of neural reality.
*   **Con:** The validation, while well-designed, is limited in scope and does not address all potential real-world challenges.

**Recommendation:**
Strong Accept.

This paper is a clear example of high-risk, high-reward research. It tackles a grand challenge with a creative and powerful idea. While the specific implementation uses a simple model and the validation is not exhaustive, the core contribution—the very concept of "stitching" non-simultaneous recordings to infer joint dynamics—is a significant leap forward. The potential impact of this work is enormous. I am willing to overlook the methodological imperfections and incomplete validation because the novelty and importance of the central idea are so high. This paper will likely inspire a great deal of follow-up work and could change the way neuroscientists think about the limits of their data. It should be showcased at the conference.
