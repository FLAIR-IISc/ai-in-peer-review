Summary and relation to prior work
The paper tackles an important and practical problem: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations from multiple partial, non-simultaneous recordings of the same neural circuit. The authors formulate “stitching” as parameter estimation in a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Each session k observes a subset of N neurons via a known selection matrix Ck; the full N-dimensional latent state represents calcium fluorescence for identified neurons. Parameters (A, B, Q, R, d) are learned by EM with Kalman smoothing over multiple sessions, and predictions for unobserved neurons follow from posterior inference. The main claims are that (i) cross-couplings (and hence noise correlations) between neurons not recorded simultaneously can be inferred, and (ii) this suffices to predict correlations and activity of unobserved neurons. The method is validated on simulations (no overlap between sessions) and on two 2p GCaMP6s datasets (awake and anesthetized mouse S1) in a “fictional split” scenario with partial overlap (c ≈ 60%). Comparisons against a “fully observed” LDS (upper bound) and a “naive” baseline that fits each session independently (lower bound) show that the stitched LDS reliably recovers noise correlations, and improves activity prediction for held-out neurons.

The formulation connects to classic system identification with missing data and subspace methods (Baum, Katayama), and to neuroscience models of shared variability (e.g., GPFA and PLDS) and latent GLMs [Pillow & Latham 2008; Macke et al. 2012; Vidne et al. 2011]. Prior work on functional connectivity from calcium [Mishchenko et al. 2011; Stetter et al. 2012] did not address non-simultaneous pairs; [Wohrer et al. 2010] made stronger assumptions about noise vs stimulus correlations. The contribution here is the explicit multi-session LDS with known cell identities and a careful empirical evaluation on imaging data.

Strengths
- Addresses a high-impact experimental bottleneck: combining fields of view or sessions to approach full-circuit models.
- Simple, scalable model and inference (LDS + Kalman + EM) that many labs can adopt; clear mapping from experimental setup to C matrices.
- Convincing synthetic demonstration (no overlap) and real-data evaluation (with overlap), including upper/lower bound comparisons.
- Predicts noise correlations (the key experimental quantity) for non-simultaneous pairs with high agreement to the “fully observed” model; also improves activity prediction of unobserved neurons relative to the naive baseline.
- Sensible discussion of extensions (sparsity, latent common input) and practical considerations (need for overlap).

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Theoretical identifiability is not established. The intuition (“identify up to a linear transform; second session resolves it”) is plausible but requires conditions (joint observability/controllability across sessions, overlap size, richness of input) to be stated precisely. The empirical finding that overlap is critical on real data suggests practical identifiability constraints that should be analyzed.
- Evaluation on real data relies on agreement with a “fully observed” LDS rather than with empirical correlations computed from the simultaneously recorded held-out segment. Since the data are in fact simultaneous, the gold standard for pairwise (noise) correlations is available; directly comparing to empirical test-set correlations would strengthen claims and avoid model-to-model circularity.
- The model treats the state as full-dimensional (N), with diagonal Q and R, which leads to a large number of parameters (A ∈ RN×N). Overfitting and instability are risks, especially as N grows; while online EM and a brief note about sparsity are mentioned, no explicit regularization, stability constraints (spectral radius), or complexity/runtime discussion is provided. A low-rank latent (shared variability) plus sparse A would likely improve robustness and interpretability (and was already suggested by cited PLDS/retinal work).
- The linear-Gaussian model directly on dF/F ignores calcium/spiking and measurement nonlinearities. This is acceptable as a first-pass statistical model, but the paper sometimes interprets A as “functional coupling”; given 3 Hz sampling and slow calcium dynamics, temporal couplings are difficult to interpret mechanistically. The authors’ caveats are appreciated but could be emphasized more.
- Baselines are weak. In addition to the naive split, comparisons to factor models shared across sessions (e.g., FA/GPFA/PLDS with missing data), or an LDS with a global low-rank latent plus neuron-specific observation matrices, would be informative. Such models can also predict cross-session correlations without requiring large N×N A.
- Some methodological clarity issues and minor typos: the measurement equation ykt = Ck(xkt + d + t) with “t ∼ N(0,R)” is confusing; indices for Rjj imply diagonal R; hk1:T appears once without definition; details on the stimulus regressors used in awake vs anesthetized data are sparse (are u and B used in the awake dataset?). It would help to state whether A is constrained for stability during EM.
- The “no-overlap” success is shown only on synthetic data with strong linear dynamics and weak stimulus; on real data no-overlap failed. Exploring how performance scales with overlap fraction and number of sessions (beyond two) would help guide experimental design.

Significance and originality
- The problem is highly relevant to current 2p imaging, and the empirical demonstration that stitching can recover non-simultaneous noise correlations is valuable. Methodologically, the approach is an application of known LDS machinery to a multi-session setting with known selection matrices; the novelty lies more in the problem framing, evaluation, and practical blueprint than in new algorithms. This is acceptable for NeurIPS if the empirical contribution is strong and well-validated.

Quality and clarity
- Overall technically sound and carefully executed, but would benefit from stronger validation against empirical ground truth, clearer exposition of assumptions/identifiability, and more competitive baselines. Writing is generally clear; a few notation issues should be fixed.

Pros for acceptance
- Practical, impactful problem with clear utility for neuroscience experiments.
- Solid empirical results on real datasets showing substantial gains over naive baselines.
- Straightforward method likely to be adopted; discussion connects to broader modeling landscape.

Cons against acceptance
- Lack of theoretical identifiability analysis and reliance on “fully observed” model as ground truth on real data.
- Limited baselines and potential over-parameterization without regularization or stability control.
- Real-data demonstration does not include truly non-simultaneous, multi-session recordings across different fields/days.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper
- Compare predicted pairwise noise correlations directly to empirical correlations on a held-out (simultaneously recorded) test segment.
- Analyze and/or empirically map performance vs overlap fraction, number of sessions, and stimulus richness; discuss identifiability conditions.
- Add regularization (e.g., sparsity on A, stability constraints) and compare to low-rank latent models (FA/GPFA/PLDS) adapted to missing observations.
- Clarify measurement model notation, stimulus usage, and provide runtime/scaling details.

Recommendation: Weak accept, contingent on addressing evaluation and clarity issues.
