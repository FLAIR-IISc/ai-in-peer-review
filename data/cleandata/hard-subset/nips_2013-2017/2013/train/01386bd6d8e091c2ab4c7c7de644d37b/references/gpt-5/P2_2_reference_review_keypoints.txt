- Paper proposes stitching partial neural recordings by fitting an LDS with missing observations via EM, assuming known cell identities and some session overlap; shows recovery of cross-couplings and noise correlations in simulations and calcium data, outperforming a naive baseline.

- Novelty lies in applying a standard LDS to the multi-session stitching problem and demonstrating recovery of non-simultaneous correlations; model class itself is standard.

- Strength: tackles an important, practical problem for 2p imaging with a simple, scalable method likely to be adopted.

- Strength: well-motivated evaluation using upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) baselines and convincing simulation and real-data results for non-simultaneous correlation prediction.

- Strength: authors acknowledge limitations and propose sensible extensions (e.g., low-rank latent inputs, sparsity priors).

- Weakness: lacks formal identifiability/conditions for success (observability/controllability, required overlap, stimulus richness); no systematic analysis of performance vs overlap.

- Weakness: model assumes diagonal process and measurement noise, which can misattribute shared variability to A and bias results; correlated-noise models are mentioned but not tested.

- Weakness: baselines are weak; should compare to FA/GPFA, PLDS, LDS with low-rank Q, subspace ID, or pCCA for correlation prediction.

- Weakness: real-data validation uses fictional splits of simultaneous data, avoiding nonstationarities and likely overestimating real-world performance; needs tests on truly separate sessions/fields of view.

- Weakness: practical details missing — stability constraints for A, initialization, regularization (sparsity/shrinkage), runtime/complexity, and sensitivity to sampling rate and noise.

- Weakness: assumes perfect cell identity matching across sessions; robustness to mislabeling is not studied.

- Quality: technically sound for LDS-with-missing-data; experimental claims supported but limited by fictional splits, weak baselines, and simplifying noise assumptions.

- Clarity: generally clear and well organized, but missing implementation details (stability, initialization, regularization, convergence criteria, train/test splits, hyperparameters).

- Originality: moderate — standard model but novel application and empirical demonstration of stitching across sessions.

- Significance: high potential impact for neuroscience, but practical constraints (need for overlap, stationarity, identity alignment) may limit direct applicability.

- Suggested improvements: quantify performance vs overlap and stimulus drive; add stronger baselines; test robustness to correlated process noise and cell mislabeling; evaluate on truly non-simultaneous recordings; report stability/regularization/runtime and sensitivity to initialization; clarify session-specific R/Q handling.

- Pros summary: important problem, simple scalable approach, strong empirical non-simultaneous correlation prediction, good upper/lower bound framing.

- Cons summary: limited theoretical grounding on identifiability, weak baselines, fictional-split validation, degradation of A recovery with N and lack of regularization/stability discussion.

- Recommendation: Weak accept — promising and useful but needs additional baselines and robustness analyses.

- Reviewer confidence: 3/5.
