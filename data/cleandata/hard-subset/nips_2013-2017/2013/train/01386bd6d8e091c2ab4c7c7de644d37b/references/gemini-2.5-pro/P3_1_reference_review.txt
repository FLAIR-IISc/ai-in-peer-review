**Summary**
This paper presents a novel computational method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings of that population where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the entire population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each partial recording is then treated as an incomplete, noisy observation of this shared latent state. The model parameters are estimated using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm that operates across all recording sessions. The authors validate this "stitching" method on both simulated data and two real 2-photon calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. They convincingly show that the method can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, a task impossible for conventional analysis approaches. The performance of the stitched model significantly exceeds a "naive" baseline and approaches an "upper bound" model trained on fully simultaneous data (in a contrived experimental scenario).

**Relation to Previous Work**
The problem of inferring functional connectivity from neural data is well-established. The authors correctly position their work relative to methods that assume simultaneous recordings (e.g., Mishchenko et al., 2011; Pillow et al., 2008) and highlight their key innovation: inference across non-simultaneous sessions. They also effectively differentiate their work from prior art on subsampled systems. Unlike Wohrer et al. (2010), their method does not rely on a strong assumption linking stimulus and noise correlations. Unlike Pillow & Latham (2008), their LDS approach is more scalable than latent GLMs. The theoretical underpinnings draw from classic results in system identification (e.g., Takens, 1981), which show that a system's dynamics can be reconstructed from partial observations. The novelty here lies in the practical and successful application of this principle to a pressing problem in experimental neuroscience, demonstrating how to resolve the inherent parameter ambiguity by combining multiple, distinct partial views of the system.

**Strengths (Reasons for Acceptance)**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental limitation in modern neuroscience. The ability to computationally combine partial recordings to model a larger, unobserved circuit is a significant step forward. This could enable analyses of circuit-level dynamics at scales currently inaccessible to experimental techniques, directly impacting how experiments are designed and analyzed.
2.  **Novel and Elegant Approach:** The framing of the problem within a latent state-space model is both elegant and powerful. While LDS and EM are standard tools, their application to "stitch" non-simultaneous recordings is novel and highly creative. The method provides a principled way to fuse information across experiments.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Validation:** The evaluation is exemplary. Using simulated data with known ground truth provides a clear demonstration of the method's capabilities in recovering both coupling parameters and noise correlations. The "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is a very clever validation strategy, allowing for a quantitative comparison against well-defined upper and lower performance bounds. The demonstration on both anesthetized and awake behaving animal data adds to the robustness of the findings.
4.  **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, motivated, and organized. The methodology is explained clearly, and the results are presented in a logical and compelling manner. The intuition behind why the method works is also explained well.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Linear-Gaussian Model Limitations:** The choice of an LDS is pragmatic for tractability but is a strong simplification of neural dynamics. Real neural activity is characterized by spiking nonlinearity and non-Gaussian statistics. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper would be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of the potential failure modes. For instance, how would the method perform on data with strong nonlinear interactions or in the presence of non-stationary dynamics, which are common in awake animals? While the method works well for the slow, filtered calcium signals, its applicability to faster, spike-resolved data is an open question that merits comment.
2.  **The Role of Overlapping Neurons:** A critical detail is mentioned in Section 2.3: the authors "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons" on the real data, forcing them to use 60% subsets instead of the 50% non-overlapping subsets used in the simulation. This seems to contradict the theoretical intuition that the ambiguity can be resolved even with fully distinct observation sets. This discrepancy is crucial and deserves more investigation. Is this a fundamental requirement for real-world data due to noise or model mismatch, or an issue of insufficient data? A more thorough analysis of how performance scales with the degree of overlap would be invaluable for guiding future experimental design.
3.  **Scalability and Identifiability:** While the LDS is more scalable than some alternatives, the EM algorithm's complexity (cubic in the total number of neurons N) could still be a bottleneck for the very large circuits this method aims to address (e.g., thousands of neurons). A discussion of computational costs would be appropriate. Furthermore, the argument for resolving the identifiability of the latent state transformation would be stronger with a more formal statement or reference regarding the conditions under which the full parameter set becomes uniquely identifiable from two or more partial views. The reported drop in coupling parameter estimation accuracy with increasing N (Fig 4, bottom right) is interesting and could be linked to near-degeneracies or identifiability issues in large, noisy systems.

**Overall Assessment**
This is an excellent paper that presents a novel, significant, and well-executed contribution. It tackles a key problem in systems neuroscience with an elegant and effective computational approach. The validation is thorough and the results are compelling. The weaknesses, primarily concerning the simplicity of the model and the under-explored role of neuron overlap, are relatively minor compared to the strengths and represent exciting avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. The paper is well-suited for the NIPS audience, bridging machine learning methods with impactful scientific application.

**Recommendation: Accept**
