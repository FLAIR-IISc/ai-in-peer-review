Summary and relation to prior work
This paper addresses a practically important problem in systems neuroscience: how to infer joint population dynamics and noise correlations across neurons that were never recorded simultaneously, by “stitching” multiple partial recordings of the same circuit. The authors cast the problem as parameter estimation in a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with session-specific selection matrices Ck that indicate which neurons were observed in each session, and fit the model via EM (with an online EM variant for efficiency). They demonstrate on simulations that cross-couplings and noise correlations between non-overlapping subsets can be recovered, and on 2-photon calcium imaging datasets (awake/anesthetized mouse S1) they create “fictional” multi-session splits from simultaneous data and show that the stitched model predicts inter-neuronal correlations for non-simultaneously recorded pairs substantially better than a naive baseline and close to a “fully observed” upper bound trained on all neurons at once.

The work connects to a large literature on latent variable state-space models for neural population activity (e.g., LDS/GPFA/latent GLMs; subspace/system identification), and to functional connectivity inference from calcium imaging (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al.). It is closest to partially observed population models (Pillow & Latham; Vidne et al.) and to multi-session alignment/identifiability ideas in system identification. The explicit “stitching” of non-simultaneous subsets via shared dynamics in an LDS is a simple, scalable instantiation tailored to calcium imaging.

Strengths
- Relevance: Tackles a high-impact, realistic constraint in modern imaging—limited field-of-view and the need to aggregate across sessions.
- Conceptual clarity: The choice of an LDS with known selection matrices provides a clean way to combine partially overlapping observations, and the EM procedure is standard and replicable.
- Empirical support: On simulated data, recovers off-diagonal couplings and noise correlations; on real data, shows strong prediction of pairwise correlations for non-simultaneous pairs relative to a naive baseline.
- Practical insight: The finding that overlap across sessions is important is actionable for experimental design; the discussion acknowledges limitations and suggests extensions (correlated latent inputs, sparsity priors).

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Evaluation against ground truth on real data: For the fictional splits, the paper uses a “fully observed” LDS as an upper bound. However, because the ground-truth joint correlations are directly available from the simultaneously recorded test segment, the most convincing evaluation would compare stitched predictions to empirical correlations computed from held-out simultaneous data (with appropriate uncertainty estimates). Please report both correlation-with-empirical and absolute error (e.g., RMSE) rather than only correlation with the “fully observed” model.
- Baselines: The “naive” baseline is very weak. Stronger comparisons are needed, e.g., (i) factor analysis/GPFA fit to each session with shared latent space alignment; (ii) an LDS with shared low-dimensional latent process and session-specific loadings (akin to multi-session GPFA); (iii) methods using shared stimulus drive plus learned low-rank noise (common input) across sessions; (iv) regularized multivariate autoregressive models with missing data. Even if not all baselines are feasible, at least one stronger latent-factor baseline should be included.
- Identifiability and overlap: The claims rely on identifiability of the joint system up to invertible transformations of unobserved dimensions; in practice, you find that overlap is “critical.” Please formalize conditions under which cross-couplings are identifiable (e.g., minimal overlap, persistent excitation of inputs, stability/observability/controllability of A, properties of Ck across sessions). A sensitivity analysis varying overlap fraction and overlap selection (random vs targeted) would be very informative.
- Model misspecification: Modeling fluorescence directly with a linear-Gaussian LDS, diagonal process noise Q, and diagonal measurement noise R neglects calcium dynamics and nonlinearities. This is acceptable as a starting point, but it weakens interpretability of A as “functional couplings” (especially at 3 Hz). At minimum, discuss how much of A reflects calcium/measurement dynamics versus network interactions, and consider a continuous-time LDS or an LDS preceded by a simple calcium kernel to mitigate this conflation. Showing robustness to non-diagonal Q (or augmenting with a low-dimensional latent input) would strengthen claims about noise-correlation recovery.
- Regularization and stability: No explicit constraints on A are described beyond stationarity. Given the potential for overfitting and near-degeneracy as N grows, please report regularization used (e.g., spectral radius constraints, ridge/sparsity penalties), initialization details, and the effect of sparsity priors you mention in passing. Include eigenvalue spectra and training diagnostics to demonstrate stability.
- Scalability and complexity: EM with Kalman smoothing scales cubically in latent dimensionality; here, the latent dimension equals N (potentially hundreds to thousands). Please characterize runtime and memory as a function of N, T, number of sessions, and overlap; report wall-clock times for the real datasets; and discuss strategies for larger populations (e.g., block-diagonal approximations, low-rank plus sparse A).
- Validation of “noise correlations”: Since “noise” is defined relative to a learned B, the inferred noise correlations depend on the model. On the anesthetized dataset with discrete whisker stimuli, you could compute empirical residual correlations after removing trial-averaged responses and compare directly. Clarify whether repeated stimuli were used and how stimulus locking was handled in the awake dataset.
- Real multi-session data: The main application uses fictional splits from one session with perfect cell identity. Stitching across real sessions faces cell-registration errors and slow drift. Even a small demonstration on genuinely separate sessions—or a robustness analysis to mis-assignments—would increase practical credibility.

Quality
Technically sound core and reasonable experimental design on simulations; real-data evaluation needs stronger baselines and direct comparison to empirical ground truth. Assumptions (linearity, diagonal Q/R, stationarity) and identifiability conditions need clearer treatment.

Clarity
Generally clear and well-organized. Some notation typos (Eq. 2: ykt = Ck(xkt + d) + εt, with εt ~ N(0,R)?) and definitions (σkj, χkj) could be clarified. Please specify whether R is diagonal. Include more details on initialization, stopping criteria, and hyperparameters.

Originality
Using an LDS with selection matrices to stitch partial recordings is a straightforward but useful synthesis; prior related work is cited but comparisons are limited. The primary novelty lies in the application/demonstration rather than in new methodology.

Significance
High potential impact for calcium imaging studies; if supported with stronger validation and more robust methodology, the approach could be adopted broadly. As is, significance is promising but would benefit from additional empirical rigor.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses an important, timely problem with a simple, scalable approach.
- Clear simulation and real-data demonstrations showing large gains over a naive baseline.
- Practical insights into the role of overlap; extensible framework.

Cons against acceptance
- Limited baselines and lack of direct comparison to empirical correlations on held-out simultaneous data.
- Weak treatment of identifiability/overlap requirements and model misspecification.
- Scalability and regularization details missing; no results on real multi-session data with cell registration challenges.

Recommendation
Borderline accept, contingent on strengthening the empirical evaluation (direct comparisons to empirical residual correlations, at least one stronger baseline) and adding clarity on identifiability, regularization, and computational scaling.
