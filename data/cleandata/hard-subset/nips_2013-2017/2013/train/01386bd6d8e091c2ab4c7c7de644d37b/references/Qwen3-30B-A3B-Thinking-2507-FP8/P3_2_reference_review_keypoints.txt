- Problem & contribution: Proposes a "stitching" method using a linear Gaussian LDS and EM/Kalman smoothing to infer joint population dynamics from multiple partial, non-simultaneous neural recordings.

- Validation: Demonstrates improved prediction of noise correlations versus a naive averaging baseline, validated on simulations with ground truth and two real calcium imaging datasets.

- Theoretical context: Builds on system identification theory for partial observations and relates to recent neural population and connectivity inference work.

- Strength: Addresses a well-motivated, significant neuroscience problem with clear potential impact for building data-driven large-circuit models.

- Strength: Solid theoretical foundation using a well-established linear LDS framework and appropriate citations to prior system-identification work.

- Strength: Rigorous empirical validation, including comparisons to a theoretical upper bound (fully observed model) and a naive lower bound.

- Strength: Methodology and technical presentation are clear, with well-explained EM/Kalman approach and evaluation metrics.

- Strength: Practical relevance shown by application to real calcium imaging data and experimental constraints.

- Weakness: Heavy reliance on a simple linear LDS that may not capture inherently nonlinear neural dynamics (spiking, calcium fluorescence) and lacks exploration of nonlinear alternatives.

- Weakness: Inadequate direct comparison with closely related work (Wohrer et al. [12]) that also addresses inferring noise correlations from non-simultaneous recordings.

- Weakness: Insufficient quantitative analysis of how much overlap between sessions is required and how performance degrades as overlap decreases.

- Weakness: Limited discussion and evaluation of scalability to more than two sessions and larger populations, including computational complexity.

- Weakness: Superficial discussion of the broader implications for neural computation and concrete examples of hypothesis testing enabled by the method.

- Quality assessment: Technically sound and well-executed, but claims are weakened by the simple LDS choice and missing comparisons/analyses.

- Clarity assessment: Very well-written and organized with reproducible detail, though some hyperparameter and compute details are missing.

- Originality assessment: Novel application of stitching non-simultaneous recordings to neural population analysis, but needs clearer distinction from related methods.

- Significance assessment: High potential impact for neuroscience; stronger ML relevance would come from linking to broader missing-data challenges.

- Acceptance pros: Solves an important problem, is theoretically sound, well-validated, and clearly presented.

- Acceptance cons: LDS limitations, missing comparison to [12], unclear overlap requirements, and scalability concerns.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions addressing comparison to [12], systematic overlap analysis, discussion of LDS limitations and scalability, and computational complexity.

- Suggested revisions: (1) Benchmark against Wohrer et al. [12]; (2) Systematically quantify performance vs. session overlap; (3) Discuss and/or test nonlinear/biophysical models; (4) Analyze computational scaling with session count; (5) Provide concrete examples of testing neural-circuit theories.
