**Summary**
This paper addresses the problem of inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The authors propose a method to "stitch" these partial observations together by modeling the entire population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). In this framework, the activity of all neurons (both observed and unobserved in a given session) constitutes the latent state, and each recording session provides a partial, noisy observation of this state. The model parameters, including the full coupling matrix that describes interactions between all pairs of neurons, are estimated using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors validate their approach on both simulated data and a "fictional" scenario created from real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex. They demonstrate that their method can successfully recover the coupling matrix and, critically, predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. This work builds on a long history of using dynamical systems for neural data analysis but is novel in its specific application to combining non-simultaneous functional recordings, a problem of significant practical importance in neuroscience. It differs from prior work which either required strong assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations or focused on anatomical rather than functional connectivity.

**Strengths**
*   **Significance:** The paper tackles a fundamental and highly relevant problem in experimental neuroscience. The inability to record simultaneously from all neurons in a circuit is a major bottleneck. A robust method for combining partial recordings to infer global dynamics would be a significant contribution, potentially enabling the analysis of circuits at a scale currently out of reach.
*   **Originality:** The core idea of framing the "stitching" problem as inference in a single LDS with changing observation matrices is elegant and appears novel in this context. While the underlying statistical tools (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to this problem is clever and effective.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The method is explained clearly, and the motivation is compelling. The experimental design for validation, particularly the creation of a "fictional" imaging scenario with clear upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds, is an excellent way to rigorously benchmark performance on real data where ground truth is unavailable. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.
*   **Quality:** The technical work is sound. The application of EM with Kalman smoothing is appropriate for the chosen model. The evaluation is thorough and convincing. The authors demonstrate strong performance on both simulated data, where they can compare against ground truth, and on real neural data, where they show performance approaching the theoretical upper bound. The authors are also forthright about some of the model's limitations (e.g., linearity).

**Weaknesses**
*   **Model Simplicity and Assumptions:** The choice of a linear-Gaussian model is a significant simplification of true neural dynamics, which involve nonlinearities and non-Gaussian, spiking activity. While the authors acknowledge this and demonstrate that the model is sufficient to predict second-order statistics (correlations), the manuscript would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of the potential limitations. It is unclear how the method would perform in circuits dominated by strong, nonlinear interactions.
*   **Identifiability and Overlap:** The authors provide an intuition that the model parameters can be identified by observing different population subsets. However, they also note that for real data, some overlap between the recorded subsets was "critical for stitching to work." This is a crucial practical detail that seems under-explored. The paper would benefit from a more systematic analysis or theoretical discussion of the conditions required for identifiability. For example, how does performance depend on the size of the overlap, the number of overlapping neurons, or the strength of the coupling between the recorded subsets? This has major implications for experimental design.
*   **Scalability:** The introduction motivates the work by referring to circuits of "a few thousand neurons." However, the standard EM algorithm for an LDS of N neurons has a computational complexity that scales with O(N^3), which would be prohibitive for such large systems. The paper reports results for up to 100 neurons. While a variant of online EM is mentioned, a more detailed discussion of the computational scalability and the practical feasibility of applying this method to truly large-scale circuits is warranted.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  The paper addresses a significant, practical problem in systems neuroscience with a novel and elegant computational approach.
2.  The method is clearly presented and rigorously validated on both simulated and real data against well-chosen upper and lower performance bounds.
3.  The results are strong and demonstrate that the method can accurately predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, a non-trivial achievement.
4.  The work is likely to be of high interest to both computational and experimental neuroscientists and could inspire new experimental designs.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  The reliance on a simple linear-Gaussian model may limit its applicability to more complex neural systems, and the implications of this simplification are not fully discussed.
2.  The critical requirement of neuronal overlap between sessions for the method to work on real data is mentioned but not systematically investigated, leaving open questions about the method's practical constraints.
3.  Claims about applicability to very large circuits are not fully supported by an analysis of the method's computational scalability.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable and novel contribution. The strengths, particularly the significance of the problem and the clarity and quality of the proposed solution and validation, outweigh the weaknesses. The weaknesses primarily represent avenues for future work rather than fundamental flaws in the current study.
