- Proposes stitching non-simultaneous neural recordings using a linear Gaussian latent dynamical system to reconstruct joint population dynamics.  
- Leverages strong local coupling of neurons to infer correlations between neurons never recorded simultaneously.  
- Validated on simulated data and two real calcium imaging datasets (anesthetized and awake) with comparisons to naive and fully observed baselines.  
- Method substantially outperforms the naive baseline in predicting noise correlations and approaches the fully observed upper bound (e.g., 0.84 vs. 0.15).  
- Technically sound: clear mathematical formulation, EM-based parameter estimation, and computational attention (online-EM).  
- Experimental validation is thorough, including a “fictional imaging scenario” to establish meaningful benchmarks.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations: need for overlap between sessions, linear model simplicity, and potential parameter degeneracy for large systems.  
- Paper is well-written and organized, with clear notation and informative figures (Figure 3 highlighted).  
- Some sections (2.1–2.2) may be challenging for readers without dynamical-systems background, but overall reproducible.  
- Novel application of latent dynamical systems to the specific problem of stitching non-simultaneous recordings.  
- Distinguished from prior work that either assumes different relationships or doesn’t scale to large populations.  
- High significance for neuroscience: enables inference of full-circuit dynamics from partial recordings and accurate noise-correlation estimates.  
- Strengths: addresses an important practical problem, strong empirical validation, clear benchmarks, candid discussion of limitations, and solid presentation.  
- Weaknesses: linear-Gaussian model may be too simple, requires substantial overlap (≥60%), scalability to much larger populations underexplored, and related-work could include more recent literature.  
- Recommendation: strong accept — significant, well-justified, and rigorously evaluated contribution.  
- Final suggestion: investigate and report scalability to larger populations more thoroughly, but this does not undermine the paper’s strengths.
