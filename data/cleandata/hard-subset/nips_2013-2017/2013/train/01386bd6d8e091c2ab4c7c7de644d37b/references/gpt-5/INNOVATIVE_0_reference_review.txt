Summary
This paper tackles a timely and important problem: how to infer joint neural population dynamics when only partial, non-simultaneous recordings of the same circuit are available. The authors propose a “stitching” approach using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations across sessions, where session-specific measurement matrices select the recorded neurons. Parameters are learned via EM (with online EM initialization), and the method exploits overlap across sessions to resolve the similarity-transform ambiguity inherent in latent state models. The key claims are that one can (i) recover cross-population couplings and (ii) predict noise correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously.

On synthetic data, the method recovers the coupling matrix reasonably well, particularly for simultaneously observed pairs, and predicts noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs with high accuracy. On two 2-photon calcium datasets from mouse barrel cortex, the authors construct “fictional” multi-session splits with partial overlap and show that the stitched model predicts cross-session pairwise correlations comparably to a “fully observed” upper bound and substantially better than a “naive” baseline that fits each session independently. The approach also enables cross-prediction of activity in unobserved neurons given activity in the observed subset.

Relation to prior work
The work is related to state-space approaches for neural population modeling (e.g., LDS/PLDS/GPFA) and to GLM-based functional connectivity estimation, but focuses on the understudied regime of serially acquired partial recordings and the recovery of cross-session interactions. Compared to Wohrer et al. (2010), which assumes monotonicity between stimulus and noise correlations, this method does not rely on that assumption. Compared to latent GLMs and PLDS, the novelty lies in using known selection matrices across sessions to merge disjoint views of the same circuit to infer correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The paper also connects to subspace/system identification and identifiability up to similarity transforms, and leverages overlap to fix this ambiguity.

Strengths
- Clear, bold idea with practical impact: “stitch” partial recordings to infer joint dynamics and noise correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons.
- Simple, computationally tractable framework (LDS + EM) that many labs could deploy immediately.
- Thoughtful evaluation with sensible upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) bounds; results on both synthetic and real data.
- Demonstrates cross-prediction of unobserved activity, increasing practical utility.
- Discusses extensions (latent inputs, sparsity) and experimental design trade-offs (overlap vs coverage).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Real-data validation uses “fictional” splits of simultaneously recorded data; no demonstration on truly separate sessions, where nonstationarity (drift, photobleaching, behavioral state changes) could degrade performance.
- Overlap appears necessary in practice; sensitivity to overlap fraction is only qualitatively described. A more systematic analysis of required overlap vs performance would strengthen claims and guide design.
- The model is a linear-Gaussian LDS on calcium fluorescence with diagonal process noise; while this can work surprisingly well, it is a strong misspecification for spiking and calcium dynamics. Comparisons to more standard latent population models (PLDS/GPFA) and/or deconvolution-based pipelines would be informative.
- Identifiability is invoked intuitively; a brief formal statement of conditions under which cross-couplings are recoverable with multiple selection matrices (with/without overlap) would add rigor.
- Baselines are limited. In addition to the naive baseline, comparisons to (i) independent LDS per session with shared parameters regularized across sessions, (ii) shared low-dimensional latent models with session-specific loadings, or (iii) subspace ID approaches could contextualize gains.
- Reporting focuses heavily on correlations with the fully observed model; additional absolute metrics (e.g., predictive likelihood on held-out neurons, uncertainty estimates) would help.

Quality
The approach is technically sound for LDSs with missing observations, and the experimental protocol is careful within the fictional split framework. Claims about predicting cross-session correlations are supported. However, the absence of experiments on genuinely separate sessions (with potential nonstationarity) is a notable gap. The paper is honest about overlap being helpful/necessary.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. The modeling assumptions and EM updates are clear. To improve: briefly detail initialization, stability constraints on A, regularization used (if any), and computational complexity. A schematic showing how overlap resolves the similarity transform would help readers.

Originality
High. While the underlying tools are standard, the problem formulation—stitching partial, non-simultaneous recordings to infer cross-session noise correlations—and the demonstration that it works in practice constitute a novel and bold contribution likely to spur follow-up work and new experimental designs.

Significance
High. If robust to realistic multi-session variability, this approach could materially expand what can be inferred from 2-photon imaging (or other modalities) and inform how labs plan serial recordings. The ability to estimate noise correlations across non-simultaneously imaged neurons is particularly impactful.

Suggestions for improvement
- Evaluate on truly separate sessions with known alignment of cells (e.g., repeated imaging days), analyzing robustness to nonstationarity and stimulus changes.
- Quantify performance as a function of overlap fraction and total coverage to guide experimental design.
- Add comparisons to PLDS/GPFA or shared-latent models with session-specific observation matrices.
- Incorporate regularization (e.g., sparsity on A) and report its effect; provide uncertainty estimates on correlations.
- Consider augmenting the LDS with a low-dimensional latent to capture global drives and session drift.
- Release code.

Pros
- Conceptually novel and practically important problem.
- Simple, scalable method; strong empirical results on synthetic and real data.
- Clear experimental protocol with upper/lower bounds.
- Likely to influence experimental practice and follow-up methods.

Cons
- No validation on truly separate sessions; sensitivity to nonstationarity unknown.
- Requires overlap in practice; lack of systematic overlap–performance trade-off study.
- Limited baselines; model misspecification not deeply probed.

Recommendation
Accept. Despite some experimental limitations, the paper introduces a compelling and novel direction—stitching partial recordings to infer cross-session correlations—with solid initial evidence and high potential impact. The bold idea outweighs the minor methodological imperfections, and the work is well suited for NeurIPS.
