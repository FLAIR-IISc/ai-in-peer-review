Summary and relation to prior work
This paper tackles the problem of inferring joint population dynamics from multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit, with the goal of predicting correlations between neurons that were not recorded simultaneously. The proposed “stitching” approach is to fit a single linear Gaussian state-space model (LDS) across sessions with different measurement matrices Ck (i.e., missing data), using EM/Kalman smoothing. The method estimates A (functional couplings), B (stimulus drive), Q (process noise), and R (measurement noise), and is evaluated on a simulated network and on two 2-photon calcium imaging datasets by creating a fictional sub-sampling scenario (split the simultaneously recorded set into two subsets with overlap). The stitched model is compared against a “fully observed” model (upper bound, trained on all neurons at once) and a naive baseline (fit separate models and zero cross-block couplings). The main claims are accurate prediction of noise correlations across non-simultaneously recorded pairs, partial recovery of couplings, and improved prediction of unobserved activity relative to the naive model.

The setup is essentially a standard LDS with missing observations and session-specific C matrices; the novelty lies primarily in the application to cross-session “stitching” for calcium imaging and the empirical evaluation. Related work on latent GLMs/PLDS and shared latent models for population activity (e.g., Yu et al., GPFA; Macke et al., PLDS; Pillow et al.) is cited, but not compared experimentally. The paper connects to subspace/system identification theory for identifiability up to invertible transforms.

Strengths
- Clear framing of a practically important problem in systems neuroscience where recording all neurons simultaneously is infeasible.
- Sensible, tractable model choice (LDS) and clean inference via EM/Kalman smoothing with heterogeneous observation matrices.
- On simulated data, convincing recovery of noise correlations and reasonable cross-prediction performance; on real data (fictional split), correlation structure is predicted substantially better than the naive baseline and approaches the fully observed model.
- The paper is generally well written and situates the contribution in prior literature, acknowledging model limitations.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty is limited: fitting an LDS with missing observations across sessions with known Ck is a standard application of EM/Kalman smoothing. The contribution is primarily empirical demonstration and a particular evaluation protocol; there is little in the way of new methodology or theory (e.g., no identifiability or sample complexity analysis for partial overlaps).
- Validation on real data is weak: the “stitching” is only tested in a fictional split of a single simultaneous recording. This guarantees stationarity and cross-session consistency by construction and likely overestimates real-world performance. No experiment with truly non-simultaneous sessions (e.g., different times/fields of view) is presented.
- Overlap is critical but under-explored: authors report failure “without any overlapping neurons” and use c=60% overlap for acceptable performance, which severely limits practical utility. There is no systematic study of overlap size vs. performance, nor theoretical guidance on minimal overlap conditions.
- Model mismatch and assumptions: linear, Gaussian, stationary dynamics with diagonal Q and R are strong assumptions for calcium imaging (slow nonlinear calcium dynamics, neuropil contamination, nonstationarity, arousal/task variables). While the paper acknowledges this, it does not quantify robustness to such violations or implement the proposed extensions (e.g., low-dimensional latent inputs for correlated noise).
- Baselines are insufficient: comparing only to a naive zero-cross-block model is a low bar. A more appropriate baseline is factor analysis/GPFA/PLDS fit across sessions with missing data (which directly targets shared variability/noise correlations), or subspace identification methods. Without these, it is hard to assess whether the stitching LDS is truly advantageous.
- Scalability and reproducibility: no runtime/memory analysis, regularization strategy, or hyperparameter selection is provided. For N≈100–200 with dense A, EM may overfit; the text mentions sparsity penalties in “unreported simulations” but does not present results.
- Stimulus modeling is underspecified, especially for the awake dataset (what is u, what covariates are included?). This matters because noise correlations are defined after subtracting stimulus contributions.

Quality
Technically sound as an LDS with EM on missing data; the inference and updates are standard. Claims about performance are supported on simulated data and in the fictional split; however, the central claim of stitching across genuinely non-simultaneous recordings is not validated experimentally. Important design choices (overlap dependence, regularization, robustness to nonstationarity) are not rigorously explored.

Clarity
Generally clear, though some equations have typesetting issues (e.g., Eq. 2) and the stimulus specification is incomplete. Provide more details on initialization, regularization, and computational cost.

Originality
Conceptual framing is interesting, but the methodological core is not novel; it is a straightforward application of LDS with missing observations. The paper would benefit from either a theoretical contribution (conditions on overlap for identifiability/performance) or a more substantive modeling advance.

Significance
Potentially useful in practice if it works with realistic non-overlapping or low-overlap multi-session data. As presented, the impact is limited by the reliance on large overlaps and the lack of validation on true multi-session recordings and against stronger baselines.

Questions and suggestions
- Provide experiments on genuinely non-simultaneous datasets (e.g., two sessions with partial overlap acquired sequentially), quantifying drift and robustness.
- Systematically analyze performance as a function of overlap fraction, session lengths, sampling rate, and nonstationarity.
- Compare to FA/GPFA/PLDS (with missing data) and subspace ID baselines for correlation prediction and activity cross-prediction.
- Include regularization (e.g., sparsity on A) with cross-validation and report its effect.
- Consider modeling low-dimensional latent inputs to capture correlated noise; show whether this improves stitching.
- Report computational cost and scaling with N, T, and number of sessions.

Pros
- Addresses a relevant and timely problem.
- Methodologically simple and computationally tractable.
- Solid simulated experiments; promising real-data proxy results.

Cons
- Limited novelty; standard LDS machinery.
- No validation on true multi-session data; heavy reliance on overlap.
- Missing stronger baselines and robustness analyses.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The idea is practical and the empirical results are encouraging, but the contribution feels incremental and the validation insufficient to justify acceptance without stronger evidence and comparisons. Confidence: high.
