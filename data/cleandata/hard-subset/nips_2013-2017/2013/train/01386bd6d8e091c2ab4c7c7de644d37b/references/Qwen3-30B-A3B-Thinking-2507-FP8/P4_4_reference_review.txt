

This paper addresses the problem of inferring joint dynamics of large neural populations from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit, a significant challenge due to experimental limitations in imaging all neurons simultaneously. The authors propose a "stitching" method that combines data from multiple imaging sessions with non-overlapping subsets of neurons to estimate correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. They model the problem as a latent dynamical system with missing observations, using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework for computational tractability.

The paper relates well to previous work at NIPS and elsewhere. The authors correctly identify that while numerous studies have addressed inferring functional connectivity from 2-photon imaging data [6, 7] or electrophysiological measurements [8, 9, 10, 11], none have attempted to infer functional connections between non-simultaneously observed neurons. They also correctly note that methods like [12] require the strong assumption that noise correlations are monotonically related to stimulus correlations, while [13] does not scale to large population sizes. The paper builds on theoretical work on reconstructing dynamical systems from partial observations [21, 22, 23].

Strengths:
1. The problem is clearly articulated and addresses a genuine experimental limitation in neuroscience. Current imaging techniques can only record a small fraction of neurons in large neural circuits, yet the proposed method leverages the strong coupling between nearby neurons to infer correlations across non-simultaneously recorded populations.

2. The method is technically sound and well-justified. The authors provide a clear mathematical formulation, with a thorough explanation of the intuition behind why the method should work (based on reconstructing a dynamical system from partial observations). The use of expectation maximization with Kalman smoothing for inference is appropriate for the problem.

3. The evaluation is comprehensive, with both simulated and real data experiments. The authors establish appropriate benchmarks by comparing against a naive approach (which cannot estimate correlations between non-simultaneously recorded pairs) and a fully observed upper bound (which would be impossible in practice but serves as a theoretical ideal).

4. The paper demonstrates clear practical utility by applying the method to two real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that their stitching method can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching the fully observed model.

Weaknesses:
1. The linear Gaussian dynamical system is a simplification of the underlying neural dynamics. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they do not thoroughly explore how this simplification might affect performance in more complex neural scenarios.

2. The method's dependence on some overlap between imaging sessions (as noted in the results section) is not deeply analyzed. The paper does not investigate how varying levels of overlap impact performance or provide guidance for experimental design.

3. The scalability to very large populations (thousands of neurons) is not thoroughly tested, despite the authors' claim that current imaging techniques are inadequate for such populations.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with well-supported claims. The authors carefully design experiments to validate their method, including appropriate baselines and error metrics. The theoretical justification is sound and the mathematical formulation is correct.

Clarity: The paper is well-written and well-organized. The mathematical formulations are presented clearly, and the figures effectively illustrate key results. The explanation of the intuition behind the method is particularly accessible.

Originality: The problem of stitching together multiple partial recordings to infer correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons is novel. The authors clearly differentiate their work from related approaches, and the method does not require the strong assumptions of previous methods. The approach represents a significant extension of previous work on neural population dynamics.

Significance: The method has substantial potential to expand the utility of existing neural recording data. By enabling the inference of correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, the approach could help build more comprehensive data-driven models of neural circuits. This is particularly valuable given current experimental limitations in imaging large neural populations, and the authors provide a clear path forward for future applications.

Pro-acceptance arguments: Novel approach to a significant problem, strong theoretical foundation, thorough experimental validation on both simulated and real data, clear demonstration of practical utility in neuroscience, well-motivated and well-executed.

Con-acceptance arguments: Linear model assumption may limit applicability to more complex neural dynamics, limited exploration of the method's performance under varying degrees of overlap between imaging sessions, scalability to very large populations not thoroughly tested.

Overall, this paper presents a technically sound, novel method for a significant problem in neuroscience with clear potential for practical impact. The method is well-justified, thoroughly validated, and addresses a genuine experimental limitation. While the linear model assumption is a limitation, the authors acknowledge this and the method still performs well on real data. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for researchers working with neural population data. I recommend acceptance.
