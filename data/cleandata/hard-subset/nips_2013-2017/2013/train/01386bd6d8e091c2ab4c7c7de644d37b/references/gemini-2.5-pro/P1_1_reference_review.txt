This paper presents a statistical method for inferring the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, non-simultaneous, partial recordings of that population. The core problem is that experimental techniques like 2-photon calcium imaging have a limited field of view, preventing simultaneous recording of all neurons in a circuit. The authors' proposed solution is to model the entire neural population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is then treated as a partial and noisy observation of this underlying system. By fitting the LDS parameters jointly across all partial recordings using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm, the method can "stitch" the data together. The key claim is that this allows for the estimation of functional couplings and, consequently, noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors validate their approach first on simulated data with known ground truth, and then on real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex using a clever "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is artificially split to test the stitching procedure.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper addresses a fundamental and highly practical problem in experimental and computational neuroscience. The ability to build coherent models of large neural circuits from partial recordings would be a major step forward, enabling data-driven modeling at a scale that is currently inaccessible. While the components of the model (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to this "stitching" problem is novel, elegant, and impactful. It provides a principled way to move beyond analyzing individual recordings in isolation.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this work. The use of a simulated network with known ground-truth connectivity provides a clear demonstration of the method's capabilities. More impressively, the "fictional imaging scenario" on real data is a thoughtful and rigorous way to assess performance in a setting where ground truth is unavailable. The use of "fully observed" and "naive" models as upper and lower performance bounds, respectively, provides clear context for the results and convincingly demonstrates that the stitching method adds substantial value over simpler approaches.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out in the introduction, the methods are described succinctly, and the results are presented in a logical progression from simulation to real data. The figures are informative and support the main claims effectively.

4.  **Impressive Results:** The central result—that the model can accurately predict noise correlations between neurons never observed together (Fig. 2b, 3d)—is surprising and compelling. The reported correlation of 0.92 on simulated data and 0.84 on the anesthetized real dataset (compared to the fully observed model) is very strong. The ability to also predict the activity of unobserved neurons significantly better than a naive model further validates the approach.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

1.  **Limitations of the Linear Gaussian Model:** The primary weakness is the simplicity of the LDS model. Neural dynamics are inherently nonlinear, and firing activity is point-process-like (non-Gaussian). The authors acknowledge this but could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why a linear model might suffice here and under what conditions it might fail. It is plausible that the slow dynamics of the GCaMP6s calcium indicator effectively low-pass filter the underlying spike trains, making the resulting fluorescence signals more amenable to a linear-Gaussian approximation. A discussion of this point would strengthen the paper. It would also be valuable to comment on whether the method would be expected to work for electrophysiological recordings (e.g., from Neuropixels probes), which have different statistical properties.

2.  **The Critical Role of Overlapping Neurons:** The authors state: "we failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons." This is a crucial practical detail that is somewhat understated. The success of stitching likely depends heavily on the number or proportion of "anchor" neurons recorded across sessions to resolve the latent state ambiguity. The paper would be significantly improved by a systematic analysis of how performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) degrades as the percentage of overlapping neurons decreases. This is a key parameter for an experimentalist designing an experiment to leverage this method.

3.  **Scalability and Computational Cost:** The paper demonstrates results on populations of up to 100 neurons. While this is relevant for current imaging experiments, the field is moving towards recording thousands of neurons. The paper lacks a discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed EM algorithm. How does the runtime scale with the number of neurons (N), the number of time steps (T), and the number of sessions (K)? A brief analysis would be valuable for understanding the method's applicability to future, larger-scale datasets.

4.  **Parameter Identifiability:** The authors provide a nice intuition for why the model parameters can be identified. However, they also report that the correlation between estimated and true coupling parameters *drops* with increasing population size on real data, which they attribute to "possible near degeneracy of parameters for large systems." This is an important finding that warrants more discussion. Does this suggest that while the model can reproduce population-level statistics like pairwise correlations, the underlying coupling matrix `A` becomes less identifiable for larger systems? This has implications for interpreting the learned parameters as representing true functional connectivity.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel, clever, and practical solution to a significant problem in neuroscience. The methodology is sound, the evaluation is rigorous, and the results are impressive. The work is well-positioned to be of high interest to both the machine learning and computational neuroscience communities at NIPS. The main weaknesses relate to the simplicity of the underlying model and the lack of analysis on key practical aspects like the required neuron overlap and computational scaling. However, these do not undermine the core contribution, which is demonstrating that this stitching approach is viable in the first place.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a highly significant and practical problem in analyzing large-scale neural data.
*   The "stitching" framing using a latent state-space model is elegant and novel.
*   The empirical validation is thorough and convincing, using both synthetic and real data with well-chosen baselines.
*   The results are strong and demonstrate a clear advance over naive approaches.
*   The paper is very well-written and accessible.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   The chosen linear-Gaussian model is a simplification of true neural dynamics; its limitations are not fully explored.
*   The crucial dependence on overlapping neurons is mentioned but not systematically analyzed.
*   Lacks discussion of computational scalability to the very large populations neuroscience is moving towards.

Overall, the arguments for acceptance strongly outweigh the arguments against. This is a high-quality contribution with clear potential for impact. I recommend acceptance.
