### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a statistical method for "stitching" together multiple, non-simultaneous recordings from different subsets of neurons within the same neural circuit. The goal is to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including properties like noise correlations between neurons that were never observed at the same time. The authors model the entire neural population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS). Each experimental recording session is treated as a partial and noisy observation of this underlying system's state. The model parameters, including the coupling matrix `A` that governs the population dynamics, are estimated from the collection of partial recordings using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The authors validate their approach first on synthetic data, showing they can accurately recover both the ground-truth coupling matrix and noise correlations. They then apply the method to 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex by creating a "fictional imaging scenario" where a fully-observed dataset is split into two partially overlapping, non-simultaneous recordings. The results demonstrate that the proposed "stitching" method can predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with an accuracy that approaches an upper bound (a model trained on the complete, simultaneous data) and significantly outperforms a naive baseline.

The work builds on a foundation of system identification theory (e.g., Takens, 1981; Katayama, 2005), which suggests a system's dynamics can be recovered from partial observations. However, its direct application to the practical and important problem of combining separate neuroscience recordings is novel. It differs from prior work on inferring functional connectivity (e.g., Pillow et al., 2008; Mishchenko et al., 2011), which requires simultaneous recordings, and from methods that infer noise correlations from stimulus correlations (Wohrer et al., 2010), which rely on stronger assumptions not needed here.

### Strengths

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental and highly practical limitation in experimental neuroscience: the inability to record from all neurons in a circuit simultaneously. The proposed method offers a principled way to overcome this barrier, potentially enabling the construction of large-scale functional models of neural circuits from existing and future datasets. This could have a significant impact on how experiments are designed and how data is analyzed, making it a valuable contribution to both the machine learning and computational neuroscience communities.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** While the underlying tools (LDS, EM) are standard, their application to "stitch" non-simultaneous recordings is a novel and clever framing of the problem. The core idea of using a shared latent dynamical model to bridge across separate recording sessions is elegant and powerful.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The evaluation is a major strength.
    *   The use of synthetic data with a known ground truth provides a clear and intuitive demonstration of the method's capabilities.
    *   The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is a creative and effective way to validate the approach in a realistic setting where ground truth is unavailable.
    *   The comparison against well-chosen upper ("fully observed") and lower ("naive") bounds provides a clear context for the performance of the proposed method, showing that it recovers a substantial amount of the missing information.
    *   The analysis across different population sizes and on two different datasets (anesthetized and awake) demonstrates the robustness of the findings.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, the methods are described concisely, and the results are presented in a logical and easy-to-follow manner. The figures are informative and effectively support the main claims.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Limitations of the Linear-Gaussian Model:** The LDS model is a significant simplification of neural dynamics, which are nonlinear and produce discrete spikes. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of the potential consequences would strengthen the paper. For instance, the learned linear couplings in matrix `A` are a first-order approximation. How might strong nonlinearities in the true circuit affect the model's ability to stitch and the interpretation of the learned parameters? While beyond the scope of this paper to implement, mentioning potential extensions to more complex models (e.g., switching LDS for handling different brain states, or generalized linear models like in Pillow et al., 2008) in the discussion would be valuable.

2.  **Crucial Role of Overlapping Neurons:** The authors state that on real data, they "failed to get good performance without any overlapping neurons," which contrasts with the non-overlapping simulation and the theoretical intuition that relies on strong indirect coupling. This is a critically important practical finding. The paper would be substantially improved by a more systematic analysis of this factor.
    *   **Suggestion:** Include an analysis (e.g., a plot in the supplementary material) showing how stitching performance (e.g., correlation prediction accuracy) varies as a function of the percentage of overlapping neurons between sessions. This would provide invaluable guidance for experimental design, which is a key motivation of this work.

3.  **Scalability to More Sessions:** The paper focuses on stitching two sessions. While the framework generalizes, the practical and computational challenges of stitching many (e.g., >10) sessions are not discussed. As the number of sessions increases, the computational cost of the EM algorithm will grow, and potential issues with local minima may become more pronounced.
    *   **Suggestion:** A brief comment in the discussion on the computational complexity and potential scalability challenges when moving from two to many sessions would make the work more complete.

4.  **Parameter Degeneracy in Large Systems:** The authors note that the correlation between estimated and "true" couplings drops with population size, positing this might be due to "near degeneracy of parameters for large systems." This is an interesting point worth expanding on. It suggests that while the model can successfully predict emergent statistics like pairwise correlations, the individual parameters of the coupling matrix may not be uniquely identifiable. This is an important subtlety for the interpretation of the model.

### Decision

**Accept.**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel, elegant, and practical solution to a significant problem in systems neuroscience. The technical approach is sound, the validation is thorough and convincing, and the paper is written with exceptional clarity. The strengths—particularly the potential impact on experimental and data-analytic practices in neuroscience—far outweigh the weaknesses, which primarily represent opportunities for future work. This paper will be of great interest to the NeurIPS community and is a clear contribution to the field.
