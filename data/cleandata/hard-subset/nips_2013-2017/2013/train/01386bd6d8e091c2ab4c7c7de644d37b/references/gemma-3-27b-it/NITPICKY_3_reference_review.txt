This paper addresses the challenging problem of inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings, a common limitation in experimental neuroscience. The core idea—stitching together information from multiple recordings via a latent dynamical system—is conceptually sound and builds upon existing work in state-space modeling and system identification. The authors demonstrate the method’s efficacy on both simulated and real calcium imaging data, showing its ability to predict correlations between neurons not simultaneously observed. 

However, while the core idea is promising, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor yet persistent flaws, ranging from imprecise language and incomplete explanations to questionable methodological choices and formatting inconsistencies. These detract significantly from the overall impact and clarity of the work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Addressing the limitations of partial recordings is crucial for advancing our understanding of neural circuits.
*   **Sound Conceptual Basis:** The application of latent dynamical systems is a reasonable approach to the problem.
*   **Demonstrated Performance:** The method performs well on simulated data and shows promise on real data, outperforming a naive baseline.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The introduction effectively sets the stage and highlights the biological relevance of the problem.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Imprecise Language & Lack of Detail:** The description of the ‘measurement matrix’ *C<sub>k</sub>* is overly simplified. It's stated that *C<sub>kij</sub>* is 1 if neuron *j* is recorded on session *k* as the *i*-th recording site. This implies a one-to-one mapping between neurons and recording sites *within* a session which is often not the case. Furthermore, it glosses over the practical issue of identifying which neuron corresponds to which recording site across sessions.
*   **Questionable Model Simplifications:** The choice to model calcium fluorescence directly, rather than inferring spiking activity, is justified, but the statement that this model "does not capture the nonlinear and non-Gaussian cascade..." feels like a substantial understatement. The implications of this simplification are not sufficiently discussed.
*   **EM Algorithm Details:** The description of the EM algorithm is cursory. The implementation details, particularly the mini-batch size and the criteria for convergence, are not provided.  The statement that the updates for *R* and *d* are “element-wise” is vague and requires more elaboration.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** The comparison to the "fully observed" model is helpful, but the description of it as an “upper bound” feels inaccurate. The fully observed model is not an upper bound on *predictive* accuracy; it *is* the optimal predictor given the model assumptions. 
*   **Simulation Details:** The simulation setup, while described, lacks crucial details. For example, the stimulus delivery and the specific type of external stimulus needs better explanation, and the impact of the stimulus on the variance of the population activity requires more explicit quantification. The truncation of coupling weights at ±0.2 is arbitrary and lacks justification.
*   **Experimental Data Description:** The description of the experimental data is surprisingly sparse. The specific parameters of the 2-photon imaging (e.g., laser power, scan speed) and the criteria for ROI identification are missing.  The definition of "fictional imaging scenario" is confusing.
*    **Formatting and Notation:** Several inconsistencies exist in the notation (e.g., use of *x<sub>kt</sub>* and *x<sub>k</sub>t*). There are several instances of awkward phrasing and grammatical errors.
*   **Missing Discussion:** The discussion section lacks a critical assessment of the limitations of the approach, specifically regarding its sensitivity to model assumptions and the potential for overfitting. There is little discussion of how the method might be adapted to handle more complex neural data (e.g., data with non-Gaussian noise).

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Addresses a significant problem in neuroscience.
*   Presents a novel application of latent dynamical systems.
*   Demonstrates promising results on both simulated and real data.

**Con:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in technical details, explanation, and writing quality.
*   Insufficient discussion of model limitations and potential biases.
*   Lack of statistical rigor in the comparison to benchmark models.
*   Incomplete description of experimental methods and simulation parameters. 



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea presented in this paper is sound and the results are encouraging, the multitude of minor flaws—technical, methodological, and presentational—detract significantly from its impact. The lack of detailed explanations and rigorous analysis leaves the reader questioning the robustness and generalizability of the findings. Although the paper addresses an important problem, the current state of the manuscript is not suitable for acceptance into NIPS. A major revision addressing the points raised above is necessary. I recommend **rejection**.
