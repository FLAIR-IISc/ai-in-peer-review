

# Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

This paper proposes a novel method for "stitching" together multiple partial recordings of a neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics of the entire population, including correlations between neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The authors model the problem as fitting a latent linear dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, where each recording session captures a subset of neurons. Their approach uses an EM algorithm with Kalman smoothing to estimate the full population dynamics from these incomplete observations.

## Summary and Relation to Previous Work
The paper addresses a critical limitation in neuroscience: the inability to simultaneously record from all neurons in a large neural circuit. While previous work has focused on inferring functional connectivity from complete recordings [6,7,8,9,10,11] or handling sub-sampled observations [12,13,14,15], none have addressed the specific challenge of inferring correlations between neurons recorded in separate sessions. The authors build on theoretical results showing that dynamical systems can be reconstructed from partial observations [21,22,23], but extend this to the practical case where later recordings of the second population allow for unique parameter identification.

## Strengths

**Quality**: The paper presents a technically sound method with thorough experimental validation. The LDS framework is well-suited for the problem, and the authors provide clear theoretical justification. Their comparison against a "fully observed" model (upper bound) and a naive baseline (lower bound) provides a rigorous evaluation framework. The simulated data experiments demonstrate the method's ability to recover true coupling matrices and noise correlations with high accuracy (correlation 0.92 for noise correlations in simulations).

**Originality**: The core idea of stitching together non-simultaneous recordings to infer joint dynamics is novel. The authors clearly distinguish their work from prior approaches, noting that [12] requires strong assumptions about noise correlations, [13] doesn't scale to large populations, and [14] deals with different temporal dynamics. This represents a meaningful extension of previous work in neural data analysis.

**Significance**: The method addresses a real experimental bottleneck in neuroscience, enabling more comprehensive analysis of neural circuits with current imaging technology. The successful application to real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex demonstrates practical relevance, with the stitching method predicting correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons with accuracy approaching the upper bound (correlation 0.84).

**Clarity**: The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the methods are clearly explained, and the results are presented with appropriate figures. The comparison between the stitching method, naive approach, and fully observed model provides clear insights into the method's performance.

## Weaknesses

**Model limitations**: While the linear Gaussian LDS provides a useful statistical description, the authors acknowledge this is a simplification. The paper could better explore how the method's performance might be affected by more complex neural dynamics (e.g., nonlinearities, non-Gaussian noise). More discussion of when the linear model might fail would strengthen the paper.

**Overlap sensitivity**: The paper notes that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for the method to work, but doesn't thoroughly investigate how performance varies with different overlap levels. A systematic exploration of this parameter would provide valuable guidance for experimental design.

**Computational scalability**: While the authors mention the method scales to the population sizes of interest, they don't provide concrete computational complexity analysis or runtime comparisons with alternative approaches. This would help assess the practical feasibility of the method for very large populations.

**Alternative baselines**: The naive baseline is appropriate, but the paper could have compared against other approaches that might handle partial observations differently, such as more sophisticated imputation methods or alternative dimensionality reduction techniques.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed paper that makes a significant contribution to the analysis of neural population data. The method addresses a genuine experimental limitation with clear practical applications, and the results are compelling. The paper meets NIPS standards for quality, with sound methodology and thorough validation.

The main weaknesses relate to the simplicity of the model and insufficient exploration of key parameters (like overlap between sessions). However, these are reasonable limitations for an initial methodological contribution, and the authors acknowledge these in their discussion.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field of neural data analysis. The method has immediate applicability to current experimental neuroscience and addresses a real bottleneck in the field. The technical soundness, thorough validation, and clear writing make this a strong candidate for NIPS.

**Suggestions for Improvement**:
1. Expand the discussion of model limitations, particularly regarding how the method might perform with more complex neural dynamics.
2. Provide a systematic analysis of how performance varies with different levels of overlap between recording sessions.
3. Consider adding a brief computational complexity analysis to help readers assess scalability.
4. Briefly discuss potential extensions to more sophisticated dynamical models (e.g., incorporating nonlinearities or spiking dynamics).

The paper represents an important step toward more comprehensive neural circuit analysis and would be valuable to the NIPS community.
