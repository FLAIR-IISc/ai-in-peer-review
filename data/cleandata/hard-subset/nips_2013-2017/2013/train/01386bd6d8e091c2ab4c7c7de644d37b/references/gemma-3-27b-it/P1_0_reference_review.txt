## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for stitching together multiple, partial recordings of the same neural circuit to infer the joint dynamics and correlations of a larger population of neurons than can be simultaneously observed with current technology. The core idea leverages a latent dynamical system (specifically, a linear Gaussian model) and uses Expectation-Maximization (EM) to estimate parameters from the combined, incomplete data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on both simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, showing that it can accurately predict noise correlations and activity patterns even for neurons never simultaneously recorded. This work addresses a crucial challenge in neuroscience: inferring the activity of large, interconnected neural circuits given the limitations of current recording technologies.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The authors correctly position their work within the context of existing approaches for inferring neural connectivity and dynamics. They acknowledge related work using detailed biophysical models of calcium signaling (e.g., [6]), information-theoretic approaches ([7]), and methods for dealing with subsampled data ([12, 13, 14, 15]).  Crucially, they highlight how their method differs from these previous approaches by explicitly addressing the problem of inferring connections and correlations *between* non-simultaneously observed neurons, a challenge not tackled by the cited works. The connection to latent dynamical systems is well-established ([13]), but the application to this specific "stitching" problem and the focus on correlation prediction are novel.  The reference to Takens' embedding theorem ([23]) provides a useful theoretical grounding.

**Strengths:**

* **Addresses an Important Problem:** The paper tackles a significant limitation in current neuroscience research – the inability to simultaneously record from large neural populations.
* **Principled Approach:** The use of a latent dynamical system provides a statistically sound framework for combining incomplete observations. The choice of a linear Gaussian model, while simplifying, allows for tractable inference and clear demonstration of the core concept.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:**  The results on both simulated and real data are compelling. The ability to predict noise correlations, particularly in the real data, is a significant achievement. The comparison to upper and lower bounds (fully observed model and naive approach) provides a clear benchmark for evaluating performance.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The methods section is detailed and explains the mathematical formulation clearly. The results are presented with informative figures and quantitative evaluations.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details about the data and methods to enable reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linear Gaussian Assumption:** The simplifying assumption of a linear Gaussian dynamical system limits the applicability of the method to more complex neural dynamics.  Non-linearities and non-Gaussianity are prevalent in neural systems, and neglecting these could lead to inaccuracies. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it's a substantial one.
* **Sensitivity to Overlap:** The authors mention that some overlap between recording sessions is critical for stitching performance, and that performance drops with fewer overlapping neurons. The impact of varying levels of overlap and optimal strategies for selecting recording locations require further investigation.
* **Scalability:** While the authors mention scaling challenges, the computational cost of the EM algorithm, particularly for very large populations, is a potential concern. More efficient inference algorithms or approximation techniques might be needed for larger-scale applications.
* **Limited Exploration of Model Complexity:** The paper focuses primarily on a single model (linear Gaussian).  Exploring other latent dynamical system models (e.g., with non-linearities or time-varying parameters) could potentially improve performance.
* **Parameter Tuning & Robustness:** The authors do not extensively discuss parameter tuning or the robustness of the method to variations in model parameters. Assessing the sensitivity of the results to these factors would strengthen the paper.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a crucial problem in neuroscience with a novel and principled approach.
* The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the effectiveness of the method.
* The paper is well-written, clear, and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.
* The method has the potential to significantly advance our understanding of neural circuit function.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The strong assumption of a linear Gaussian model limits the applicability of the method.
* The sensitivity to overlap between recording sessions and scalability concerns could restrict its practical use.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of neural data analysis. While the linear Gaussian assumption is a limitation, the method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. The ability to predict noise correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons is particularly impressive. The strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. 

**Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  

I recommend accepting this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I suggest the authors:

*   Discuss the implications of the linear Gaussian assumption and potential avenues for incorporating non-linearities in future work.
*   Provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of varying levels of overlap between recording sessions.
*   Address the scalability concerns and discuss potential strategies for improving computational efficiency.
*   Include a brief discussion of parameter tuning and the robustness of the method.
