### NIPS Review

**Summary:**
This paper introduces a statistical method to infer the joint dynamics of a large neural population from multiple, partial recordings where different subsets of neurons are observed in different sessions. The core idea is to model the entire population as a single latent linear dynamical system (LDS), where each recording session provides a partial, noisy observation of the full system's state. The authors use an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to fit the parameters of this unified model. The main contribution is demonstrating that this "stitching" approach can successfully estimate the functional connectivity and, crucially, predict noise correlations between pairs of neurons that were never recorded simultaneously. The method is validated on both simulated data with a known ground truth and on real 2-photon calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, where a "fictional imaging scenario" is cleverly used for evaluation against an upper and lower bound.

**Evaluation against Reviewing Criteria:**

**Quality: High**
The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by a thoughtful and robust set of experiments.

*   **Strengths:** The experimental validation is a major strength.
    1.  The use of simulated data with a known ground truth provides a clear and convincing proof-of-concept, demonstrating that the model can recover both coupling parameters and noise correlations for non-simultaneously observed pairs.
    2.  The "fictional imaging scenario" applied to real data is a clever and effective way to create a pseudo-ground-truth for a problem where the real ground truth is inaccessible.
    3.  Benchmarking the "stitching" method against both a "fully observed" (upper bound) and a "naive" (lower bound) model provides a clear, quantitative context for the performance, showing it performs significantly better than a simple baseline and approaches the theoretical best-case scenario.

*   **Weaknesses:** The primary weakness lies in the simplicity of the model chosen. The authors correctly acknowledge that a linear-Gaussian model is a significant simplification of true neural dynamics, which involve non-linearities, non-Gaussian noise, and spiking activity. While they demonstrate it works well for predicting second-order statistics (correlations), it is unclear how well this approach would generalize if the underlying dynamics were more complex or if the goal were to predict higher-order statistics. Furthermore, the paper notes that the recovery of individual coupling parameters degrades with increasing population size, which raises questions about the method's scalability to the very large circuits mentioned in the introduction (e.g., thousands of neurons).

**Clarity: High**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the methods are explained concisely, and the results are presented logically.

*   The introduction clearly frames the problem and its importance in the context of experimental neuroscience.
*   The description of the LDS model with partial observations (Eq. 1 and 2) is standard but effectively explained. The use of the measurement matrix C_k to handle different recording sessions is elegant and easy to follow.
*   The figures are informative and support the main claims of the paper well. Figure 2, in particular, provides a clear visual demonstration of the method's success on simulated data.
*   The authors provide a good intuition in Section 3.1 for why the method works, framing the problem in terms of system identification from partial observations.

**Originality: High**
While the components of the method (LDS, EM) are standard machine learning tools, their application to this specific problem of stitching non-simultaneous neural recordings is novel and creative. Previous work has either focused on inferring connectivity from fully-observed data or used much stronger assumptions (e.g., relating noise and signal correlations). This paper presents a principled, data-driven framework for a problem of significant practical importance that has not been adequately addressed before. The formulation of the problem itself—as a single latent system observed through different partial measurement channels—is the key original contribution.

**Significance: Very High**
The potential impact of this work is substantial. It directly addresses a fundamental limitation in experimental neuroscience: the trade-off between the number of neurons recorded and the recording duration/quality. This method provides a computational means to bypass this limitation, potentially enabling the construction of functional models of much larger circuits than can be currently observed simultaneously. If this technique proves robust and scalable, it could become a standard tool for analyzing data from techniques like 2-photon microscopy, allowing researchers to build more comprehensive models of neural circuits. The problem is important, the proposed solution is elegant, and the results suggest it is practical. This work is therefore highly likely to be used and built upon by both computational and experimental neuroscientists.

**Strengths and Weaknesses Summary:**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Addresses a critical, unsolved problem in systems neuroscience with high potential impact.
*   Proposes a novel and principled statistical framework for combining non-simultaneous recordings.
*   The validation methodology is rigorous and convincing, using both simulated and real data with well-designed controls (upper and lower bounds).
*   The paper is extremely well-written, clear, and easy to understand.
*   The results are strong, showing accurate prediction of correlations between never-simultaneously-observed neurons.

**Arguments for Rejection (or points for improvement):**
*   The model (LDS) is a simplification of neural dynamics. While sufficient for the demonstrated task, this is a key limitation.
*   The paper reports that some overlap between recorded populations was necessary for good performance on real data. This is a crucial practical constraint that is not explored systematically. An analysis of how performance varies with the degree of overlap would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   Scalability concerns are not fully addressed. Performance on coupling parameter estimation degrades with size, and the method is only tested on up to 100 neurons, far from the thousands mentioned as the ultimate goal.

**Overall Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper that I would strongly recommend for acceptance. The significance of the problem, the novelty of the approach, and the quality of the execution and presentation are all very high. The weaknesses, particularly the simplicity of the LDS model and the unexamined role of neuronal overlap, are valid limitations but are better viewed as avenues for future work rather than fatal flaws. This work represents a significant step forward in our ability to model large-scale neural circuits and is a perfect fit for the NIPS audience.
