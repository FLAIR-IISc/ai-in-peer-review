- Proposes “stitching” partially overlapping recordings by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations, learning shared A/B and session-specific selection matrices Ck to recover cross-couplings and noise correlations.  
- Builds on classical LDS/Kalman smoothing/EM and connects to subspace ID, multi-view latent models, and prior connectivity/latent-factor work; specifically targets non-simultaneous recording with known neuron identities.  

Strengths:
- Clear, tractable formulation using known selection matrices and straightforward EM learning/inference.  
- Convincing synthetic results showing recovery of off-diagonal A blocks, noise correlations, and cross-prediction of unobserved activity.  
- Real-data demos on two 2-photon datasets show improved prediction of correlations for non-simultaneously measured pairs versus a naive baseline and approach a model-based upper bound.  
- High practical relevance and easy-to-implement method with extensions possible.

Weaknesses / Clarifications needed:
- Limited methodological novelty: mainly an application of standard LDS with missing data and shared parameters.  
- No identifiability analysis: paper claims overlap is “critical” but lacks theoretical/empirical study on required overlap fraction, stimulus richness, observability/controllability, or when latent-space transforms can be fixed.  
- Model misspecification: treats fluorescence as the latent state, ignores calcium/spiking nonlinearity and AR kinetics, and assumes diagonal Q/R which can bias inferred couplings and noise correlations.  
- Evaluation choices questionable: relies on a “fully observed LDS” upper bound rather than comparing predicted noise correlations to empirical held-out simultaneous data after removing stimulus drive.  
- Weak baselines: naive baseline insufficient; missing comparisons to FA/GPFA, low-rank LDS, subspace ID with missing outputs, or static graphical models.  
- Scalability and regularization concerns: full N×N A is high-dimensional (N≈100–200), no reported regularization/stability constraints, no held-out likelihoods, and unsupported claims about scaling to thousands of neurons.  
- Nonstationarity and experimental details unclear: fictional split may violate shared-parameter assumption due to slow drifts; stimulus and behavioral covariate definitions (awake data) not clarified.  

Quality:
- Technically sound as an application of LDS/EM, but evaluation and assumptions (diagonal Q/R, linear fluorescence) limit strength of conclusions.

Clarity:
- Generally clear and organized, but has notation issues (e.g., Eq. 2), and lacks EM schedule, stability constraint details, and stimulus definitions.

Originality:
- Moderate: uses standard methods in a useful application (stitching multi-session imaging) but not a major methodological advance.

Significance:
- High practical relevance for neuroscience labs; moderate novelty/significance for a methods conference without stronger analysis and evaluation.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Provide theoretical or empirical identifiability analysis and ablations over overlap fraction and stimulus richness.  
- Compare predicted noise correlations to empirical correlations on held-out simultaneous data, with confidence intervals.  
- Add stronger baselines (FA/GPFA, low-rank LDS, subspace ID, static covariance EM).  
- Include regularization (sparsity, stability constraints) or low-rank components to capture common inputs and report effects.  
- Consider modeling calcium dynamics or using spike deconvolution to reduce model mismatch.  
- Report computational complexity, runtimes, and discuss scaling strategies.

Pros:
- Simple, practical, easy to adopt; demonstrated on real datasets; addresses a common experimental problem.

Cons:
- Limited methodological novelty; incomplete analysis of identifiability/overlap; evaluation relies on model-based upper bound and weak baselines; strong modeling assumptions.

Recommendation:
- Borderline accept: timely and useful with encouraging empirical results, but needs stronger evaluation and identifiability analysis to increase impact.
