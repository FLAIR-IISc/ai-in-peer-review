## Review of "Inferring neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of inferring the dynamics of a complete neural population from incomplete, spatially-separated recordings. The authors propose a statistical method – essentially applying a latent dynamical system (LDS) framework with the EM algorithm – to "stitch" together observations from multiple imaging sessions, effectively leveraging the strong coupling between nearby neurons. They demonstrate, both on simulated data and real calcium imaging data from mouse somatosensory cortex, that their method can accurately predict correlations, including noise correlations, between neurons that were *not* simultaneously recorded. This is a significant step towards reconstructing the activity of larger circuits than currently feasible with standard imaging techniques.

**Relation to Previous Work:**

The introduction accurately situates this work within the broader field of neural connectivity inference. The authors acknowledge existing work on functional connectivity estimation from calcium imaging and electrophysiology (e.g., [6, 7, 8, 9, 10, 11]), as well as attempts to deal with sub-sampled observations ([12, 13, 14, 15]).  Crucially, they clearly articulate how their approach differs – focusing specifically on inferring connections between *non-simultaneously* observed neurons, a gap not addressed by previous methods. The comparison to [12] is particularly helpful in highlighting the advantage of avoiding strong assumptions about the relationship between stimulus and noise correlations.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The core idea of leveraging spatial proximity and strong local coupling to infer the dynamics of a larger, unobserved population is novel and addresses a practical limitation in current neuroscience research.  While the underlying statistical tools (LDS, EM) are established, their application to this specific problem is a meaningful contribution.
* **Soundness:** The mathematical formulation is clear and the use of a linear Gaussian dynamical system, while simplifying, provides a tractable framework for analysis and theoretical understanding. The simulations convincingly demonstrate the method’s ability to recover ground truth parameters.
* **Empirical Validation:**  Applying the method to real calcium imaging data adds significant weight to the findings.  The comparison to both a fully observed model and a naive approach provides a useful benchmark and demonstrates the clear advantage of their stitching method.  The results on the awake dataset, with higher correlation levels, are particularly compelling.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The explanations of the model and the experimental setup are clear and concise. The figures are helpful and visually illustrate the key concepts and results.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Linearity Assumption:** The reliance on a linear Gaussian dynamical system is a limitation. Neural circuits are inherently non-linear, and calcium dynamics are also non-Gaussian. While the authors acknowledge this, the extent to which the linearity assumption impacts the accuracy of the results for real data is not fully explored.  A brief discussion of how the framework could be extended to incorporate non-linearities would be valuable.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention using online-EM and mini-batches to improve computational efficiency, the scaling of the method to truly large populations (thousands of neurons) is not thoroughly addressed. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential bottlenecks would be beneficial.
* **Overlap Requirement:**  The finding that some overlap between recording sessions is crucial for successful stitching is important but not fully explored. What is the minimum overlap required?  How does performance degrade as overlap decreases? This could be further investigated and discussed.
* **Parameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t delve into the sensitivity of the results to parameter choices (e.g., the diagonal assumption on the noise covariance matrix Q). A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the robustness of the conclusions.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem in neuroscience with a novel and well-motivated approach. The technical contributions are sound, the empirical validation is convincing, and the paper is clearly written. The limitations, primarily related to the linearity assumption and computational cost, are acknowledged but could be further discussed.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Addresses a significant bottleneck in current neural circuit analysis.
* Provides a novel and theoretically sound method for inferring population dynamics.
* Demonstrates compelling results on both simulated and real data.
* Is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The linearity assumption may limit the applicability of the method to more complex neural circuits.
* Computational scalability to very large populations is not fully addressed.
* Parameter sensitivity is not thoroughly investigated.

**Rating:**

Strong Accept (7/10) –  I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance to NIPS. Addressing the weaknesses identified above in a future iteration would further strengthen the work. I encourage the authors to consider extensions to incorporate non-linearities and to provide a more detailed analysis of computational scalability.
