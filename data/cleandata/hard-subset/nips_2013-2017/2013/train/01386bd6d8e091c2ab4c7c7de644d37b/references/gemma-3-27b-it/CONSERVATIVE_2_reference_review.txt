This paper addresses the problem of inferring neural population dynamics from partial recordings of the same neural circuit. The core idea – “stitching” together data from multiple, non-simultaneous recordings – is intuitively appealing given the limitations of current imaging technology. The authors propose a latent dynamical system framework, specifically a linear Gaussian model, to combine these partial observations and predict correlations between neurons not simultaneously recorded. They demonstrate the approach on simulated data and two calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex.

The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The motivation is well-explained, and the mathematical formulation, while fairly standard, is presented concisely. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work, including methods for inferring connectivity and dealing with subsampled data, and they clearly differentiate their approach. The experimental setup and data analysis are described with sufficient detail to allow for replication, which is commendable. 

However, despite these strengths, I find myself unconvinced of the paper’s overall significance and novelty for NIPS. My primary concern stems from the reliance on a very simple, linear Gaussian dynamical system. While the authors acknowledge this simplification and state that it’s for tractability, the extent to which the findings generalize beyond this specific model is unclear. The core contribution, the ability to predict correlations from partial observations, feels largely driven by the properties of the linear Gaussian model itself and the inherent identifiability of such systems, as alluded to by the authors referencing Takens’ embedding theorem. The authors demonstrate that the method *can* work, but the demonstration feels more like a validation of existing theoretical principles than a breakthrough in methodology. 

Furthermore, the presentation of results lacks sufficient critical evaluation. The authors consistently compare their method to an upper bound (“fully observed” model) and a lower bound (“naive” model). While useful, this framing doesn’t address the more relevant question: how does this method perform compared to other, more sophisticated approaches for dealing with incomplete data, or more advanced dynamical systems models? The comparisons to existing literature in the introduction are cursory, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial improvement over existing techniques in a realistic scenario. The “fictional imaging scenario” seems contrived, and the parameter choice of 60% overlap feels arbitrary. The performance gains over the naive approach, while statistically significant, seem predictable given the assumptions of the model. The authors highlight that their model performs close to the fully observed model. But the fully observed model is not an achievable result in practice and is, thus, a weak benchmark.

The simulation results, while demonstrating the method’s ability to recover ground truth parameters, are not particularly compelling. The simulated network is relatively simple, and the parameters are chosen to facilitate recovery. The fact that performance is better for observed pairs than non-observed pairs is expected and doesn't represent a major achievement. The discussion of the impact of population size on correlation recovery is interesting, but the observed trend (decreasing correlation with increasing size) warrants further investigation and is not fully explained.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Clear presentation of a technically sound method.
*   Demonstration of the method on real calcium imaging data.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem in neuroscience.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Reliance on a simplistic linear Gaussian model limits generalizability.
*   Lack of comparison to more advanced techniques for dealing with incomplete data.
*   Results primarily validate existing theoretical principles rather than presenting a novel methodological contribution.
*   Insufficient critical evaluation of the method’s limitations and assumptions.
*   The significance of the results, beyond demonstrating a theoretically expected property of linear systems, is questionable.



Overall, while this paper presents a well-executed study, I believe it falls short of the originality and significance criteria for NIPS. The method's core contribution feels more like an application of existing theoretical concepts than a groundbreaking innovation. I would recommend rejection at this time, but encourage the authors to consider submitting a revised version to a more specialized neuroscience journal after addressing the concerns outlined above, perhaps by exploring more complex dynamical systems models and comparing their performance to more sophisticated existing methods.
