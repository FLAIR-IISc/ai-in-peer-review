- Summary: Proposes “stitching” partial neural recordings by fitting a shared LDS (shared A,B,Q,R) with session-specific observation matrices Ck, learned via EM/online EM, to predict cross-session correlations and impute activity.  
- Relation to prior work: Builds on LDS/PLDS/GPFA and Kalman smoothing with missing data; novelty is framing and empirical demonstration of stitching non-simultaneous partial observations rather than a new model class.  

Strengths
- Problem relevance: Addresses an important experimental bottleneck—inability to image large circuits simultaneously.  
- Simplicity and scalability: Uses a standard, tractable LDS amenable to mini-batch EM and multiple sessions via distinct Ck.  
- Empirical performance: Simulations recover cross-block couplings and noise correlations; on real data stitched models match a “fully observed” upper bound and beat naive baselines.  
- Clear framing and benchmarking: Distinguishes noise vs stimulus correlations and uses upper/lower bounds for comparison.  

Weaknesses / Concerns
- Identifiability not formalized: No rigorous conditions given for recovering A,B,Q from partial sequences; intuition is insufficient.  
- Zero-overlap ambiguity: Off-diagonal blocks of A may be unidentifiable without overlap unless strong assumptions hold; simulations succeed but real data fails without overlap—needs explanation.  
- Ambiguity of state interpretation: The LDS linear-transformation ambiguity and identifiability as a function of overlap, controllability/observability, and input excitation are not clarified.  
- Real-data evaluation limited: Main real-data tests use fictional splits of simultaneous recordings, which may overestimate utility on truly non-simultaneous fields with drift/nonstationarity.  
- Upper-bound comparison unclear: Unclear if reported correlations are compared to empirical held-out correlations or only to a fully observed model fit.  
- Modeling mismatch: Linear-Gaussian dynamics on slow calcium signals is a coarse approximation and impact is not quantified.  
- Noise model constraints: Q and R forced diagonal could misattribute correlated noise to A; no ablation with low-rank Q or extra latent factors.  
- Stationarity assumption: Assumes parameters constant across sessions despite likely behavioral/arousal changes; no checks for parameter drift.  
- Missing methodological detail: Sparse on EM initialization, regularization, spectral radius constraints, convergence/runtimes, and sample complexity.  
- Literature/context gaps: Needs better positioning relative to GPFA, multi-view factor analysis, subspace identification, and missing/incomplete references.  

Key questions for authors
- What are the precise identifiability conditions when overlap is zero—can you provide a theorem, counterexample, or phase diagram over overlap, T, SNR, and stimulus richness?  
- How were B and inputs u specified for the real datasets (especially awake data), and how sensitive are results to u misspecification?  
- Do reported correlation metrics compare to empirical held-out test correlations or only to the fully observed model’s predictions?  
- How were EM initialized and regularized, were there spectral-radius or stability constraints, and what are convergence diagnostics?  
- How does performance change when allowing low-rank Q or adding latent dimensions for common input?  

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide theoretical or empirical analysis of identifiability/performance versus overlap, stimulus richness, SNR, and T, including failure modes.  
- Demonstrate stitching on truly non-simultaneous fields from the same animal with known overlap (e.g., sequential microscope moves).  
- Compare to richer latent models (PLDS/GPFA, LDS with extra latents) and subspace identification baselines; include low-rank Q ablation.  
- Report direct accuracy against empirical held-out noise correlations and imputation with confidence intervals across random splits, plus runtime and convergence stats.  
- Clarify preprocessing, construction of u/B, and fix missing/incomplete references.  

Overall evaluation
- Quality: Solid empirical demonstration but lacks theoretical guarantees and robust real-world validation.  
- Clarity: Generally clear but some methodological and evaluation choices need sharpening.  
- Originality: Novel application (stitching) though modeling tools are standard.  
- Significance: Potentially impactful for population imaging if robust to non-simultaneous, nonstationary data.  

Pros for acceptance
- Timely, practical problem with a simple, scalable solution and strong simulated/promising real-data results.  

Cons for acceptance
- No formal identifiability characterization; real-data validation relies on fictional splits; limited exploration of model misspecification and alternatives.  

Recommendation
- Weak accept, contingent on clarifying identifiability, strengthening real-data validation on truly separate recordings, and expanding model comparisons.
