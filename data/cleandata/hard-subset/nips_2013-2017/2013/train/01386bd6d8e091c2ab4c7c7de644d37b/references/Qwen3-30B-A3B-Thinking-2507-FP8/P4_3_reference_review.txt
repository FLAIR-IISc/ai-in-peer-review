

This paper proposes a method to infer neural population dynamics from multiple partial recordings of the same neural circuit by "stitching" together data from different imaging sessions. The authors model the problem as fitting a latent dynamical system with missing observations using a linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) framework. They demonstrate that this approach can predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons, addressing a significant experimental limitation in neuroscience where current imaging technology cannot simultaneously record from large neural populations.

The paper clearly relates to previous work, noting that while numerous studies have addressed inferring functional connectivity from imaging data [6-11], none have tackled the specific problem of inferring correlations between non-simultaneously observed neurons. They properly distinguish their work from related approaches [12-15], which either make strong assumptions (e.g., [12] requiring monotonic relationship between noise and stimulus correlations) or are not applicable to their problem setting (e.g., [14] requires observations at different time points within the same session).

Quality: The method is technically sound, using a well-established LDS framework with appropriate EM algorithm for parameter estimation. The experimental evaluation is thorough, using both synthetic data (with ground truth for comparison) and real calcium imaging datasets from mouse somatosensory cortex. The comparison to both an upper bound ("fully observed" model) and a naive lower bound provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness. The authors carefully evaluate both strengths and limitations, acknowledging that some overlap between imaging sessions is critical for their method to work and that more complex models could improve performance.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with helpful figures that illustrate key findings. The methodological details are sufficiently detailed for reproducibility. The authors provide a good explanation of the intuition behind why the method might work, which aids understanding of the core contribution.

Originality: The core idea of stitching together multiple partial observations of the same neural circuit is novel in the context of neural population dynamics. The paper clearly articulates how this work differs from previous contributions, particularly by addressing the non-simultaneous observation problem without making strong assumptions about the relationship between noise and stimulus correlations.

Significance: The problem addressed is highly relevant to neuroscience, as current imaging technology cannot simultaneously record from large neural populations. The method has the potential to significantly advance the field by enabling the analysis of existing datasets that would otherwise be unusable for studying correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons. The results on real data demonstrate practical utility, with the method approaching the performance of the theoretical upper bound and substantially outperforming the naive approach.

Weaknesses: The linear Gaussian model is a simplification of neural dynamics, though the authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential extensions. The paper could provide more systematic analysis of how performance varies with different levels of overlap between imaging sessions. The real data experiments are limited to two datasets (anesthetized and awake mice), which might not fully demonstrate generalizability across different neural circuits or experimental conditions.

The paper excels in all four NIPS evaluation criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), clearly written (Clarity), presents a novel approach to a significant problem (Originality), and addresses a practical challenge with important implications for neuroscience (Significance). The method's practical utility, combined with strong theoretical and empirical validation, makes it a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper provides a valuable tool for neuroscience researchers working with partial neural recordings, with clear potential for broad adoption and impact. The method's ability to predict correlations between non-simultaneously recorded neurons represents a meaningful advance over existing approaches.
