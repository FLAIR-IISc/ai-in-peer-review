- Proposes a method to "stitch" multiple partial recordings into a joint latent linear dynamical model to infer population dynamics, including pairs never recorded simultaneously.  
- Leverages latent linear dynamical systems with missing observations to predict correlations across non-simultaneously recorded neurons.  
- Validated on synthetic data and real calcium imaging, outperforming a naive baseline and approaching a fully observed model (e.g., correlation 0.84 on real data).  
- Addresses an important practical limitation in neural recordings and offers a potentially useful tool for experimentalists.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: uses LDS and an invertible linear transformation argument to justify parameter recovery for unobserved neurons.  
- Rigorous validation strategy using synthetic ground truth plus upper (fully observed) and lower (naive) performance bounds.  
- Clear and reproducible presentation of the LDS model and EM fitting procedure; linear model is an appropriate proof of concept.  
- Main limitation: linear Gaussian LDS is a strong simplification — neural and calcium dynamics are often nonlinear and non-Gaussian.  
- Does not sufficiently analyze effects of model violations or robustness to nonlinear/non-Gaussian data.  
- Assumes same neural circuit across sessions but fails to address session-to-session non-stationarities (state changes, adaptation, plasticity).  
- Requires substantial overlap between sessions (authors report ~60%), but lacks systematic exploration of overlap vs. performance and theoretical bounds.  
- Benchmarking via splitting simultaneous recordings is somewhat artificial and may not reflect realistic low-overlap scenarios.  
- Limited comparison to other recent partial-observation methods and alternatives (e.g., topology priors, nonlinear/state-space extensions).  
- Does not test whether more sophisticated models (nonlinear LDS, sparsity priors) would yield better stitching performance.  
- Statistical reporting is incomplete: lacks significance testing for improvements over baseline and context on absolute magnitudes of predicted noise correlations.  
- Theoretical gaps: no bound on required overlap or scaling of performance with population size; potential parameter degeneracy for large systems is noted but not analyzed.  
- Specific technical gaps: measurement matrix C^k assumed known and neuron identities assumed matched across sessions; no treatment of identity ambiguity.  
- EM details missing: "online-EM with mini-batches" is mentioned but not described sufficiently for reproducibility.  
- Real-data details incomplete: stimulus structure and consistency across sessions are not fully specified, which could affect stitching validity.  
- Noise-correlation computation is underspecified — method for subtracting stimulus contributions is not detailed.  
- Computational claims lack empirical runtime scaling or comparisons for different population sizes.  
- Recommended revisions: address linear/Gaussian limitations, analyze overlap requirements, add statistical significance tests, discuss non-stationarity, and consider integration with more realistic neural models.  
- Pro arguments: tackles a fundamental experimental bottleneck, strong theoretical grounding, real-data demonstration, clear methodology, and high immediate impact potential.  
- Con arguments: relies on simplifying assumptions, limited discussion of failure modes, artificial benchmarking scenario, and unclear scalability to larger/different systems.  
- Final recommendation: accept, contingent on addressing suggested revisions to clarify limitations and overlap/robustness analyses.
