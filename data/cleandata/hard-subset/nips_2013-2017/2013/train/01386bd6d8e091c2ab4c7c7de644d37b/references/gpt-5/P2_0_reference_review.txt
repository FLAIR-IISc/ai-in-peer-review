Summary and relation to prior work
The paper addresses an important and timely problem in systems neuroscience: inferring the joint dynamics and noise correlations of large neural populations when experimental constraints prevent simultaneous recording of all neurons. The authors propose “stitching” together multiple partially overlapping recordings by fitting a single linear Gaussian dynamical system (LDS) with missing observations. Each session is modeled with a known selection matrix Ck indicating which neurons were observed; parameters A (functional coupling), B (stimulus drive), Q (process noise), and R (measurement noise) are learned via EM with Kalman smoothing, with a stationarity prior on x0 and diagonal Q, R. The key claim is that when different sessions share some neurons, one can recover cross-session couplings and accurately predict noise correlations for pairs never observed simultaneously.

The approach is evaluated on synthetic data (with known ground-truth connectivity and stimulus drive) and on two two-photon calcium imaging datasets (anesthetized and awake mouse S1) using a “fictional imaging” protocol: split simultaneous recordings into two overlapping subsets to emulate non-simultaneous sessions, hold out data for testing, and compare to a “fully observed” LDS (upper bound) and a “naive” baseline (separate LDS per subset combined post hoc). The stitched model recovers cross-session couplings moderately well and predicts noise correlations across non-simultaneous pairs with high accuracy relative to the fully observed model.

This sits at the intersection of system identification with missing data and neural population modeling. It is distinct from methods focusing on inferring connectivity from calcium (e.g., Mishchenko et al., Stetter et al.) in that it explicitly addresses non-simultaneous recordings, and from latent GLMs (Pillow et al.) in scalability and application. It is related to subspace identification and factor models with missing observations, but adapted to neuron-identity-aligned partial recordings.

Strengths
- Clear and practical problem formulation with an elegant, computationally tractable solution using standard LDS machinery.
- Convincing synthetic demonstration with ground-truth, including recovery of off-diagonal couplings and accurate prediction of noise correlations for unobserved pairs.
- Real-data results on two datasets showing stitched correlations approach a fully observed LDS upper bound and outperform a naive baseline, with robustness across population sizes.
- Useful discussion of overlap between sessions and identifiability intuition; acknowledges limitations and suggests extensions (sparsity, latent inputs).

Weaknesses and concerns
- Validation uses “fictional” splits of simultaneous datasets. This controls for nonstationarities and registration issues but weakens the main practical claim (combining truly non-simultaneous sessions). Without a prospective experiment on genuinely sequential recordings, external validity is uncertain.
- Dependence on overlap: authors note failure without overlap. This is an important limitation; quantitative characterization of how much overlap is needed (as a function of N, T, SNR) is missing.
- Modeling assumptions are strong: linear-Gaussian dynamics on calcium traces, diagonal Q and R, global stationarity across sessions, perfect cell identity alignment. These may misattribute shared variability and bias couplings in real data; the paper would benefit from robustness analyses to violations (e.g., session-specific offsets/gains, slow drifts, correlated observation noise, mismatch in calcium dynamics).
- Baselines are weak. A “naive” LDS per session is easy to beat. Stronger alternatives include:
  - Shared-factor models with overlapping neurons (e.g., FA/GPFA with Procrustes alignment on the overlap).
  - Subspace system identification with missing outputs.
  - Low-rank plus sparse coupling models or PLDS with shared parameters across sessions anchored by the overlap.
  - Multi-view CCA/CCA-LDS variants leveraging overlapping identities.
- Scalability: the state dimension equals the number of neurons, making inference O(N^3) per time step per EM iteration, which will not scale to thousands of neurons envisioned in the motivation. The paper should either report runtime/complexity and practical limits or adopt a low-rank latent state with neuron-specific loadings.
- Clarity/notation: a few typos and ambiguities (e.g., measurement equation formatting, definition of t and hk, Tnj). The E/M step updates for R and d are terse; reproducibility would be improved with algorithmic details and hyperparameters.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: Technically sound in the LDS framework; EM and Kalman smoothing with missing observations are appropriate. Claims about identifiability are heuristic; no formal guarantees or sample-complexity analysis. Experiments support the core claims within the simplified setting; lack of truly non-simultaneous validation and stronger baselines are the main quality gaps.
- Clarity: Generally well written and organized. The intuitive explanation is helpful. Some notation issues and missing implementation details could be improved; include runtime, convergence criteria, and code availability to enhance reproducibility.
- Originality: The algorithmic components are standard; the novelty lies in the application and the idea of using overlap to align and infer cross-session couplings/noise correlations. Relative to prior work, the focus on non-simultaneous calcium imaging with identity-anchored partial observations is new and relevant.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for systems neuroscience practice, enabling estimation of cross-population correlations beyond the field of view. Broader ML significance is moderate given the methodological simplicity. Significance would be strengthened by a prospective sequential-imaging experiment and stronger comparative baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a real sequential recording experiment with cell registration across days/sessions to demonstrate robustness to nonstationarities.
- Quantify overlap requirements and sample complexity empirically (vary overlap percentage, session lengths, SNR).
- Include stronger baselines as above; at minimum, a shared-factor/GPFA baseline aligned via overlap.
- Explore session-specific offsets/gains and correlated observation noise; report robustness.
- Discuss and/or adopt a low-rank LDS or sparse A with scalable inference; report computational complexity and runtimes.
- Clarify notation/typos and provide implementation details; consider releasing code.

Pros
- Addresses an important practical problem with a simple, effective approach.
- Strong synthetic and decent real-data results; clear improvement over naive practice.
- Insight about the role of overlap is useful for experimental design.

Cons
- No demonstration on truly non-simultaneous data.
- Strong modeling assumptions; limited robustness analysis.
- Weak baselines and scalability concerns.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The problem is important and the results are promising; with added validations and stronger baselines, this could be a solid contribution.

Confidence: Medium.
