Summary
The paper studies convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten-norm regularization. It contrasts two convex formulations: (i) the “overlapped” approach, which enforces low rank on all mode unfoldings via a sum (or Sp/q aggregation) of nuclear norms, and (ii) the “latent” approach, which decomposes the tensor into a sum of mode-specific components, each regularized to be low rank only in one unfolding. The authors’ main theoretical contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm; in particular, the dual of the latent S1/1 norm is the overlapped S∞/∞ norm (max spectral norm across unfoldings). Building on this, they prove consistency and denoising error bounds for the latent approach. A key inequality shows the latent S1/1 norm is bounded by the minimal square root of the mode ranks (vs the sum for the overlapped norm), leading to MSE bounds scaling with min mode-k rank rather than the average (sqrt) rank. They provide deterministic and Gaussian noise bounds under an incoherence-type condition and show, empirically on synthetic denoising tasks, that latent achieves error scaling predicted by the theory and often outperforms overlapped even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across modes. The work connects convex tensor estimation to structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso).

Relation to prior work
The paper positions itself against earlier convex tensor methods using sums of nuclear norms of unfoldings (e.g., Liu et al., Signoretto et al., Tomioka et al., Recht et al.), and builds on decomposable-regularizer analysis (Negahban et al.) and matrix denoising rates (Agarwal et al.). The latent formulation is related to “dirty models” and latent group lasso (Jacob/Obozinski/Vert; Jalali et al.); the duality result mirrors known overlapped/latent sparsity dualities but is new for Schatten norms on tensors. It contrasts with the overlapped approach’s statistical bound (Tomioka et al. NIPS 2010), and aligns with lower bounds (Mu et al.) depending on min mode rank.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution: formulates and proves a clean duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms, extending structured sparsity insights to tensors.
- Statistically meaningful advances: consistency and denoising bounds for the latent approach with dependence on min mode rank; explains when and why latent outperforms overlapped.
- Technically sound high-level approach: uses appropriate norm duality, decomposable regularizer machinery, and Gaussian width/concentration arguments.
- Empirical validation: synthetic denoising experiments corroborate scaling predictions, including dependence on tensor dimensions and regularization.
- Practical implication: latent approach can adapt to the “easiest” mode without prior knowledge of which mode is lowest rank.

Weaknesses and questions
- Assumptions and constraints: The main deterministic bound requires additional spectral-norm constraints on cross-mode unfoldings (parameter α). It is unclear whether these constraints are enforced in the experiments and how α is chosen in practice; please clarify. If not enforced, how do results relate to the theorem?
- Experimental scope: Only Gaussian denoising with full observations is considered. Given the prominence of tensor completion, even a limited experiment on missing data would strengthen the case. Comparisons are only to the overlapped convex baseline; comparisons to Tucker/CANDECOMP (with model selection) or recent nonconvex algorithms would contextualize practical performance.
- Practical selection of λ: Theoretically optimal λ depends on σ and dimensions (and α), but experiments use grid search. Guidance for estimating σ and setting λ without tuning would improve applicability.
- Notational clarity: The distinction between mode-k rank of W* (rk) versus the ranks of latent factors (r̄k) can be confusing; more intuition and a succinct summary table of symbols would help.
- Tightness/optimality: While the min-rank dependence is appealing, a discussion of lower bounds in the same denoising setting (not just recovery lower bounds) would situate the sharpness of the rates.

Evaluation by review criteria
- Quality: Generally solid. The claims are well-supported at a high level, with proofs in the supplement. The duality lemma and key inequalities are plausible and consistent with known results. The introduction of incoherence via spectral constraints is reasonable but should be better motivated and tested empirically. Experimental evidence is limited in scope but aligns with theory.
- Clarity: The paper is mostly well-organized, with definitions and statements clearly laid out. However, multiple similar norms and ranks make the exposition dense; more intuition and a small illustrative example of the latent decomposition and the effect of the dual norm would help. Please state explicitly whether constraints (11) are used in practice.
- Originality: High. The duality result for structured Schatten norms and the latent approach’s statistical analysis for tensors appear novel. The paper connects several threads (tensor norms, structured sparsity, decomposable regularizers) in a new, coherent way.
- Significance: Moderate to high. Understanding when latent convex formulations outperform overlapped ones is important for tensor estimation. The results can influence method choice and analysis in multiway data problems. Extending to completion would further increase impact.

Pros
- New duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
- Better statistical rates for latent (min mode-rank) with clear, interpretable consequence.
- Theoretical results match empirical behavior.
- Bridges tensor methods and structured sparsity theory.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation narrow (denoising only; single baseline).
- Additional constraints in theory not clearly reflected in experiments.
- Practical guidance for hyperparameter selection is limited.
- Some notational and explanatory density.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper offers a clear theoretical advancement with practical implications and reasonable empirical support. Addressing the clarifications about the α-constraints and adding brief guidance on λ (and, if space permits, a small completion experiment or discussion) would further strengthen the paper.

Confidence: High (familiar with convex tensor methods and structured sparsity).
