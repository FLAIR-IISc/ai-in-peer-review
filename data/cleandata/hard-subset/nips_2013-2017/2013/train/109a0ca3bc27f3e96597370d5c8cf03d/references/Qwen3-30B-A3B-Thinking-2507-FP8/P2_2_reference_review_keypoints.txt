- Paper studies two convex tensor decomposition methods: overlapped Schatten-1 norm and latent (mixture) Schatten norms.  
- Establishes a novel duality: the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm (Lemma 1).  
- Provides rigorous proofs for all main results (Theorems 1–3) with both deterministic and probabilistic bounds.  
- Shows latent approach MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, while overlapped scales with the average mode-k ranks.  
- Explains theoretically why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach, especially when low-rankness is concentrated in one mode.  
- Experimental results validate theory: MSE scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures and latent outperforms overlapped in synthetic tests.  
- Connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity/group lasso literature, generalizing known dualities to tensors.  
- Paper is well-written, organized, and includes supplementary proofs for technical details.  
- Novelty: first rigorous analysis of the latent convex approach and its superiority over overlapped methods.  
- Significance: advances theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition with potential practical impact (neuroimaging, collaborative filtering).  
- Weakness: experiments only compare overlapped vs. latent; no benchmarking against non-convex methods (CP, Tucker).  
- Weakness: lacks discussion and empirical evaluation of computational complexity and scalability of the latent method.  
- Weakness: experiments use synthetic data only; no real-world tensor datasets to demonstrate practical utility.  
- Weakness: limited connection/positioning with the most recent tensor decomposition advances (e.g., Mu et al. [19]).  
- Recommendation: accept — technically sound, novel, and empirically supported despite experimental scope limitations.  
- Suggested improvements: add comparisons with non-convex methods, discuss computational efficiency, include real-world experiments, clarify relation to recent lower-bound work, and summarize key proofs in the main text.
