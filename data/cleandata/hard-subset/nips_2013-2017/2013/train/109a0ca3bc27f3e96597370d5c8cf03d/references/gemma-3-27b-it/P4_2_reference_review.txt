This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing an "overlapped" approach to a "latent" approach. The authors aim to theoretically explain the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. They establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, present consistency results for the latent approach, and derive bounds on the denoising performance demonstrating a dependence on the *minimum* mode-k rank, contrasting with the average rank dependency of the overlapped approach. The results are supported by numerical experiments.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of tensor decomposition, its challenges, and the motivation for exploring convex relaxations. The contrast between the overlapped and latent approaches is well-defined.
* **Novelty - Duality Result:** The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a significant contribution, connecting the two approaches and generalizing existing results from structured sparsity literature.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical results, particularly the bound on the denoising performance dependent on the minimum rank, provide a strong explanation for the empirical observations.
* **Rigorous Proofs:** The paper includes a substantial amount of theoretical work, with proofs primarily relegated to the supplementary material, demonstrating a thorough approach.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings, confirming the scaling behavior and showcasing the benefits of the latent approach.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The paper expertly places its contributions within the broader context of tensor decomposition, convex optimization, and structured sparsity.  The references are comprehensive and relevant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity - Notation:** The paper utilizes a lot of notation, some of which (e.g., Sp/q norms) could benefit from more detailed initial explanation. While definitions are provided, the flow could be smoother.
* **Clarity -  Explanation of Intuition:** While the math is rigorous, the *intuition* behind *why* the latent approach leverages the minimum rank more effectively could be elaborated further.  A more intuitive explanation of the duality result would also be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical results, while supportive, are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring different tensor sizes, noise levels, and rank configurations could strengthen the empirical validation.  The experiments focus heavily on Gaussian noise; exploring other noise distributions could provide a more complete picture.
* **Assumptions in Theorems:** The theorems rely on specific assumptions (e.g., the incoherence condition in Theorem 2). A discussion of the practical implications and sensitivity to these assumptions would be valuable.




**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The duality result and the refined error bound for the latent approach represent a substantial advance in the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition.
* **Strong Empirical Support:** The numerical results confirm the theoretical predictions and provide practical evidence for the benefits of the latent approach.
* **Well-Written and Rigorous:** The paper is generally well-written, mathematically rigorous, and clearly structured.
* **Relevance to NIPS Audience:**  The topic aligns well with the NIPS focus on machine learning theory, optimization, and statistical learning.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* **Notationally Dense:** The paper's density of notation could make it challenging for readers unfamiliar with the field to fully grasp the details.
* **Limited Experimentation:** While supportive, the experiments could benefit from greater breadth and robustness.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the empirical validation is supportive. While there are minor areas for improvement in terms of clarity and the scope of experimentation, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
