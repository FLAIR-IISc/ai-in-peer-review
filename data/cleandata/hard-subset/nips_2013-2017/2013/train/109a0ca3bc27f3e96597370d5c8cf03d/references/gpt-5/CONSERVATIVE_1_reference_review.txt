Summary
This paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families of norms: the overlapped Sp/q norm (penalizing all mode-unfoldings simultaneously) and a latent Sp*/q* norm obtained via infimal convolution over mode-specific low-rank components. The main theoretical contribution is a duality result showing the overlapped Sp/q norm is dual to the latent Sp*/q* norm, generalizing known dualities from structured sparsity. The authors analyze the “latent” Schatten-1 approach for noisy tensor denoising, providing: (i) a consistency bound in terms of the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise; (ii) a deterministic error bound under an incoherence-like spectral-norm constraint across components; and (iii) Gaussian noise rates with MSE scaling with the minimum mode rank of the true tensor. They contrast these with prior results for the overlapped approach, which scale with the average of the mode ranks, and present small-scale synthetic experiments that align with their rate predictions and show advantages of the latent approach.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of overlapped vs. latent structured Schatten norms and an explicit duality result, connecting tensor norms to structured sparsity literature.
- Theoretical analysis of the latent approach for denoising, with consistency, deterministic, and Gaussian noise bounds. The scaling with min mode rank provides a plausible explanation for empirical advantages over the overlapped approach.
- Experiments qualitatively support the predicted scaling and show cases where latent outperforms overlapped.
- The exposition is generally clear, with helpful background and definitions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and gap between theory and practice: The stronger deterministic/Gaussian results rely on the incoherence-style condition (10) and the constrained optimization (11) with spectral norm constraints across cross-mode unfoldings. It is unclear if these constraints are enforced in the experiments (the text suggests using ADMM from [25] without mentioning the additional constraints). If not enforced, the empirical section does not validate the main theorems’ regime.
- Practicality of tuning: Several results require λ ≥ ||E||S∞/∞ or λ depending on unknown α. In practice, neither the spectral norm bound α nor the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of noise is known. The paper claims the “optimal λ” depends only on size, but no data-driven selection method (e.g., cross-validation, SURE) is evaluated. This weakens the practical significance.
- Incremental novelty: The duality between overlapped/latent norms mirrors established results in group lasso with overlaps and decomposable regularizers (e.g., Jenatton et al., Obozinski et al., Negahban et al.). Extending these arguments to tensor Schatten norms is natural. The main new element is the specific rate characterization for the latent method. While useful, the conceptual leap beyond prior structured norms seems modest.
- Comparisons are limited: The empirical section compares only to the overlapped convex approach. Widely used non-convex baselines (ALS/HOOI for Tucker, CP-ALS) are not included despite the introduction positioning convex methods as alternatives to these. Without such baselines, it is hard to assess practical significance. No real-data experiments are provided.
- Scope of experiments: Relatively small synthetic sizes, no missing data/completion setting (where prior work focused), and limited exploration of K > 3. The distributions of ranks tend to be simultaneously low, which may not exercise the most interesting regimes. Fairness and tuning parity between methods are not discussed in detail (e.g., are λ grids comparable in effect across methods?).
- Rates and constants: The bounds hide unspecified universal constants and introduce a factor F with multiple dimension- and α-dependent terms; their tightness is unclear. There is no lower bound for the latent approach to claim optimality/minimaxity, and the connection to known lower bounds (e.g., “Square Deal”) remains informal.

Quality
The theoretical development appears technically sound at a high level, relying on known convex duality and decomposable regularizer techniques. However, key proofs are deferred to the supplement, and the use of additional constraints in (11) raises concerns about whether the claimed rates reflect the practical unconstrained latent approach. Empirically, the validation is limited and does not include established non-convex baselines.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written and organized. Notation is heavy but mostly standard. Some minor typos and grammar issues exist (e.g., “betters”), and some references are incomplete. The role and necessity of the α-constraints should be clarified prominently in the main text, including whether they are used in experiments.

Originality
Moderate. The duality result extends known group-sparsity dualities to tensor Schatten norms. The rate analysis for the latent approach is the main novel contribution, albeit built on established frameworks.

Significance
Potentially useful for those developing convex tensor methods, as it offers a cleaner rationale for when latent formulations should outperform overlapped ones. However, without stronger empirical support against standard non-convex methods and real data, the broader impact is uncertain.

Pros
- Solid theoretical framing and connection to structured sparsity.
- Intuitive and plausibly meaningful rate improvements (min rank vs. average rank).
- Some empirical evidence in line with theory.

Cons
- Theory depends on constraints and parameters (α, ||E||S∞/∞) that are impractical or unverified in experiments.
- Limited, small-scale experiments; no comparisons to standard non-convex tensor methods; no real data.
- Novelty feels incremental.

Questions for authors
1. Are the spectral-norm constraints in (11) enforced in your experiments? If not, please discuss the gap between the analyzed and implemented algorithms.
2. How should practitioners set λ without knowing ||E||S∞/∞ or α? Can you provide a data-driven rule with empirical validation?
3. How does the latent approach compare to HOOI/ALS on denoising and completion with cross-validated ranks?
4. Can you comment on computational scalability and wall-clock performance versus the overlapped approach?
5. Do your results extend to completion (missing data) and to higher-order tensors (K > 3) in practice?

Recommendation: Weak reject
While the theory is clean and aligns with intuition, the empirical validation is too narrow, and the key assumptions/constraints used in the analysis appear impractical and are not matched in experiments. Strengthening empirical comparisons (including non-convex baselines and real data), clarifying/enforcing the theoretical constraints, and providing practical tuning guidance would significantly improve the paper.

Confidence: 3/5
