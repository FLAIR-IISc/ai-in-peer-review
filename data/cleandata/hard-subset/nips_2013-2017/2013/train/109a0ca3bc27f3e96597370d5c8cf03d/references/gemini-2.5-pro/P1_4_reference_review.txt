**Summary of Main Ideas**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a convex optimization-based method for tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent approach". This approach models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is constrained to be low-rank along a specific mode. This contrasts with the more common "overlapped approach," which encourages the tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes. While the latent approach has been empirically shown to outperform the overlapped approach, particularly when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode, a theoretical justification has been lacking.

The main contributions of this paper are threefold:
1.  It formalizes both the latent and overlapped approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms." It introduces the latent Schatten norm and establishes a novel duality between it and the overlapped Schatten norm, thereby connecting convex tensor decomposition to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., latent/overlapped group Lasso).
2.  It provides the first theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The key result is an error bound demonstrating that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor.
3.  This result is then contrasted with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical explanation for the superiority of the latent approach in settings where ranks are imbalanced across modes. These theoretical scaling predictions are then carefully validated through numerical experiments.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:**
*   **Strengths:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound and builds upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics. The claims are well-supported by both rigorous proofs (provided in the appendix) and well-designed experiments. The experiments are particularly strong as they directly test the derived theoretical scaling laws by defining "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" from the bounds and showing a linear relationship with the empirical MSE. The duality result (Lemma 1) is elegant and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the work.
*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis for the tighter bounds (Theorem 2 and 3) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes the hyperparameter `α`. The practical guidance on choosing `α` is limited, and the theorem requires the regularization parameter `λ` to depend on `α`, which could be problematic. While such assumptions are common in the literature, a discussion on the sensitivity to `α` or how it might be chosen in practice would strengthen the paper.

**Clarity:**
*   **Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction provides excellent motivation by clearly contrasting the two approaches and highlighting the gap in the literature that this paper fills. The mathematical formalism is precise, and the key theoretical results are stated clearly and their implications are discussed thoughtfully. The comparison between the two approaches in Section 3.4 is particularly insightful.
*   **Weaknesses:** The explanation for how the error bound transitions from depending on the sum of component ranks (`sum r̄_k` in Eq. 12) to the minimum mode-k rank (`min_k r_k` in Eq. 13) is a bit brief. The authors state this is possible due to the "arbitrariness of the decomposition" allowing a "singleton decomposition." While correct, this is a crucial step in the argument, and a slightly more detailed explanation in the main text would benefit the reader's understanding.

**Originality:**
*   **Strengths:** This work makes several original contributions. While the latent algorithm itself was proposed in prior work [25], this paper provides the first theoretical analysis, which is a significant and novel contribution. The formalization of structured Schatten norms and the establishment of their duality is a novel conceptual contribution that beautifully links tensor decomposition with the well-studied field of structured sparsity. The key insight that the latent approach's performance is governed by the minimum, rather than the average, rank is a powerful and new result.
*   **Weaknesses:** The proof techniques are adapted from related work on structured sparsity and M-estimators [1, 20]. This is not a significant drawback, as the adaptation to the tensor setting is non-trivial and yields important new insights for this domain. The authors are transparent about this connection.

**Significance:**
*   **Strengths:** The paper's findings are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners in fields that use tensor methods. It provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence to guide the choice of regularization for tensor decomposition, suggesting that the latent approach should be preferred in many noisy settings. The work significantly advances the theoretical understanding of convex relaxations for tensor problems. By bridging tensor norms with structured sparsity norms, it also opens up avenues for future research and cross-pollination of ideas.
*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis is restricted to the tensor denoising setting (fully observed data). A very common use case for these methods is tensor completion (recovering from missing entries), which is a more challenging setting. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but the absence of completion results somewhat limits the immediate applicability of the specific bounds derived.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Fundamental Contribution:** Provides the first and much-needed theoretical guarantees for the "latent" tensor decomposition method.
2.  **Key Insight:** Delivers a clear and impactful result: the latent approach adapts to the "easiest" mode (minimum rank), explaining its empirical success.
3.  **Conceptual Elegance:** The unified framework of structured Schatten norms and their duality is a novel and valuable contribution that connects disparate fields.
4.  **High Quality:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides strong empirical validation for its theoretical claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope:** The analysis is for the denoising case only, not the arguably more impactful tensor completion setting.
2.  **Practicality of Assumptions:** The reliance on an incoherence parameter `α` that must be known for the optimization problem is a practical limitation.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The strengths—a novel theoretical analysis, an elegant duality framework, and a clear explanation for an important empirical phenomenon—far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The paper is well-executed and the results are important for the machine learning community. I strongly recommend acceptance.
