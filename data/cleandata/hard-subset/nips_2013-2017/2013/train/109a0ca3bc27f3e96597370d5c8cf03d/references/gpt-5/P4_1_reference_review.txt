Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex approaches to tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families of norms on tensors obtained from mode-wise matricizations: (i) the overlapped Sp/q norms, which sum/aggregate Schatten p-norms across unfolded modes (including the commonly used sum of nuclear norms as S1/1), and (ii) the latent Sp*/q* norms, which model a tensor as a sum of components, each penalized to be low-rank in a particular mode. A central result is a duality between these two classes: the dual of an overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm. This generalizes known dualities in structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso).

Building on this framework, the paper analyzes the “latent approach” to tensor denoising under additive noise and establishes performance guarantees: a basic consistency bound in terms of the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise, and stronger deterministic and probabilistic bounds when the signal admits a latent low-rank decomposition with an incoherence-type condition on cross-mode unfoldings. Importantly, the error scales with the minimal mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to prior results for the overlapped approach [26], which scale with the average of the square roots of the mode ranks. The analysis follows techniques used for decomposable regularizers in high dimensions [1, 20], adapted to the latent Schatten setting. Experiments corroborate the predicted scaling and show empirical improvements over the overlapped approach, even when the truth is simultaneously low-rank.

Strengths
- Clear conceptual framework that unifies overlapped and latent Schatten norms and proves their dual relationship (Lemma 1), extending well-known results from structured sparsity to tensor norms.
- Technically solid analysis for the latent approach: consistency (Theorem 1), deterministic bounds under an incoherence-style assumption with explicit dependence on ranks (Theorem 2), and Gaussian noise bounds (Theorem 3) with a rank-scaling that matches the intuition behind the latent model.
- Insightful comparison with prior overlapped-norm results [26], explaining when and why the latent approach yields superior performance (dependence on mink rk vs averages).
- Empirical results align with theory, investigating scaling with tensor sizes, ranks, and regularization, and using a solver based on ADMM as in [25].
- Relevance and scope: fits well within NeurIPS topics on convex optimization, structured regularization, and multilinear models.

Weaknesses and points for clarification
- Additional constraints in the main estimator (11): The deterministic and Gaussian bounds rely on spectral-norm incoherence constraints across modes (∥W(l)(k)∥S∞ ≤ α for l ≠ k). This is stronger than the unconstrained formulation (5) commonly used in practice. It would help to clarify:
  - Whether experiments enforce these constraints or use the unconstrained latent formulation. If unconstrained in experiments, discuss the gap between theory and practice.
  - Practical guidance for selecting α and sensitivity of the bounds to α (the factor F depends on α).
- Regularization selection: The theory prescribes λ as a function of σ, sizes, and α, but σ and α may be unknown. Discuss practical estimators or data-driven selection (e.g., cross-validation) and whether the guarantees are robust to such choices.
- Tightness and lower bounds: While [19] is cited, more discussion on minimax optimality for the denoising setting would contextualize the sharpness of the latent approach’s mink rk dependence (e.g., can a matching lower bound be shown under similar assumptions?).
- Completeness of empirical study: The experiments are informative but limited to full observation with Gaussian noise and K=3. Including:
  - Results with missing data (tensor completion) or other noise models.
  - Comparisons to non-convex baselines (e.g., ALS/HOOI) under noise for fairness.
  - More detail on computational cost and convergence behavior of the ADMM solver.
- Notational clarity: A few places could be more explicit:
  - Distinguish clearly between multilinear ranks (r1,…,rK) of W* and the latent factor ranks (r̄1,…,r̄K) in (9).
  - State assumptions like n1 ≥ … ≥ nK where first used.
  - Provide intuition for Lemma 2 akin to (3) to aid readers less familiar with these norm inequalities.

Quality
- The results are technically sound overall, building on well-established convex-analytic tools for duality and decomposable regularizers. The use of overlapped S∞/∞ for noise control and the translation to Frobenius error is standard. The main caveat is the reliance on additional spectral-norm incoherence constraints for the sharper bounds; their necessity and practical enforcement merit more discussion.

Clarity
- The manuscript is generally well written and organized. Definitions and statements are precise, with most proofs relegated to the supplement. Adding the clarifications noted above would further improve readability.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, and the denoising analysis yielding mink rk scaling for the latent approach, appear novel and extend prior work [25, 26] meaningfully. The connection to structured sparsity dualities is insightful.

Significance
- The work advances theoretical understanding of convex tensor methods and provides actionable guidance on when to prefer latent decompositions. Given the prevalence of tensor data, the results are likely to influence both methodology and analysis in this area.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify and justify the spectral-norm constraints in (11), their role in proofs, and whether the same guarantees (or relaxed variants) can be obtained without explicit constraints.
- Provide practical guidance for λ and α selection, and discuss robustness to misspecification.
- Expand experiments to include tensor completion and comparisons to non-convex baselines; report runtime and convergence.
- Discuss potential lower bounds for denoising to contextualize tightness.
- Minor: ensure consistent notation between ranks rk and r̄k; fix small typographical issues.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel and well-motivated theory establishing a clear advantage for latent Schatten regularization under noise.
- Conceptual contribution via duality unifying overlapped and latent formulations.
- Empirical validation aligns with theoretical scaling laws.

Arguments against acceptance
- Strong additional constraints (incoherence via spectral norms) in the main estimator may limit practical applicability; unclear if experiments match the theoretical setting.
- Empirical scope is somewhat narrow; limited benchmarking and settings.
- Practical guidance for hyperparameter selection is limited.
