Summary
This paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation, contrasting two families of structured Schatten-norm regularizers: an “overlapped” norm that jointly penalizes the mode-unfoldings, and a “latent” norm that represents the tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a single mode. The main contributions are:
- A general duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm. This extends known dualities for overlapped/latent group lasso to Schatten norms on matricizations.
- New analysis of the latent approach for denoising: (i) a basic consistency result, (ii) deterministic error bounds that scale with the minimum mode-k rank of the ground truth, and (iii) Gaussian-noise rates with explicit λ that do not depend on unknown ranks. The key inequality bounding the latent Schatten-1 norm by sqrt(min rk) times the Frobenius norm explains adaptation to the easiest mode.
- Empirical verification on synthetic denoising tasks that the MSE scales as predicted, and that the latent approach often improves over overlapped regularization—even when the tensor is simultaneously low rank across modes.

Relation to prior work: The convex overlapped approach (sum of nuclear norms of unfoldings) and its performance for completion/denoising have been studied (e.g., Liu et al., Tomioka et al. 2011/2013). The latent formulation was proposed in earlier work (Tomioka et al. 2011), but a denoising-performance analysis was lacking. The duality connects to structured sparsity literature (group lasso with overlaps and latent group lasso). The comparison to recent lower bounds (Mu et al.) is useful.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The duality lemma is natural and appears correct; it is a nontrivial and useful generalization. The inequality bounding the latent norm by sqrt(min rk)||W||F is the core that drives the adaptation result and is well-motivated. The theorems follow the modern M-estimator/decomposability toolkit (Agarwal/Negahban/Wainwright), adapted to tensor norms. The Gaussian choice of λ is plausible given concentration of spectral norms of Gaussian matricizations and union bounds over K modes.
- Assumptions: The deterministic and Gaussian bounds require an incoherence-type condition expressed via spectral norm cross-mode constraints (10), and these constraints are explicitly added to the estimator (11). This is a strong modeling choice: (a) α is unknown; (b) the constraints are not part of the standard latent estimator used in practice; (c) it is unclear whether the experiments enforced these constraints. The paper should clarify whether (11) was used in experiments and discuss the impact when these constraints are omitted.
- Tightness/comparability: The latent bound adapts to mink rk (or to Σ r̄k via a specific decomposition), while the overlapped result scales with averages of sqrt rk. This matches intuition and the lower bounds cited. A nuanced point: comparing (15) to (16) mixes multilinear ranks rk with component ranks r̄k; the manuscript addresses this by allowing singleton decompositions, but more discussion on when Σ r̄k ≈ mink rk vs. ≫ mink rk would help.
- Algorithms: Optimization relies on prior ADMM implementations; no new algorithmic contribution is claimed. That is fine, but it would help to mention computational scaling and convergence behavior for the latent formulation (and whether constraints in (11) are tractable).

Clarity
- Generally well written and organized; preliminaries are sufficient for an expert reader. However, there is notational ambiguity: the same S1/1 symbol is used for both overlapped and latent norms, which can confuse readers. Please adopt distinct notation (e.g., |||·|||over, |||·|||lat) throughout.
- Proof sketches of the duality and the key latent inequality in the main text would improve readability; currently both are deferred to the supplement.
- Some references are incomplete (missing years/venues). Please clean up.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* Schatten norms for tensors is novel and valuable. The statistical analysis showing min-rank adaptation for the latent approach in denoising is, to my knowledge, new and addresses a gap in the literature.

Significance
- Conceptually clarifies when latent vs. overlapped regularization is preferable. The min-rank adaptation is important for practitioners facing heterogeneous rank structure across modes. The analysis should influence how convex tensor models are chosen.
- The empirical section confirms scaling but is limited to synthetic denoising without missing data; stronger impact would come from: (i) tensor completion experiments; (ii) real data; (iii) comparisons against baselines such as choosing the best single unfolding nuclear norm via CV, Tucker/CP with ALS, or alternative tensor nuclear norms (e.g., t-SVD-based).

Strengths
- Clear conceptual contribution (duality) tying tensor norms to structured sparsity.
- Theoretical guarantees with interpretable adaptation to mink rk and λ independent of unknown ranks.
- Empirical trends consistent with theory.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Incoherence constraints: justify practicality, report whether enforced in experiments, and discuss bounds without these constraints or with soft penalties.
- Notation: disambiguate overlapped vs. latent norms.
- Experiments: add tensor completion and at least one real dataset; include a baseline that selects a single unfolding for nuclear norm minimization via validation; report sensitivity to K and to highly unbalanced dimensions.
- Clarify constants and the factor F in Theorem 3, and provide guidance for setting λ in practice beyond the Gaussian case.
- Discuss identifiability: component-wise error bounds depend on a particular W* decomposition; emphasize that recovery is for the sum W* rather than unique factors.

Decision rationale
Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and general duality result.
- New statistical analysis that explains and formalizes the empirical advantages of the latent approach.
- Solid theoretical development with supportive experiments.

Cons against acceptance:
- Practical gap between the constrained estimator analyzed and the unconstrained one likely used.
- Limited experimental scope and missing real/complete benchmarking.
- Notational ambiguity and some deferred proofs.

Overall, I lean toward acceptance conditional on clarifying the role of the extra constraints, improving notation, and modestly strengthening experiments or at least discussing the practical use without constraints.
