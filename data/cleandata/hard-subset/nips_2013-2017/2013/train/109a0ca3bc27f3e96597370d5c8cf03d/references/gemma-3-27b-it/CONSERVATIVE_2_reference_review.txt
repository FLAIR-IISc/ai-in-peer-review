This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution lies in theoretically analyzing the latent approach, demonstrating a performance bound scaling with the minimum mode-k rank – an explanation for its empirical superiority in certain scenarios. The paper also establishes a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms.

The paper is well-structured and presents a reasonably clear exposition of the mathematical framework. The motivation for studying the latent approach, particularly its better performance when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode, is well-articulated. The connection to existing work on structured sparsity (group lasso) is a positive aspect, providing context. However, despite the mathematical rigor, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a significant contribution warranting acceptance to NIPS.

My primary concern is the incremental nature of the work. While the theoretical analysis is a welcome addition, it largely *explains* an empirically observed phenomenon (from [25]) rather than presenting a truly novel breakthrough. The theoretical results, particularly the bounds on error, feel somewhat standard within the convex optimization/low-rank recovery literature and don't offer surprising new insights. The authors claim the latent approach performs better even when the tensor *is* simultaneously low-rank, but the empirical evidence presented (Figure 2) is not overwhelmingly decisive. The improvements appear modest, and the scaling analysis doesn’t dramatically outperform existing bounds for similar problems. 

The paper relies heavily on existing literature for the optimization algorithms used (citing Tomioka et al. [25]). While not necessarily a weakness, it highlights that the novelty is primarily in the analysis rather than the algorithmic development.  The connection to lower bounds from Mu et al. [19] is insightful but feels more like a post-hoc justification than a driving force behind the theoretical development.

Furthermore, the assumptions required to achieve the tighter bounds (Theorem 2, specifically the incoherence assumption relating to the spectral norm of the components) are potentially strong and could limit the practical applicability of the theoretical results.  The discussion of how to choose the regularization constant, while acknowledging its independence from ranks, lacks concrete guidance for practitioners.

**Strengths:**

*   Rigorous mathematical analysis of the latent approach.
*   Clear explanation of the theoretical basis for the observed performance differences.
*   Established duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
*   Well-written and organized presentation.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental contribution: primarily explains existing empirical observations.
*   Theoretical results are not significantly novel compared to the broader literature on low-rank recovery.
*   Empirical improvements are modest and not consistently demonstrated.
*   Strong assumptions may limit practical applicability.
*   Lack of concrete guidance for parameter selection.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical foundation for an empirically successful approach.
*   Contributes to the understanding of structured Schatten norms.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Incremental contribution, lacking a significant breakthrough.
*   Empirical results do not overwhelmingly support the theoretical claims.
*   Theoretical results rely on potentially strong assumptions.
*   The paper doesn't significantly advance the state-of-the-art beyond explaining existing phenomena.



**Overall Assessment:**

I lean towards **rejecting** this paper. While technically sound and well-presented, it doesn’t meet the bar for originality and significance expected at NIPS. The work is a valuable addition to the theoretical understanding of these methods, but feels more suited to a more specialized venue. The authors should consider publishing in a journal dedicated to optimization or machine learning theory where the incremental nature of the contribution will be better appreciated. I would encourage the authors to focus on more substantial algorithmic improvements or explore the practical implications of their theoretical findings in a wider range of applications.
