- Introduces and analyzes two structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition: overlapped (penalizes all modes) and latent (decomposes into mode-specific low-rank tensors).  
- Proves a key duality: the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm (Lemma 1).  
- Shows latent approach MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank, while overlapped scales with the average of square roots of mode-k ranks, explaining latent’s empirical advantages.  
- Provides rigorous theoretical results (Theorems 1–3) with proofs and a careful relation between the two approaches.  
- Empirical experiments validate theory: latent MSE follows LR complexity (Theorem 3) and overlapped follows TR complexity as predicted.  
- Authors address regularization choice, noting optimal λ is independent of true tensor ranks and aligns with theory.  
- Paper is well-written, well-organized, and accessible to experts, with clear definitions and helpful figures (e.g., Figure 1).  
- Minor clarity issue: analysis assumes constant K (number of modes) but doesn’t discuss scaling with K.  
- Original contribution: first rigorous explanation for latent’s superiority in certain settings and generalization to structured sparsity literature.  
- Distinguishes itself from prior work by providing noisy tensor decomposition analysis (vs. prior noiseless focus).  
- Significant practical implication: helps practitioners choose methods by showing error scales with minimum mode rank, useful when one dominant low-rank mode exists.  
- Weakness: computational complexity comparison between overlapped and latent approaches is not discussed.  
- Weakness: experiments only on 3-way tensors (K=3); scalability with K not empirically shown.  
- Weakness: lacks practical guidance for selecting regularization parameter λ beyond theoretical statements.  
- Weakness: limited concrete examples of real-world applications demonstrating latent’s benefits.  
- Recommendation: Accept — high-quality theoretical contribution with strong writing and empirical support.
