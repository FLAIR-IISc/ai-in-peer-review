This paper investigates convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the “overlapped” and “latent” approaches based on structured Schatten norm regularization. The core contribution is a theoretical analysis demonstrating why the latent approach, which penalizes low-rankness along individual modes, often outperforms the overlapped approach that penalizes low-rankness across all modes simultaneously. The authors establish consistency results and bounds on the mean squared error for the latent approach, scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank, a key advantage highlighted through both theoretical analysis and empirical validation. The paper also presents a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature.

**Strengths:**

* **Clear Problem Statement & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing tensor decomposition methods (particularly the overlapped approach) and motivates the study of the latent approach.
* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis, including consistency results and error bounds, which are crucial for understanding the performance of the proposed method. The derivation of the duality between the norms is a significant theoretical contribution.
* **Novelty:** The theoretical justification for the empirical success of the latent approach is novel and provides valuable insight into the behavior of these algorithms. The connection to structured sparsity literature is well-established.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results corroborate the theoretical findings, demonstrating the superior performance of the latent approach in many scenarios. The comparison against the overlapped approach is well-designed.
* **Well-Structured & Written:** The paper is logically organized, and the mathematical notation is clear and consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on synthetic data with Gaussian noise.  Evaluating the methods on real-world datasets with more complex noise structures and missing data would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Assumptions for Tighter Bounds:** The tighter error bounds in Theorem 2 rely on somewhat strong assumptions about the structure of the true tensor (low-rank in specific modes) and the incoherence between the factors.  Discussing the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions would be beneficial.
* **Computational Complexity:** While the paper presents theoretical scaling results, a discussion of the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach would be valuable, especially in the context of large-scale tensors.
* **Related Work:** While a good amount of related work is discussed, a more thorough review of recent advancements in tensor decomposition and convex relaxation techniques would be helpful to fully situate the contribution within the broader research landscape.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition, providing a rigorous justification for the observed benefits of the latent approach.
* The results are novel and advance the state of the art in this area.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a strong empirical validation of the theoretical findings.
* The work addresses a challenging problem in a principled and mathematically sound manner.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

* The empirical evaluation is limited to synthetic data.
* The theoretical results rely on specific assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.
* A deeper discussion of computational complexity is missing.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical contributions. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful, and the empirical results provide compelling evidence for the superiority of the latent approach. While the limitations regarding the scope of experiments and the assumptions made in the analysis are worth noting, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work.  I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the literature on tensor decomposition and is worthy of acceptance to NeurIPS.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
