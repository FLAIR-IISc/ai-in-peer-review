

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper introduces two structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition: the overlapped Schatten norm and the latent Schatten norm, establishing a duality between them. The authors claim that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach by scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, providing theoretical guarantees and empirical validation.

**Strengths:** The paper makes a valuable contribution by formalizing the duality between these two structured norms, which connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature. The theoretical analysis is generally sound, and the empirical results align with the theoretical predictions. The paper properly situates its work within existing literature on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

**Weaknesses:** Despite the promising theoretical contribution, the paper contains several significant technical and presentation flaws that undermine its credibility:

1. **Notational inconsistency:** The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout. For example, it alternates between "S1/1" and "S1/1-norm" without clear justification. In equation (4), the dual norm is written as "Sp*/q*" but the paper consistently uses "Sp*/q*" elsewhere, creating confusion. The subscript formatting in mathematical expressions is also inconsistent (e.g., "|||W|||_S1/1" vs "|||W|||_S1/1").

2. **Theoretical gaps:** Lemma 1 claims the dual norm is "latent Sp*/q*-norm" but doesn't provide a clear proof for the general case (p,q), only referencing the supplementary material. Theorem 2 states "any solution Ŵ" without establishing uniqueness, which is critical for the interpretation of the bound. The paper also fails to clarify how the "optimal" regularization constant λ is determined in practice, despite claiming it's "independent of the ranks of the truth."

3. **Empirical justification:** The numerical results section claims to "numerically confirm" theoretical predictions but lacks sufficient detail. The explanation of how "the minimum of the sum of ranks found by the run and min_k r_k" is computed is unclear. The paper states regularization constants are "scaled by roughly 1.6" without justification, which is a serious omission in a paper claiming precise scaling behavior.

4. **Ambiguous claims:** The paper claims the latent approach "performs as if it knows the mode with the minimum rank" without explaining how this information is obtained without prior knowledge. The comparison in Section 3.4 cites inequality (16) from [26] but doesn't clarify what [26] actually proved, making it difficult to assess the comparison's validity.

5. **Formatting issues:** The paper contains minor but persistent formatting errors, such as inconsistent capitalization ("Schatten 1-norm" vs "Schatten 1-norm") and inconsistent use of mathematical notation (e.g., "mode-k rank" vs "mode-k rank"). The paper also references "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" without providing the actual figures.

**Critical flaw:** The most serious issue is the lack of clarity about the practical implementation of the latent approach. The paper claims the regularization constant λ can be chosen without knowledge of the tensor's rank, but the numerical experiments use regularization constants that appear to be tuned to the specific data, contradicting the theoretical claim.

**Recommendation:** The paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, but the numerous technical inconsistencies, theoretical gaps, and implementation ambiguities significantly undermine its contribution. While the main idea is potentially valuable, the paper fails to properly establish its claims with rigorous technical details. The quality of the theoretical analysis is compromised by the lack of precise definitions and inconsistent notation. 

I recommend rejection. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address the notational inconsistencies, clarify the theoretical claims (particularly regarding the choice of λ), and provide a more complete empirical validation that aligns with the theoretical predictions. Without these revisions, the paper does not meet the standards expected for publication at NeurIPS.
