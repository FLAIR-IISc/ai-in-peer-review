Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation via structured Schatten norms. It contrasts two regularization strategies: (i) an “overlapped” Schatten norm that sums nuclear norms of all mode unfoldings, and (ii) a “latent” approach that decomposes the tensor into a sum of components, each penalized to be low-rank in a specific mode. The main technical contributions are: (a) a duality result showing that the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm is a latent Sp*/q*-norm; (b) non-asymptotic recovery guarantees for the latent Schatten-1 regularizer in a denoising setting; and (c) bounds that scale with the minimum mode rank (rather than an average) under certain incoherence-like constraints, thereby explaining empirically observed gains over the overlapped approach. Synthetic experiments illustrate the predicted scaling.

Relation to prior work is adequate: the overlapped approach and its analysis have appeared before; the latent approach was proposed earlier but lacked statistical theory. The paper connects tensor norms to structured sparsity literature.

Strengths
- Clear unifying viewpoint on structured Schatten norms and an explicit duality relation that ties overlapped and latent norms; this adds conceptual clarity.
- The theoretical analysis is careful in framing conditions and gives consistency and denoising bounds with explicit dependencies (e.g., on min mode rank, dimensions, noise).
- The comparison with prior bounds for the overlapped norm is illuminating and helps explain when latent regularization is advantageous.
- The empirical studies, though modest, align with the theory’s predicted scaling and the dependence on regularization magnitude.

Weaknesses
- Practicality of assumptions: The main deterministic and Gaussian-noise results require additional spectral norm constraints on cross-mode unfoldings (parameter α), which are nonstandard and difficult to verify/estimate in practice. Moreover, the optimization problem used in theory (with these constraints) does not seem to be the one solved in experiments (ADMM solver from [25] without such constraints), creating a gap between theory and practice.
- Incremental novelty: The latent formulation itself is not new; the duality result follows fairly directly from known structured sparsity duality arguments, and the analysis, while useful, largely adapts decomposability techniques. The conceptual advance over [25,26] is moderate.
- Limited empirical validation: Only synthetic denoising is considered on small tensors; no tensor completion, no real datasets, and no comparisons to standard nonconvex baselines (CP-ALS, HOOI/Tucker-ALS), nor to simple matricization + nuclear norm on the best mode. No runtime/scaling analysis.
- Use of oracle information in evaluation: The “LR complexity” used to demonstrate linear scaling is computed using an oracle decomposition of the noiseless ground truth, which is not available in practice. This weakens the empirical support.
- Tuning and constants: The choice of λ in practice depends on unknown σ and α; the provided formula is not actionable without additional estimation. Constants and the factor F obscure how tight the bounds really are.
- Scope: The paper emphasizes denoising, while many applications (e.g., completion) are left as future work.

Quality
Technically careful overall, but the strongest bounds hinge on assumptions and constraints that are hard to satisfy or enforce. Some claims (e.g., practical choice of λ) are not convincingly operationalized. Proofs are deferred to the supplement; key ideas appear standard adaptations.

Clarity
Generally well-written and organized. Definitions are clear. However:
- The distinction between multilinear ranks rk and component ranks r̄k should be emphasized earlier and repeatedly.
- The role and practical meaning of α and how it is set should be clarified.
- The experimental methodology (how λ is chosen, whether constraints are enforced, computational cost) needs more detail.

Originality
Moderate. The duality is a natural extension of known overlapped/latent sparsity dualities. The bounds adapting decomposability to the tensor setting for the latent norm are useful but incremental relative to existing analyses for overlapped norms and group-sparsity “dirty models.”

Significance
Potentially useful for researchers working on convex tensor methods, offering insight into when latent regularization helps. However, the practical impact is limited by the strength of assumptions and modest experiments. Broader adoption would require demonstrations on realistic tasks and comparison to established nonconvex baselines.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually unifies overlapped and latent tensor norms via duality.
- Provides nontrivial error bounds for latent convex tensor denoising with interpretable dependence (min mode rank).
- Explains empirical phenomena observed in prior work; experiments qualitatively align with theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty over prior proposals; theory relies on restrictive and unvalidated constraints.
- Experimental section is narrow, uses oracle information, and lacks comparisons to standard, widely-used baselines and real data.
- Mismatch between the theoretically analyzed constrained estimator and the implemented solver.
- Practical guidance (λ selection, estimation of σ/α) is insufficient.

Suggestions for improvement
- Remove or relax the spectral norm constraints (or show how to enforce/estimate α in practice) and align experiments with the analyzed estimator.
- Add tensor completion and real-data experiments; compare against CP-ALS, HOOI, and best-mode nuclear norm baselines; report runtimes.
- Provide a data-driven procedure for λ (e.g., cross-validation or noise estimation) and analyze its effect.
- Quantify constants in bounds and discuss tightness; explore lower bounds for the latent approach.
- Clarify the LR complexity measure without oracle access, or evaluate using quantities available in practice.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper offers a clean unifying perspective and useful bounds, but the incremental nature, restrictive assumptions, and limited empirical validation against established baselines make it below the bar for NeurIPS. Strengthening the practical aspects and experimental comparisons would significantly improve the case for acceptance.
