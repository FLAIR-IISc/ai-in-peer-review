

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for convex tensor decomposition by establishing a duality between two structured Schatten norms: the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical explanation for why the latent approach empirically outperforms the overlapped approach in tensor decomposition tasks, particularly when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one mode. This represents a significant theoretical advancement over previous work, which had observed the empirical superiority of the latent approach but lacked theoretical justification.

The core novelty lies in the authors' demonstration that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, while the overlapped approach scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This fundamental theoretical distinction provides a clear explanation for the empirical observations reported in the literature. The paper's main theoretical contribution is Lemma 1, which establishes the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms—a connection that generalizes known results from structured sparsity literature.

The paper makes a strong case for the theoretical and practical significance of the latent approach. The authors demonstrate that the latent approach can achieve better performance without requiring prior knowledge of which mode is low-rank, effectively "knowing" the optimal mode without being explicitly told. This is particularly valuable in applications where the underlying structure of the tensor is unknown. The empirical results in Section 4 provide strong validation of the theoretical claims, showing that the mean squared error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures.

I find the paper's theoretical contributions to be highly significant. While previous work had proposed both approaches (overlapped and latent), this paper provides the first theoretical explanation for their relative performance, which is a major gap in the literature. The duality between the norms (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and generalizes known results from structured sparsity, making it a valuable contribution to the broader machine learning community.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with a strong connection to relevant literature. The authors properly situate their work within the tensor decomposition and structured sparsity literatures, and they carefully distinguish their contributions from prior work. The theoretical analysis is sound, with proofs provided in the supplementary material.

This paper excels in originality and significance. It doesn't merely propose a new algorithm or tweak an existing one—it provides a new theoretical lens through which to view tensor decomposition, explaining why certain approaches work better than others. This theoretical insight has the potential to guide future work in tensor decomposition and related areas.

While the paper is theoretically strong, I should note that the empirical validation could be slightly expanded to include more diverse tensor structures beyond the specific cases tested. However, given the paper's primary focus on theoretical contributions, this is a minor limitation that does not detract from the paper's overall significance.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I am particularly impressed by the paper's theoretical novelty. The authors have identified a key theoretical gap in the convex tensor decomposition literature and filled it with a clean, elegant explanation that resolves an empirical observation. This is precisely the kind of bold, conceptually rich contribution that advances the field.

The paper makes a clear contribution to the state of the art. Previous work had empirically observed that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain settings, but without theoretical justification. This paper provides the missing theoretical foundation, which is a significant step forward. The connection to structured sparsity literature further elevates the contribution, as it demonstrates how tensor decomposition fits into a broader theoretical framework.

In summary, this paper offers a significant theoretical advance that explains an important empirical observation in tensor decomposition. The duality between the two structured Schatten norms is novel, elegant, and generalizes to structured sparsity literature. The theoretical results are sound, the empirical validation is strong, and the implications for practice are clear.

**Recommendation: Accept**

The paper's theoretical novelty and significance far outweigh any minor limitations. It provides a fundamental theoretical explanation for an empirical observation that has been recognized in the community but not previously understood. This is exactly the kind of contribution that advances the field in a meaningful way. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the NIPS proceedings, offering both theoretical depth and practical relevance to the tensor decomposition community.
