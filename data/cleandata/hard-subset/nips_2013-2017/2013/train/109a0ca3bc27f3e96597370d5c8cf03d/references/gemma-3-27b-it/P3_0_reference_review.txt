## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a strong theoretical and empirical contribution to the growing field of convex tensor decomposition. The authors rigorously analyze the latent Schatten norm approach, demonstrating its advantages over the more commonly used overlapped approach, particularly when dealing with tensors low-rank in only one mode. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles a significant problem with strong technical results. I believe this paper is a solid contribution to NIPS and recommend acceptance.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper addresses the challenge of decomposing tensors into low-rank components, a problem prevalent in diverse fields like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. While traditional approaches rely on non-convex optimization, recent work explores convex relaxations using Schatten norm regularization. The authors focus on two main approaches: the ‘overlapped’ approach (penalizing all mode unfoldings simultaneously) and the ‘latent’ approach (decomposing the tensor into a mixture of low-rank tensors, each low-rank in a specific mode). Building on previous work [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], they provide a theoretical justification for the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode. A key contribution is establishing a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, drawing connections to structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21]. The paper clearly positions itself within the existing literature and effectively highlights its novelty.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis, proving consistency and deriving performance bounds for the latent approach. The bound scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank is a significant and insightful result.
* **Novel Duality:**  The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable theoretical contribution, connecting tensor decomposition to broader sparsity literature.
* **Clear Explanation of Empirical Results:** The theory convincingly explains the empirical observations from [25] regarding the better performance of the latent approach in specific scenarios.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The numerical results corroborate the theoretical findings, demonstrating the scaling behavior and highlighting the advantages of the latent approach, even in cases where the overlapped approach *should* perform well.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is remarkably clear and well-structured. The definitions are precise, and the arguments are logically presented. 
* **Connections to Existing Literature:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of related work and clearly articulate the contribution of their work within the broader field.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:**  The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions like the spectral norm bound on the latent factors (Assumption 10 in Theorem 2). While reasonable, the impact of violating this assumption could be discussed further.  It would be valuable to explore how robust the results are to deviations from this incoherence assumption.
* **Computational Cost:** While the theoretical analysis demonstrates the benefits of the latent approach, the paper doesn’t delve into the computational complexity of solving the optimization problem (11) compared to the overlapped approach. A discussion of this aspect would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments primarily focus on synthetic data. While this allows for controlled analysis, results on real-world datasets (e.g., neuroimaging data mentioned in the introduction) would further strengthen the conclusions.
* **Connection to Tucker Decomposition:** The paper mentions the Tucker decomposition in the introduction but doesn’t directly relate the latent approach to it. A discussion of the relationship between the latent approach and Tucker decomposition, and how the former might be advantageous, would be interesting.




**4. Pro & Con Acceptance Arguments:**

**Pro:**

* Significant theoretical contribution that resolves an open question regarding the latent approach.
* Rigorous mathematical analysis with insightful results.
* Strong empirical validation.
* Clear writing and organization.
* Potential to impact the field of tensor decomposition and related applications.

**Con:**

* Assumptions in the theoretical analysis could be limiting.
* Lack of computational complexity analysis.
* Limited experimentation with real-world datasets.




**5. Specific Comments & Questions:**

* **Lemma 2:** The connection between Lemma 2 and the superior performance of the latent approach is clearly stated. It would be beneficial to explicitly highlight this link in the introduction as well.
* **Theorem 3:** The factor *F* in Theorem 3 appears complex. Could the authors provide a more intuitive understanding of its dependence on the tensor dimensions and noise level?
* **Future Work:** The authors mention tensor completion as a potential future work.  Exploring the application of the latent approach to other tensor problems (e.g., tensor completion with missing entries) would be valuable.




**6. Rating:**

**Strong Accept (8/10)**

This paper represents a significant advance in the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are compelling, the empirical validation is convincing, and the writing is excellent. While there are minor areas for improvement, the overall quality of the work warrants strong acceptance to NIPS.
