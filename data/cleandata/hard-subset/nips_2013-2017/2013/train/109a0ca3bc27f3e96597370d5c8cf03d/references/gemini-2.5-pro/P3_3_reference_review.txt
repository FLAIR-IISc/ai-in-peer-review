### Paper Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex approach to tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of K components, with each component being low-rank in a specific mode. The authors formalize this approach by introducing a "latent Schatten norm" and place it within a broader framework of "structured Schatten norms," which also includes the previously studied "overlapped Schatten norm" (sum of trace norms of all unfoldings).

The main contributions are:
1.  A novel duality result is established between the proposed latent Sp*/q*-norm and the overlapped Sp/q-norm, connecting this work to the literature on structured sparsity (e.g., latent vs. overlapped group LASSO).
2.  A rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key result is an error bound showing that the mean squared error of the estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank (`min_k r_k`) of the true underlying tensor.
3.  This result is contrasted with the existing bound for the overlapped approach, which scales with the *average* of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical explanation for why the latent approach is superior, especially for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode.
4.  Numerical experiments are provided to validate the theoretical scaling laws, confirming that the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly with the derived complexity measure.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound and builds upon established techniques from high-dimensional statistics and convex optimization. The main result (Theorem 3) is significant and provides a clear, interpretable message. The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical derivations and the carefully designed experiments. The authors are also honest about the assumptions needed for their tighter bounds (e.g., the incoherence assumption in Eq. 10) and acknowledge the limitations of the current work (focus on denoising rather than completion).

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is established from the outset with a clear example (Figure 1). The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches is explained lucidly. The introduction of the structured Schatten norm framework is a valuable contribution to clarity, unifying the two approaches and highlighting their dual relationship. The notation is standard, and the flow of the argument from the duality lemma to the final error bounds is logical and easy to follow. The experimental section is also very clear, explaining exactly how the theoretical complexity measures were calculated and plotted against the empirical MSE.

**Originality:** The paper is highly original. While the latent approach itself was proposed in [25], it lacked a rigorous theoretical justification. This paper provides the first such analysis. The key original contributions are:
1.  The formalization and generalization of the latent/overlapped concepts into a "structured Schatten norm" framework. The duality shown in Lemma 1 is a novel and elegant result that connects tensor methods to the broader structured sparsity literature in a new way.
2.  The derivation of the error bound that depends on `min_k r_k`. This is a new and powerful result that fundamentally advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition.
3.  The connection to the lower bound from Mu et al. [19] is insightful, suggesting that the latent approach may be achieving a statistically optimal rate that the overlapped approach does not.

**Significance:** The results are very significant for the machine learning community interested in tensor methods. The choice between different convex relaxations for tensor decomposition has been an open question. This paper provides a compelling theoretical argument, backed by empirical evidence, in favor of the latent approach. This result is likely to influence which methods are used in practice and will guide future research. The finding that the latent approach automatically "finds" and adapts to the lowest-rank mode without being explicitly told is a profound insight. Researchers and practitioners working on problems from neuroimaging to recommender systems will find this guidance valuable.

### Detailed Comments and Suggestions

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance):**

*   **Strong Theoretical Result:** The paper's main takeaway—that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k r_k` versus the overlapped approach's average rank dependency—is a clean, impactful, and important theoretical contribution. It resolves a key question about which convex formulation to prefer.
*   **Elegant Unifying Framework:** The introduction of structured Schatten norms and the associated duality (Lemma 1) is a beautiful piece of theory. It provides a deeper understanding of the relationship between the two main convex tensor decomposition methods and connects them to established concepts in structured sparsity.
*   **Well-Designed Experiments:** The experiments are not just "bake-off" comparisons. They are specifically designed to verify the scaling laws predicted by the theory (MSE vs. TR/LR complexity). This provides strong evidence for the correctness and relevance of the theoretical analysis.
*   **Clarity and Insight:** The paper does an excellent job of building intuition. It starts with an empirical puzzle (Figure 1) and ends with a complete theoretical explanation.

**Weaknesses (Areas for improvement):**

*   **The Incoherence Assumption (Eq. 10):** The tighter bound in Theorem 2 relies on an incoherence assumption (`||W*(l)(k)||S∞ <= alpha`) and a more complex optimization problem (11) with additional constraints. It would be beneficial to discuss the practical implications of this. How restrictive is this assumption? How does one set the hyperparameter `alpha` in practice? Does the practical performance degrade significantly if these constraints are omitted? While the unconstrained consistency result in Theorem 1 is also provided, the main `min_k r_k` result seems tied to this constrained formulation.
*   **Computation of LR Complexity:** In the experiments, the LR complexity is computed by running the algorithm on the *noiseless* true tensor `W*` to find an optimal decomposition. While this is a valid way to test the theoretical prediction, it's an oracle procedure. It might be helpful to explicitly state this is for verification purposes and briefly discuss the challenge of estimating this complexity from data.
*   **Focus on Denoising:** As the authors note, the analysis is for the fully-observed denoising case. Tensor completion is arguably a more common application. While this is noted as future work, it is a key limitation of the paper's immediate applicability.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of tensor decompositions. It is technically sound, highly original, and presents its significant results with exceptional clarity. The theoretical analysis elegantly explains the empirical superiority of the latent decomposition approach, and the connection to the structured sparsity literature is insightful. This work will be of great interest to both theorists and practitioners in machine learning. I strongly recommend acceptance.
