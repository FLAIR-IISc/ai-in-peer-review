### Summary

This paper investigates a convex approach to tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. This stands in contrast to the more common "overlapped approach," where a single tensor is penalized for being simultaneously low-rank across all its modal unfoldings. The paper's main contribution is to provide the first rigorous theoretical analysis of this latent approach.

The authors begin by elegantly framing both approaches within a general framework of "Structured Schatten Norms," drawing a powerful analogy to the well-studied overlapped and latent group sparsity norms. A key technical contribution is a novel duality result between the overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm for tensors. Leveraging this duality, the authors derive performance bounds for the latent approach in a noisy setting. Their central theoretical finding is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This provides a formal explanation for why the latent approach can dramatically outperform the overlapped approach, whose error bound depends on the *average* of the mode-k ranks, particularly when the tensor's rank is highly asymmetric across modes. The paper concludes with numerical simulations on synthetic data that corroborate these theoretical scaling laws.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's most significant strength is its conceptual contribution. By introducing the "Structured Schatten Norms" framework, it unifies two seemingly distinct tensor decomposition methods and, more importantly, connects them to the broader literature on structured sparsity. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a new and insightful way to think about convex regularizers for tensors. The duality result (Lemma 1) is a particularly elegant piece of theory that is of independent interest and could form the basis for future work.

2.  **Important and Clear Theoretical Insight:** The paper tackles a crucial open question: why does the latent approach, which has been observed to work well empirically, actually perform so well? The answer provided is both surprising and compelling. The finding that the error bound scales with `min_k rk` (the minimum mode-k rank) rather than an average is a fundamental insight. It provides a crisp theoretical justification for the method's strength and clarifies the precise scenarios where it should be preferred. This result essentially shows that the latent regularizer is "adaptive" and can automatically find and exploit the lowest-rank structure across all modes without this information being provided.

3.  **Bold and Justified Claims:** The paper makes a strong claim that the latent approach is generally preferable for noisy tensor decomposition. This is a bold statement, but it is well-supported by a combination of rigorous theory (the `min_k rk` bound) and targeted experiments that directly validate the derived scaling laws. The work moves beyond simply proposing an algorithm and instead provides a deep understanding of its behavior.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are well-designed to confirm the paper's theoretical claims regarding scaling behavior, and they succeed in this. However, they are confined to synthetic data. While this is acceptable for a theory-focused paper, the argument for the practical superiority of the latent approach would be significantly bolstered by a demonstration on at least one real-world problem where the tensor is hypothesized to have asymmetric ranks. This would bridge the gap between theoretical prediction and practical impact.

2.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumptions:** The tighter theoretical bounds presented in Theorem 2 and 3 rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and corresponding constraints in the optimization formulation (Eq. 11). While such assumptions are standard and often necessary in high-dimensional statistics to obtain strong guarantees for decomposable models, it does mean the sharpest results do not apply to the "pure" latent norm minimization problem. A brief discussion on the practical implications of these constraints and whether they can be relaxed would be beneficial.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   The paper introduces a novel and unifying conceptual framework for convex tensor decomposition that connects it to the rich field of structured sparsity.
*   It provides the first, and much-needed, theoretical analysis for the "latent" approach to tensor decomposition.
*   The core theoretical result—that performance scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is a significant, non-obvious, and highly impactful finding.
*   The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a strong technical contribution in its own right.
*   The work addresses a clear and acknowledged weakness in existing state-of-the-art convex methods.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   The experiments, while supportive of the theory, are not extensive and do not demonstrate the method's efficacy on real-world data.
*   The strongest theoretical results depend on incoherence assumptions that may not hold in all practical scenarios.

### Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of tensor methods. Its core strength lies in its bold conceptual framing and the deep theoretical insight it provides into a promising but previously un-analyzed method. While the experiments could be more comprehensive, they successfully validate the central theoretical claims. The paper's weaknesses are minor compared to its strengths, which align perfectly with what NIPS values: originality, significance, and the introduction of powerful new ideas. I strongly recommend acceptance.
