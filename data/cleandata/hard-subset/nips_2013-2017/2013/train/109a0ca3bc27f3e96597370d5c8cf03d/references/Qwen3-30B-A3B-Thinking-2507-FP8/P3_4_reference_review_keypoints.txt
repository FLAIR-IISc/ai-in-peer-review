- Introduces and analyzes overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms for convex tensor decomposition, proving a key duality (Lemma 1).  
- Shows theoretically and empirically that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in denoising, with error scaling tied to minimum mode-k rank.  
- Theoretical contribution: establishes duality and tight error bounds (Theorems 2 and 3), explaining empirical performance differences.  
- Empirical validation: experiments confirm predicted linear scaling relationships (TR and LR complexity) and support theoretical claims.  
- Paper is well-organized and clearly written, linking tensor decomposition to structured sparsity with consistent notation.  
- Practical implication: recommends the latent approach for noisy tensor decomposition because it adapts to the minimum mode-k rank automatically.  
- Weakness: analysis is limited to the S1/1 norm; extension to other Sp/q norms is not shown.  
- Weakness: assumes a known decomposition W* = ∑W*(k) with rank properties, but practical methods to find this decomposition are unclear.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the latent approach versus the overlapped approach.  
- Weakness: empirical claim slightly overstated—methods perform similarly when the tensor is low-rank across all modes; latent is most advantageous when low-rank in only one mode.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; paper is high quality with minor issues to address.  
- Suggestion: clarify that latent is particularly advantageous when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode and similar otherwise.  
- Suggestion: briefly discuss computational complexity and whether latent’s theoretical benefits incur higher runtime costs.  
- Suggestion: state that the optimal latent decomposition is unknown in practice but the method is robust to decomposition choice.  
- Suggestion: mention potential extensions to other Sp/q norms as future work.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel theoretical contribution with solid empirical support; highly relevant and recommend acceptance.
