Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation via structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families: (i) overlapped Sp/q norms that penalize all mode unfoldings jointly (recovering prior “overlapped nuclear norm” approaches), and (ii) latent Sp*/q* norms that decompose the tensor into a sum of mode-specific low-rank components. The core technical contributions are: (a) a duality result showing the overlapped Sp/q norm is dual to a latent Sp*/q* norm, generalizing dualities known in structured sparsity; (b) analysis of the latent S1/1 approach, including a basic consistency bound, a deterministic bound under an incoherence-type assumption and auxiliary spectral norm constraints, and high-probability bounds for Gaussian noise; and (c) a comparison showing the latent approach’s error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, versus the overlapped approach that scales with an average of ranks (aligning with empirical observations in prior work [25] and contrasting analysis of [26]). Synthetic experiments support the predicted scaling and suggest that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped one, even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank.

Quality:
- The mathematical development is largely sound and well-motivated. The duality lemma is natural and useful; the latent norm bound by sqrt(min rk) times Frobenius norm is the key insight underpinning sharper rates.
- The deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds use a decomposability-style analysis akin to Agarwal/Negahban/Wainwright, appropriately leveraging the dual norm and restricted strong convexity-like arguments in Frobenius geometry.
- A significant caveat is the introduction of additional spectral norm constraints across non-native modes (Eq. 11, condition (10)). These constraints appear necessary to control cross-mode correlations for the latent decomposition, but they alter the estimator relative to the unconstrained latent formulation (Eq. 5/6). The paper should clarify: (i) whether the experiments enforce these constraints (it appears not), (ii) how α is set in practice, and (iii) whether similar guarantees can be obtained without explicit constraints (e.g., via an added penalty or under milder assumptions expressed through compatibility constants).
- The Gaussian choice of λ is reasonable (based on the max spectral norm of Gaussian unfoldings), but the bound is conservative and depends on n1 and N/nK; a tighter characterization of maxk ∥E(k)∥S∞ (possibly via minimizing over k) would sharpen constants and dimensional dependencies.
- Experiments convincingly show scaling trends, but are limited to synthetic data, modest sizes, and do not assess sensitivity to α/constraints or performance under missing data (completion), where these methods are often applied. The computation of LR complexity relies on a decomposition of the noiseless truth obtained by running the latent solver, which is mildly circular as a predictor; nevertheless, it serves to check proportionality.

Clarity:
- Overall well-organized with clear definitions and notation. The connection to structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso) helps intuition.
- Some notation is dense; a compact table summarizing norms and duals would help. A few typos (e.g., “unfold1” footnote marker, incomplete reference metadata).
- It would help to explicitly state algorithmic steps for the constrained problem (11), including how spectral norm constraints are integrated into ADMM and how α is chosen.

Originality:
- The duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms for tensors is a clear conceptual contribution generalizing known vector/matrix results to multi-way arrays.
- The error analysis showing dependence on min mode-k rank for the latent approach appears novel and explains known empirical performance gaps with overlapped norms.
- The framework unifies and extends several strands (tensor nuclear norms, structured sparsity, convex recovery), which is valuable.

Significance:
- Understanding when latent vs overlapped formulations are preferable is important for practitioners. The insight that latent behaves as if it “knows” the easiest mode is compelling and likely to influence model choice.
- The theoretical rates, while conservative, concretely guide λ selection and explain observed scaling.
- Broader impact could be strengthened by including completion results (missing data), larger K, and real data.

Strengths:
- Clear unifying framework and duality result.
- Sharp qualitative insight: latent error scales with min mode rank.
- Rigorous analysis (consistency, deterministic, probabilistic).
- Empirical validation of scaling; code availability.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Theory–algorithm gap: main guarantees rely on added spectral norm constraints (α), seemingly absent in experiments; α selection is unclear.
- Experimental scope limited (synthetic only; no completion; modest sizes).
- LR complexity used for plotting is derived from a solver run on the noiseless truth.
- Constants and dimensional dependencies may be conservative; discussion of tightness is limited.
- Some references incomplete; minor clarity issues.

Questions for the authors:
- Do your experiments enforce the cross-mode spectral norm constraints? If not, can you discuss the gap and whether guarantees can be proved for the unconstrained latent formulation?
- How should α be chosen in practice? Is there an adaptive or data-driven scheme?
- Can you extend the analysis to tensor completion (missing entries) and report experiments there?
- What is the computational complexity of your ADMM solver for large-scale tensors and higher K? Any convergence diagnostics?

Minor comments:
- Fix typos and complete reference entries (years, venues). Clarify footnotes and figure references.
- Consider adding a brief algorithm description for the constrained latent problem.

Overall assessment:
This paper provides a solid and insightful theoretical contribution with practical implications for convex tensor estimation, supported by clean analysis and indicative experiments. Addressing the constraint/α issue and broadening experiments would strengthen it further. I lean toward acceptance.
