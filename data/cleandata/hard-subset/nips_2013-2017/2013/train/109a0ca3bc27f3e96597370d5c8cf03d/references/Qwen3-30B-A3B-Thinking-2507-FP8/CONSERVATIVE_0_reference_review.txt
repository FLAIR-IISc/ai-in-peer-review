

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper examines two convex approaches to tensor decomposition: the overlapped approach (previously proposed in [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]) and the latent approach (introduced in [25]). The authors establish a duality between the two approaches through structured Schatten norms, and theoretically demonstrate that the latent approach has superior performance guarantees, with error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average mode-k rank as in the overlapped approach.

The paper's main strengths are its rigorous theoretical analysis and clear exposition. The authors establish a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-3) are well-supported with proofs in the supplementary material, and the numerical experiments (Section 4) confirm the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

However, I have significant reservations about the paper's contribution and significance. The core "latent approach" was already proposed in [25], and the paper's main contribution is the theoretical analysis of this approach. While the theoretical analysis is technically sound, the paper overstates the novelty and practical significance of the work. The authors claim "the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach" but provide only limited empirical evidence supporting this claim.

The numerical results in Section 4 are problematic for several reasons:
1. The experiments use synthetic data only, with no real-world applications (e.g., neuroimaging or collaborative filtering where tensor decomposition is commonly applied).
2. The improvement of the latent approach over the overlapped approach is described as "milder than that in Figure 1" and "in many cases" the approaches perform similarly.
3. The paper acknowledges that "the latent approach perform[s] at least as well as the overlapped approach in such situations as well," suggesting the improvement is marginal in common scenarios.

I'm particularly concerned about the lack of computational complexity analysis. The latent approach requires decomposing the tensor into K components (one for each mode), which is computationally more intensive than the single-component approach of the overlapped method. The paper doesn't discuss whether the theoretical improvements justify this increased computational cost.

The paper also fails to adequately address the limitations of the latent approach. For instance, it claims the latent approach "performs better in many cases" but doesn't identify specific scenarios where it outperforms the overlapped approach by a substantial margin. The paper's Figure 1 (which the authors claim demonstrates the superiority of the latent approach) isn't fully explained in the main text, and the numerical validation in Section 4 doesn't strongly support the claim of superiority.

The theoretical results are sound, but the paper doesn't sufficiently establish why these theoretical results translate to meaningful practical improvements. The claim that "the latent approach is preferable" is not strongly supported by evidence. The authors show that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, but they don't demonstrate that this minimum rank is typically small in real-world applications.

I'm also skeptical about the paper's claim that "the latent approach performs as if it knows the mode with the minimum rank, although such information is not given." This is a theoretical curiosity but doesn't necessarily translate to practical advantages.

The paper's connection to structured sparsity literature (Section 2) is well-executed, but this connection alone doesn't justify the paper's significance. The field of structured sparsity is well-established, and the paper doesn't demonstrate how this specific connection advances the field beyond what's already known.

In summary, this paper presents a technically sound theoretical analysis of an existing approach to tensor decomposition. While the theoretical results are valuable, the paper overstates the novelty and significance of the work. The practical implications are modest at best, and the paper fails to provide compelling evidence that the latent approach should replace the overlapped approach in practice.

**Recommendation:** Weak rejection

**Pro:**
- Strong theoretical foundation with rigorous proofs
- Clear exposition of connections to structured sparsity literature
- Well-written and well-organized
- Provides a novel duality between two approaches

**Con:**
- Core approach (latent tensor decomposition) is not new (previously proposed in [25])
- Limited empirical validation (only synthetic data)
- Fails to adequately address computational complexity
- Overstates the practical significance of theoretical results
- Doesn't provide convincing evidence that the latent approach is substantially better in practice
- No real-world applications tested

The paper contributes to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition but doesn't provide a compelling case for why practitioners should adopt the latent approach over the established overlapped approach. The marginal theoretical improvements don't justify the increased computational complexity without stronger empirical evidence of substantial practical benefits.
