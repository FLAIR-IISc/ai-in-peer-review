

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of convex optimization-based tensor decomposition, comparing two approaches: the "overlapped approach" (which penalizes all tensor modes to be jointly low-rank using the overlapped Schatten 1-norm) and the "latent approach" (which decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors, each low-rank in a specific mode, using the latent Schatten 1-norm). The authors establish a novel duality between these two structured Schatten norms, proving that the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm is the latent Sp*/q*-norm. They then provide theoretical guarantees showing that the latent approach's mean squared error scales with the minimal mode-k rank of the underlying tensor (rather than the average mode-k rank as with the overlapped approach), explaining its empirical superiority in cases where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper connects these tensor decomposition techniques to structured sparsity literature, generalizing the overlapped/latent group sparsity framework.

This work builds on recent papers by Tomioka et al. (2011, 2012) and Mu et al. (2013) who proposed convex approaches to tensor decomposition. While the overlapped approach has been analyzed in [26], the authors provide the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, filling an important gap in the literature.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) and providing tight error bounds for the latent approach (Theorems 2 and 3). The key insight that the latent approach's error scales with the minimal mode-k rank (rather than the average) is a major theoretical advance that explains the empirical superiority observed in Figure 1.

2. **Novel Connection to Structured Sparsity**: The authors successfully connect their tensor decomposition framework to structured sparsity literature, showing how their approach generalizes the overlapped/latent group sparsity norms. This broadens the applicability of the work and demonstrates the paper's relevance to a wider audience.

3. **Clear Theoretical Framework**: The paper provides a well-structured theoretical framework with precise definitions, clear statements of theorems, and detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The technical exposition is rigorous and accessible to experts in the field.

4. **Empirical Validation**: The numerical experiments (Section 4) confirm the theoretical predictions, showing that the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly with the LR complexity (which depends on the minimal mode-k rank), while the overlapped approach scales with the TR complexity (which depends on the average mode-k rank).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Numerical Scope**: The experiments are limited to tensor completion with Gaussian noise and no missing values. The authors should extend their experiments to include missing values (a common scenario in tensor completion) to better match practical applications.

2. **Comparison with Non-Convex Methods**: The paper only compares the overlapped and latent approaches. It would be valuable to include a comparison with non-convex state-of-the-art tensor decomposition methods (e.g., CP decomposition, Tucker decomposition) to better establish the relative performance of the latent approach.

3. **Computational Discussion**: While the paper mentions using ADMM for optimization, it lacks discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach or other methods. This is an important consideration for practical applications.

## Quality

The paper is technically sound with well-supported theoretical claims. The proofs are rigorous and detailed in the supplementary material. The empirical validation appropriately supports the theoretical claims. The authors carefully discuss both the strengths and limitations of their approach, particularly noting that the latent approach performs as well as (or better than) the overlapped approach even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The authors provide precise definitions, clear explanations of the main contributions, and logical flow from theoretical development to empirical validation. The connection to structured sparsity literature is well-explained, making the paper accessible to readers familiar with that field. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce the results.

## Originality

This paper makes a novel contribution by providing the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach to tensor decomposition. While both approaches have been previously proposed (e.g., in Tomioka et al., 2011), the authors are the first to rigorously analyze the latent approach and establish its theoretical superiority in terms of error scaling. The duality result between the two types of norms is also novel and generalizes results from structured sparsity literature.

## Significance

The results have significant practical implications for tensor decomposition applications (e.g., neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, environmental monitoring), as they provide a principled explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The connection to structured sparsity literature also broadens the applicability of the work. The paper advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition by providing a theoretical foundation for choosing between convex decomposition approaches.

## Recommendation

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition, providing a rigorous explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The work is well-written, technically sound, and has clear practical implications. The minor weaknesses (limited numerical scope, lack of comparison with non-convex methods) do not detract from the significant theoretical contribution.

**Recommendation**: Accept

## Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments

**Pros:**
- First rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach to tensor decomposition
- Establishes a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms
- Provides tight error bounds showing the latent approach depends on minimal mode-k rank
- Empirical results confirm theoretical predictions
- Connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, broadening applicability

**Cons:**
- Limited numerical experiments (no missing values)
- No comparison with non-convex state-of-the-art tensor decomposition methods
- Limited discussion of computational complexity

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition with clear theoretical and practical implications. The minor weaknesses do not diminish the strength of the theoretical contribution, and the paper would be of great interest to the NIPS community.
