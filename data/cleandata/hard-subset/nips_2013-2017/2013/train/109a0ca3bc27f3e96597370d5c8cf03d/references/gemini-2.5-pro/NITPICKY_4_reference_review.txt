This paper studies a convex approach to tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," where a tensor is modeled as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the "overlapped approach," where a single tensor is penalized to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper formalizes the regularizers used in these two approaches as the "latent Schatten norm" and "overlapped Schatten norm," respectively, and establishes a novel duality between them. This connects the tensor decomposition problem to the well-studied literature on structured sparsity (e.g., latent vs. overlapped group LASSO). Second, it provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a noisy, denoising setting. The key result is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true tensor, unlike the overlapped approach whose error scales with the average of the ranks. This provides a theoretical justification for the superior empirical performance of the latent approach when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode. Third, the paper presents numerical experiments to validate this theoretical scaling.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper addresses an important and timely problem: providing theoretical understanding for the burgeoning field of convex tensor decomposition methods.
*   The formalization of the latent and overlapped Schatten norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is an elegant and novel contribution. It provides a unifying framework and connects this specific problem to broader principles in structured regularization.
*   The main theoretical result (Theorem 3 and inequality 15), which shows that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k rk`, is a significant finding. It provides a clear and compelling explanation for previously observed empirical phenomena and offers strong guidance on when to prefer the latent model over the overlapped one. The insight that the method automatically adapts to the lowest-rank mode is particularly powerful.
*   The empirical validation, in principle, directly tests the theoretical predictions about scaling with respect to rank and dimensionality.

**Weaknesses and Concerns:**

Despite the significance of the core ideas, the paper suffers from a number of methodological and presentational issues that detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Clarity and Notation:**
    *   The notation `W(k)(k)` for the mode-k unfolding of the k-th component tensor `W(k)` is notationally dense and can be a source of confusion.
    *   Footnote 1 in the introduction ("For a K-way tensor, there are K ways to unfold a tensor into a matrix. See Section 2.") is abrupt. Such a fundamental concept should be integrated more smoothly into the main text.
    *   In the description of the experimental setup, the definition of the "Latent rank (LR) complexity" is convoluted and appears circular. It requires running the algorithm on the *noiseless true tensor* `W*` to find an effective decomposition `W* = Σ W*(k)` and then computing the complexity based on the ranks of these discovered components. This makes the x-axis of the middle panel in Figure 2 dependent on an oracle procedure, weakening the validation of the theory, which should ideally predict performance based on a priori properties of `W*`.

2.  **Theoretical Assumptions and their Practical Implications:**
    *   The analysis for the tighter bound (Theorem 2) hinges on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10): `||W*(l)(k)||_S∞ ≤ α`. While such assumptions are common, the paper does not discuss its practical implications or provide intuition for when it would hold for real-world tensors.
    *   More critically, the proposed optimization problem (11) directly incorporates this assumption as a hard constraint: `||W(l)(k)||_S∞ ≤ α`. This implies that the parameter `α`, which is a property of the *unknown true tensor*, must be known beforehand to run the algorithm. This is a very strong oracle requirement that is not acknowledged or justified in the paper. It severely limits the practical relevance of the specific algorithm analyzed in Theorems 2 and 3.

3.  **Experimental Methodology:**
    *   The paper states that results are reported for "three representative values" of the regularization constant λ. This is not a transparent or rigorous methodology. Standard practice would be to select λ via cross-validation and report the results for that choice, or at least show the full regularization path. The current presentation could be perceived as cherry-picking values that best support the theoretical claims.
    *   In the experiments, the authors state "the regularization constants are scaled by roughly 1.6 to account for the difference in the dimensionality." This scaling factor appears arbitrary and ad-hoc. The theory presented in Theorem 3 provides a precise scaling for λ; the experimental section should explicitly state whether this ad-hoc scaling is consistent with the theory and justify its use.
    *   The text claims that "the optimal choice of the regularization constant is independent of the rank of the true tensor," citing one series in the plot (△) being "mostly below other series." This is a weak visual argument and not a robust demonstration of the claim.

4.  **Formatting and Minor Issues:**
    *   The distinction between `rk` (mode-k rank of `W*`) and `r̄k` (mode-k rank of component `W*(k)`) is critical but could be emphasized more clearly.
    *   The factor `F` in Theorem 3 is described as a factor that "mildly depends on the dimensionalities," but its form suggests it could be non-trivial. A more detailed discussion of its magnitude and dependencies would improve the rigor of the claims.

**Summary of arguments for/against acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel and elegant duality result and provides the first theoretical analysis for the latent approach to tensor decomposition. The main finding, that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, is a significant and impactful contribution to the field.
*   **Con:** The theoretical analysis relies on a strong, unacknowledged oracle assumption (knowledge of the incoherence parameter `α`). The experimental validation is weakened by a lack of transparency in parameter selection, ad-hoc scaling, and a circular definition of the complexity measure being tested. Multiple minor clarity and notational issues compound these more significant problems.

**Recommendation:**
While the core theoretical ideas in this paper are novel and significant, the execution has too many flaws. The reliance on an oracle parameter `α` in the analyzed algorithm is a major methodological issue that is not addressed. Furthermore, the experimental validation is not conducted with sufficient rigor to be fully convincing. Based on this substantial number of minor and major issues, I recommend **rejection**. A revised version that addresses the oracle assumption (e.g., by analyzing an algorithm that does not require `α` or by robustly discussing this limitation) and presents a more transparent and methodologically sound empirical study would be a strong candidate for acceptance.
