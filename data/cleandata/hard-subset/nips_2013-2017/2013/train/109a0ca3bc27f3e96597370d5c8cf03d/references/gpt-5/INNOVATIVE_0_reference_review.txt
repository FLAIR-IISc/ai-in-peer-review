Summary
This paper proposes a unified framework of structured Schatten norms for tensors, making a clear distinction between “overlapped” and “latent” constructions and establishing an elegant duality between them: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm equals the latent Sp*/q* norm. Building on this, the paper analyzes the “latent” convex tensor decomposition approach (sum of tensors, each low-rank in a single mode) and proves new recovery guarantees. Key results include: (i) a generic consistency bound driven by the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise; (ii) deterministic bounds under an incoherence-style assumption that yield an error rate scaling with the sum of component ranks and, crucially, an overall error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor; and (iii) Gaussian-noise bounds with a practical choice of λ independent of the unknown ranks. The analysis explains and predicts when the latent approach improves upon the overlapped approach, which scales with averages of square-root ranks. Experiments on synthetic data corroborate the predicted scalings and often show improvements of the latent approach even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: The latent/overlapped duality for structured Schatten norms generalizes known results from structured sparsity to the tensor setting and provides a unifying lens on prior convex tensor methods.
- Theoretical contribution: New error bounds that depend on the minimum mode rank are compelling and offer a precise explanation of when latent models beat overlapped ones. The proofs (outlined here, detailed in the supplement) appear technically sound and use appropriate tools (dual norms, decomposability-style arguments).
- Practical guidance: The λ choice in the Gaussian setting does not depend on unknown ranks, which is attractive in practice.
- Empirical support: Simulations are thoughtfully designed to test scaling, and the outcomes match theory across sizes and ranks.

Weaknesses
- Notation/clarity: The paper uses the same symbol S1/1 to denote both the overlapped Schatten 1-norm (sum of nuclear norms of unfoldings) and the latent Schatten 1-norm (infimum over decompositions). This is confusing; these are different norms and deserve distinct notation throughout.
- Incoherence and auxiliary constraints: The deterministic bound introduces spectral-norm constraints (with parameter α) to decouple components. It is unclear whether these constraints are enforced in practice, how α is chosen, and how sensitive the results are to α. The experiments should explicitly report α and whether constraints were active.
- Limited empirical scope: Only synthetic denoising is considered. Tensor completion (with missing entries), robustness to non-Gaussian or structured noise, and real-world datasets (e.g., video, EEG) would strengthen the empirical case and better demonstrate practical impact.
- Baselines and scalability: Comparisons are confined to the overlapped convex method. Including standard nonconvex baselines (e.g., Tucker/CP with Riemannian or ALS optimization) and reporting runtimes or scalability would be informative.
- Constants and tightness: The factor F in the Gaussian bound and the dependence on min nk in the consistency result look potentially loose. Some discussion on tightness and whether improvements are possible would be useful.

Quality
Overall technically solid. The duality result is well-motivated and useful, and the error bounds are carefully derived in the appropriate convex-regularization framework. The main caveat is the reliance on additional spectral constraints and the lack of clarity on their use in experiments. The empirical section is limited but aligns well with the theoretical claims it aims to validate.

Clarity
Generally well-organized and readable, with clear definitions and motivation. However, the notation collision between overlapped and latent S1/1 is a significant clarity issue. Also, more explicit guidance on choosing λ and α (and whether constraints are enforced in experiments) would improve reproducibility. A brief proof sketch of the duality lemma in the main text would help.

Originality
High. The extension of latent/overlapped duality to structured Schatten norms, coupled with the min-mode-rank-based error analysis for latent tensor decomposition, offers a fresh and insightful perspective beyond prior overlapped analyses.

Significance
High. The results provide a principled reason to prefer latent formulations for noisy tensor estimation, likely influencing both theory and practice. The min-mode-rank dependence is an appealing and actionable insight for modelers. The duality connection should spur further work on designing and analyzing new structured tensor norms.

Questions and suggestions
- Clarify the notation: use distinct symbols for overlapped vs latent norms throughout and restate the duality with the new notation.
- Specify whether the spectral-norm constraints in (11) are enforced in experiments; if so, how is α chosen; if not, can you provide a version of the theory without them?
- Comment on tightness of constants and whether the min nk factor in Theorem 1 is improvable.
- Add experiments on tensor completion and at least one real dataset; include a nonconvex baseline and runtime comparisons.
- Provide a short proof sketch of Lemma 1 in the main text to aid intuition.

Pros for acceptance
- Strong conceptual contribution (duality) and new, informative error rates favoring latent models.
- Clear theoretical explanations for empirical phenomena; practical guidance on λ.
- Likely to stimulate follow-up work on structured tensor norms and algorithms.

Cons against acceptance
- Notational confusion and incomplete experimental evaluation (no real data, limited baselines).
- Dependence on auxiliary constraints with unclear practical status.

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a significant and novel conceptual framework with rigorous analysis that advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. Despite some clarity and empirical-scope issues, the core ideas are strong and impactful, and the work opens a promising direction.
