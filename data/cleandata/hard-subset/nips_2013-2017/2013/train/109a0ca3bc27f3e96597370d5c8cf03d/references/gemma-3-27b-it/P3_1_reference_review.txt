## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical analysis of the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition, providing insights into its superior performance over the overlapped approach, particularly when dealing with tensors low-rank in only one mode. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly written. The theoretical results are significant and supported by empirical validation. While the topic is somewhat niche within NIPS, the connections to broader fields like structured sparsity and high-dimensional statistics make it a worthwhile contribution. I recommend **acceptance**, with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The paper investigates two approaches to convex tensor decomposition: the overlapped approach (penalizing all modes simultaneously with the Schatten 1-norm) and the latent approach (decomposing into a mixture of tensors, each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors demonstrate a key duality between the corresponding Schatten norms and, most importantly, establish theoretical guarantees for the latent approach. Specifically, they prove that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank, explaining its improved performance in scenarios where the tensor is predominantly full-rank except in one mode. The paper also provides empirical evidence supporting these theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Contribution:** The core result – bounding the MSE by the minimum mode-k rank – is a significant theoretical advance, providing a concrete explanation for the observed success of the latent approach. The connection to duality theory in structured sparsity is also novel and well-presented.
* **Clear and Well-Organized Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The concepts are explained clearly, with appropriate mathematical notation, and the paper flows logically.
* **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments, while not extensive, effectively corroborate the theoretical predictions.  The inclusion of a direct comparison to the overlapped approach further strengthens the findings.
* **Connection to Related Work:** The authors carefully position their work within the existing literature on tensor decomposition, convex optimization, and structured sparsity. They appropriately cite relevant papers and highlight the novelty of their contributions.
* **Addresses a Fundamental Problem:** Tensor decomposition is a crucial tool in numerous fields, and improving our understanding of its theoretical properties is of high value.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While they support the theory, exploring a wider range of tensor dimensions, ranks, and noise levels would provide further confidence in the findings. The choice of a specific noise level (σ=0.1) is not thoroughly justified.
* **Assumptions in Theoretical Analysis:** The deterministic bound (Theorem 2) relies on the additional incoherence assumption (Equation 10). While common in similar analyses, the impact of violating this assumption isn’t fully discussed.
* **Practical Implications:** While theoretically sound, the paper doesn't delve deeply into the practical challenges of implementing the latent approach, such as efficient optimization algorithms or parameter selection strategies (beyond the observation that the optimal lambda is independent of the rank).
* **NIPS Relevance:** While technically solid, the core audience for this work may lie more within the mathematical optimization or signal processing communities. The connection to more "core" NIPS areas (e.g., deep learning) isn't explicit, though tensor decomposition has seen increasing application in those areas.



**Detailed Comments & Questions:**

* **Lemma 1:** The proof sketch in Appendix A would benefit from a bit more detail for readers unfamiliar with duality theory.
* **Theorem 3:** It would be helpful to briefly discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of σ.
* **Figure 2:** The improvement shown in the right panel is not dramatic. Perhaps a more detailed discussion of cases where the latent approach *doesn’t* perform better would be informative.
* **Regularization Constant Selection:** The statement that the optimal lambda is independent of rank is intriguing.  Can this be explained more intuitively? Is this truly independent or just less sensitive?
* **Future Work:** The discussion of future work is excellent, particularly the mention of tensor completion.  Perhaps also consider exploring extensions to non-convex settings or different regularization schemes.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and novel insights into the latent approach.
* Clear and well-written presentation.
* Supporting empirical evidence.
* Significant contribution to understanding tensor decomposition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited empirical scope.
* Some reliance on strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
* Relatively niche topic within the broader NIPS community.




**Final Recommendation:**  **Accept**.  This paper presents a valuable contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. While minor revisions to broaden the empirical evaluation and discuss the limitations of the assumptions would be beneficial, the strengths of the work clearly outweigh the weaknesses. I believe this paper will be of interest to researchers working in tensor decomposition, convex optimization, and related fields.
