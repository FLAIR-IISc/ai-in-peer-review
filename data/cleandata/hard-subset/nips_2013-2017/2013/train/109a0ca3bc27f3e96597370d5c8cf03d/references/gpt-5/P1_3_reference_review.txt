Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition based on structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families of norms: (i) overlapped Sp/q norms that penalize all mode-unfoldings simultaneously (generalizing the commonly used sum of nuclear norms), and (ii) latent Sp*/q* norms that regularize a decomposition into a sum of tensors, each low-rank in a specific mode. A key theoretical contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm (an infimal convolution), generalizing known dualities in overlapping group sparsity.

Building on this framework, the paper analyzes the “latent approach” to denoising from fully observed data Y = W* + E. Main results:
- Consistency (Theorem 1): with λ ≥ overlapped S∞/∞ norm of noise, the Frobenius error of the latent estimator scales as O(λ √mink nk).
- Deterministic bounds under an incoherence-style constraint (Theorem 2): if W* admits a decomposition into K components, each low-rank in one mode, and cross-mode spectral norms are bounded by α, then an estimator solving a constrained latent problem achieves
Σk ||Ŵ(k) − W*(k)||_F^2 ≤ c λ^2 Σk r̄k and ||Ŵ − W*||_F^2 ≤ c λ^2 min_k r_k.
- Gaussian noise (Theorem 3): with high probability and λ set by σ and dimensions (plus α terms), the MSE scales linearly with Σk r̄k/nK and, for a singleton decomposition, with min_k r_k/nK, up to mild dimension-dependent factors.

Empirical simulations on synthetic 3-way tensors corroborate the predicted linear scaling and show that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach, even when the true tensor is simultaneously low rank. The paper also clarifies why: latent bounds depend on min mode-k rank, whereas overlapped bounds depend on an average of square-root ranks.

Relation to prior work
This work connects and extends lines of research on convex tensor recovery (e.g., overlapped nuclear norms) and structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso). It generalizes duality results known for group sparsity to the tensor Schatten-norm setting. It complements prior analyses focused on noiseless completion or the overlapped approach by providing statistical guarantees for the latent approach under noise. The comparison to Mu et al.’s lower bounds and to earlier overlapped bounds (e.g., Tomioka et al. 2011/2012) is appropriate and illuminating.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: unifying framework of overlapped and latent Schatten norms, with a clean duality result.
- Theoretical novelty: consistency plus deterministic and probabilistic error bounds for the latent approach; scaling with min mode-k rank is both insightful and practically relevant.
- Technical soundness: arguments follow standard convex-analytic techniques (dual norms, infimal convolution) and high-dimensional analysis (Gaussian maxima), adapted carefully to tensors.
- Practical implications: explains when and why latent decompositions win, and provides λ choices that do not require unknown ranks.
- Empirical corroboration: simulations match the predicted scaling and the dependence on dimensions and λ, and fairly compare against the overlapped approach.

Weaknesses and points needing clarification
- Use of incoherence constraints and α: The deterministic and Gaussian bounds (Theorems 2–3) rely on additional spectral norm constraints across modes and on a known α in λ. It is unclear whether these constraints are enforced in experiments and how α is chosen in practice. Without this, the gap between theory and practice is nontrivial. Please clarify if (11)’s constraints were active in experiments and report α.
- Scope: The analysis targets denoising with full observations. Tensor completion is a central motivation; discussing whether the techniques extend (e.g., RSC/RE-type conditions for sampling operators) would strengthen impact.
- Experimental breadth: Only synthetic 3-way tensors are considered; no real data and no comparisons to nonconvex baselines (ALS Tucker/CP, HOOI) that practitioners use. Even small-scale comparisons would help contextualize the gains.
- Tightness and constants: The factor F and constants in bounds are left abstract; discussing their magnitudes and whether they can be improved (or matched by lower bounds) would be useful.
- Notation clarity: Distinguish clearly between rk (mode-k rank of W*) and r̄k (rank of W*(k)’s unfolding). Some readers may find the switch from Σ r̄k to min rk via singleton decompositions subtle—adding a brief explanation in the main text would help.
- Practical selection of λ, σ: The theoretical λ uses σ and dimension terms; in practice σ is unknown. A short note on robust/scale-free choices or cross-validation would be valuable.

Quality
The paper appears technically solid and honest about limitations. The proofs are relegated to the supplement but are sketched sufficiently in the main text to understand the approach. The gap between the constrained estimator in (11) and the algorithm used in experiments should be clarified.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Suggestions:
- Include a brief proof sketch or intuition for Lemma 2 in the main text (why min√rk arises).
- State explicitly whether the experiments solve (11) with spectral norm cross-constraints, and specify α.
- Fix minor typos and improve notation consistency (S1/1 naming; “betters”; consistent use of r̄k vs rk).

Originality
High: the duality extension, the new statistical guarantees for the latent approach with min-rank dependence, and the careful comparison to overlapped norms contribute new insights.

Significance
High for machine learning and signal processing communities using tensor methods. The results provide guidance on when to prefer latent convex formulations and how their error scales.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, principled theory explaining empirical advantages of latent tensor decompositions.
- General duality result tying tensor norms to structured sparsity literature.
- Solid and interpretable bounds; practical guidance on λ.
- Empirical validation consistent with theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on additional spectral norm constraints and α not fully resolved in practice/experiments.
- Limited experimental scope; no real data or nonconvex baselines.
- Focused on denoising; no completion results.

Questions for authors
1) Were the spectral norm constraints in (11) enforced in experiments? What α was used?
2) How sensitive are results to λ mis-specification, and how do you select λ without knowing σ?
3) Can your techniques extend to tensor completion (subsampling) under standard incoherence and sampling assumptions?
4) How does the latent approach scale computationally with K and dimensions compared to overlapped and ALS baselines?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept to accept. The theoretical contributions are strong and timely, and the paper is well written. Addressing the α/constraint clarification and adding a brief baseline or real-data experiment (or an expanded discussion in the supplement) would strengthen the case further. Confidence: high.
