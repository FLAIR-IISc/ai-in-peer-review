### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach and the "latent" approach. The overlapped approach, analyzed in prior work [26], regularizes a tensor by penalizing the sum of the trace norms of its unfoldings across all modes, thereby encouraging the tensor to be simultaneously low-rank. The latent approach, proposed empirically in [25], models the target tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. While the latent approach has shown strong empirical performance, particularly for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode, it has lacked theoretical analysis.

The main contributions of this work are threefold. First, it introduces a general framework of "structured Schatten norms" that formally defines the regularizers used in both the overlapped and latent approaches. Second, it establishes a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing similar results from the structured sparsity literature (e.g., latent group lasso). Third, and most significantly, the paper provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The key theoretical result is an error bound demonstrating that the performance of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. This is in sharp contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the average of the mode-k ranks. Finally, the authors present numerical experiments that empirically validate these theoretical scaling laws, confirming the predicted performance and superiority of the latent approach in many settings.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality: Is the paper technically sound?**

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical contributions, including the duality lemma and the main error bounds, appear sound and are well-motivated by connecting them to established analysis techniques from high-dimensional statistics and structured sparsity. The theoretical analysis provides a clear and compelling explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The experimental methodology is well-designed to directly test the predictions of the theory, comparing the mean squared error against the theoretically-derived complexity measures for both the latent and overlapped methods. The results provide strong support for the paper's claims.

A potential weakness in the analysis is the reliance on the incoherence assumption in Equation (10) to derive the tighter error bounds in Theorem 2. While such assumptions are common in the literature for decomposable models, the practical implications could be further discussed. The optimization problem in (11) includes this assumption as a constraint with a parameter `alpha`, which may need to be known or estimated in practice. A discussion on the sensitivity to this parameter or how one might set it would strengthen the work.

**Clarity: Is the paper clearly written and well-organized?**

The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature, clearly distinguishing between the overlapped and latent approaches and motivating the need for a theoretical analysis of the latter. Section 2 provides a clean and unified mathematical framework for the structured Schatten norms, which greatly aids in understanding the connection between the two methods. The main theorems are stated precisely, and the comparison with the results for the overlapped approach in Section 3.4 is particularly insightful. The experimental section is also clear, with well-defined complexity metrics that directly link the empirical results back to the theory. The figures are well-designed and support the paper's narrative effectively.

**Originality: Are the problems or approaches new?**

The paper makes several original contributions. While the latent and overlapped models themselves have been previously proposed [25], this work is the first to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach. The central finding—that the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is a novel and significant insight. Furthermore, the formalization of structured Schatten norms and the proof of their duality is a novel contribution that connects the field of convex tensor decomposition to the broader literature on structured sparsity. This connection provides both new analytical tools and a deeper conceptual understanding of these regularizers. The paper successfully fills an important theoretical gap in the literature.

**Significance: Are the results important?**

The results presented in this paper are significant for both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data. The theoretical analysis provides strong evidence and clear guidance on when to prefer the latent approach over the more established overlapped approach. The finding that the latent method automatically adapts to the "easiest" (i.e., lowest-rank) mode without prior knowledge is a powerful result. This could have a substantial impact on applications like neuroimaging or collaborative filtering where the underlying tensor might have such an asymmetric rank structure. The unified framework of structured Schatten norms is also a valuable contribution that could inspire the development of new regularization techniques for tensors.

The main limitation on the paper's significance is that the analysis is confined to the tensor denoising setting with fully observed tensors. Many practical applications, including those mentioned in the introduction (e.g., collaborative filtering, missing data imputation), involve tensor completion. While the authors correctly identify this as an important direction for future work, the absence of completion results somewhat tempers the immediate practical impact of the current analysis.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition, filling a notable gap in the literature.
2.  **Significant Insight:** The key result, that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, provides a clear and principled explanation for the method's empirical performance and offers valuable guidance for model selection.
3.  **Elegant Framework:** The paper introduces a unifying framework of structured Schatten norms and proves a novel duality theorem, connecting tensor decomposition to the well-studied area of structured sparsity.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and convincingly support the theoretical claims about the scaling behavior of the error.
5.  **High Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, making complex theoretical concepts accessible.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The theoretical results are restricted to the denoising setting and do not address the tensor completion problem, which is a major application area for these methods.
2.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter theoretical bounds depend on an incoherence assumption and a constrained optimization problem that may be difficult to apply in practice without prior knowledge of the constraint parameter `alpha`.

Overall, the strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a novel, technically sound, and significant contribution to the understanding of convex methods for tensor decomposition. The theoretical insights are impactful and are likely to influence future research and practice in this area.
