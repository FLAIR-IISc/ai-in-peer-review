

# NIPS Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper introduces and analyzes two structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" Schatten norm and the "latent" Schatten norm. The overlapped approach (previously proposed in [9,15,23,25]) penalizes all modes of a tensor to be simultaneously low-rank, while the latent approach (proposed in [25]) decomposes a tensor into a mixture of tensors that are each low-rank in a specific mode. The authors establish a key duality between these two norms (Lemma 1), showing that the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm. They then provide theoretical analysis showing that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This explains the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach in cases where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper is well-organized, provides rigorous theoretical results, and includes empirical validation of the theoretical predictions.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-3) are well-supported by proofs (referenced to the appendix), and the authors carefully establish the relationship between the two approaches. The duality result (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and generalizes existing results in structured sparsity literature [2,13,17,21]. The paper clearly explains the fundamental reason why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach (Lemma 2 and subsequent analysis), which is a significant theoretical contribution.

The empirical results in Section 4 provide strong validation of the theoretical claims. The numerical experiments confirm that the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly with the LR complexity (18), as predicted by Theorem 3, while the overlapped approach's MSE scales with the TR complexity (17), as predicted by the results in [26]. The authors also carefully address the issue of regularization parameter selection, noting that the optimal choice is independent of the ranks of the true tensor, which aligns with their theoretical conditions.

The paper is complete as a theoretical contribution, not merely a position paper, and the authors are careful to acknowledge both the strengths and limitations of their approach. The connection to structured sparsity literature is well-motivated and properly contextualized.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and motivation, and the mathematical definitions are consistently presented throughout the paper. The connection between the two approaches (overlapped vs. latent) is explained with clarity, and the figures (particularly Figure 1) effectively illustrate the key insight.

The paper follows a logical progression: from defining the norms and establishing duality, to providing theoretical analysis of the latent approach, to empirical validation. The writing is accessible to researchers in the field of tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, and the authors provide sufficient detail for an expert reader to reproduce their results.

The only minor clarity issue is that the paper assumes K (the number of modes) is constant throughout the analysis, but doesn't explicitly discuss how the results scale with K, which might be important for applications with many modes.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. While both approaches were previously proposed [25,26], this paper provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain cases, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

The connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity is novel and well-executed. The paper clearly differentiates itself from previous work, particularly [26], which primarily focused on noiseless tensor completion, while this paper provides a comprehensive analysis for noisy tensor decomposition.

The paper is sufficiently different from previous NIPS papers on tensor decomposition. While there are several NIPS papers on tensor decomposition [10,14,18,25,26], this paper provides a theoretical explanation for empirical observations that was previously missing.

## Significance

The results of this paper have significant practical implications for tensor decomposition. By providing a theoretical explanation for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in many cases, the paper helps practitioners make informed decisions about which method to use. The key insight that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank (rather than the average) is particularly valuable for practitioners working with real-world data where tensors often have one dominant low-rank mode.

The paper advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition by providing rigorous theoretical guarantees where previous work was largely empirical. The connection to structured sparsity literature also makes the results relevant to a broader audience beyond tensor decomposition.

The empirical validation in Section 4 confirms the theoretical predictions, which adds to the significance of the results. The paper also makes clear suggestions for future work, such as analyzing the latent approach for tensor completion.

## Weaknesses

1. The paper doesn't discuss how the computational complexity of the two approaches compares. While the theoretical error bounds are analyzed, it's unclear whether the latent approach has a significant computational overhead compared to the overlapped approach.

2. The numerical experiments only consider 3-way tensors (K=3), though the theory is general for K-way tensors. It would be valuable to see how the results scale with K.

3. The paper doesn't provide specific guidance for practitioners on how to select the regularization parameter λ in practice, beyond what's stated in the theory.

4. The connection to practical applications beyond the synthetic experiments could be strengthened. While the paper mentions neuroimaging and environmental monitoring in the introduction, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how the latent approach could improve these applications.

## Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The paper is well-written, the theoretical results are sound, and the empirical validation strongly supports the theoretical claims. The paper clearly advances the state of the art by providing a rigorous explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach.

The paper meets all NIPS criteria at a high level. The theoretical contribution is substantial, the writing is clear, the results are significant, and the paper is technically sound. The minor weaknesses noted above are not severe enough to impact the overall quality of the paper.

**Overall Recommendation: Accept**

The paper provides a valuable theoretical foundation for understanding and choosing between two approaches to convex tensor decomposition. The insights about the relationship between the two approaches and their theoretical performance guarantees are important contributions to the field, and the results are likely to be of interest to both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data.
