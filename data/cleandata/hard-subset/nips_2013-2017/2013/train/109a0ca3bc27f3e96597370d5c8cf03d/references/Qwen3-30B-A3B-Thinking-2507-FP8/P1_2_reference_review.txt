

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition by rigorously analyzing two convex optimization approaches: the "overlapped" approach and the "latent" approach. The authors establish a duality between the overlapped Schatten norms and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes the duality between overlapped/latent group sparsity norms. The key theoretical contribution is showing that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, explaining why it outperforms the overlapped approach in cases where the tensor is only low-rank in one mode. The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature and provides both theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence for the superiority of the latent approach in noisy settings.

The work builds upon previous research on convex tensor decomposition (e.g., [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]) and extends the connection to structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21]. The paper is well-positioned at the intersection of tensor methods, convex optimization, and structured sparsity, making it relevant to NIPS readers.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor**: The paper establishes a fundamental duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which is a significant theoretical contribution. The proof techniques are sound and well-motivated, connecting to established methods in structured sparsity.

2. **Clear Explanation of Empirical Phenomenon**: The paper successfully explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain scenarios (Figure 1), which was previously only observed empirically in [25]. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 2-3) provides a clear scaling with the minimum mode-k rank, contrasting with the average rank dependency of the overlapped approach.

3. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides both consistency guarantees (Theorem 1) and deterministic bounds (Theorem 2) that incorporate the low-rank structure of the problem. The Gaussian noise analysis (Theorem 3) is particularly valuable for practical applications.

4. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The numerical experiments (Section 4) carefully validate the theoretical predictions, showing linear scaling of MSE with the predicted complexity measures (TR and LR complexity). The experiments are well-designed to isolate the key theoretical claims.

5. **Clear Connections to Broader Literature**: The paper effectively connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, showing how the latent/overlapped approaches correspond to latent/overlapped group sparsity. This contextualization greatly enhances the paper's significance.

6. **Practical Relevance**: The paper addresses a practical concern in tensor decomposition (model selection for rank) and provides a theoretically grounded alternative to the standard overlapped approach, which has been widely used in the literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: While the paper mentions using ADMM-based algorithms [25], it doesn't thoroughly discuss the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. This is a significant practical consideration for large-scale tensor decomposition problems.

2. **Assumptions in Theoretical Results**: The deterministic bound (Theorem 2) requires an incoherence assumption (equation 10) that might be restrictive in some applications. The paper doesn't discuss how common this assumption is in real-world tensor data.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Non-Convex Methods**: The paper focuses on convex approaches but doesn't compare the latent approach to non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., alternating least squares, higher-order orthogonal iteration). This would strengthen the claim that the latent approach is superior.

4. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: While the paper shows the optimal regularization constant λ is independent of the rank (Theorem 3), it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how sensitive the latent approach is to the choice of λ in practice.

5. **Theoretical Optimality**: The paper shows the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach but doesn't establish a lower bound to show that the latent approach achieves the minimax optimal rate.

## Specific Comments

1. **Section 2.1 (Overlapped Schatten norms)**: The connection to group sparsity is well-made, but it would be helpful to include a brief comparison to the standard (non-overlapped) group sparsity to better illustrate the novelty.

2. **Lemma 1 (Duality)**: This is a key contribution. The proof sketch in the supplementary material should be expanded to make it more accessible to readers without deep background in convex optimization.

3. **Theorem 2**: The incoherence assumption (equation 10) is crucial but not well-motivated. The authors should discuss whether this is a reasonable assumption for real-world tensor data (e.g., neuroimaging, collaborative filtering).

4. **Section 4 (Numerical Results)**: The experiment design is good, but it would be beneficial to include results on a real-world dataset (e.g., neuroimaging data) to demonstrate practical utility beyond synthetic data.

5. **Related Work**: The paper cites relevant literature, but could better distinguish its contribution from [26] (which focuses on noiseless tensor completion) and [25] (which introduced the latent approach empirically).

## Recommendation

This is a strong theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides a clear explanation for an empirically observed phenomenon. The theoretical results are novel and well-supported by experiments.

**Acceptance Recommendation**: Accept

## Justification for Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for the latent approach to tensor decomposition. The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a key theoretical insight that generalizes existing results in structured sparsity. The paper successfully explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in many cases, which was previously only an empirical observation.

The theoretical results are sound, with well-constructed proofs (in the supplementary material) and clear experimental validation. The paper is well-positioned at the intersection of tensor methods, convex optimization, and structured sparsity, making it highly relevant to NIPS readers.

While there are some limitations (computational complexity, incoherence assumptions), these do not undermine the paper's main contributions. The paper advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition in a demonstrable way, addressing a significant gap in the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field that is likely to be influential for future work in tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.
