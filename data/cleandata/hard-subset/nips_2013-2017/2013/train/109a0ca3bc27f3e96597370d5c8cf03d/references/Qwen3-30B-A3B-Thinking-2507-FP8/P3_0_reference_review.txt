

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in tensor decomposition: comparing two convex optimization approaches for tensor completion and denoising. The authors rigorously analyze the "latent approach" (proposed in [25]) versus the "overlapped approach" (studied in [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]), establishing a theoretical foundation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach. The core contribution is a duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, and a performance analysis showing that the latent approach's error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, while the overlapped approach's error scales with the *average* (square root) of the mode-k ranks. This explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in cases where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode (as demonstrated in Figure 1).

The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, generalizing the duality between overlapped/latent group sparsity norms to the tensor setting. This is a significant theoretical contribution that bridges two important fields in machine learning. The authors provide strong theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-3) and validate them through carefully designed numerical experiments (Section 4).

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution by establishing the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) and providing a clean theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The key insight that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank (Theorem 2, 3) is elegant and impactful.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The theoretical analysis is thorough and well-structured. The authors carefully distinguish between different regimes (small noise vs. large noise) and provide tight bounds that incorporate the low-rank structure of the problem. The connection to structured sparsity literature ([2, 13, 17, 21]) is well-motivated and insightful.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The numerical experiments (Section 4) are well-designed to test the theoretical predictions. The authors demonstrate linear scaling of MSE with the predicted complexity measures (TR complexity and LR complexity), confirming their theoretical results. The comparison between the two approaches across different tensor sizes and ranks is convincing.

4. **Clear Exposition**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem, the technical sections are clearly presented, and the figures (particularly Figure 2) are highly informative. The authors balance mathematical rigor with accessibility.

5. **Practical Implications**: The paper provides clear guidance for practitioners: when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode, the latent approach is preferable. The authors also note that the latent approach performs at least as well as the overlapped approach in cases where the tensor is simultaneously low-rank (which is common in practice).

## Weaknesses

1. **Practical Implementation Details**: While the paper discusses the theoretically optimal choice of λ (Theorem 3), it doesn't provide concrete guidance for practitioners on how to choose λ in real-world applications. The authors mention that the optimal λ is independent of the ranks of the true tensor, but this may not be immediately intuitive for practitioners who don't have access to the true tensor. A brief discussion of practical heuristics for λ selection would strengthen the paper.

2. **Computational Considerations**: The paper focuses on theoretical performance but doesn't address computational complexity or scalability of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. Given that both approaches use convex optimization, it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of the computational cost (e.g., number of iterations, per-iteration complexity) to help practitioners make informed choices.

3. **Assumptions and Limitations**: The theoretical analysis assumes a specific model where the true tensor is a mixture of K tensors each low-rank in a specific mode (Equation 9). While this is a reasonable assumption for the latent approach, the paper doesn't thoroughly discuss how restrictive this is in practice. It would be beneficial to explore how the results hold when the true tensor doesn't perfectly fit this model.

4. **Comparison to Non-Convex Methods**: The paper focuses on convex approaches, but it would be valuable to briefly compare the latent approach to non-convex tensor decomposition methods (like higher-order SVD or alternating least squares) to provide a more complete picture of the landscape. The authors mention that non-convex methods have been "empirically successful" but don't discuss how the latent approach compares to them in terms of performance or computational efficiency.

5. **Connection to Applications**: While the introduction mentions neuroimaging and collaborative filtering as applications, the paper doesn't provide specific examples from these domains. A brief case study or application example would strengthen the connection between the theory and real-world use cases.

## Technical Assessment

The paper makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The duality result (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and generalizes existing results in structured sparsity. The theoretical bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are tight and well-motivated, and the empirical validation in Section 4 provides strong support for the theoretical claims.

The mathematical proofs appear sound, with the authors appropriately deferring more technical details to the supplementary material. The paper correctly cites relevant prior work, including the key papers on convex tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] and structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21].

## Significance and Impact

This paper addresses a fundamental question in tensor decomposition: which convex approach is better for noisy tensor completion? The authors provide a clear theoretical answer with practical implications. The insight that the latent approach "performs as if it knows the mode with the minimum rank" is particularly compelling and could influence future work in tensor methods.

The paper's connection to structured sparsity literature is significant and will likely be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning. The theoretical results could inform the design of future convex tensor decomposition methods, and the empirical validation provides practitioners with clear guidance.

## Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides clear theoretical and empirical justification for the superiority of the latent approach in noisy settings. The main weaknesses (lack of practical guidance for λ selection, computational considerations) are relatively minor and could be addressed in a revision.

I recommend **acceptance** for NeurIPS. This paper advances our theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition and provides valuable insights for practitioners. It is a well-executed piece of research that makes a clear contribution to the field.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a brief discussion in the conclusion about practical heuristics for choosing λ in real-world applications.

2. Include a short section comparing computational complexity and scalability of the two approaches.

3. Provide a brief example from a real-world application (e.g., neuroimaging) to illustrate the practical relevance.

4. Discuss the limitations of the assumption that the true tensor can be decomposed as a sum of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode (Equation 9).

5. Consider adding a comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., higher-order SVD) to provide a more complete picture of the tensor decomposition landscape.

This paper is a valuable contribution to the field and will be of interest to a broad audience at NeurIPS. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical validation is strong, and the writing is clear and well-organized.
