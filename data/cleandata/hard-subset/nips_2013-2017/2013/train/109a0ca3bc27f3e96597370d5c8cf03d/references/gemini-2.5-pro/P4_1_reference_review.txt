### Summary

This paper investigates a convex optimization framework for tensor decomposition, focusing on what the authors term the "latent approach." This approach models a target tensor as a sum of K latent tensors, where each component is regularized to be low-rank along a specific mode using the Schatten 1-norm (trace norm). This contrasts with the more established "overlapped approach," which regularizes a single tensor by penalizing the sum of the trace norms of all its matricizations.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold. First, it introduces a general framework of "structured Schatten norms" (overlapped and latent Sp/q-norms), generalizing the norms used in these tensor decomposition methods. Within this framework, the authors establish a novel duality between the overlapped and latent norms, which connects this line of work to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped/latent group lasso).

Second, and most significantly, the paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the statistical performance of the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The authors derive error bounds showing that the mean squared error of the estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying true tensor. This result provides a clear theoretical explanation for the empirical observation that the latent approach can significantly outperform the overlapped approach, whose error bounds (established in prior work by Tomioka et al., 2012) depend on the *average* of the mode-k ranks.

Third, the paper presents numerical experiments that empirically validate the derived theoretical scaling laws. The results confirm that the latent approach's performance is predicted by its dependence on the minimum rank, and demonstrate its advantage over the overlapped approach in practice.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis appears sound, building upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics. The derivation of the duality between the generalized overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is an elegant result that provides a solid foundation for the subsequent analysis. The main theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) are derived rigorously, providing a non-asymptotic error bound for the latent estimator. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs (provided in the appendix) and carefully designed numerical experiments that directly test the theoretical predictions.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. It begins by providing excellent motivation, using Figure 1 to immediately illustrate the practical scenario where the latent approach excels. The distinction between the overlapped and latent approaches is explained precisely, both intuitively and formally. The theoretical results are stated in an accessible manner, and their implications are discussed thoughtfully, particularly in the comparison with the overlapped approach (Section 3.4). The experimental setup and results are also presented clearly, effectively linking the empirical MSE to the theoretically derived complexity measures.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions. While the latent decomposition algorithm itself was previously proposed in Tomioka et al. (2010), this paper provides the first rigorous statistical analysis of its performance. The key novel insight is the theoretical characterization of its error bound in terms of the minimum mode-k rank, which formally explains its advantages. Furthermore, the generalization to structured Sp/q Schatten norms and the associated duality theorem is a novel contribution that unifies tensor decomposition methods with concepts from the structured sparsity literature, potentially opening avenues for future work. The work is well-situated within the literature, and its contributions are clearly distinguished from prior art, especially the analysis of the overlapped approach in Tomioka et al. (2012).

**Significance:** The results presented are significant for the field of tensor methods and high-dimensional statistics. Tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with wide-ranging applications, and convex relaxations offer a promising, principled alternative to classical non-convex methods. This paper provides crucial theoretical guidance on the choice between two competing convex formulations. The finding that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k r_k` rather than `sum_k sqrt(r_k)` is a powerful result. It demonstrates that the latent method can adaptively find the "easiest" (lowest-rank) mode of decomposition without this information being provided, substantially advancing the theoretical understanding of these methods. This insight is likely to be impactful for both researchers developing new tensor algorithms and practitioners applying them to real-world data.

### Arguments for and against acceptance:

**Pro:**
1.  **Important Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first rigorous error bounds for the latent tensor decomposition method, explaining its empirically observed advantages.
2.  **Novel Duality Framework:** Introduces a general framework for structured Schatten norms and proves a key duality result, connecting tensor methods to the broader field of structured sparsity.
3.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The results offer clear guidance on when to prefer the latent approach over the overlapped approach, a question of practical importance.
4.  **High Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, well-written, and the claims are convincingly supported by both theory and experiments.

**Con:**
1.  **Restrictive Assumptions for Tighter Bound:** The analysis for the main result (Theorem 2) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires constraining the solution space (Eq. 11). While common in related literature, a deeper discussion on the practical implications of this assumption and the selection of the parameter `alpha` would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Limited Scope (Denoising only):** The analysis is confined to the tensor denoising setting with fully observed entries. An analysis for the tensor completion (missing data) setting, a very common application, is left for future work.
3.  **Oracle-like Complexity Measure in Experiments:** The calculation of the "LR complexity" in the experiments relies on knowing the true tensor `W*`, which makes it an oracle measure. While appropriate for verifying a theoretical bound, it is not a practical measure. This is a minor point, as the primary goal was theory verification.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a strong, high-quality contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition. Its main result—that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is both novel and significant. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and the findings are well-supported. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate primarily to the scope of the analysis and the assumptions required, which are standard for this area of research. The paper's strengths far outweigh its weaknesses, and it represents a clear advance in the field. It should be accepted for publication.
