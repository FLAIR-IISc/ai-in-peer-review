- Overall assessment: valuable theoretical analysis favoring the latent approach; recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Paper compares overlapped (all-mode Schatten-1) vs latent (mixture of mode-wise low-rank) convex tensor decomposition.  
- Key theoretical result: MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank, explaining latent approach advantages when only one mode is low-rank.  
- Novel duality established between the corresponding structured Schatten norms.  
- Empirical experiments corroborate theoretical predictions, including direct comparisons to the overlapped approach.  
- Strength: strong and significant theoretical contribution clarifying latent approach behavior.  
- Strength: clear, well-organized, and well-written presentation.  
- Strength: empirical validation, though limited, supports theory.  
- Strength: solid positioning within related work on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.  
- Strength: addresses an important foundational problem in tensor methods.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited in scope (few dimensions, ranks, and a single noise level σ=0.1).  
- Weakness: deterministic bound (Theorem 2) depends on an incoherence assumption whose violation effects are not fully discussed.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of practical implementation challenges (efficient optimization, parameter selection).  
- Weakness: topic may be somewhat niche for NIPS; links to core NIPS areas (e.g., deep learning) are not explicit.  
- Detailed comment: Lemma 1 proof sketch needs more detail for readers unfamiliar with duality.  
- Detailed comment: discuss sensitivity of Theorem 3 results to noise level σ.  
- Detailed comment: Figure 2’s improvement is modest; include cases where latent does not outperform.  
- Detailed comment: clarify and give intuition for claim that optimal λ is independent (or insensitive) to rank.  
- Suggestion for future work: extend empirical evaluation, consider tensor completion, non-convex settings, and alternative regularizers.  
- Arguments for acceptance: novel theory, clarity, and supporting experiments.  
- Arguments against acceptance: limited empirical scope, reliance on strong assumptions, and niche audience.
