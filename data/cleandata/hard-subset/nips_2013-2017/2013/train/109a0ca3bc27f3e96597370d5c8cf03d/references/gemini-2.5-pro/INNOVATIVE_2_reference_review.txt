### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes all matrix unfoldings of a tensor to be simultaneously low-rank, and the "latent" approach, which models a tensor as a sum of components, each being low-rank in a specific mode. The primary contributions are:
1.  It formalizes the regularizers for these two approaches within a general framework of "structured Schatten norms" (overlapped and latent Sp/q-norms).
2.  It establishes a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, drawing a powerful connection to the well-studied literature on structured sparsity and group lasso norms.
3.  It provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor denoising. The key finding is that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor. This is contrasted with the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks.
4.  It presents numerical experiments that validate this new theoretical scaling, confirming that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped one, particularly when the complexity measures derived from the theories predict it should.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

The paper's main strength is its significant conceptual novelty. By introducing the framework of structured Schatten norms and proving the duality between the overlapped and latent variants, the authors provide a fresh and elegant perspective that unifies two distinct tensor decomposition methods. This contribution is not merely an application of existing tools; it builds a new bridge between the worlds of convex tensor analysis and structured sparsity, which is likely to inspire future work.

The theoretical analysis of the latent approach is another major strength. Previous work [25] had proposed the method and shown its empirical promise, but a theoretical understanding was missing. This paper fills that gap convincingly. The main result—that the latent approach's performance depends on the *minimum* mode-k rank—is both powerful and intuitive. It provides a clear, principled explanation for why this method can succeed dramatically where the overlapped approach fails (i.e., when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode). This is a substantial advance over the prior analysis of the overlapped approach in [26] and moves the theoretical understanding of convex tensor methods forward significantly.

The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the definitions are precise, and the theoretical results are stated and interpreted effectively. The connection to structured sparsity is explained well, making the work accessible and contextualized.

**Weaknesses:**

The analysis for the tighter performance bounds (Theorem 2 and 3) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and adds corresponding constraints to the optimization problem. While such assumptions are common in high-dimensional statistics and necessary for this type of fine-grained analysis, it does reduce the generality of the strongest claims.

The empirical validation, while successfully demonstrating the predicted scaling behavior, shows relatively modest improvements of the latent approach over the overlapped one in the presented experiments. The authors honestly acknowledge this, explaining it is due to the nature of their randomly generated tensors. While the experiments effectively support the theoretical claims about scaling, they don't showcase the dramatic performance gains one might hope for after reading the introduction (e.g., Figure 1). However, the primary contribution here is the novel theory, and the experiments serve their purpose in verifying it.

The analysis is confined to the tensor denoising setting. While this is a complete and valuable contribution in its own right, a discussion or analysis of the more common tensor completion setting would have broadened the paper's immediate impact. The authors identify this as future work.

### Arguments for and against acceptance

**Pro-acceptance:**
*   **High Novelty and Originality:** The introduction of the structured Schatten norm framework and the proof of duality is a beautiful and significant theoretical contribution.
*   **Important Theoretical Advance:** The paper provides the first theoretical guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition model, explaining its practical advantages with a clear and insightful dependence on the minimum mode-k rank.
*   **Conceptual Significance:** The work elegantly connects tensor decomposition to the broader field of structured sparsity, which is a valuable conceptual unification.
*   **Challenges Existing Approaches:** It provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence for preferring the latent approach over the more established overlapped approach in noisy settings, guiding future practice and research.

**Con-acceptance:**
*   **Limited Scope:** The analysis does not cover the tensor completion setting, which is a very common application area.
*   **Reliance on Assumptions:** The strongest results require a standard but non-trivial incoherence assumption.
*   **Modest Empirical Gains:** The new experiments, while confirming the theory, show less dramatic improvements than might be expected from the motivation.

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The conceptual framework of structured Schatten norms and their duality is elegant and insightful. The core theoretical result, which explains the performance of the latent approach through its dependence on the minimum mode-k rank, represents a major advance in our understanding of these methods. While the empirical validation could be more dramatic and the analysis is restricted to the denoising case, these are minor weaknesses in the face of the paper's bold ideas and deep theoretical insights. This work prioritizes a novel conceptual direction over incremental refinement, which is precisely the kind of contribution the field needs. I strongly recommend acceptance.
