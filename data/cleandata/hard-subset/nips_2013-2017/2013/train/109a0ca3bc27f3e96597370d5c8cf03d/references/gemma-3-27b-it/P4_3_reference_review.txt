This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing the “overlapped” and “latent” approaches. The core idea is to theoretically analyze the performance of the latent approach, demonstrating its superior scaling properties compared to the overlapped approach, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The authors establish a consistency result and analyze the denoising performance, supported by numerical experiments.

The paper builds upon recent work in convex tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] and relates it to structured sparsity literature [1, 2, 13, 17, 21]. The key contribution lies in a duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms and the subsequent theoretical analysis showing the latent approach’s error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank, explaining its empirical success (observed in [25]).

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a theoretical analysis, including consistency and denoising bounds, which is a notable contribution to a field often dominated by empirical studies.
* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The problem of tensor decomposition and the limitations of existing methods are clearly articulated. The motivation for exploring the latent approach is well-explained.
* **Novel Duality Result:** The duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a novel and valuable finding, connecting the two approaches and the broader field of structured sparsity.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The numerical results support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the better performance of the latent approach, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode. The careful design of the experiments to highlight the scaling behavior is commendable.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a strong awareness of relevant literature, appropriately citing and relating their work to existing research in tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Density of Notation:** The paper is mathematically dense, making it potentially difficult for readers not deeply familiar with tensor algebra and convex optimization. While the notation is standard within the field, more careful explanations could improve accessibility.
* **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications:** While the theoretical and empirical results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical implications of using the latent approach. For example, the computational cost and scalability of the proposed method compared to other tensor decomposition algorithms are not thoroughly addressed.
* **Assumptions in Theorem 2 & 3:** The assumption of incoherence in Theorem 2 (bound on off-diagonal elements) feels somewhat strong and may not always hold in real-world scenarios. The conditions on the regularization parameter λ in Theorem 3, while theoretically justified, could be challenging to determine in practice.
* **Overlapping Citations:** There are some instances where a single citation ([10], [14], etc.) is used to support multiple statements. While this isn't a major issue, more specific citations would enhance clarity and allow readers to easily locate relevant information.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a practically relevant problem in tensor decomposition.
* The identified duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a novel and significant contribution.
* The empirical results strongly support the theoretical findings.
* The work advances the state-of-the-art in convex tensor decomposition.
* The paper is well-written and demonstrates a thorough understanding of the relevant literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The mathematical density may limit the accessibility of the paper to a broader audience.
* The practical implications and scalability of the proposed approach are not fully explored.
* The assumptions required for the tighter bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) could be restrictive in some applications.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are rigorous, the empirical validation is compelling, and the novelty of the duality result is noteworthy. While there are minor weaknesses regarding accessibility and practical considerations, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to improve clarity and discussion of practical limitations. The authors should consider adding a section discussing potential computational challenges and future research directions.
