Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition based on structured Schatten norms. Two families are contrasted: (i) the “overlapped” approach, which enforces low rank across all mode unfoldings by summing Schatten-1 norms of the unfoldings; and (ii) the “latent” approach, which represents the tensor as a sum of K components, each low-rank in one mode, and regularizes by a latent Schatten-1 norm. The paper’s conceptual contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm, generalizing dualities known for structured sparsity/group lasso with overlaps. On the statistical side, the paper proves consistency and denoising bounds for the latent approach. A generic consistency result yields an error bound that scales with λ and the minimum mode dimension. Under an incoherence-type condition that upper-bounds cross-mode spectral norms of the components, the authors derive sharper deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds. Notably, the overall MSE under Gaussian noise scales with the minimum mode-k rank (up to dimension-dependent factors), in contrast to the overlapped approach whose known bounds depend on averages of (square roots of) mode ranks (Tomioka et al., NIPS 2011). Synthetic experiments confirm the different scaling behaviors. The work builds on convex tensor completion/decomposition literature (e.g., Liu et al., Gandy et al.), generalization bounds for decomposable regularizers (Agarwal et al.; Negahban et al.), and connects to structured sparsity (Bach et al.; Obozinski et al.). It also relates to recent lower bounds for overlapped norms (Mu et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear conceptual link between overlapped and latent tensor norms via a general duality that mirrors overlapped/latent group lasso; likely of independent interest.
- New statistical analysis for the latent approach in a noisy denoising setting: generic consistency, deterministic bounds under an incoherence constraint, and Gaussian-noise rates.
- The main qualitative message—latent approach scales with min mode rank while overlapped scales with averages—is well-motivated and theoretically justified.
- Experiments, while synthetic, are carefully constructed to test the predicted scaling and confirm independence of the optimal λ from the ranks.
- Provides an implementable ADMM solver and uses it to compare methods.

Weaknesses:
Quality:
- The sharper bounds (Theorems 2–3) rely on additional constraints (spectral norm bounds α on cross-mode unfoldings of each component) that are not standard in practical latent formulations. The choice and tuning of α in optimization and its role in experiments (was it enforced?) are not fully clarified. If constraints are not used in practice, the gap between analysis and the algorithm evaluated should be discussed.
- The identifiability and feasibility of decomposing an arbitrary W into K components that separately meet spectral norm constraints is not deeply examined; what happens if the true tensor violates (10)?
- Constants and dimensional factors (e.g., F in Theorem 3, the √min nk in Theorem 1) may be loose; a brief discussion of tightness or potential improvements would help.
- Empirical evaluation is limited to small/moderate synthetic denoising; no experiments on missing data (completion), real datasets, or comparisons with nonconvex baselines (e.g., ALS for CP/Tucker). As a result, the practical significance is not fully validated.
Clarity:
- While generally well-written, some parts could be clearer: (i) explicit statement of all assumptions next to each theorem; (ii) a brief proof sketch in the main text (especially for Lemma 1 duality and Lemma 2 inequality) to convey key ideas; (iii) a more explicit algorithmic description for solving the latent problem and the role of α.
- Notation for ranks r̄k (component rank) vs rk (mode-k rank of W) is easy to confuse; a small table of symbols would help.
Originality:
- The duality between overlapped/latent Schatten norms and the latent denoising guarantees appear novel, beyond prior NIPS work on overlapped norms. However, parts of the setup build on known ideas from the dirty model and structured sparsity.
Significance:
- The message that latent approaches adapt to the easiest mode (min rank) is valuable for practitioners and could influence how convex tensor models are designed and analyzed. However, broader empirical validation (including completion) would strengthen the impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean duality connecting two families of norms with broader implications.
- New statistical theory for latent tensor denoising with clear interpretability (min-rank scaling).
- Empirical results that align with theory and clarify when latent dominates overlapped.

Cons for acceptance:
- Reliance on an additional spectral norm incoherence constraint without full empirical or practical guidance on setting α.
- Limited experimental scope (no real data, no missingness, no nonconvex baselines, modest scales).
- Some bounds may be conservative; tightness and practical effect of constants not fully explored.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify whether the α constraints in (11) were enforced in experiments; if not, add experiments with and without these constraints and discuss sensitivity to α.
- Provide guidance on setting α in practice (e.g., scaling rules, cross-validation ranges) and discuss violation of (10).
- Include experiments on tensor completion and at least one real dataset (e.g., neuroimaging or video), and compare to ALS-based CP/Tucker methods to contextualize convex methods’ advantages/trade-offs.
- Add a short proof sketch and a unifying view of how the duality yields the key inequalities used in the bounds.
- Discuss computational complexity and scalability of the ADMM solver; include wall-clock and iterations vs size.
- Consider weighted/normalized versions of the norms to reduce dependence on anisotropy of mode sizes, and discuss potential improvements in constants.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a neat theoretical advance—both the duality and min-rank error scaling for latent tensor decomposition—with experiments that support the main claim. Strengthening the practical side (α constraints, broader experiments) would further solidify the contribution.
