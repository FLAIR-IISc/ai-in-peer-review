Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. Two families of norms are considered: (i) overlapped Schatten norms, which penalize the sum (or ℓq aggregation) of Schatten p-norms of all mode unfoldings, encouraging simultaneous low-rankness across modes; and (ii) latent Schatten norms, which decompose the tensor into a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode, and regularize the sum of their Schatten norms. The authors establish a duality between these two norm families (overlapped Sp/q vs. latent Sp*/q*), generalizing known dualities in structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs. latent group lasso).

Building on this, they analyze the statistical performance of the latent approach in a denoising setting Y = W* + E. They prove: (a) a general consistency bound depending on the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise; (b) a deterministic bound under an incoherence-type spectral norm constraint on cross-mode unfoldings of the latent components; and (c) high-probability Gaussian noise bounds with explicit regularization choices. A key message is that the estimation error for the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor (or with the sum of latent component ranks, divided by the smallest mode size), contrasting prior bounds for the overlapped approach that depend on the average of mode ranks. Empirical results on synthetic data corroborate the predicted scaling and show that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank.

The work connects to convex low-rank tensor recovery (e.g., Gandy et al.; Liu et al.; Signoretto et al.) and to statistical analyses for decomposable regularizers (Agarwal/Negahban/Wainwright; Negahban et al.). It complements prior analyses of overlapped norms (Tomioka et al.) and aligns with lower bounds depending on minimal mode rank (Mu et al.).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The duality result is natural and appears correct, extending group sparsity dualities. The error analysis uses standard tools for decomposable regularizers and operator-norm concentration, adapted to tensors via unfoldings. The separation into a generic consistency bound, a deterministic bound under incoherence, and a Gaussian bound is well-structured. However:
  - The deterministic and Gaussian results require spectral norm constraints on cross-mode unfoldings (∥W*(l)(k)∥S∞ ≤ α, l ≠ k), and these constraints are also incorporated into the estimator (11). It is unclear how α is chosen in practice and whether these constraints were enforced in experiments. Please clarify if (11) is the method used in Section 4 (and how α was set), or if experiments used the unconstrained latent norm formulation.
  - The choice λ ≥ ∥E∥S∞/∞ is standard but conservative; the derived λ for Gaussian noise includes √(N/nK) + √n1 terms. This can be large in high-order tensors; discussion of tightness and whether data-dependent choices (e.g., via cross-validation) materially change the empirical scaling would help.
  - Constants and factors F in Theorem 3 are not tight; while acceptable for scaling claims, a brief sanity check against known matrix special cases would strengthen confidence.

Clarity:
- The paper is mostly clear, with good motivation and connections to structured sparsity. Suggestions:
  - Unify notation between W(k) (component index) and W(k) (mode-k unfolding); the superscript vs. parenthetical is easy to confuse. Early, explicitly distinguish indices for components vs. unfoldings.
  - Clarify the relationship between multilinear ranks rk of W* and latent component ranks r̄k, emphasizing that singleton decompositions are allowed (which underpins the min-rank bound).
  - Provide a short proof sketch or intuition for Lemma 1 and Lemma 2 in the main text.
  - Explain how λ was chosen in experiments, and whether α-constraints were used.
  - Minor language issues: e.g., “performs betters” → “performs better,” “In Prof. ICCV” → “In Proc. ICCV.”

Originality:
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms for tensors and the statistical analysis of the latent approach with min-mode-rank dependence are, to my knowledge, new and nontrivial. The work thoughtfully adapts decomposable-regularizer analysis to the tensor setting and bridges to structured sparsity. The conceptual link that the latent norm “acts as if it knows” the easiest mode is insightful.

Significance:
- The results provide theory that explains empirical observations and offer practical guidance: when one mode is markedly “easier” (low rank), the latent approach is preferable. This is relevant for many applications (e.g., spatio-temporal data). The empirical validation supports the theory’s scaling predictions. That said, experiments are limited to synthetic denoising with Gaussian noise; adding real data or missing data (completion) would broaden impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear theoretical contributions (duality; consistency; deterministic/Gaussian error bounds).
- Error scaling with minimal mode rank is novel and practically meaningful.
- Solid connection to structured sparsity literature and prior tensor results.
- Empirical validation showing predicted scaling and improvements over overlapped norms.
- Code availability improves reproducibility.

Cons against acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation is limited: synthetic only, no real datasets, no missing-data/ completion benchmarks, few baselines beyond overlapped S1/1.
- Unclear if α-constrained estimator was actually used; missing guidance for selecting α in practice.
- Bounds can be conservative; constants and dependence on √(N/nK) may be loose.
- Some notation/clarity issues and minor typos.

Questions and suggestions:
- Clarify whether the spectral norm constraints (and α value) were used in experiments. If not, discuss how results might change with/without these constraints.
- Add experiments on tensor completion (with varying sampling rates) and at least one real dataset (e.g., video denoising, fMRI, environmental data).
- Compare with additional baselines (e.g., other tensor nuclear norms, t-SVD/tubal nuclear norm variants for third-order tensors, if applicable).
- Provide a brief complexity/runtime discussion for the ADMM solver and how it scales with dimensions and K.
- Include a sanity check reducing to the matrix case (K=2) to compare with known nuclear-norm denoising rates.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The paper offers a principled and novel analysis that advances understanding of convex tensor decomposition, with a useful duality and practically relevant error bounds. Strengthening the experimental section and clarifying the role of α would make this a stronger contribution.
