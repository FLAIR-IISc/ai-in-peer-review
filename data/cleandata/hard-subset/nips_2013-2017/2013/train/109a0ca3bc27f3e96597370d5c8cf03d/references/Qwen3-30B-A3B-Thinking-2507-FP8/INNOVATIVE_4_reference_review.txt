

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition by introducing a novel theoretical framework that rigorously explains the empirical success of the latent approach over the overlapped approach. The key innovation is the identification of the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which provides a clean theoretical explanation for why the latent approach performs better when tensors are low-rank in only one mode.

The authors establish a fundamental result: the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, while the overlapped approach scales with the average mode-k rank. This theoretical insight directly addresses a gap in the literature - while [25] observed empirically that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain scenarios, no theoretical explanation existed. The paper fills this gap by showing that the latent approach's error bound is bounded by the minimal rank, which is often significantly smaller than the average rank.

The duality result between the two norms is particularly elegant and generalizes previous work on structured sparsity. The authors connect their framework to the broader literature on group sparsity (notably [1, 12, 17, 21]), showing that the latent/overlapped Schatten norms correspond to the latent/overlapped group sparsity norms. This connection provides a unifying perspective that should be valuable to researchers working on both tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

The theoretical analysis is comprehensive, with careful treatment of both consistency and deterministic error bounds. The authors also provide a Gaussian noise analysis that confirms their theoretical predictions. The numerical experiments successfully validate the theoretical scaling behavior, demonstrating that the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly with the LR complexity (defined as the sum of the latent ranks), while the overlapped approach scales with the TR complexity (based on the average rank).

The paper's main strength is its novel theoretical insight that explains a previously observed empirical phenomenon. This represents a significant step forward in understanding tensor decomposition methods, moving beyond empirical observations to provide a principled theoretical explanation. The duality result is particularly elegant and could have broader implications beyond tensor decomposition.

While the paper focuses on tensor denoising rather than tensor completion (which is mentioned as future work), this limitation doesn't detract from the theoretical contribution. The authors clearly position their work in relation to previous studies [25, 26], acknowledging that their results complement rather than contradict prior work.

The paper is well-written and organized, with clear definitions and proofs. The supplementary material contains the detailed proofs that are referenced in the main text. The experimental design is appropriate for validating the theoretical claims, and the results clearly support the authors' claims.

In terms of novelty, this paper offers a fresh perspective on tensor decomposition that connects to structured sparsity literature in a meaningful way. It's not merely an incremental improvement to existing methods but provides a new theoretical framework that explains why certain approaches work better than others. This aligns perfectly with the type of bold, novel contribution that NIPS values.

The paper's significance lies in its theoretical explanation for an empirical observation that has been noted in the literature but not previously understood. This theoretical insight will guide future work in tensor decomposition and potentially influence how practitioners select between these approaches.

While there are minor limitations (e.g., the focus on denoising rather than completion, and the synthetic nature of the experiments), these do not diminish the paper's core contribution. The theoretical novelty and explanatory power of the work outweigh these minor shortcomings.

In summary, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a duality between two types of structured Schatten norms and providing a clean theoretical explanation for why the latent approach to tensor decomposition outperforms the overlapped approach in many practical scenarios. The results are novel, well-supported by theory and experiments, and have the potential to guide future work in tensor decomposition.

**Strengths:**
- Novel theoretical framework explaining empirical observations
- Elegant duality result between overlapped and latent norms
- Clear theoretical analysis with meaningful error bounds
- Good connection to structured sparsity literature
- Strong empirical validation of theoretical predictions

**Weaknesses:**
- Focuses on denoising rather than tensor completion (though noted as future work)
- Experiments limited to synthetic data

**Recommendation:** Accept. This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that explains a previously unexplained empirical phenomenon, with clear connections to broader literature. The novelty and theoretical insight outweigh minor limitations, making it a strong candidate for NIPS.
