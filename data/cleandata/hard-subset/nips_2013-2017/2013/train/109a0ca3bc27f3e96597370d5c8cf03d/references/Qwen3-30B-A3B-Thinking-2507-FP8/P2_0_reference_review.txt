

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper investigates two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped approach" and the "latent approach." The overlapped approach penalizes all tensor modes to be jointly low-rank using the overlapped Schatten 1-norm, while the latent approach decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode, using the latent Schatten 1-norm. The authors establish a duality between these two approaches, showing that the dual of the overlapped norm is the latent norm and vice versa. Their key theoretical contribution is proving that the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, while the overlapped approach's error scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in previous work. The paper provides theoretical guarantees, including consistency results and deterministic bounds, and validates these with numerical experiments.

The work connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature (e.g., group lasso), providing a unified framework that extends previous results. It builds upon and generalizes the recent work by Tomioka et al. [25, 26] by providing rigorous theoretical analysis for the latent approach.

## Quality

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous proofs, as evidenced by the appendix containing detailed proofs of all key lemmas and theorems. The main contribution - the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms - is a significant theoretical insight that generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. The authors clearly establish the connection between the two approaches to tensor decomposition and the structured sparsity literature, citing relevant works appropriately.

The theoretical analysis is thorough, with Theorems 1-3 providing clear consistency and performance bounds. The analysis of the latent approach's error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank (Theorem 2) directly explains the empirical observations in Figure 1. The numerical experiments in Section 4 validate the theoretical predictions, showing that the MSE scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (TR and LR complexity).

The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations, noting that the latent approach may not always be superior in simultaneously low-rank settings (Section 4) and identifying tensor completion as a future direction.

## Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and well-organized. The introduction clearly sets up the problem and motivation, the background section provides precise mathematical definitions, and the theoretical results are presented logically. The authors use Figure 1 effectively to illustrate the key conceptual difference between the two approaches.

The paper is sufficiently detailed for experts to understand and potentially reproduce the results. The mathematical notation is consistent throughout, and the definitions of the structured Schatten norms are clearly presented. The experimental section provides sufficient detail about the tensor generation, noise model, and evaluation metrics.

## Originality

This paper makes a significant original contribution by establishing the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which was previously only observed empirically. While the latent approach was proposed in [25], this paper provides the first theoretical explanation for its superior empirical performance. The connection to structured sparsity literature (group lasso) is insightful and well-executed.

The paper distinguishes itself clearly from previous work. The authors properly cite and differentiate their work from [25, 26], which focused on empirical comparisons without theoretical analysis. The duality result generalizes results from structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21], making it a novel contribution to both tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

## Significance

The paper addresses a fundamental question in tensor decomposition: which convex approach is theoretically superior in noisy settings. This is highly significant for practitioners who must choose between these two approaches. The theoretical insight that the latent approach "performs as if it knows the mode with the minimum rank" is a valuable contribution that guides practical implementation.

The results advance the state of the art by providing a theoretical explanation for empirical observations, filling an important gap in the literature. The paper's contribution is not just theoretical but has clear practical implications, as the latent approach is generally preferable for noisy tensor decomposition.

The significance is further enhanced by the paper's connection to broader structured sparsity literature, suggesting potential applications beyond tensor decomposition.

## Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**
1. Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear duality results
2. Theoretical explanation for empirical observations in prior work
3. Strong experimental validation that matches theoretical predictions
4. Clear writing and organization
5. Important practical implications for tensor decomposition practitioners

**Weaknesses:**
1. Limited discussion of computational complexity differences between the two approaches
2. No comparison with non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., Tucker or CP decomposition), which are the standard in tensor analysis
3. The paper focuses on the noisy tensor decomposition setting but doesn't address tensor completion with missing values, which was mentioned as a primary application in the introduction
4. The incoherence assumption in Theorem 2 might be restrictive for some applications

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that explains empirical observations
- The results are rigorously proven and validated experimentally
- The work connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, broadening its relevance
- The paper advances the state of the art in convex tensor decomposition
- The results have clear practical implications for practitioners

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- Limited scope in addressing tensor completion (missing values), which is a major application area
- No comparison with non-convex tensor decomposition methods, which are the standard in the field
- The paper could provide more discussion of computational considerations

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and insightful, and the theoretical analysis explaining the empirical superiority of the latent approach is rigorous and well-supported. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and the experimental results confirm the theoretical predictions.

While there are some limitations in scope (particularly the lack of comparison with non-convex methods and the focus on noisy decomposition rather than completion), these do not undermine the paper's core theoretical contribution. The paper's main contribution is the theoretical explanation for the empirical observations, not a direct comparison with all possible methods.

The paper advances the state of the art in convex tensor decomposition and provides valuable insights for both researchers and practitioners. The results are important and well-supported, making this a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend acceptance at NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement (if accepted)

1. Briefly mention the computational complexity of the latent approach versus the overlapped approach, as this is an important practical consideration.
2. Add a sentence or two discussing the relationship between the latent approach and non-convex methods like CP or Tucker decomposition, perhaps in the conclusion.
3. Consider expanding the discussion of the incoherence assumption in Theorem 2 to discuss when this might be violated in practice.
4. In the experimental section, it would be helpful to explicitly state the computational time for each method to provide a more complete comparison.

The paper is already strong, but these minor additions would further strengthen it.
