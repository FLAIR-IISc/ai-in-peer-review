This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The authors present theoretical analysis supporting the empirical observation that the latent approach performs better when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. They also establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms.

While the paper addresses a relevant and interesting problem – convex tensor decomposition – and presents some novel theoretical results, I find numerous shortcomings, ranging from minor notational inconsistencies to substantial gaps in the theoretical justification and presentation, ultimately leading me to recommend rejection.

**Strengths:**

*   **Problem Relevance:** Tensor decomposition is a crucial technique with applications across diverse fields.
*   **Duality Result:** The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a potentially valuable theoretical contribution.
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** The attempt to provide a theoretical grounding for the latent approach is commendable.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The inclusion of empirical results, and the attempt to connect them to the theoretical findings, is positive.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Notational Inconsistency & Clarity:** The paper suffers from inconsistent notation. For example, the definition of the Frobenius norm uses `∣∣∣∣∣∣W∣∣∣∣∣∣F` and then later simply `||W||F` without explicit redefinition. The definition of the unfolded tensor `W(k)` is also used without sufficient clarity regarding the ordering of indices, which is crucial for correct interpretation. This impacts readability and trust in the technical correctness.
2.  **Lack of Rigor in Theorem Statements:** Several theorem statements lack sufficient detail and clarity. Theorem 2, for instance, introduces the assumption of “incoherence” without adequately defining what constitutes a reasonable level of incoherence or how it affects the results.  The meaning of `r̄k` is unclear as to whether it's the rank of the *true* tensor or the decomposed factor.
3.  **Missing Justification of Key Steps:** Several steps in the proofs are glossed over without sufficient explanation.  The jump from the analysis of individual components `Ŵ(k)` to an overall error bound in Theorem 2 appears too simplistic and lacks a clear justification.  The appendix is mentioned but not accessible for review.
4.  **Overstated Claims:** The claim that the latent approach "performs better in many cases" even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank is not strongly supported by the presented numerical results. Figure 2 shows marginal improvement, and the improvement is highly sensitive to the choice of the regularization parameter.
5.  **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The experiments are limited to relatively small tensor sizes (50x50x20 or 80x80x40).  It is unclear whether the observed results would generalize to higher-dimensional tensors, where the advantages of the latent approach might be more pronounced or, conversely, overshadowed by computational complexities.
6.  **Connection to Prior Work:** While the authors cite relevant work, the discussion often lacks depth. For instance, the connection to structured sparsity literature (group lasso) is mentioned but not fully explored. The nuances in applying these techniques to tensor decomposition are not adequately addressed.
7. **Ambiguous definitions**: The definition of the noise "E" is not clear. Is it element wise noise? It's unclear.

**Pro & Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
*   The attempt to provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach.

**Con:**

*   Numerous notational inconsistencies and clarity issues.
*   Lack of rigor and justification in the theoretical analysis.
*   Limited scope of numerical experiments and weak empirical evidence.
*   Overstated claims regarding the performance of the latent approach.
*   Missing Appendix.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite addressing a relevant research question, the paper’s shortcomings in clarity, rigor, and experimental validation outweigh its strengths. The lack of a readily accessible appendix further hinders proper assessment. The theoretical contributions, while interesting, are not presented with sufficient detail and justification to be fully convincing. The empirical results are not strong enough to support the broad claims made by the authors.  Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. A significant revision addressing the issues outlined above, including a thorough proofreading and clarification of the mathematical derivations, along with more extensive and robust numerical experiments, would be necessary for reconsideration.
