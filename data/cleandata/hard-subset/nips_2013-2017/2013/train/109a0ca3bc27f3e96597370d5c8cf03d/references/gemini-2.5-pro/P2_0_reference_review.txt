### **Summary**

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex approach to tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of component tensors, each of which is low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the more established "overlapped approach," where a single tensor is penalized to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

The main contributions are:
1.  **A Unifying Framework:** The paper formalizes the regularizers for both approaches as "overlapped" and "latent" structured Schatten norms. It establishes a novel duality between these two norms, elegantly connecting the tensor decomposition literature to the broader field of structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped and latent group LASSO).
2.  **Theoretical Performance Bounds:** The paper derives error bounds for the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key theoretical result is that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor (`min_k r_k`), whereas prior work has shown the overlapped approach scales with the *average* of the square root of the mode-k ranks (`(Σ sqrt(r_k))^2`). This provides a rigorous explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach when a tensor is highly asymmetric in its rank structure.
3.  **Empirical Validation:** The authors present numerical experiments that confirm their theoretical predictions, showing that the estimation error of the latent approach scales linearly with a complexity measure derived from their theoretical bounds.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

#### **Quality**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is rigorous, building from the fundamental duality of the proposed norms (Lemma 1) to the key inequality for the latent norm (Lemma 2) and culminating in the main performance bounds (Theorems 1-3). The analysis is a complete piece of work, providing consistency, deterministic bounds under an incoherence assumption, and probabilistic bounds for Gaussian noise. The experiments are well-designed, not just to show that one method is better, but specifically to validate the theoretical scaling laws derived in the paper. This is a significant strength, as it directly connects the theory to practice.

*   **Weaknesses:** The analysis relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) for the tighter bounds in Theorem 2. While the authors correctly note that such assumptions are common in related literature (e.g., for robust PCA or multi-task learning), a brief discussion on the practical implications or restrictiveness of this assumption for tensors would strengthen the paper. For instance, are naturally occurring tensors likely to satisfy this condition? Additionally, the analysis is confined to the denoising setting, while tensor completion (recovering from missing entries) is another major application area. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it is a current limitation.

#### **Clarity**

*   **Strengths:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction does an excellent job of motivating the problem by contrasting the two approaches and clearly stating the paper's contributions. Section 2 provides clear definitions of the structured Schatten norms and their properties, making the subsequent theoretical development easy to follow. The distinction between the multilinear rank of the overall tensor `W*` and the ranks of the component tensors `W*(k)` is handled carefully. The experimental section is also very clear in its setup and interpretation of results.

*   **Weaknesses:** I have no significant complaints regarding clarity. The exposition is logical and the mathematical notation is standard and well-defined.

#### **Originality**

*   **Strengths:** The paper makes several original contributions. While the latent approach itself was proposed in a technical report by some of the same authors [25], this paper is the first to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis explaining *why* and *when* it outperforms the overlapped approach. The central originality lies in this analysis. The formalization of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a novel and elegant theoretical result that unifies concepts from tensor decomposition and structured sparsity. The derivation of an error bound that depends on `min_k r_k` is a new and powerful insight that advances our understanding of convex tensor methods.

*   **Weaknesses:** As noted, the algorithm/optimization problem itself is not new. However, this is not a significant weakness, as the paper's primary contribution is clearly framed as a theoretical one, which is substantial and novel in its own right.

#### **Significance**

*   **Strengths:** The results presented are highly significant for researchers and practitioners working with tensor data.
    1.  **Practical Guidance:** The paper provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence that the latent approach is a more robust choice than the overlapped approach. It performs better in cases where the rank is concentrated in one mode and performs at least as well in cases favorable to the overlapped method. This is a clear, actionable takeaway.
    2.  **Theoretical Insight:** The result that the latent approach implicitly adapts to the lowest-rank mode without being given this information is a deep and important finding. It advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of convex relaxations for tensor problems.
    3.  **Future Work:** The framework of structured Schatten norms and their duality opens the door for further research, including analyzing other norms in the family (e.g., S1/∞) or extending the analysis to other problems like tensor completion. The connection to structured sparsity may also inspire cross-pollination of ideas between the two fields.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

1.  The calculation of the "LR complexity" for the experiments involved finding an optimal decomposition of the *true* tensor `W*`. This is reasonable for validating the theory, but it would be interesting to know how the ranks of the *estimated* components `W-hat(k)` relate to this oracle decomposition. Does the estimator find a decomposition that reflects the minimal rank structure?
2.  In Theorem 3, the regularization parameter `λ` depends on `α`. In practice, `α` is an unknown quantity related to the incoherence of the ground truth tensor. How sensitive is the method to the choice of `α` in the constrained optimization problem (11)? A comment on how one might set this hyperparameter would be useful.

### **Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that provides a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. It is technically sound, clearly written, and the results are both novel and important. It resolves an open question regarding the performance of two competing convex tensor decomposition methods and provides a strong theoretical foundation for preferring the latent approach. The connection it draws to the structured sparsity literature is also a valuable contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent tensor decomposition approach.
*   Establishes an elegant duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, connecting two important fields.
*   Provides the key insight that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, explaining its empirical success.
*   Well-designed experiments that directly validate the theoretical scaling predictions.
*   Clearly written and highly significant for both theory and practice.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis is for the denoising case, not the arguably more common tensor completion case.
*   The tighter theoretical bounds rely on an incoherence assumption whose practical implications are not fully discussed.
(These are minor limitations and do not detract from the overall strength of the paper.)
