This paper studies the theoretical properties of a convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. This approach was previously proposed in [25], but its theoretical properties were not well understood. The authors' main contributions are twofold. First, they establish a novel duality between the "latent Schatten norm" (used in the latent approach) and the "overlapped Schatten norm" (used in a competing convex approach). This elegantly connects the tensor decomposition problem to the broader literature on structured sparsity, such as the latent and overlapped group LASSO. Second, they provide a rigorous performance analysis for the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key result is an error bound that scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks [26]. This provides a compelling theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper concludes with numerical experiments designed to validate these theoretical scaling laws.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The analysis of the latent approach is a valuable contribution. The error bound's dependence on the minimum mode-k rank is an important and intuitive finding that clarifies the mechanism behind the method's success.
2.  **Novel Duality Result:** The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is elegant and provides a unifying perspective, linking this line of work to fundamental concepts in structured sparsity.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper effectively contrasts the overlapped and latent approaches, using Figure 1 to motivate the need for a deeper analysis of the latter.
4.  **Targeted Experiments:** The numerical results are specifically designed to test the derived theoretical scaling laws, which is a commendable practice. The experiments provide good evidence supporting the claim that the MSE of the latent approach scales with the proposed "Latent rank (LR) complexity."

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the strong conceptual contributions, the paper suffers from a significant number of issues in its presentation, methodological clarity, and technical precision, which detract from its overall quality.

1.  **Clarity and Precision of Technical Details:**
    *   The mathematical notation is often imprecise. For instance, the definitions of the latent norms in (4) and (6) use the unconventional notation `inf(X(1)+···+X(K))=X`, which should be more formally written as an infimum over a set, e.g., `inf_{X(1),...,X(K) s.t. ΣX(k)=X}`.
    *   The formulation of the main optimization problem for the analysis in (11) is deeply confusing. The problem is stated as an `argmin` over `W`, yet it includes constraints on the latent components `W(k)`, which are witnesses to the latent norm of `W`. It is unclear how this constrained problem over `W` is a convex program or how one would solve it. This should be reformulated as an optimization over the components `W(1), ..., W(K)`. This ambiguity undermines the clarity of the central theoretical setup.
    *   In the last sentence of Section 2.2, a typo exists: "performs betters than".
    *   In Theorem 3, the assumption that dimensions are sorted is not "without loss of generality," as the bound explicitly depends on `nK`, which is defined as the smallest dimension under this sorting. This is a subtle but important imprecision.

2.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Experiments:**
    *   A major issue is the gap between the algorithm analyzed and the algorithm implemented. The theoretical analysis for the tighter bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) relies on the constrained optimization problem (11), which requires knowledge of an incoherence parameter `α`. However, the paper gives no indication of how `α` would be set in practice, nor does it state that this constrained version was used in the experiments. The experiments appear to use the unconstrained algorithm from [25], which corresponds to problem (5). This discrepancy calls into question whether the theoretical analysis truly explains the empirical results presented.

3.  **Characterization of Prior Work:**
    *   The introduction mischaracterizes the work of Tomioka et al. [26] as focusing on the "noise-less tensor completion setting." The title of [26] is "Statistical performance of convex tensor decomposition," and the work explicitly analyzes performance under noisy observations. The primary difference between this paper's setting and that of [26] appears to be denoising (fully observed) versus completion (partially observed), not the presence of noise. This is a significant error in situating the paper's contribution relative to closely related work.

4.  **Minor Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   The definition of "Latent rank (LR) complexity" in Section 4 is convoluted, relying on an oracle procedure (running the algorithm on the noiseless true tensor). Since the authors state that an optimal decomposition is typically a singleton, a simpler and clearer definition based directly on the minimum mode-k rank would have been more effective.
    *   The reference list is poorly formatted and inconsistent. Citation styles vary (e.g., "In Proc. of" vs. "In Advances in NIPS"), journal and conference names are inconsistently abbreviated or omitted, and there is a typo in [15] ("Prof. ICCV" instead of "Proc."). This lack of attention to detail is concerning.

**Summary and Recommendation:**

This paper addresses an important problem and presents novel theoretical ideas that have the potential for high impact. The core results on the duality of structured Schatten norms and the performance bounds for the latent approach are significant.

However, the manuscript in its current form is not ready for publication at NIPS. The work is undermined by a lack of meticulousness, leading to a number of clarity issues, technical imprecisions, a critical disconnect between the analyzed theory and the experiments, and an inaccurate representation of prior work. While the central ideas are strong, the execution is flawed. The sheer volume of minor and major errors suggests the paper was prepared in a hurry and did not undergo sufficient internal review.

Therefore, I recommend **rejection**. I would strongly encourage the authors to undertake a major revision to address the numerous issues raised. With careful correction and clarification, particularly regarding the formulation in (11) and its connection to the experiments, this work could become a very strong contribution.
