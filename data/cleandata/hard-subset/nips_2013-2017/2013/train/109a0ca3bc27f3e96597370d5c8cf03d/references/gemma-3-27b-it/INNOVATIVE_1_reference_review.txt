This paper tackles the problem of convex tensor decomposition, specifically contrasting two approaches: an "overlapped" method penalizing all modes simultaneously, and a “latent” method focusing on low-rank structure in individual modes. The core contribution lies in theoretically analyzing the latent approach, demonstrating a performance bound scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank – a key explanation for its empirical superiority observed in prior work (Tomioka et al., 2015). The authors also establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature.

The paper’s strength is undeniably its **novelty**. The connection between structured sparsity and tensor decomposition through the duality result is insightful, and the theoretical analysis providing a bound dependent on the *minimum* rank is a significant step forward. This directly addresses a long-standing gap in understanding the performance of these convex relaxation methods for tensor decomposition. The empirical validation, while not extensive, complements the theory and supports the claims. I particularly appreciate the explicit connection to prior empirical observations (Figure 1 in Tomioka et al., 2015) and the attempt to quantify the improvement with a theoretical bound. The paper also clearly positions itself within the existing literature on both tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, providing appropriate citations.

However, the work isn't without weaknesses. The theoretical analysis, while impressive, relies on several assumptions (incoherence, noise model) which, while standard, aren’t deeply discussed regarding their impact on real-world applicability. The numerical experiments are relatively limited, and primarily serve to confirm the theoretical scaling rather than to explore the method’s behavior in more complex scenarios or on real-world datasets. While the authors acknowledge this ("most of the randomly generated low-rank tensors were simultaneously low-rank to some degree"), a stronger empirical evaluation would bolster the claims. The discussion of the optimization algorithm is brief; details on computational cost and scalability would be valuable. Finally, while the paper highlights the benefits of the latent approach, a more thorough investigation of *when* the latent approach truly excels (beyond the single-mode low-rank case) would be beneficial. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   **Significant Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of the latent approach and the duality result are genuinely novel contributions.
*   **Addresses an Important Problem:** Understanding the theoretical properties of convex tensor decomposition is crucial for wider adoption and application.
*   **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally clear and well-organized, with a logical flow of ideas.
*   **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The analysis is rigorous and builds on established results in related fields.
*   **Empirical Support:** The experiments, while limited, support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments lack robustness and don't explore diverse datasets or scenarios.
*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on assumptions that could limit its practical applicability.
*   **Brief Discussion of Optimization:** Lack of detail on computational complexity and scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses, I strongly lean towards **acceptance**. The paper presents a novel and theoretically grounded contribution to a challenging problem. The insight that the latent approach benefits from the minimum rank is a significant finding. While a more comprehensive empirical evaluation would be desirable, the current validation is sufficient to support the claims, given the theoretical justification. This work opens up new avenues for research in convex tensor decomposition and has the potential to significantly impact the field.  The risks of accepting a paper with minor flaws are outweighed by the potential reward of fostering a new direction in research.
