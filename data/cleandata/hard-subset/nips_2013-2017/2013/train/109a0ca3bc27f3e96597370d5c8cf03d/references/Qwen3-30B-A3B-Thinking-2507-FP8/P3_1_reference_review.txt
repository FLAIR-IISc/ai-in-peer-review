

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper investigates two approaches to convex tensor decomposition: the "overlapped approach" (which penalizes all modes simultaneously to be low-rank) and the "latent approach" (which decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors introduce two structured Schatten norms (overlapped and latent) and establish a key duality between them, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. The main theoretical contribution is showing that the latent approach's mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average (square root) of the mode-k ranks. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach and empirically confirm their predictions on synthetic data.

The paper builds on recent work by Tomioka et al. [25, 26] who proposed these approaches, but provides the first theoretical analysis of the latent approach. The connection to structured sparsity (particularly group lasso) is well-motivated and provides a useful conceptual bridge between tensor decomposition and other areas of machine learning.

## Quality

**Strengths:**
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured. The authors provide clear consistency results (Theorem 1), deterministic bounds (Theorem 2), and Gaussian noise analysis (Theorem 3) that progressively build on each other.
- The duality result (Lemma 1) between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a significant theoretical contribution that connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature. This is a valuable conceptual advance.
- The proof techniques are sound, and the authors provide detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The connection to Agarwal et al. [1] is well-executed.
- The empirical results (Section 4) effectively validate the theoretical predictions, showing the linear scaling of MSE with the predicted complexity measures.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational complexity. While the authors mention using ADMM, they don't compare the computational cost of the latent approach versus the overlapped approach, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The incoherence assumption (10) is somewhat restrictive and not well-justified. The paper doesn't discuss how often this assumption holds in practice or how to verify it for real data. This limits the applicability of the theoretical results.
- The experimental section relies solely on synthetic data. While this is appropriate for validating the theoretical scaling, the paper would be significantly strengthened by including at least one real-world application to demonstrate practical utility.
- The paper doesn't address the sensitivity of the latent approach to the regularization parameter λ. The theoretical bounds depend on λ, but the paper doesn't provide practical guidance for choosing λ without knowing noise levels or ranks.

## Clarity

**Strengths:**
- The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation.
- Mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined throughout.
- The supplementary material provides detailed proofs, which is essential for verifying the theoretical claims.
- The paper effectively uses Figure 1 to illustrate the key conceptual difference between the two approaches.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper uses "mode-k rank" and "multilinear rank" somewhat interchangeably, which could confuse readers. The distinction between these concepts should be clarified (mode-k rank refers to the rank of the mode-k unfolding, while multilinear rank is the tuple of mode-k ranks).
- The connection to structured sparsity literature (group lasso) is mentioned but not fully developed. A more detailed explanation of how the tensor decomposition problem maps to the group sparsity problem would improve clarity.
- The intuition for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode is somewhat technical. A more intuitive explanation would help readers grasp the key insight.

## Originality

**Strengths:**
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a novel theoretical contribution that generalizes results from structured sparsity literature.
- The paper provides the first theoretical analysis of the latent approach, which was previously only studied empirically [25].
- The connection to structured sparsity (particularly the latent group lasso) provides a fresh perspective on tensor decomposition.

**Weaknesses:**
- The paper builds heavily on the work of Tomioka et al. [25, 26], which already proposed the latent and overlapped approaches. While the theoretical analysis is new, the core ideas of the approaches themselves are not novel.
- The structured Schatten norms are extensions of existing norms used in matrix and tensor analysis. The novelty is primarily in the duality result and the theoretical analysis of the latent approach.
- The paper doesn't explore the full space of structured Schatten norms (e.g., Sp/q for different p, q). A more comprehensive exploration would strengthen the originality.

## Significance

**Strengths:**
- The paper addresses a fundamental problem in tensor decomposition: the choice between overlapped and latent approaches. The theoretical results provide a clear explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach.
- The connection to structured sparsity literature makes the results relevant to a broader audience in machine learning.
- The paper has potential to influence future work in convex tensor decomposition and related fields.

**Weaknesses:**
- The significance is somewhat limited by the focus on the denoising problem (recovery from noisy measurements) rather than tensor completion (with missing entries), which is a more common application. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't address how their results extend to missing data.
- The paper doesn't compare the latent approach to non-convex tensor decomposition methods, which are the standard in practice. It's unclear whether the convex approach (whether overlapped or latent) is competitive with non-convex methods in terms of accuracy or computational efficiency.
- The practical impact is limited by the lack of real-world applications. The synthetic experiments are useful for theory validation but don't demonstrate the approach's utility in real-world scenarios.

## Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This is a technically sound paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of convex tensor decomposition. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis of the latent approach provides important insights that explain its empirical success.

However, the paper has several limitations that prevent it from being a strong candidate for NIPS. The lack of real-world applications, the restrictive incoherence assumption, and the absence of computational complexity analysis limit the practical significance of the work. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including real-world experiments and a more thorough discussion of computational considerations.

**Recommendation: Weak Reject**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Novel theoretical contribution (duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms)
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with well-supported claims
- Clear empirical validation of theoretical predictions
- Connection to structured sparsity literature provides broader relevance

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited to synthetic data, no real-world applications
- Restrictive incoherence assumption without practical justification
- No comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods
- No discussion of computational complexity or practical implementation details
- Focus on denoising rather than the more common tensor completion problem

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Include real-world experiments:** Add at least one application to a real-world tensor dataset (e.g., neuroimaging data, video data) to demonstrate practical utility.

2. **Expand on computational considerations:** Discuss the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach and to non-convex methods. Provide guidance on choosing λ in practice.

3. **Clarify incoherence assumptions:** Discuss how often the incoherence assumption (10) holds in practice and suggest ways to verify it for real data.

4. **Expand on connections to structured sparsity:** Provide a more detailed explanation of how the tensor decomposition problem maps to the group lasso problem, making the connection more accessible to readers.

5. **Compare to non-convex methods:** Include a comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., alternating least squares, higher-order orthogonal iteration) to demonstrate the practical competitiveness of the convex approach.

6. **Address tensor completion:** Extend the theoretical results to the tensor completion setting (with missing entries) to make the work more relevant to the broader tensor decomposition community.

This paper has strong theoretical foundations and makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. With the suggested improvements, particularly adding real-world applications and addressing computational considerations, it would be an excellent candidate for NIPS. As it stands, the limitations prevent it from being a strong submission for the conference.
