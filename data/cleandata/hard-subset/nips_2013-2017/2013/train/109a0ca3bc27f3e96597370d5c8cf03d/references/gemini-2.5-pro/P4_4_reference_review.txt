**Summary**

This paper investigates a convex optimization approach for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," where a tensor is modeled as a sum of component tensors, each of which is constrained to be low-rank in a specific mode. This method was empirically introduced in Tomioka et al. [25] as an alternative to the more standard "overlapped approach" [9, 15, 23, 26], which regularizes a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

The main contributions of this paper are threefold. First, it provides a unifying framework of "structured Schatten norms" that formally defines the regularizers used in both the overlapped and latent approaches. Second, it establishes a novel duality between the "overlapped Sp/q-norm" and the "latent Sp*/q*-norm," generalizing similar results from the structured sparsity literature [13, 17, 21]. Third, and most importantly, the paper presents the first theoretical performance analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key result is an error bound showing that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor. This contrasts with the known error bound for the overlapped approach, which scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks [26]. This theoretical finding provides a rigorous explanation for why the latent approach can significantly outperform the overlapped approach, particularly when the underlying tensor has a low-rank structure in only one or a few modes. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments that validate the predicted scaling behavior of the error.

**Strengths (Arguments for acceptance)**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper provides the first theoretical guarantees for the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition. The main result—that the statistical complexity of the latent approach depends on the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average—is a significant and impactful finding. It provides a clear theoretical justification for preferring the latent formulation over the overlapped one in certain scenarios, advancing our understanding of convex relaxations for tensor problems. This result helps bridge the gap between empirical observations [25] and theoretical understanding.

*   **Technical Quality:** The technical contributions are solid. The formalization of the problem through structured Schatten norms is elegant and provides a valuable connection to the extensive literature on structured sparsity. The derivation of the duality between the overlapped and latent norms (Lemma 1) is a key technical tool that is of independent interest. The main performance guarantees (Theorems 2 and 3) are derived rigorously, building upon established techniques from high-dimensional statistics [1, 20]. The assumptions made, such as the incoherence condition, are standard in related work on decomposing signals into constituent parts.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly motivates the problem by contrasting the two main approaches. The theoretical framework is introduced logically, and the main results are stated precisely with clear interpretations. The comparison with the prior analysis of the overlapped approach [26] is direct and effective at highlighting the paper's core contribution. The experiments are well-designed to specifically test the theoretical predictions, making the connection between theory and practice explicit and convincing.

**Weaknesses (Arguments against acceptance / Questions for authors)**

*   **Gap between Theory and Practice in the Optimization Problem:** The main performance bound in Theorem 2 is derived for a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that explicitly incorporates an incoherence parameter α. However, the numerical experiments appear to be conducted using the unconstrained formulation (Eq. 5), which is what one would likely use in practice. While the authors state that the constraints are introduced to control correlations between components for the analysis, it would be helpful to clarify the relationship between the analyzed algorithm and the one used in the experiments. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of α, and how would one set this parameter in a real-world application?

*   **Practicality of the "Latent Rank Complexity":** The experimental validation relies on a "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" measure (Eq. 18) defined by the ranks `r̄_k` of an optimal decomposition of the *true* noiseless tensor `W*`. This requires an oracle procedure to compute, which is not available in practice. While the paper correctly notes that this can be upper-bounded by using a singleton decomposition (which relates to `min_k r_k`), the plots are based on the oracle `r̄_k`. It would strengthen the empirical section to also show how the error scales with the more practical `min_k r_k`, as this is the key takeaway from the theoretical bound in Eq. (13) and (15).

*   **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the denoising problem where the tensor is fully observed. A primary motivation for low-rank tensor methods is the completion problem, where many entries are missing. While the authors acknowledge this as an important area for future work, the current analysis does not cover this widely applicable setting. This limits the immediate practical implications of the derived bounds, although the denoising setting is a crucial and standard first step for theoretical analysis.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. It provides a rigorous and insightful analysis of the latent approach, establishing a key theoretical result that explains its empirical advantages. The connection to structured sparsity via the new framework of structured Schatten norms is elegant and valuable. Despite some minor weaknesses regarding the gap between the analyzed and implemented algorithms and the scope of the analysis, the strengths of the paper in terms of originality, technical soundness, and clarity are substantial. The work meaningfully advances the field's understanding of convex methods for tensor problems.
