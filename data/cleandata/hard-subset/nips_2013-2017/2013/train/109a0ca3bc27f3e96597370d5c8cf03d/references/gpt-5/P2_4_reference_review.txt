Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation and denoising, focusing on two families of structured Schatten norms: the “overlapped” norms (sum over mode unfoldings) and the “latent” norms (infimal convolution over mode-specific low-rank components). It establishes a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm, extending known results from overlapped/latent group sparsity to tensors. Building on this, the authors analyze the statistical performance of the latent approach under noise. They prove a consistency bound for the latent Schatten-1 estimator, a deterministic bound under an incoherence-type constraint, and high-probability bounds for Gaussian noise. Crucially, the latent approach yields error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, contrasting with prior results for the overlapped approach that scale with (averages of) mode ranks. Synthetic experiments verify the predicted scalings and show that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach, even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across modes.

Quality
- Theory: The duality lemma appears technically sound and aligns with known sparsity-norm dualities. The consistency bound (Theorem 1) and Gaussian-noise bounds (Theorem 3) are plausible and dimensionally consistent; the role of the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of noise in tuning λ is standard. The deterministic bound (Theorem 2) uses an incoherence-type condition and additional spectral norm constraints to control inter-component correlations, reminiscent of the “dirty model” analysis. The key improvement—dependence on mink rk—is well justified by the latent norm inequality (Lemma 2) and the flexibility of singleton decompositions.
- Assumptions: The added feasibility constraints ∥W(l)(k)∥S∞ ≤ α (l ≠ k) are important for the deterministic analysis but their practical enforceability and choice of α are not fully discussed; this creates a theory–practice gap if experiments did not enforce them. The Gaussian noise setting is standard, but the bounds may be conservative.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments carefully probe scaling vs. rank and dimension. The definitions of TR and LR “complexities” are reasonable proxies, though computing LR complexity by running the latent method on noiseless W* is somewhat circular for validation. There is no real-data evaluation or tensor completion experiment, and no timing or convergence comparisons.

Clarity
- The paper is well structured (definitions → duality → theory → experiments). However, notation overload is a minor issue: both overlapped and latent S1/1 are denoted by the same symbol; distinguishing them with subscripts or different glyphs would reduce confusion. The role of the α-constraints in the algorithm and in experiments should be clarified (were they enforced? how was α chosen?). Algorithmic details are delegated to prior work, which is acceptable, but a brief summary of computational complexity and implementation choices would help reproducibility.

Originality
- The duality result for structured Schatten norms is a clean and nontrivial extension of known overlapped/latent group sparsity dualities to tensors. The performance analysis that pinpoints mink rk as the key complexity measure for the latent approach provides a clear theoretical explanation for empirical observations and improves on prior results that favored the overlapped approach. This constitutes a meaningful conceptual and technical advance.

Significance
- The work addresses a central issue in tensor learning: how to regularize for low-rank structure robustly. Showing that the latent approach adapts to the most favorable mode via mink rk is both theoretically insightful and practically useful, and can guide the choice of regularization in tensor denoising and potentially completion. The lack of real-data and completion results limits immediate practical impact, but the theoretical contribution is likely to influence follow-up work on convex tensor methods.

Strengths
- Clear and general duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms.
- New error bounds for the latent approach with favorable dependence on mink rk.
- Consistency results and Gaussian noise rates with explicit, rank-free tuning of λ.
- Experiments that align with theoretical scaling predictions.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The deterministic analysis hinges on additional spectral norm constraints; it is unclear if these were enforced in practice, how α is set, and how sensitive results are to α.
- Experimental scope is limited: synthetic denoising only, no missing-data (completion) setting, no real data, limited size, and no comparisons to nonconvex baselines or alternative tensor norms (e.g., tubal nuclear norm/t-SVD).
- Notational ambiguity between overlapped and latent S1/1; minor clarity issues.
- The method’s computational cost and scalability are not discussed.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify whether the α-constraints in (11) are used in experiments; if so, specify α selection; if not, discuss the gap between theory and practice.
- Add experiments on tensor completion and at least one real dataset; include runtime and convergence comparisons.
- Compare against additional baselines (nonconvex CP/Tucker with model selection, tubal nuclear norm methods).
- Disambiguate notation for overlapped vs latent norms and add intuition for the duality and its algorithmic implications.

Overall assessment
Technically solid with a clear theoretical contribution and a compelling explanation for when and why the latent approach should be preferred. Clarity is good but could be improved with better notation and explicit discussion of constraints. The empirical section supports the theory but is narrow. On balance, the paper meets the NeurIPS criteria well in quality and originality, moderately in clarity, and reasonably in significance; I lean toward acceptance, especially for its principled extension of structured sparsity duality to tensors and the resulting statistical insights.
