### Paper Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, first introduced in Tomioka et al. [25]. The method models a tensor as a sum of K component tensors, where each component is constrained to be low-rank in a specific mode via a trace norm penalty. The paper formalizes the regularizer used in this approach as the "latent Schatten norm" and establishes a novel duality between it and the "overlapped Schatten norm" used in competing convex formulations. This duality result connects tensor decomposition to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The main contribution is a rigorous performance analysis of the latent approach in a noisy setting. The authors derive error bounds showing that the mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor (`min_k rk`). This contrasts favorably with the known bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the average of the mode-k ranks. The paper concludes with numerical experiments on synthetic data that confirm these theoretical scaling predictions.

### Strengths

1.  **Rigorous Theoretical Contribution:** The primary strength of this paper is its thorough theoretical analysis of the latent approach to tensor decomposition. It provides the first performance guarantees for this method, filling an important gap in the literature. The derived error bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) offer clear, interpretable insights into the method's behavior.

2.  **Novel Duality Framework:** The formalization of overlapped and latent Schatten norms and the proof of their duality is an elegant and valuable contribution. This framework provides a principled connection between recent work in convex tensor decomposition and the well-established theory of structured sparsity (e.g., latent/overlapped group LASSO), which may spur further theoretical developments.

3.  **Clear Explanation of Competing Methods:** The paper provides a strong theoretical argument for why the latent approach may be preferable to the overlapped approach. By showing that the error bound depends on `min_k rk` rather than `sum_k sqrt(rk)`, the authors provide a compelling explanation for the empirical observation that the latent method excels when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on synthetic data. While these simulations are well-designed to verify the derived theoretical scaling laws, they do not provide sufficient evidence to support the broad claim that the "latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach" in practice. The introduction motivates the problem with real-world applications like neuroimaging, but the paper presents no results on such data. A convincing case for a new method requires demonstration of its effectiveness on real-world problems, not just confirmation of its theoretical behavior in idealized settings.

2.  **Uncertainty of Assumptions:** The main performance guarantees (Theorems 2 and 3) depend on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and require solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes spectral norm constraints on cross-modal components. The practical implications of these assumptions are not sufficiently discussed. In particular, the parameter `alpha` must be known beforehand to formulate the optimization problem, which seems unrealistic in practice. This dependency on an unknown oracle parameter significantly undermines the practical applicability of the analyzed estimator.

3.  **Narrow Comparison to Baselines:** The paper's comparison is focused almost exclusively on the "overlapped" convex method. While this is the most relevant competitor from a theoretical standpoint, it is a narrow view of the field. Established non-convex methods like Alternating Least Squares (ALS) for CP or Tucker decompositions are still the standard in many application domains. For the paper's claims of superiority to be fully substantiated, a comparison against these widely-used, established baselines would be necessary. The current evidence only shows that one recent convex approach might be better than another under certain conditions.

4.  **Novelty of the Method:** It is important to note that the latent decomposition algorithm itself is not new, having been proposed in previous work [25]. The contribution of this paper is purely theoretical. While the analysis is valuable, the paper could be clearer in positioning itself as a theoretical investigation of an existing method rather than the introduction of a novel one.

### Recommendation

This paper provides a solid, methodologically sound theoretical analysis of the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition. The duality result is elegant, and the error bounds offer significant insight. From a theoretical perspective, this is a good contribution.

However, my enthusiasm is tempered by a number of concerns. The reliance on an incoherence parameter `alpha` that must be known *a priori* for the main theoretical results is a significant practical limitation. Furthermore, the empirical evidence is confined to synthetic data and a narrow comparison to a single competing convex method. The claims of general superiority are therefore not fully supported. While the theory is strong, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that this new approach provides a tangible advantage over established, widely-used tensor decomposition methods in realistic scenarios.

Given the high standards of NeurIPS, a paper should ideally offer both theoretical novelty and a clear demonstration of practical significance. This paper delivers on the former but falls short on the latter. Nonetheless, the theoretical contributions are sound and advance our understanding of convex relaxation for tensor problems.

**Final Decision:** Weak Accept.

I would recommend acceptance on the grounds of the strong theoretical contribution. However, I would strongly urge the authors to revise the manuscript to:
1.  Tone down the claims of general superiority of the latent approach.
2.  Add a detailed discussion on the limitations imposed by the incoherence assumption and the practical difficulty of setting the `alpha` parameter in the estimator.
3.  Acknowledge the need for future work comparing the method against traditional non-convex baselines on real-world datasets.
