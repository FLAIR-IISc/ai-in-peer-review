### **Summary**

This paper investigates a convex approach to tensor decomposition based on what the authors term the "latent Schatten norm." This approach models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. The authors contrast this with the more established "overlapped Schatten norm" approach, where a single tensor is penalized for being simultaneously low-rank across all its modes.

The main contributions are:
1.  Formalizing the "overlapped" and "latent" structured Schatten norms for tensors and establishing a duality relationship between them, connecting this framework to the literature on structured sparsity.
2.  Providing a theoretical analysis of the denoising performance of the latent approach. The key result is an error bound that scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error bound depends on the *average* of the mode-k ranks.
3.  Empirically validating the derived theoretical error bounds on synthetic data, showing that the mean squared error scales as predicted and that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novel Theoretical Insight:** The paper provides the first rigorous performance analysis for the latent tensor decomposition model, which was previously only empirically justified [25]. The main theoretical result—that the error scales with `min_k rk` rather than an average—is a significant finding. It offers a clear and compelling explanation for why this method performs well, especially for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode.

2.  **Elegant Formalism:** The framework of "structured Schatten norms" and the duality between the overlapped and latent variants (Lemma 1) is an elegant contribution. It unifies the two main convex approaches to tensor decomposition and connects them to the well-studied concepts of overlapped and latent group sparsity, providing a broader context for the work.

3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments in Section 4 are well-designed to specifically test the theoretical predictions. The demonstration that the MSE scales linearly with the proposed "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" (Figure 2, middle panel) is a strong piece of evidence supporting the analysis in Theorem 3.

### **Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution, it suffers from several methodological and presentational flaws that undermine its overall quality.

1.  **Major Discrepancy Between Theory and Experiments:** The main theoretical results for the noisy case (Theorems 2 and 3) are derived for a modified optimization problem (11) which includes an explicit incoherence constraint: `∥W(l)(k)∥S∞ ≤ α, ∀l ≠ k`. This is a crucial assumption for the proof. However, the experiments in Section 4 appear to solve the original, unconstrained problem (5), citing an algorithm from [25] that does not handle such constraints. This is a fundamental disconnect. The theory is developed for a different, more constrained, and harder optimization problem than the one that is actually evaluated. The paper does not address this gap, leaving the reader to question whether the theoretical guarantees actually apply to the method as it is used in practice.

2.  **Impractical Assumptions and Setup:**
    *   The incoherence assumption (10) and the corresponding constraint in the optimization problem (11) depend on a hyperparameter `α`. This parameter is derived from the properties of the unknown true tensor `W*`, making it impossible to set in practice. The paper offers no guidance on how one might choose `α`, which is a significant practical limitation.
    *   The theoretically optimal choice for the regularization parameter `λ` in Theorem 3 also depends on this unknown `α`. This undermines the claim that the choice of `λ` is independent of the unknown ranks.
    *   The analysis in Section 3.2 assumes a specific generative model `W* = ∑ W*(k)` where components are low-rank. While necessary for the analysis, the implications of this assumption on real-world data are not discussed.

3.  **Lack of Clarity and Inconsistent Notation:**
    *   The notation used to distinguish the overlapped and latent norms is extremely subtle and confusing. The overlapped S1/1-norm is denoted `||| · |||_S1/1` while the latent norm is denoted `||| · |||_S1/1` (with a dot). This is a poor notational choice that significantly hinders readability. More descriptive notation, such as `|||·|||_O` and `|||·|||_L`, would have been far clearer.
    *   The paper uses `rk` to denote the mode-k rank of the tensor `W` and then introduces `r̄k` as the rank of the k-th component `W*(k)`. The similar notation for different concepts can cause confusion.
    *   The generalization to Sp/q norms in Section 2.1 feels superfluous, as the remainder of the paper focuses exclusively on the S1/1 and S∞/∞ cases. This adds unnecessary complexity to the preliminary sections without any payoff in the main results.

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:**
    *   Figure 1 is repeatedly cited as a key motivating example for the entire paper, but it is not included in the manuscript. This is a major omission.
    *   There is a grammatical error in the introduction: "...generalize the results..." should be "...generalizes the results...".
    *   The consistency result in Theorem 1 provides a bound proportional to `sqrt(min_k nk)`, which is quite loose and depends on dimension rather than rank. The paper itself notes that the bound in Theorem 2 is tighter in most relevant regimes, which makes the contribution of Theorem 1 questionable.

### **Decision**

I recommend **rejection**.

While the core theoretical idea—linking the latent model's performance to the minimum mode-k rank—is strong and significant, the paper is marred by a critical flaw: the theoretical analysis is conducted on a constrained optimization problem (11) that does not match the unconstrained problem seemingly used in the experiments. This disconnect invalidates the empirical validation of the main theorems. Furthermore, the practical utility of the theory is questionable due to its reliance on an unknown incoherence parameter `α`. These major issues, combined with numerous problems in clarity, notation, and presentation (such as the missing Figure 1), prevent me from recommending acceptance. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution, but it requires a substantial revision to address the gap between theory and practice and to improve its overall clarity and rigor.
