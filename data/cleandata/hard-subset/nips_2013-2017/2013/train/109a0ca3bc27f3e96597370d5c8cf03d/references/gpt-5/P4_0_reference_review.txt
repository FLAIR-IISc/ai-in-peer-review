Summary and relation to prior work
The paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition and denoising via structured Schatten norms. It contrasts two paradigms: (i) the “overlapped” approach, which penalizes the sum of Schatten norms of all mode unfoldings to encourage simultaneous low-rankness across modes, and (ii) the “latent” approach, which decomposes the tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode, penalized with mode-wise Schatten-1 norms. The authors formalize a general overlapped Sp/q norm and introduce the latent Sp*/q* norm as its dual, generalizing dualities known from structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs latent group lasso). For the practically relevant case of S1/1, they show the dual is the overlapped S∞/∞ norm.

Main theoretical contributions include: a basic consistency bound for the latent approach that depends on the tensor dimensions via √(min nk); deterministic error bounds under an incoherence-type assumption and additional spectral-norm constraints that scale with the sum of latent ranks of the components and, for the aggregate error, with min mode-k rank; and high-probability bounds for Gaussian noise with a choice of λ independent of unknown ranks. The analysis explains empirical observations that the latent approach performs particularly well when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The work relates closely to prior convex tensor methods using overlapped norms [e.g., Liu et al.; Tomioka et al.], high-dimensional M-estimation with decomposable regularizers [Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.], and structured sparsity with overlapped/latent group norms [Obozinski et al.; Jenatton et al.]. Compared to [26], which analyzes the overlapped approach, the central novelty is an analysis for the latent approach with error scaling controlled by the minimal mode rank rather than averages across modes. Empirical results on synthetic data validate the predicted scaling.

Quality (technical soundness)
- The duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms is natural and consistent with existing results in structured sparsity. The specialized duality between the latent S1/1 norm and the overlapped S∞/∞ norm is standard and seems correct.
- The basic consistency result follows standard decomposability analyses: the error bound involves the dual norm of the noise (overlapped S∞/∞) and a norm compatibility constant that here scales as √(min nk). This is plausible given the operator norm of the unfolding maps and the nuclear/spectral norm duality.
- The deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds leverage an incoherence-type condition and additional spectral-norm constraints on cross-mode unfoldings of the latent components. This framework mirrors analyses for robust PCA/dirty models and appears technically consistent. The resulting bounds—sum of component errors scaling with the sum of latent ranks and overall error scaling with min mode rank—are informative and align with intuition and experiments.
- One caveat: the added spectral norm constraints (parameter α) are not part of the original latent formulation (Eq. 5/6). The theoretical bounds depend on α (and on including these constraints in the optimization), yet the experiments appear to use the unconstrained solver from [25]. This mismatch should be discussed more explicitly, including how α would be set in practice and whether similar guarantees hold without the constraints.
- Constants are not optimized and the factor F is somewhat opaque; however, the qualitative scaling conclusions are clear.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well organized: definitions, duality, main theorems, and experiments flow logically. The connection to structured sparsity is well motivated.
- Lemmas and theorems are clearly stated, though proofs are deferred to the supplement. A short proof sketch for the key inequality (latent S1/1 ≤ (min √rk) ||W||F via singleton decomposition and nuclear ≤ √rank Frobenius) would improve readability.
- Some notation is heavy but standard for tensors. Minor editorial issues remain (typos, reference formatting).
- The role and selection of α in the constrained problem (11) should be clarified in the main text.

Originality
- Extending overlapped/latent norm duality from group sparsity to tensor Schatten norms is a neat and useful generalization.
- The analysis for the latent tensor approach under noise, with bounds depending on min mode rank and comparisons to overlapped approaches, appears novel relative to prior work that focused on overlapped norms or noiseless completion.

Significance
- Understanding when and why latent decompositions outperform overlapped ones is important for practitioners working with high-order data (imaging, spatiotemporal, recommender systems).
- The results provide guidance on expected error scaling and on regularization selection independent of unknown ranks.
- The empirical validation on synthetic tensors supports the theory; additional real-data experiments would further demonstrate impact.

Pros
- Clear identification and analysis of latent vs overlapped Schatten norms; useful duality result.
- Theoretical bounds explaining empirical advantages of the latent approach, with dependence on min mode rank.
- Consistency result and Gaussian-noise high-probability bounds with explicit λ choices.
- Experiments confirm predicted scaling behavior.

Cons
- Theoretical analysis relies on additional spectral-norm constraints and an α parameter; experiments use the unconstrained solver, creating a theory–practice gap.
- No comparison to strong nonconvex baselines (ALS/HOOI), which are standard in tensor decomposition.
- Limited discussion on computational complexity and scalability beyond referencing ADMM.
- Empirical study restricted to synthetic data and K=3; no real-world datasets.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the necessity and practical selection of the spectral-norm constraints (α): are similar bounds achievable without them, or can they be enforced efficiently in practice? Report whether experiments used them.
- Include a brief proof sketch or intuition for key inequalities and the norm compatibility constants in the main text.
- Add comparisons to common nonconvex baselines and, if possible, a real-data case to illustrate practical benefits.
- Discuss per-mode regularization (e.g., different λk) and whether that can further exploit mode heterogeneity.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor typos.

Overall, the paper provides a technically sound and informative analysis that advances understanding of convex tensor decompositions, especially the latent approach. The main weakness is the gap between the constrained theoretical setting and the unconstrained practical solver; addressing this would strengthen the contribution.
