### Summary of the paper

This paper investigates two convex relaxation approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach and the "latent" approach. The overlapped approach, previously studied in the literature, regularizes a tensor `W` by the sum of the nuclear norms of its matrix unfoldings, encouraging `W` to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes. The latent approach, proposed empirically but lacking theoretical analysis, decomposes `W` into a sum of tensors, `W = sum_k W^(k)`, where each component `W^(k)` is regularized to be low-rank in its specific mode `k`.

The main contributions of this paper are:
1.  It formalizes these two approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms," defining generalized overlapped and latent Sp/q norms.
2.  It establishes a novel duality between the overlapped Sp/q norm and the latent Sp*/q* norm, drawing a clear and insightful parallel to the duality between overlapped and latent group norms in the structured sparsity literature.
3.  It provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor denoising. The key result is an error bound showing that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor (`min_k r_k`).
4.  This result is contrasted with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the *average* of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical explanation for the superiority of the latent approach when a tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes.
5.  The paper presents numerical experiments that empirically validate the derived theoretical scaling laws for both methods and confirm the practical advantage of the latent approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development, from the definition of the structured norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) to the derivation of the main error bounds (Theorems 2 and 3), is rigorous and well-executed. The analysis leverages established techniques from high-dimensional statistics and adapts them effectively to the tensor setting. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs (provided in the appendix) and carefully designed numerical experiments. The experiments specifically target the verification of the derived scaling laws, providing strong evidence for the paper's central theoretical claims. The work represents a complete and solid contribution to the theory of tensor methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It begins by clearly motivating the problem and lucidly explaining the distinction between the overlapped and latent approaches. The notation is consistent and the mathematical concepts are introduced logically. The connection drawn to the structured sparsity literature is particularly helpful for situating the work in a broader context. The experimental section is also clear, explaining the setup, the metrics used (TR and LR complexity), and how the results support the theoretical predictions. The figures are illustrative and effectively convey the main empirical findings.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the latent approach was proposed empirically in prior work [25], this paper provides its first theoretical analysis, which is a significant contribution in itself. The formalization of overlapped and latent Schatten norms and, crucially, the establishment of their duality, is a novel theoretical result that deepens the connection between tensor factorization and structured sparsity. The main finding—that the latent approach's performance depends on the minimum, rather than the average, of the mode-k ranks—is a new and important insight. This work clearly distinguishes itself from previous contributions, such as the analysis of the overlapped method in [26], by providing a compelling theoretical case for a different, and often better, model.

**Significance:** The results presented are significant and likely to influence future research and practice in tensor decomposition. The paper provides clear theoretical guidance on when to prefer the latent approach over the more established overlapped approach. The finding that the latent method can "automatically" adapt to the most low-rank mode without prior knowledge is a powerful result. This could lead practitioners to adopt the latent formulation more widely, particularly for datasets where the low-rank structure is unknown or asymmetric across modes. The conceptual bridge built between structured Schatten norms for tensors and structured sparsity norms for vectors is also a valuable contribution that may spur further theoretical developments.

**Weaknesses / Areas for Improvement:**

1.  **Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter error bound in Theorem 2 relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10), which is a common tool in related literature. However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of this assumption's implications in the context of tensors. For instance, providing intuition on what types of tensors would satisfy or violate this condition would strengthen the practical relevance of the result.

2.  **Experimental Setup vs. Theory:** The theory for the tighter bound is developed for a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes the incoherence constraints. The paper states that the experiments use algorithms from [25]. It should be clarified whether the implemented algorithm explicitly handles these spectral norm constraints. If the experiments were run on the unconstrained formulation, there is a minor disconnect between the specific model analyzed in Theorem 2 and the one evaluated empirically.

3.  **Scope of Analysis:** The paper focuses exclusively on the tensor denoising setting, where the tensor is fully observed but corrupted by noise. A primary application of tensor decomposition is tensor completion, i.e., recovering a tensor from a small subset of its entries. The paper acknowledges this as future work, but it remains a limitation. The applicability of these theoretical results to the completion setting is not guaranteed and represents an important open question.

### Summary of Arguments

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition.
*   Establishes a novel and elegant duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms, creating a strong link to the well-studied field of structured sparsity.
*   The main theoretical result—that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is significant, insightful, and provides clear practical guidance for model selection.
*   The paper is technically strong, clearly written, and presents well-designed experiments that directly validate its theoretical claims.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis for the sharpest bounds relies on an incoherence assumption that is not fully justified or discussed in the tensor context.
*   The analysis is confined to the denoising problem and does not address the tensor completion setting, which is a major application area.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It makes a significant, original, and technically sound contribution to the understanding of convex methods for tensor decomposition.
