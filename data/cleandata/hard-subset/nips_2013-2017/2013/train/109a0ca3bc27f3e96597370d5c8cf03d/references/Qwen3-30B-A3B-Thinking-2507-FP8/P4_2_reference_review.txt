

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary of Main Ideas and Relation to Previous Work

This paper introduces and analyzes two structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition: the overlapped Schatten norm and the latent Schatten norm. The authors establish a duality between these two norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. Previous work [25, 26] introduced the overlapped approach for convex tensor decomposition but did not provide theoretical justification for its limitations compared to the latent approach, which was empirically observed to outperform it for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode. The paper extends the theoretical analysis of tensor decomposition by providing rigorous bounds on the performance of the latent approach, showing that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach's error scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks.

The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, noting parallels with group lasso approaches. It builds upon the foundational work by [26] that analyzed the overlapped approach in the noiseless tensor completion setting, but extends this to a noisy denoising setting with theoretical guarantees.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness**: The paper provides rigorous theoretical analysis with multiple theorems establishing consistency and error bounds for the latent approach. The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a significant theoretical contribution that connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature.

2. **Clear Theoretical Contribution**: The paper clearly demonstrates that the latent approach's performance is governed by the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank, explaining why it outperforms the overlapped approach in certain scenarios (as shown in Figure 1). This theoretical insight is novel and well-supported.

3. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation**: The authors provide numerical experiments that confirm their theoretical predictions about the scaling behavior of mean squared error with respect to tensor dimensions and ranks. The experimental setup is well-designed to test the theoretical claims.

4. **Clear Organization**: The paper is well-structured, with a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation. The mathematical notation is consistently defined and used.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty Contextualization**: While the paper establishes a duality between the two norms and provides theoretical analysis, it does not sufficiently distinguish its contributions from [25] (which proposed the latent approach) and [26] (which analyzed the overlapped approach). The paper should more explicitly clarify how their theoretical analysis differs from and extends previous work.

2. **Practical Implementation Considerations**: The paper notes that "the ranks of the factors are not easy to compute" but doesn't provide guidance on how practitioners might estimate these ranks in real applications. The claim that "the theoretically optimal choice of regularization constant λ is independent of the ranks of the truth" is somewhat misleading since practitioners would need to estimate λ without knowing these ranks.

3. **Empirical Scope Limitations**: The experiments in Section 4 show that the latent approach's advantage is "milder than that in Figure 1" because most randomly generated tensors were "simultaneously low-rank to some degree." This suggests the paper's main contribution might be limited to specific tensor structures that are not common in practice.

4. **Technical Detail Gaps**: The paper references supplementary material for proofs, but the main text lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the technical soundness of some claims. For instance, the incoherence assumption in Theorem 2 (Equation 10) is not well-motivated for tensor decomposition applications.

## Arguments for and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- The paper establishes a theoretically sound duality between two structured Schatten norms, which is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition.
- The theoretical analysis provides a clear explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach over the overlapped approach in certain scenarios.
- The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, broadening the relevance of the work.
- The empirical results support the theoretical claims, demonstrating the predicted scaling behavior.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- The novelty contribution is somewhat incremental given the existing work on convex tensor decomposition [25, 26].
- The practical relevance is limited by the fact that the advantage of the latent approach is most pronounced only for tensors that are low-rank in a single mode, which may not be the most common scenario in applications.
- The paper does not provide guidance for practitioners on how to implement the latent approach in real-world scenarios, particularly regarding the estimation of key parameters like the rank of the tensor factors.
- The theoretical analysis is limited to the denoising setting, while many tensor decomposition applications involve missing data or tensor completion, which the paper acknowledges as future work but does not address.

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods, particularly by establishing a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms and providing rigorous error bounds for the latent approach. The theoretical insights explain the empirical observations from prior work [25] and provide a clear framework for understanding when the latent approach will outperform the overlapped approach.

However, the novelty of the work is somewhat limited by the incremental nature of the theoretical extension from previous papers [25, 26], and the practical relevance is constrained by the specific tensor structures where the latent approach provides significant advantages. The paper would benefit from more thorough contextualization of its contribution relative to prior work and clearer guidance for practitioners on implementing the latent approach.

The paper meets the quality and clarity criteria for NIPS, with a sound theoretical foundation and well-presented results. The significance of the work is moderate, as it provides important theoretical insights but does not address the most common tensor decomposition applications (e.g., tensor completion with missing data). The contribution is valuable for the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition but may have limited impact on the broader machine learning community compared to more application-focused work.
