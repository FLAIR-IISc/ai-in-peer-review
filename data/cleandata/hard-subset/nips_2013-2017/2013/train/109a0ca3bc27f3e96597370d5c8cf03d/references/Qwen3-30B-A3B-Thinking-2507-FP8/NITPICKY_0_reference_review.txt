

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper introduces a framework for structured Schatten norms in tensor decomposition, comparing the overlapped and latent approaches. The authors establish a duality between these norms and provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, showing it achieves error bounds proportional to the minimal mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. The empirical results confirm the theoretical predictions.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and well-motivated. The theoretical guarantees are rigorous, and the numerical experiments appropriately validate the theoretical claims. The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, providing a broader context for their work.

## Minor Flaws and Inconsistencies

1. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for the Schatten norms. In Equation (1), the norm is denoted as |||W|||_{S1/1}, but in Equation (7), the dual norm is written as |||X|||_{S∞/∞}. The standard notation would use a subscript with p and q (e.g., |||W|||_{S_{1,1}}), and the use of "S1/1" is confusing. This inconsistency appears throughout the paper.

2. **Definition Clarity**: In Theorem 1, the authors state "λ ≥ |||E|||_{S∞/∞}" but fail to explicitly define what the S∞/∞ norm is in the theorem statement. They define it in Equation (7), but this creates a disconnect between the theorem statement and its supporting definition.

3. **Reference Mismatch**: The paper cites "Tomioka et al. [25]" for the latent approach, but the reference [25] in the bibliography is "Tensor completion for estimating missing values in visual data" by Liu et al., not Tomioka et al. This is a critical error that undermines the credibility of the paper.

4. **Proof Reference**: The authors state "proof is presented in Appendix A" for Lemma 1, but Appendix A is not referenced in the main text. This creates confusion about where to find the proof.

5. **Technical Precision**: The statement "the mean squared error of the latent approach scales no greater than the minimum mode-k rank" (in the abstract) is imprecise. The bound scales with the minimum mode-k rank, not the MSE itself. This phrasing could mislead readers.

6. **Empirical Setup**: In the numerical experiments, the authors define TR complexity as (Σ√(1/nk))²(1/K Σ√rk)², but the reference [26] uses a different normalization. This makes the comparison less straightforward than claimed.

7. **Reference Formatting**: Several references in the bibliography have incomplete information (e.g., Agarwal et al. lists "The Annals of Statistics,, , ," with extra commas and missing page numbers).

8. **Theoretical Assumption**: The incoherence assumption in Theorem 2 (Equation 10) is strong, yet the paper doesn't discuss how to choose α in practice or how sensitive the results are to this parameter.

9. **Notation Confusion**: The paper uses "N" to denote both the total number of tensor entries (N = ∏nk) and the dimensionality of the tensor. In Equation (8), "min k nk" is used, but nk are dimensions, not ranks, creating confusion.

## Recommendation

While the paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to tensor decomposition, the numerous minor notational inconsistencies, reference errors, and imprecise statements significantly undermine its credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these technical inconsistencies to be substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the paper's theoretical interest and empirical validation.

The reference mismatch between [25] and the cited Tomioka work is particularly problematic as it suggests a lack of attention to detail. The notational inconsistencies (S1/1 vs. S_{1,1}, S∞/∞ vs. S_{∞,∞}) create confusion that could mislead readers. The imprecise phrasing in the abstract and theorem statements further diminishes the paper's technical rigor.

The paper would be significantly improved with careful attention to notational consistency, proper reference management, and precise mathematical statements. Given the stringent standards of NIPS and the importance of technical precision in theoretical machine learning, I recommend rejection at this stage.

The authors have identified an interesting theoretical gap and provided sound analysis, but the current presentation contains too many avoidable errors that would make it difficult for readers to fully trust the results. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these minor but significant issues before being reconsidered for publication.
