This paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach to convex tensor decomposition, contrasting it with the more established "overlapped" approach. The core idea of the latent approach is to model a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is constrained to be low-rank in only a single mode. This stands in contrast to the overlapped approach, which encourages the observed tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes. While the latent approach was proposed empirically in prior work [25], it lacked theoretical justification.

The main contributions of this paper are threefold. First, it formalizes the regularizers for both approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms," naming them the latent and overlapped Schatten norms. Second, it establishes a novel and elegant duality between these two norms, connecting this line of work to the broader literature on structured sparsity and latent/overlapped group lasso. Third, and most significantly, it provides a theoretical performance analysis for the latent approach. The authors prove that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor. This provides a clear theoretical explanation for why the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, whose error is known to scale with the *sum* (or average) of the mode-k ranks [26].

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Originality:** The central contribution—the theoretical framework for latent Schatten norms and the duality result—is highly novel and conceptually significant. By placing the two competing convex tensor methods into a principled framework and connecting them to the well-studied field of structured sparsity, the authors provide a much deeper understanding of the problem. This is not an incremental improvement but a new theoretical lens through which to view these methods.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Insight:** The paper's main result—that the latent approach's error scales with `min(r_k)` rather than `sum(sqrt(r_k))`—is of major importance. It demonstrates that the latent method can adaptively find the most compact representation of the tensor without being explicitly told which mode is low-rank. This provides a powerful theoretical justification for preferring the latent approach, especially for tensors that may have a sparse rank structure (i.e., low-rank in only one or a few modes), a common scenario in practice.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It clearly articulates the limitations of the existing overlapped approach, introduces the latent alternative, and systematically builds the theory to support its advantages. The comparison in Section 3.4 is particularly effective at crystallizing the key difference between the two methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** While the experiments successfully confirm the predicted theoretical scaling on synthetic data, they are not exhaustive. The performance gains shown in Figure 2 are described as "milder" than in the motivating example, and the paper would have benefited from evaluation on a wider range of real-world problems to demonstrate the practical impact of the theoretical findings more forcefully.

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is focused on the tensor denoising problem. A major application of low-rank tensor methods is tensor completion (i.e., recovering a tensor from a small subset of its entries). While the authors rightfully note this as an important direction for future work, the absence of a completion analysis limits the immediate applicability of the theoretical guarantees to one of the field's most prominent problems.

3.  **Dependence on Incoherence Assumptions:** The tighter theoretical bounds in Theorem 2 rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and additional constraints in the optimization problem. While such assumptions are standard in high-dimensional statistics and are well-justified by related literature, they represent an idealization that may not perfectly hold in all practical settings.

**Summary of pros and cons for acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** Introduces a novel and elegant theoretical framework for structured Schatten norms, establishing a key duality result.
*   **Pro:** Provides a highly significant theoretical result that explains the performance of the latent approach, showing its error scales with the minimum mode-k rank.
*   **Pro:** Creates a strong conceptual bridge between convex tensor decomposition and the broader structured sparsity literature.
*   **Con:** The empirical validation is somewhat limited and does not showcase dramatic performance gains on a wide array of problems.
*   **Con:** The theoretical analysis does not cover the important tensor completion setting.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The theoretical contributions are of very high quality and offer a significant and novel conceptual advance in our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The discovery that the latent approach's performance is governed by the minimum mode-k rank is a powerful and important finding. While the experimental section could be stronger and the analysis does not yet cover tensor completion, these are acceptable limitations. The paper's primary value lies in its bold theoretical ideas, which provide a clear and principled foundation for future work in this area. This is precisely the type of foundational and conceptually rich work that NIPS should champion.
