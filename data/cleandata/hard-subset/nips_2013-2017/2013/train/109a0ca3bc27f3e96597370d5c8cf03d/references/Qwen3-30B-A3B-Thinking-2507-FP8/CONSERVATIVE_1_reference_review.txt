

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper proposes a theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for tensor decomposition, contrasting it with the established "overlapped approach." The authors introduce structured Schatten norms, establish a duality between overlapped and latent norms, and theoretically demonstrate that the latent approach has error bounds scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank, as claimed for the overlapped approach. They support this with empirical results showing the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in specific scenarios.

## Strengths
The paper makes a technically sound contribution with the duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which is a clean theoretical advance. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-3) provides a clear explanation for the empirical observations in Figure 1. The paper is well-structured, with precise mathematical definitions and thorough theoretical development. The empirical validation in Section 4 generally aligns with the theoretical predictions, providing some support for the claims.

## Weaknesses
Despite the theoretical contribution, the paper suffers from significant limitations that undermine its significance for the NIPS community:

1. **Overstated practical significance:** The paper claims the latent approach "is preferable to the overlapped approach" but only demonstrates this in specific scenarios where the tensor is low-rank in a single mode (the "most difficult case" for the overlapped approach). The empirical results in Section 4 show only modest improvements (Figure 2), with the authors acknowledging that "the latent approach perform[s] at least as well as the overlapped approach" in many cases but not significantly better. This is far from the "superior performance" claimed in the abstract.

2. **Lack of comparison with standard methods:** The paper exclusively compares the two convex approaches (overlapped vs. latent) without benchmarking against established tensor decomposition methods like Tucker or CP decomposition, which are widely used in practice for neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, and other applications mentioned in the introduction. Without this comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the latent approach represents a meaningful advance over existing practice.

3. **No computational analysis:** The paper completely omits any discussion of computational complexity or runtime. For tensor decomposition applications, especially with large-scale data (e.g., neuroimaging with channels × timepoints × subjects), computational efficiency is critical. The latent approach may require significantly more computation than the overlapped approach, which would be a major drawback in practice.

4. **Limited empirical validation:** The experiments only test "randomly generated low-rank tensors" where the true tensor is simultaneously low-rank to some degree (Section 4). This is precisely the scenario where the overlapped approach should perform well, making the observed improvements (which are modest) less impressive. The paper doesn't demonstrate the latent approach's advantage in the more common cases where tensors are not low-rank in a single mode.

5. **No clear practical guidance:** The paper provides no practical guidance on when to use the latent approach versus the overlapped approach or other standard methods. The theoretical advantage only applies under specific structural assumptions that may not hold in real-world applications.

## Critical Evaluation

While the theoretical contribution (duality result) is interesting, it represents a modest advance in a well-studied area. The paper's main claim—that the latent approach is "preferable" for noisy tensor decomposition—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The empirical results show only marginal improvements in specific, somewhat artificial scenarios, with no demonstration of practical utility in real-world applications.

The paper's limitation to comparing two convex approaches without benchmarking against standard tensor decomposition methods is particularly problematic. The field has moved beyond merely comparing convex formulations to establish whether they solve practical problems better than existing methods. Without evidence that the latent approach outperforms standard methods in real applications, the contribution is limited.

The authors cite previous work [25, 26] that focused on noise-less tensor completion, but they don't address whether their theoretical results extend to the more common noisy tensor completion setting, which is the focus of their empirical work. The theoretical bounds (Theorem 3) assume Gaussian noise but don't establish how the latent approach performs relative to non-convex methods that are commonly used in practice.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper contains technically sound theoretical results, it fails to demonstrate that the proposed method provides meaningful practical advantages over existing approaches. The empirical validation is limited to a narrow set of scenarios that don't represent typical tensor decomposition problems, and the paper doesn't address computational efficiency—a critical factor for tensor decomposition applications. The significance of the work is overstated, and the paper would benefit from more extensive validation against standard methods and real-world datasets before being considered for publication.

The theoretical contribution is interesting but insufficient to justify publication at NIPS without stronger empirical evidence of practical impact. The field would benefit more from papers that demonstrate clear improvements over established methods on real-world problems, rather than incremental theoretical advances that don't translate to practical benefits.
