- Analyzes overlapped vs latent convex tensor decomposition and establishes a duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms.  
- Key theoretical result: latent approach MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank, explaining its empirical superiority over the overlapped method.  
- Provides consistency guarantee (Theorem 1), deterministic bound (Theorem 2), and Gaussian-noise analysis (Theorem 3).  
- Connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity, generalizing overlapped/latent group sparsity duality.  
- Rigorous, well-motivated proof techniques; Lemma 1 (duality) is a central theoretical contribution.  
- Strong empirical validation: experiments show linear MSE scaling consistent with theoretical predictions.  
- Positions the work at the intersection of tensor methods, convex optimization, and structured sparsity—relevant to NIPS readers.  
- Practical relevance: offers a theoretically grounded alternative for rank model selection in tensor problems.  
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and practical cost of the latent approach versus overlapped methods.  
- Deterministic bound depends on an incoherence assumption (eq. 10) that may be restrictive and is not well-justified for real data.  
- Lacks comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., ALS, HOOI), omitting important baselines.  
- Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity (choice of regularization λ) in practical settings.  
- Does not establish minimax optimality or provide lower bounds to prove the latent approach is theoretically optimal.  
- Suggests expanding the proof sketch for Lemma 1 in the supplement for accessibility.  
- Recommends discussing whether the incoherence assumption is realistic for domains like neuroimaging or collaborative filtering.  
- Advises adding real-world dataset experiments to demonstrate practical utility beyond synthetic data.  
- Suggests clearer distinction from related work, especially [25] (empirical latent approach) and [26] (noiseless tensor completion).  
- Overall recommendation: Accept — strong theoretical contribution with solid experiments despite noted limitations.
