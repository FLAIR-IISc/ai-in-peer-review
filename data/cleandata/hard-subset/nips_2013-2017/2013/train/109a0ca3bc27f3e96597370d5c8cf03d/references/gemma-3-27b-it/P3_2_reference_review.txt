## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical and empirical contribution to the growing field of convex tensor decomposition. The authors rigorously analyze the latent Schatten norm approach, demonstrating its advantages over the overlapped approach, particularly when dealing with tensors low-rank in only one mode. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, clearly written, and offers insightful connections to related work in structured sparsity. While the empirical results are somewhat limited in scope, they effectively support the theoretical findings. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**1. Summary & Related Work:**

The paper tackles the problem of decomposing tensors into low-rank components, a crucial task in various data analysis applications. Traditional methods rely on non-convex optimization, lacking strong theoretical guarantees. Recent convex approaches utilize Schatten norm regularization, penalizing the unfolded matrices to encourage low-rank solutions.  This paper focuses on two variants: the "overlapped" approach (penalizing all modes simultaneously) and the “latent” approach (decomposing into a mixture of tensors low-rank in specific modes). The authors demonstrate a duality between the respective norms and, most importantly, provide theoretical justification for the superior performance of the latent approach in scenarios where the tensor exhibits low-rank structure in only a single mode.

The paper is well-grounded in the existing literature. It correctly cites key works in tensor decomposition (De Lathauwer et al., Kolda & Bader), convex optimization for low-rank problems (Candes & Recht, Recht et al.), and structured sparsity (Jenatton et al., Obozinski et al.).  It effectively positions its contribution within this landscape, building on prior work and offering a novel perspective.

**2. Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Analysis:** The core strength of this paper lies in its rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent Schatten norm approach.  The duality result (Lemma 1) is a significant contribution, linking tensor decomposition to broader results in structured sparsity. The consistency and deterministic bounds (Theorems 1 & 2) provide valuable insights into the performance of the method and explain the empirical observations. The theoretical bounds that scale with the *minimum* rank are particularly noteworthy.
* **Clear Explanation of the Latent Approach's Advantage:** The authors successfully explain *why* the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach, linking it to the scaling of the error bounds with the minimum mode rank. This is a critical contribution, providing a principled understanding beyond just empirical observation.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is clear, and the presentation is logical. The connection between different concepts (e.g., overlapped/latent norms, duality, optimization) is well-articulated.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments, while relatively simple, provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical findings. The comparison with the overlapped approach demonstrates the benefits of the latent approach, especially in scenarios aligned with the theory.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors thoughtfully connect their work to related research in convex optimization, sparse recovery, and tensor decomposition.

**3. Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small tensors and a limited range of rank configurations. While they support the theoretical claims, more extensive experiments on larger datasets and more diverse tensor structures would strengthen the conclusions.  Specifically, testing on real-world datasets would be valuable.
* **Assumptions for Deterministic Bound:** The assumptions required for the tighter deterministic bound (Theorem 2), especially the incoherence assumption (equation (10)), may be restrictive in practice.  Discussing the sensitivity of the results to this assumption would be beneficial.
* **Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper focuses on theoretical performance bounds but lacks a discussion of the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. Analyzing the scaling of the optimization algorithm's runtime would be a valuable addition.
* **Missing Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares the latent and overlapped approaches, it doesn't compare its results to more recent tensor decomposition techniques (e.g., those employing alternating least squares with more sophisticated stopping criteria).



**4. Arguments For and Against Acceptance:**

**For Acceptance:**

*  Strong theoretical justification for the latent approach.
*  Clear explanation of why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach.
*  Well-written and organized presentation.
*  Empirical results supporting the theoretical claims.
*  Significant contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition.

**Against Acceptance:**

*  Limited scope of empirical validation.
*  Restrictive assumptions for the tighter theoretical bound.
*  Lack of discussion of computational complexity.
*  Missing comparison with more recent tensor decomposition methods.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you discuss the computational complexity of solving the optimization problem associated with the latent approach (11), and compare it to the overlapped approach?
*  The incoherence assumption (equation (10)) seems potentially strong.  How sensitive are your theoretical results to violations of this assumption?
*  Do you have plans to evaluate the latent approach on real-world datasets? If so, could you briefly describe the datasets and expected results?
*  How does the latent approach scale to higher-order tensors (K > 3)? Are there any theoretical or practical limitations?

**6. Final Recommendation:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper with minor revisions. Specifically, I encourage the authors to address the weaknesses regarding the limited empirical scope and the assumptions for the deterministic bound. Adding a discussion of computational complexity and comparison with more recent methods would also enhance the paper's impact. Overall, this is a significant and well-executed contribution to the field of tensor decomposition, offering a valuable theoretical foundation for the latent approach and providing insights into its advantages over existing methods.
