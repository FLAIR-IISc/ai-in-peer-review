Summary
This paper studies convex tensor decomposition through a family of structured Schatten norms, unifying and extending two lines: (i) overlapped norms that penalize all mode-unfoldings simultaneously (Schatten-1 over multiple unfoldings), and (ii) latent norms that decompose the tensor into a sum of mode-specific low-rank components. The core technical contribution is a clean duality: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm (Lemma 1), generalizing well-known dualities in structured sparsity. Building on this, the authors provide new recovery/denoising guarantees for the latent approach. A key inequality (Lemma 2) shows the latent Schatten-1 norm is controlled by the minimal mode-k rank, leading to error bounds that scale with min_k r_k rather than averages over all modes as in prior analyses of the overlapped approach. Theorems 1–3 give consistency, deterministic bounds under incoherence-style constraints, and Gaussian-noise bounds with explicit choices of λ. Experiments on synthetic denoising validate the predicted scaling and show advantages of the latent approach even when the truth is simultaneously low-rank.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: The latent vs overlapped norm duality for tensors is neat and broadly useful. It connects tensor norms to the “dirty model” and overlapped/latent group sparsity in a principled way.
- Theoretical insight: The min-rank scaling (via Lemma 2 and Theorems 2–3) crisply explains when latent models beat overlapped ones—precisely when low-rankness is concentrated in one mode. This aligns with empirical observations and provides clear guidance.
- Practical takeaway: The suggested λ choice depends only on dimensions and noise level, not unknown ranks; empirically, optimal λ appears relatively insensitive to rank.
- Empirical validation: Synthetic denoising experiments match the derived scaling laws and show consistent gains, not only in adversarial cases.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions/tuning: The stronger deterministic/Gaussian results introduce additional spectral norm constraints (α) across components (Eq. 11). How to set/estimate α in practice is unclear; experiments do not demonstrate sensitivity to α or its necessity.
- Scope of experiments: Only denoising with complete observations and relatively small sizes; no tensor completion, no real data, and limited comparisons beyond overlapped Schatten-1. This limits practical impact.
- Tightness/optimality: While improved scaling is shown, the results do not include matching lower bounds for the latent approach (only related lower bounds for overlapped are cited). The factor F in Theorem 3 is somewhat opaque, and rates may not be minimax-tight.
- Clarity in notation: The paper uses r_k for mode-ranks of W* and r̄_k for ranks of latent components; this is easy to confuse. The singleton decomposition trick is clever but could be emphasized earlier to avoid confusion.
- Algorithmic discussion: The optimization relies on prior ADMM solvers; scalability and per-iteration costs versus overlapped norms are not analyzed.

Quality
Technically sound overall. The duality and inequalities are plausible and consistent with structured sparsity theory. The proof sketches are deferred, but the statements are clear, and the empirical section supports the theoretical scaling. The reliance on α-constrained formulations raises questions about practical estimation and whether comparable rates can be obtained without explicit incoherence constraints. More discussion on conditions (e.g., decomposability/RSC) would strengthen the results.

Clarity
Well-written and organized, with a helpful background on tensor unfoldings and ranks. Some notation could be streamlined (r_k vs r̄_k). Clarify the conditions under which Lemma 2 is tight, and better motivate the α-constraints operationally. The factors in λ and F could be interpreted more intuitively.

Originality
High. The latent/overlapped Sp/q duality for tensors is novel and conceptually unifying. The min-rank-driven error bounds for latent tensor regularization, leveraging the duality and structured sparsity techniques, provide a new angle relative to prior overlapped analyses.

Significance
High. Tensor methods are widely used, and understanding when latent mode-specific low-rank models outperform overlapped penalization is important. The results provide actionable guidance for method choice and regularization. The work should stimulate further developments (e.g., completion, real-data studies, algorithms exploiting the duality).

Questions and suggestions
- Practical α: Can you provide a data-driven heuristic or upper bound for α and show empirical robustness to mis-specification?
- Beyond denoising: Any insight on extending the analysis to completion with missing entries (e.g., restricted isometry/spikiness assumptions akin to “Square Deal”)? Even a brief discussion would be valuable.
- Competing baselines: Add comparisons to Tucker/CP with rank selection (even if nonconvex) to contextualize performance.
- Tightness: Can you comment on whether min_k r_k dependence is optimal for latent norms under your noise model, or provide examples indicating near-tightness?
- Notation: Unify/clarify r_k vs r̄_k earlier; restate the singleton decomposition argument immediately after Theorem 2 to improve readability.

Pros for acceptance
- Introduces a compelling, general duality and cleanly connects tensor norms with structured sparsity.
- Provides new, interpretable bounds that explain empirical behavior and offer practical guidance.
- Empirical results align with theory and suggest latent regularization is broadly preferable.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental scope is narrow; practical deployment details (α, σ) are under-discussed.
- Some results depend on additional constraints that may be hard to tune in practice.
- No real-data demonstration or tensor completion experiments.

Recommendation
Accept (clear accept). The paper advances the field with a novel conceptual framework and meaningful theory that will influence how convex tensor methods are designed and analyzed. Minor revisions to address clarity and practical tuning would strengthen the final version.

Confidence
High.
