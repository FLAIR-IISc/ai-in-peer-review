### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," originally proposed in [25]. This approach models a tensor as a sum of K components, where each component is regularized to be low-rank along a specific mode using the nuclear norm. The main contribution of this work is a rigorous theoretical analysis of this method in a noisy setting. The authors establish an error bound for the latent approach that scales with the minimum of the mode-k ranks of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts with the existing "overlapped" approach, whose error scales with the average of the mode-k ranks. The authors formalize this by defining "overlapped" and "latent" structured Schatten norms and proving a duality between them, which connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments on synthetic data that confirm the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The primary strength of this paper is its detailed and sound theoretical analysis. The derivation of the denoising performance bound (Theorem 2 and 3) that depends on the *minimum* mode-k rank provides a solid theoretical foundation for why the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, especially for tensors that are low-rank along only one or a few modes. This formalizes an intuition and an empirical observation from prior work.

2.  **Novel Conceptual Framework:** The introduction of the generalized structured Schatten norms (overlapped and latent Sp/q-norms) and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is an elegant and valuable contribution. It provides a unifying perspective that connects these two tensor decomposition methods and links them to established concepts in structured sparsity, such as the overlapped and latent group lasso. This helps situate the work within a broader context.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, contrasting the two convex approaches and highlighting the gap in the theoretical understanding of the latent method. The theoretical development is presented in a clear, step-by-step manner.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Method:** The paper's core contribution is the analysis of an existing method. The latent approach itself was proposed and empirically explored in Tomioka et al. [25]. While the analysis is novel and important, the paper should be more explicit that it is providing a theoretical justification for a known algorithm rather than proposing a new one.

2.  **Restrictive Assumptions and Unexplored Implications:** The tighter theoretical bounds in Theorem 2 and 3 rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and a correspondingly constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11). The practical implications of this assumption are not discussed. It is unclear how restrictive the constraint `||W(l)(k)||_S∞ ≤ α` is for real-world tensors, or how one would choose the hyperparameter `α` in practice. The analysis hinges on this constraint, but its effect on the solution quality or its validity on real data is not evaluated, which weakens the practical applicability of the theoretical result.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation, while effective at confirming the theoretical scaling laws, is narrow in scope and falls short of demonstrating the practical superiority of the method.
    *   **Lack of Established Baselines:** The experiments compare the latent approach only against the overlapped convex approach. The introduction rightfully criticizes conventional non-convex methods (e.g., ALS for PARAFAC/CANDECOMP, HOSVD for Tucker decomposition) for their theoretical shortcomings, but these remain the most widely used and established baselines in practice. A convincing argument for a new method requires demonstrating its superiority over these standards. Without such a comparison, the claim that the latent approach is "preferable" is not fully substantiated. It is possible that for a given noise level, a well-tuned ALS could still outperform both convex methods.
    *   **Synthetic Data Only:** The evaluation is confined to randomly generated synthetic data. While useful for verifying theory, it does not demonstrate the method's effectiveness on real-world problems, such as the neuroimaging application mentioned in the introduction. The performance on real data, which may not perfectly align with the paper's modeling assumptions, is a crucial test of significance.
    *   **Underwhelming Performance Gains:** As the authors note, the performance improvements in the main experiment (Figure 2) are "milder" than in the motivating example (Figure 1). This suggests that the significant advantage of the latent approach may be confined to specific, and possibly niche, cases where a tensor has a very low rank in one mode but is nearly full-rank in others. The evidence for its general-purpose utility over the simpler overlapped approach is not compelling.

### Overall Assessment and Recommendation

This paper provides a valuable and technically sound theoretical analysis of the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition. The error bounds derived are significant, and the conceptual framework of structured Schatten norms is a nice contribution.

However, the work's overall impact is limited by the lack of methodological novelty and, more critically, by an insufficient empirical evaluation. The claims of the method's superiority are based on a comparison to a single, closely related convex baseline, while ignoring the well-established non-convex methods that are standard practice. Furthermore, the reliance on an unevaluated incoherence assumption for the main theoretical results, combined with experiments only on synthetic data, leaves the practical significance of the approach in question.

While the theoretical contribution is solid, a NIPS paper should ideally demonstrate a more substantial advance in the state of the art. In its current form, the paper feels more like a strong theoretical note on an existing method rather than a complete work that convincingly establishes a new, superior approach for practical tensor decomposition.

Therefore, I am hesitant to recommend acceptance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive experimental section that includes comparisons to standard non-convex baselines on both synthetic and real-world datasets, and a discussion of the practical implications of the incoherence assumption.

**Recommendation:** Reject
