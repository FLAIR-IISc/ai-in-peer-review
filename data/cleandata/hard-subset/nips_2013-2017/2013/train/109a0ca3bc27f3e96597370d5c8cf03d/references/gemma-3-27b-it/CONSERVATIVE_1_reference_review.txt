This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution lies in theoretically analyzing the latent approach, demonstrating a performance bound scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank, and establishing a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms. The authors also provide empirical validation supporting their theoretical findings.

While the topic of convex tensor decomposition is relevant and the connection to structured sparsity is well-motivated, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing as a NIPS submission. My primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution and the reliance on relatively standard techniques without significant novelty. The theoretical results, while correct, feel largely like an application of existing tools (duality results from structured sparsity, consistency analysis) to a specific tensor decomposition setting. The novelty of applying these tools to the *latent* approach is not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at NIPS. 

The empirical validation, while present, is not particularly strong. The figures demonstrate the predicted scaling, but the improvement over the overlapped approach seems modest, and the experimental setup feels somewhat limited. The claim of outperforming the overlapped approach “even when the unknown tensor is simultaneously low-rank” is not overwhelmingly supported by the results presented. The use of randomly generated low-rank tensors, while common, lacks the grounding in a real-world application that would elevate the work. Furthermore, the comparison to existing baselines beyond the “overlapped” method is absent. How does this latent approach compare to, say, Tucker decomposition with cross-validation for rank selection, or other established tensor decomposition techniques?

The paper’s clarity is acceptable, but could be improved. The notation is dense, and the motivation for some of the mathematical manipulations isn’t always clear. A more intuitive explanation of the duality result, and its practical implications, would be beneficial. The connection to the “group lasso” literature, while present, feels tacked on and doesn't substantially enhance the core message.

**Strengths:**

*   The theoretical analysis provides a formal justification for the empirically observed benefits of the latent approach.
*   The identification of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a mathematically interesting result.
*   The empirical results, while limited, support the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental contribution: The work largely applies existing techniques to a specific tensor decomposition setting.
*   Limited empirical evaluation: The experimental setup is simple, lacks comparison to strong baselines, and the performance improvements are modest.
*   Lack of real-world application: The experiments rely on synthetic data, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the approach.
*   Not particularly novel: The core ideas build directly on recent work in structured sparsity and convex optimization.
*   The theoretical results, while correct, do not represent a significant breakthrough.



**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Provides a theoretical foundation for a recently proposed tensor decomposition method.
*   Identifies a potentially useful duality between different Schatten norm regularizations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The contribution is incremental and lacks significant novelty.
*   The empirical evaluation is limited and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods.
*   The paper doesn’t address a particularly pressing problem or offer a transformative advance in the field.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. 

This paper is a solid, technically sound piece of work, but it falls short of the originality and significance required for acceptance at NIPS. It feels more appropriate for a more specialized conference or journal focused on tensor decomposition or convex optimization. I encourage the authors to consider expanding the empirical evaluation, comparing their method to a wider range of baselines, and exploring applications to real-world datasets to strengthen their claims.
