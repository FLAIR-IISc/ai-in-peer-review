## Peer Review: Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization

**Summary:**

This paper investigates convex approaches to tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the “overlapped” and “latent” Schatten norm regularization techniques. The authors provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating why the latent approach, which decomposes a tensor into a mixture of low-rank components along individual modes, outperforms the overlapped approach (penalizing low rank across all modes simultaneously), particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. They establish a consistency result for the latent approach and show its denoising performance scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank, a key improvement over the overlapped approach. The theoretical findings are supported by empirical validation.  The paper builds upon prior work in tensor decomposition [14, 18], convex relaxation [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] and structured sparsity [1, 2, 13, 17, 21]. A key contribution is demonstrating a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis, proving consistency and establishing bounds on the denoising performance of the latent approach. The scaling result regarding the minimum mode-k rank is a particularly significant theoretical insight.
* **Clear Explanation of the Latent Approach’s Advantage:** The paper effectively explains *why* the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach, linking it directly to the scaling of the error bounds with the minimum rank.
* **Novel Duality Result:**  The identification of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable theoretical contribution, connecting tensor decomposition to a broader class of structured sparsity problems.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions and demonstrate the superior performance of the latent approach in many scenarios, including cases where the overlapped approach should theoretically excel.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized, making it relatively easy to follow the theoretical developments and experimental setup.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:**  The paper carefully positions its contributions within the existing literature on tensor decomposition and convex optimization, citing relevant works appropriately.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The numerical experiments, while supportive, are somewhat limited.  Exploring a wider range of tensor sizes, rank configurations, and noise levels would further strengthen the empirical validation. It would also be valuable to compare against other tensor decomposition methods (e.g., Tucker decomposition with cross-validation for rank selection).
* **Assumptions in Theoretical Analysis:**  The deterministic bounds in Theorem 2 rely on an "incoherence assumption" (Equation 10) which may not always hold in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of the practical implications and robustness of the results under violations of this assumption would be beneficial.
* **Complexity of Theorem 3:** Theorem 3 is fairly complex and the scaling of the bound involves multiple factors. A more intuitive explanation of the practical implications of this bound for different scenarios would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost associated with implementing the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach.  While theoretically superior, a significantly higher computational burden could limit its practical applicability.



**Quality:**  The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work with a strong theoretical justification and supporting empirical evidence. The authors are careful in their analysis and acknowledge potential limitations.

**Clarity:** The paper is generally clearly written and well-organized. However, some sections (particularly those presenting the theoretical bounds) could benefit from further simplification and more intuitive explanations.

**Originality:**  The combination of theoretical analysis and empirical validation of the latent approach, along with the demonstration of the duality between the norms, constitutes a novel contribution to the field. The connections drawn to structured sparsity literature are also insightful.

**Significance:**  The results are significant because they provide a theoretical justification for the superior performance of the latent approach, leading to more effective tensor decomposition algorithms. This has the potential to impact various applications of tensor decomposition in fields such as neuroimaging, data mining, and machine learning.




**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical foundation and insightful analysis.
* Clear explanation of the advantages of the latent approach.
* Novel duality result connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity.
* Empirical validation supporting the theoretical findings.
* Addresses a challenging problem in tensor decomposition with demonstrable improvement over existing methods.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Limited scope of numerical experiments.
* Reliance on a potentially restrictive incoherence assumption in the deterministic bound.
* Lack of discussion of computational cost.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**

I recommend accepting this paper for NIPS. The theoretical contributions are significant and the empirical results are supportive. Addressing the weaknesses mentioned above—particularly expanding the numerical experiments and providing a more intuitive explanation of the theoretical bounds—would further strengthen the paper.  The paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of tensor decomposition and is likely to be of interest to a broad audience within the NIPS community.
