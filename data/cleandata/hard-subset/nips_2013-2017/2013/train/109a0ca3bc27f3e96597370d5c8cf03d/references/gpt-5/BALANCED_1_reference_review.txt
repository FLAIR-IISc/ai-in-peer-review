Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. Two families of norms are considered: (i) the “overlapped” Sp/q norm, which penalizes all mode unfoldings simultaneously (sum or ℓq aggregation of Schatten-p norms of each unfolding); and (ii) the “latent” Sp/q norm, which models the tensor as a sum of K components, each low-rank in a specific mode, with an infimal convolution across components. The authors establish a duality between these two norm families (overlapped Sp/q is dual to latent Sp*/q*), generalizing known dualities in structured sparsity/group lasso with overlaps.

The main technical contribution is a statistical analysis of the latent Schatten-1 approach for denoising (and, by extension, decomposition). They prove: (a) a consistency bound depending on the overlapped spectral-norm of the noise; (b) a deterministic error bound under an incoherence-like assumption that controls cross-mode spectral norms of the latent components; and (c) high-probability bounds for Gaussian noise with a recommended regularization λ independent of unknown ranks. A key message is that the MSE of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the ground-truth tensor, contrasting with prior bounds for the overlapped approach [Tomioka et al., NIPS 2011/2013], which scale with the average (square root) of mode ranks. Synthetic experiments confirm linear scaling with appropriately defined complexity measures and show latent generally outperforms overlapped even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across modes.

This work connects tensor nuclear norm approaches [e.g., Liu et al.; Gandy et al.; Signoretto et al.] with structured sparsity (dirty models and overlapped/latent group lasso) [Obozinski et al.; Jenatton et al.; Agarwal et al.; Negahban et al.], and discusses related lower bounds for tensor recovery [Mu et al.].

Quality:
- The theory is technically sound and consistent with established matrix/tensor results. The duality lemma is natural and appears correct; the key inequalities relating norms to Frobenius norm are standard extensions. The deterministic and Gaussian bounds are plausible and carefully state assumptions, with recommendations for λ supported by concentration.
- One caveat is the added spectral-norm constraints (parameter α) used in Theorem 2/3. It is not clear whether these constraints are enforced in practice in the experiments, how α should be set, and how sensitive results are to α. This creates a gap between theory and practice that should be addressed or at least discussed more explicitly.
- Proof details are deferred to the supplement; from the main text, the arguments are clear.

Clarity:
- Generally well written and organized. The connection to structured sparsity is helpful. However, notation could be improved to avoid ambiguity: both “overlapped S1/1” (sum of nuclear norms) and “latent S1/1” (infimal convolution of nuclear norms) use the same symbol—please disambiguate (e.g., S1/1^ov vs S1/1^lat).
- The experimental section is clear but referential to figures not included here (Figure 1). Some references in the bibliography lack complete citation details.

Originality:
- The duality between overlapped and latent tensor Schatten norms (general Sp/q) and the first generalization-bound analysis for the latent tensor nuclear norm appear novel and timely. The insight that latent scales with min mode rank is new in this context and helps explain empirical behavior observed in prior work.

Significance:
- The results advance understanding of convex tensor models and will likely influence which convex surrogate to use in practice. The theoretical message is crisp (min-rank dependence) and supported empirically. The latent formulation is an important alternative to the widely used overlapped nuclear norms, particularly when low-rank structure is concentrated in one mode.

Strengths:
- Clear identification and formalization of latent vs overlapped tensor norms and their duality.
- New error bounds for latent approach with interpretable min-mode-rank scaling.
- Guidance on λ that does not depend on unknown ranks.
- Experiments that match the predicted scaling and compare against overlapped baselines.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Assumption and enforcement of α: The deterministic and Gaussian results rely on constraints ∥W^(l)_(k)∥_S∞ ≤ α for l ≠ k. Please clarify:
  - Whether these constraints are used in the solver/experiments.
  - How α should be chosen in practice (data-driven estimates? dependence on σ/K?).
  - Sensitivity of the bounds to α and whether similar results can be obtained without explicit constraints (e.g., via decomposability arguments).
- Practicality and scalability: Include computational complexity, convergence behavior, and scalability to larger tensors. How does ADMM scale with K, dimensions, and target ranks? Any wall-clock comparisons?
- Real-data evaluations: A real-world dataset (e.g., neuroimaging or video) would strengthen the case beyond synthetic Gaussian noise.
- Comparison breadth: Consider adding comparisons to non-convex CP/Tucker methods with rank selection, to show advantages/limitations beyond convex baselines.
- Notation and exposition: Disambiguate norms, fix minor typos, and provide complete citations.
- Tensor completion: Since this is a key application area, adding even preliminary results or a discussion of how the analysis might extend to missing data would increase impact.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and clean theoretical contribution (min-mode-rank scaling for latent tensor nuclear norm).
- Conceptual clarity via duality; connects tensor norms to structured sparsity literature.
- Empirical results support the theory.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theory–practice gap due to α-constraints not clearly handled in experiments.
- Limited empirical scope (synthetic only) and lack of computational analysis.
- Notational ambiguity and some missing citation details.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The core theory is solid and impactful for the NeurIPS community; addressing the α issue and strengthening empirical/implementation details would make the paper even stronger.
