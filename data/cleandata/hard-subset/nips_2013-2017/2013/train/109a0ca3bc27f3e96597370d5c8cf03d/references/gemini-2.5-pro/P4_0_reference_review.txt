This paper investigates a convex optimization approach for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of components, each being low-rank in a specific mode. This work contrasts the latent approach with the more established "overlapped approach," where a single tensor is regularized to be simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings. The primary contributions of this paper are threefold. First, it introduces a formal framework of "structured Schatten norms" (overlapped and latent) that unifies these two approaches and connects them to the literature on structured sparsity. A key theoretical result within this framework is a novel duality between the overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm. Second, the paper provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The authors derive error bounds showing that the mean squared error (MSE) scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This is a significant finding, as previous analyses of the overlapped approach [26] showed an error dependency on the average of the mode-k ranks. Third, the paper presents numerical experiments that empirically validate the derived theoretical scaling behavior and demonstrate that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, particularly when the rank structure of the tensor is imbalanced across modes.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The paper makes a significant and original contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition. While the latent approach was previously proposed in [25], its theoretical properties were not understood. This paper provides the first performance guarantees, establishing that its error scales with `min_k rk`. This result is highly significant because it provides a clear theoretical justification for why the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach in certain, common scenarios (e.g., when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode). This insight is crucial for practitioners choosing a decomposition method and for researchers building upon these models. The generalization of structured sparsity norms to structured Schatten norms for tensors is also a novel and elegant conceptual contribution.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The technical quality of the paper is high. The derivation of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a key technical tool that is interesting in its own right and could be useful for future analyses. The main theoretical results (Theorems 1-3) are derived rigorously, building upon techniques from high-dimensional statistics [1]. The proofs provided in the appendix appear sound. The analysis clearly delineates the dependencies of the error bounds on the noise level, tensor dimensions, and, most importantly, the rank structure of the underlying tensor.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem by first introducing the two competing convex approaches and highlighting the empirical shortcomings of the overlapped method with Figure 1. The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" concepts is maintained clearly throughout the paper. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and their implications are explained well, especially in the direct comparison between the error bounds for the two approaches (Section 3.4). The experimental section is also clear, with the defined complexity measures directly linking the empirical results back to the theoretical predictions.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Incoherence Assumption:** The main deterministic error bound (Theorem 2), which establishes the `min_k rk` dependency, relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that explicitly includes this constraint. While the authors correctly note that such assumptions are common in related literature [1, 11], the practical implications and restrictiveness of this assumption for tensors are not discussed in detail. It would strengthen the paper to provide more intuition on when this assumption might hold or fail in practice, and to comment on the potential difficulty of solving the constrained version of the problem compared to the unconstrained one.

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is focused entirely on the tensor denoising problem, where the tensor is fully observed but corrupted by noise. A major application area for tensor decomposition is tensor completion, where many entries are missing. While the comparison to the analysis in [26] is fair (as it also focused on denoising), the lack of analysis for the completion setting is a limitation. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but the current results may not directly translate to the completion problem, where the sampling operator introduces additional complexity.

3.  **Experimental Complexity Metric:** In the numerical results, the "Latent rank (LR) complexity" used for validation is determined by running the algorithm on the ground truth tensor to find an optimal decomposition that minimizes the theoretical bound. While this procedure effectively demonstrates that a decomposition exists for which the bound holds, it feels somewhat circular. The key theoretical message is that the error is bounded by a term proportional to `min_k rk` (since a singleton decomposition is always a valid choice). It might have been more direct and impactful to plot the MSE directly against `min_k rk`, which would more simply test the primary claim of inequality (15).

**Summary of Arguments:**

*   **Pro-acceptance:**
    *   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.
    *   The key result—that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is a significant and impactful finding that advances the field.
    *   The paper introduces a novel and elegant framework of structured Schatten norms and their duality.
    *   The work is technically sound, clearly written, and empirically validated.

*   **Con-acceptance:**
    *   The main theoretical result relies on an incoherence assumption whose practical implications are not fully explored.
    *   The analysis is limited to the denoising setting, leaving the more general tensor completion problem as future work.

Overall, the strengths of this paper substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The theoretical contributions are novel, significant, and provide fundamental insights into the behavior of convex tensor decomposition methods. This work offers clear guidance on model selection and is likely to be highly influential for both theorists and practitioners in the field.
