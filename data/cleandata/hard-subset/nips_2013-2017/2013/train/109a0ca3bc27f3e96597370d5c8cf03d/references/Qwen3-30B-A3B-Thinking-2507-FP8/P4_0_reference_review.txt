

Summary: This paper introduces a theoretical framework for structured Schatten norms in tensor decomposition, comparing two convex approaches: the "overlapped approach" (penalizing all tensor modes to be jointly low-rank) and the "latent approach" (decomposing a tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors establish a duality between the overlapped Schatten norm and latent Schatten norm, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. They prove that the latent approach's mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, while the overlapped approach depends on the average mode-k rank. The paper also provides empirical validation showing the latent approach generally outperforms the overlapped approach.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical contribution: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-3) showing the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank, explaining its empirical superiority over the overlapped approach. This fills a significant gap as no theoretical analysis existed for the latent approach prior to this work.

2. Duality result: The authors establish a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which generalizes previous results in structured sparsity literature. This connection to broader theoretical frameworks strengthens the paper's contribution.

3. Empirical validation: Section 4 provides compelling numerical evidence confirming the theoretical predictions, with MSE scaling linearly with the LR complexity as predicted by Theorem 3.

4. Careful analysis: The authors properly address both strengths and weaknesses of their approach, particularly noting that the latent approach performs well even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank, which is favorable for the overlapped approach.

Weaknesses:
1. Computational complexity: The paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach, despite mentioning ADMM for optimization.

2. Experimental scope: The experiments focus on specific tensor sizes (50×50×20 and 80×80×40) and noise levels. More diverse tensor structures (e.g., highly asymmetric tensors) would strengthen the empirical claims.

3. Assumptions: The deterministic bound (Theorem 2) requires an incoherence assumption (Equation 10), which might limit applicability in some real-world scenarios.

Quality: The paper is technically sound with rigorous theoretical analysis supported by empirical results. The proofs are well-structured and referenced to supplementary materials. The claims are carefully supported by both theoretical analysis and experiments.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written with well-organized sections and appropriate mathematical notation. The relationship between the overlapped and latent approaches is well-explained, and the paper provides sufficient background to understand the context of the problem.

Originality: The paper makes a significant contribution by establishing the duality between the two types of norms and providing the first theoretical analysis of the latent approach. This work extends previous studies on convex tensor decomposition (e.g., [25, 26]) by offering a rigorous theoretical foundation for the empirical success of the latent approach.

Significance: The results provide a theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach, which has practical implications for tensor decomposition in applications like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. The findings advance the state of the art in convex tensor decomposition by demonstrating that the latent approach can achieve better performance with a more favorable theoretical scaling.

Arguments for acceptance: The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution that fills an important gap in the literature. The duality result generalizes previous work, and the theoretical analysis is rigorously supported by empirical evidence. The findings have direct practical implications for tensor decomposition practitioners.

Arguments against acceptance: The computational complexity discussion is insufficient, and the experimental scope could be broader. However, these are minor weaknesses that don't significantly detract from the paper's strong theoretical contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition with sound analysis, clear exposition, and empirical validation. The duality result and theoretical guarantees for the latent approach represent a significant advancement beyond previous work. The paper is well-suited for NIPS as it provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for an important practical problem in machine learning.
