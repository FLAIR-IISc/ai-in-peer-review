### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, a method that models a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. The authors formalize the regularizers used in this and the related "overlapped approach" as "latent Schatten norms" and "overlapped Schatten norms," respectively. The core theoretical contributions are: (1) establishing a novel duality between these two classes of norms, which generalizes similar results from the structured sparsity literature, and (2) leveraging this duality to derive a performance bound for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The key finding is that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This result provides a strong theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach, particularly when a tensor is highly anisotropic in its rank structure (i.e., low-rank in only one mode). The paper supports its theoretical claims with numerical experiments that confirm the predicted error scaling.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound and builds upon established techniques from high-dimensional statistics and convex optimization.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical analysis is rigorous. The main result in Theorem 3, showing that the error bound for the latent approach scales as `O(min_k r_k)`, is a significant and well-supported claim. The derivation relies on a novel duality result (Lemma 1), which is itself an interesting contribution.
    *   The experimental evaluation is well-designed and directly validates the theoretical predictions. Figure 2 compellingly shows the linear relationship between the MSE and the proposed complexity measures (LR and TR complexities), confirming the different scaling behaviors of the two methods.
    *   The authors are careful in their analysis, clearly stating the incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) required for their tighter bound, which is a standard practice in this line of research.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The analysis is restricted to the tensor denoising setting with i.i.d. Gaussian noise. While this is a standard and important setting, the paper does not address the tensor completion problem (recovering a tensor from a small subset of its entries), which is arguably a more common application. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it does limit the immediate applicability of the current theoretical results.
    *   The practical implications of the additional spectral norm constraints in the proposed optimization problem (Eq. 11) are not fully discussed. While necessary for the proof, it would be useful to understand their effect on the solution and the computational complexity of the solver.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction provides excellent motivation, clearly distinguishing between the overlapped and latent approaches and stating the paper's goals.
    *   Section 2 gives precise mathematical definitions of the structured Schatten norms, and the duality result is stated cleanly. The connection to overlapped/latent group lasso is insightful and helps place the work in a broader context.
    *   The main theoretical results are stated in a clear, progressive manner, moving from a general consistency result (Theorem 1) to a tighter deterministic bound (Theorem 2) and finally to the specific case of Gaussian noise (Theorem 3).
    *   The experimental section is easy to follow, and the figures are effective at communicating the main results. Providing a link to the code is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

**Originality:** The paper makes several original contributions to the field of tensor analysis.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The primary contribution—the first theoretical performance guarantee for the latent tensor decomposition approach that explains its superiority over the overlapped method—is highly original and significant. Prior work had observed this phenomenon empirically, but this paper provides the missing theoretical foundation.
    *   The formalization of the latent/overlapped Schatten norms and the proof of their duality is a novel and elegant theoretical result. It successfully bridges the gap between recent work in convex tensor decomposition and the more established literature on structured sparsity, providing a unified view.
    *   While building on analysis techniques from papers like Agarwal et al. (2012), the application and adaptation to the tensor setting are non-trivial and represent a novel technical contribution.

**Significance:** The results presented in this paper are highly significant and likely to have a lasting impact on the field.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper addresses a fundamental question in tensor decomposition: which convex relaxation should one use? It provides a clear, theoretically-backed answer: the latent approach is generally preferable as it automatically adapts to the most low-rank structure across the modes, a property the overlapped approach lacks. This provides valuable guidance for both researchers and practitioners.
    *   The result that the latent method performs "as if it knows the mode with the minimum rank" is a powerful and intuitive conclusion that advances the state of the art in understanding convex relaxations for tensor problems.
    *   The connection to structured sparsity may inspire new types of regularizers for tensors and other structured data types, opening up new avenues for future research.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Strong theoretical contribution:** Provides the first performance guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition method, explaining its empirical advantages.
*   **Novel duality result:** Establishes an elegant and useful connection between two important classes of tensor norms and the broader structured sparsity literature.
*   **Clear practical implication:** The findings strongly suggest that the latent formulation should be preferred over the more common overlapped formulation for noisy tensor recovery, a valuable piece of advice for practitioners.
*   **High quality and clarity:** The paper is technically sound, well-written, and easy to follow.
*   **Solid empirical validation:** The experiments are not just for show; they are carefully designed to confirm the scaling laws predicted by the theory.

### Arguments for Rejection

*   **Limited scope of analysis:** The theoretical results are currently confined to the denoising setting, not the more general tensor completion setting.
*   **Unexplored practical aspects:** The paper does not discuss the computational trade-offs of the proposed optimization formulation compared to the overlapped approach, which could be a factor in practice.

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the results are insightful, and the practical implications are clear. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to the scope of the analysis, which is reasonable for a single conference paper. The paper is well-written, and its findings are likely to be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
