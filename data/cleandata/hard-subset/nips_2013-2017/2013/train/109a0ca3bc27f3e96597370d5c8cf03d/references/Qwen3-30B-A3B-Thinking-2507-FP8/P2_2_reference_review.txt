

# Review for "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper investigates two convex approaches to tensor decomposition: the "overlapped approach" and the "latent approach." The overlapped approach penalizes all modes of a tensor to be jointly low-rank using the overlapped Schatten 1-norm, while the latent approach decomposes a tensor into a mixture of tensors that are each low-rank in a specific mode. The authors establish a duality between these two approaches (Lemma 1), showing that the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm. They then provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, showing that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average of the mode-k ranks. The paper demonstrates through experiments that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. This work extends the structured sparsity literature to tensor decomposition, connecting it to group lasso approaches.

## Strengths

### Quality
The paper presents a technically sound theoretical contribution with rigorous proofs for all key results (Theorems 1-3). The authors establish a novel duality relationship between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which is a significant theoretical contribution. The paper provides both deterministic and probabilistic bounds for the latent approach, showing that its error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, which explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach over the overlapped approach. The experimental section (Section 4) provides strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims, with MSE scaling linearly with the predicted complexity measures. The paper is careful to acknowledge limitations (e.g., the need for additional incoherence assumptions in Theorem 2) and to position their work relative to prior literature.

### Originality
The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the literature on convex tensor decomposition. While both approaches (overlapped and latent) were previously proposed, this is the first paper to rigorously analyze the latent approach and explain why it outperforms the overlapped approach theoretically. The duality result (Lemma 1) generalizes the duality between overlapped/latent group sparsity norms to the tensor case, which is a novel contribution. The connection to structured sparsity literature (e.g., group lasso) is insightful and broadens the applicability of the results. The paper's key insight about the minimum mode-k rank dependency represents a substantial theoretical advance over previous work.

### Significance
The paper addresses an important practical problem in tensor decomposition (model selection and performance comparison of convex approaches), providing both theoretical justification and empirical evidence for the superiority of the latent approach. This has direct implications for applications like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering where tensor decomposition is commonly used. The paper advances the state of the art by providing a more complete theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The connection to structured sparsity literature also opens up possibilities for further cross-fertilization between these fields.

### Clarity
The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and contrasts the two approaches. The definitions of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms are presented clearly in Section 2. The theoretical results are presented in a logical sequence (consistency, deterministic bounds, Gaussian noise analysis), with the key insights highlighted. The experimental section clearly demonstrates the theoretical predictions. The supplementary material provides additional proofs, which is helpful for readers interested in the technical details.

## Weaknesses

### Limited Scope of Experimental Evaluation
While the experiments in Section 4 confirm the theoretical predictions for the latent approach, they only compare the two convex approaches (overlapped vs. latent) and do not benchmark against non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., CP decomposition, Tucker decomposition). It would strengthen the paper to show how the latent approach compares to these standard non-convex methods in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency.

### Practical Implementation Details
The paper mentions using "algorithms based on alternating direction method of multipliers" from Tomioka et al. [25], but doesn't provide details on the computational complexity or scalability of the latent approach compared to other methods. For a practical application, understanding the computational cost is essential.

### Real-World Data Validation
The experiments use synthetic data with controlled rank structures. While this is valuable for validating the theoretical claims, the paper would be stronger with experiments on real-world tensor data (e.g., neuroimaging data, collaborative filtering data) to demonstrate practical utility.

### Connection to Recent Work
The paper references recent work on tensor completion (e.g., [19] by Mu et al.), but doesn't explicitly connect to the most recent advances in tensor decomposition. It would be helpful to position the work more clearly against the current state of the art in tensor decomposition.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of convex tensor decomposition, establishing a novel duality between two approaches and providing rigorous theoretical justification for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and provides strong empirical validation of the theoretical claims. While there are some limitations in the experimental evaluation (lack of comparison to non-convex methods and real-world data), these do not undermine the core theoretical contributions, which are substantial and well-supported.

## Detailed Comments for Authors

1. **Experimental Comparison**: Consider adding a comparison with non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., CP decomposition) to better position the latent approach in the broader tensor decomposition landscape.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: Include a discussion of computational complexity and scalability of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach and other tensor decomposition methods.

3. **Real-World Applications**: If possible, add experiments on a real-world tensor dataset (e.g., from neuroimaging or collaborative filtering) to demonstrate practical utility.

4. **Connection to Recent Work**: Clarify how your results relate to the recent work by Mu et al. [19] on lower bounds for tensor decomposition, particularly regarding the minimum mode-k rank dependency.

5. **Supplementary Material**: The supplementary material is mentioned but not detailed. Consider including a brief overview of the key proofs in the main paper to help readers understand the main technical contributions without needing to consult the supplement.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the field of convex tensor decomposition. The insights provided will be valuable to researchers working on tensor methods and structured sparsity.
