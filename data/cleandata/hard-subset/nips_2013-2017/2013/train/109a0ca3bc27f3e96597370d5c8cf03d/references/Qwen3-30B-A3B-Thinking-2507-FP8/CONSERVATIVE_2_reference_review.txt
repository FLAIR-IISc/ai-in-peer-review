

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper proposes a "latent approach" to tensor decomposition that contrasts with the previously proposed "overlapped approach" for convex optimization-based tensor decomposition. The authors claim that the latent approach has superior theoretical properties (error bounds scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank) and better empirical performance. The paper establishes a duality between the two approaches and provides theoretical error bounds for the latent approach.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a clear contribution to the theoretical understanding of structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is mathematically sound and connects to structured sparsity literature. The theoretical analysis, particularly the error bounds in Theorems 2 and 3, is rigorous and provides a clear explanation for the empirical observations in Figure 1. The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate mathematical notation.

**Weaknesses:**
Despite the theoretical soundness, I have significant concerns about the paper's contribution and validation:

1. **Lack of comprehensive empirical validation:** The paper's main claim is that the latent approach "performs better in many cases" but provides only limited empirical evidence. The numerical experiments (Section 4) are restricted to synthetic data with specific tensor sizes (50×50×20 and 80×80×40) and a narrow range of rank configurations. The paper does not compare with non-convex tensor decomposition methods (which are the standard in practice), nor does it demonstrate performance on real-world datasets. The claim that "the latent approach performs better in many cases" is not substantiated with sufficient empirical evidence across diverse scenarios.

2. **Questionable practical significance:** The theoretical advantage (error bound scaling with minimum mode-k rank) assumes knowledge of the minimum rank, which is not available in practice. The paper does not explain how practitioners would determine which mode has the minimum rank or how to implement the latent approach without this information. The regularization constant λ is theoretically independent of the ranks, but in practice, the optimal λ selection would still require knowledge of the rank structure.

3. **Limited comparison with established methods:** The paper only compares against the overlapped approach, which is another convex method. It fails to address the dominant non-convex tensor decomposition approaches (e.g., Tucker decomposition, CP decomposition) that are widely used in practice and have strong empirical performance. Without comparison to these standard methods, the paper's claim of superiority lacks context.

4. **Overstated claims about "preference":** The paper states "for noisy tensor decomposition, the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach" but only demonstrates this in specific synthetic scenarios. The experiments show "milder improvements" than Figure 1 (which is not provided in the review), suggesting the advantage may be marginal in many cases. The claim that the latent approach "performs better in many cases" is not sufficiently supported.

5. **Lack of computational considerations:** The paper does not address computational complexity or scalability, which is critical for tensor decomposition in real-world applications with large-scale data. The optimization problem (11) with spectral norm constraints may be significantly more computationally expensive than the overlapped approach.

**Originality:**
The paper builds on the existing work of Tomioka et al. [25, 26] but does not significantly advance the state of the art. The duality result is interesting but not groundbreaking, and the theoretical analysis appears to be a natural extension of existing structured sparsity theory. The paper does not sufficiently differentiate itself from prior work in terms of practical contributions.

**Significance:**
The paper's significance is limited by its narrow scope and lack of practical validation. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper fails to demonstrate how this approach would be practically useful in real-world tensor decomposition problems. Without compelling evidence of superior performance on realistic datasets or computational advantages, the contribution does not advance the field in a meaningful way.

**Overall Assessment:**
This is a technically sound paper with rigorous theoretical analysis, but it lacks the necessary empirical validation to support its main claims about practical superiority. The paper makes strong claims about the latent approach being preferable without demonstrating this across a range of realistic scenarios. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper's empirical evidence insufficient to justify its claims. The paper would be significantly stronger with comparisons to non-convex tensor decomposition methods on real-world datasets and a clearer explanation of how the latent approach would be practically implemented without knowledge of the minimum rank.

**Recommendation:**
Reject. The paper makes a modest theoretical contribution but fails to provide sufficient evidence for its practical significance. The empirical validation is limited and does not address the most relevant comparison (non-convex methods) or real-world applications. The theoretical advantage (minimum rank dependence) is not practically actionable without prior knowledge of the rank structure, which undermines the practical value of the approach.
