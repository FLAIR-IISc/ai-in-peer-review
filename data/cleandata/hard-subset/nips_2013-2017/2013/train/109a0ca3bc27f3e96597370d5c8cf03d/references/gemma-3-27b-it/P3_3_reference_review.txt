## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

**Overall Assessment:** This paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the growing field of convex tensor decomposition. It establishes a strong theoretical foundation for the "latent" approach, demonstrating its advantages over the more commonly used "overlapped" approach. The analysis is rigorous, the connection to structured sparsity literature is well-made, and the empirical validation supports the theoretical findings. While the paper is technically dense, the clarity is sufficient for experts in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

**Summary:**

The paper investigates two approaches to convex tensor decomposition based on Schatten norm regularization: the "overlapped" approach which penalizes low-rankness across all modes simultaneously, and the "latent" approach which decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors, each low-rank in a specific mode. The authors demonstrate a duality between the norms used in these two approaches, and crucially, prove that the latent approach achieves a better reconstruction error bound, scaling with the *minimum* rank of the underlying tensor, compared to the overlapped approach, which scales with the *average* rank. This theoretical result explains the empirical success of the latent approach observed in prior work. The paper reinforces these theoretical findings with numerical experiments.

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The core contribution – the theoretical analysis of the latent approach and its performance guarantee – is significant. The bound on the mean squared error, scaling with the minimum rank, is a key result that clarifies the strengths of this method. The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is insightful and generalizes existing results.
* **Clear Motivation & Problem Setting:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of traditional tensor decomposition (non-convexity, rank selection) and motivates the need for convex alternatives.  The connection to applications like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering is well-established.
* **Connections to Related Work:** The authors appropriately situate their work within the broader literature on structured sparsity (group lasso, etc.) and highlight the relationship to existing work on tensor decomposition (De Lathauwer et al., Tomioka et al.).
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments, while not extensive, provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical results, particularly demonstrating the scaling behavior predicted by the theory. The comparison with the overlapped approach validates the benefits of the latent method even in scenarios where the overlapped approach is expected to perform well.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** Despite the technical complexity, the paper is relatively well-organized and clearly presents the key ideas and results. The notation is standard and the arguments are generally easy to follow for someone familiar with tensor decomposition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Technical Density:** The paper is heavily laden with mathematical notation and theoretical derivations. While necessary for a rigorous analysis, it may make the paper inaccessible to a broader audience within NIPS.  A more intuitive explanation of the core concepts alongside the formal analysis could improve accessibility.
* **Limited Empirical Scope:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope. While they validate the theoretical findings, exploring a wider range of tensor sizes, rank configurations, and noise levels would strengthen the empirical support. It would be valuable to demonstrate performance on real-world datasets.
* **Assumptions:**  The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions (e.g., incoherence) that might not always hold in practice. Discussing the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions and exploring potential relaxations would be beneficial.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost associated with the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. This is an important practical consideration for potential users.
* **Lack of discussion on extensions to tensor completion**: While the authors mention this as a future work, a short discussion on the challenges or potential extensions would be good.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition.
* The analysis provides a clear explanation for the observed empirical success of the latent approach.
* The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable addition to the literature.
* The empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The technical density might limit accessibility for some readers.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
* The assumptions made in the theoretical analysis could be a limitation.




**Specific Suggestions for Improvement:**

* **Improve Accessibility:** Consider adding more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical derivations.  Perhaps include a high-level overview of the key ideas in the introduction.
* **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Explore a wider range of tensor sizes, rank configurations, noise levels, and potentially real-world datasets.
* **Discuss Computational Cost:** Provide a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach.
* **Address Assumptions:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the assumptions made in the theoretical analysis and potential relaxations.
* **Clarify Connection to Tensor Completion:**  Expand the discussion about the potential applicability to tensor completion problems, mentioning expected challenges.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to a growing and important area of research.  Addressing the minor weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.  I believe this work is well-suited for presentation at NIPS.
