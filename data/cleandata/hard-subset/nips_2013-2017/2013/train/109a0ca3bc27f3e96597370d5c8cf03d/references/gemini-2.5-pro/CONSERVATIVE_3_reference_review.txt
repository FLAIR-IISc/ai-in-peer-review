**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes the sum of Schatten-1 norms of all mode-k unfoldings, and the "latent" approach, which models the tensor as a sum of K components, where each component is constrained to be low-rank in a specific mode. The authors introduce a unifying framework of "structured Schatten norms" to describe these methods and establish a novel duality between the overlapped and latent norms, connecting this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting. The authors prove error bounds showing that the mean squared error of the latent method scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts with previous work on the overlapped approach, where the error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result provides a formal explanation for why the latent approach can outperform the overlapped one, particularly when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper concludes with numerical experiments on synthetic data designed to verify these theoretical scaling laws.

**Strengths**

1.  **Rigorous Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's primary strength is its sound theoretical analysis. The introduction of the structured Schatten norm framework and the proof of duality between the overlapped and latent norms (Lemma 1) is an elegant contribution that unifies these tensor methods with established concepts in structured sparsity.

2.  **Clear Explanatory Power:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 3, Eq. 15) provides a clear and compelling explanation for the performance difference between the latent and overlapped approaches. The finding that the latent approach's error bound depends on `min_k r_k` rather than an average is a significant insight and formally justifies the intuition presented in the introduction.

3.  **Methodological Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the distinction between the two convex formulations. The problem setup, theoretical assumptions, and main results are presented in an organized and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses**

While the theoretical analysis is sound, the paper's claims of practical superiority are not fully substantiated due to significant shortcomings in the empirical evaluation and the scope of the investigation.

1.  **Insufficient Baselines:** A major weakness is the limited comparison to established methods. The paper focuses exclusively on comparing the "latent" approach to the "overlapped" convex approach. However, for tensor decomposition and denoising, non-convex methods like Alternating Least Squares (ALS) for CP decomposition or Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD) for Tucker decomposition are the standard, widely-used baselines. These methods have been used for decades and are known to be empirically effective. Without a thorough comparison against these conventional techniques, it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed approach. The paper does not establish whether either convex method is competitive with these established non-convex workhorses.

2.  **Lack of Real-World Validation:** The evaluation is performed solely on synthetic data. While this is appropriate for verifying theoretical scaling laws, it fails to demonstrate the method's effectiveness on real-world problems, such as the neuroimaging or collaborative filtering applications mentioned in the introduction. Real data often presents challenges not captured by simple Gaussian noise models. The absence of experiments on real datasets significantly weakens the paper's impact and makes the claims of practical preference feel premature.

3.  **Limited Problem Scope:** The analysis and experiments are restricted to the denoising setting with fully observed tensors. A primary motivation for low-rank tensor methods, particularly convex ones, is the completion problem (i.e., recovering a tensor from a small subset of its entries). The paper acknowledges analyzing tensor completion as future work, but this is a critical omission that limits the current contribution's scope and relevance, as the performance characteristics in completion can differ from denoising.

4.  **Gap Between Theory and Practice:** The tighter theoretical bounds (Theorem 2 and 3) rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and an additional spectral norm constraint in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). It is not clear from the text whether the algorithm used for the experiments actually implements this constraint. If not, there is a potential disconnect between the theory that is proven and the method that is evaluated empirically. This lack of clarity on implementation details raises questions about the direct applicability of the theoretical results to the presented experiments.

**Overall assessment and recommendation**

In summary, the paper presents a solid and elegant theoretical contribution by analyzing the performance of the latent approach for convex tensor decomposition and framing it within a broader structured norm framework. The theoretical results are insightful and provide a valuable explanation for the method's behavior.

However, the empirical validation is not sufficiently thorough to support the claims of general superiority. The lack of comparison to standard non-convex baselines and the exclusive reliance on synthetic data are significant weaknesses. For a venue like NIPS, which values contributions that demonstrably advance the state of the art, the empirical evidence presented here is unconvincing. While the theory is strong, the paper feels incomplete as a practical guide for researchers.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent tensor decomposition method.
*   Establishes an elegant duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity.
*   Clearly explains why the latent approach can be superior to the overlapped approach in specific, well-defined scenarios.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Fails to compare against standard, established non-convex baselines (e.g., ALS, HOSVD).
*   Experimental validation is limited to synthetic data, with no demonstration on real-world problems.
*   The scope is restricted to denoising, omitting the important problem of tensor completion.
*   Potential discrepancy between the assumptions in the theory and the algorithm used in practice.

Given the high standards at NIPS for both theoretical novelty and convincing empirical validation, I lean towards **rejection**. The paper would be significantly stronger and more suitable for publication with a more comprehensive experimental section that includes standard baselines and real-world datasets.
