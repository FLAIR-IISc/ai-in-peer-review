This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an "overlapped" approach with a "latent" approach. The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the latent approach achieves a better error bound – scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank – compared to the overlapped approach, which depends on the average rank. The authors provide a duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, and empirically validate their theoretical findings.

The paper is well-motivated, connecting tensor decomposition to applications in neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of existing non-convex methods and the potential benefits of convex relaxations.  The authors correctly position their work within the recent literature on convex tensor decomposition (citing [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]) and relate their findings to established results in structured sparsity [1, 2, 13, 17, 21]. The paper is organized logically, with clear definitions and a progressive development of the theoretical results.  The inclusion of numerical results strengthens the claims.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, establishing a consistency result and a denoising performance bound. The scaling result (error scaling with the minimum rank) is a significant theoretical contribution.
* **Duality Result:** The identification of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and provides deeper insight into the properties of these regularization techniques.
* **Empirical Validation:**  The numerical experiments corroborate the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical benefits of the latent approach, even in scenarios favorable to the overlapped approach.
* **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the concepts and results.  The notation is consistent, and the presentation is logical.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper effectively links the proposed work to related areas, such as structured sparsity and low-rank matrix decomposition.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:**  Theorem 2 relies on a somewhat strong incoherence assumption (Equation 10) and the assumption of a specific decomposition structure (Equation 9). The practical implications of these assumptions, and their typical validity in real-world applications, aren't fully discussed.  How sensitive are the results to violations of this structure?
* **Practical Considerations:** While the paper establishes theoretical performance guarantees, it doesn’t delve into practical aspects of implementing the latent approach.  Specifically, the computational cost of solving the optimization problem (11) compared to other methods isn't addressed. Are there efficient algorithms beyond those referenced from [25]?
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are primarily focused on demonstrating the scaling behavior predicted by the theory. Expanding the experiments to include a wider range of tensor sizes, noise levels, and rank configurations would strengthen the empirical validation.  Exploring datasets beyond randomly generated tensors would be beneficial.
* **Lemma 1 - Clarity:** While technically correct, Lemma 1 and its explanation could be made more accessible.  A more intuitive explanation of the dual norm would improve understanding.
* **Discussion of Existing Limitations:** The paper mentions noise-less tensor completion in relation to previous work [25, 26]. A more comprehensive discussion of the current limitations of convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., scalability, sensitivity to noise) would provide further context.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a significant theoretical advance in understanding the performance of convex tensor decomposition methods.
* The duality result provides valuable insights into the properties of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
* The empirical results support the theoretical findings and demonstrate the practical benefits of the latent approach.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and connects to relevant literature.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

* The assumptions underlying the theoretical results may be restrictive in some applications.
* The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical considerations of implementing the latent approach.
* The experiments are somewhat limited in scope.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful, and the empirical results provide compelling evidence for the benefits of the latent approach.  While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.

**Suggested Revisions:**

* **Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the incoherence assumption (Equation 10).**  Provide guidance on when this assumption is likely to hold in practice.
* **Address the computational complexity of implementing the latent approach.**  Compare it to existing methods and discuss potential optimization strategies.
* **Expand the numerical experiments** to include a wider range of tensor sizes, noise levels, and rank configurations, and explore real-world datasets.
* **Provide a more intuitive explanation of Lemma 1 and the dual norm concept.**
* **Include a discussion of the current limitations of convex tensor decomposition methods.**
