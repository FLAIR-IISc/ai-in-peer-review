This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex approach to tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of K components, each being low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the more established "overlapped approach," which encourages a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its modes. The authors introduce a unifying framework of "structured Schatten norms" that elegantly connects these tensor methods to the literature on structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped and latent group LASSO). The main technical contributions are: 1) a novel duality result between the proposed "overlapped" and "latent" Schatten norms, and 2) a rigorous error analysis for the latent approach in a noisy setting. The key theoretical finding is that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas prior work has shown the overlapped approach scales with the *sum* of the (square root of) mode-k ranks. This theoretical result provides a clear explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent method when the true tensor is highly anisotropic in its rank structure (i.e., low-rank in only one or a few modes). The paper supports its theoretical claims with numerical experiments that validate the predicted error scaling.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamental question in the nascent field of convex tensor decomposition: which regularizer should one use? By providing the first rigorous performance guarantee for the latent approach, it offers a clear, theoretically-backed answer. The finding that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k rk` is a powerful and intuitive result that significantly advances our understanding. It essentially shows that the latent method can adaptively find the "easiest" decomposition without prior knowledge, a highly desirable property. This work is likely to be influential for both theorists and practitioners working with tensor data.

2.  **Novelty and Originality:** The contributions are highly original. While the latent approach was proposed empirically in [25], this paper provides its first theoretical justification. The framework of structured Schatten norms and the associated duality theorem (Lemma 1) is an elegant and novel contribution in its own right. It beautifully unifies the tensor decomposition problem with the broader, well-studied field of structured sparsity, which may open up new avenues for research.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically very strong. The duality result is a key enabler for the analysis, and its derivation appears sound. The subsequent error analysis (Theorems 1-3) builds on established techniques for high-dimensional M-estimators but applies them skillfully to this new tensor setting. The theoretical results are precise and directly comparable to existing results for the overlapped approach, making the comparison sharp and unambiguous.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts by clearly motivating the problem and positioning the two competing convex approaches. The introduction of the structured norms is lucid, and the main theoretical results are stated clearly with their implications discussed. The experiments are well-designed to specifically verify the theoretical scaling laws, which they do convincingly.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Incoherence and Constrained Optimization:** The main error bound (Theorem 2) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes these assumptions as explicit constraints. While such assumptions are common in the literature (e.g., for matrix decomposition), their practical implications could be discussed in more detail. Is the modified optimization problem significantly harder to solve? How restrictive is the assumption `||W*(l)(k)||_S∞ ≤ α` in practice for real-world tensors? A deeper discussion on this would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are focused on denoising for fully observed tensors. A major application area for these methods is tensor *completion* (i.e., recovering a tensor from a small subset of its entries). While the paper appropriately flags this as future work, the current analysis and experiments do not address this setting, which limits the immediate practical takeaway for that important problem.

3.  **The Practicality of LR Complexity:** The "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" used to validate the theory in experiments requires finding an optimal low-rank decomposition of the *true, noiseless tensor* `W*`. This is a valid approach for verifying a theoretical bound, but it highlights that the key quantity `Σ r̄k` governing the performance is itself difficult to know or even estimate from data. A brief comment on the practical challenge of estimating this complexity would be valuable.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.
*   The main result—that error scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is a clean, significant, and impactful finding.
*   The novel framework of structured Schatten norms and the associated duality result is an elegant theoretical contribution that connects two important fields.
*   The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides strong empirical evidence for its claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The analysis for the tighter bounds requires a modified optimization problem with an incoherence assumption, the practical necessity and implications of which are not fully explored.
*   The study is confined to the denoising setting, leaving the important tensor completion problem for future work.

**Conclusion:**

This is an excellent paper that makes a fundamental contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. It resolves an important open question regarding the performance of the latent convex relaxation, providing a clear and compelling theoretical explanation for its advantages. The technical quality is high, the results are significant, and the presentation is clear. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate more to the scope and potential extensions rather than any flaw in the core contribution. This work clearly meets the high standards of NIPS and should be accepted. It is a complete and insightful piece of research that others in the field will undoubtedly build upon.
