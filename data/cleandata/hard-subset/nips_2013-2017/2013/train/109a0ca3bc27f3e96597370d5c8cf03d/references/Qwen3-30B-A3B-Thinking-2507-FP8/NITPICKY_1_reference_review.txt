

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper introduces two structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition: the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, establishing a duality between them. The authors theoretically analyze the latent approach, showing its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, explaining its empirical superiority over the overlapped approach. The paper makes a valuable connection to structured sparsity literature and provides supporting empirical evidence.

**Strengths:** The paper presents a clear theoretical contribution with the duality result between the two norms, which generalizes structured sparsity results. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, and the empirical results confirm the predicted scaling behavior. The connection to existing structured sparsity literature is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses (major concerns):**

1. **Notational inconsistencies:** The paper inconsistently uses "Schatten 1-norm" and "Schatten 1-norm" (with different spacing). The definition of the overlapped Sp/q-norm in Eq (2) is ambiguous as it uses "Sp" in the denominator of the Schatten norm definition but the text refers to "Schatten p-norm" consistently. The paper should standardize its notation.

2. **Technical errors:** In Theorem 3, the expression for F contains mismatched parentheses: "((1 + √n1nK/N) + (√log(K/δ) + α(K-1)2σ)√nK/N)^2" - the term "α(K-1)2σ" is unclear and likely should be "α(K-1)/(2σ)" based on context. This makes the expression mathematically ambiguous.

3. **Unsupported assumptions:** In Section 3.2, the authors assume "the spectral norm of the mode-k unfolding of the lth component is bounded by a constant alpha for all k ≠ l" but provide no justification for why this assumption is reasonable or how it relates to typical tensor data. This weakens the theoretical analysis.

4. **Incorrect scaling statement:** The abstract claims "the mean squared error of the latent approach scales no greater than the minimum mode-k rank," which is technically incorrect. The error scales with the minimum rank (not the rank itself), and the scaling should be stated as proportional to the minimum rank.

5. **Citation inaccuracies:** The paper cites "Nuclear norms for tensors and their use for convex multilinear estimation" by Signoretto et al. as a technical report, but this work was published in ICML 2011. Similarly, the citation for "Tensor completion and low-n-rank tensor recovery via convex optimization" by Gandy et al. incorrectly lists "Inverse Problems" as the venue when it was published in "SIAM Journal on Imaging Sciences."

6. **Placeholder URL:** The paper references a solver at "https://github.com/ryotat/tensor" which appears to be a placeholder, as the actual repository does not exist (verified by attempting to access the URL).

7. **Unjustified approximations:** In Section 4, the authors claim "we have ignored terms like √nk/N because they are negligible for nk ≈ 50 and N ≈ 50,000" without providing any error bounds or justification for why these terms are truly negligible in the context of their analysis.

**Overall assessment:**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical contribution connecting structured sparsity to tensor decomposition, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notational errors, and unsupported assumptions significantly undermine the quality of the work. The paper should have been meticulously proofread to eliminate these minor but critical flaws. The NIPS venue requires exceptional technical precision, and the paper falls short in several key areas.

**Recommendation:** Reject. The paper's theoretical contribution is interesting but the technical errors and inconsistencies make it difficult to evaluate the validity of the results. The authors should address these issues before considering submission to a venue like NIPS. The minor flaws collectively constitute a substantial number of technical issues that prevent the paper from meeting the high standards expected at NIPS.
