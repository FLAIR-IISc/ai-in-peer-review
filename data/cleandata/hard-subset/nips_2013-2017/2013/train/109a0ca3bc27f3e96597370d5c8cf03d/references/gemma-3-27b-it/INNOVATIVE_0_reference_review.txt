This paper presents a compelling analysis of structured Schatten norm regularization for tensor decomposition, specifically contrasting an "overlapped" and a "latent" approach. The core contribution – a duality result between the two norms and a theoretical analysis demonstrating superior performance of the latent approach when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode – is highly interesting and potentially significant. While the presentation could benefit from increased clarity in places, the novelty of the theoretical results and the potential impact on the field are substantial.

**Summary:** The authors investigate convex relaxation techniques for tensor decomposition. They highlight a lesser-known "latent" approach and rigorously demonstrate, through a duality result and theoretical bounds, why this approach outperforms the more common “overlapped” method when dealing with tensors low-rank in only a subset of modes. They also provide empirical validation supporting their theoretical findings.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty:** The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a genuinely novel contribution, connecting this work to related literature on structured sparsity. The theoretical justification for the latent approach’s superior performance is also novel and addresses an empirical observation highlighted in prior work [25].
* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the latent approach, including consistency results and error bounds. The analysis goes beyond merely stating the latent approach is better; it quantifies *why* it's better, linking performance to the minimal rank across modes.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors effectively position their work within the broader landscape of tensor decomposition and convex optimization, drawing connections to relevant literature and highlighting the limitations of existing methods.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments provide supporting evidence for the theoretical findings, reinforcing the claim that the latent approach is preferable in many cases.
* **Clear Problem Statement:** The motivation for the work is well articulated, and the challenges of traditional tensor decomposition methods (e.g., rank selection) are clearly explained.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Clarity of Notation:** The paper is dense with notation. While mathematically precise, the introduction of several norms (Sp/q, Sp*/q*, Schatten norms) in quick succession can be challenging for the reader to follow. More intuitive explanations or visual aids would be beneficial.
* **Assumptions:** The theoretical bounds rely on specific assumptions (e.g., incoherence) that might not always hold in practice. A discussion of the robustness of the results to violations of these assumptions would be valuable.
* **Practical Implications:** While the theoretical results are strong, the paper doesn’t fully explore the practical implications of the findings. How easily can the latent approach be implemented? Are there computational trade-offs compared to the overlapped approach?  Further discussion on these points would strengthen the paper.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments focus on Gaussian noise and relatively small tensors.  Exploring the performance with different noise distributions or larger-scale datasets would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* The paper presents genuinely novel theoretical results with the potential to significantly impact the field of tensor decomposition.
* The analysis provides a clear explanation for the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach.
* The paper is technically sound and well-motivated.
* The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the related literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The paper is dense and could benefit from improved clarity and more intuitive explanations.
* The theoretical results rely on assumptions that may not always be realistic.
* The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses in clarity and scope, this paper represents a significant contribution to the field. The novelty of the duality result and the rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach are particularly compelling. The authors successfully identify and address an important open problem in tensor decomposition. I believe the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses, and I **recommend acceptance**.  The minor flaws are not critical and could be addressed with further revisions. This work introduces a new perspective on tensor decomposition that is likely to stimulate further research in this area.
