### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, a method for recovering a low-rank tensor from noisy observations. The authors contrast this with the more established "overlapped approach," where a single tensor is regularized to be simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings via a sum of nuclear norms. The latent approach, in contrast, models the target tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in only one specific mode.

The key contributions of this work are threefold. First, it introduces a general framework of "structured Schatten norms" (overlapped and latent Sp/q-norms), which unifies these two approaches and connects them to the broader literature on structured sparsity. Second, it establishes a novel and elegant duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, a result of independent interest. Third, and most importantly, the paper leverages this framework to prove performance guarantees for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The main theoretical result shows that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true tensor. This provides a formal explanation for prior empirical observations that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped one, whose error is known to scale with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. The authors conclude with numerical experiments that validate these theoretical scaling predictions.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper closes an important gap in the literature by providing the first performance guarantees for the latent approach to tensor decomposition. The main finding—that the error scales with `min_k r_k` rather than `sum_k sqrt(r_k)`—is a strong and important result. It provides a clear, quantitative justification for preferring the latent approach, especially for tensors that are highly anisotropic in their rank structure (i.e., low-rank in only one mode).

2.  **Elegant Unifying Framework:** The formalization of overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable conceptual contribution. It elegantly connects two seemingly distinct tensor decomposition methods and places them in a broader context of structured regularization, drawing explicit and insightful parallels to the well-studied overlapped/latent group lasso for vectors. The duality result (Lemma 1) is novel and serves as a key technical tool for the subsequent analysis.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction, the theoretical contributions are stated precisely, and the relationship to prior work is well-articulated. The progression from the general norm framework to the specific denoising analysis is logical and easy to follow.

4.  **Strong and Well-Designed Experiments:** The numerical results in Section 4 are a model for how to validate theory. Rather than simply comparing performance on benchmark datasets, the experiments are carefully designed to test the specific scaling laws derived in the paper. Figure 2 provides compelling evidence that the MSE of the latent and overlapped methods scales linearly with the "LR complexity" and "TR complexity," respectively, which strongly supports the theoretical claims.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Practicality of the Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter performance bound in Theorem 2 is derived for a constrained optimization problem (11) that includes an incoherence assumption, `||W*_(l)(k)||_S_inf <= alpha`. While such assumptions are standard in related literature, the paper does not discuss the practical implications of this constraint.
    *   How should `alpha` be chosen in practice?
    *   Was this explicit constraint used in the algorithm for the numerical experiments? The referenced algorithm in [25] does not appear to include it. If the constraint was not used, there is a small disconnect between the theory and the empirical validation that warrants discussion.

2.  **Focus on Denoising vs. Completion:** The analysis is restricted to the tensor denoising setting, where the tensor is fully observed but corrupted by noise. A more common and arguably more challenging application of low-rank tensor methods is tensor completion, where a large fraction of entries are missing. While the authors rightly note this as future work, the current analysis's significance is somewhat limited by its scope.

3.  **Post-Hoc Nature of "LR Complexity":** In the experiments, the Latent Rank (LR) complexity used for validation is computed by finding an optimal decomposition of the *true, noiseless* tensor `W*`. While this is a perfectly valid method for verifying the correctness of the theoretical bound, it is a post-hoc calculation that relies on access to the ground truth. A brief comment acknowledging this and distinguishing it from an a-priori computable complexity measure would add clarity.

### **Evaluation**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound, the theoretical results are non-trivial, and the claims are well-supported by both proofs and rigorous experiments. It is a complete and polished piece of research.
*   **Clarity:** Excellent. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and easy to understand. The notation is consistent, and the structure is logical.
*   **Originality:** High. While the latent decomposition method itself was previously proposed, the theoretical analysis is entirely novel. The generalization to structured Schatten norms and the associated duality proof are also original contributions that bridge research areas in a meaningful way.
*   **Significance:** High. This work provides a definitive theoretical answer to the question of when and why the latent approach is superior to the overlapped approach. This is likely to influence both future theoretical work and the practical application of convex tensor methods. The formal framework may also inspire new types of regularizers.

### **Arguments for Acceptance**
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.
*   The main result, showing error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank, is strong and elegantly explains previous empirical findings.
*   Introduces a novel and unifying framework of structured Schatten norms and proves an interesting duality result.
*   Theory is strongly validated by carefully designed experiments.

### **Arguments against Acceptance**
*   The analysis for the main result relies on an incoherence assumption whose practical implementation and sensitivity are not fully discussed.
*   The analysis is limited to the tensor denoising setting, not the more common completion setting.

### **Suggestions for Improvement**

I strongly recommend this paper for acceptance. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. To further improve the manuscript, I suggest the authors:

*   Add a brief paragraph in the experiment section (or appendix) clarifying whether the incoherence constraint from Eq. (11) was implemented in the solver. If it was not, please discuss why the experimental results still align so well with a theory that relies on it.
*   Briefly elaborate on the challenges of extending the current analysis to the tensor completion setting in the conclusion or future work section. This would further contextualize the contribution.
*   Consider adding a sentence in Section 4 to clarify that the calculation of "LR complexity" is an oracle procedure used for theoretical validation.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a substantial contribution to our understanding of convex tensor decomposition.
