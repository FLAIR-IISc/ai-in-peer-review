

This paper proposes a "latent approach" to tensor decomposition, claiming it outperforms the existing "overlapped approach" for tensors low-rank in only one mode. The authors establish theoretical bounds showing the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, while the overlapped approach's error scales with the average mode-k rank. They provide empirical validation on synthetic data where the tensor is low-rank in a single mode.

The paper's main strength lies in its theoretical analysis, particularly the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms. The connection to structured sparsity literature is well-motivated, and the theoretical results are technically sound within their specific scope. The paper clearly articulates the distinction between the two approaches and provides a compelling explanation for the empirical observations in Figure 1.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that undermine its broader claims of superiority:

1. The empirical validation is limited to synthetic data with carefully chosen parameters. The experiments only demonstrate the latent approach's superiority when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode (the scenario where the overlapped approach struggles), but the authors acknowledge that "most of the randomly generated low-rank tensors were simultaneously low-rank to some degree." The paper shows the latent approach performs "at least as well" in these more common cases, but fails to demonstrate consistent superiority.

2. The claim that "the latent approach performs better in many cases" lacks sufficient empirical support. Figure 2 shows improvements only when the TR complexity to LR complexity ratio is high, which corresponds precisely to the specific scenario where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper provides no evidence that this scenario is common in real-world applications.

3. The paper doesn't compare against established tensor decomposition methods beyond the overlapped approach. It ignores recent advances in tensor decomposition (e.g., Mu et al., 2013; Liu et al., 2013) that have been widely adopted in practice, making it impossible to assess the real-world impact of the proposed approach.

4. The paper doesn't address computational complexity or scalability. The latent approach involves solving a more complex optimization problem (equation 11), but the paper provides no analysis of computational efficiency or scalability to large tensors.

5. The theoretical analysis assumes an optimal choice of regularization constant λ, which is not practical since the ranks of the true tensor are unknown. The paper acknowledges this limitation but offers no practical solution for choosing λ in real applications.

6. The paper's main contribution appears to be a theoretical refinement of an existing approach rather than a substantial advancement. The "superiority" claim is limited to a narrow scenario (tensor low-rank in only one mode), which the authors themselves admit is not the typical case.

The paper's claim that "the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach" is not sufficiently supported by evidence. The authors demonstrate a theoretical advantage in a specific scenario but fail to show this translates to consistent practical benefits. The empirical validation is insufficient to justify the broad claims made in the abstract and conclusion.

As a reviewer skeptical of unproven approaches without extensive validation, I find the paper's contribution incremental rather than transformative. While the theoretical analysis is sound, the lack of comprehensive empirical validation against established methods and the limited scope of the claimed advantages make it difficult to assess the paper's significance for the broader machine learning community.

The paper would be better suited for a specialized venue focused on tensor methods rather than NIPS, where the bar for significance is higher. To be acceptable for NIPS, the authors would need to provide more compelling evidence of the practical advantages of their approach, including comparisons against established methods on real-world datasets and a clearer demonstration of why the latent approach would be preferred in general cases where the tensor structure is unknown.

I recommend rejection as the paper does not meet NIPS's standards for significance and empirical validation. The contribution is interesting but limited in scope, and the evidence provided does not sufficiently support the broad claims of superiority over established methods.
