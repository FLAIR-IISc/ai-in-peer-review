Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor decomposition and denoising. It contrasts two families of norms: (i) the overlapped Schatten Sp/q norms that sum nuclear norms of all mode unfoldings, and (ii) the latent Schatten norms defined via an infimal convolution over a decomposition into mode-specific low-rank components. The authors establish a duality between the overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms (generalizing known dualities in overlapping/latent group sparsity), derive basic inequalities relating these norms to the Frobenius norm, and analyze the statistical performance of the latent S1/1-regularized estimator in the additive Gaussian noise setting. Key results show consistency and denoising error bounds that scale with the minimal mode-k rank of the true tensor, contrasting with prior bounds for the overlapped approach that depend on averages of mode ranks. Experiments on synthetic data corroborate the predicted scaling and often show improved MSE for the latent approach.

Relation to prior work
This work sits squarely within convex tensor recovery literature (e.g., Liu et al., Gandy et al., Tomioka et al.). The overlapped nuclear-norm approach has been analyzed (Tomioka et al. 2010/2011/2013), but to my knowledge a rigorous statistical analysis for the latent approach was missing; this paper fills that gap. The duality result connects to latent/overlapped group lasso (Obozinski et al., Bach et al.) and the decomposable regularizer framework (Negahban et al., Agarwal et al.). The comparison to Mu et al. lower bounds is appropriate.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Clear framing of two convex surrogates for tensor low-rankness and a precise duality between them. The latent norm is cleanly identified as an infimal convolution over mode-wise nuclear norms.
- Theory: Consistency (Theorem 1) and denoising bounds (Theorems 2–3) for the latent approach, with a favorable dependence on min_k r_k. The Gaussian-noise bound gives an explicit λ and dimension dependence; the norm inequalities (Lemmas 1–2) are useful.
- Insight: The analysis quantitatively explains empirical observations that latent formulations help when only one mode is low-rank.
- Empirics: Synthetic experiments demonstrate linear scaling with the proposed complexity measures and illustrate regimes where latent improves over overlapped.

Weaknesses and questions
- Practicality of constraints and tuning: The main denoising bound (Theorem 2) requires additional spectral norm constraints between components (Eq. 11) with a known α, and the prescribed λ in Theorem 3 depends on α. In practice, α is unknown; the paper does not discuss how to set it or the impact of misspecification. Can similar results be proved for the unconstrained latent estimator (Eq. 5), perhaps with an additional penalty rather than hard constraints?
- Identifiability and interpretability: The latent decomposition is non-unique; the analysis leverages this by allowing singleton decompositions to achieve min_k r_k. While this is statistically advantageous, it limits interpretability of component tensors. Some discussion of identifiability (or lack thereof) and whether the algorithm tends to singleton solutions would be useful.
- Tightness and constants: The bound in Theorem 1 (||Ŵ−W*||_F ≤ 2λ sqrt(min n_k)) seems loose and insensitive to rank; it is only for consistency but could be sharpened or at least contextualized against matrix denoising results. The factor F in Theorem 3 could be simplified; a clearer statement of dominant terms in common regimes would help.
- Comparison fairness and breadth: The comparison uses prior bound (16) for overlapped; however, overlapped approaches with alternative weightings (e.g., S1/∞, or dimension-normalized nuclear norms) might narrow the gap. Including such baselines empirically would strengthen the case. No comparisons to nonconvex methods (CP/Tucker with model selection) or recent tensor norms are provided; while this is a convex recovery paper, some empirical context would help.
- Experiments: Only synthetic full-observation denoising is considered. Given the motivation (imputation/collaborative filtering), experiments on tensor completion and at least one real dataset would enhance significance. Moreover, computing LR complexity uses a decomposition of noiseless W*, unavailable in practice; while fine for validating theory, this should be stated more explicitly.
- Clarity/notation: The same notation S1/1 is used for overlapped (sum of nuclear norms) and latent (infimal convolution). This is potentially confusing; distinct symbols (e.g., ||·||overlap and ||·||latent) would improve readability. Some minor typographical and reference formatting issues exist.

Quality
The paper is technically sound overall, with results building on well-established convex-analytic tools (duality, decomposability, Gaussian width). Proofs are referenced to appendices; providing brief proof sketches for the key lemmas/theorems in the main text would aid readers. The additional incoherence constraints are a caveat for practical deployment and should be addressed.

Originality
The duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms in the tensor setting and the statistical analysis for the latent estimator are, to my knowledge, new and natural generalizations of known results in structured sparsity. The theoretical message—that latent scaling depends on min mode rank—is insightful and clarifies empirical behavior.

Significance
Understanding which convex surrogate to use for tensor problems is important. The paper offers a principled reason to prefer latent formulations in noisy denoising tasks and may influence future practice. Extensions to completion would further increase impact.

Suggestions for improvement
- Discuss how to set α, or remove the hard constraints and provide a result for the unconstrained latent estimator (even if with weaker constants).
- Add sensitivity analysis to α and λ in experiments; include at least one real dataset and a completion setting.
- Consider additional baselines (e.g., dimension-normalized overlapped norms, S1/∞ variants) to test robustness of the conclusion.
- Clarify notation and provide short proof sketches for Lemmas 1–2 and Theorem 2.
- Comment on computational aspects: proximal operators, scalability, and convergence for the latent norm solver.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general duality result and new theory for latent tensor norms.
- Bounds with favorable dependence on min mode rank that match experiments.
- Addresses a relevant and timely question in convex tensor methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Reliance on additional constraints with unknown α; limited guidance for practice.
- Narrow empirical evaluation (no completion, no real data).
- Potentially unfair/limited baseline space; unclear tightness of some bounds.

Recommendation
Weak accept. The conceptual clarity and theoretical contributions outweigh the empirical limitations, and the work should be of interest to the NeurIPS audience working on convex methods for tensors and structured regularization.
