This paper explores convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an "overlapped" approach with a "latent" approach. The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the latent approach exhibits superior performance, scaling with the minimum mode-k rank, unlike the overlapped approach. The paper establishes a duality between the two norms and provides empirical validation.

While the problem setting is relevant, and the identification of a theoretical advantage for the latent approach is a valuable contribution, the paper suffers from a plethora of minor, yet cumulatively significant, flaws. My assessment leans towards rejection due to the sheer number of these issues.

**Strengths:**

*   **Relevant Problem:** Tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with applications across numerous fields.
*   **Theoretical Insight:** The identification and proof of the scaling advantage of the latent approach based on the minimum rank is a noteworthy contribution.
*   **Duality Result:** Establishing the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a potentially useful theoretical result.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The empirical results, although not overwhelmingly conclusive, lend some support to the theoretical findings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Rigor in Notation & Definitions:** Several definitions are imprecise. For example, defining the dot product as `vec(W)⊤vec(X)` is mathematically correct but obscures the underlying structure. This impacts readability. The definition of the Frobenius norm is also unnecessarily verbose. The explanation of mode-k unfolding is acceptable, but a visual illustration would be beneficial.
*   **Overly Verbose Introduction:** The introduction is excessively long and contains meandering discussion. The connection between neuroimaging and general multivariate data feels forced. The phrase "widely open" is vague and should be replaced with more precise language.
*   **Poorly Explained Motivation:** The motivation for the latent approach, beyond a brief reference to Figure 1, isn't thoroughly explained. Why *intuitively* is it better suited for tensors low-rank in only one mode?
*   **Lemma 1 – Insufficient Justification:** The statement that Lemma 1 "generalizes" results in structured sparsity literature is not adequately justified. A more concrete connection should be established. The proof location is also relegated to the appendix without sufficient preview.
*   **Lemma 2 – Limited Impact:** While correct, Lemma 2’s significance isn't fully leveraged. Its role in the subsequent analysis could be strengthened.
*   **Theoretical Sections Lack Clarity:** The jump from Lemma 2 to Theorem 1 feels abrupt. The assumptions in Theorem 2 (especially regarding α) are not sufficiently motivated. The condition λ ≥ 2 ∣∣∣∣∣∣E∣∣∣∣∣∣ S∞/∞ + α(K − 1) is presented without clear explanation of how α is determined in practice.
*   **Theorem 3 - Overly Strong Assumptions:** Assuming *independent* zero-mean Gaussian noise is a strong assumption that significantly limits the theorem’s applicability.
*   **Numerical Experiment Details are Sparse:** The numerical experiments are poorly described. Details about the algorithms used for optimization are insufficient (citing a GitHub repository isn’t a substitute for a detailed description). The rationale behind the choice of regularization constants is unclear. The definition of "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" are unnecessarily cumbersome. The statement about scaling being “roughly” by 1.6 feels hand-wavy.
*   **Conclusion – Repetitive and Self-Congratulatory:** The conclusion largely restates previously presented information and is overly enthusiastic.
*   **Formatting and Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., varying spacing around equations). There are several typos throughout the manuscript ("e.g." used incorrectly in several places, “singleton” used instead of “single-tensor”). The citation style is inconsistent. (e.g., [1] vs. [1,3,12]).

**Pro/Con Acceptance:**

**Pro:**

*   Novel theoretical result regarding the scaling of the latent approach.
*   Identifies a potential advantage of the latent approach over existing methods.

**Con:**

*   Numerous minor flaws in notation, clarity, and justification.
*   Overly verbose and repetitive writing style.
*   Strong assumptions in the theoretical analysis.
*   Insufficient detail in the numerical experiments.
*   Formatting and typographical errors.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the core idea is interesting, the presentation is significantly flawed. The abundance of minor issues detracts from the paper’s overall quality and hinders its understanding. A substantial revision addressing these concerns is necessary before this paper would be considered acceptable for NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. The paper, as it stands, does not meet the NIPS standards for quality, clarity, and rigor.
