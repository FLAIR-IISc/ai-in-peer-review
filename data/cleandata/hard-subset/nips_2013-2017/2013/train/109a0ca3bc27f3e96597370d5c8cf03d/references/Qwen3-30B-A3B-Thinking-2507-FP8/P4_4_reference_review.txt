

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates convex optimization approaches for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors introduce two structured Schatten norms: the overlapped Schatten norm (used in the overlapped approach) and the latent Schatten norm (used in the latent approach). They establish a duality between these two norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. The main theoretical contribution is proving that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, while the overlapped approach's error scales with the average (square root) of the mode-k ranks. This explains why the latent approach performs better in certain scenarios, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper provides theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-3) and confirms these predictions through numerical experiments.

The paper builds on previous convex tensor decomposition work by Tomioka et al. (2011) and Signoretto et al. (2010), as well as structured sparsity literature (e.g., Jenatton et al., 2011). It extends these works by providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for the latent approach, which had only been empirically observed to outperform the overlapped approach.

**Strengths:**
1. Theoretical novelty: The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a significant theoretical contribution that connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature. This duality is clearly established and generalizes known results.

2. Technical soundness: The paper provides rigorous theoretical guarantees (Theorems 1-3) with detailed proofs in the supplementary material. The bounds are well-motivated and appropriately derived using techniques from Agarwal et al. (2012) and structured sparsity literature.

3. Empirical validation: The numerical experiments in Section 4 confirm the theoretical predictions, showing that the MSE scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (TR complexity for overlapped approach, LR complexity for latent approach).

4. Clarity: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions of the two structured Schatten norms. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the paper builds logically from basic concepts to more complex theoretical results.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The paper's main theoretical contribution (the duality between the two norms) is somewhat technical and might be challenging for readers unfamiliar with structured sparsity. While the paper connects to structured sparsity literature, it could more explicitly discuss how this specific duality extends previous results.

2. The experimental section could be strengthened by comparing with more recent tensor decomposition methods beyond the overlapped and latent approaches. While the focus is on comparing these two specific approaches, it would be valuable to see how they perform relative to state-of-the-art tensor decomposition methods.

3. The paper claims the latent approach "is preferable to the overlapped approach" in general, but the theoretical results only show superiority in specific regimes (when the minimum mode-k rank is much smaller than the average rank). The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of when each approach is preferable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
- The paper presents a clear theoretical contribution with a novel duality between two structured Schatten norms.
- The theoretical results are rigorously proven and supported by empirical validation.
- The paper advances the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods, which is an important area in machine learning and data analysis.
- The connection to structured sparsity literature provides valuable theoretical context.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
- The main theoretical contribution, while sound, may not be sufficiently novel compared to recent work in structured sparsity (e.g., the duality between overlapped and latent group sparsity norms has been studied in other contexts).
- The paper focuses on a specific comparison between two approaches without providing a broader context of how these approaches fit into the current landscape of tensor decomposition methods.
- The empirical section could be strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

Overall, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of convex tensor decomposition by establishing a theoretical foundation for the latent approach. The duality result is novel and significant, and the theoretical guarantees are well-supported. The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for the superiority of the latent approach in certain settings. While the novelty might be somewhat incremental compared to the most recent structured sparsity literature, the paper provides a valuable theoretical explanation for an empirically observed phenomenon.

The paper meets NIPS criteria for quality, clarity, and significance. The theoretical results are sound, the paper is clearly written, and the findings advance the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The significance is demonstrated through both theoretical insights and empirical validation.

The main weakness is that the paper could better contextualize its novelty relative to recent structured sparsity literature, but this does not undermine the technical soundness or significance of the work. The paper provides a valuable theoretical contribution that will be useful for researchers working on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

Given the technical soundness, clarity, and significance of the contributions, I recommend acceptance of this paper.
