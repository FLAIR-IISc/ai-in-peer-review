### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" to convex tensor decomposition, a method for recovering a low-rank tensor from noisy observations. The authors contrast this approach with the more established "overlapped approach." While the overlapped method regularizes a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings, the latent method models the target tensor as a sum of several component tensors, where each component is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode.

The key contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Unifying Framework:** The paper introduces "structured Schatten norms" to formalize and generalize both the overlapped and latent approaches.
2.  **Duality:** It establishes a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which elegantly connects this line of work to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., latent vs. overlapped group LASSO).
3.  **Performance Guarantees for the Latent Approach:** The central contribution is deriving theoretical error bounds for the latent approach. The authors show that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true underlying tensor. This provides a theoretical justification for the empirical observation that the latent method excels when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode but high-rank in others.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The paper presents numerical experiments that confirm the derived theoretical scaling laws, showing that the error of the latent approach is well-predicted by a "Latent Rank complexity" measure derived from their bounds.

This work fills an important theoretical gap, as the latent approach, introduced in Tomioka et al. [25], had demonstrated empirical success but lacked the theoretical backing that existed for the overlapped approach [26].

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

*   **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main achievement is providing the first performance guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition method. The key insight—that the error scales with `min_k r_k` (the minimum mode-k rank) as opposed to the overlapped method's dependence on `(Σ_k sqrt(r_k))^2`—is both important and intuitive. It delivers a clear explanation for a previously observed empirical phenomenon and significantly advances our understanding of convex tensor methods.
*   **Novel and Elegant Framework:** The formalization of overlapped and latent Schatten norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is a strong, original contribution. It creates a valuable connection to the well-studied field of structured sparsity and provides a unified perspective on these seemingly different tensor regularization schemes.
*   **High-Quality Empirical Support:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to do more than just show that one method outperforms another. By plotting the mean squared error against the theoretically derived complexity measures (TR and LR complexity), the authors provide compelling evidence that their analysis correctly captures the scaling behavior of the algorithm with respect to both rank and tensor dimensions. This is a strong form of validation for their theory.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. The motivation is clear, the distinction between the two approaches is carefully explained, and the narrative flows logically from definitions and duality to the main theoretical results and their empirical verification.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Mismatch Between Analyzed and Implemented Algorithms:** The main theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) are derived for a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11), which requires an incoherence assumption (`||W_(l)(k)||S∞ ≤ α`). However, the experiments use an unconstrained algorithm from [25] that solves a simpler problem (Eq. 5). While the results appear to hold, the paper would be stronger if it explicitly addressed this gap. The authors could either discuss why the analysis for the constrained problem is relevant to the unconstrained one or comment on the practical feasibility of solving the constrained formulation directly.
*   **Practical Implications of the Incoherence Assumption:** The incoherence parameter `α` is central to the tighter bounds, as the choice of the regularization parameter `λ` depends on it. A discussion of the practical implications of this assumption would be beneficial. For example, how restrictive is this assumption for real-world tensors? In a practical setting where the true components `W*(k)` are unknown, how would one set or estimate `α`?
*   **Explanation of the Singleton Decomposition:** The critical insight that the error bound depends on `min_k r_k` arises from the flexibility of using a "singleton decomposition" for the true tensor `W*`. This point is crucial but is stated somewhat briefly. The authors could make this argument more explicit in Section 3.2 by explaining that one can always satisfy the decomposition `W* = Σ_k W*(k)` by choosing `W*_(k') = W*` for `k' = argmin_k r_k` and setting all other components `W*(l)` to zero. This simple but powerful example would further clarify the origin of the `min_k r_k` term.

### **Detailed Comments**

The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The claims are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and experimental results. The connection to the structured sparsity literature is well-drawn and adds depth to the contribution. The comparison with the prior analysis of the overlapped approach [26] is direct and highly effective at highlighting the significance of the new results.

### **Summary of Recommendation**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of tensor decompositions. It provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for the latent approach, explains its empirical advantages through rigorous analysis, and validates this analysis with well-designed experiments. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate primarily to clarifying the connection between the theory and practice, which can be addressed in a final version. The paper is well-written, impactful, and of clear interest to the NeurIPS community working on machine learning theory, optimization, and large-scale data analysis. I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous performance guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition method.
*   The main theoretical result elegantly explains the superiority of the latent approach in specific, important regimes (i.e., when rank is low only in certain modes).
*   Introduces a novel and unifying framework of structured Schatten norms and their duality.
*   Presents strong empirical evidence that directly validates the scaling laws predicted by the theory.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
*   A potential disconnect between the constrained problem analyzed in the theory and the unconstrained algorithm used in experiments.
*   The practical implications of the required incoherence assumption are not fully discussed.
