### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates convex relaxations for low-rank tensor decomposition, focusing on a method termed the "latent approach." This approach models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the more established "overlapped approach," which simultaneously penalizes the nuclear norm of all unfoldings (matricizations) of a single tensor.

The authors make several key contributions. First, they formalize these two approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms," defining the overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp/q-norm. This framework elegantly connects convex tensor decomposition to the broader literature on structured sparsity, such as the overlapped and latent group Lasso. A core theoretical result is the establishment of a novel duality between these two classes of norms (Lemma 1), showing that the latent Sp*/q*-norm is the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm.

The main thrust of the paper is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor denoising. The authors prove its consistency (Theorem 1) and derive a non-asymptotic error bound (Theorem 2). The key insight from this analysis is that the mean squared error of the estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. This provides a formal justification for the empirical superiority of the latent approach over the overlapped approach, whose error bound is known to scale with the *sum* (or average) of the mode-k ranks. The analysis is then specialized to the case of Gaussian noise (Theorem 3).

Finally, the authors conduct numerical experiments to validate their theoretical findings. The results confirm that the empirical error of both the latent and overlapped methods scales precisely as predicted by their respective theoretical bounds. They also demonstrate that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach, especially when the ratio of the theoretical complexity measures is high.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant and Novel Theoretical Contribution:** This work provides the first rigorous theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition. The derived error bound, which depends on `min_k rk`, is a significant result that clearly distinguishes it from the overlapped approach and provides a strong theoretical basis for preferring the latent method in many scenarios.

2.  **Elegant Unifying Framework:** The formulation of the problem in terms of "overlapped" and "latent" structured Schatten norms is clear, elegant, and insightful. It successfully connects the recent line of work on convex tensor methods to the well-established literature on structured sparsity, providing a deeper understanding of the underlying principles. The duality result (Lemma 1) is a novel and technically sound contribution in its own right.

3.  **Explanation of Empirical Phenomena:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical explanation for previously observed empirical results (e.g., in [25]) where the latent approach outperforms the overlapped one, particularly for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode. The theory precisely captures that the latent approach behaves as if it adaptively identifies and exploits the mode with the lowest rank.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed and provide strong support for the paper's theoretical claims. Rather than simply showing one method is better, the experiments directly test the predicted scaling laws for both methods by plotting MSE against the derived complexity measures (TR and LR complexity). The excellent linear fit in Figure 2 is a powerful validation of the theory's accuracy.

5.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and logically structured. The motivation is clear, the technical concepts are introduced systematically, and the progression from the duality result to the main performance bounds is easy to follow.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Incoherence Assumption and Constrained Optimization:** The main results in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and require solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes additional spectral norm constraints.
    *   **Discussion:** While the authors correctly cite prior work using similar assumptions, the practical implications could be discussed in more detail. How restrictive is this assumption for real-world tensors? A brief discussion on the nature of tensors that might violate this assumption would be valuable.
    *   **Theory vs. Practice:** There appears to be a potential disconnect between the theory and the experiments. The experiments use an ADMM solver from [25], which was designed for the unconstrained problem (Eq. 5), not the constrained version (Eq. 11). Please clarify if the experimental implementation actually enforces the spectral norm constraints from Eq. 11. If not, the excellent fit between theory and experiments is even more interesting, suggesting the result might hold more generally, but this gap should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed.

2.  **Scope of Analysis (Tensor Completion):** The analysis is performed for the tensor denoising setting (fully observed tensor with additive noise). However, a primary motivation for low-rank tensor methods is tensor completion (recovering from partial observations). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of the challenges in extending this analysis to the completion setting, which is arguably of greater practical interest. While this is noted as future work, a more explicit discussion of the limitations of the current analysis in this regard would be helpful.

3.  **Comparison to Non-Convex Methods:** The paper does an excellent job comparing two convex approaches. However, the field of tensor decomposition has long been dominated by non-convex methods like alternating least squares (ALS). While convex methods offer theoretical guarantees, they can be computationally more expensive. A brief discussion or even a small experimental comparison against a standard non-convex baseline would help contextualize the significance of the results for the broader community and highlight the trade-offs between computational cost, statistical performance, and provability.

4.  **Calculation of LR Complexity:** The method for calculating the "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" for the experiments is slightly ad-hoc, as it involves running the algorithm on the ground truth tensor. This is reasonable for validating the theory, but it would be helpful to clarify that this is an oracle quantity used for verification purposes. Is there a more direct way to characterize the LR complexity of a given tensor `W*` without running an optimization?

### Final Assessment

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and novel contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The introduction of the structured Schatten norm framework is elegant, the duality result is novel, and the performance bounds for the latent approach provide crucial theoretical insights. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous proofs and validated by carefully designed experiments. The weaknesses identified are minor and relate mostly to the scope of the analysis and potential areas for clarification, rather than fundamental flaws in the work. The paper is well-written, impactful, and advances the state of the art in our understanding of convex methods for tensor analysis.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous performance analysis of the latent approach for convex tensor decomposition.
*   The error bound's dependence on the minimum mode-k rank is a key insight that explains its practical advantages.
*   The structured Schatten norm framework and the associated duality result are novel and of independent interest.
*   Strong empirical validation that directly supports the theoretical scaling laws.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on a potentially strong incoherence assumption, and there is a possible mismatch between the constrained problem in the theory and the unconstrained solver used in the experiments.
*   The analysis is limited to the denoising setting, not the more general tensor completion problem.

The arguments for acceptance are significantly stronger. This is a solid theoretical paper with clear results and good validation.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
