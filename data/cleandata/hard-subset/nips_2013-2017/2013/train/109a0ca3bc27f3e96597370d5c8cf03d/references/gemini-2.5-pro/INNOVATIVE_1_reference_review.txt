**Summary**

This paper investigates two convex relaxation approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes the sum of Schatten norms of matrix unfoldings across all modes, and the "latent" approach, which models the tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a single, specific mode. While the latent approach was previously proposed and shown empirically to outperform the overlapped method in certain cases, it lacked a theoretical foundation. The main contribution of this paper is to provide this missing theoretical analysis.

The authors introduce a unifying framework of "structured Schatten norms," drawing a powerful and novel analogy to the structured sparsity literature (e.g., overlapped vs. latent group lasso). Within this framework, they establish a key duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms. Leveraging this duality, they derive performance bounds for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. Their central theoretical result shows that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor. This contrasts sharply with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This finding provides a rigorous explanation for the previously observed superiority of the latent method, particularly when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper concludes with synthetic experiments that validate these theoretical scaling laws.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Contribution:** The paper's most significant strength is its conceptual reframing of the problem. By introducing the "structured Schatten norm" framework and proving the duality between the overlapped and latent norms, the authors provide a deep and elegant connection between convex tensor methods and the well-established field of structured sparsity. This is not an incremental improvement but a genuine conceptual advance that unifies disparate lines of research and offers a new lens through which to view these problems.

2.  **Significant Theoretical Insight:** The main theoretical result—that the latent approach's error bound depends on `min_k(rk)` while the overlapped approach's depends on `mean(rk)`—is a powerful and non-obvious finding. It provides a crisp, formal explanation for an empirical phenomenon and delivers a clear, actionable insight: the latent approach is inherently adaptive to the most compact representation of the tensor across its modes, without needing to know which mode that is in advance. This is a bold and important claim, well-supported by the analysis.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly defining the two approaches, and methodically building their theoretical argument from the duality property to the final performance bounds. The comparison with prior work is direct and insightful.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Reliance on Additional Assumptions:** The tighter theoretical bounds in Theorem 2 require an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and the introduction of explicit spectral norm constraints into the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While such assumptions are not uncommon in high-dimensional statistics to make analysis tractable, they do represent a slight departure from the simpler, unconstrained formulation. This is a minor methodological imperfection, but one worth noting.

2.  **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while well-designed to confirm the derived theoretical scaling, are purely synthetic. The paper would have been stronger if it demonstrated the practical advantage of the latent approach on a real-world dataset where the underlying rank structure is unknown and potentially anisotropic.

3.  **Scope Limited to Denoising:** The analysis is focused on the denoising setting. While important, a major application of these methods is tensor completion. An analysis of the latent approach's performance for completion is noted as future work but remains a key open question.

**Recommendation**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

This work exemplifies the kind of research that pushes the field forward. Instead of offering a minor tweak to an existing algorithm, it provides a foundational theoretical understanding of a promising approach. The central idea of establishing a duality between the two main convex tensor decomposition norms is bold and elegant, and the resulting performance bounds are significant, offering clear and practical guidance.

While the analysis has some technical dependencies (e.g., the incoherence assumption) and the experiments are not exhaustive, these are minor flaws in the context of the paper's core contribution. The conceptual novelty and the importance of the main theoretical result are outstanding. This paper provides a definitive answer to why and when the latent approach should be preferred, and it does so by building a beautiful theoretical bridge to the structured sparsity literature. It is a complete and insightful piece of work that other researchers will undoubtedly build upon.
