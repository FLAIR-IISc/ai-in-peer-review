This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution lies in rigorously analyzing the latent approach, demonstrating a performance bound scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank – a crucial insight explaining its empirical success in scenarios where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper also establishes a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature.

The work is highly appealing due to its boldness and focus on theoretical understanding of a practically relevant topic. Tensor decomposition is a cornerstone of many machine learning applications, and the shift towards convex relaxations is a significant trend. The authors don’t simply present another algorithm; they dissect existing approaches, identify a key limitation, and provide a strong theoretical justification for a superior alternative. The connection to structured sparsity and the demonstrated duality add further depth and theoretical elegance. While the paper isn’t necessarily *immediately* applicable (the theoretical bounds still involve constants and assumptions that might be hard to verify in practice), it provides a crucial foundation for future work and a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms at play.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The theoretical analysis of the latent approach, and particularly the bound scaling with the minimum rank, is a significant contribution. This isn’t incremental work; it fundamentally shifts our understanding of the latent approach's performance.
*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides formal proofs and a clear theoretical framework for the analysis, supported by established results in related fields. The duality result is particularly strong.
*   **Relevance:** Tensor decomposition is a widely applicable technique, making the findings potentially impactful across several domains.
*   **Clear Connection to Empirical Observations:** The theory neatly explains previously observed empirical results (Figure 1 in [25]) regarding the latent approach’s superiority in certain cases.
* **Well-structured and clear writing:** The paper is logically organized and the technical concepts are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on certain assumptions (incoherence, specific noise models) that may not always hold in real-world applications. The impact of violating these assumptions isn't fully explored.
*   **Practical Considerations:** While the theoretical bounds are strong, they contain constants that are not fully characterized, hindering direct translation to practical parameter tuning. Determining appropriate regularization constants in practice remains a challenge.
*   **Limited Empirical Validation:** The numerical results are somewhat limited in scope. While they support the theory, a more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets and tensor structures would strengthen the claims.
* **Missing Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed algorithm, which is important for assessing its practical feasibility.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical contribution regarding the scaling with the minimum rank is novel and insightful.
*   The established duality between the norms provides a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematical structure.
*   The paper addresses a fundamental question in tensor decomposition and provides a convincing answer.
*   The work represents a significant step towards a more principled understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The theoretical results rely on assumptions that may limit their applicability.
*   The empirical validation is somewhat limited.
*   The lack of discussion on computational complexity might hinder adoption.

**Overall Assessment:**

I strongly recommend **acceptance**. While the paper isn’t flawless, its novelty, theoretical depth, and potential impact far outweigh its weaknesses. The authors have tackled a challenging problem and provided a significant advance in our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The assumptions, while present, are not uncommon in this field, and the empirical results provide reasonable support for the theory. The limitations related to practical considerations and computational complexity are areas for future work but do not detract from the overall quality of this contribution. The paper introduces a genuinely new perspective and has the potential to influence future research in this area. This is the type of bold, theoretically driven work that NIPS should champion.
