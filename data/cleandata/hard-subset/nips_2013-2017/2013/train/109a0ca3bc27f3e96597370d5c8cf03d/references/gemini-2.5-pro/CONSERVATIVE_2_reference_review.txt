**Summary**

This paper investigates a convex optimization approach for noisy tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent" approach. This method models a tensor as a sum of K components, where each component is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode via the nuclear norm. This work builds upon recent developments in convex tensor factorization that primarily utilized an "overlapped" regularization scheme, where the nuclear norms of all matricizations of a single tensor are summed.

The main contributions are: (1) formalizing the "latent Schatten norm" and establishing a duality with the "overlapped Schatten norm," drawing a parallel to the latent and overlapped group Lasso literature; (2) providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach's denoising performance, showing that its mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor; and (3) presenting synthetic experiments that empirically validate this theoretical scaling behavior and compare it to the overlapped approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper's primary strength is its solid theoretical analysis. The establishment of a formal duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is an elegant result that connects this line of work to the broader field of structured sparsity.
2.  **Clear Motivation:** The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 3 and its comparison to the prior analysis of the overlapped method, provide a very clear and compelling explanation for why the latent approach should outperform the overlapped one when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes. The error bound's dependence on `min_k r_k` versus the overlapped method's dependence on the average of ranks is a sharp and insightful distinction.
3.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The concepts of overlapped and latent regularization are explained well, and the theoretical development is easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the limited scope of its empirical evaluation. The experiments are conducted exclusively on synthetic data. While these simulations are useful for verifying the derived theoretical scaling laws, they fail to provide evidence that the proposed method is practically useful on real-world problems. The central claim is that the latent approach is preferable for noisy tensor decomposition, yet its performance is not demonstrated on any real datasets from domains like neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, or environmental monitoring, which are mentioned as motivation in the introduction.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's empirical comparison is confined to the "overlapped" approach, which is another recent convex relaxation method. This is a very narrow comparison. For decades, the standard methods for tensor decomposition have been non-convex algorithms like Alternating Least Squares (ALS) for CANDECOMP/PARAFAC decomposition and Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD) for Tucker decomposition. A convincing argument for a new tensor decomposition method requires demonstrating its advantages over these widely used and well-understood baselines, especially in the noisy setting. Without such a comparison, the practical significance of the work remains unsubstantiated.
3.  **Practicality of the Model and Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis for the tighter bounds (Theorem 2 and 3) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a more complex optimization problem with additional spectral norm constraints (Eq. 11). The paper provides no discussion on the practical implications of this assumption or how one would choose the crucial hyperparameter `alpha` in practice. This makes the proposed estimator difficult to apply directly and raises questions about its robustness if the assumptions are not met or `alpha` is misspecified.
4.  **Limited Novelty of the Approach:** While the analysis is novel, the latent decomposition model itself was previously proposed by Tomioka et al. [25], as the authors acknowledge. The analytical techniques are also heavily borrowed from established frameworks for analyzing M-estimators with decomposable regularizers. Therefore, the contribution is more of a rigorous theoretical justification for a pre-existing idea, rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new approach.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a rigorous and technically sound analysis of the latent convex tensor decomposition model.
*   The theoretical finding that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank is a clear and important insight that advances our understanding of this class of estimators.
*   The duality result is a valuable theoretical contribution that unifies concepts from structured sparsity and tensor methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The work lacks empirical validation on real-world data, making it impossible to assess its practical significance.
*   The paper fails to compare its method against standard, established tensor decomposition algorithms (e.g., ALS, HOSVD), which is a critical omission for any work proposing a new method in this area.
*   The practical applicability of the proposed estimator is questionable due to additional assumptions and hyperparameters (`alpha`) that are not adequately discussed.
*   The overall contribution is more of an incremental theoretical analysis of a known model rather than a groundbreaking new method.

**Conclusion**

This is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical core. The analysis providing an error bound that scales with the minimum mode-k rank is a valuable contribution to the theory of convex tensor methods. However, the work falls short of the standards expected for a NIPS paper due to its profound lack of convincing empirical validation. The exclusive reliance on synthetic data and the failure to compare against established, non-convex workhorses in the field make the practical claims of the paper unsupported. While the theory is sound, the significance of the contribution is severely undermined by the absence of evidence that this approach offers a tangible benefit over existing methods on real-world problems. I therefore recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. The work would be much more compelling if it included a thorough empirical study on real datasets with comparisons to standard baselines.
