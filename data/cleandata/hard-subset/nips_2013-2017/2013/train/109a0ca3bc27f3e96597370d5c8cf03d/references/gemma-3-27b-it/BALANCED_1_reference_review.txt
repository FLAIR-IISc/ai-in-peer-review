This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the latent approach exhibits a superior scaling behavior in terms of mean squared error – scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank, as opposed to the sum of ranks in the overlapped approach. The authors also establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms and empirically validate their theoretical findings.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the challenges of traditional tensor decomposition and the recent shift towards convex relaxations. The connection to structured sparsity (group lasso) is clearly articulated, providing valuable context. The theoretical analysis is a significant contribution, offering a formal justification for the empirically observed advantages of the latent approach, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only a single mode. The presentation is generally clear and the paper is well-organized. The inclusion of numerical experiments provides further support for the theoretical results. The referencing is thorough and includes relevant works in tensor decomposition, convex optimization, and structured sparsity.

However, the paper does have some weaknesses. The notation, while standard for the field, could be more consistently applied. For example, the definition of the Frobenius norm and the dot product appear somewhat disconnected, and a more explicit connection could be made. The presentation of the duality result (Lemma 1) feels somewhat abrupt. While the proof is relegated to the appendix, a more intuitive explanation of *why* this duality holds would be helpful. The experimental section, while supportive, feels limited in scope. The experiments primarily focus on randomly generated low-rank tensors. It would be valuable to see results on real-world datasets to demonstrate the practical relevance of the latent approach. Furthermore, the discussion surrounding the choice of regularization parameter λ feels a bit hand-wavy. While the authors mention an optimal value independent of the ranks, a more detailed explanation of how this value is determined (or estimated) in practice would be beneficial. Finally, the discussion in Section 3.3 regarding Gaussian noise feels unnecessarily complex and the gains from this more refined analysis aren’t clearly highlighted.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the latent approach, establishing a performance bound that explains its advantages.
*   **Novelty:** The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a novel contribution.
*   **Clear Motivation and Context:** The paper clearly articulates the problem and positions the work within the broader literature.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments support the theoretical findings.
*   **Connection to Structured Sparsity:** The link to established concepts in structured sparsity strengthens the paper's contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Notational Consistency:** Some minor inconsistencies in notation.
*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** Primarily focuses on synthetic data.
*   **Regularization Parameter Discussion:**  Insufficient detail on practical selection of λ.
*   **Complex Gaussian Noise Analysis:**  The benefits of the refined analysis in Section 3.3 aren’t clear.
*   **Abrupt Duality Presentation:** Lemma 1 lacks intuitive explanation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel theoretical analysis with significant implications for the field of tensor decomposition.
*   The results convincingly explain the empirical superiority of the latent approach in certain scenarios.
*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The duality result is a valuable contribution to the understanding of structured Schatten norms.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The experimental validation is limited and lacks real-world applications.
*   Some aspects of the presentation could be improved for clarity and accessibility.
*   The added complexity of the Gaussian noise analysis doesn’t substantially enhance the paper’s core message.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution that warrants acceptance, despite its minor weaknesses. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and insightful, and the empirical validation, while limited, supports the main findings. The paper addresses a significant problem in tensor decomposition and offers a compelling solution. Addressing the points raised above, particularly expanding the experimental section with real-world data, would further strengthen the paper.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
