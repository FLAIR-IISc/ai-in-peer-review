This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex relaxation method for tensor decomposition, referred to as the "latent approach." This approach models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. This work is situated in the recent line of research on convex methods for tensor problems, which aim to overcome the local minima and difficult hyperparameter tuning associated with traditional non-convex methods like Alternating Least Squares (ALS) or Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD).

The primary contributions of this paper are theoretical. First, the authors formalize the regularizer used by the latent approach as a "latent Schatten norm" and establish a novel duality between this norm and the "overlapped Schatten norm" used in a competing convex approach. This duality elegantly connects the tensor decomposition problem to the broader literature on structured sparsity, particularly the overlapped and latent group lasso models. Second, and most importantly, the paper provides the first rigorous performance bounds for the latent approach in a noisy setting. The key result is that the mean squared error of the estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with the known bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the sum or average of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result provides a clear explanation for prior empirical work [25] that suggested the latent approach is superior when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode. Finally, the paper presents numerical experiments on synthetic data designed to validate these theoretical scaling laws.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Methodological Rigor:** The paper's main strength is its rigorous theoretical analysis. It provides a solid mathematical foundation for the latent decomposition method, an approach that previously only had empirical justification. The derivation of the duality between the norm structures is a valuable contribution, and the resulting error bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are a significant theoretical insight.

2.  **Clarity of Contribution:** The paper clearly identifies and addresses a gap in the existing literature. The derived error bound, which depends on `min_k r_k`, offers a compelling and intuitive explanation for the relative performance of the latent and overlapped approaches.

3.  **Connection to Established Frameworks:** By framing the problem in terms of structured Schatten norms and drawing parallels to structured sparsity, the authors place this work within a well-understood context. This helps to unify different lines of research on high-dimensional regularized estimation.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Method:** The core algorithm, the "latent approach," was previously proposed by Tomioka et al. [25]. The contribution of this paper is therefore limited to the theoretical analysis of this existing method. While a valuable contribution, it does not propose a new technique.

2.  **Reliance on Additional Assumptions and Constraints:** The main theoretical results (Theorems 2 and 3) hinge on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and the introduction of additional spectral norm constraints into the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While such assumptions are common in the analysis of these methods, they move the analyzed algorithm away from the simpler, more practical form originally proposed. The paper does not provide guidance on how to set the hyperparameter `alpha` associated with these constraints, nor does it discuss the sensitivity of the method to this choice. This weakens the practical relevance of the theoretical guarantees.

3.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The experiments are designed solely to confirm the derived theoretical scaling laws, which they do successfully. However, they are insufficient to establish the practical superiority of the method. The evaluation is limited to a comparison against a single, closely related convex baseline (the overlapped approach) on synthetic data. A thorough validation would require comparison against established and widely-used non-convex methods like ALS or Tucker decomposition, which are often highly effective in practice. Furthermore, the experiments do not include any real-world datasets, making it difficult to assess the significance of the approach for practitioners.

4.  **Questionable Practical Significance:** The authors themselves note that the performance improvements shown in their main experiment (Figure 2) are "milder" than in the motivating example (Figure 1). While the theoretical scaling is provably better, the experiments do not provide overwhelming evidence that this translates into a consistently large practical advantage over the simpler overlapped approach, let alone over traditional non-convex methods.

**Summary of Recommendation:**

This paper provides a solid, technically sound theoretical analysis of an existing method for convex tensor decomposition. The insights derived, particularly the error bound's dependence on the minimum mode-k rank, are a valuable contribution to the understanding of these models.

However, the paper's overall impact is limited by several factors. The method itself is not new, and the analysis requires additional constraints that complicate the original formulation. Most critically, the empirical evaluation is too narrow. Without a comprehensive comparison against standard, established baselines (such as ALS) on both synthetic and real-world data, the claims about the practical preference for the latent approach remain unsubstantiated. While the theoretical work is commendable, a NIPS paper should ideally demonstrate a more significant advance for the field, either through a novel method or through analysis that is more directly tied to practical, state-of-the-art performance.

Given the solid theoretical contribution, the paper is borderline. However, the lack of rigorous and comprehensive validation against established methods is a significant concern that prevents a strong endorsement.
