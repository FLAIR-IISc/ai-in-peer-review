This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex approach for tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent approach." This method models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. This stands in contrast to the more common "overlapped" approach, where a single tensor is penalized for being simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings.

The main contributions of this work are threefold. First, the authors formalize these two approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms," defining the overlapped and latent Schatten norms. Second, and most significantly, they establish a novel duality between these two norm families. This result is elegant and connects the recent work on convex tensor decomposition to the broader and more established literature on structured sparsity, particularly the duality between overlapped and latent group Lasso norms. Third, leveraging this duality, the paper provides the first rigorous performance guarantees for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The key theoretical finding is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor, whereas prior analysis showed that the error of the overlapped approach scales with the sum or average of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result provides a clear and compelling explanation for why the latent approach can dramatically outperform the overlapped one, especially when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. Finally, the paper includes numerical simulations on synthetic data that directly validate these theoretical scaling predictions.

**Strengths:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The core contribution—establishing the framework of structured Schatten norms and the duality between the latent and overlapped versions—is a beautiful and important theoretical result. It provides a new lens through which to understand convex regularizers for tensors and elegantly unifies this subfield with the principles of structured sparsity. This is not an incremental improvement but a genuine conceptual advance.

2.  **Major Theoretical Insight:** The paper resolves an important open question. The latent approach was proposed empirically in prior work [25], but without theoretical justification. This paper provides that justification in a very strong form. The finding that the latent approach's error scales with `min(r_k)` versus `sum(sqrt(r_k))` for the overlapped approach is a crucial insight. It demonstrates that the latent method implicitly adapts to the easiest structure in the data without being told which mode is low-rank. This is a powerful property and a significant step forward in our understanding of these methods.

3.  **Clarity and Quality of Exposition:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly defines the problem, introduces the necessary concepts, and builds its argument logically. The connection to the structured sparsity literature is explained well, making the work accessible to a broader audience. The theoretical claims are stated precisely, and the experiments are designed specifically to test and confirm the new theory, which they do convincingly.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The theoretical analysis is confined to the tensor denoising setting (i.e., recovery from additive Gaussian noise on a fully observed tensor). A major application area for tensor decomposition is tensor completion, where entries are missing. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the absence of completion results somewhat limits the immediate practical impact of the theory.

2.  **Technical Assumptions in the Analysis:** The tighter performance bounds (Theorem 2 and 3) rely on an incoherence assumption and the use of explicit spectral norm constraints in the optimization problem. These are common tools in high-dimensional analysis but can be seen as limitations, as their practical necessity or validity is not fully explored.

**Recommendation:**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper.

The weaknesses identified are minor in the context of the paper's contribution and are typical of foundational theoretical work. The lack of a tensor completion analysis is a clear avenue for future research, but it does not diminish the importance of the results presented for the denoising case. The primary value of this paper lies in its bold conceptual and theoretical contributions. The discovery of the duality between latent and overlapped Schatten norms is a significant and elegant piece of theory that fundamentally advances our understanding of convex tensor methods. It provides a crisp, rigorous explanation for a previously unexplained empirical phenomenon and convincingly argues for the superiority of the latent approach.

This is exactly the type of work that NIPS should champion: it introduces a novel and powerful idea, supports it with rigorous theory, and in doing so, has the potential to shift the direction of future research in the area. While the methodological validation is not exhaustive (e.g., no tensor completion, synthetic data only), the core idea is so strong and the supporting theory so compelling that these are acceptable limitations. This work prioritizes a significant new concept over incremental perfection, and for that, it deserves high praise.
