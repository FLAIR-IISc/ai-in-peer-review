This paper presents a compelling analysis of structured Schatten norm regularization for tensor decomposition, specifically contrasting an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution – a rigorous theoretical justification for the empirical success of the latent approach, coupled with a demonstration of duality between the corresponding norms – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. While the theoretical analysis relies on standard tools, its application to tensor decomposition, and the resulting insights regarding the scaling of error with minimal rank, represent a significant step forward. I particularly appreciate the clear connection drawn to structured sparsity literature and the acknowledgment of limitations, along with a thorough empirical validation.

**Summary:** The authors investigate convex relaxation techniques for tensor decomposition, focusing on two strategies: overlapping Schatten norm regularization and a novel latent approach. They establish a theoretical framework demonstrating the latent approach achieves error bounds scaling with the *minimum* mode rank, explaining its superior performance in scenarios where tensors are low-rank in only one mode. A duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is also proven, linking this work to broader structured sparsity concepts.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The primary strength lies in the theoretical analysis of the latent approach, providing a solid mathematical foundation for its empirical benefits. The duality result is also a valuable contribution, extending existing results in structured sparsity.
*   **Significance:** The identified scaling behavior – error dependent on the minimum rank – is a key insight. This directly addresses a limitation of the overlapped approach and suggests a preferable strategy for many practical applications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The definitions of the structured Schatten norms are clear, and the connection to existing work is well-established. The notation is consistent.
*   **Thoroughness:** The empirical validation reinforces the theoretical findings and demonstrates the latent approach’s effectiveness even in scenarios favorable to the overlapped approach. The inclusion of a detailed comparison and discussion of the limitations enhances credibility.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The deterministic bounds (Theorem 2) require an incoherence assumption (Equation 10), which, while common, could be a limiting factor in some applications. The discussion of its impact could be expanded.
*   **Practicality of Rank Estimation:** While the theoretical results are strong, accurately determining the minimal rank *a priori* might be challenging in practice. A brief discussion of practical methods for rank estimation could strengthen the paper.
*   **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** The experiments focus on Gaussian noise. Exploring performance under other noise distributions or in scenarios with missing data (mentioned in the introduction) would enhance the practical relevance of the findings.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper tackles a challenging problem in tensor decomposition with a novel and well-supported approach.
*   The theoretical results are significant and provide a clear explanation for the observed performance of the latent approach.
*   The connection to structured sparsity literature broadens the impact of the work.
*   The empirical validation is convincing.
*   The writing is clear and the paper is well-organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results rely on assumptions that might not always hold in practice.
*   The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope.
*   The paper doesn't fully address the practical challenges of rank estimation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a strong submission that offers a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The novelty of the theoretical analysis, combined with its practical implications and clear presentation, outweigh the minor weaknesses. While the assumptions and limited scope of experiments could be addressed in future work, the current work is sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance. I lean strongly towards accepting this paper, prioritizing its novel theoretical contributions and potential impact over methodological refinements. I would rate this as a **Strong Accept**.
