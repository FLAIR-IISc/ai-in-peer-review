### Summary

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach for convex tensor decomposition, contrasting it with the more established "overlapped" approach. The core idea of the latent approach is to model a tensor as a sum of K component tensors, `W = W(1) + ... + W(K)`, where each component `W(k)` is penalized for having a high nuclear norm only in its k-th unfolding. The authors' main contributions are threefold: 1) They introduce a unifying framework of "Structured Schatten Norms," formalizing the norms used by both the overlapped and latent methods and connecting them to the structured sparsity literature. 2) They establish a novel duality between the "overlapped Sp/q-norm" and the "latent Sp*/q*-norm," which is a key technical tool for their analysis. 3) They derive performance bounds for the latent approach in a denoising setting, proving that its mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor (`min_k r_k`), whereas the overlapped approach's error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result provides a rigorous explanation for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only a subset of its modes. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical experiments that validate the predicted scaling behavior of the error.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a crucial open question. While the latent approach was proposed empirically in [25], its theoretical properties were not understood. This paper provides the first rigorous performance guarantees, making a significant contribution to the theory of convex tensor methods. The main result—that the latent approach's error bound depends on `min_k r_k` while the overlapped approach's depends on `sum_k sqrt(r_k)`—is both elegant and impactful. It provides clear, actionable guidance on when to prefer one method over the other.

2.  **Novel and Unifying Framework:** The introduction of the "Structured Schatten Norms" framework is a strong conceptual contribution. It elegantly unifies the overlapped and latent approaches and establishes a clear parallel with the well-studied overlapped and latent group lasso norms in the structured sparsity literature. The duality result (Lemma 1) is novel and of independent interest, providing a powerful tool for analysis and potentially for developing new optimization algorithms.

3.  **Technical Soundness:** The proofs appear to be sound, skillfully adapting techniques from high-dimensional statistics and structured sparsity (e.g., from [1]) to the tensor setting. The derivation of the performance bounds via the dual norm is a standard but well-executed approach, and the resulting bounds are insightful.

4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The distinction between the overlapped and latent approaches is clearly motivated and defined. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed thoughtfully. The connection to prior work is well-established.

5.  **Targeted Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed. Instead of aiming for state-of-the-art performance on a benchmark task, they are specifically constructed to verify the theoretical scaling laws derived in Section 3. The linear relationship shown between MSE and the proposed "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" measures (Figure 2) provides strong empirical support for the paper's central theoretical claims.

### Weaknesses / Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter performance bound in Theorem 2 relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) with a parameter `alpha`. While the authors correctly note that this is a common technique in related literature, the practical implications could be discussed in more detail. How sensitive is the method to the choice of `alpha`? How restrictive is this assumption in practice? The paper would be strengthened by a discussion on whether this constraint is primarily a proof device or a necessary component for the algorithm to work well in practice.

2.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the denoising setting where the tensor is fully observed. A major application area for these methods is tensor *completion*, where entries are missing. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but the current analysis does not cover this important case. The techniques might not extend straightforwardly, as the analysis of missing data often requires stronger incoherence conditions (like those in matrix completion literature) on the singular vectors of the unfolded matrices. A brief comment on the potential challenges of extending the analysis to the completion setting would be valuable.

3.  **Clarity on Latent Rank Complexity:** In the experiments, the "LR complexity" is determined by running the algorithm on the ground truth tensor `W*` to find an optimal decomposition. This makes sense for verifying the theory but is not practically available. The paper rightly points out that the bound can be simplified to depend on `min_k r_k` by considering a singleton decomposition. It might be clearer to directly plot the MSE against `min_k r_k / n_K` to show that this simpler, more intuitive bound also holds, as this is the paper's key takeaway message.

### Overall Evaluation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a substantial and timely contribution to the literature on tensor decomposition. It provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for the latent approach, resolving a key question about its performance relative to the overlapped method. The introduction of the structured Schatten norm framework and the associated duality result are elegant contributions of broader interest. The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and its theoretical claims are convincingly validated by experiments. The weaknesses identified are relatively minor and do not detract from the overall significance of the work.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first and highly insightful theoretical analysis for the "latent" tensor decomposition method.
*   The `min_k r_k` error dependency is a clear and important result with practical implications.
*   The "Structured Schatten Norm" framework and the duality result are novel and connect tensor methods to the broader field of structured sparsity.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.
*   The experiments are well-designed to specifically support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on a potentially restrictive incoherence assumption for the tightest bounds.
*   The analysis is limited to the denoising case and does not cover tensor completion.

The strengths of this paper significantly outweigh its weaknesses. It presents a complete and polished piece of research that advances our understanding of convex tensor methods. I strongly recommend acceptance.
