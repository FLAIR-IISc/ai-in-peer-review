

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper introduces a theoretical framework for tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically comparing two approaches: the "overlapped approach" (using overlapped Schatten 1-norm) and the "latent approach" (using latent Schatten 1-norm). The authors establish a key duality between these approaches (Lemma 1), showing that the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm. Their main theoretical contribution is Theorem 3, which demonstrates that the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the tensor, while the overlapped approach scales with the average mode-k rank. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in previous work [25] and provides a theoretical foundation for its use in noisy tensor decomposition. The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature [1, 12, 17, 21], making it relevant to broader machine learning communities.

## Strengths

**Technical Quality and Theoretical Contribution (High):** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution with a novel duality result (Lemma 1) and tight error bounds (Theorems 1-3). The authors clearly identify that the key difference between the approaches is the scaling with minimum vs. average mode-k rank, which elegantly explains the empirical performance differences. The proofs are well-constructed and build logically from basic concepts. The paper addresses a fundamental gap in understanding why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach empirically.

**Clarity and Organization (High):** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors provide precise definitions, motivate their approach effectively, and build logically from foundational concepts to complex theoretical results. The connection to structured sparsity literature is particularly well-executed, making the work accessible to a broader audience. The experimental section effectively validates the theoretical claims with clear figures and analysis.

**Originality (High):** While tensor decomposition via convex optimization has been studied [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], the duality between overlapped and latent approaches and the theoretical analysis with respect to minimum mode-k rank is novel. The connection to structured sparsity literature [1, 12, 17, 21] is insightful and well-executed, placing the work in a broader context that will resonate with the NIPS community.

**Significance (High):** The results have clear practical implications for tensor decomposition problems, especially in noisy settings. The paper demonstrates that the latent approach is theoretically superior to the overlapped approach in a wide range of scenarios, providing practitioners with a better tool for tensor decomposition. The theoretical guarantees are tight and the experimental validation is thorough.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Experiments (Moderate):** The experiments focus on tensor completion with Gaussian noise but don't explore more challenging scenarios like missing data (which is common in tensor decomposition applications) or higher-order tensors (K > 3). While the theoretical results hold for any K, the experiments only consider 3-way tensors, which limits the generalizability of the empirical validation.

**Comparison to Recent Work (Moderate):** While the paper references [26] (which analyzes the overlapped approach), it could better address recent work on tensor decomposition, such as [19] (which provides lower bounds for tensor decomposition). The authors briefly mention [19] but could more thoroughly discuss how their results relate to these lower bounds.

**Practical Considerations (Moderate):** The paper focuses on theoretical guarantees but doesn't discuss computational complexity in detail. For large-scale tensors, the computational cost of the latent approach (which requires solving a minimization over decompositions) might be prohibitive compared to the overlapped approach. The paper mentions using ADMM but doesn't compare computational times.

## Arguments for Acceptance

- Strong theoretical contribution with novel duality result
- Clear explanation of why latent approach outperforms overlapped approach
- Well-motivated experiments that validate theoretical claims
- Connects tensor decomposition to broader structured sparsity literature
- Addresses an important problem in tensor analysis with practical implications
- Provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for the superiority of the latent approach

## Arguments Against Acceptance

- Limited scope of experiments (no missing data, only 3-way tensors)
- Could better connect to recent lower bound work [19]
- Doesn't thoroughly address computational costs

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The duality result (Lemma 1) is elegant and general, and the error bounds (Theorems 2-3) provide clear theoretical justification for the empirical success of the latent approach. The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the field of tensor analysis and structured sparsity.

The weaknesses noted are important but do not significantly undermine the main contribution. The experimental section could be strengthened by including more challenging scenarios, but the current experiments are sufficient to validate the theoretical claims. The connection to structured sparsity literature is particularly valuable as it places the work in a broader context that will be familiar to the NIPS community.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. It makes a clear theoretical contribution that is well-supported by both analysis and experiments, and it advances our understanding of tensor decomposition methods in a meaningful way.

**Recommendation:** Accept
