### Paper Summary

This paper investigates two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach, which regularizes the sum of Schatten norms of all matricizations of a tensor, and the "latent" approach, which models a tensor as a sum of several tensors, each being low-rank in a specific mode. While prior work introduced these methods and analyzed the overlapped approach, this paper provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting.

The authors' key contributions are threefold. First, they formalize both approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms," drawing a compelling analogy to structured sparsity literature (e.g., overlapped and latent group LASSO). Within this framework, they establish a novel and elegant duality between the overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm. Second, using this duality, they derive performance bounds for the latent approach estimator when recovering a tensor from noisy observations. Their main theoretical result shows that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the sum (or average) of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical finding provides a clear justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach, especially for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode. Third, they provide numerical experiments that validate their theoretical predictions, showing that the empirical MSE scales linearly with their derived complexity measure.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's primary strength is its rigorous and novel theoretical analysis. The establishment of the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a beautiful result that connects this line of work to the broader and well-understood field of structured sparsity. This provides a solid and elegant foundation for the subsequent analysis.

2.  **Clear Explanation of an Empirical Phenomenon:** The paper provides a definitive answer to why the latent approach can outperform the overlapped one. The core insight—that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k rk` while the overlapped approach's scales with `sum_k sqrt(rk)`—is both powerful and intuitive. It effectively shows that the latent method can adaptively identify and exploit the most "low-rank" mode without this information being provided explicitly.

3.  **Well-Structured and Clear Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. It starts by clearly motivating the problem, defines the necessary concepts in a structured manner, presents its theoretical results logically, and validates them with targeted experiments. The connection to structured sparsity is made explicit and helps readers familiar with that area to quickly grasp the core ideas.

4.  **Sound Empirical Validation:** The experiments in Section 4 are well-designed to specifically test the theoretical claims. By defining the "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" based on the theoretical bounds, the authors directly show the linear relationship between their theoretical complexity measures and the empirical MSE. This provides strong evidence for the correctness and relevance of their analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumptions and Constraints:** The tighter performance bound in Theorem 2 and the subsequent results rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and the inclusion of corresponding constraints in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While this is a standard technique in high-dimensional statistics (e.g., in matrix decomposition literature), it makes the proposed estimator slightly less practical and more complex than the original formulation. A discussion on the role of the parameter `α`, its practical selection, and the sensitivity of the results to this constraint would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Limited Scope (Denoising vs. Completion):** The analysis is confined to the tensor denoising setting, where the tensor is fully observed but corrupted by noise. A primary motivation for tensor decomposition, as mentioned in the introduction, is tensor completion from missing entries. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the absence of analysis for the completion setting is a notable limitation. It is not immediately clear if the `min_k rk` dependency would carry over to the sampling setting, which is arguably of greater practical interest.

3.  **Comparison to Non-Convex Methods:** The paper frames its motivation by contrasting convex methods with traditional non-convex approaches (like HOSVD/ALS). However, the subsequent analysis and experiments are purely a comparison between two convex relaxations. Recent years have seen a surge in theoretical analyses of non-convex methods, often showing they achieve optimal statistical rates under certain conditions (e.g., with good initialization). A brief discussion contextualizing the derived rates for the latent convex approach with what is known for non-convex algorithms would provide a more complete picture of the state of the art.

### Detailed Comments

*   The duality result in Lemma 1 is a cornerstone of the paper and is very well presented. It elegantly generalizes similar results from the vector (structured sparsity) setting to tensors.
*   The comparison between inequality (3) for the overlapped norm and Lemma 2 for the latent norm is the key theoretical insight and is very clearly explained. This is the "aha!" moment of the paper.
*   In the experimental section, the definition of LR complexity is based on an oracle decomposition of the true tensor `W*`. This is perfectly valid for verifying the theory, but it's worth clarifying that this quantity is not accessible in practice for model selection.
*   The results in Figure 2 are convincing. The fact that the scaling holds across different tensor sizes (blue and red series overlapping) is a strong confirmation of the theory.

### Summary of Recommendation

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant and elegant theoretical contribution to the literature on convex tensor decomposition. It provides the first rigorous analysis of the "latent" approach and uses it to deliver a clear, actionable insight: the latent approach is theoretically more robust to heterogeneous rank structures across modes and should be preferred over the "overlapped" approach in the noisy setting. The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and the conclusions are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The weaknesses, such as the focus on the denoising setting and the use of incoherence constraints, are acceptable limitations for a paper of this scope and are correctly identified as directions for future work.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Novel and important theoretical results (duality, `min_k rk` error bound).
*   Provides a clear explanation for a previously observed but unexplained empirical phenomenon.
*   Connects the tensor decomposition literature to the broader structured sparsity literature.
*   High quality of writing and presentation.
*   Strong empirical validation of the theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   Analysis is limited to the fully-observed denoising setting, not the more general completion setting.
*   The tighter bounds rely on additional constraints in the optimization problem that might limit practical applicability.

Overall, the strengths far outweigh the weaknesses. This paper is a solid contribution to the field and is well-suited for NIPS. I strongly recommend acceptance.
