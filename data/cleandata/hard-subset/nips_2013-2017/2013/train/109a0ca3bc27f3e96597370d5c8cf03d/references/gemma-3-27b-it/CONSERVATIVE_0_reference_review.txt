This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating the performance of the latent approach, showing its error bounds scale with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor – explaining its empirical superiority when low-rankness is localized to a single mode. The paper also establishes a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, connecting this work to broader structured sparsity literature.

The paper is generally well-written and the mathematical exposition is reasonably clear, though dense. The motivation for studying tensor decomposition is well-established, and the authors correctly position their work within the existing literature on convex relaxations for this problem. The connection to group lasso approaches is also a valuable observation, providing intuition. 

However, despite the claimed theoretical contributions, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing as a strong NIPS submission. My primary concern revolves around the limited novelty and the relatively weak empirical validation. While the theoretical analysis is presented as a justification for existing empirical observations (Figure 1 from [25]), it feels somewhat *post hoc*. The core idea of exploiting the minimum rank across modes seems intuitively sensible, but the theoretical justification feels overly complex given the simplicity of the observation it aims to explain. Furthermore, the theorems are largely asymptotic, relying on conditions (e.g., incoherence assumptions) that are not thoroughly discussed in relation to the experimental setup. 

The empirical evaluation (Section 4) is also insufficient. The experiments primarily serve to *confirm* the theoretical scaling, rather than to demonstrate a significant practical advantage of the latent approach over well-established alternatives beyond the specific scenario highlighted in the original [25] paper.  The comparison is largely limited to the overlapped approach. A more compelling evaluation would include comparisons against standard non-convex methods (e.g., alternating least squares) and other recent convex approaches.  The fact that the “optimal” regularization parameter is independent of the rank—while a positive result—isn’t explored in sufficient depth.  The limited scope of the experiments prevents me from being confident that the observed benefits generalize beyond the controlled settings used here.

The paper’s reliance on existing work – particularly [25] and [26] – creates a sense of incrementalism. While solid theoretical analysis is valuable, it needs to be paired with a more substantial demonstration of practical impact. The duality result between the norms, while interesting, feels somewhat abstract and its direct implications for algorithm design or analysis are not fully explored.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, providing a justification for its observed performance.
*   Clear connection to established concepts in structured sparsity and convex optimization.
*   Demonstration of the theoretical scaling through numerical experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited novelty; primarily a theoretical justification for previously observed empirical results.
*   Insufficient empirical validation, lacking comparisons to standard non-convex methods and a wider range of convex alternatives.
*   Theorems rely on potentially strong assumptions without detailed discussion.
*   Incremental contribution—the paper feels more like an analysis of existing methods than a breakthrough innovation.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While technically sound and well-written, the paper does not present a sufficiently novel or significant contribution to warrant acceptance to NIPS. The theoretical results, while interesting, feel largely confirmatory, and the empirical validation is inadequate to demonstrate a compelling practical advantage. I would be more receptive to a revised version that included a more comprehensive empirical evaluation and a clearer articulation of the practical implications of the theoretical findings.
