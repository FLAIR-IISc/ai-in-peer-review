

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates structured Schatten norm regularization for tensor decomposition, introducing two approaches: the overlapped approach and the latent approach. The authors claim the latent approach is theoretically and empirically superior to the overlapped approach, particularly when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The main contribution is establishing a duality between the two structured norms and proving error bounds for the latent approach that scale with the minimal mode-k rank rather than the average rank.

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity literature, which is valuable for the broader machine learning community. The theoretical results (Theorems 1-3) are well-motivated, and the numerical experiments (Section 4) generally support the theoretical claims. The duality result (Lemma 1) is a nice theoretical contribution that generalizes existing results in structured sparsity.

## Weaknesses

1. **Notational Inconsistency**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for mode-k unfolding. At times it's written as W^(k) (equation 1), sometimes as W^{(k)} (equation 2), and sometimes as W^(k)(k) (equation 4). This creates confusion about whether the second superscript refers to the mode or the unfolding operation. The paper should standardize this notation throughout.

2. **Technical Error in Theorem 3**: In Theorem 3, the regularization constant λ includes the term √n1, which appears to be a typo. The spectral norm of a mode-k unfolding matrix (n_k × N/n_k) should be related to √(n_k N/n_k) = √N, not √n1. This error undermines the theoretical result and needs correction.

3. **Incorrect Duality Statement**: Lemma 1 claims the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm is the latent Sp*/q*-norm. However, the standard definition of dual norm requires ⟨X,Y⟩ ≤ |X|_p |Y|_q for dual norms p and q. The paper does not verify this fundamental property, making the duality claim incomplete.

4. **Formatting Issues**: 
   - The URL in Section 4 ("https://github.com/ryotat/tensor.") ends with a period, making it a broken link.
   - The paper inconsistently uses "Schatten 1-norm" and "Schatten 1-norm" (with or without space).
   - The phrase "the mean squared error of the latent approach scales no greater than the minimum mode-k rank" (in the abstract) is misleading. The error scales with the minimal rank, not that the error is bounded by the rank itself.

5. **Minor Technical Oversight**: In Theorem 2, the bound (12) states "∑|Ŵ(k) - W*(k)|_F^2 ≤ cλ^2 ∑r_k" but does not clarify that r_k refers to the mode-k rank of W*(k), not W*. This is critical for understanding the bound.

6. **Lack of Comparison**: The paper claims the latent approach "performs better in many cases" but does not provide a clear comparison of computational complexity between the two approaches. This is a significant omission for practitioners.

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically interesting connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, and the main results are generally sound. However, the technical errors, notational inconsistencies, and formatting issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility. The typo in Theorem 3 is particularly concerning as it directly impacts the theoretical results. While the main contribution is novel and relevant, the paper fails to meet the high technical standards expected at NIPS due to these avoidable issues.

The paper is not fundamentally flawed, but the cumulative effect of these minor but pervasive errors makes it difficult to fully trust the theoretical claims. The authors should carefully revise the notation, correct the error in Theorem 3, and verify the duality claim before considering this work for publication.

## Recommendation

**Reject**

The paper's theoretical contribution is valuable, but the technical flaws and notational inconsistencies are too numerous to overlook. The errors in Theorem 3 and the incomplete duality statement are particularly concerning for a NIPS submission, which demands mathematical precision. While the authors have made a meaningful contribution to the field, the current version does not meet the standards of rigor expected at NIPS.
