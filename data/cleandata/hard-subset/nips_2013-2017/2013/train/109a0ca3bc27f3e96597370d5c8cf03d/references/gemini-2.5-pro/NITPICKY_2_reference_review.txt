### **Summary**

This paper investigates a convex optimization approach for tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent approach." This method models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is constrained to be low-rank in a specific mode. The authors formalize this by defining a "latent Schatten norm" and establish a novel duality relationship with the "overlapped Schatten norm" used in prior work. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the denoising performance of the latent approach. The authors prove that its mean squared error scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the underlying true tensor. This contrasts favorably with the overlapped approach, whose error is known to scale with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical finding provides a compelling explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach in cases where a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. Numerical experiments are presented to validate these theoretical scaling laws.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first theoretical performance guarantee for the latent approach to tensor decomposition. The main result—that the error scales with `min_k r_k` rather than an average of ranks—is both novel and important. It elegantly explains why this method is preferable in certain scenarios and provides a solid theoretical foundation for what was previously just an empirical observation.

2.  **Unifying Framework:** The work successfully connects convex tensor decomposition methods to the broader literature on structured sparsity. By defining overlapped and latent Schatten norms and proving their duality, the authors create a unifying framework that mirrors developments in structured vector norms (e.g., overlapped/latent group LASSO). This provides valuable conceptual clarity.

3.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated by a clear and well-understood limitation of the existing "overlapped" convex approach. The comparison between the two methods is central to the paper and effectively highlights the significance of the contributions.

### **Weaknesses**

Despite the interesting core contributions, the paper suffers from a significant number of flaws, ranging from notational inconsistencies to critical methodological omissions that undermine the credibility of the experimental validation.

1.  **Clarity and Notational Precision:**
    *   In Section 2.2, the text introduces the latent S1/1-norm as `|||W|||~S1/1` (with a tilde), but Equation (6) defines the same quantity as `|||W|||_S1/1` (without a tilde). This notational inconsistency for a central object of study is confusing.
    *   The paper introduces general overlapped Sp/q-norms in Equation (2), but the subsequent theoretical development and experiments focus exclusively on the S1/1 and S∞/∞ norms. The introduction of the general case adds clutter without providing any payoff, and the paper would be more streamlined by focusing only on the relevant norms.

2.  **Critical Methodological Omissions in Experiments:**
    *   The main theoretical result (Theorem 2) and the corresponding optimization problem used in the experiments (Equation 11) rely on an incoherence assumption and an explicit constraint of the form `||W(l)(k)||_S∞ ≤ α`. The parameter `α` is a crucial hyperparameter of the model. However, the manuscript provides **no information whatsoever** on how `α` was chosen or set in the numerical experiments. This is a major methodological flaw that makes the experiments entirely non-reproducible and casts doubt on the validity of the results presented in Figure 2. Without knowing how `α` was handled, it is impossible to assess whether the experimental setup faithfully reflects the theory it purports to validate.

3.  **Disconnect Between Theory and Practice/Experiments:**
    *   The authors claim in Section 4 that "the optimal choice of the regularization constant is independent of the rank of the true tensor and only depends on the size; this agrees with the condition on λ in Theorem 3." This statement is a misinterpretation of their own theory. The theoretical choice for `λ` in Theorem 3 explicitly depends on `α(K-1)`. Since `α` (defined in Eq. 10) is a property of the true underlying tensor components `W*(l)`, the theoretically optimal `λ` is *not* independent of the ground truth. The experimental finding that a fixed `λ` works well across different ranks is simply an empirical result from a grid search, and connecting it to Theorem 3 in this way is misleading.
    *   The procedure for calculating the "Latent rank (LR) complexity" (Eq. 18) for the plots is convoluted. It requires running the algorithm on the noiseless ground truth `W*` to find an optimal decomposition that minimizes the complexity term. This feels like an ad-hoc procedure designed to produce a clean plot, rather than a straightforward validation of the theory. The bound in Eq. (15) depends on `min_k r_k`, which would have been a more direct and transparent quantity to plot against.

4.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   The introduction of unfolding via a footnote ("See Section 2") is unhelpful for a reader unfamiliar with tensors. A citation to a standard reference (e.g., Kolda & Bader, 2009) would be more appropriate.
    *   The right panel of Figure 2 is claimed to show that the relative success of the latent approach is "correlated with high TR complexity to LR complexity ratio." This is a qualitative observation; a quantitative correlation analysis would make this point more convincingly.

### **Decision**

While the paper presents a valuable and novel theoretical analysis, the execution is marred by a substantial number of flaws. The lack of clarity in notation is a minor annoyance, but the methodological omissions regarding the hyperparameter `α` are a critical failure that makes the experimental section irreproducible and its conclusions unsupported. Furthermore, the misrepresentation of the theoretical result for `λ` demonstrates a lack of care in connecting the theory to the experiments. In its current state, the paper does not meet the high standards of technical soundness and careful evaluation expected for NIPS. I therefore recommend **rejection**. The authors have a strong theoretical core, and I would encourage them to resubmit after thoroughly addressing these significant issues, particularly by providing a complete and reproducible experimental methodology.
