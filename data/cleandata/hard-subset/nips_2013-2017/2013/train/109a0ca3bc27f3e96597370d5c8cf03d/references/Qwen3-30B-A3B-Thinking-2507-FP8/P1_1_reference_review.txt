

# Peer Review for "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary of Main Ideas

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition by rigorously analyzing two convex optimization approaches: the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors introduce structured Schatten norms to formalize these approaches and establish a key duality between the overlapped Schatten norm and the latent Schatten norm (Lemma 1). The main theoretical contribution is showing that the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach's error scales with the average (square root) of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in previous work [25], particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode (as shown in Figure 1).

The paper connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature (e.g., group lasso), showing how the two approaches correspond to the overlapped and latent group sparsity paradigms. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach (Theorems 1-3) and validate their analysis with numerical experiments (Figure 2) that confirm the predicted scaling behavior.

This work builds upon recent advances in convex tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] but provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. It connects to broader structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21] while making specific contributions to tensor analysis.

## Strengths

### Quality
- **Theoretical rigor**: The paper provides complete theoretical analysis with well-structured proofs of key lemmas and theorems (Theorems 1-3). The duality result (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and general.
- **Empirical validation**: The experiments (Figure 2) confirm the theoretical predictions with clear linear scaling of MSE against the predicted complexity measures (TR and LR complexity).
- **Comprehensive analysis**: The paper carefully distinguishes between the noiseless and noisy settings, and between consistency and deterministic bounds.
- **Honest evaluation**: The authors acknowledge limitations, noting that their analysis is for denoising rather than tensor completion (which was the focus of [25, 26]).

### Clarity
- **Well-organized**: The paper flows logically from background to definitions, to theory, to experiments.
- **Clear notation**: The definitions of overlapped and latent Schatten norms are well-explained with appropriate notation.
- **Effective use of figures**: Figure 1 effectively illustrates the conceptual difference between approaches, while Figure 2 provides strong empirical support for the theoretical claims.
- **Supplementary material**: The mention of detailed proofs in the supplementary material indicates the paper is technically complete.

### Originality
- **Novel duality result**: The connection between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a significant theoretical contribution that generalizes previous work on structured sparsity.
- **Theoretical explanation for empirical observations**: The paper provides the first rigorous explanation for why the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach in certain settings (Figure 1).
- **Advancement beyond previous work**: While [25, 26] demonstrated the empirical success of the latent approach, this paper provides the theoretical foundation that was previously missing.

### Significance
- **Practical implications**: The results provide clear guidance for practitioners on which approach to use (latent is preferable in most cases).
- **Theoretical advancement**: The paper advances the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods.
- **Broader connections**: The work connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, making it relevant to a wider audience in machine learning.
- **Clear explanation of limitations**: The authors properly frame their contribution within the context of previous work (noting the focus on denoising rather than completion).

## Weaknesses

### Quality
- **Limited scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on the denoising setting, while previous work [25, 26] focused on tensor completion. The authors should explicitly address how their results might extend to completion.
- **Computational considerations**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the two approaches, which is important for practical implementation.
- **Assumptions**: The incoherence assumption (10) in Theorem 2 might be restrictive in some applications.

### Clarity
- **Technical depth**: Some of the more technical results (e.g., the proof of Lemma 1) might be challenging for readers without a strong background in optimization.
- **Comparison with other methods**: The paper compares only the two approaches (overlapped vs. latent) but doesn't compare against other tensor decomposition methods (e.g., Tucker decomposition, CP decomposition).

### Originality
- **Limited novelty in tensor decomposition**: While the duality result is novel, the overall framework for tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms builds directly on previous work [9, 15, 23, 25, 26].

### Significance
- **Narrow focus**: The paper's results are most relevant when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode, which might not be the most common scenario in practice.
- **Practical implementation details**: The paper doesn't provide guidance on how to choose the regularization constant λ in practice, though it does provide theoretical guidance.

## Arguments for and against Acceptance

### Arguments for Acceptance
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by providing the first rigorous explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach.
- The duality result (Lemma 1) is elegant and generalizes to structured sparsity literature.
- The theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by empirical evidence.
- The paper advances the field by connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity, which is a well-established area in machine learning.
- The results have clear practical implications for practitioners working with tensor decomposition.

### Arguments against Acceptance
- The focus on denoising rather than tensor completion limits the scope of the contribution (tensor completion was the main focus of previous work [25, 26]).
- The paper doesn't provide sufficient discussion of computational complexity, which is crucial for practical implementation.
- The experimental section could have included more comparisons with other tensor decomposition methods.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The paper provides a rigorous explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach, which has been observed in previous work but lacked theoretical foundation. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is particularly elegant and general, connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and provides both theoretical and empirical validation of its claims. While the focus on denoising rather than tensor completion is a limitation, this is a reasonable starting point for the theoretical analysis, and the authors acknowledge this limitation.

The significance of the work is high, as it provides a clear theoretical foundation for practitioners to choose between these two approaches in tensor decomposition. The results explain why the latent approach is generally preferable, which has important practical implications.

## Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper at NIPS. The theoretical contribution is significant, the analysis is rigorous, and the results provide valuable insights that advance the field. The paper is well-written and makes a clear contribution to the literature on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

I suggest the authors address the following minor points in the camera-ready version:
1. Add a brief discussion of how their results might extend to tensor completion (even if only as a future work direction).
2. Provide a more detailed comparison with other tensor decomposition methods in the experimental section.
3. Include a discussion of computational complexity for the two approaches.

These would strengthen the paper without requiring major revisions.
