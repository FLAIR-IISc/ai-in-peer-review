### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a theoretical and empirical investigation into two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach and the "latent" approach. The central contribution is a rigorous performance analysis of the latent approach, which was previously shown to be empirically effective but lacked theoretical backing. The authors model both approaches using a framework of "structured Schatten norms." They introduce the overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm and establish a novel duality between them, drawing a parallel to the well-studied overlapped and latent group lasso norms in structured sparsity.

The main theoretical result is a denoising error bound for the latent approach. The authors prove that the mean squared error of the estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, which depend on the sum or average of the mode-k ranks. This key finding provides a clear theoretical explanation for why the latent approach is superior when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes. The paper concludes with numerical experiments that validate these theoretical scaling laws, confirming that the error of the latent method scales with the proposed "Latent rank (LR) complexity."

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper fills a crucial gap in the literature by providing the first theoretical performance guarantee for the latent tensor decomposition method. The result that the error bound depends on `min_k r_k` rather than `sum_k sqrt(r_k)` is both elegant and highly impactful, providing clear justification for using this method.
2.  **Novel Unifying Framework:** The introduction and analysis of structured Schatten norms and their duality is a strong conceptual contribution. It elegantly connects the tensor decomposition literature with the broader and more mature field of structured sparsity, potentially opening up avenues for cross-pollination of ideas.
3.  **Strong Explanatory Power:** The theory presented doesn't just bound the error; it provides a crisp explanation for previously observed empirical phenomena (as in the paper's Figure 1). This is a hallmark of high-quality theoretical work.
4.  **Careful Empirical Validation:** The experiments are well-designed and directly test the predictions of the theory. By defining the "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" measures, the authors provide a clear visual confirmation (Figure 2) that the MSE scales precisely as predicted by their analysis and prior work. This adds significant credibility to the theoretical claims.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter performance bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and the inclusion of an additional spectral norm constraint in the optimization objective (Eq. 11). The practical implications of this assumption and the selection of the parameter `alpha` are not fully explored. While common in related literature, a discussion on the restrictiveness of this assumption would be beneficial.
2.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments are focused on the denoising setting with i.i.d. Gaussian noise and fully observed tensors. While this perfectly matches the theoretical setting, the introduction also motivates the work with applications like tensor completion. Demonstrating the method's performance on a completion task, even without a corresponding theorem, would have strengthened the paper's practical significance.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Non-Convex Methods:** The paper's narrative is centered on the comparison between two convex relaxations. However, traditional non-convex methods (e.g., ALS for Tucker/CP decomposition) are still widely used and often perform well. A brief experimental comparison could provide important context for practitioners on when these convex methods are preferable to the classical, often faster, non-convex alternatives.

### Detailed Evaluation

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development, particularly the duality in Lemma 1 and the subsequent error analysis, appears sound (pending verification of the proofs in the appendix). The claims are well-supported by both the theoretical derivations and the targeted numerical experiments. The authors are honest about the assumptions required for their main results, such as the incoherence property. The work is a complete contribution that successfully bridges an empirical observation with a rigorous theoretical explanation.

**Clarity:** The paper is very clearly written and well-organized. The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches is established early and maintained throughout. The notation is consistent, and the definitions of the structured norms are precise. Section 2 does an excellent job of setting up the mathematical framework, and Section 3 logically progresses from a general consistency result to the main, tighter bounds. The connection between the theoretical bounds (Eqs. 15 and 16) and the experimental validation (Figure 2) is explicit and easy to follow.

**Originality:** This work is highly original. While the latent approach itself was proposed in [25], it lacked any theoretical analysis. This paper provides the first such analysis, which is a novel and substantial contribution. Furthermore, the framework of structured Schatten norms and the proof of their duality is a novel conceptual contribution that unifies ideas from different fields. This work is clearly distinct from previous NIPS papers (e.g., [26]) that analyzed the overlapped approach; in fact, it directly builds upon and provides a compelling alternative to that work.

**Significance:** The results are significant for both researchers and practitioners in fields that use tensor decompositions. The key takeaway—that the latent approach automatically adapts to the "easiest" (lowest-rank) mode without prior knowledge—is a powerful insight. This provides clear guidance on which convex method to use, especially for datasets that might be high-rank in some modes but low-rank in others (a plausible real-world scenario). The connection to structured sparsity may also inspire new regularizers and algorithms for tensor problems. This work significantly advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of convex tensor methods.

**Final Recommendation:**
This is an excellent paper that combines novel theory with strong empirical validation. It solves an open problem by providing the first performance guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition method, and in doing so, delivers a clear and important message to the community. The conceptual framework of structured Schatten norms is an elegant contribution in its own right. The weaknesses are minor and relate mostly to the scope of the experiments and discussion of assumptions, which do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
