### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex approach to tensor decomposition, referred to as the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each being low-rank in a specific mode. This stands in contrast to the more established "overlapped approach," which regularizes a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  It formally establishes a framework of "structured Schatten norms" that encompasses both the overlapped and latent approaches.
2.  It proves a novel and elegant duality between the overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm, providing a principled connection between the two methods and linking them to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped/latent group LASSO).
3.  It derives the first theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key result is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor.
4.  This theoretical result sharply contrasts with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.
5.  The paper includes numerical experiments on synthetic data that are specifically designed to, and successfully do, validate these theoretical scaling laws.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper's main contribution—showing that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k r_k`—is a strong and important result. It provides a clear, quantitative explanation for the performance difference between the two main convex tensor decomposition methods. This moves the field from empirical observation to theoretical understanding.
2.  **Elegant Duality Framework:** The formulation of overlapped and latent Schatten norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is a novel and insightful contribution. It unifies these two approaches under a common theoretical umbrella and elegantly connects tensor decomposition to well-studied concepts in structured sparsity. This is a high-quality theoretical result in its own right.
3.  **High Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, precisely define the concepts, and logically build their argument. The comparison between the overlapped and latent approaches is a consistent theme that makes the paper's message very clear.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments are not merely a demonstration of superiority but a careful validation of the derived theoretical rates. The definition and use of "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" to plot the MSE directly tests the scaling laws predicted by the theory (Eqs. 14-18), providing compelling evidence for the analysis's correctness.
5.  **Completeness:** The analysis is thorough, providing a consistency result (Theorem 1), a deterministic bound under an incoherence assumption (Theorem 2), and a final rate for the practical Gaussian noise case (Theorem 3). This constitutes a complete piece of theoretical work.

**Weaknesses (Suggestions for Improvement):**

1.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter performance bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and an explicit spectral norm constraint in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While such assumptions are common in high-dimensional statistics, the paper could benefit from a brief discussion on the practical implications of this. For instance, how restrictive is this assumption, and how would one set the parameter `α` in practice?
2.  **Scope of Comparison:** The paper provides a very strong comparison between two convex methods. However, it largely omits a comparison to traditional non-convex methods (e.g., ALS for Tucker decomposition). While a full empirical comparison is outside the scope, a brief discussion in the introduction or conclusion about the relative merits (e.g., statistical guarantees vs. scalability and practical performance) would provide valuable context for the reader.
3.  **Limited to Denoising:** The analysis is performed for the tensor denoising setting where all entries are observed. The authors correctly identify tensor completion (with missing entries) as an important direction for future work. This is not a flaw in the current work but a clear limitation of its scope.

### Detailed Comments on Review Criteria

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by the provided lemmas and theorems (with proofs deferred to the appendix). The analysis is rigorous and builds upon modern techniques in high-dimensional statistics. The experimental design is sound and effectively corroborates the theoretical findings. The work is complete and self-contained.

**Clarity:** The paper's clarity is excellent. The authors have done a superb job of explaining complex mathematical concepts in an intuitive way. The introduction sets up the problem perfectly, and Figure 1 provides a compelling visual motivation. The distinction between the overlapped and latent norms is clearly articulated both mathematically and intuitively. The paper is a pleasure to read.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the latent approach algorithm was proposed in a technical report [25], this paper provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance. The key original contributions are the structured Schatten norm framework, the duality result, and the `min_k r_k` error bound. These results are novel and significantly advance the understanding of convex relaxations for tensor problems. The connection to structured sparsity is also a novel and valuable contribution.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the machine learning community interested in tensor methods and high-dimensional statistics. The paper effectively resolves the question of which convex relaxation to prefer for tensor decomposition, providing strong theoretical and empirical evidence in favor of the latent approach. The clean and interpretable error bounds are likely to be influential and built upon in future research on tensor completion, robust tensor PCA, and other related problems. This work advances the state of the art in the theoretical understanding of tensor methods.

### Final Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. It is technically sound, exceptionally well-written, and its findings are both important and impactful. I strongly recommend acceptance.
