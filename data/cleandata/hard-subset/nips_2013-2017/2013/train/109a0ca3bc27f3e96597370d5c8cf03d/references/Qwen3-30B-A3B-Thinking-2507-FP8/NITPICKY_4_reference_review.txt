

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper proposes a framework for tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, focusing on the duality between two approaches: the "overlapped" approach (which penalizes all modes to be jointly low-rank) and the "latent" approach (which decomposes the tensor into components each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors establish theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, showing that its error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. The paper connects these tensor decomposition techniques to structured sparsity literature and provides empirical validation.

**Strengths:**
The paper makes a theoretically interesting contribution by establishing a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes previous work on structured sparsity. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-3) provides clear bounds on the denoising performance, with the key insight that the latent approach's error scales with the minimal mode-k rank. The numerical experiments generally support the theoretical claims, showing the expected scaling behavior with the LR complexity.

**Critical weaknesses:**

1. **Notational inconsistencies:** The paper suffers from significant notational confusion throughout. For instance, in Lemma 1, the dual norm is defined as ||X||_{S_{p*}/q*} = inf_{X^(1)+...+X^(K)=X} (sum ||X^(k)(k)||_{q*}^{p*})^{1/q*}, but the notation X^(k)(k) is ambiguous - is this the mode-k unfolding of X^(k)? The paper uses inconsistent notation for mode-k unfolding (W^(k) vs. W^(k)(k)), which makes the definitions hard to follow. In Theorem 2, the authors switch between r̄_k (defined as rank(W^(k)(k))) and r_k without clear explanation.

2. **Theoretical inconsistencies:** Theorem 1 claims consistency for the latent approach, but the condition λ ≥ ||E||_{S∞/∞} uses the overlapped S∞/∞ norm, which is inconsistent with the latent approach being analyzed. The dual norm of the latent S1/1-norm is correctly identified as the overlapped S∞/∞-norm in Lemma 1, but this isn't properly reflected in the theorem statement.

3. **Unjustified simplifications:** In Section 4, the authors state "We have ignored terms like √(nk/N) because they are negligible for nk ≈ 50 and N ≈ 50,000" without justification or analysis of the error introduced by this simplification. This is particularly problematic for a paper claiming theoretical rigor.

4. **Empirical validation gaps:** The paper claims "the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly against the LR complexity (18) as predicted by Theorem 3," but Figure 2 shows the relationship is not perfectly linear. The paper doesn't report the exact scaling factors or R² values to quantify this relationship. The LR complexity definition (18) uses r̄_k (ranks of the latent factors), but the experiments use "the minimum of the sum of ranks found by the run and min_k r_k," which is inconsistent with the theoretical definition.

5. **Missing comparison:** The paper compares only the overlapped and latent approaches but doesn't compare with other state-of-the-art tensor decomposition methods (e.g., non-convex methods like Tucker decomposition or other convex approaches). This limits the significance of the empirical claims.

6. **Formatting and reference issues:** The reference list contains inconsistent formatting (e.g., "SIAM J. Matrix Anal. Appl.,, , ,") and some entries are missing key information. The paper also claims "the solver is available online" but doesn't provide the actual URL in the main text.

**Recommendation:**

While the paper presents an interesting theoretical connection between structured sparsity and tensor decomposition, the numerous technical inconsistencies, notational confusion, and unjustified simplifications undermine the rigor of the theoretical claims. The empirical validation lacks sufficient detail to fully support the theoretical predictions, and the paper fails to compare with other relevant approaches. These issues are substantial enough to warrant rejection, as they significantly impact the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

The paper has potential but requires significant revision to address these technical issues before it could be considered for publication. The authors should carefully revise the notation, provide full theoretical justification for all simplifications, and strengthen the empirical validation with more rigorous analysis of the scaling behavior.
