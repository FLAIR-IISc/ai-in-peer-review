This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, a method that models a tensor as a sum of components, each of which is low-rank in a specific mode. The authors contrast this with the more common "overlapped approach," which encourages a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its modes.

The key contributions of the paper are:
1.  It formalizes the regularizers used by both approaches as "overlapped" and "latent" structured Schatten norms and establishes a novel duality between them. This elegantly connects the tensor decomposition problem to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs. latent group Lasso).
2.  It derives a statistical performance bound for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The main theoretical result is that the mean squared error of the latent approach estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor.
3.  This result is contrasted with prior work [26] on the overlapped approach, where the error scales with the sum (or average) of the square root of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach when the true tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes.
4.  The paper includes numerical experiments that are carefully designed to verify the derived theoretical scaling laws, showing a strong correspondence between the empirical mean squared error and the proposed theoretical complexity measures.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Theoretical Significance:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution. The duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is an elegant result that unifies the two main convex approaches to tensor decomposition and links them to well-understood concepts in structured sparsity. This provides a new and insightful perspective on these methods.

2.  **Clarity of the Main Result:** The core message is clear and impactful. The finding that the latent approach's performance depends on the `min(r_k)` while the overlapped approach's depends on `sum(sqrt(r_k))` provides a simple and powerful explanation for their differing behaviors. It formalizes the intuition that the latent method can adaptively find the "easiest" low-rank structure in the tensor, a property the overlapped method lacks.

3.  **Well-Designed Experiments:** The empirical validation is a major strength. Rather than just showing that one method outperforms another on a few examples, the authors design experiments to explicitly test the theoretical scaling laws (Theorems 3 and Eq. 16). Plotting the MSE against the proposed "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" measures provides compelling evidence for the correctness of the theoretical analysis.

4.  **Importance of the Problem:** Tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem in machine learning and data analysis. Providing a rigorous theoretical understanding of when and why a particular method should be preferred is of great value to both researchers and practitioners. This work advances the state of the art in the theory of convex tensor methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the tensor denoising setting, where the tensor is fully observed but corrupted by additive noise. A major application area for these methods is tensor completion, where entries are missing. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, the absence of an analysis for the completion setting is a notable limitation. It is not immediately clear if the results would extend directly.

2.  **Clarity on the Optimization Problem:** There appears to be a slight disconnect between the theory and the experiments regarding the optimization problem. The main theoretical result (Theorem 2) is derived for a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes incoherence constraints (`||W(l)(k)||_S∞ <= α`). However, the experiments use an algorithm from [25] that solves the unconstrained problem (Eq. 5). While the incoherence assumption is standard in related literature, the paper would be stronger if it discussed the implications of this assumption more thoroughly and clarified whether the experimental solver implicitly enforces a similar condition or if the theory holds more generally.

3.  **Practicality of the "LR Complexity":** The definition of the Latent Rank (LR) complexity used in the experiments is based on finding an optimal low-rank decomposition of the *true, noiseless tensor*. While this is perfectly valid for the purpose of verifying the theory, it is not a quantity that can be computed in practice. A brief discussion on this point would be beneficial to manage reader expectations.

**Detailed Comments and Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you please clarify the relationship between the constrained problem in Eq. (11) (used for Theorem 2) and the unconstrained problem in Eq. (5) (apparently solved in the experiments)? Does the incoherence assumption primarily serve as a proof technique, or is it a necessary condition for the result to hold?
*   In Section 3.2, the paper states that the bound in Eq. (13) is obtained by considering a "singleton decomposition." This is a crucial and clever step in the analysis. It might be helpful to elaborate slightly more on why this is a valid decomposition to consider for bounding the error on `W*`, as it underpins the final `min(r_k)` result.
*   The connection to structured sparsity is excellent. It might be worth explicitly stating that the primary novelty of this work is the *analysis* of the latent approach, as the approach itself was previously proposed in [25]. The current phrasing is clear, but making this distinction explicit could further highlight the paper's contribution.

**Summary of Evaluation:**

*   **Quality:** High. The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are novel, interesting, and well-supported by rigorous proofs (delegated to the appendix) and carefully designed experiments.
*   **Clarity:** Very Good. The paper is well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, and the central theoretical result is explained and contrasted with prior work effectively. The minor point about the constrained vs. unconstrained optimization could be clarified.
*   **Originality:** High. While the latent decomposition algorithm itself has been proposed before, the rigorous statistical analysis is novel. The duality result between the two norm structures is a significant and original contribution that provides a new conceptual framework.
*   **Significance:** High. This work provides a definitive theoretical explanation for the performance difference between two key convex tensor decomposition methods. The insight that the latent approach adapts to the minimum-rank mode is important and likely to influence future work on algorithmic design and analysis for tensor problems.

**Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with significant theoretical and practical implications. I recommend acceptance. The strengths, particularly the novel duality theory and the clear, empirically verified performance bounds, far outweigh the minor weaknesses. The work represents a substantial step forward in our understanding of convex relaxations for tensor decomposition.
