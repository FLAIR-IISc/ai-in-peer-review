### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, a method previously shown empirically to outperform the more standard "overlapped approach" in certain settings. The core problem is recovering a low-rank tensor from noisy observations. The authors formalize two convex relaxation strategies based on structured Schatten norms: the overlapped norm, which penalizes the sum of trace norms of all matrix unfoldings of a single tensor, and the latent norm, which penalizes the sum of trace norms of component tensors that sum up to the target tensor.

The main contributions of the paper are:
1.  **A Unified Framework:** It introduces a general framework of "structured Schatten norms," which encompasses both the overlapped and latent approaches, drawing a clear parallel to the structured sparsity literature (e.g., overlapped and latent group lasso).
2.  **Novel Duality:** It proves a novel duality relationship between the generalized overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm. This is a key technical result that facilitates the subsequent analysis.
3.  **Performance Guarantees for the Latent Approach:** The paper presents the first theoretical performance bounds for the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key finding is that the mean squared error of the estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank (`min_k r_k`) of the underlying tensor.
4.  **Theoretical Justification and Empirical Validation:** This theoretical result provides a clear explanation for why the latent approach is superior when a tensor is highly low-rank in only one mode, a scenario where the overlapped approach (whose error scales with the *average* of the ranks) struggles. The authors validate this scaling behavior through well-designed numerical experiments on synthetic data.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics (e.g., analysis of M-estimators with decomposable regularizers). The claims are well-supported by both theoretical proofs (referenced in the appendix) and carefully designed experiments.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The theoretical results are strong and insightful. Theorem 3, which bounds the estimation error in the Gaussian noise setting, is particularly significant as it directly shows the dependency on `min_k r_k`. This provides a concrete, quantitative explanation for a previously observed empirical phenomenon.
    *   The experiments are excellent. Rather than just showing one method is better than another, the authors directly test their theoretical predictions by plotting the Mean Squared Error (MSE) against the derived theoretical complexity measures ("TR complexity" and "LR complexity"). The observed linear scaling provides compelling evidence for the correctness of their analysis.
    *   The work is a complete piece of research, taking the problem from framework definition and theoretical development through to empirical validation.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   The analysis for the tighter bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and additional spectral norm constraints in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While such assumptions are common in the literature for de-mixing problems (e.g., robust PCA), a brief discussion on the practical implications or restrictiveness of this assumption would be beneficial.
    *   The analysis is confined to the denoising problem with complete observations. While this is a standard and important setting, an extension or at least a discussion of the challenges in the tensor completion (missing data) setting would broaden the paper's scope, as this is a primary motivation for tensor decomposition.

**Clarity:**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly distinguishing between the two approaches, and structuring their arguments logically.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The introduction provides a clear roadmap and effectively situates the work within the existing literature.
    *   The formal definitions of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms in Section 2 are precise. The parallel drawn to structured sparsity helps build intuition for readers familiar with that domain.
    *   The comparison between the error bounds for the two approaches in Section 3.4 is sharp and effectively communicates the paper's central message.

*   **Weaknesses:**
    *   No significant weaknesses in clarity. The paper is a model of good scientific writing.

**Originality:**
The paper is highly original and makes several novel contributions.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   To my knowledge, this is the first work to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition. This fills a significant gap in the literature.
    *   The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a novel and elegant technical contribution. It provides a deeper connection between the two main convex relaxation strategies and links the tensor literature more formally to the well-developed structured sparsity literature.
    *   The key insight that the latent approach's performance adapts to the *minimum* mode-k rank is a fundamental contribution that advances our understanding of these methods.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant and likely to have a substantial impact on the field.

*   **Strengths:**
    *   The paper provides clear, theoretically-backed guidance to practitioners on which convex tensor decomposition method to use. It suggests that the latent approach is often a more robust and powerful choice than the overlapped approach, especially when there is no prior knowledge about the tensor's rank structure.
    *   The theoretical framework of structured Schatten norms and the associated analysis could serve as a foundation for future work, for example, in analyzing tensor completion, developing faster algorithms, or designing new types of regularizers for tensors.
    *   The paper resolves an open question from previous work [25] which had introduced the latent model and demonstrated its empirical success but provided no theoretical guarantees. This work solidifies the standing of the latent approach as a principled and high-performing method.

### Summary of Pros and Cons

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Important Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first performance guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition model, resolving an important open problem.
2.  **Novel Insights:** The key result—that the error scales with `min_k r_k`—is a significant and non-obvious finding that fundamentally clarifies the advantage of the latent approach.
3.  **Elegant Technical Results:** The duality between the overlapped and latent norms is a novel and valuable contribution in its own right.
4.  **High Quality and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound, exceptionally well-written, and easy to understand.
5.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to directly validate the derived theoretical scaling laws.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope:** The analysis is restricted to the denoising setting and does not cover the equally important problem of tensor completion.
2.  **Reliance on Incoherence Assumption:** The main results depend on an incoherence assumption which may not hold in all practical scenarios. A deeper discussion of this limitation would be welcome.

### Conclusion and Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on tensor decomposition. It provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for the latent approach, elegantly explaining its empirical advantages through a rigorous analysis. The results are insightful, the technical quality is high, and the presentation is clear. The weaknesses are minor and relate more to the scope of the work than to any flaws within it. The paper clearly advances the state of the art and will be of great interest to researchers in machine learning, statistics, and signal processing. I strongly recommend acceptance.
