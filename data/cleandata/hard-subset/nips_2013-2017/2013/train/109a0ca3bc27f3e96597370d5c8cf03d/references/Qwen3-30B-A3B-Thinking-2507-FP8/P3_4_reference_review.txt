

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper introduces and rigorously analyzes two structured Schatten norms for convex tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" Schatten norm (used in prior work [9,15,23,25]) and the "latent" Schatten norm (proposed in [25]). The authors establish a key duality between these norms (Lemma 1) and demonstrate theoretically and empirically that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in denoising tasks. Their main theoretical contribution shows that the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach's error scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach in cases where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

## Strengths

**Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. This duality is elegant and provides deep insight into the relationship between these two approaches. The authors then leverage this duality to establish tight error bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) that clearly explain the empirical performance differences.

**Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments in Section 4 provide strong empirical support for the theoretical claims. The authors carefully measure MSE against the theoretically predicted complexity measures (TR complexity and LR complexity) and demonstrate the linear scaling relationships as predicted by their theory. The results are well-designed to isolate the key theoretical claims.

**Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The connections to structured sparsity literature are well-explained, and the mathematical notation is consistent throughout. The authors effectively bridge the gap between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, making the paper accessible to a broad audience in machine learning.

**Practical Implications:** The paper provides clear practical guidance for practitioners: the latent approach should be preferred over the overlapped approach for noisy tensor decomposition, as it automatically adapts to the minimum mode-k rank without requiring this information as input.

## Weaknesses

**Limited Scope of Analysis:** The paper focuses exclusively on the S1/1 norm, but the results would be more compelling if they could be extended to other Sp/q norms. The authors briefly mention this as future work, but it would strengthen the contribution to establish whether the key insights generalize to other norms.

**Practical Decomposition Choice:** The paper assumes the true tensor can be decomposed as W* = ∑W*(k) with specific rank properties (Equation 9), but it's not clear how to determine this decomposition in practice. The authors mention using the latent approach on the noiseless tensor to find the optimal decomposition, but this is not feasible in real applications where the true tensor is unknown.

**Computational Considerations:** The paper makes no mention of computational complexity or scalability of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. For large-scale tensor decomposition tasks, this could be a significant practical consideration that the authors should address.

**Nuance in Empirical Results:** The claim that "the latent approach performs better in many cases" is slightly overstated. The numerical results (Figure 2) show that the two approaches perform similarly when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes. The authors acknowledge this but could better emphasize that the latent approach is particularly advantageous when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

## Recommendations

The paper makes a strong theoretical contribution that clarifies an important empirical observation in tensor decomposition. The duality result (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and valuable. The empirical validation is thorough and well-executed.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper is of high quality, makes a significant contribution to the field, and is clearly written. The minor weaknesses noted above could be addressed with brief additions to the paper.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In Section 3.4, clarify that the latent approach is particularly advantageous when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode, but performs similarly to the overlapped approach when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

2. Briefly discuss the computational complexity of the latent approach versus the overlapped approach, and mention whether the theoretical benefits of the latent approach are offset by higher computational cost.

3. In the conclusion, more explicitly state that the optimal decomposition (for the latent approach) is not known in practice, but the method is robust to the choice of decomposition.

4. Consider adding a sentence or two about the potential extension of these results to other Sp/q norms, even if it's only a brief mention of this as future work.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a clear theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and elegant, and the theoretical analysis provides a satisfying explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and the empirical results strongly support the theoretical claims. The minor weaknesses noted above do not detract significantly from the paper's overall quality and contribution.

The paper addresses a significant problem in tensor decomposition with both theoretical and practical implications. The results advance the state of the art by providing a rigorous explanation for empirical observations and offering clear guidance to practitioners. The work is highly relevant to NIPS and would be of interest to a broad audience in machine learning.

Given the paper's technical soundness, clarity, originality, and significance, I recommend acceptance.
