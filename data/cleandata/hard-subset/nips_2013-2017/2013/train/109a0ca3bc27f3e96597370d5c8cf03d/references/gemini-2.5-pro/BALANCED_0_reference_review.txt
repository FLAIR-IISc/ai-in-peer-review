### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of a convex approach for tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent approach". This method models a tensor as a sum of component tensors, each of which is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. This stands in contrast to the more common "overlapped approach" [9, 15, 23] which regularizes a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings. While the latent approach was proposed and empirically shown to be effective in [25], particularly for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode, it has lacked a formal theoretical justification.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  It formalizes the concepts of "overlapped" and "latent" regularizers for tensors within a general framework of "structured Schatten norms", drawing a clear parallel to the literature on structured sparsity [13, 21].
2.  It establishes a novel and elegant duality between the proposed overlapped and latent Schatten norms, a key technical result that facilitates the theoretical analysis.
3.  It provides the first theoretical performance bounds for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The key result (Theorem 3) shows that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks [26]. This formally explains the empirical advantages of the latent method.
4.  The paper includes numerical experiments that validate the derived theoretical scaling laws, showing that the empirical error of the latent approach closely follows the predicted bound.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:**
The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics [1, 20]. The central theoretical contributions—the duality in Lemma 1 and the error bounds in Theorems 2 and 3—are sound and well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix. The authors are careful to state their assumptions, such as the incoherence assumption (Eq. 10), and to relate them to similar assumptions in prior work. The experiments are well-designed to specifically verify the theoretical predictions about the scaling of the estimation error, providing strong empirical support for the paper's claims.

**Clarity:**
The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly established in the introduction with a helpful illustrative figure. The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches is explained exceptionally well. The formal definitions of the structured Schatten norms in Section 2 are precise, and the connection to the structured sparsity literature is enlightening. The main theoretical results are stated clearly, and their implications are discussed effectively in Section 3.4, where the comparison to the overlapped approach is made explicit. The experimental section is also easy to follow.

**Originality:**
The paper makes several original contributions. While the latent algorithm itself was proposed in [25], this work is the first to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance. The formulation of the problem in terms of general structured Schatten norms and, most notably, the proof of the duality relationship between the overlapped and latent norms, are novel and significant contributions. This duality provides a unifying perspective and connects the field of convex tensor methods more deeply with the broader literature on structured regularization. The resulting error bound, which depends on the minimum rank, is a new and important finding that advances our understanding of these methods.

**Significance:**
The results presented are significant and likely to be impactful for both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data. The paper provides a clear theoretical answer to the practical question of when and why the latent decomposition approach should be preferred over the standard overlapped approach. The key insight—that the latent method automatically adapts to the "easiest" mode (the one with the minimum rank)—is a powerful one. This work solidifies the foundation for the latent approach, encouraging its wider adoption and further study. It also opens avenues for future research, such as extending the analysis to the tensor completion setting or exploring other structured Schatten norms from the proposed general family.

**Weaknesses and Constructive Suggestions:**

1.  **Practicality of Assumptions and Constraints:** The main deterministic bound (Theorem 2) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes spectral norm constraints on cross-modal unfoldings. While the authors correctly note that such assumptions are common, a deeper discussion of their practical implications would be valuable. For instance, how restrictive is this assumption in practice? The parameter `α` appears in the choice of `λ`; how might one set this in a real-world scenario? A brief comment on how the solver from [25] handles these additional constraints would also improve the paper's self-containedness.

2.  **Calculation of "LR complexity"**: In the experiments, the Latent Rank (LR) complexity used for plotting is determined by running the algorithm on the noiseless tensor `W*`. This makes the validation slightly circular, as the complexity measure is derived from the algorithm's own behavior on the ground truth. While the authors justify this by noting a singleton decomposition is always an option, the presentation could be improved. It would be clearer to state upfront that `min_k rk` provides a simple, direct upper bound on the latent complexity and then show that the empirical MSE scales with this quantity. This would make the connection between the theory and the experiment more direct.

3.  **Context with Non-Convex Methods:** The paper provides an excellent comparison between two convex relaxation methods. However, it would benefit from a brief discussion placing these results in the context of traditional non-convex tensor decomposition algorithms (e.g., ALS). For the cases where the latent approach excels (e.g., rank-(r, R, R) where R is large), how would ALS-based methods perform? While a full empirical comparison is beyond the scope, a short, qualitative discussion would help readers from the broader community appreciate the contribution.

4.  **Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the tensor denoising setting with i.i.d. Gaussian noise. This is a standard and important setting for foundational analysis. However, a major application for these methods is tensor completion (i.e., recovering from missing entries). The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a limitation of the current paper.

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis for the "latent" convex tensor decomposition method, filling an important gap in the literature.
*   **Pro:** The novel duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is an elegant and fundamental contribution that links tensor methods to the broader field of structured sparsity.
*   **Pro:** The main theoretical result—that the error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank—provides a clear and compelling explanation for the method's empirical success and gives concrete guidance on when to prefer it.
*   **Pro:** The paper is exceptionally clear, well-written, and the experiments are carefully designed to support the theoretical claims.
*   **Pro:** The work is highly significant and advances the state of the art in our understanding of convex relaxations for tensor problems.

### Arguments Against Acceptance

*   **Con:** The analysis relies on a somewhat strong incoherence assumption and a constrained version of the optimization problem, whose practical implications could be discussed more thoroughly.
*   **Con:** The analysis does not cover the tensor completion setting, which is a key application area for these methods.

### Final Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of tensor decompositions. The theoretical results are novel, rigorously proven, and provide important insights into the behavior of convex relaxation methods. The paper is exceptionally well-written and the empirical results convincingly support the theory. The weaknesses identified are minor and primarily relate to the scope and suggestions for further clarification, rather than fundamental flaws. The paper clearly meets the high standards of NeurIPS.

**Decision: Accept**
