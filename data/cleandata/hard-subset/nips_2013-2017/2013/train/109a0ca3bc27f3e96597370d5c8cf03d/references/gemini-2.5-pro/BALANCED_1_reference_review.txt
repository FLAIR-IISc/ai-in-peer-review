### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex approach for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," where a tensor is modeled as a sum of several component tensors, each being low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the more established "overlapped approach," which encourages a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its matrix unfoldings.

The main contributions are:
1.  **Formalization:** The paper formalizes these two approaches within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms," defining the overlapped and latent Schatten norms. It draws a clear and insightful parallel to the literature on overlapped and latent group sparsity.
2.  **Duality:** A key technical contribution is proving a novel duality between the generalized overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm. This result is of independent interest and serves as a crucial tool for the subsequent analysis.
3.  **Theoretical Analysis:** The paper presents the first theoretical performance bounds for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The main result (Theorem 3) shows that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying true tensor (`min_k r_k`). This contrasts sharply with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the *average* of the mode-k ranks [26]. This theoretical result elegantly explains why the latent approach is empirically superior for tensors that are low-rank in only one or a few modes.
4.  **Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct numerical experiments that directly test and confirm their theoretical predictions. The results show that the estimation error of the latent method scales linearly with the proposed complexity measure, validating the analysis.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a clear and important gap in the literature. While the latent approach had been proposed empirically [25], it lacked a theoretical foundation. This work provides that foundation, rigorously explaining *why* and *when* it should be preferred over the overlapped approach. The finding that its error bound depends on `min_k r_k` is a significant and non-obvious result that advances our understanding of convex tensor methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The paper is technically sound and rigorous. The formulation of structured Schatten norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is elegant and connects the tensor literature to the broader field of structured sparsity. The proofs of the main theorems build on established techniques from high-dimensional statistics but are adapted non-trivially to the tensor setting. The assumptions are clearly stated.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches is made clear from the beginning and serves as a consistent theme. The motivation is well-established with reference to prior work and illustrative examples (Figure 1). The theoretical results are stated precisely and their implications are discussed lucidly.

4.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The experiments are not merely illustrative; they are carefully designed to verify the specific scaling laws derived in the theory. Figure 2 provides compelling evidence that the MSE of the latent approach scales with the proposed "Latent Rank (LR) complexity," thereby validating the core theoretical contribution of the paper. This closes the loop between theory and practice effectively.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

1.  **Practicality of the Incoherence Assumption:** The analysis in Theorem 2 relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) where the incoherence parameter `α` is known. This is a common device in theoretical analyses of this type, but it raises questions about practical application.
    *   **Suggestion:** It would strengthen the paper to add a brief discussion on the role of this assumption. For instance, is the unconstrained latent estimator (Eq. 5) expected to perform similarly in practice? How sensitive might the results be to the choice of `α`, and could it be estimated from data? Clarifying that this is primarily a tool for the analysis, if that is the case, would also be helpful.

2.  **Scope of the Analysis (Denoising vs. Completion):** The analysis is performed for the tensor denoising setting, where the tensor is fully observed with additive Gaussian noise. While this is a standard and important setting, many practical applications of tensor decomposition, such as collaborative filtering, involve missing data (tensor completion).
    *   **Suggestion:** The authors rightly point to tensor completion as future work. However, the paper would be more complete if it briefly speculated on the challenges of extending the analysis to the completion setting. For instance, would the analysis require adapting concepts like matrix coherence to the latent tensor formulation?

3.  **Clarity on Singleton Decompositions:** A key insight for achieving the `min_k r_k` bound is that the latent formulation allows for a "singleton" decomposition (e.g., `W* = W*(k)` for some `k`, and `W*(k') = 0` for `k' != k`). This is a powerful and perhaps unintuitive aspect of the model.
    *   **Suggestion:** This idea could be highlighted more prominently, perhaps with a short explanatory sentence in Section 2.2 when the latent norm is introduced. This would help the reader grasp the mechanism behind the main result earlier in the paper. The empirical observation that the optimal decomposition was often a singleton is very interesting and supports this point.

### Decision

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of convex tensor decompositions. It is technically sound, clearly written, and provides strong empirical evidence for its claims. The theoretical result neatly explains previously observed empirical phenomena and provides clear guidance on when to prefer the latent model over the overlapped one. The weaknesses identified are minor and do not detract from the core contribution. I strongly recommend acceptance.
