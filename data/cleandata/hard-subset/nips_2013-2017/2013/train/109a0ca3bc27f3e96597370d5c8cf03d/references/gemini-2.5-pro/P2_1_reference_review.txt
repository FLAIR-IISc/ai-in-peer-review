### Paper Summary

This paper investigates a convex optimization approach for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode. The authors provide the first rigorous theoretical analysis for this method in a denoising setting. The core contributions are threefold: 1) They introduce a general framework of "structured Schatten norms" for tensors, establishing a novel duality between the "overlapped" norm (used in prior work) and the "latent" norm (analyzed here). This elegantly connects tensor decomposition to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., latent group lasso). 2) They derive a mean squared error bound for the latent approach, demonstrating that its performance scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true underlying tensor. This provides a theoretical explanation for empirical results showing the latent approach's superiority, especially for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode. 3) They conduct numerical experiments on synthetic data that directly validate the derived theoretical scaling behavior, showing a strong linear relationship between the empirical error and their proposed complexity measure.

### Review

This is an excellent paper that provides a significant theoretical contribution to the growing field of convex tensor methods. It is technically sound, clearly written, and the results are both novel and important for researchers in this area.

**Quality:** The paper is of very high technical quality. The theoretical development is rigorous and well-motivated. The establishment of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is an elegant and fundamental result that provides a unifying perspective. The main performance bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are derived carefully, building on techniques from high-dimensional statistics while adapting them skillfully to the tensor setting. The assumptions, such as the incoherence property (Eq. 10), are standard in related literature and are clearly stated.

The experimental evaluation is a major strength. Rather than simply comparing methods on a few datasets, the authors design experiments specifically to verify the scaling laws predicted by their theory. Defining the "LR complexity" and "TR complexity" measures and plotting the MSE against them provides strong, convincing evidence for their theoretical claims. The authors are also honest about the limitations and nuances of their results, for example, by noting that the improvements in their experiments are milder than in the motivating Figure 1 because the randomly generated tensors were low-rank in multiple modes. This demonstrates careful and credible scientific practice.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The introduction effectively situates the work within the existing literature, clearly distinguishing between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches and articulating the gap in theoretical understanding that this paper fills. The notation is standard and introduced systematically. The flow from the general norm definitions (Section 2) to the main theoretical results (Section 3) and their empirical validation (Section 4) is logical and easy to follow. The direct comparison of their error bound (Eq. 15) with the prior bound for the overlapped approach (Eq. 16) is particularly effective at highlighting the paper's core message.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the latent approach algorithm itself had been proposed previously [25], it lacked any theoretical performance guarantees. This paper provides the first such analysis, which is a substantial and non-trivial contribution. The formulation of the problem in the language of structured Schatten norms and the proof of their duality is novel and connects two previously distinct lines of research (convex tensor methods and structured sparsity). This conceptual framing is a significant contribution on its own. The main result—that the latent approach's error adapts to the *minimum* mode-k rank—is a novel insight that fundamentally advances our understanding of these methods.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the machine learning and signal processing communities. Convex relaxations for tensor decomposition are of great practical and theoretical interest. This paper provides clear guidance on which regularizer to prefer in noisy settings, backed by strong theory. The finding that the latent approach "performs as if it knows the mode with the minimum rank" is a powerful and memorable conclusion. Researchers working on tensor methods are very likely to build upon these results, for example, by extending the analysis to the tensor completion setting (as the authors suggest) or by exploring other norms within the proposed structured Schatten norm framework. The theoretical techniques used may also be applicable to other structured estimation problems.

**Strengths (Pro-Acceptance):**

1.  **Novel and Important Theoretical Contribution:** Provides the first performance guarantees for the latent tensor decomposition approach, explaining its empirical success.
2.  **Elegant Duality Result:** Establishes a fundamental connection between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, unifying this area with the broader structured sparsity literature.
3.  **Clear and Testable Main Result:** The key finding—that error scales with the *minimum* rank, not the average—is a simple, powerful, and impactful insight.
4.  **Strong, Theory-Driven Experiments:** The numerical simulations are carefully designed to validate the specific scaling laws predicted by the theory, providing compelling evidence for the paper's claims.
5.  **Exceptional Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written, making a complex technical topic accessible and the contributions easy to understand.

**Weaknesses (Con-Acceptance):**

1.  **Limited to Denoising:** The analysis is restricted to the fully-observed, noisy setting. While important, a key motivation for tensor methods is completion from missing entries. The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it remains a limitation of the current paper.
2.  **Dependence on Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter bounds in Theorems 2 and 3 rely on an incoherence assumption. While standard, a brief discussion on the practical implications or restrictiveness of this assumption would have been beneficial.

These weaknesses are minor compared to the paper's substantial strengths. The work represents a complete and significant step forward in our understanding of convex tensor decomposition.

**Overall Recommendation:** This is a clear accept. It is a high-quality paper that makes a foundational theoretical contribution to an important problem, supported by excellent, well-designed experiments.
