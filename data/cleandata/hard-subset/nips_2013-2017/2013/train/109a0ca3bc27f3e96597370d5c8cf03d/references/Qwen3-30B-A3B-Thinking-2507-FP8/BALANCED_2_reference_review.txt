

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper studies two convex approaches to tensor decomposition: the "overlapped approach" using the overlapped Schatten 1-norm and the "latent approach" using the latent Schatten 1-norm. The authors establish a duality between these two types of norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. They then provide theoretical analysis showing that the latent approach has better performance guarantees, with error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, explaining why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain scenarios. The paper connects these tensor decomposition techniques to the broader structured sparsity literature (e.g., group lasso) and provides empirical validation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Novelty and Rigor:** The paper's main contribution is establishing the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which is both novel and significant. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-3) is rigorous and well-structured, with clear connections to the structured sparsity literature. The key insight that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average (as in the overlapped approach) provides a principled explanation for empirical observations.

2. **Clear Connection to Prior Work:** The paper effectively situates its contributions within the tensor decomposition and structured sparsity literature. The authors properly cite relevant NIPS papers (e.g., [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]) and demonstrate how their work extends prior results. The connection to group sparsity norms (Lemma 1) is particularly well-executed.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments in Section 4 successfully confirm the theoretical predictions. The authors demonstrate the linear scaling of MSE with the predicted complexity measures (TR and LR complexity) and show that the latent approach consistently outperforms the overlapped approach, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

4. **Practical Implications:** The paper provides valuable guidance for practitioners, showing that the latent approach is preferable for noisy tensor decomposition without requiring prior knowledge of the tensor's rank structure. The theoretical optimal regularization constant is independent of unknown ranks, which is practically useful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical results focus primarily on the denoising problem (Y = W* + E), but the paper's main motivation is tensor decomposition for data analysis, which often involves missing values (tensor completion). While the authors mention tensor completion as future work, the theoretical analysis doesn't address this important application scenario. The connection between denoising and completion in the context of these norms could be strengthened.

2. **Computational Considerations:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. For large-scale problems, the computational cost of solving the latent optimization problem (which involves decomposing the tensor into K components) could be significantly higher than the overlapped approach, which processes the tensor as a whole.

3. **Narrow Noise Assumption:** The Gaussian noise analysis (Theorem 3) is limited to independent zero-mean Gaussian noise. Real-world data often has more complex noise structures, and the paper doesn't explore how the results might extend to other noise models.

4. **Clarification Needed on Rank Definitions:** The paper uses two different rank definitions (multilinear rank vs. latent factor ranks) that could confuse readers. The relationship between these ranks and their practical implications for the latent approach could be clarified.

**Pro/Con Arguments for Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing a duality between two important tensor decomposition approaches.
- The theoretical analysis is rigorous and provides clear explanations for empirical observations.
- The empirical results strongly support the theoretical claims.
- The work connects tensor decomposition to the broader structured sparsity literature in a meaningful way.

*Con:*
- The theoretical analysis is limited to denoising rather than the more common tensor completion problem.
- The paper lacks discussion of computational complexity, which is crucial for practical applications.
- The noise model assumption is relatively narrow.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable theoretical contribution to tensor decomposition. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and significant, and the theoretical analysis provides important insights into why the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach. The empirical results strongly support the theoretical claims.

The main limitations are the restricted scope of the theoretical analysis (denoising vs. completion) and the lack of computational complexity discussion. However, these are relatively minor compared to the paper's substantial theoretical contribution. The connection to structured sparsity literature is particularly strong and well-executed.

I recommend acceptance. The paper advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition and provides both theoretical and empirical justification for preferring the latent approach. With minor revisions to address the scope limitation (perhaps adding a brief discussion of how the results might extend to completion) and computational considerations, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The paper meets all four criteria: it is technically sound (Quality), well-written (Clarity), offers a novel theoretical connection (Originality), and has significant implications for tensor decomposition applications (Significance).
