This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex approach to tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent approach." This approach models a tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is constrained to be low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the more common "overlapped approach," which encourages a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all its modes. The main contributions of the paper are: (1) formalizing the norms underlying these two approaches (the latent and overlapped Schatten norms) and establishing a novel duality between them, linking this area to the broader literature on structured sparsity; (2) providing a rigorous theoretical analysis of the denoising performance of the latent approach; (3) showing that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas the overlapped approach's error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks; and (4) empirically validating these theoretical scaling laws with numerical experiments. This work builds upon previous papers that introduced these convex relaxations [25] and analyzed the overlapped approach [26], providing the missing theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent method.

### Strengths:
1.  **High Significance and Impact:** The paper addresses a fundamentally important question in the growing field of convex tensor methods: which convex relaxation should be used and why? By providing a clear theoretical answer backed by empirical evidence, the paper offers valuable guidance to both researchers and practitioners. The key finding—that the latent approach's error scales with `min_k r_k` while the overlapped approach's scales with `(average_k sqrt(r_k))^2`—is a simple, powerful, and memorable result that elegantly explains why the latent approach can be dramatically better, especially for tensors that are low-rank in only one or a few modes.

2.  **Excellent Theoretical Quality:** The theoretical contributions are novel and technically sound. The formulation of the generalized overlapped and latent Sp/q norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is an elegant result that unifies these tensor norms within the well-established framework of structured sparsity (e.g., latent group LASSO). The main error bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are derived rigorously and provide a clear, quantitative explanation for the performance difference between the two methods. The analysis appears correct and builds upon modern techniques for high-dimensional statistics.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly motivate the problem, precisely define the two approaches, and build their argument logically from the norm definitions and their properties (Sec. 2) to the main theoretical analysis (Sec. 3) and finally to the experimental validation (Sec. 4). The comparison between the two approaches is a constant theme and is very effectively communicated.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The numerical results in Section 4 are a model for how to validate theory. Instead of simply showing that one method outperforms another on some datasets, the authors design experiments to explicitly test the predicted scaling laws. By defining "TR complexity" and "LR complexity" based on their theoretical bounds and plotting the MSE against these quantities, they show a convincing linear relationship that validates their analysis not just qualitatively but quantitatively. This is a major strength.

### Weaknesses:
1.  **Incoherence Assumption and Optimization Constraints:** The main analysis in Theorem 2 requires an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) on the true tensor components and, more importantly, imposes this as an explicit constraint in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While the authors correctly note that such assumptions are common in related literature, the practical implications could be discussed in more detail. How does one choose the value of `alpha` in practice? The paper notes that the singleton decomposition (where one `W*(k) = W*` and the others are zero) is often optimal and trivially satisfies the condition with `alpha=0`. This is a crucial point that perhaps could be highlighted earlier and more forcefully in Section 3.2, as it provides a strong justification for the `min r_k` bound in Eq. (13) without needing to worry about a complex decomposition or a non-zero `alpha`.

2.  **Focus on Denoising vs. Completion:** The analysis is performed in the denoising setting, where the tensor is fully observed but corrupted by noise. While this is an important problem, a major motivation for low-rank tensor methods is the completion problem (recovering a tensor from a small subset of its entries). The authors acknowledge this as future work in the conclusion, but the paper's impact would be even broader if the analysis could be extended to the completion setting.

3.  **Clarity on LR Complexity:** The definition of the Latent Rank (LR) complexity in Eq. (18) depends on the ranks `r_bar_k` of the latent factors of the true tensor `W*`. The paper explains that this is estimated for the experiments by running the algorithm on the clean tensor. This makes the LR complexity a slightly less direct property of `W*` compared to the Tucker ranks `r_k`. While the paper correctly circumvents this by using the singleton decomposition to get the cleaner `min_k r_k` bound (Eq. 15), this distinction could be made clearer. The primary message is the `min_k r_k` dependency, which is a direct and easily interpretable property of `W*`.

### Summary of evaluation:

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (provided in the appendix), and the experimental methodology is sound and effectively validates the theory. This is a complete and polished piece of work.

**Clarity:** The paper is extremely clear, well-organized, and well-written. The core ideas are communicated effectively, making a potentially complex topic accessible.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the algorithms themselves were proposed in prior work, this paper provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach. The duality result between the two norm classes is a novel and valuable contribution that connects disparate lines of research.

**Significance:** The results are very significant. They provide a definitive theoretical explanation for the performance of a key method in convex tensor decomposition and offer clear guidance for practitioners. The finding that the latent approach adaptively finds the "easiest" (lowest-rank) mode to exploit is an important insight that will likely influence future work in this area.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis for the "latent" tensor decomposition approach.
*   The key result—error scaling with `min_k r_k`—is simple, impactful, and explains previously observed empirical results.
*   The novel duality result elegantly connects tensor norms to the broader structured sparsity literature.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and the experiments provide strong validation of the theory.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on an incoherence assumption that is incorporated as a constraint in the optimization, which might raise questions about its practical application (though the singleton decomposition argument largely mitigates this).
*   The analysis is restricted to the denoising setting and does not cover tensor completion.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its weaknesses. It is a significant, original, and high-quality contribution to the literature on tensor methods and convex optimization. I strongly recommend acceptance.
