Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms. It contrasts two families: (i) the “overlapped” approach that penalizes the sum of nuclear norms of mode unfoldings, and (ii) the “latent” approach that decomposes a tensor into a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode, penalizing the nuclear norm of each component’s designated unfolding. The authors formalize these as overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms and prove a duality between them (latent is the dual of overlapped). They then analyze the statistical performance of the latent approach for denoising: a generic consistency bound, a deterministic bound under an incoherence-style assumption (with added spectral norm constraints), and a Gaussian-noise bound showing MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank, in contrast to prior bounds for the overlapped approach that scale with the average of mode ranks. Synthetic experiments corroborate the predicted scaling and show cases where the latent approach outperforms the overlapped one.

Relation to prior work
The latent-vs-overlapped distinction mirrors known results in structured sparsity (e.g., latent group lasso vs overlapped group lasso). The paper connects these ideas to tensor Schatten norms, extending techniques from convex matrix estimation (Agarwal/Negahban/Wainwright; Negahban et al.) and prior tensor nuclear-norm work (e.g., Signoretto; Liu et al.; Tomioka et al.). The empirical observation that latent can outperform overlapped when only one mode is low-rank is known; the contribution here is to provide bounds that formalize this intuition.

Strengths
- Clear formalization of overlapped and latent Schatten norms for tensors and a useful duality result that aligns with structured sparsity literature.
- Error bounds for the latent approach in denoising: a general consistency inequality and sharper bounds under incoherence that depend on min mode rank; this provides a clean theoretical explanation for when latent is preferable.
- Synthetic experiments illustrate the scaling predicted by the theory and highlight scenarios where latent has an advantage.
- The choice of regularization parameter does not require rank knowledge in the theory, which is practically appealing.

Weaknesses
- The deterministic and Gaussian bounds rely on additional spectral norm constraints (α) and an incoherence-style condition that are not clearly justified for realistic data. It is unclear how α is chosen in practice and whether these constraints are enforced in the experiments; the ADMM solver referenced does not obviously incorporate them.
- The novelty of the duality result appears incremental: it is a natural extension of known overlapped/latent group norm dualities to the Schatten/tensor setting. The proofs are in the supplement, and the main text provides limited new proof techniques beyond standard decomposable-regularizer arguments.
- Empirical validation is limited to small synthetic denoising problems with full observations. There are no real datasets, no tensor completion tasks, no robustness tests to model misspecification, and no comparisons to established nonconvex baselines (e.g., Tucker/HOOI, CP-ALS), which are widely used in practice.
- The comparison to prior overlapped bounds risks being non–apples-to-apples: the settings, noise models, and constants differ. The factor F and α(K − 1) term in the regularization further complicate practical interpretation; the bound may be loose in common regimes.
- Some references are incomplete and formatting is inconsistent. The paper would benefit from clearer statements of assumptions and tighter connection between theory and implemented algorithms.

Quality
Technically sound at a high level, with claims supported by standard tools (dual norms, decomposable regularization, spectral norm concentration). However, the added spectral constraints and α are under-specified and seemingly absent from experiments. The theoretical improvements are plausible but depend on conditions that may be strong.

Clarity
Generally well organized with clear definitions. However:
- Be explicit about whether (and how) constraints in (11) are used in experiments.
- Clarify the setting match when comparing to [26].
- Provide guidance for choosing λ and α in practice.

Originality
Moderate. The latent-vs-overlapped duality is a natural generalization of known group-lasso results. The error bounds for latent tensor norms are useful but largely adapt existing frameworks to this setting.

Significance
Potentially useful theoretically, as it sharpens understanding of when latent regularization is preferable. Practical impact is limited by the experimental scope and by the gap between theoretical assumptions and implemented methods.

Questions/clarifications for the authors
- Are the spectral norm constraints in (11) enforced in your implementation? If not, how do the theoretical guarantees relate to the reported experiments?
- How is α chosen in practice? Can you provide sensitivity analyses?
- Can you include comparisons to Tucker/HOOI, CP-ALS, and to overlapped/latent methods in tensor completion with missing data?
- Is the comparison to [26] done under identical observation/noise models? Please clarify.

Pros for acceptance
- Solid theoretical analysis clarifying the advantage of latent Schatten norms.
- Clean duality result tying tensor norms to structured sparsity frameworks.
- Empirical trends align with theoretical scaling.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; theory largely extends known ideas.
- Practical assumptions (α, incoherence) and constraints are not matched in experiments.
- Limited empirical validation (only synthetic denoising; no real data; no nonconvex baselines).
- Unclear practical guidance for parameter selection beyond asymptotic forms.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper offers a careful theoretical extension and helpful perspective, but the empirical validation is too limited and the novelty is modest. Strengthening the experimental section (including real data and standard baselines), clarifying the role of constraints and parameter choices, and ensuring apples-to-apples comparisons would significantly improve the case for acceptance.

Confidence
High.
