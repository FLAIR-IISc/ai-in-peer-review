### **Review Summary**

This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, a method that models a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the more established "overlapped approach," which encourages simultaneous low-rankness across all modes.

The main contributions are threefold:
1.  It formalizes the regularizer for the latent approach as the "latent Schatten 1-norm" and, in a key theoretical result, establishes a novel duality between this norm and the "overlapped Schatten norm." This elegantly connects the tensor decomposition literature to the broader field of structured sparsity (e.g., latent vs. overlapped group lasso).
2.  It derives the first performance guarantees for the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key finding is that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, `min_k r_k`.
3.  This theoretical result explains why the latent approach empirically outperforms the overlapped approach, whose error bounds depend on the *sum* of mode-k ranks (`sum_k sqrt(r_k)`). The paper provides compelling numerical experiments on synthetic data that precisely validate these predicted scaling behaviors.

### **Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is sound, building upon established techniques from high-dimensional statistics and applying them to this new context. The derivation of the duality between the structured Schatten norms is a particularly strong and elegant contribution. The claims are well-supported by both theory (Theorems 1-3) and experiments. The authors are careful to state their assumptions (e.g., incoherence) and present a balanced view. The experiments are meticulously designed to verify the specific theoretical scaling laws derived, and they do so convincingly.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" paradigms is introduced clearly and maintained throughout. The motivation is strong, starting from a clear empirical observation (Figure 1) and then developing the theory to explain it. The notation is consistent and the mathematical developments are easy to follow for an expert in the field.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the latent decomposition model itself was proposed in prior work [25], this paper is the first to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance. The central contribution—the duality between latent and overlapped Schatten norms—is novel and provides a significant conceptual bridge between tensor methods and structured sparsity. This connection alone is a valuable contribution. The resulting error bounds and the clear comparison to the overlapped approach's bounds are new and insightful.

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for the field. The overlapped trace-norm penalty has become a standard approach for convex tensor decomposition. This paper provides strong theoretical and empirical evidence that the latent approach is a superior alternative, particularly when the underlying tensor's rank structure is unknown or imbalanced across modes. The finding that the latent approach's performance adapts to the "easiest" mode (the one with minimum rank) is a powerful result. This work is likely to shift the default choice of regularizer for practitioners and inspire further research into structured tensor norms.

---

Despite the paper's many strengths, there are a few areas that could be improved:

1.  **The Incoherence Assumption and Constraint:** The main deterministic bound (Theorem 2) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and, more practically, an explicit spectral norm constraint in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). While such assumptions are common in related analyses (e.g., [1, 3]), the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of its implications. For instance, how restrictive is this assumption in practice? Was the solver used in the experiments [25] modified to handle this additional constraint, or was it ignored? Clarifying this is important for reproducibility and for understanding the practical applicability of the theoretical guarantees.

2.  **Definition of "Latent Rank (LR) Complexity":** In the numerical results, the LR complexity (Eq. 18) is defined using the ranks of the latent components (`r_bar_k`). The paper states these were found by running the algorithm on the noiseless true tensor `W*`. This is a non-standard way to define a complexity measure, as it depends on having access to the ground truth and the output of the algorithm itself. While the authors correctly argue that this complexity is often dominated by the singleton decomposition related to `min_k r_k`, a more direct comparison with `min_k r_k` in the plots, or a clearer discussion of this measure, would strengthen the experimental section.

3.  **Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are excellent for verifying the theoretical scaling laws but are limited to denoising fully-observed synthetic tensors. The introduction motivates the problem with applications like tensor completion and collaborative filtering. While a full empirical study is beyond the scope, demonstrating the advantage of the latent approach on a simple tensor completion task or a real-world dataset would have made the paper's practical impact even more evident.

### **Summary of Pros and Cons**

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical analysis of the latent approach for convex tensor decomposition.
*   Presents a novel and elegant duality result connecting tensor norms to the well-established theory of structured sparsity.
*   The key theoretical result—that the latent method's error scales with `min_k r_k`—is significant and clearly explains its empirical advantages over the standard overlapped method.
*   The paper is exceptionally well-written and the experiments strongly support the theoretical claims.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   The practical implications of the incoherence assumption and the constrained optimization problem are not fully discussed.
*   The experimental validation, while technically sound, is confined to a synthetic denoising setting, which may limit the assessment of its broader practical utility.

### **Final Recommendation**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the literature on tensor methods and high-dimensional statistics. The theoretical insights are deep, and the connection established between tensor norms and structured sparsity is both novel and enlightening. The weaknesses are minor and relate primarily to clarifying assumptions and broadening the experimental scope, rather than fundamental flaws in the methodology or theory. This work provides a clear and compelling case for preferring the latent decomposition approach and will undoubtedly be influential. I strongly recommend acceptance.
