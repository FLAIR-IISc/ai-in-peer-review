

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition by rigorously analyzing the latent approach to convex tensor decomposition, establishing a key duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, and providing a compelling explanation for why the latent approach empirically outperforms the overlapped approach.

The authors present a novel duality result between two structured Schatten norms used in convex tensor decomposition: the overlapped Schatten norm (which penalizes all modes to be jointly low-rank) and the latent Schatten norm (which decomposes a tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). This duality is closely related to structured sparsity literature but extends it to the tensor domain. The key theoretical insight is that the error bound for the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, while the overlapped approach scales with the *average* mode-k rank. This explains the empirical observation in Figure 1 that the latent approach performs better when the tensor is only low-rank in one mode.

The paper's main contributions are:
1. A rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach (Theorems 1-3), showing consistency and denoising performance bounds
2. The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1)
3. Empirical confirmation that the mean squared error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (TR complexity for overlapped approach, LR complexity for latent approach)

This work builds upon and extends the convex tensor decomposition framework initiated in [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], but provides the first theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach. Previous work [26] analyzed the overlapped approach but did not establish a theoretical basis for why the latent approach might be better in certain scenarios.

**Strengths:**
- **Novelty:** The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is genuinely novel and provides a deep theoretical insight that connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature.
- **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper establishes a clear theoretical explanation for an empirical observation, showing that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, which is a significant theoretical advance.
- **Significance:** The result has practical implications for tensor decomposition in applications like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering, where tensors often have one or two dominant low-rank modes.
- **Rigor:** The theoretical analysis is thorough, with well-motivated theorems and proofs in the appendix.
- **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions about scaling behavior.

**Weaknesses:**
- The empirical section is somewhat limited to two tensor sizes (50×50×20 and 80×80×40) and could include more diverse experimental settings.
- The paper doesn't address how the latent approach handles missing data, which is a common use case for tensor decomposition (though this might be beyond the scope of the current theoretical contribution).
- The comparison with other non-convex tensor decomposition methods (like Tucker decomposition) is limited, though the focus is on convex approaches.

**Overall Assessment:**
This paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to tensor decomposition that goes beyond incremental improvements. The key insight about the error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank is novel and significant, providing a clear theoretical explanation for empirical observations. The duality result between the two structured Schatten norms is elegant and connects tensor decomposition to broader structured sparsity literature.

While the paper doesn't present a new algorithmic approach (the latent approach was previously proposed in [25]), it provides the first rigorous theoretical foundation for why this approach works better in certain scenarios. This theoretical understanding is a major contribution to the field, and the paper successfully bridges the gap between empirical observation and theoretical justification.

The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a compelling case for the superiority of the latent approach in noisy tensor decomposition settings. The theoretical analysis is sound, and the empirical results strongly support the claims. The paper's main contribution is the theoretical insight, not the algorithm itself, which is appropriate given the focus on the underlying theory.

**Recommendation:** Accept

This paper represents a significant theoretical advance that will likely influence future work on tensor decomposition. The novelty of the duality result and the clear explanation for the empirical performance difference between the two approaches make this a strong contribution to NIPS. The theoretical insight about the minimum mode-k rank dependency is particularly valuable and demonstrates a bold approach to understanding tensor decomposition problems.
