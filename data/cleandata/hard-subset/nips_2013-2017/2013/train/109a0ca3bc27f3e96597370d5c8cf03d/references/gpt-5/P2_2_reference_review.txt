Summary
The paper studies convex approaches to tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms. It contrasts two families: (i) overlapped norms that penalize all mode unfoldings simultaneously (e.g., sum of nuclear norms), and (ii) latent norms that decompose a tensor into a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode, and penalize the sum of their mode-specific nuclear norms. The authors’ main contributions are: (a) a duality result showing that the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm; (b) statistical guarantees for the latent approach in a denoising setting, including a consistency result, a deterministic error bound under an incoherence-type condition, and a high-probability bound for Gaussian noise; and (c) empirical evidence that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, with error scaling governed by the minimum mode rank rather than the average of mode ranks. The work builds on and connects to frameworks for decomposable regularizers and structured sparsity (e.g., latent group lasso) and complements prior analyses of overlapped tensor norms.

Relation to prior work
The overlapped approach and its analysis have been studied (e.g., Tomioka et al. 2011/2013). The latent approach was proposed empirically but lacked rigorous denoising guarantees. The duality between overlapped and latent group norms is known in structured sparsity; the paper extends this to Schatten norms over tensor unfoldings, which is nontrivial. The statistical analysis adapts ideas from decomposable regularizers (Agarwal/Negahban/Wainwright) to the tensor setting and to the latent decomposition with additional spectral incoherence constraints. Compared to prior bounds for overlapped norms, the latent bounds depend on min mode rank, explaining observed empirical advantages when only one mode is low-rank.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The results are carefully stated, with proofs deferred to the appendix. The duality lemma is plausible and valuable, and the consistency and Gaussian-noise bounds follow the modern M-estimation template. The deterministic bound employs an incoherence condition and adds spectral norm constraints across mismatched modes to control component interactions; this mirrors “dirty model” analyses and appears technically sound.
- Assumptions: The incoherence condition and the additional constraints involving α are important to the bounds; they may be strong in practice and require guidance on how to set α. The choice of λ is explicit and independent of unknown ranks, which is appealing.
- Completeness: The theoretical development for denoising is complete. Tensor completion is left as future work.
- Experiments: Synthetic denoising experiments corroborate the predicted scaling with size and latent ranks and show improved performance over the overlapped approach, including in some regimes where the tensor is simultaneously low-rank. However, experiments are limited to Gaussian noise, moderate sizes, no missing data, and the LR complexity is computed using a decomposition of the noiseless W*, which is not available in practice (acceptable for validating scaling, but should be acknowledged).

Clarity
The paper is generally well organized and readable for the target audience. Definitions (unfolding, norms) are precise; the connection between optimization formulations and induced norms is clear. Some notation is heavy; a small table of symbols would help. The role and practical setting of α deserve more discussion. There are minor typos and copyediting issues.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped tensor Schatten norms and latent tensor Schatten norms appears novel and conceptually neat, generalizing known results from group sparsity to the tensor/Schatten setting.
- The denoising guarantees for the latent approach, especially the scaling with min mode rank, are new and sharpen understanding relative to existing overlapped-norm analyses.
- The work synthesizes and extends existing tools (decomposable regularizers, latent models) in a meaningful way.

Significance
The results advance the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition. Showing that latent norms can adapt to the easiest mode (min rank) provides clear guidance for practice and explains empirical behavior. The contribution is primarily theoretical but relevant to applications in multiway data analysis, collaborative filtering with context, and spatiotemporal modeling. That said, the empirical section could be strengthened (larger tensors, missing-data settings, real data, sensitivity to α and λ). The added spectral constraints may limit immediate adoption unless guidance is provided.

Strengths
- Clear, general duality between overlapped and latent tensor Schatten norms.
- Sharp denoising bounds for the latent approach with explicit λ, adapting to min mode rank.
- Conceptual bridge to structured sparsity literature; principled comparison to overlapped approach.
- Experiments that match theoretical scaling predictions; code availability.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality of the spectral incoherence constraints and α: provide a data-driven way to set α, sensitivity analysis, or results without explicit constraints.
- Experiments limited in scope; add tensor completion and real-data benchmarks; explore K > 3 and larger scales.
- The LR complexity used for plotting relies on decomposing W* without noise; discuss how to estimate or approximate relevant complexity in practice.
- Improve exposition of assumptions and constants in Theorem 3 (factor F), and add intuition for scaling terms.
- Minor typos and notation density.

Questions for the authors
- Can the deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds be obtained without explicit spectral constraints, perhaps under standard incoherence on factor spaces?
- How sensitive is performance to misspecification of α, and can α be tuned jointly with λ?
- Does the duality extend to weighted/mixed norms and to non-uniform groupings across modes?
- What is the computational complexity and convergence behavior of the ADMM solver as K and dimensions grow?

Overall assessment
The paper meets the NeurIPS criteria well: technically solid, clearly written, original, and significant within convex tensor methods. I lean toward acceptance. Strengthening the empirical section and clarifying the role of α would further improve the paper.
