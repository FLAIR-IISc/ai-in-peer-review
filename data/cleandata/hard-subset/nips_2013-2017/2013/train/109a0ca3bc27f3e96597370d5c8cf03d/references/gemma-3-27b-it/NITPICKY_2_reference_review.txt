This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically contrasting an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating the superior performance of the latent approach, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The authors establish consistency results and provide bounds on the estimation error, highlighting the role of the minimum mode-k rank. They also present a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms and empirically validate their findings.

While the problem setting is relevant and the theoretical results are presented with a degree of rigor, several issues detract from the overall quality of the paper. The presentation suffers from numerous minor, yet persistent, inconsistencies and a lack of precision that, cumulatively, diminish the impact of the work. 

First, the notation is occasionally ambiguous and inconsistent. For example, the subscript notation for tensors (W ∈ Rn1×···nK) is clear, but the repeated use of W(k) to denote both a tensor and its k-th mode unfolding creates confusion. A clearer distinction, such as W(k) for the tensor and W^[k] for the unfolding, would be beneficial. Similarly, the definition of the Frobenius norm uses both ||.||F and √⟨.,.⟩, without clear justification for the interchangeability. 

Second, several technical points lack sufficient detail. The proof sketches mentioned (“proof is presented in Appendix A”) are unacceptable for a NIPS submission; the appendices must be complete and self-contained. The connection to the broader field of structured sparsity, while mentioned, isn't fully exploited. The authors state that their results "generalize" previous work but don’t fully elucidate *how* they generalize it beyond a superficial comparison. Lemma 2, stated as a key step for understanding the benefits of the latent norm, is not demonstrably more insightful than a direct application of the inequality (3) used for the overlapped norm. The benefit isn't immediately obvious, and further discussion is needed.

Third, the empirical validation, while present, lacks robustness. The experiment uses only one specific tensor size (50x50x20 or 80x80x40) and a single noise level (σ = 0.1). This limits the generalizability of the results. The authors claim that the optimal regularization constant is independent of the rank and only depends on the size, but they scale the regularization constants by “roughly 1.6” without explanation—a precise scaling factor should be provided and justified.  The phrase "series with △... is mostly below other series” is vague and requires a more quantitative analysis. A statistical test to confirm the improvement in performance should be presented.

Fourth, the introduction suffers from repetitive phrasing and a lack of focus. The motivation is presented multiple times, and the flow feels disjointed. The sentence “Thus we provide both theoretical and empirical evidence…” is overly emphatic and unnecessary.

Fifth, minor typographical errors and grammatical inconsistencies are present throughout the manuscript, further detracting from its polish. For instance, the use of "tucker rank" instead of "Tucker rank" (proper noun) and inconsistencies in punctuation.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The problem of tensor decomposition is important in many application areas.
*   The theoretical analysis, while not entirely novel, provides some insight into the performance of the latent approach.
*   The empirical results, although limited, support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The presentation is marred by numerous minor errors and inconsistencies.
*   The theoretical results lack sufficient depth and clarity.
*   The empirical validation is limited in scope and lacks statistical rigor.
*   The connection to related work is not fully explored.
*   The writing quality is subpar for a NIPS submission.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the potential novelty of the theoretical results, the numerous shortcomings in presentation, technical detail, and empirical validation outweigh the strengths of this paper. The paper feels incomplete and lacks the polish expected of a NIPS submission. While the ideas are potentially interesting, the current manuscript requires substantial revision before it would be suitable for publication.

**Recommendation:** Reject. A major revision addressing the concerns outlined above is required before reconsideration. The authors should focus on clarifying the notation, providing complete proofs, strengthening the empirical validation, and improving the overall presentation and writing quality.
