## Peer Review for "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates convex approaches to tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" Schatten norm regularization methods. It provides a theoretical analysis of the latent approach, demonstrating that its error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, explaining its empirical superiority when dealing with tensors low-rank in only a few modes.  The authors establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing related results in structured sparsity literature.  They support their theoretical findings with numerical experiments, confirming the predicted scaling behavior and further showcasing the latent approach’s benefits.

**Related Work & Context:**

The paper builds on a growing body of work utilizing convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, addressing a key challenge in the field – the often intractable non-convex nature of traditional methods. It acknowledges prior work on Tucker decomposition and related approximation bounds (e.g., De Lathauwer et al., 2005) and recent convex approaches (e.g., Gandy et al., 2014). Importantly, it distinguishes itself by providing theoretical justification for the empirically observed advantages of the latent approach (Tomioka et al., 2015) and exploring the underlying mathematical connections between different regularization strategies. The work also nicely connects to the broader literature on structured sparsity and group lasso methods (e.g., Jenatton et al., 2011, Obozinski et al., 2013).

**Strengths:**

* **Strong Theoretical Analysis:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical framework for understanding the latent approach. The key result demonstrating the error scaling with the minimum rank is a significant contribution.
* **Novel Duality Result:** Establishing the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms offers valuable insights into the relationships between these regularization techniques. This expands the understanding of structured sparsity.
* **Clear Explanation of Empirical Success:** The theoretical findings elegantly explain the performance observed by Tomioka et al. (2015), solidifying the latent approach’s value.
* **Complementary to Existing Work:**  The paper builds upon and complements previous research on tensor decomposition and convex optimization. It addresses gaps in the theoretical understanding of the latent approach.
* **Numerical Validation:** The experimental results provide compelling evidence supporting the theoretical predictions. The comparison with the overlapped approach strengthens the claims.
* **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to follow the theoretical developments.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** Theorem 2 relies on the relatively strong assumption of incoherence (Equation 10), which may not always hold in practice. While the authors acknowledge this, a discussion of the robustness of the results to violations of this assumption would be valuable.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not delve into the computational complexity of the algorithms used, especially for larger tensors. A discussion of the scalability of the latent approach would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** While the numerical results are supportive, they are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring a wider range of tensor sizes, rank configurations, and noise levels would further strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Connection to Tensor Completion:** The authors mention tensor completion as a future work direction. Briefly discussing the potential challenges and modifications required to adapt their analysis to the tensor completion setting would be useful.
* **Loss Function in (5):** The formulation in Equation 5 uses a generic loss function L. Clarifying what specific loss function is typically used in practice (e.g., squared error) would enhance clarity.



**Qualitative Evaluation:**

* **Quality:**  Strong. The paper presents a technically sound and well-supported analysis. The theoretical results are novel and significant.
* **Clarity:** Good. The paper is generally clearly written, although the mathematical notation can be dense at times. 
* **Originality:** High. The theoretical analysis of the latent approach and the established duality are original contributions.
* **Significance:**  High.  The results have the potential to significantly impact the field of tensor decomposition and convex optimization, providing a better understanding of the properties and performance of different regularization techniques. Researchers and practitioners working with tensor data are likely to find these insights valuable.

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for the empirically observed success of the latent approach to tensor decomposition.
* Introduces a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, expanding our understanding of structured sparsity.
* Offers practical implications for selecting the appropriate tensor decomposition method based on the characteristics of the data.
*  Is a valuable contribution to the growing body of work on convex optimization for tensor problems.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Relies on somewhat strong assumptions regarding incoherence.
* Lacks detailed discussion of computational complexity and scalability.
* Numerical experiments could be more comprehensive.




**Overall Recommendation:**

**Strong Accept.** This paper presents a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The authors have convincingly demonstrated the benefits of the latent approach through rigorous analysis and supporting numerical experiments. While some minor weaknesses exist, they do not detract from the overall quality and impact of the work.  This paper is a valuable addition to the NIPS program.



**Confidence:** 9/10
