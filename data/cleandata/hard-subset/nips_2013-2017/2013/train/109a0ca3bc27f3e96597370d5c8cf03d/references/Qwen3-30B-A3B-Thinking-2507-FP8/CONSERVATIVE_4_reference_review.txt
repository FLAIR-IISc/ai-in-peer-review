

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper proposes a "latent approach" for convex tensor decomposition, contrasting it with the previously studied "overlapped approach." The authors claim that their latent approach, which employs a "latent Schatten norm," outperforms the overlapped approach both theoretically and empirically. They establish a duality between the two approaches and prove that the error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. They provide empirical evidence supporting their claims.

## Strengths
The paper is technically sound in its mathematical development and provides a clear duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms. The theoretical analysis builds on established structured sparsity literature, and the paper demonstrates consistency of the latent approach. The empirical results align with their theoretical predictions for the scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

## Weaknesses

### Insufficient Validation of Key Claims
The paper's main claim is that the latent approach is superior to the overlapped approach, particularly in the challenging case where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. However, their empirical validation (Section 4) fails to adequately test this scenario. As the authors themselves admit, "most of the randomly generated low-rank tensors were simultaneously low-rank to some degree," which is actually the case where the overlapped approach should perform well. Their experiments don't sufficiently cover the regime where the latent approach is supposed to excel. Figure 2 shows only "milder" improvements, and the authors acknowledge that "the improvements here are milder than that in Figure 1" because their test cases weren't sufficiently skewed toward the single-mode low-rank scenario.

### Lack of Comparison with Established Methods
The paper only compares against the overlapped approach, without benchmarking against standard tensor decomposition methods (e.g., Tucker decomposition, CANDECOMP/PARAFAC, or other convex methods). Without these comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the latent approach offers any meaningful advantage over the current state of the art in tensor decomposition. The paper's title and claims overstate the novelty and significance of the contribution by not situating it within the broader landscape of tensor decomposition methods.

### Limited Empirical Scope
The experiments are limited to synthetic data with specific parameter ranges (50×50×20 and 80×80×40 tensors), without testing on real-world datasets where tensor decomposition is commonly applied (e.g., neuroimaging, collaborative filtering). The paper doesn't address computational complexity or scalability, which are crucial for real-world applications.

### Overstated Significance
The paper claims that "for noisy tensor decomposition, the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach" and "the latent approach performs better in many cases." However, the evidence provided only shows it performs better in cases where the tensor is low-rank in a single mode, which the authors acknowledge is not the typical case in their experiments. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this scenario is common in real applications.

### Theoretical Contribution
The theoretical analysis appears to be a straightforward application of existing structured sparsity concepts rather than a novel theoretical contribution. The key novelty is in applying known structured sparsity techniques to tensor decomposition, not in developing new theory. The paper doesn't establish a new theoretical framework but rather applies existing tools to a specific problem.

## Overall Assessment

While the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the analysis of convex tensor decomposition methods, it fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its main claim that the latent approach is generally preferable. The empirical validation is limited and doesn't adequately test the most challenging case where the authors claim the latent approach excels. The paper also doesn't compare against standard tensor decomposition methods, making it difficult to assess the significance of their contribution.

The paper's theoretical analysis is sound but not sufficiently novel, and the empirical results don't provide compelling evidence of the latent approach's superiority in practical scenarios. The authors should extend their experiments to cover the single-mode low-rank case more thoroughly and compare against established tensor decomposition methods before making claims about the superiority of their approach.

## Recommendation

I recommend rejection. The paper makes a marginal contribution that is not sufficiently validated against the relevant baseline methods and real-world scenarios. The empirical evidence provided does not adequately support the paper's main claims, and the significance of the contribution is overstated. For a paper to be accepted at NIPS, it should demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods with rigorous validation, which this paper does not do.
