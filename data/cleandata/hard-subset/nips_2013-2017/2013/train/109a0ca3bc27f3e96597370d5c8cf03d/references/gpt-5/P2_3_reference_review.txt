Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families: overlapped Sp/q norms that penalize all mode-unfoldings simultaneously, and latent Sp*/q* norms that model a sum of components, each low-rank along a specific mode. A central contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm. Specializing to S1/1, this shows the latent formulation used in prior empirical work is the convex dual of the commonly used overlapped S1/1 norm. The authors then analyze the denoising performance of the latent approach. Key inequalities show the latent S1/1 norm is bounded by the Frobenius norm times the minimal square root of mode ranks (as opposed to a sum in the overlapped case). Theorems establish: (i) a basic consistency bound under a mild condition on λ; (ii) a deterministic error bound under an incoherence-like assumption and additional spectral norm constraints on cross-mode components, yielding an overall error scaling with the minimum mode rank; (iii) a Gaussian-noise bound with explicit λ that is independent of unknown ranks and yields MSE scaling with σ^2 times the sum of latent ranks divided by the smallest dimension, up to mild factors. Comparisons to prior bounds for the overlapped approach [26] show strictly better scaling when only one mode is low-rank, explaining empirical observations. Experiments on synthetic tensors corroborate the predicted scaling and often show the latent approach outperforming the overlapped one. A solver is referenced.

Quality
- Theory: The duality lemma generalizes known overlapped/latent group-sparsity dualities to tensor Schatten norms and appears technically sound. The latent norm inequality leveraging the minimum mode-rank is the pivotal insight that drives improved statistical rates. The consistency and Gaussian error bounds follow the decomposable-regularizer template (e.g., Negahban-Wainwright) adapted to tensor matricizations. The deterministic bound relies on an additional incoherence-like assumption and explicit spectral norm constraints between components; this is reasonable but somewhat strong. The choice of λ in the Gaussian case is carefully derived from spectral norm concentration for unfolded Gaussian matrices; the factors seem plausible.
- Rigor/caveats: Most proofs are deferred to the supplement, which is common. One potential gap is the mismatch between the analyzed estimator in (11), which includes spectral norm constraints with parameter α, and the practical solver description; it is not fully clear whether these constraints were actually enforced in experiments. The Gaussian bounds and λ do not require knowledge of ranks, which is practical; however, the LR complexity used to interpret experiments is defined via an oracle decomposition obtained by running the latent method on noiseless W*, which is not available in practice.
- Experiments: Synthetic experiments are controlled and do validate the claimed scaling, including size dependence and λ independence from rank. However, they are limited in scope: moderate sizes, no missing data/tensor completion, no comparison to non-convex baselines (ALS, HOOI/CP), and no stress tests for violations of incoherence.

Clarity
- The paper is generally well-organized: clear definitions of unfoldings, tensor ranks, and norms; a clean separation of norm properties, main theorems, and experiments. The narrative consistently relates theory to intuition (min vs sum of ranks). Figures are used appropriately to illustrate scaling.
- Minor issues: A few typos/formatting glitches and references missing details; explicit mention of whether the spectral norm constraints in (11) are enforced in practice would help. Some notation (e.g., r̄k vs rk) could be emphasized more to avoid confusion. A brief discussion on computational complexity and convergence behavior of the ADMM solver would improve reproducibility.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel in the tensor setting and a natural but nontrivial generalization of latent group lasso dualities. The statistical analysis of the latent S1/1 approach for tensor denoising appears new and complements prior results focused on the overlapped norm [26] or noiseless completion [25].
- The insight that the latent approach adapts to the minimum mode rank—and the precise rates—is a clear conceptual contribution.

Significance
- Tensors are central in many ML applications; providing sharper guarantees and guidance on when latent vs overlapped convex models are preferable is impactful. The results should influence practitioners’ choice of regularizers and inspire follow-up work on completion, robustness, and more general norms.
- The work advances state of the art both theoretically (rates depending on min mode rank) and empirically (consistent scaling). Broader empirical validation and comparisons would further increase impact, but the current contributions are valuable.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advances: duality, norm inequality, consistency and Gaussian bounds with favorable scaling.
- Conceptual clarity explaining the observed superiority of the latent approach when only one mode is low-rank.
- Experiments that align with theory and a public solver.
- Solid connections to structured sparsity literature.

Weaknesses
- Assumptions and estimator in (11) include spectral norm constraints with parameter α; enforcement and practical selection of α are not clearly demonstrated in experiments.
- Experiments limited to denoising on synthetic data; no tensor completion, robustness tests, or comparisons with standard non-convex baselines.
- LR complexity used for plotting is oracle-based, which limits practical interpretability.
- Proofs deferred; some constants/factors could be discussed more to gauge tightness.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify whether and how the spectral norm constraints are enforced in experiments; discuss sensitivity to α and how to choose it.
- Add comparisons with CP/Tucker ALS or HOOI under similar settings to contextualize gains.
- Provide results for tensor completion and report how missingness affects the theory/λ selection.
- Discuss computational costs and scalability of the ADMM solver; include convergence diagnostics.
- Expand on the potential of Sp/q with p≠1,q≠1 and whether the analysis extends.

Arguments for acceptance
- Novel, technically sound theoretical contributions that resolve an important question (why latent helps) with clean rates.
- Clear link to and extension of existing structured sparsity theory.
- Practical guidance (λ independent of rank) and empirical validation.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental scope is narrow and does not fully stress the proposed method against realistic or competitive baselines.
- Some analytical assumptions (spectral constraints) are not convincingly instantiated in practice.

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The theoretical contributions and clarity outweigh the experimental limitations; addressing the noted clarifications would make this a strong NeurIPS paper.
