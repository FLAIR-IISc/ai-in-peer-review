

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper introduces two structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition: the overlapped Schatten norm and the latent Schatten norm. The authors establish a duality between these norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature (e.g., group lasso). They then provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, showing that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. The paper connects to recent work in convex tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] while providing a more comprehensive theoretical framework. The authors validate their theoretical claims with empirical results demonstrating the scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature. This duality is both elegant and insightful.

2. **Rigorous Analysis:** The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-3) is thorough and well-supported. The authors provide both consistency guarantees and deterministic error bounds that clearly explain why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain scenarios.

3. **Clear Explanatory Power:** The paper successfully explains the empirical observation that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach (Figure 1) by showing that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank. This is a major conceptual advance.

4. **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments in Section 4 effectively validate the theoretical claims, demonstrating linear scaling of MSE with the predicted complexity measures (TR and LR complexity).

5. **Careful Comparison with Prior Work:** The authors properly contextualize their work with respect to previous approaches [25, 26], acknowledging both the strengths and limitations of prior work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Clarity of Key Concepts:** While the paper is generally well-written, the latent Schatten norm (Equation 6) could be explained more intuitively in the main text. The abstract nature of this definition might hinder understanding for readers unfamiliar with tensor decomposition.

2. **Limited Computational Perspective:** The paper lacks discussion of computational complexity comparisons between the two approaches. For practitioners, understanding the computational cost of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach would be valuable.

3. **Narrower Experimental Scope:** The numerical experiments focus on specific tensor sizes (50×50×20 and 80×80×40) and noise levels. A more comprehensive experimental comparison across a wider range of tensor dimensions and noise regimes would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. **Practical Application Discussion:** While the paper mentions applications in neuroimaging and collaborative filtering, it doesn't provide concrete examples of how the latent approach would specifically improve real-world applications compared to existing methods.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of convex tensor decomposition. The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable insight that connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-motivated, and the empirical results effectively validate the theoretical claims.

The paper's main limitations are the somewhat abstract presentation of the latent Schatten norm and the narrow scope of the numerical experiments. However, these are relatively minor issues compared to the paper's substantial theoretical contribution.

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The authors should:
1. Add a more intuitive explanation of the latent Schatten norm in the main text
2. Include a brief discussion of computational complexity comparisons
3. Extend the numerical experiments to cover a broader range of tensor dimensions

The paper advances our theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition and provides a clear explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. It represents a meaningful contribution to the field that would be valuable to both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data. The connection to structured sparsity literature is particularly strong, making this work relevant to a broader audience at NeurIPS.

The paper meets all four criteria: it is of high quality, clear (with minor improvements possible), original, and significant for the field. The theoretical insights provide a foundation for future work in tensor decomposition and structured learning.
