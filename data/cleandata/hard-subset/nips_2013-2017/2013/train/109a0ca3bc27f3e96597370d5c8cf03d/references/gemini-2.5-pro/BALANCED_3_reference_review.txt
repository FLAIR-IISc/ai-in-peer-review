### Summary

This paper investigates two convex approaches for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes the sum of Schatten norms of all matrix unfoldings, and the "latent" approach, which models the tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. The paper's main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting.

The key contributions are:
1.  It formalizes the concepts of "overlapped" and "latent" Schatten norms for tensors, drawing a clear and insightful parallel to similar concepts in the structured sparsity literature (e.g., overlapped/latent group LASSO).
2.  It establishes a novel duality between these two classes of norms (Lemma 1), which is a valuable technical result in its own right.
3.  It provides the first theoretical performance guarantee for the latent approach. The main result (Theorems 2 and 3) shows that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks (`min_k r_k`) of the underlying tensor.
4.  This result is contrasted with prior work on the overlapped approach [26], whose error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical explanation for the empirical observation that the latent approach excels when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode but potentially full-rank in others.
5.  The theoretical scaling predictions are carefully validated through numerical experiments.

### Strengths

*   **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a crucial piece of theory that has been missing in the literature on convex tensor decomposition. The error bound's dependence on `min_k r_k` is a clean, intuitive, and important result. It formalizes the intuition that the latent model can adaptively find the "easiest" decomposition, effectively performing a kind of implicit model selection over the modes.
*   **Novelty and Elegance of the Duality Framework:** The duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is a novel and elegant contribution. It provides a unifying mathematical framework that connects this line of work to the broader and well-studied field of structured sparsity, which can facilitate future theoretical developments.
*   **Exceptional Clarity:** The paper is extremely well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem, clearly defining the two competing approaches, and situating their work within the existing literature. The comparison between the two approaches is sharp and insightful.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are not just for showing state-of-the-art performance but are specifically designed to verify the theoretical scaling laws derived in the paper. Figure 2 provides compelling evidence that the MSE scales linearly with the proposed complexity measures, lending strong support to the theoretical analysis.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While this is a very strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or improved:

1.  **Mismatch between Theory and Experiments regarding Incoherence:** The main theoretical analysis (Theorem 2) relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and, more importantly, incorporates this as an explicit constraint in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). However, the experiments seem to use the algorithm from [25], which solves the unconstrained problem (Eq. 5). This creates a potential gap between the theory that is proven and the method that is tested. It would strengthen the paper to:
    *   Explicitly acknowledge this gap.
    *   Discuss whether the theoretical results are expected to hold even without the explicit constraint, perhaps under slightly different assumptions.
    *   Clarify how the parameter `alpha` in the theoretical regularization constant `lambda` was handled, given that the constraint involving `alpha` was not used in the optimization.

2.  **Clarity of the Latent Rank (LR) Complexity:** The LR complexity measure used in the experiments (Eq. 18) is based on the sum of ranks of the latent factors (`sum r_bar_k`). The main theoretical result for the overall error (Eq. 15), however, is stated in terms of the much simpler `min_k r_k` of the true tensor `W*`. While the paper connects these by arguing a singleton decomposition is always possible, the experimental validation could be more direct. A plot of the MSE of the latent approach directly against `min_k r_k` would provide a more direct and compelling confirmation of the paper's central claim in Eq. (15).

3.  **Scope of the Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the denoising setting with a fully observed tensor. While this is a standard and important setting, many practical applications (e.g., collaborative filtering) involve tensor completion from a small number of observations. While the conclusion rightly points to this as future work, the paper's significance could be slightly enhanced by a brief discussion in the main text about the challenges of extending this analysis to the completion setting (e.g., how the sampling operator would interact with the dual norm).

### Decision

This paper presents a high-quality, technically sound, and significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The work is original, clearly presented, and the findings are well-supported by both theory and experiments. The weaknesses are minor and primarily relate to points of clarification that do not undermine the core contributions.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.
*   The `min_k r_k` error dependence is a novel and important insight with clear practical implications.
*   The duality framework is an elegant and foundational contribution.
*   The paper is exceptionally clear and well-written.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   There is a minor disconnect between the theoretical model (with incoherence constraints) and the experimental implementation.

The arguments for acceptance are overwhelmingly stronger. This paper provides fundamental insights into a popular class of models and will likely be highly cited. I strongly recommend acceptance.
