

# Peer Review: "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in tensor decomposition: the comparison between two convex optimization approaches for low-rank tensor recovery. The authors formalize and analyze two methods: the "overlapped approach" (which penalizes all tensor unfoldings simultaneously) and the "latent approach" (which decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The key contribution is establishing a duality between the corresponding norms (overlapped Schatten vs. latent Schatten) and proving that the latent approach's error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank rather than the average mode-k rank as in the overlapped approach. This provides a theoretical explanation for the empirical observation that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain scenarios (as shown in Figure 1). The paper connects these tensor decomposition methods to structured sparsity literature, showing they are natural extensions of overlapped/latent group sparsity.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), which is novel and generalizes existing results in structured sparsity. The performance guarantees (Theorems 2 and 3) are rigorous and provide a clear theoretical explanation for the empirical observations.

2. **Clear Explanation of Empirical Observation**: The paper successfully explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in Figure 1 through the theoretical bound in Equation (15), which scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach (Equation 16). This is a major contribution to the field.

3. **Comprehensive Theoretical Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough theoretical analysis of the latent approach, including consistency (Theorem 1), deterministic bounds (Theorem 2), and Gaussian noise analysis (Theorem 3). The authors carefully consider the incoherence assumption (Equation 10), which is common in matrix and tensor recovery literature.

4. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The numerical experiments (Section 4) provide compelling empirical support for the theoretical claims. The MSE scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (TR complexity for overlapped approach, LR complexity for latent approach), confirming the theoretical predictions.

5. **Connections to Broader Literature**: The paper effectively connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, showing how the two approaches correspond to overlapped/latent group sparsity. This broadens the relevance of the work beyond tensor decomposition.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope to Denoising Setting**: The theoretical analysis focuses exclusively on the denoising setting (Equation 9), while tensor decomposition is most commonly used for tensor completion (with missing values). The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, but it's a significant gap. The analysis doesn't directly apply to the more common tensor completion problem, which is a major application of tensor decomposition.

2. **Questionable Assumptions in Theoretical Analysis**: The incoherence assumption (Equation 10) that the spectral norm of mode-k unfoldings of the lth component is bounded by α for all k ≠ l is quite strong. While common in the literature, it may not hold in practical applications. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this assumption is reasonable in typical tensor data scenarios.

3. **Lack of Practical Guidance for λ Selection**: The paper notes that the theoretically optimal λ doesn't depend on the unknown ranks (Theorem 3), but doesn't provide practical guidance for selecting λ in real-world applications. The numerical experiments use a grid search over regularization constants, which is not scalable to large tensors.

4. **Ambiguous Terminology**: The term "latent approach" is not clearly explained. While it's referenced as in [25], the paper doesn't provide a clear intuition for why this approach is called "latent" (as opposed to "overlapped"). This could confuse readers unfamiliar with the previous work.

5. **Limited Comparison with Other Methods**: The paper only compares the two approaches (overlapped vs. latent), but doesn't compare them against other state-of-the-art tensor decomposition methods (e.g., non-convex methods like CP decomposition or Tucker decomposition). This limits the significance of the comparison.

## Technical Assessment

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with rigorous theoretical results. The duality between the norms (Lemma 1) is a novel and important contribution that generalizes the relationship between overlapped/latent group sparsity norms. The performance bounds (Theorems 2 and 3) are well-justified and provide a clear theoretical explanation for the empirical observations.

The key insight that the latent approach's error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank (Equation 15) rather than the average (Equation 16) is compelling and well-supported by the theory. This explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in the case where the tensor is only low-rank in one mode (as in Figure 1).

However, the assumption that the unknown tensor can be decomposed into components each low-rank in a specific mode (Equation 9) is restrictive. In practice, the true tensor might not have such a decomposition, and the paper doesn't address how the method would perform when this assumption is violated.

The numerical experiments are well-designed and confirm the theoretical predictions, but they're limited to randomly generated tensors that are simultaneously low-rank to some degree. This might not fully capture the worst-case scenario where the latent approach's advantage is most pronounced (as in Figure 1).

## Related Work

The paper properly cites the relevant literature on convex tensor decomposition ([9, 15, 23, 25, 26]) and connects to structured sparsity literature ([2, 13, 17, 21]). However, it misses a key connection to the recent work on tensor completion, particularly the paper "Tensor Completion and Low-n-Rank Tensor Recovery via Convex Optimization" by Gandy et al. (2011), which is foundational in the field. While the authors reference [25, 26], they don't explicitly connect their work to this earlier work on tensor completion.

The paper also doesn't discuss the relationship to non-convex tensor decomposition methods, which are the dominant approach in practice. The authors acknowledge that their work is complementary to previous studies on noise-less tensor completion, but they don't discuss how their convex approach compares to non-convex methods in terms of computational efficiency or performance.

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong theoretical contribution that provides a clear explanation for an empirical observation in tensor decomposition. The duality between the norms and the performance guarantees are novel and significant. The paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is sound.

However, the focus on the denoising setting rather than the more common tensor completion problem is a significant limitation. The paper would be stronger if it addressed how the theory extends to tensor completion or at least acknowledged this limitation more thoroughly.

The paper's main contribution is explaining why the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach in certain scenarios. This is a valuable contribution to the field, particularly for practitioners choosing between these approaches.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The duality between the norms and the performance guarantees are novel and well-supported. While the focus on the denoising setting is a limitation, the theoretical insights are valuable and the empirical results strongly support the claims.

The paper would benefit from addressing the tensor completion setting in future work, but as a theoretical contribution, it stands on its own merits. The connection to structured sparsity literature and the clear explanation of the empirical observation in Figure 1 are particularly strong.

The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a clear contribution to the field. I believe it would be a valuable addition to NIPS.

## Specific Suggestions for Improvement

1. In the conclusion, explicitly state that the theoretical analysis applies to denoising, not tensor completion, and briefly discuss how the theory might extend to tensor completion.

2. Provide more intuition for why the approach is called "latent" and clarify the terminology.

3. Include a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach (the paper mentions using ADMM, but doesn't compare the computational requirements).

4. Consider adding a comparison to at least one non-convex tensor decomposition method in the empirical section to better contextualize the results.

5. In the numerical experiments, include a case where the tensor is only low-rank in one mode (as in Figure 1) to more clearly demonstrate the latent approach's advantage.

## Summary of Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Clear theoretical explanation for empirical observation in Figure 1
- Novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms
- Rigorous performance guarantees for the latent approach
- Strong empirical validation of theoretical predictions
- Good connection to structured sparsity literature

**Cons:**
- Focus on denoising rather than tensor completion (the more common application)
- Strong incoherence assumption (Equation 10) without justification
- Limited practical guidance for regularization parameter selection
- Ambiguous terminology ("latent approach")
- Limited comparison with non-convex methods

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution that addresses an important question in tensor decomposition. The paper is well-written and the results are significant for the field.
