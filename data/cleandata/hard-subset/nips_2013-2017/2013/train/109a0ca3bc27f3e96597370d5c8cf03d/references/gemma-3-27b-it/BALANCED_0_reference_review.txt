This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing an "overlapped" approach to a "latent" approach. The core contribution is a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the latent approach achieves a better error bound, scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average rank. This explains empirically observed performance differences and is supported by numerical experiments. The paper establishes a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing related work on structured sparsity.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant issue in tensor decomposition – the difficulty of model selection (choosing ranks for each mode). The convex relaxation approach offers an appealing alternative, and the distinction between the overlapped and latent methods is clearly articulated. The theoretical results are the strongest aspect of the work, offering a formal justification for the superior performance of the latent approach. The connection to structured sparsity and the duality result add theoretical depth. The numerical results provide supporting evidence, albeit with the caveat that the simultaneous low-rank setting, favored by the overlapped approach, doesn’t fully showcase the latent approach’s advantage.

However, several weaknesses are present. The paper suffers from a dense presentation and a lack of intuitive explanations, especially in the technical sections. While the math is likely correct, it’s often difficult to grasp the significance of the lemmas and theorems without significant effort. The notation is somewhat heavy, with many symbols introduced without sufficient motivation.  Section 2, defining the structured Schatten norms, is particularly dense and could benefit from a more gradual introduction. The connection to existing work in structured sparsity, while acknowledged, feels superficial; a more detailed discussion of how this tensor setting differs and generalizes the prior work would strengthen the contribution. The discussion of related work is limited, particularly concerning recent advancements in tensor decomposition beyond the references provided.  For example, recent work on non-convex approaches with provable guarantees might be relevant to contrast.

The empirical evaluation, while helpful, feels limited. The experimental setup is relatively simple, and more complex datasets or scenarios (e.g., tensors with more pronounced low-rank structure in only one mode) would be valuable. The parameter selection process isn’t fully described, and sensitivity analysis to the regularization parameter would improve confidence in the results. The comparison to other tensor decomposition methods beyond the two variants studied is missing. The paper also lacks a discussion of the computational cost associated with each approach. While the optimization algorithms used are referenced, a more detailed comparison of their scalability would be useful.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical results providing a justification for the superior performance of the latent approach.
*   Clear identification and analysis of a key distinction between two prevalent convex tensor decomposition methods.
*   Novel duality result relating the overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
*   Supporting empirical evidence.
*   Addresses a practically relevant problem in tensor decomposition (model selection).

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Dense and difficult-to-follow presentation.
*   Limited discussion of related work.
*   Somewhat limited empirical evaluation.
*   Lack of discussion of computational complexity.
*   Notation is heavy and occasionally unmotivated.



**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents a valuable theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The analysis of the latent approach and the derivation of its error bounds are significant. However, the presentation and limited empirical evaluation detract from its overall impact. I believe the paper, as is, falls short of the bar for NeurIPS acceptance.  

**Recommendation:**

Reject. However, I strongly encourage the authors to revise and resubmit. Addressing the weaknesses in clarity, expanding the related work discussion, and strengthening the empirical validation would substantially improve the paper’s quality and increase its chances of acceptance. Specifically, I suggest:

1.  **Rewrite Sections 2 & 3 for Clarity:** Focus on intuitive explanations and simplify the notation where possible.
2.  **Expand Related Work:** Provide a more thorough review of recent advances in tensor decomposition, including non-convex methods and alternative convex approaches.
3.  **Strengthen Empirical Evaluation:** Use more diverse datasets, conduct a sensitivity analysis of the regularization parameter, and compare the latent approach to other relevant methods.
4.  **Discuss Computational Complexity:** Analyze the computational cost of the two approaches.
5.  **Provide More Intuition:** Explain the implications of the duality result in a more accessible way.
