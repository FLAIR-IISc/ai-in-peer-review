This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing an “overlapped” approach with a “latent” approach. The core idea—leveraging Schatten norm regularization for tensor decomposition—isn’t entirely new, building on recent work (citations 9, 15, 23, 25, 26), but the authors focus on a nuanced comparison and, crucially, attempt a theoretical justification for the empirically observed benefits of the latent approach. 

The paper is generally well-structured and the mathematical notation is clear. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature – a theoretical underpinning for the latent approach’s superior performance when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a potentially interesting theoretical contribution, and the attempt to provide generalization bounds for the latent approach is commendable.

However, despite the authors’ efforts, I find the paper ultimately unconvincing as a strong NIPS submission. My primary concern lies with the incremental nature of the work and the limitations of the theoretical results. The consistency result (Theorem 1) is quite weak, scaling with `√nk`, which feels unsatisfactory. While Theorem 2 improves upon this, the required incoherence assumption (10) feels strong and is not thoroughly justified. The experimental validation, while present, doesn’t overwhelmingly demonstrate the superiority of the latent approach, particularly when the tensor *is* simultaneously low-rank, as suggested by the introduction. The scaling analysis in Section 4, while confirming the theory, feels descriptive rather than transformative.

More critically, the paper doesn't adequately address the computational complexity of the latent approach. Convex relaxation, while providing theoretical guarantees, often comes at a significant computational cost. The authors mention using ADMM-based algorithms (footnote 2), but do not discuss scalability or runtime comparisons with the overlapped approach, or other standard tensor decomposition methods. A practical algorithm capable of handling large-scale tensors is a crucial aspect of a successful NIPS submission, and this is missing here. The discussion framing the latent approach as solving a mixture of low-rank tensors each in a specific mode is intuitive but makes the optimization problem more complex, and this increased complexity isn’t discussed.

The paper frequently references existing work (e.g., [1, 2, 3]), but often without fully explaining *how* their results are utilized or adapted.  The connection to group lasso approaches feels somewhat superficial.  The claim that this work is “complementary” to [25, 26] feels weak; rather, it seems more like a theoretical elaboration on empirical observations made in those papers.

**Strengths:**

*   Clear presentation of the problem and related work.
*   Attempt to provide theoretical justification for the latent approach.
*   Duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
*   Confirmation of theoretical scaling through numerical experiments.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Incremental nature of the contribution.
*   Weak consistency result (Theorem 1).
*   Strong incoherence assumption (10) in Theorem 2.
*   Lack of discussion on computational complexity and scalability.
*   Limited experimental validation, particularly in the simultaneous low-rank case.
*   Superficial connections to related work.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The duality result is a potentially valuable theoretical contribution.
*   The paper provides a formal analysis of the latent approach, which was previously lacking.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The theoretical results are not sufficiently strong or general to justify acceptance at NIPS.
*   The paper lacks a thorough discussion of computational considerations.
*   The experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage of the latent approach.
*   The contribution feels incremental and does not represent a major advance in the field.



**Overall Assessment:** Reject. While the paper is technically sound and well-written, it does not meet the NIPS standard for originality and significance. The theoretical contributions are limited, and the lack of discussion regarding computational complexity is a major drawback. I recommend rejection with the suggestion that the authors focus on improving the theoretical guarantees, providing a more comprehensive empirical evaluation (including scalability tests), and addressing the computational challenges of the latent approach before resubmitting.
