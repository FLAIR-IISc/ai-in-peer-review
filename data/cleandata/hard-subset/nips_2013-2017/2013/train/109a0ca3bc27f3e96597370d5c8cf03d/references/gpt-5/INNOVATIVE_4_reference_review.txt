Summary
This paper develops a unified framework of structured Schatten norms for tensors, focusing on two families: overlapped Sp/q norms (penalizing all mode unfoldings jointly) and latent Sp/q norms (mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors’ main conceptual contribution is to establish a duality between these two tensor norms: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm. Building on this, they analyze the “latent” tensor decomposition/denoising estimator and prove:

- A consistency bound for latent S1/1 regularization with an S∞/∞ control on the noise.
- A deterministic error bound under an incoherence-type condition that enforces limited cross-mode interactions among components; crucially, the overall error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, not the average across modes.
- High-probability bounds for Gaussian noise that again scale with min_k rk (up to mild factors) and identify a rank-independent choice of λ.
- A comparison to existing results for the overlapped S1/1 approach, which scales with the average of the square roots of mode ranks, clarifying when latent is superior.

Experiments on synthetic tensors corroborate the predicted scaling and typically favor the latent approach, even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across modes.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects and generalizes ideas from structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso) to the tensor/Schatten setting, formalizing a duality analogous to results known in vector/group-norm contexts. It advances beyond prior convex tensor work on overlapped nuclear norms (e.g., Tomioka et al. NIPS’11) and the empirical latent approach (Tomioka et al. 2013) by providing the first rigorous performance analysis for latent Schatten regularization. It also situates the results relative to lower bounds and matrix/tensor recovery theory (e.g., Mu et al.).

Strengths
- Novel, clean conceptual advance: the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms for tensors. This is a natural but nontrivial generalization of latent/overlapped group-norm duality and directly informs algorithmic design and analysis.
- Theoretical insight with practical implications: key inequality (Lemma 2) bounding the latent S1/1 norm by sqrt(min rank) times Frobenius norm, versus the sum-of-sqrt-ranks for overlapped. This explains, in a sharp way, why latent excels when only one mode is low-rank (a regime frequently troublesome for Tucker-style relaxations).
- Nonasymptotic denoising guarantees, including high-probability Gaussian bounds with a rank-independent tuning for λ, and a direct comparison to the best-known overlapped rates.
- The analysis and guarantees echo the “dirty model” and decomposable-regularizer frameworks, but tailored to tensors; the transfer is done carefully and yields informative, mode-aware rates.
- Experiments demonstrate the predicted linear scaling with the proposed complexity measures and show latent’s advantage across a range of settings; code availability supports reproducibility.

Weaknesses and points for improvement
- Practicality of constraints: The deterministic analysis imposes additional spectral-norm constraints across modes (parameter α). How should α be set in practice? Are the theorems robust to misspecification, or can α be absorbed into λ via a penalty rather than a hard constraint? Some guidance or empirical sensitivity analysis would help.
- Tightness and constants: The dimension dependence in Theorem 1 (∥Ŵ−W*∥F ≤ 2λ√min_k n_k) and the factor F in Theorem 3 may be pessimistic. A brief discussion of tightness and whether improved peeling or chaining arguments could sharpen these terms would be useful.
- Scope of experiments: Only synthetic denoising is shown. Given the motivating applications (completion, neuroimaging), adding at least one tensor completion experiment and a small real-data case would strengthen the empirical story and the paper’s impact.
- Hyperparameter selection: While the theory recommends a rank-independent λ, in practice λ is cross-validated. A short discussion on how to estimate noise level or choose λ in practice (beyond grid search) would be appreciated.
- Clarity of decomposition notions: The paper uses both multilinear rank (r1,…,rK) and the ranks of latent components r̄k. While this is explained, the reader would benefit from a concise paragraph highlighting when the singleton decomposition is optimal and the implications for interpreting the bounds.
- Minor: Please add more intuition around Lemma 1’s duality (e.g., simple 3-way example), and clarify whether the duality extends cleanly to p≠1 or q≠1 settings used in practice.

Quality
Technically sound and careful overall. The main claims are supported by precise statements and proofs (albeit in the supplement). The extrapolation from structured sparsity to structured Schatten norms is executed well.

Clarity
Generally well written and organized. Some sections (Sec. 3.2) could more clearly explain the role of α and the constraints. Figures are informative; adding one schematic of the latent vs overlapped decompositions would aid intuition.

Originality
High. The tensor-norm duality and the min-rank scaling results for the latent approach constitute a substantive conceptual and theoretical advance over prior convex tensor work.

Significance
High. The paper reframes how we should regularize tensors in noisy settings, with concrete guidance that is likely to influence practice and follow-up theory. It also tightens the conceptual link between structured sparsity and tensor methods.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear, general duality result for tensor Schatten norms
- Sharp and interpretable rates favoring latent regularization
- Bridges two strands of literature elegantly; likely to catalyze further work
- Reproducible experiments confirming theoretical scaling

Cons against acceptance
- Additional constraints (α) may limit practicality; empirical guidance limited
- Experiments do not include completion or real data
- Some bounds may have loose constants/dimension factors

Recommendation
Accept. The paper introduces a compelling conceptual framework and delivers nontrivial theory that explains and improves upon prior approaches. The novelty and potential impact outweigh the relatively modest empirical scope and minor practical gaps. Confidence: high.
