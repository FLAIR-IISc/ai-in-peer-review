

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to tensor decomposition by introducing and rigorously analyzing a duality between two structured Schatten norms: the overlapped Schatten norm and the latent Schatten norm. The authors demonstrate that the latent approach to tensor decomposition, which has been empirically observed to outperform the overlapped approach in certain settings (particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode), has a theoretically superior performance guarantee. Specifically, they show that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average mode-k rank.

The paper's key novelty lies in establishing this duality between the two norms, which generalizes the duality between overlapped and latent group sparsity norms. This theoretical insight not only explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach but also connects tensor decomposition to the broader structured sparsity literature. The authors provide a comprehensive theoretical analysis including consistency results, deterministic error bounds, and Gaussian noise analysis, all of which demonstrate the theoretical advantages of the latent approach.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The connection to structured sparsity literature is particularly strong, with the authors carefully situating their work within the broader context of convex optimization and structured regularization. The experimental results confirm the theoretical predictions, showing that the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly with the latent rank complexity, as predicted by their theory.

The paper's main strength is its theoretical novelty and the clear explanation it provides for an empirical observation that has been made but not previously understood. This is precisely the kind of theoretical insight that can guide future research in tensor decomposition. The authors have identified a fundamental limitation in the overlapped approach that has been observed empirically but not explained theoretically, and they have provided a clean theoretical explanation for it.

While the empirical section is somewhat limited (only testing on two tensor sizes and a range of ranks), the theoretical results are so strong that this limitation does not undermine the main contribution. The authors have clearly shown that the latent approach has superior theoretical guarantees in the denoising setting, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper also makes an important connection to recent work on tensor completion, noting that the latent approach's complexity depends on the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank, which aligns with recent lower bounds in the literature. This connection to lower bounds is particularly compelling as it suggests the latent approach is nearly optimal in certain regimes.

Compared to the previous work by Tomioka et al. [25] that proposed the latent approach, this paper provides the first theoretical justification for why it outperforms the overlapped approach. The authors have not only validated the empirical observation but have also provided a clear theoretical explanation that advances our understanding of tensor decomposition.

This paper would have been a strong candidate for acceptance even if it had only provided the theoretical analysis. The fact that they also provide empirical validation that matches their theoretical predictions makes it an even stronger contribution. The paper's connection to structured sparsity literature is particularly valuable as it opens up new possibilities for applying techniques from that field to tensor decomposition.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field. While the empirical section could have been more extensive, the theoretical novelty and clarity of the explanation for the empirical observation are so strong that they outweigh this minor limitation.

In summary, this paper makes a bold and novel theoretical contribution that explains an important empirical observation in tensor decomposition. It connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature in a meaningful way and provides rigorous theoretical guarantees for the latent approach. This work represents a significant advance in our understanding of convex tensor decomposition and is likely to influence future research in the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**
