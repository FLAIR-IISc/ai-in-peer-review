

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

## Summary
This paper introduces a theoretical framework for structured Schatten norms in tensor decomposition, specifically comparing two approaches: the "overlapped" approach (which penalizes all modes simultaneously) and the "latent" approach (which decomposes the tensor into components each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. They then provide theoretical guarantees showing that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, while the overlapped approach scales with the average of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in previous work. The paper also provides empirical validation confirming the theoretical predictions.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Contribution**: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by establishing the duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes structured sparsity literature. This connection to broader concepts in optimization is particularly valuable.

2. **Rigorous Analysis**: The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis, including consistency results (Theorem 1), deterministic bounds (Theorem 2), and Gaussian noise analysis (Theorem 3). The analysis clearly explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in specific scenarios.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The numerical experiments effectively confirm the theoretical predictions, showing that the mean squared error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (TR and LR complexity). The comparison between the two approaches in Figure 2 is particularly convincing.

4. **Clarity of Presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions of key concepts and a logical flow from theoretical development to empirical validation. The connection to related work in structured sparsity (e.g., group lasso) is well-explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Analysis**: While the paper convincingly explains the performance difference in the denoising setting, it doesn't thoroughly address when the latent approach might be inferior to the overlapped approach. The paper states that "even when the unknown tensor is simultaneously low-rank, which is a favorable situation for the overlapped approach, the latent approach performs better in many cases," but doesn't provide a clear characterization of when this is true.

2. **Practical Implementation Details**: The paper lacks sufficient discussion about the practical implementation of the latent approach, particularly regarding the choice of regularization parameter. While the theory provides conditions for λ, it's unclear how these would be applied in practice when the true ranks are unknown.

3. **Computational Complexity**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. Given that the latent approach involves solving for K components, this is an important consideration for real-world applications.

4. **Broader Impact**: The paper could better articulate the broader impact of these theoretical results. While it mentions potential extensions (e.g., using overlapped S1/∞-norm), it doesn't clearly state how these results could impact practitioners beyond the specific tensor decomposition problem.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition, with rigorous analysis that explains empirical observations from previous work. The duality result between the two types of norms is particularly elegant and valuable. However, the paper could be strengthened by addressing the practical implementation concerns and providing a more comprehensive analysis of when the latent approach is preferable.

I recommend **acceptance** with the following suggestions for improvement:

1. Add a subsection discussing practical considerations for choosing the regularization parameter λ in real-world applications, perhaps including a comparison of computational cost between the two approaches.

2. Expand the discussion on when the latent approach might be inferior to the overlapped approach, providing a more nuanced characterization of the conditions under which each method excels.

3. Include a brief comparison of computational complexity between the two approaches, as this is crucial for practitioners.

4. Consider adding a short discussion on how these theoretical results might impact the design of future tensor decomposition algorithms beyond the specific cases studied.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition methods, and with these minor improvements, it would be an excellent fit for NIPS. The work clearly advances the state of the art in this area, with both theoretical and empirical significance.
