This paper proposes a study of the “latent” approach to convex tensor decomposition, contrasting it with a previously proposed “overlapped” approach. The core idea is to decompose a tensor into components low-rank in *specific* modes, rather than attempting simultaneous low-rankness across all modes via a Schatten 1-norm penalty. The authors establish a duality between the corresponding norms and provide theoretical analysis supporting the empirical observation that the latent approach performs better when the tensor is low-rank in only a few modes. Numerical results are presented to validate the theory.

While the problem addressed – improving tensor decomposition via convex relaxation – is relevant, and the observation regarding the performance difference between the two approaches is potentially valuable, I find the paper lacking in several critical aspects. My overall assessment is that the work, as presented, does not meet the standards for acceptance at NeurIPS. 

First, the theoretical contributions, while mathematically presented, are largely extensions of existing results. The duality between the norms is interesting, but feels more like a natural consequence of existing structured sparsity theory (citations 2, 13, 17, 21) than a fundamental breakthrough. The consistency and denoising analyses build upon prior work [1, 26] and, while formally correct, don’t offer radically new insights. The claimed improvement in scaling – bounding the error by the *minimum* rank versus the average rank – is the most significant theoretical result, but its practical impact feels limited. This is because real-world tensors rarely exhibit such extreme anisotropy in rank. The strong assumptions regarding the decomposition structure (Eq. 9 and 10) severely limit the applicability of Theorem 2.

Second, the empirical validation, while present, is not sufficiently comprehensive. The experiments focus primarily on synthetic data. While this allows for controlled evaluation, it lacks the robustness and generalizability demonstrated by studies on real-world datasets. The comparison to the “overlapped” approach is reasonable, but the paper doesn't explore performance against more established, non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., alternating least squares, higher-order power iteration).  A comparison to these methods, including computational time, is crucial to assess the practical value of the proposed approach. The claim that “the latent approach performs better in many cases” (even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank) feels unsupported by the presented results. The right panel of Figure 2 shows only modest improvements.

Third, the paper’s clarity suffers from a density of mathematical notation and a lack of intuitive explanation. While the authors define the norms, the rationale behind using them and their connection to the underlying tensor decomposition problem are not always clear. A more accessible explanation of the intuitions driving the latent approach would significantly improve the paper’s impact.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Addresses a relevant problem in tensor decomposition.
*   Provides a theoretical analysis, albeit incremental, supporting the observed performance difference between the two approaches.
*   Includes empirical validation, demonstrating scaling behavior.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Theoretical contributions are largely extensions of existing work and lack substantial novelty.
*   Empirical validation is limited to synthetic data and lacks comparison to standard non-convex methods.
*   The strong assumptions required for theoretical guarantees limit practical applicability.
*   Clarity and intuitive explanation are lacking.
*   The benefits of the latent approach over established methods are not convincingly demonstrated.



**Overall Recommendation:** Reject.

The paper presents a technically sound but incremental contribution. While the theoretical analysis is correct, it doesn’t fundamentally advance the field. The empirical results are limited and do not provide a compelling argument for adopting the latent approach in practical applications. I recommend rejection, with encouragement to the authors to expand the empirical evaluation, compare against standard methods, and refine the clarity of the presentation.
