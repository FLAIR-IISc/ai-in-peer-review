Summary
The paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation, contrasting two families of structured Schatten norms: (i) “overlapped” norms that sum Schatten norms of all K mode unfoldings of a single tensor, and (ii) “latent” norms that decompose the tensor into a sum of K tensors, each penalized to be low-rank in one mode. The core technical claims are: (a) a duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms; (b) upper bounds for noisy tensor denoising under the latent approach, including a consistency result and deterministic/Gaussian-noise bounds; (c) bounds scale with the minimum mode-k rank (rather than the average of square roots of ranks as in prior overlapped-norm analyses), explaining empirical advantages of the latent approach in certain regimes. Experiments on synthetic data illustrate the predicted scaling.

Strengths
- Addresses an important question in convex tensor estimation: when and why a latent (mixture) formulation can outperform overlapped penalization.
- Clear identification of a duality between overlapped and latent tensor Schatten norms, connecting to the known overlapped/latent group-lasso dualities.
- Theoretical error bounds that highlight dependence on min rank, matching empirical behavior when one mode is much lower rank than others.
- Empirical plots attempt to verify scaling with problem size and latent-rank complexity; code reference provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Notation and clarity: The manuscript uses the same symbol S1/1 to denote two different norms (overlapped and latent). This is highly confusing and unacceptable for a theoretical paper. Distinct notation (e.g., ||·||over, p/q vs ||·||lat, p/q) is essential throughout. Related inconsistencies occur with r̄k vs rk and frequent use of W(k)(k), which is hard to parse.
- The deterministic bound (Theorem 2) and Gaussian bound (Theorem 3) critically rely on additional spectral-norm incoherence constraints between components (Eq. (10) and constraints in (11)), with a parameter α. It is unclear how α is chosen in practice, whether the solver enforces these constraints, and whether the experiments actually used (11) or the unconstrained latent formulation. The theory-experiment mismatch is a major issue.
- The prescribed λ depends on α (Theorem 3) and on the spectral norms of Gaussian noise via terms as large as √(N/nK). The magnitude and practicality of these choices are not discussed; the experimental λ selection appears ad hoc (“scaled by roughly 1.6”).
- Experiments are limited: only synthetic Gaussian denoising without missing data; no tensor completion, no real data, no runtime or scalability discussion, and no comparisons to alternative models (e.g., CP/Tucker ALS, RPCA variants, or recent max-norm/tubal norms). The evaluation uses LR complexity computed by running the latent method on the noiseless ground truth to infer ranks, which is circular for the sake of plotting and obscures practical applicability.
- The empirical setting seems not to reflect the additional constraints required by the theory; thus the presented guarantees may not justify the observed results.
- The “duality” result is valuable but largely expected from general results on overlapped/latent group norms. The novelty beyond adapting known constructions from structured sparsity to tensor-Schatten norms is limited; the paper should be more explicit about what is new versus straightforward extension.
- Comparison with the overlapped approach uses bounds with different normalizations and ignores some dimension-dependent terms ad hoc. This makes the comparison harder to interpret theoretically; the paper should present apples-to-apples bounds with matching noise assumptions and dimension factors.
- Proof-level details are deferred, and some statements feel loose (e.g., Theorem 1’s bound scales with min nk, which is very coarse and not illuminating; constants and conditions are opaque).
- Formatting and references: numerous typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “betters,” “Prof. ICCV,” “prepint”), footnotes embedded awkwardly, several references lack years/venues/page numbers, and equations have spacing/parentheses issues.

Quality
- Technical direction is sound, but key results hinge on strong/unclear constraints and produce loose bounds. The methodological novelty is modest (adaptation of latent group-norm ideas). Experimental validation is too limited and partly circular. As written, I am not fully convinced of the practical impact or tightness of the theory.

Clarity
- The notation collision for S1/1 is a serious flaw. Several definitions and assumptions are hard to follow on first read. The paper needs substantial editing for precision and readability, with explicit, consistent symbols and careful distinction between multilinear ranks rk and latent component ranks r̄k.

Originality
- The duality is a natural generalization of known results in structured sparsity. The min-rank-based bounds for the latent approach in tensors are useful, but the conceptual leap over prior art is moderate.

Significance
- Understanding when latent formulations help is important. However, the current theory’s applicability is constrained by the extra spectral-norm constraints, and experiments do not convincingly demonstrate real-world benefits or broader settings (e.g., completion). Impact is limited in its current form.

Specific suggestions
- Use distinct notation for overlapped vs latent norms throughout; restate the norms clearly at the outset and never overload symbols.
- Clarify the role of α: how is it chosen, is it known, and is it enforced in experiments? If not enforced, provide matching theory for the unconstrained problem or adjust experiments.
- Provide experiments on tensor completion and at least one real dataset; include runtime and convergence behavior; compare to strong baselines.
- Align theoretical comparisons: present bounds under the same noise model and normalization; avoid dropping dimension terms in plots.
- Discuss uniqueness/identifiability of the induced latent decomposition and how Ŵ(k) is extracted in practice.
- Fix references, typos, and formatting.

Pros for acceptance
- Addresses a timely problem; connects tensor norms to structured sparsity dualities.
- Provides useful intuition and some bounds explaining empirical phenomena.
- Potentially of interest to the community working on convex tensor methods.

Cons for acceptance
- Serious clarity/notation issues; theory-experiment mismatch; limited novelty; limited empirical validation; numerous minor errors.

Overall recommendation
- Reject. The paper has promising ideas but requires substantial revision to fix notation, align theory with practice, strengthen experiments (including completion and real data), and clean up presentation and citations.

Confidence
- High.
