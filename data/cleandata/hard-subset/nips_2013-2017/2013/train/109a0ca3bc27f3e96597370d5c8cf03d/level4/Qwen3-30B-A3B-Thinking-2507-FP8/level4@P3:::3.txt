

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, focusing on the theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach compared to the previously studied "overlapped" approach. The authors establish a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, demonstrating that the latent approach achieves error bounds dependent on the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank, which explains its empirical superiority in certain settings. This work builds upon recent convex optimization approaches to tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] and connects to structured sparsity literature [1, 2, 13, 17, 21], making it relevant to the NIPS community's interest in structured regularization techniques.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it provides the first theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach over the overlapped approach in tensor decomposition. The authors establish a duality result (Lemma 1) showing that the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm, which generalizes known results in structured sparsity. This duality is a theoretically interesting contribution that stands on its own. Second, the paper establishes rigorous error bounds for the latent approach (Theorems 2 and 3), showing that the mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank, which explains the empirical performance observed in Figure 1. Third, the authors confirm through numerical experiments that their theoretical predictions match the observed scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

The paper excels in quality. The theoretical analysis is sound, with well-constructed proofs and clear connections to relevant literature. The authors carefully address both strengths and weaknesses of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach, providing a balanced analysis that acknowledges the limitations of both methods. The experimental section, while concise, effectively validates the theoretical claims, showing that the error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (LR complexity in equation 18). The authors appropriately acknowledge that the latent approach is preferable when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode, while noting that both approaches perform similarly when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

The paper is exceptionally clear in its presentation. The organization follows a logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation. Definitions are precise, the mathematical notation is consistent, and the key results are well-explained. The supplementary material provides necessary technical details without overwhelming the main text.

The originality of the paper is strong. While both overlapped and latent approaches to tensor decomposition have been proposed in prior work [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], the duality result between these two structured norms is novel and extends the structured sparsity literature. The theoretical analysis of the latent approach's error bounds, particularly the dependence on the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank, represents a meaningful advancement over previous work. The authors properly contextualize their contribution relative to prior NIPS publications on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

The significance of the work is substantial. Tensor decomposition is a fundamental technique with applications in neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, and environmental monitoring. The paper demonstrates that the latent approach provides a theoretically superior method for noisy tensor decomposition, with practical implications for practitioners. The connection to structured sparsity literature also broadens the potential impact of the work beyond tensor decomposition. The theoretical insights about the relationship between norm duality and rank complexity could inform future work in structured learning.

The main weaknesses of the paper are related to the experimental evaluation. While the numerical results confirm the theoretical predictions, the authors could provide more detail about the experimental setup. Specifically, it would be valuable to know how the Tucker ranks (r₁,...,rₖ) were chosen for the synthetic tensors and whether these choices reflect typical real-world scenarios. Additionally, the paper does not compare the latent approach with non-convex tensor decomposition methods (such as higher-order SVD or alternating least squares), which are currently the dominant approaches in practice. A brief comparison with these methods would strengthen the significance argument by demonstrating the relative advantage of the convex approach.

The paper also briefly mentions that the theoretical optimal choice of regularization constant is independent of the unknown ranks, but does not fully elaborate on how practitioners might practically determine this constant. While the authors note that the regularization constant scales with tensor dimensions, it would be helpful to provide more specific guidance for implementation.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Provides the first theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach
- Establishes a novel duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms
- Demonstrates rigorous error bounds showing the latent approach's advantage
- Confirms theoretical predictions with numerical experiments
- Makes a clear connection to structured sparsity literature
- Well-written and well-organized

Arguments against acceptance:
- Experimental section lacks detail on how Tucker ranks were selected for synthetic data
- Does not compare with non-convex tensor decomposition methods
- Limited discussion of practical implementation considerations for the regularization constant

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to tensor decomposition and structured sparsity. The main weakness is the limited experimental comparison with non-convex approaches, but this does not undermine the theoretical contributions. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes an important advance in understanding convex tensor decomposition methods. I recommend acceptance for NIPS.
