This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex tensor decomposition method that uses the latent Schatten norm as a regularizer. This approach, which models a tensor as a sum of components that are each low-rank in a specific mode, is contrasted with the more established "overlapped" Schatten norm approach, where a single tensor is encouraged to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

The paper's primary contributions are theoretical. First, it establishes a novel and elegant duality between the general classes of overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms, which connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. Second, using this duality, the authors derive a performance bound for denoising with the latent Schatten norm. The key result of their analysis is that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor. This provides a rigorous explanation for why the latent approach can significantly outperform the overlapped approach, whose error bound depends on the average of the mode-k ranks, especially when the underlying tensor's rank is highly anisotropic across modes.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Originality and Depth:** The paper's main strength is its theoretical contribution. The duality result between the latent and overlapped Schatten norms is a clean and interesting finding that is of independent interest beyond the immediate application to tensor decomposition.
*   **Clarity of Insight:** The analysis provides a clear and compelling theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach in certain settings. By showing that the error bound depends on the minimum mode-k rank, the authors offer a rigorous explanation for behavior that was previously supported only by empirical evidence. This insight is a valuable contribution to the field.
*   **Significance:** As tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with broad applications, this work is significant. It advances the theoretical understanding of convex relaxation methods for this problem by clarifying the relative merits of two competing regularization strategies. The paper is well-written, and the core ideas are presented clearly.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

While the theoretical contributions are strong, the empirical evaluation is rather brief and could be expanded to better demonstrate the practical relevance of the findings. Specifically:

*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experiments primarily serve to confirm the derived theoretical scaling laws but leave several practical questions unanswered. The comparison is confined to the overlapped and latent approaches. To better situate the work, a comparison against standard non-convex methods (e.g., those based on alternating least squares) would be highly valuable. This would help readers understand the trade-offs in accuracy and computational cost relative to widely used baselines.
*   **Lack of Experimental Detail:** For clarity and reproducibility, more detail on the experimental setup is needed. For example, it is not specified how the multi-linear ranks (r1, r2, r3) were chosen for the synthetic data generation. Were they selected randomly, or chosen specifically to highlight certain scenarios?
*   **Further Exploration of Norms:** The paper introduces a general framework of structured Sp/q norms but focuses the analysis on the S1/1 case. While the conclusion mentions exploring other norms as future work, a brief discussion or experiment could have strengthened the paper. For instance, in the case where a tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes—a scenario described as favorable to the overlapped approach—it would be interesting to investigate if another norm from the authors' framework (e.g., the S1/∞ norm) might outperform both of the tested regularizers.

In summary, this is a well-written paper with a solid theoretical contribution. The duality result is novel, and the analysis provides valuable insight into convex methods for tensor decomposition. The main weakness is the limited scope of the experimental section. Expanding the empirical evaluation with additional details and more comprehensive baseline comparisons would make this a more complete and impactful contribution.
