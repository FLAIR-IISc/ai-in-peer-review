This paper introduces a general framework for structured Schatten norms for tensors and applies it to the problem of convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition. The framework encompasses two main approaches: the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes the trace norms of matrix unfoldings across all modes simultaneously, and the "latent" approach, which models the tensor as a sum of components, each being low-rank in a specific mode.

The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach, which was previously observed to outperform the "overlapped" method in certain empirical settings. The authors prove that the estimation error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks, rather than their sum or average. This provides a clear theoretical justification for its superior performance when the true tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes. A key technical tool developed in this work is a novel duality result between the overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms, which is of independent interest and connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The theoretical findings are validated through numerical experiments.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance):**

*   **Novelty and Significance:** The paper provides the first theoretical performance guarantees for the "latent" approach to convex tensor decomposition, elegantly explaining its empirically observed advantages. The main result—that the error bound depends on the minimum mode-k rank—is a significant and insightful contribution that advances our understanding of these methods. It shows that the latent approach effectively adapts to the underlying structure without prior knowledge of the lowest-rank mode.
*   **Technical Depth:** The introduction of a general framework for structured Schatten norms and the accompanying duality result (Lemma 1) is technically sound and provides a unifying perspective. This formally connects tensor decomposition methods to well-studied concepts in structured sparsity, which could inspire further research in both areas.
*   **Clarity and Empirical Support:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The theoretical claims are well-supported by numerical simulations that clearly demonstrate the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error for both the latent and overlapped approaches.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper makes strong technical contributions, several aspects could be improved to enhance its clarity, impact, and completeness.

*   **Motivation and Context for Machine Learning:** The paper would be more accessible to the broader NIPS audience if the motivation was strengthened from a machine learning perspective. For instance, the authors could introduce a latent variable model (e.g., where hidden variables `h_k` influence observed variables `x_k`) whose moment tensor naturally has a low multilinear rank structure. This would help readers understand scenarios where one might expect a tensor to be low-rank in only a few modes, which is the key setting where the proposed method excels. A graphical model representation would be particularly helpful.
*   **Clarity of Technical Details and Proofs:**
    *   **Theorem 2:** The statement of the theorem regarding the recovery of individual components `Ŵ^(k)` should be more precise. Since the decomposition is not unique, the components are likely recovered only up to a permutation, which should be explicitly mentioned. Furthermore, the derivation of inequality (13) from (12) is stated as being immediate but would benefit from a more detailed justification.
    *   **Supporting Lemmas:** The proof for Lemma 5, which appears crucial for the proof of Theorem 2, seems to be missing from the supplementary material. For the sake of completeness and reproducibility, it would be better to provide an explicit proof for Lemma 4 rather than only referring to another paper.
*   **Discussion and Related Work:**
    *   **Incoherence Assumption:** The paper compares its incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) to those in prior work [1, 3, 11] on sparse+low-rank matrix recovery. This comparison may be misleading, as the conditions in those papers typically involve incoherence between singular vectors and standard basis vectors. The condition here, which bounds the spectral norm of cross-mode unfoldings, appears different. The authors should clarify this distinction to avoid potential confusion.
    *   **Broader Context:** The introduction could be strengthened by briefly discussing alternative tensor decomposition methods like Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD) and its variants. Mentioning that the "mode-k rank" used here is also known as "multilinear rank" and contrasting it with other notions like CP rank would also provide valuable context.
    *   **Computational Cost:** A short discussion on the computational complexity of solving the optimization problems for both the latent and overlapped approaches would be a valuable addition for practitioners considering these methods.
*   **Minor Notational and Typographical Issues:**
    *   The notation `Ŵ^(k)` for latent components is easily confused with the mode-k unfolding `W(k)`. Using a different symbol would improve readability.
    *   The notation for mode-k rank should be used consistently throughout the paper.
    *   There is a typo in the abstract: "structures Schatten" should be "structured Schatten".
    *   A parenthesis appears to be missing in the last line of page 12 in the supplementary material.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that makes novel and significant theoretical contributions to the field of convex tensor decomposition. The analysis of the latent approach and the duality result are particularly impressive and important. Addressing the points on motivation, technical clarity, and discussion of related work would further strengthen an already excellent paper. I recommend acceptance.
