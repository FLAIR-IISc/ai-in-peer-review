This paper investigates convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms. The authors focus on the “latent” approach, recently proposed and empirically shown to outperform the “overlapped” approach in certain settings, and provide a theoretical justification for this observation. The work is well-written and makes a technically significant contribution to understanding the performance of these methods. Several minor clarifications and additions would further strengthen the paper.

The paper begins by outlining the problem of tensor decomposition and its applications, relating it to previous work in areas like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. It correctly positions recent convex relaxation approaches [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] as alternatives to traditional non-convex methods like alternating least squares. The core contribution lies in analyzing the latent approach, demonstrating theoretically that it achieves performance comparable to knowing the mode with the smallest rank, explaining its empirical success. A novel duality result for structured Schatten norms is also presented, with broader relevance to structured sparsity literature. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations confirming the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, which was previously lacking. This is a significant contribution, solidifying the empirical observations made in [25].
*   **Duality Result:** The established duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable result, potentially extending beyond tensor decomposition to other structured sparsity problems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the mathematical concepts involved.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments effectively validate the theoretical predictions, demonstrating the scaling behavior of the mean squared error.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** The paper appropriately references and contextualizes its work within the existing literature on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Motivation for Machine Learning Applications:** The paper would benefit from stronger motivation of the tensor decomposition problem within a machine learning context. Specifically, illustrating a generative model (perhaps with a graphical model) where this tensor decomposition naturally arises would improve accessibility for a broader audience. For example, a model where latent variables connect to observed variables via a linear model, with arbitrary dependence among the latent variables, could be used to demonstrate how the tensor decomposition relates to multilinear rank. Highlighting scenarios where some latent variables have low dimensions while others do not would further strengthen this point.
*   **Technical Clarifications:**
    *   **Theorem 2 & Permutations:** The statement of Theorem 2 should acknowledge that the recovered components are only guaranteed up to a permutation.
    *   **Derivation of (13) from (12):** The transition from equation (12) to (13) in Theorem 2 requires more explicit justification.
    *   **Missing Proof (Lemma 5):** The proof of Lemma 5 is missing from the submitted material, hindering a complete understanding of the analysis.
    *   **Lemma 4 Clarification:** While likely straightforward, clarifying the proof of Lemma 4 instead of solely referencing another paper would improve readability.
*   **Related Work – HOSVD & Tensor Rank:** Expanding the related work section to include a discussion of Higher-Order Singular Value Decomposition (HOSVD) and its approximation bounds [e.g., Kolda’s review] would provide valuable context. Clarifying the different notions of tensor rank (e.g., mode-rank vs. multilinear rank) in the introduction would also be beneficial.
*   **Incoherence Discussion:** The comparison to incoherence conditions used in [1, 3, 10] seems questionable. The current context does not involve sparse noise, and the type of incoherence discussed in those papers (relating singular vectors to basis vectors) differs from the energy distribution assumption made here. Removing this comparison would be advisable.
*   **Notational Issues:**
    *   The superscript notation for components (e.g.,  \hat{\cal{W}}^{(k)}) is potentially confusing; consider alternative symbols.
    *   The use of both \bar{r}k and \underline{r}k for k-mode rank is ambiguous. Introducing a consistent notation, such as `mult_rank(tensor)`, would improve clarity.
    *   A missing parenthesis in the last line of page 12 of the supplementary material should be addressed.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods.
*   The theoretical results are novel and well-supported by numerical experiments.
*   The duality result presented has broader applicability beyond the specific context of tensor decomposition.
*   The paper is well-written and generally clear, making a valuable contribution to the field.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of strong motivation for machine learning applications limits the paper's broader impact.
*   The technical clarifications requested (proof of Lemma 5, justification of (13), discussion of Theorem 2) are essential for a complete and rigorous evaluation.
*   The notational inconsistencies and minor issues detract from the overall clarity.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a significant theoretical contribution. Addressing the identified weaknesses, particularly the technical clarifications and strengthening the motivation, would substantially improve the paper and make it suitable for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing the raised concerns.
