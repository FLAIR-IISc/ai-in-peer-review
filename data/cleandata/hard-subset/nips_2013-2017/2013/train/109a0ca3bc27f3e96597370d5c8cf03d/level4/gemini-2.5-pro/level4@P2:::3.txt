### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates convex optimization-based methods for tensor decomposition from noisy observations. It focuses on a class of regularizers termed "structured Schatten norms," which generalize two recently proposed approaches: the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes the sum of Schatten norms of matrix unfoldings across all modes, and the "latent" approach, which models the tensor as a sum of components, each of which is low-rank in a specific mode.

The main contributions of the paper are threefold. First, it introduces a general framework for structured Schatten norms and establishes a novel duality between the overlapped and latent norm formulations. This result is interesting in its own right and connects this line of work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. Second, the paper provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the denoising performance of the latent approach. The key finding is that the mean squared error of the latent method scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with the known bound for the overlapped approach, which depends on the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result formally explains why the latent approach can significantly outperform the overlapped one, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one (unknown) mode. Finally, the authors present numerical experiments that confirm this predicted scaling behavior.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Depth and Novelty:** The paper's primary strength lies in its theoretical contributions. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is elegant and provides a unified perspective on these regularizers, connecting them to similar concepts in structured sparsity.
*   **Important Problem:** Noisy tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with wide applications. This work provides valuable theoretical insight into the behavior of competing convex relaxation methods.
*   **Clear Explanation of an Empirical Phenomenon:** The paper provides a clear and rigorous theoretical justification for the previously observed empirical success of the latent approach over the overlapped one. The main result—that the latent approach adapts to the lowest-rank mode without prior knowledge—is a significant and powerful insight.
*   **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The theoretical development from the general norm definitions to the specific performance bounds is logical and well-motivated.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental section, while effectively validating the theoretical scaling laws, feels somewhat preliminary. It does not provide a comprehensive comparison against other state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Lack of Comparison to Non-Convex Methods:** A significant omission is the lack of comparison to classical non-convex tensor decomposition algorithms like Alternating Least Squares (ALS) or Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD). While the paper's focus is on comparing two convex methods, demonstrating how they perform against these widely-used baselines is crucial for assessing their practical significance.
*   **Unexplored Aspects of the Framework:** The paper introduces a general family of structured Schatten Sp/q norms but focuses its analysis and experiments almost exclusively on the S1/1 case.

**Detailed Comments**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical analysis, including the duality proof and the derivation of the performance bounds, appears rigorous. The claims are well-supported by the theoretical development. The experiments serve as a solid proof of concept for the theory. However, the experimental evaluation could be strengthened to make the work more complete. Specifically:
*   The methodology for generating the random low-rank tensors for the experiments could be described in more detail. For instance, how were the multilinear ranks (r1, r2, r3) chosen? Were they selected to explore a diverse range of scenarios, such as the "simultaneously low-rank" case versus the "low-rank in one mode" case?
*   The paper's conclusion mentions exploring other norms, like the overlapped S1/∞-norm. A brief discussion on when such norms might be advantageous (e.g., for tensors that are known to be simultaneously low-rank in all modes) would have added depth.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-organized. The authors do an excellent job of defining the problem, introducing the necessary concepts (overlapped vs. latent norms), and building up to their main theoretical results. The connection to the structured sparsity literature is also clearly articulated.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While the overlapped and latent approaches existed previously, this paper is the first to place them in a unified framework, prove the fundamental duality between them, and provide a rigorous performance guarantee for the latent method that explains its advantages. This analysis is a novel and substantial contribution that deepens our understanding of convex tensor methods.

**Significance:** The results are significant for both researchers and practitioners in fields where tensor decomposition is applied. The paper provides clear theoretical guidance on when to prefer the latent approach over the overlapped one, which is a practical and important contribution. The theoretical insight that the latent regularizer implicitly finds and leverages the most compact representation of the tensor is a key takeaway that advances the state of the art. Furthermore, the connection established between structured Schatten norms and structured sparsity norms is likely to inspire follow-up work in both areas.

**Conclusion:**

This is a strong paper that presents novel and significant theoretical results for the problem of convex tensor decomposition. Its main contribution is a rigorous analysis that explains why the latent Schatten norm regularization is superior to the overlapped version in many settings. The paper is well-written and the theoretical claims are well-supported. While the experimental section could be more comprehensive by including comparisons to non-convex baselines, this does not detract from the importance of the core theoretical contributions. I recommend acceptance.
