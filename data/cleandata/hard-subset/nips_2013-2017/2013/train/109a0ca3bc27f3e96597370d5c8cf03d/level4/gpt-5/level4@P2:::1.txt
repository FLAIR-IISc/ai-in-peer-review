This paper studies convex, norm-regularized tensor decomposition with structured Schatten norms, focusing on the “latent” approach of [25] and its comparison to the earlier “overlapped” approach. The authors introduce a generalized class of structured Schatten Sp/q norms for tensors and prove a duality between overlapped and latent versions, extending known dualities from structured sparsity. The main contributions are theoretical: (1) a consistency bound for latent Schatten-1 regularization under a spectral-norm noise condition; (2) deterministic error bounds under an incoherence assumption that decompose error across latent components; and (3) rates under Gaussian noise showing that the mean squared error scales with the minimum mode rank (or the sum of latent factor ranks), contrasting with the prior overlapped analysis [26], which depends on averages of mode ranks. Empirical simulations corroborate the predicted scaling and suggest that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, especially when the true tensor is low-rank primarily in a single mode. The paper also observes that the theoretically optimal regularization parameter can be chosen independently of the true ranks.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on convex methods for tensor completion and decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25] and on statistical guarantees for the overlapped Schatten-1 norm [26]. The duality result connects to and generalizes dualities in structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs latent group lasso) [1, 12, 17, 21]. The comparison of error rates clarifies when the latent approach should be preferred.

Strengths:
- Clear problem formulation and careful connection to structured sparsity.
- A useful duality theorem for structured Schatten norms that generalizes well-known norms for overlapping groups.
- Error bounds that align with empirical behavior and articulate concrete advantages of the latent approach when only one mode is low-rank.
- Simulations that validate the predicted scaling and provide practical guidance on regularization choices.

Weaknesses:
- Scope is narrow and largely technical; the paper is only loosely tied to core NIPS machine learning applications. The experiments are synthetic and do not demonstrate benefits on real ML tasks (e.g., collaborative filtering, spatiotemporal prediction).
- The advances feel incremental: they formalize and tighten guarantees for a recently proposed method already known empirically to work, using standard techniques; the results are intuitively unsurprising.
- No lower bounds are provided. Since the paper argues for superiority of one convex relaxation over another via upper bounds, the absence of complementary lower bounds limits the conclusiveness of the comparison.
- The generalized Sp/q framework is primarily definitional; no new algorithms or analyses exploiting these generalized norms are developed or evaluated.

Reasons to accept:
- Technically solid analysis that clarifies when and why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach.
- General duality result of independent interest to researchers working on structured sparsity and convex tensor methods.
- Provides actionable insights (e.g., rank-independent choice of λ) for practitioners using convex tensor decomposition.

Reasons to reject:
- Limited novelty and impact for the broader NIPS audience; contributions are specialized and mostly extend existing lines of work.
- Lack of real-world machine learning demonstrations or tasks where the theory translates into practical gains.
- Missing lower bounds and limited exploration of the generalized norm family reduce the breadth of the contribution.

Minor comments:
- The abstract opens with “We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...”. This overstates the contribution relative to the paper’s focus on analyzing two existing norms (overlapped and latent). The generalized Sp/q class is introduced and a duality is proved, but no algorithms or empirical studies are built around these generalized norms.
- Figure 1 appears in the introduction before the necessary background; consider moving it later or adding brief interpretive guidance.
- Wording/typos:
  - “Tensor is” → “Tensor modeling is” (around line 38).
  - Add a comma after “Conventionally” (around line 40).
  - “also in such situations” → “in such situations as well” (around line 395).

Overall assessment: The paper offers a careful and useful theoretical analysis of latent vs overlapped Schatten-norm tensor decompositions, along with a duality result that broadens connections to structured sparsity. While technically sound and clearly written, the contribution feels incremental and somewhat peripheral to the mainstream NIPS audience, with limited empirical breadth and no lower bounds. The work will be of interest to researchers focused on convex tensor methods and structured norms, but its broader impact may be modest.
