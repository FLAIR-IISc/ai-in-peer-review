

Quality: The paper establishes theoretical bounds for convex tensor decomposition regularized by the latent Schatten norm, improving upon prior results using overlapped Schatten norm regularization. The dual characterization of these norms is a key technical contribution, offering valuable insights into their relationship. However, the experimental section is underdeveloped and lacks critical details. Specifically: (1) The selection criteria for Tucker ranks (r₁, ..., rₖ) in the examples are not justified, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the results; (2) The interplay between group regularization (e.g., ℓ₁,∞) and simultaneous low-rank structure across all tensor modes is not explained, despite this being a central premise of the method; (3) A comparison against state-of-the-art non-convex tensor decomposition approaches is missing, which is essential for evaluating the practical trade-offs of the convex formulation. Without these clarifications, the empirical validation remains superficial.

Clarity: The paper is well-structured and logically organized, with clear exposition of the theoretical framework. The mathematical derivations are presented coherently, and the flow from problem definition to results is easy to follow. Minor improvements in notation consistency (e.g., standardizing subscript usage for ranks) would further enhance readability.

Originality: The dual characterization of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms represents a novel and significant theoretical advance. This result is not only central to the paper's main contribution but also holds independent interest for optimization and low-rank matrix/tensor analysis. The extension of Schatten norm regularization to the latent case, while building on prior work, demonstrates a non-trivial conceptual leap that enables the improved bounds.

Significance: Tensor decomposition is a fundamental challenge in machine learning with broad applications in signal processing, neuroscience, and recommendation systems. This work addresses a key gap in the theoretical understanding of convex tensor regularization, providing tighter bounds than previous approaches. The improved theoretical guarantees could inform more robust and efficient algorithms for real-world tensor problems. While the paper’s primary impact is theoretical, the results have clear potential to influence future methodological developments. The significance is further strengthened by the paper’s strong mathematical foundation and the novelty of the duality result.

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition. The core theoretical advance is sound and well-motivated, and the writing is generally clear. However, the insufficient experimental detail—particularly the lack of justification for hyperparameters, inadequate explanation of the group regularization mechanism, and absence of comparisons to non-convex baselines—weakens the paper’s practical impact and limits the reader’s ability to assess its applicability. Addressing these gaps would substantially strengthen the work. The theoretical contribution alone warrants consideration, but the current experimental shortcomings prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. The authors should revise to provide a more comprehensive empirical evaluation.
