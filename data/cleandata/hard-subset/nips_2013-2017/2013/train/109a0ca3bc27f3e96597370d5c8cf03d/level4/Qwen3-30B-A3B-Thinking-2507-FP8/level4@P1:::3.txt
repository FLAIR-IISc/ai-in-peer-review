

This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors establish a theoretical foundation for the latent approach, which has been empirically observed to outperform the overlapped approach in certain scenarios. The paper makes two primary contributions: (1) a novel duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes structured sparsity literature; and (2) a rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating that the latent approach's mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. This explains why the latent approach performs better in the settings where the underlying tensor is nearly full rank in all but one mode.

The paper relates to previous work by connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature, particularly drawing parallels with overlapped and latent group lasso approaches. The authors build upon the convex optimization framework for tensor decomposition introduced in [25, 26], but provide the first theoretical justification for the empirical superiority of the latent approach. Their analysis complements prior work on noiseless tensor completion by addressing the more practical noisy setting.

Quality: The theoretical contributions are strong and well-justified. The duality result (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and appears to be a significant contribution to the literature on structured norms. The main theorems (Theorems 1-3) provide clear performance bounds for the latent approach under various conditions, with careful attention to the relationship between the bounds and the underlying tensor structure. The experimental section, while brief, successfully validates the theoretical predictions, demonstrating that the mean squared error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures. A notable weakness is the limited discussion of practical implementation details and comparisons with non-convex approaches. The paper does not address how the latent approach might perform relative to traditional non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., higher-order orthogonal iteration) on real-world datasets, which would strengthen the practical significance of the results.

Clarity: The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical exposition is precise, with well-chosen notation that facilitates understanding of the technical content. The introduction effectively frames the problem and motivation, while the technical sections build logically on each other. The connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity is particularly well-explained. The only minor issue is the brief experimental section, which could benefit from more detailed descriptions of the implementation choices and parameter selection procedures.

Originality: The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is the paper's most significant contribution and represents a novel insight in the field. This result is not merely a technical extension but provides a deeper theoretical understanding of the relationship between two seemingly distinct approaches to tensor decomposition. The authors successfully position this contribution within the broader context of structured sparsity literature, showing how their work generalizes previous results on group sparsity. The paper's theoretical analysis of the latent approach's performance is also novel, as it provides the first rigorous explanation for the empirical success observed in previous work.

Significance: Tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with wide-ranging applications in areas such as neuroimaging, environmental monitoring, and collaborative filtering. The paper addresses a critical gap in the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods, which has been a topic of growing interest in the machine learning community. By demonstrating that the latent approach performs better theoretically and empirically than the overlapped approach in a variety of settings, the paper provides clear guidance for practitioners. The finding that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the mode with the smallest rank (without requiring this information) is particularly noteworthy and has important implications for practical tensor decomposition tasks. The paper's theoretical insights could also inspire further developments in structured sparsity methods beyond tensor decomposition.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results, including specific guidance on parameter selection for real-world applications. Additionally, a more comprehensive comparison with non-convex tensor decomposition methods would strengthen the significance claim by demonstrating how the latent approach performs relative to established methods. However, these are minor points in an otherwise strong contribution.

Overall, this paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition, with clear practical implications. The duality result is particularly elegant and broadly applicable, while the performance analysis provides valuable insights into the strengths of different convex optimization approaches. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the literature. While the experimental section could be expanded, the theoretical results are compelling enough to warrant acceptance.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution with novel duality result; Clear explanation of relationship between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity; Rigorous analysis of performance bounds with practical implications; Well-written and well-organized.

Con: Experimental section is brief and lacks comparison with non-convex methods; Limited discussion of practical implementation details; Could provide more concrete guidance for parameter selection in real-world applications.

I recommend acceptance as a strong theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition.
