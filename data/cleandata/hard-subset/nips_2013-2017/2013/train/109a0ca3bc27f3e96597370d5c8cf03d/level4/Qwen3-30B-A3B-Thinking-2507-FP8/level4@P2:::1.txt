

This paper investigates convex optimization approaches for tensor decomposition, focusing on a recently proposed "latent" norm approach (from [25]) and comparing it to the established "overlapped" norm approach. The authors provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, demonstrating that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. They establish a duality relationship between the two structured Schatten norms and empirically validate their theoretical predictions through numerical simulations. The paper also generalizes the norms and proves a novel duality result that connects to structured sparsity literature.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem definition to theoretical analysis to empirical validation. The theoretical contributions are technically sound, with the duality result (Lemma 1) providing an important connection between the two approaches and the structured sparsity literature. The numerical experiments (Section 4) effectively confirm the theoretical scaling behavior of the mean squared error, which is a significant strength of the paper.

The main strengths of the paper include the rigorous theoretical analysis that explains the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach, the clear exposition of the duality between the two norms, and the careful experimental validation. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods, particularly in explaining why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in specific settings.

However, the paper has several notable limitations. First, while the authors claim to propose a "new class of structured Schatten norms," the paper primarily analyzes two existing approaches rather than introducing novel algorithms or methods. The generalization is limited to defining the norms and proving duality (Lemma 1), with no new algorithms or empirical validation of the generalized norms. This slightly misrepresents the novelty of the contribution, as noted in the abstract.

Second, the paper lacks connection to broader machine learning applications. While tensor decompositions have applications in ML (as mentioned in the introduction), the paper does not examine the proposed approaches in the context of actual ML problems, either theoretically or experimentally. This limits the potential impact of the work for the NIPS audience, which typically prioritizes contributions with clear connections to machine learning.

Third, the theoretical results lack lower bounds, which would strengthen the comparison between the two approaches. The authors rely solely on upper bounds to argue for the superiority of the latent approach, which is potentially problematic as the gap between upper and lower bounds could be significant.

Finally, while the paper is technically sound, the results are somewhat incremental. The latent approach was already empirically shown to work well in [25], and this paper provides theoretical justification for that empirical observation. The techniques used (e.g., adapting analysis from [1, 26]) do not appear particularly novel.

In terms of clarity, the paper is well-written with good organization. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively illustrate the main findings. However, Figure 1 in the introduction is presented without sufficient context, making it difficult to interpret without reading the subsequent sections.

The paper makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of tensor decomposition methods, but its narrow focus on the technical comparison between two existing approaches without broader application context limits its significance for NIPS. While the results are technically sound and the analysis is rigorous, the paper would benefit from connecting the tensor decomposition methods to actual machine learning problems or datasets to demonstrate practical impact.

Overall, the paper is of moderate quality, with clear strengths in theoretical analysis and empirical validation, but limited significance due to its narrow focus and lack of connection to broader machine learning applications. It would be a marginal acceptance at best, as the contributions appear incremental rather than transformative for the field. The authors could strengthen their case by demonstrating how these tensor decomposition methods could be applied to specific machine learning problems.
