This paper considers convex norm-regularized optimization approaches for factorization of approximately low-rank tensors, focusing on the "latent" norm approach and its comparison to the previously used "overlap" norm approach. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the "latent" norm mean-squared-error behavior under several noise assumptions, which compare favorably to the existing analysis on the overlap norm, supporting its better empirically-observed performance. The theory is also confirmed via numerical simulations. Additionally, the paper considers a generalization of both these norms and proves a duality theorem for it.

The paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow, with a clear presentation of the main ideas and results. The technical soundness of the paper appears to be good, although I haven't checked the proofs in the supplementary material carefully. The results may also be useful for researchers focusing on optimization methods for tensor decompositions.

However, I have some concerns regarding the paper's relevance and impact. The results are narrow and of a technical nature, only peripherally related to machine learning. While the authors make the justified case that tensor factorizations can crop up in machine learning, the paper does not attempt to examine the proposed approaches in the context of machine learning problems, either theoretically or experimentally. This lack of connection to machine learning may limit the paper's potential impact at NIPS.

Furthermore, the results seem a bit incremental, and I didn't find them to be particularly surprising. They seem to provide some additional theoretical backing to a specific recently proposed approach, which was already argued empirically to work well. The techniques used also don't seem particularly novel.

A more minor defect is that the upper bounds do not come with any lower bounds. This is potentially dangerous, since the upper bounds are used to compare between different algorithms and argue for the superiority of one over the other.

In terms of the paper's quality, I have some minor comments:

- The abstract begins with "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...". However, I think this is slightly misleading. The focus of the paper is devoted to analyzing two existing approaches (overlapped and latent). Unless I missed something, the generalization only comes up by defining it in section 2.1 and proving what is the dual norm (Lemma 1). In particular, no particular algorithms are proposed based on this generalized Schatten norms, their behavior is not analyzed, nor are they tested experimentally.

- Figure 1 is presented in the introduction, but at this stage is not interpretable without the background appearing in section 2 onwards.

- Line 38: "Tensor is" -> "Tensor modelling is"(?)

- Line 40: Missing comma after "Conventionally"

- Line 395: "also in such situations" -> "in such situations as well"

Overall, while the paper is technically sound, its relevance and impact may be limited due to its narrow focus and lack of connection to machine learning. The results, although useful for researchers in optimization methods for tensor decompositions, may not be surprising or novel enough to warrant publication in a top-tier conference like NIPS.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a thorough analysis of the "latent" norm approach for low-rank tensor decomposition.
* The theoretical bounds derived in the paper compare favorably to existing analysis on the overlap norm.
* The paper confirms its theory via numerical simulations.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The results are narrow and of a technical nature, only peripherally related to machine learning.
* The paper does not attempt to examine the proposed approaches in the context of machine learning problems.
* The results seem incremental and not particularly surprising.
* The techniques used are not particularly novel.
* The upper bounds do not come with any lower bounds.
