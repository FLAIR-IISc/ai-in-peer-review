Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies convex-optimization–based tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. It unifies two classes of norms—overlapped and latent—and establishes a duality between them. The main theoretical contribution analyzes the latent approach, showing consistency and denoising guarantees with mean-squared-error (MSE) scaling governed by the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, rather than an average of ranks as in prior results for the overlapped approach. The paper also presents empirical results that match the predicted scaling.

This work builds on Tucker/HOSVD tensor decompositions and the convex relaxation literature, including nuclear norm methods and structured sparsity (group lasso, overlapped vs. latent). It relates to tensor completion and denoising [e.g., Liu et al.; Gandy et al.; Tomioka et al.], classical tensor decompositions [Kolda & Bader], and convex-geometry analyses of low-rank estimation.

Motivation and broader impact for ML:
To make the contribution accessible to a broader ML audience, the paper should explicitly motivate when and why tensor decompositions arise in learning. I recommend adding a latent-variable model (with a graphical model figure) where the proposed decomposition recovers meaningful parameters: consider hidden variables h1, h2, …, each connected linearly to observed variables x1, x2, …, with arbitrary dependence among the hi. Under a linear observation model, the observed moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition whose multilinear rank equals dim(h1), dim(h2), …. This clarifies that the multilinear rank corresponds to latent dimensionalities. It should also be emphasized that some hidden variables may be low dimensional while others are not—precisely the regime where the latent approach’s guarantees (depending on the minimum mode-k rank) are most advantageous.

Strengths:
- A clear and useful framework for structured Schatten norms on tensors, including a novel duality between overlapped and latent norms that generalizes analogous results in structured sparsity.
- Sharp analysis of the latent approach: consistency bounds and denoising guarantees that scale with the minimum mode-k rank; comparison to prior bounds for the overlapped approach explains empirical performance differences.
- Solid empirical validation: MSE scaling matches theory, and results suggest the recommended regularization is independent of latent ranks.

Main technical clarifications and corrections:
- Theorem 2 (component recovery): components are identified only up to a permutation. Please state this explicitly.
- The step from inequality (12) to (13) needs a brief justification. The argument relies on the arbitrariness of the decomposition and the availability of a singleton decomposition, which replaces a sum over ranks by the minimum mode-k rank; please make this step explicit.
- Lemma 5: the proof is missing, yet Lemma 5 is central to Theorem 2. Please include it.
- Lemma 4: since the argument is straightforward, it would improve readability to provide the proof rather than only citing another paper.

Incoherence discussion after (10):
You describe condition (10) as an “incoherence” assumption and state it is the same as in [1, 3, 10]. However, those works address sparse + low-rank recovery and use incoherence conditions of singular vectors with respect to the standard basis, while the present assumption controls spectral norms of off-mode unfoldings (“energy spread across mixture components and modes”). Since the noise here is not sparse and the assumptions differ in form and purpose, I do not see them as the same. I suggest removing this comparison or clearly distinguishing the notions.

Related work:
- Please expand the discussion of HOSVD/Tucker methods and known approximation bounds (see Kolda & Bader, SIAM Review, Section 4), and situate your results relative to classical nonconvex methods such as ALS/HOOI.
- Clarify in the introduction that “mode-k rank” is also called “multilinear rank” and that multiple notions of tensor rank exist (e.g., CP rank vs. multilinear rank).

Computational aspects:
A brief discussion of computational complexity would be helpful: per-iteration costs of the ADMM-based solvers, cost drivers (SVDs of mode unfoldings), dependence on K and nk, and practical scalability.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance:
- Quality: The analysis is technically sound overall, with meaningful new bounds for the latent approach. Please address the missing proof (Lemma 5) and the minor issues noted below.
- Clarity: Generally well written, but adding the ML motivation, the latent-variable example/figure, and the above clarifications would significantly improve accessibility.
- Originality: The duality result for structured Schatten norms and the latent-approach bounds are novel and useful.
- Significance: Results advance understanding of convex tensor decomposition, offering guarantees that practitioners can leverage, particularly when only one (or a few) modes are low rank.

Minor issues and notation/typos:
- Superscript notation for components, e.g., \hat{\mathcal{W}}^{(k)}, is confusing. Consider alternative notation for components to avoid overloading superscripts.
- Both \bar{r}_k and \underline{r}_k are used for the k-mode rank; please unify. Introducing a function such as mult_rank(tensor) would help.
- Supplementary material: last line of page 12 appears to be missing a parenthesis.
- Abstract: “result for structures Schatten” should be “result for structured Schatten.”
- Please briefly report computational complexity and practical runtime behavior for the solvers used.

Pros and cons for acceptance:
Pros:
- Strong and novel theoretical results for the latent approach, with clear advantages over the overlapped alternative in important regimes.
- Useful duality perspective that ties tensor norms to structured sparsity literature.
- Empirical validation closely matches theoretical scaling.

Cons:
- Needs stronger ML motivation and a concrete latent-variable example/figure to broaden impact.
- Minor technical gaps (permutation ambiguity; justification of (12)→(13); missing proof of Lemma 5).
- Incoherence discussion conflates distinct assumptions; should be revised.
- Some notation/typos and missing details on computational complexity.

Overall:
A technically solid and relevant contribution. With stronger motivation, the latent-variable example, the missing proof and clarifications, and minor fixes, the paper would be a valuable addition. I lean positive, contingent on addressing the above points.
