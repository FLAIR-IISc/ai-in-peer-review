## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission – Convex Norm-Regularized Tensor Factorization

This paper investigates convex norm-regularized optimization methods for the factorization of approximately low-rank tensors, with a focus on comparing the recently proposed “latent” norm approach [25] to the established “overlap” norm approach. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the mean-squared error performance of the latent norm under various noise assumptions, demonstrating improvements over existing analyses for the overlap norm – results that support the latent norm’s previously observed empirical advantages. These theoretical findings are complemented by numerical simulations, and the paper concludes with a generalization of both norms and a corresponding duality theorem.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, and the technical content appears sound, though a detailed verification of the supplementary material’s proofs was beyond the scope of this review. The results are potentially valuable to researchers developing optimization methods for tensor decompositions. However, I have several concerns regarding the scope, novelty, and potential impact of this work within the NIPS context.

My primary concern is the paper’s narrow focus and limited connection to core machine learning problems. While the authors acknowledge the relevance of tensor factorizations to machine learning, the work remains largely theoretical and lacks any application or evaluation within a machine learning framework – either through theoretical analysis or empirical experimentation. The results, while technically interesting, feel somewhat peripheral to the typical NIPS audience.

Secondly, the contributions appear incremental. The paper primarily provides theoretical justification for a recently proposed approach [25] that had already demonstrated promising empirical performance. The techniques employed do not represent a significant advancement in the field, and the results, while mathematically rigorous, did not strike me as particularly surprising. 

A further point is the absence of corresponding lower bounds to accompany the presented upper bounds. This is problematic as the upper bounds are used to directly compare the performance of different algorithms and establish the superiority of one over another. Without lower bounds, it is difficult to assess the tightness of these bounds and the extent to which they genuinely reflect algorithmic performance.

Finally, several minor points detract from the clarity and presentation:

*   The abstract’s claim of proposing a “new class of structured Schatten norms” is misleading. The paper primarily *analyzes* two existing approaches (overlap and latent). The generalization is introduced later in Section 2.1, primarily to establish a duality result (Lemma 1), and isn't used to develop or evaluate new algorithms. 
*   Figure 1, presented in the introduction, requires background knowledge from Section 2 to be properly understood and its placement feels premature.
*   Line 38: “Tensor is” should be revised to “Tensor modelling is” (or a similar phrasing).
*   Line 40: A comma is missing after “Conventionally”.
*   Line 395: “also in such situations” should be “in such situations as well”.

In summary, the paper presents technically sound theoretical error bounds for a recent low-rank tensor decomposition approach. However, the incremental nature of the results and the lack of connection to practical machine learning applications raise concerns about its overall impact at NIPS. I recommend further refinement of the paper to either demonstrate a stronger connection to machine learning or to significantly expand the theoretical contributions to justify its inclusion in the conference.
