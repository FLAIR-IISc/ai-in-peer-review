

This paper investigates convex norm-regularized optimization for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, focusing on comparing the "latent" norm approach (from [25]) with the established "overlap" norm. The authors derive theoretical mean-squared-error bounds for the latent norm under various noise models, demonstrating favorable comparisons to existing overlap norm analysis. They empirically validate these results via simulations and prove a duality theorem for a generalized norm framework. The technical content is largely sound and well-structured, with clear exposition.  

However, the paper's primary limitations concern its scope and impact for NIPS 2013. The work is narrowly focused on theoretical properties of two specific norms without connecting to machine learning applications. While the authors note tensor factorizations *can* arise in ML, the paper fails to examine the proposed methods in actual ML contexts—neither theoretically (e.g., as components in learning algorithms) nor empirically (e.g., on standard ML tasks like recommendation systems or computer vision). This omission significantly diminishes its relevance to NIPS, which prioritizes work with clear ML impact.  

The technical contributions also appear incremental. The latent norm analysis builds directly on [25], which already established its empirical superiority. The new bounds provide additional theoretical justification but do not introduce novel techniques or unexpected insights. The duality theorem for the generalized norm is a minor extension (defined in Section 2.1, with no algorithmic proposals or empirical validation), further reducing novelty. Consequently, the work offers limited advancement beyond existing literature.  

A critical technical gap is the absence of matching lower bounds. The paper uses upper bounds to argue the latent norm's superiority over the overlap norm, but without lower bounds, the claimed advantage lacks rigorous justification—upper bounds alone cannot prove optimality or dominance. This weakens the central theoretical claim.  

**Minor issues:**  
- The abstract overstates novelty: "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms" misrepresents the contribution. The "generalization" is merely a technical extension (Section 2.1) with no new algorithms, analysis, or experiments.  
- Figure 1 in the introduction is uninterpretable without Section 2's background.  
- Typos: Line 38 ("Tensor is" → "Tensor modeling is"), Line 40 (missing comma after "Conventionally"), Line 395 ("also in such situations" → "in such situations as well").  

**Summary:** The paper presents technically competent analysis of existing tensor norms but lacks ML context, novelty, and rigorous comparative guarantees. Its narrow focus on theoretical properties—without demonstrating utility in machine learning—limits its significance for NIPS. While the results may interest tensor decomposition specialists, they fall short of the conference’s typical standards for impact. The absence of lower bounds further undermines the core argument.  

**Recommendation:** Reject. The work is incrementally technical with insufficient ML relevance and unresolved theoretical gaps. It does not advance the state of the art in a manner that would justify acceptance at NIPS 2013.
