Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms, focusing on two families: overlapped norms (penalizing all mode unfoldings simultaneously) and latent norms (decomposing a tensor into a sum of mode-specific low-rank components). The authors establish a duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms, and provide statistical guarantees for the latent approach in a denoising setting. They prove consistency, deterministic error bounds under a mild incoherence condition, and high-probability bounds for Gaussian noise. A key insight is that the latent approach achieves error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, contrasting with the overlapped approach whose complexity scales with an average of ranks. Experiments verify the predicted scaling and show that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach, even when the tensor is jointly low-rank.

This work connects convex tensor methods [e.g., nuclear norm for matrices and prior convex tensor completion/decomposition] with structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso). It complements prior results on overlapped norms and HOSVD/Tucker decomposition, and helps explain empirical findings favoring the latent approach.

Strengths:
- Clear, unified framework for structured Schatten norms and a useful duality result linking overlapped and latent formulations.
- Theoretical guarantees that are interpretable and align with practice: latent regularization adapts to the mode with smallest rank without knowing it in advance.
- Empirical validation that closely tracks the theory’s scaling predictions.
- Connection to structured sparsity literature is insightful and broadens impact.

Weaknesses and requested revisions:
Post-rebuttal motivation for a broader ML audience:
- Please better motivate the setting via a latent variable model and graphical depiction. For example, consider observed variables x1,…,xK with hidden variables h1,…,hK connected linearly (xi depends linearly on hi), allowing arbitrary dependence among the hi. The resulting observed moment tensor has a Tucker decomposition whose multilinear ranks are dim(h1),…,dim(hK). This makes the relevance to ML more concrete and highlights your main advantage: only some hidden dimensions may be small, which is exactly where the latent approach shines.

Clarifications on Theorem 2 and proofs:
- Component identifiability: components are recoverable only up to permutation. Please state this explicitly.
- From (12) to (13): provide a justification. One approach is to use the arbitrariness of the decomposition to take a singleton decomposition aligned with the smallest mode rank, turning the sum of ranks into min_k r_k.
- Lemma 5: the proof is missing, yet it is central to Theorem 2. Please include it.
- Lemma 4: rather than only citing prior work, add a brief explicit proof or a self-contained argument sketch.

Related work and background:
- Expand discussion of HOSVD/Tucker methods and their approximation bounds; Kolda and Bader’s review (section 4) is a good entry point. Position your guarantees relative to these non-convex approaches.
- Clarify rank terminology early. What you call mode-k rank is commonly referred to as multilinear rank; also note that other tensor rank notions exist (e.g., CP rank).

Computational aspects:
- Briefly discuss computational complexity and practical scalability of the proposed optimization (e.g., ADMM). Report per-iteration costs (dominated by SVDs on mode-k unfoldings of size nk × (N/nk)) and how K and dimensions affect runtime and memory.

Incoherence condition:
- Please refine the comparison to “incoherence” in prior work. The condition you use bounds spectral norms of cross-mode unfoldings of other components to control cross-talk across modes/components. This is different from the standard incoherence between singular vectors and the canonical basis used in sparse+low-rank models (e.g., robust PCA). Avoid claiming equivalence to those conditions; instead, explain your condition’s role and how it differs.

Minor notational/typo issues:
- Superscript notation for components like Ŵ^(k) is confusing; consider a different sub/superscript convention for components vs modes.
- r̄_k and r̲_k both appear to denote mode-k rank in different places; unify notation (e.g., use mult_rank(W) = (r1,…,rK) and reserve r̄_k for ranks of latent components).
- Fix the missing parenthesis on the last line of page 12 of the supplement.
- Abstract: “structures Schatten” → “structured Schatten”.

Pros for acceptance:
- Novel and useful duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms.
- Sharp, interpretable statistical guarantees demonstrating adaptation to the smallest mode rank.
- Empirical results that validate the predicted scaling and practical benefit of the latent approach.
- Bridges convex tensor methods and structured sparsity frameworks.

Cons / concerns:
- Insufficient motivation for a general ML audience; lacking an intuitive generative model and figure.
- Gaps/ambiguities in Theorem 2 and supporting lemmas (permutation ambiguity, (12)→(13) justification, missing Lemma 5 proof).
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and practical scalability.
- Overstated comparison of your “incoherence” condition to classic notions in sparse+low-rank recovery.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-written and technically solid paper that advances convex tensor decomposition through a principled analysis of latent vs overlapped Schatten norms and a valuable duality result. With stronger motivation, clarified proofs (especially for Theorem 2 and Lemma 5), refined discussion of incoherence, and improved notation, the paper would be accessible to a broader audience and ready for publication.
