Additional comments after the rebuttal:
The paper makes technically important contributions on convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, particularly the overlapped Schatten norm, and provides improved reconstruction guarantees. The manuscript is generally well written. With stronger motivation for the machine learning audience and several clarifications and notational fixes, the paper would be substantially clearer and more broadly accessible.

Motivation and modeling:
- Please better motivate the problem in a machine learning context by explicitly presenting a latent-variable model for which your tensor decomposition recovers meaningful parameters. A simple and illustrative example is:
  - Latent variables h1, h2, … each connect linearly to observed variables x1, x2, … (one latent per observed), with arbitrary dependence allowed among the hi’s.
  - Under a linear observation model from hidden to observed variables, the observed moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition whose multilinear ranks are dim(h1), dim(h2), …
  - This connection explains both why Tucker structure arises and how the multilinear ranks tie to latent dimensionalities. Including a graphical model figure for this setup would significantly improve accessibility.
- Emphasize that it may be realistic for only some hidden variables to have small dimension while others are large, and that your results exploit this heterogeneity—an important practical advantage of your approach.

Main contributions:
- The paper studies tensor decomposition via convex programs with structured Schatten norms and demonstrates that the overlapped Schatten norm yields stronger recovery guarantees. The theoretical results and algorithmic framing are valuable and timely.

Technical clarifications and corrections:
- Theorem 2:
  - Identifiability: Please state explicitly that components are recovered only up to permutation (and, if applicable, scaling where relevant). This is standard but should be acknowledged.
  - Derivation: The step from (12) to (13) is currently described as immediate. Please provide a clear justification, indicating the inequalities or algebraic identities used and any required assumptions.
  - Lemma 5: The proof is missing, yet it is central to Theorem 2. Please include a complete proof in the main text or appendix.
  - Lemma 4: While the argument may be straightforward, it would aid readability to sketch the proof in the paper rather than referring readers to an external source.
- Incoherence discussion (after Eq. (10)):
  - Your text uses “incoherence” to mean that energy is spread across components and modes (no single component/mode has a dominating singular value). You equate this with incoherence in [1, 3, 10], which typically pertains to sparse+low-rank recovery and involves singular vectors being incoherent with the canonical basis.
  - Because your noise model is not sparse and the conditions differ conceptually, the comparison is potentially misleading. I suggest removing or carefully rephrasing that claim to avoid conflation.

Related work and context:
- Please expand the related work on Tucker/HOSVD and other tensor decompositions. In particular:
  - Summarize known approximation guarantees for higher-order SVD (HOSVD) and its variants; Kolda and Bader’s review (see Section 4) provides a concise entry point.
  - Contrast your convex formulation and guarantees with HOSVD-style algorithms, clarifying when each approach is preferable.
- Clarify terminology on rank:
  - The “mode-rank” you use is commonly called the multilinear rank. Please state this in the introduction and briefly note that multiple notions of tensor rank exist (e.g., CP rank vs. multilinear rank).

Computational aspects:
- Provide a brief complexity discussion for the proposed optimization procedures:
  - Per-iteration cost (e.g., mode-wise SVDs), memory requirements, and scaling with tensor dimensions and multilinear ranks.
  - Any acceleration techniques (warm starts, randomized SVD, parallelization) and practical runtime behavior on your datasets.

Notation and presentation:
- The superscript notation for components, e.g., \hat{W}^{(k)} (or \hat{\cal W}^{(k)}), is confusing because superscripts commonly denote modes or iterations. Consider alternative symbols or subscripts for components to avoid overload.
- The use of both \bar{r}_k and \underline{r}_k for the k-mode rank is ambiguous. Please standardize. Introducing a function such as mult_rank(tensor) or a vector r = (r1, …, rK) would be clearer.
- Ensure all displayed equations have consistent indexing and summation conventions, and define all symbols upon first use.

Typos and minor issues:
- Abstract: “result for structures Schatten” should be “result for structured Schatten.”
- In the last line of page 12 of the supplementary material, a parenthesis appears to be missing—please correct.
- Check for minor grammatical errors and punctuation throughout; a light proofread would improve polish.

Overall evaluation:
- Strengths:
  - Technically solid contributions on convex tensor decomposition with structured Schatten norms and improved recovery guarantees.
  - Clear problem setup and potential practical benefits, especially when only a subset of modes/components are low-rank.
- Weaknesses and opportunities:
  - Insufficient motivation for a broad ML audience; adding a concrete latent-variable example with a graphical model would greatly help.
  - Several technical omissions (Theorem 2 details, Lemma 5 proof) and notational inconsistencies reduce readability.
  - Related work on HOSVD and rank notions should be expanded, and the “incoherence” comparison with sparse+low-rank literature should be corrected.

Recommendation:
With the above revisions—especially clarifying Theorem 2 and lemmas, strengthening the ML motivation via a latent-variable model and figure, cleaning up notation, and refining the related work—the paper would be significantly improved and well-positioned for publication. The core ideas are strong, and the results are likely to be of interest to the tensor methods and broader machine learning communities.
