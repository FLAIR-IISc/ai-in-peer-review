

This paper investigates convex norm-regularized optimization approaches for low-rank tensor factorization, with a specific focus on the "latent" norm approach recently proposed in [25] and its comparison to the established "overlapped" norm approach. The authors derive theoretical error bounds for the latent norm under various noise assumptions, demonstrating that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. They provide a theoretical explanation for the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach, confirmed through numerical simulations. Additionally, the paper establishes a duality relationship between the two structured Schatten norms, connecting their work to broader literature on structured sparsity.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the numerical experiments support the theoretical claims, and the connection to structured sparsity literature is well-motivated. The authors have clearly demonstrated that the latent approach performs as well as knowing the mode with the smallest rank, which provides a compelling theoretical explanation for its empirical success.

However, I have several concerns about the paper's significance and contribution to NIPS. First, while tensor decomposition has applications in machine learning, the paper does not engage with any machine learning problems or datasets. It remains firmly in the realm of mathematical optimization without demonstrating any practical impact on machine learning tasks, which is a significant limitation for a NIPS submission. The authors claim tensor decomposition "arises naturally in many real world data analysis problems" but provide no evidence that their specific contribution addresses practical machine learning challenges.

Second, while the theoretical bounds are technically sound, they do not appear to be particularly novel or surprising. The paper essentially provides theoretical backing for an approach that was already empirically shown to work well in [25]. The techniques used (duality, error bounds for structured norms) are standard in the field, and the results don't appear to significantly advance the state of the art beyond what was already suggested by the empirical evidence.

Third, the paper lacks lower bounds for the error rates, which is problematic since the authors use upper bounds to argue for the superiority of their approach. Without lower bounds, it's difficult to assess whether the error rates they've derived are tight or if there might be a more favorable approach.

I also have some specific concerns about the presentation:
- The abstract claims the authors "propose a new class of structured Schatten norms," but the paper primarily analyzes two existing approaches (overlapped and latent), with the generalization being a minor technical extension (Lemma 1) rather than a substantive contribution.
- Figure 1 is introduced in the introduction but remains uninterpretable without the background in Section 2, which would have been better placed later in the paper.
- The paper does not clearly distinguish between the "latent" approach (which they analyze) and the "latent norm" they define, which could confuse readers.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but its contribution appears incremental and narrowly focused on a specific technical question without clear connections to broader machine learning challenges. While the results may be of interest to researchers working specifically on tensor decomposition algorithms, they do not represent a significant advance in machine learning as a field. The lack of machine learning context and the incremental nature of the contribution make this paper less suitable for NIPS, which prioritizes work with broader impact on machine learning.

Strengths:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with clear connections to structured sparsity literature
- Well-executed numerical experiments that validate the theoretical predictions
- Clear exposition of the relationship between the overlapped and latent approaches

Weaknesses:
- Limited connection to machine learning applications or problems
- Results appear incremental, providing theoretical backing for an empirically observed approach
- Absence of lower bounds for error rates
- Generalization of the Schatten norms is minor and not used to develop new algorithms or prove new results
- The paper does not demonstrate how these techniques would improve machine learning performance on real tasks

Pro: The paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition.
Con: The contribution lacks clear relevance to machine learning and does not advance the field beyond the empirical results already established in [25].

Overall, while the paper is technically sound, its narrow focus and limited connection to machine learning make it a marginal fit for NIPS. The results are of interest to the tensor decomposition community but do not demonstrate sufficient significance to the broader machine learning community.
