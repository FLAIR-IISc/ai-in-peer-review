

This paper addresses convex optimization for tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically analyzing the "latent" approach (proposed in Tomioka et al., NIPS 2013) against the established "overlapped" approach (Gandy et al., NIPS 2011; Tomioka et al., NIPS 2013). The authors introduce a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, proving that the latent approach achieves error bounds scaling with the *minimal* mode-k rank of the true tensor—unlike the overlapped approach, which depends on the *average* rank. This resolves a key empirical gap observed in prior work (Tomioka et al., NIPS 2014), where the latent method outperformed overlapped in scenarios with low-rank structure in a single mode. The paper also provides tight denoising guarantees under Gaussian noise and validates predictions via numerical experiments.

**Strengths:**  
*Quality:* The theoretical contributions are robust and well-motivated. The duality lemma (Lemma 1) generalizes structured sparsity results (e.g., Obozinski et al., NIPS 2011) to tensors, and the error bounds (Theorems 2–3) are sharp and practically insightful. The empirical validation precisely confirms the predicted scaling behavior of mean squared error against latent rank complexity (Figure 2), addressing a critical gap in prior convex tensor decomposition work (Tomioka et al., NIPS 2014).  
*Originality:* The paper establishes a novel connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, offering the first theoretical explanation for the latent approach’s empirical superiority. It extends NIPS 2013–2014 work (Tomioka et al.) by resolving the "why" behind observed performance differences, moving beyond empirical comparisons.  
*Significance:* This work directly impacts practitioners using convex tensor methods (e.g., in neuroimaging or collaborative filtering), providing a theoretically grounded preference for the latent approach in noisy settings. The minimal-rank dependency (Theorem 3) is a significant advancement over average-rank scaling in prior work (Mu et al., NIPS 2013), with clear implications for model selection.  
*Clarity:* The paper is well-organized and technically precise, with clear definitions of structured Schatten norms. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**  
*Clarity (Minor):* The motivation for tensor decomposition in machine learning lacks concrete ML context. The paper would significantly strengthen accessibility by adding a graphical model example (e.g., latent variables *h*<sub>1</sub> → *x*<sub>1</sub>, *h*<sub>2</sub> → *x*<sub>2</sub> with arbitrary *h* dependencies), showing how the multilinear rank equals dim(*h*<sub>i</sub>) and why only some *h*<sub>i</sub> might be low-dimensional (a key scenario for the latent approach). A figure illustrating this would be valuable.  
*Quality (Technical):*  
- Theorem 2’s statement requires clarification: Components *Ŵ*<sup>(k)</sup> are recovered up to permutation (not uniquely), a nuance critical for interpretation.  
- The derivation from (12) to (13) needs justification (the authors exploit the singleton decomposition *W*<sup>∗(k)</sup> = *W*<sup>∗</sup> for optimal *k*).  
- Lemma 5 (used in Theorem 2) is referenced but not proven; its inclusion would bolster rigor.  
- The incoherence condition (10) is misaligned with prior work (e.g., Agarwal et al., NIPS 2012 uses incoherence for *sparse+low-rank* recovery, not noise). The authors should clarify the condition’s purpose (energy spreading across components) and remove the misleading comparison.  
*Originality (Context):* The paper should explicitly contrast its structured norms with standard tensor methods (e.g., HOSVD; Kolda & Bader, SIAM Review 2009), noting that HOSVD yields approximation bounds but lacks convex optimization guarantees for noisy settings. The term "mode-rank" should be clarified as "multilinear rank" (standard in tensor literature).

**Pro/Con Acceptance Arguments:**  
*Pro:*  
- Resolves a long-standing empirical gap in convex tensor decomposition with tight, interpretable theory.  
- The minimal-rank error bound (Theorem 3) is a substantial theoretical advance over prior average-rank dependencies.  
- Empirical results perfectly align with theory, demonstrating predictive power for practitioners.  
*Con:*  
- Missing ML context reduces accessibility for broader NIPS audience.  
- Minor technical gaps (Theorem 2, Lemma 5) require clarification but do not invalidate core contributions.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a strong, novel contribution to convex tensor decomposition with clear theoretical and empirical significance. The weaknesses are addressable via minor revisions (adding ML context, clarifying Theorem 2, removing misaligned incoherence claims) and do not undermine the paper’s core advances. The work directly extends NIPS 2013–2014 contributions (Tomioka et al.) and provides a definitive theoretical foundation for the latent approach, which is increasingly adopted in practice. The duality result (Lemma 1) also offers broader utility for structured sparsity research.  

**Specific Revisions Suggested:**  
1. Add a paragraph in Section 1 with a graphical model example (e.g., latent variables *h*<sub>1</sub>, *h*<sub>2</sub> → observed *x*<sub>1</sub>, *x*<sub>2</sub>) to motivate the tensor decomposition problem in ML, noting how multilinear rank corresponds to dim(*h*<sub>i</sub>) and why only some *h*<sub>i</sub> being low-dimensional is critical.  
2. Clarify Theorem 2: "Components *Ŵ*<sup>(k)</sup> are recovered up to permutation; the bound (13) holds for the optimal permutation."  
3. Justify (12)→(13) by noting: "Since *W*<sup>∗</sup> = *W*<sup>∗(k)</sup> + Σ<sub>l≠k</sub> 0 is a valid decomposition for any *k*, the sum Σ<sub>k</sub> *r̄*<sub>k</sub> can be replaced by min<sub>k</sub> *r*<sub>k</sub>."  
4. Remove incoherence comparison with Agarwal et al. (NIPS 2012); replace with a brief explanation of condition (10) as "requiring singular values of *W*<sup>∗(l)</sup><sup>(k)</sup> to be bounded for *l*≠*k* to prevent component interference."  
5. Define "multilinear rank" early (e.g., "the *multilinear rank* (r<sub>1</sub>, ..., r<sub>K</sub>) denotes mode-*k* ranks") and cite Kolda & Bader (2009) for HOSVD context.  

The paper is technically excellent and addresses a meaningful gap in convex tensor decomposition. With the suggested revisions, it will be an impactful contribution to NIPS.
