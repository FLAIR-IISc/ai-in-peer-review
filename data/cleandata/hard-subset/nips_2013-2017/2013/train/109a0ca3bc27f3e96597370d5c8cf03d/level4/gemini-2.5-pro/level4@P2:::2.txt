### **Summary**

This paper investigates a convex optimization framework for tensor decomposition using a class of structured Schatten norms. It specifically focuses on analyzing the "latent" Schatten norm approach, which was empirically shown in prior work to outperform the "overlapped" Schatten norm approach in certain settings. The main theoretical contribution is a rigorous analysis demonstrating that the estimation error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This provides a clear theoretical justification for its superior performance, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode, as the overlapped approach's error scales with the average of the mode-k ranks. As a secondary contribution, the paper establishes a novel duality result between the latent and overlapped Schatten norms, connecting this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The theoretical claims are supported by numerical experiments that confirm the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first rigorous performance analysis for the latent Schatten norm approach to tensor decomposition. The key result—that its error is governed by the minimum mode-k rank—is an important and insightful finding. This elegantly explains previously observed empirical phenomena and demonstrates that the method can perform as well as if the lowest-rank mode were known in advance.

2.  **Novel Duality Result:** The duality established between the overlapped and latent norms is a valuable theoretical contribution in its own right. It provides a deeper connection between convex tensor decomposition and the well-studied field of structured sparsity, which could inspire further work in both areas.

3.  **Clarity and Empirical Validation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The theoretical results are presented clearly, and the numerical experiments are well-designed to specifically validate the derived error bounds. The comparison between the "Tucker Rank (TR) complexity" and "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" provides a clear, empirical confirmation of the theory.

### **Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper is technically strong but could be improved in terms of motivation, technical completeness, and discussion. Addressing the following points would significantly strengthen the manuscript.

**1. Motivation and Accessibility:**
*   For the paper to be accessible to a broader machine learning audience, the motivation for this specific type of tensor decomposition could be strengthened. The authors should consider providing a concrete latent variable model where this problem arises naturally. For instance, a model with latent variables `h_k` each linearly generating observed variables `x_k`, with arbitrary dependencies among the `h_k`. The resulting moment tensor has a Tucker decomposition where the multilinear rank corresponds to the dimensions of the latent variables. A graphical model depicting this would be highly beneficial.
*   Building on this example, the authors could then clearly articulate the advantage of their method: it performs well even if only a subset of the latent variables has low dimensionality, a key scenario highlighted by their theoretical results.

**2. Technical Clarity and Completeness:**
*   **Proof of Lemma 5:** The proof for Lemma 5 appears to be missing from the supplementary material, yet it is a key component in the proof of the main result (Theorem 2). This is a critical omission that must be addressed.
*   **Justification for (13) in Theorem 2:** The paper states that inequality (13) follows from (12). This step is not obvious and requires a clear justification, particularly regarding how the decomposition of W* is chosen to replace the sum of ranks with the minimum rank.
*   **Permutation Ambiguity:** The statement of Theorem 2 implies that the individual components `W^*(k)` are recovered. However, these components can likely only be recovered up to a permutation. This is a minor but important detail that should be explicitly mentioned.
*   **Proof of Lemma 4:** While the proof may be straightforward, for the sake of completeness and readability, it would be better to include it explicitly rather than referring to another paper.

**3. Discussion and Context:**
*   **Incoherence Condition:** The paper claims the incoherence assumption in (10) is similar to those in [1, 3, 11]. This comparison may be misleading. The conditions in those papers are for sparse+low-rank matrix recovery and typically require singular vectors to be incoherent with standard basis vectors. The condition here, which bounds the spectral norm of cross-component unfoldings, seems different in nature. The authors should either clarify this connection precisely or remove the comparison.
*   **Computational Complexity:** A discussion of the computational complexity of solving the optimization problems for both the latent and overlapped approaches is currently missing. This is important information for any potential practitioner and should be included.
*   **Related Work and Terminology:** The introduction would benefit from briefly mentioning other tensor decomposition methods (e.g., HOSVD and its approximation bounds, as reviewed by Kolda and Bader) and acknowledging the different notions of tensor rank to better situate the work. Additionally, noting that "mode-k rank" is also known as "multilinear rank" would be helpful for readers.

**4. Minor Notational/Typographical Issues:**
*   The superscript notation `W^(k)` for the components can be confused with the mode-k unfolding notation `W(k)`. Using a different symbol for the latent components would improve clarity.
*   The abstract contains a typo: "result for structures Schatten" should be "structured Schatten".
*   In the supplementary material (last line of page 12), there appears to be a missing parenthesis.

In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The primary concerns relate to the completeness of the proofs and the need for improved motivation and discussion to broaden the paper's impact. I am confident the authors can address these points in a revision.
