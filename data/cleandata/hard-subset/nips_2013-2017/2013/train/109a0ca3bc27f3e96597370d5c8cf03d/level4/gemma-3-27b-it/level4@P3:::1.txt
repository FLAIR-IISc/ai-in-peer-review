This paper analyzes convex norm-regularized optimization approaches for tensor factorization, focusing on a recent “latent” norm approach [25] and comparing it to a previously used “overlap” norm approach [9, 15, 23, 25]. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the mean-squared-error behavior of the “latent” norm under various noise assumptions, demonstrating improved performance compared to the “overlap” norm – a result that supports empirical observations from a previous work. These theoretical findings are further validated through numerical simulations. The paper also generalizes both norms and establishes a duality theorem for this generalized formulation.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, though a careful examination of the proofs in the supplementary material was not possible within the scope of this review. The results are likely to be of interest to researchers specializing in optimization methods for tensor decompositions. However, the work’s limited scope and technical nature raise concerns about its potential impact at NIPS. While the authors acknowledge the applicability of tensor factorizations to machine learning problems, the paper does not explore these connections theoretically or empirically. This lack of engagement with broader machine learning applications diminishes the potential relevance for the NIPS audience. 

The primary weakness is the incremental nature of the results. The paper provides theoretical justification for an approach [25] that was already shown to perform well empirically. The techniques employed, while rigorously applied, do not appear substantially novel. The absence of complementary lower bounds is also a concern, as the presented upper bounds are used to compare algorithms and argue for the superiority of the latent approach. Without lower bounds, it is difficult to assess the tightness of the bounds and the optimality of the approach.

A minor point is the framing of the paper’s contributions. The abstract claims the introduction of “a new class of structured Schatten norms,” which is somewhat misleading. The paper primarily analyzes *existing* approaches, with the generalization appearing later in Section 2.1 and primarily serving to prove a duality result (Lemma 1). No new algorithms based on this generalized norm are proposed, analyzed, or experimentally tested. 

The placement of Figure 1 in the introduction is also suboptimal, as its interpretation requires background knowledge presented in Section 2. Minor editorial suggestions include: Line 38: change “Tensor is” to “Tensor modelling is”; Line 40: add a comma after “Conventionally”; Line 395: change “also in such situations” to “in such situations as well.”

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent Schatten norm for tensor decomposition.
*   The derived error bounds provide valuable insights into the performance of the latent approach.
*   The duality result for structured Schatten norms is a novel contribution with potential relevance beyond tensor decomposition.
*   The numerical simulations support the theoretical findings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The results are incremental and may not represent a significant advance in the field.
*   The work lacks a connection to broader machine learning applications.
*   The absence of lower bounds limits the assessment of the derived upper bounds.
*   The scope is narrow, potentially limiting the appeal to the NIPS audience.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While technically sound and presenting a solid contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition, the limited scope, incremental nature of the results, and lack of connection to broader machine learning challenges make it a less compelling submission for NIPS. The paper would be more suitable for a conference with a more specialized focus on tensor methods or optimization.
