This paper studies convex tensor decomposition with structured Schatten norms. It unifies the “overlapped” and “latent” approaches by introducing a class of structured norms and proving a duality between them. Building on this framework, the paper analyzes the latent approach and establishes denoising guarantees showing that its mean-squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, rather than the average (square root) of ranks as in prior analyses of the overlapped approach. Theoretical results include consistency, deterministic error bounds under incoherence constraints, and high-probability bounds under Gaussian noise; the analysis also clarifies the choice of regularization and the role of the dual norm. Empirical simulations support the predicted scaling.

Relation to prior work: The overlapped Schatten 1-norm (sum of nuclear norms of mode unfoldings) has been used for tensor completion/decomposition and analyzed previously, notably [26], while the latent approach was proposed in [25] without comparable theory. The paper connects these tensor norms to structured sparsity literature (overlapped vs latent group lasso) and extends duality results to structured Schatten norms. It also relates to recent lower bounds for tensor recovery [19], noting that the latent approach matches the dependence on the minimum mode rank.

Quality: The theoretical contributions are technically sound and clearly stated. The dual characterization of overlapped vs latent Schatten norms is particularly elegant and broadly useful. The bounds for the latent approach are sharper in settings where the true tensor is low-rank primarily in one mode, and the analyses carefully track the dependence on dimensions, ranks, and noise. The role of the spectral-norm constraints (α) and the regularization level λ is explicit. However, the experimental section is brief. Important details are missing or only sketched, limiting reproducibility and interpretability. In particular:
- How were the Tucker ranks (r1, …, rK) of the synthetic examples selected?
- How does group regularization such as ℓ1,∞ behave when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes?
- How does the latent approach compare empirically to standard non-convex baselines (ALS, HOOI/Tucker-HOOI, CP-ALS) in both accuracy and runtime?
- How sensitive is performance to λ, and how is λ chosen in practice without access to noise variance?
- What is the computational cost and scalability of the ADMM solver relative to overlapped formulations and to non-convex methods?

Clarity: The paper is well organized and generally easy to follow. Definitions and assumptions are stated clearly, and the connection to structured sparsity is helpful. The exposition would benefit from expanding the experimental methodology and providing more comprehensive comparisons.

Originality: The main novelty is the duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms and the resulting performance guarantees for the latent approach. This duality seems of independent interest beyond tensor decomposition.

Significance: Tensor decomposition is widely used in machine learning and signal processing. Showing that the latent approach can provably achieve error scaling with the minimum mode rank—and often outperform the overlapped approach—meaningfully advances the theory and may influence practice. The contribution is significant, though stronger empirical validation would increase impact.

Pros for acceptance:
- Clear, general duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms.
- Sharper denoising guarantees for the latent approach (dependence on min mode rank).
- Consistency and high-probability bounds with interpretable dependence on dimensions and noise.
- Conceptual unification with structured sparsity literature; results likely of broader interest.

Cons for acceptance:
- Experimental evaluation is brief and lacks key methodological details.
- No empirical comparison to standard non-convex tensor decomposition algorithms.
- Limited exploration of settings where tensors are simultaneously low-rank in all modes and of alternative group penalties (e.g., ℓ1,∞).
- Practical guidance on choosing λ and the feasibility of spectral-norm constraints (α) is limited.

Suggestions for the authors:
- Specify how the Tucker ranks were chosen and report results across a wider range of rank configurations, including truly simultaneously low-rank tensors.
- Include baselines with ALS/HOOI/CP-ALS (with model selection) and report both accuracy and runtime.
- Empirically evaluate group regularization variants (e.g., ℓ1,∞) and discuss when they may be preferable.
- Provide a principled procedure for tuning λ (e.g., based on noise estimation or cross-validation) and assess sensitivity.
- Report computational complexity and scalability of the ADMM solver, including convergence criteria and wall-clock times.

Overall assessment: This is a well-written and theoretically strong paper that improves upon existing results for convex tensor decomposition. The duality and error bounds are interesting and valuable. Expanding the empirical section as suggested would make the contribution more compelling.
