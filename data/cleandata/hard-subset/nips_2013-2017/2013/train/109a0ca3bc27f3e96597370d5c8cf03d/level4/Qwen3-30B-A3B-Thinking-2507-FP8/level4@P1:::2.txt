

This paper studies structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches to convex optimization-based tensor decomposition. The authors establish a novel duality between these two norms and provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, showing that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in prior work, particularly when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode.

The paper makes a technically significant contribution by rigorously analyzing the latent approach, which was previously only supported by empirical evidence. The authors derive a duality relationship between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. They establish theoretical bounds for the latent approach that clearly demonstrate its advantages over the overlapped approach, particularly when the unknown tensor has low-rank structure in a single mode. The numerical experiments confirm that the mean squared error scales as predicted by their theory, with the latent approach consistently outperforming the overlapped approach in the tested scenarios.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a theoretically sound explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach, addressing a gap in the literature.
2. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature.
3. The theoretical bounds are carefully derived and the numerical experiments support the theoretical claims.
4. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, with sufficient technical details for reproducibility.

Weaknesses:
1. The motivation for the problem is insufficiently developed for a machine learning audience. The authors should include a latent variable model (with graphical representation) demonstrating how tensor decomposition can learn parameters in a practical ML setting. For instance, they could consider a model where latent variables h₁, h₂, ... connect to observed variables x₁, x₂, ... via linear mappings, with arbitrary dependencies among the hᵢ. In this case, the observed moment tensor would have a Tucker decomposition with multilinear rank equal to dim(h₁), dim(h₂), etc. This would clarify why it matters that only some hidden variables might have small dimensions.
2. Theorem 2 contains a technical error: the components are only recovered up to permutation, which the authors should acknowledge. Additionally, the transition from inequality (12) to (13) requires justification.
3. The proof of Lemma 5 is missing, yet it forms a key part of Theorem 2.
4. The authors' reference to "incoherence" after equation (10) is misleading. They use the term to describe the condition that energy should be spread across mixture components, but this differs from the incoherence conditions in [1,3,10] which apply to sparse+low-rank recovery (not relevant here since the noise is not sparse). The comparison to these papers should be removed.
5. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity and more comprehensive related work on HOSVD and other tensor decomposition methods (referencing Kolda's review, section 4).
6. The notation needs improvement: the superscript notation for components (Ŵ⁽ᵏ⁾) is confusing, and the paper uses both r̄ₖ and r̲ₖ for k-mode rank. A consistent notation like mult_rank(tensor) would be preferable.

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition. The technical arguments are sound, and the empirical results support the theoretical claims. However, the proposed clarifications would substantially strengthen the paper's accessibility and technical rigor.

Pros for acceptance: 
- Theoretical novelty in establishing the duality between structured Schatten norms
- Rigorous analysis explaining the empirical superiority of the latent approach
- Clear numerical validation of theoretical predictions
- Connection to broader structured sparsity literature

Cons for acceptance:
- Insufficient motivation for machine learning practitioners
- Technical issues in Theorem 2 and its proof
- Misleading reference to incoherence conditions
- Inconsistent notation and missing technical details

Overall, this is a strong theoretical contribution to tensor decomposition with clear practical implications. With the suggested revisions, the paper would make a significant contribution to NIPS. The authors should address the technical issues and strengthen the motivation to ensure the work is accessible to the broader machine learning community.
