This paper considers convex norm-regularized optimization approaches for factorization of approximately low-rank tensors, focusing on a recently proposed "latent" norm approach and its comparison to a previously used "overlap" norm approach. The paper derives theoretical bounds on the "latent" norm mean-squared-error behavior under several noise assumptions, which compare favorably to the existing analysis on the overlap norm, supporting its better empirically-observed performance in a previous paper. The theory is also confirmed via some numerical simulations. Additionally, the paper considers a generalization of both these norms and proves a duality theorem for it.

The paper is generally easy to follow and appears to be technically sound, although a thorough check of the proofs in the supplementary material is required for confirmation. The results may also be useful for researchers focusing on optimization methods for tensor decompositions. However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's potential impact and novelty.

Firstly, the results are narrow and of a technical nature, only peripherally related to machine learning, and probably of limited potential impact at NIPS. While the authors make the justified case that tensor factorizations can crop up in machine learning, the paper does not attempt to examine the proposed approaches in the context of machine learning problems, either theoretically or experimentally. It would be beneficial to see how these approaches can be applied to real-world machine learning problems.

Secondly, the results seem a bit incremental, and it is not entirely clear how surprising or novel they are. They seem to provide some additional theoretical backing to a specific recently proposed approach, which was already argued empirically to work well. The techniques used also do not seem particularly novel. A more significant contribution would be to provide a more comprehensive analysis or a new perspective on the problem.

A more minor defect is that the upper bounds do not come with any lower bounds. This is potentially dangerous, as the upper bounds are used to compare between different algorithms and argue for the superiority of one over the other. Providing lower bounds would strengthen the paper's claims and provide a more complete understanding of the problem.

Additional comments:

* The abstract begins with "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...". However, this is slightly misleading, as the focus of the paper is devoted to analyzing two existing approaches (overlapped and latent). Unless missed, the generalization only comes up by defining it in section 2.1 and proving what is the dual norm (Lemma 1). In particular, no particular algorithms are proposed based on this generalized Schatten norms, their behavior is not analyzed, nor are they tested experimentally.
* Figure 1 is presented in the introduction, but at this stage is not interpretable without the background appearing in section 2 onwards.
* Line 38: "Tensor is" -> "Tensor modelling is"(?)
* Line 40: Missing comma after "Conventionally"
* Line 395: "also in such situations" -> "in such situations as well"

Overall, the paper's main contribution is theoretical error bounds for a recently proposed low-rank tensor decomposition approach. While the paper seems technically sound, the results are somewhat incremental and may suffer from limited impact at NIPS.

Arguments For Acceptance:

* The paper provides a thorough analysis of the "latent" norm approach and its comparison to the "overlap" norm approach.
* The paper derives theoretical bounds on the "latent" norm mean-squared-error behavior under several noise assumptions.
* The theory is confirmed via some numerical simulations.

Arguments Against Acceptance:

* The results are narrow and of a technical nature, only peripherally related to machine learning.
* The results seem a bit incremental, and it is not entirely clear how surprising or novel they are.
* The paper does not attempt to examine the proposed approaches in the context of machine learning problems, either theoretically or experimentally.
* The upper bounds do not come with any lower bounds.
