

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for convex tensor decomposition, comparing two approaches: the "overlapped" method (penalizing all modes simultaneously) and the "latent" method (decomposing into tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors establish a novel duality between these norms and provide theoretical guarantees showing the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the true tensor is low-rank in a single mode. This explains empirical observations in prior work [25] where the latent approach showed superior performance. The authors further demonstrate that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, whereas the overlapped approach depends on the average rank, providing a precise theoretical explanation for the empirical success.

The paper makes a significant contribution by connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature [2,13,17,21], particularly through the duality result between overlapped and latent norms. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, with Theorems 1-3 establishing consistency, deterministic bounds, and Gaussian noise performance. The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions about error scaling with tensor dimensions and ranks. The authors also provide a clear comparison with the overlapped approach, explaining why the latent method performs better in scenarios where the tensor is nearly full-rank in all but one mode.

Strengths:
1. Theoretical novelty: The duality result for structured Schatten norms is both interesting and generalizable to structured sparsity contexts.
2. Clear theoretical contribution: The paper precisely explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach, addressing an empirical observation without theoretical support in prior work.
3. Strong experimental validation: The numerical results confirm the theoretical scaling behavior of the mean squared error.
4. Well-written: The paper is clearly structured and presents complex concepts accessibly.

Weaknesses:
1. Motivation: The connection to machine learning applications is underdeveloped. The authors should provide a concrete latent variable model (e.g., with graphical representation) where tensor decomposition learns parameters, showing how only some hidden variables may have small dimensions—this is the scenario where the latent approach excels. This would make the work more accessible to the broader NIPS audience.
2. Theoretical clarifications: Theorem 2 requires refinement—the components are only recovered up to permutation, and the transition from (12) to (13) needs justification. Lemma 5's proof is missing, yet it's crucial for Theorem 2.
3. Notation: The use of both $\bar{r}_k$ and $\underline{r}_k$ for mode-k rank is confusing; adopting a consistent notation like "multilinear rank" would improve clarity. The superscript notation $\hat{\cal{W}}^{(k)}$ for components is also confusing.
4. Related work: The paper should cite Kolda's tensor decomposition review [14] to discuss HOSVD and its approximation bounds, and clarify that the "mode-rank" they use is commonly called "multilinear rank" in the literature.
5. Incoherence condition: The authors incorrectly compare their incoherence condition to [1,3,10], which deals with sparse+low-rank recovery, whereas their noise is not sparse. This comparison should be removed.

The paper is well-positioned to make a meaningful contribution to the field of tensor methods in machine learning. The theoretical results provide a clear explanation for empirical observations, and the numerical validation strongly supports their claims. With the suggested clarifications, the paper would be significantly strengthened.

Pro (reasons to accept):
- Theoretical novelty in connecting structured Schatten norms to tensor decomposition with precise performance guarantees
- Clear explanation of why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach, addressing a gap in prior work
- Strong experimental validation of theoretical predictions
- Connection to broader structured sparsity literature

Con (reasons to reject):
- Underdeveloped motivation for machine learning applications
- Several technical clarifications needed for theoretical results
- Confusing notation and inconsistent terminology
- Inaccurate comparison to incoherence conditions in sparse recovery

The authors have made a technically sound contribution with clear significance for tensor decomposition. The paper would be excellent with the suggested clarifications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the theoretical and presentation issues outlined above. The work advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition by providing precise theoretical guarantees that explain empirical observations, making it highly relevant for the NIPS community.
