Summary
The paper studies convex tensor decomposition with a new family of structured Schatten norms that unifies the “overlapped” and “latent” formulations. The authors establish a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm equals the latent Sp*/q* norm, extending known dualities in structured sparsity. Building on this, they analyze the latent S1/1 norm for noisy tensor denoising/decomposition and prove finite-sample error bounds. A key message is that, when the true tensor is low-rank primarily in one (unknown) mode, the latent approach achieves error rates that scale with the minimum mode-k rank, improving upon prior results for the overlapped S1/1 norm, whose error depends on averages across modes. They provide consistency bounds, deterministic bounds under incoherence-type constraints, and high-probability bounds under Gaussian noise. Simulations on synthetic tensors validate the predicted scaling and often show better performance for the latent approach than the overlapped formulation, even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across modes.

Quality
- Theory: The technical development is solid and builds on tools from decomposable regularization and random matrix theory. The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is clean and of independent interest. The error bounds are interpretable, with a favorable dependence on the minimal mode rank for the latent approach. Assumptions (e.g., spectral-norm incoherence) are reasonable but should be discussed in terms of verifiability and practical relevance.
- Scope/claims: The results cover denoising with full observations; the connection to completion is mentioned as future work. The conditions on the regularization parameter are clear, and the observation that optimal λ does not depend on unknown ranks is useful. It would help to include more discussion on the tightness of constants and whether bounds are minimax (or close) in representative regimes.
- Experiments: The empirical section is concise. Additional details would improve reproducibility and credibility. Specifically:
  - How were the Tucker ranks (r1,…,rK) and the latent factor ranks chosen in synthetic generation?
  - How sensitive is performance to the incoherence parameter α and to violations of the assumptions (e.g., when components are correlated across modes)?
  - How does the latent approach compare with standard non-convex baselines (e.g., HOOI/Tucker, HOSVD truncation, CP-ALS) in both accuracy and runtime?
  - Can the authors provide a practical rule for setting λ (and α) from data?

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and easy to follow. Definitions (unfolding, multilinear rank, latent vs. overlapped norms) are precise. The statements of lemmas and theorems are clear, and the narrative linking duality to the statistical analysis is coherent.
- Suggestions: Provide more intuition for the latent decomposition (e.g., a figure illustrating the “mixture of mode-specific low-rank” idea), and give a brief, self-contained discussion of the ADMM solver and computational costs. Summarize the assumptions (including α constraints) in one place and discuss how to check or approximate them in practice.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a notable contribution that generalizes known results from the group-sparsity literature to the tensor norm setting.
- The statistical analysis for the latent norm that achieves dependence on the minimal mode rank (rather than averages) is an important and nontrivial refinement over previous overlapped-norm results.

Significance
- Tensor methods are widely used in signal processing, neuroscience, and recommendation. Understanding when latent vs. overlapped formulations are preferable is valuable for both theory and practice.
- The improved scaling in the bounds can influence algorithm selection and design in noisy settings. The work should interest researchers working on structured convex regularization and multiway data.

Specific questions and suggestions
- Experimental design: Clarify how (r1,…,rK) are sampled and how latent factor ranks are chosen/estimated in the LR complexity measure. Include experiments where the tensor is strongly low-rank in all modes versus dominantly low-rank in one mode, and report cases where the overlapped approach is competitive or superior.
- Baselines: Add comparisons to HOOI/Tucker truncation and CP-ALS on the same synthetic setups (and, if possible, a small real dataset), reporting both error and runtime.
- Hyperparameters: Provide a data-driven guideline for λ (e.g., based on noise estimation and dimensions) and discuss the role/selection of α. Report sensitivity analyses.
- Practicality: Discuss computational scalability of the latent approach relative to the overlapped approach (per-iteration cost, convergence behavior, and wall-clock time in the provided experiments).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, general duality result linking overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
- Stronger statistical guarantees for the latent approach with dependence on the minimal mode rank.
- Well-written and well-structured presentation; contributions are positioned relative to prior work.
- Empirical results align with theory and show advantages of the latent approach in relevant regimes.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited experimental evaluation (synthetic only; minimal baseline comparisons; few details on setup and hyperparameter selection).
- Practical guidance on choosing λ and α, and on verifying assumptions, is brief.
- No evaluation on tensor completion or real data, which would broaden impact and demonstrate robustness.

Overall, this is a well-executed theoretical paper with a meaningful contribution and a clear message. Strengthening the empirical section and adding practical guidance would further increase its impact.
