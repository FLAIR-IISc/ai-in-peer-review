This paper investigates convex optimization methods for low-rank tensor decomposition, focusing on the comparison between two approaches based on structured Schatten norm regularization: the "overlapped" norm and the more recent "latent" norm from Tomioka et al. [25]. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting. The authors derive mean-squared-error bounds that demonstrate the latent approach's performance scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. This provides a theoretical justification for its empirically observed superiority over the overlapped approach, whose performance was previously shown to depend on the *average* of the mode-k ranks. As a secondary contribution, the paper introduces a generalized class of structured Schatten norms that encompasses both approaches and establishes a novel duality result between them, connecting this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations that confirm the predicted scaling behavior of the error.

**Strengths**

1.  **Theoretical Justification:** The paper's main strength is providing a solid theoretical foundation for an empirically observed phenomenon. It rigorously explains *why* the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, particularly when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. This moves the understanding of these methods from empirical intuition to a well-supported theoretical claim.

2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and generally easy to follow. The theoretical development is presented logically, starting from the definitions of the generalized norms, establishing their duality, and then using these tools to derive the main performance bounds.

3.  **Generalization and Duality:** The formulation of a general class of structured Schatten norms and the proof of the duality between the overlapped and latent types (Lemma 1) is an elegant contribution. This result is interesting in its own right and connects tensor decomposition methods to established concepts in structured sparsity, which could be beneficial for future work in this area.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Originality and Incremental Nature:** The paper's contributions, while technically sound, feel somewhat incremental. The core focus is on analyzing an existing method [25] and theoretically confirming its known advantages. The analytical techniques appear to be adaptations of existing tools from the high-dimensional statistics and structured sparsity literature, rather than introducing a fundamentally new proof technique.

2.  **Narrow Scope and Impact for NIPS:** While the introduction rightly points out that tensor decomposition is relevant to machine learning, the paper itself remains entirely focused on the theoretical analysis of the optimization procedure for a synthetic denoising problem. There is no attempt to connect the analysis or experiments back to a real-world machine learning application (e.g., collaborative filtering, neuroimaging). This narrow technical focus may limit the paper's interest and impact for the broader NIPS audience.

3.  **Absence of Lower Bounds:** The central argument for the superiority of the latent approach rests on a comparison of upper bounds on estimation error (Inequalities (15) and (16)). This can be misleading, as the tightness of these bounds is unknown. Without corresponding lower bounds that match the rates, it is difficult to be certain that the derived upper bounds reflect the fundamental statistical limits of the problem or the intrinsic properties of the estimators.

**Detailed Comments and Suggestions**

*   The abstract's opening sentence, "We study a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...", is slightly misleading. The paper's primary focus is the analysis of two specific, existing norms. The generalized class is defined and a duality result is shown, but no new algorithms are proposed or analyzed based on this generalization. Rephrasing to something like, "We analyze a class of structured Schatten norms for tensors..." would more accurately reflect the paper's contributions.

*   Figure 1 is introduced to motivate the work but is difficult to fully interpret until the concepts of "overlapped" and "latent" approaches are formally defined later in the paper. While this is a minor point, moving the figure or adding a more detailed caption could improve readability for a reader unfamiliar with the prior work.

*   The numerical simulations successfully confirm the theoretical scaling laws, which is a good validation of the analysis. However, it would strengthen the paper to also show results on a semi-synthetic or real dataset to better illustrate the practical significance of the performance difference between the two methods.

**Summary of Arguments**

*   **For Acceptance:** The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and provides a valuable and rigorous theoretical explanation for the superior performance of the latent tensor decomposition approach. The duality result is an elegant and potentially useful contribution.
*   **Against Acceptance:** The work is largely a theoretical justification of a previously proposed method, making the contribution feel incremental. Its narrow focus on the analysis of an optimization method, without a clear link or application to a machine learning problem, may limit its significance and appeal to the NIPS community.

Overall, this is a solid piece of theoretical work. However, its incremental nature and limited scope in the context of the NIPS conference are significant concerns.
