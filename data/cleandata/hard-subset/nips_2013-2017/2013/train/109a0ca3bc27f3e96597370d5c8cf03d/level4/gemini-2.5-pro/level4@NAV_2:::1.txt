This paper investigates convex norm-regularized optimization for the decomposition of approximately low-rank tensors. The primary contribution is a theoretical analysis of the "latent" structured Schatten norm approach, which was recently introduced in [25] and empirically shown to outperform the more conventional "overlapped" norm approach in some scenarios. The authors derive mean-squared error bounds for the latent method in a noisy setting, demonstrating that its performance scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor. This contrasts with the overlapped method's dependence on the average of the mode-k ranks, thereby providing a formal explanation for the latent approach's observed advantages. The theoretical findings are validated through numerical simulations that confirm the predicted scaling behavior. On a more general level, the paper introduces a broader class of structured Schatten norms that unifies both the latent and overlapped variants and establishes a novel duality theorem for this class, connecting the work to the literature on structured sparsity.

**Strengths**

*   The paper provides a rigorous and valuable theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach over the overlapped approach for tensor decomposition. The derived error bounds clearly explain why the latent approach is advantageous, particularly when the underlying tensor possesses a low rank in only a single, unknown mode.
*   The work appears to be technically sound. The formulation of a generalized class of structured Schatten norms and the accompanying duality result (Lemma 1) is an elegant contribution that helps unify different approaches and connects them to the broader field of structured sparsity.
*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The theoretical claims are well-supported by numerical experiments that effectively validate the predicted error scaling with respect to both tensor rank and size.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Significance:** While tensor decomposition has clear applications in machine learning, the paper's contributions are framed in a primarily theoretical context. The analysis and experiments focus on a synthetic denoising task, and it is not demonstrated how the performance gains would translate to downstream machine learning problems. This narrow focus might limit the paper's potential impact and appeal to the broader NIPS audience.
*   **Originality:** The work is largely an analysis of a method recently proposed in [25]. While providing theoretical backing for existing methods is an important contribution, the results, which confirm prior empirical findings, may be perceived as incremental. Furthermore, the analytical techniques employed appear to be standard for this line of research.
*   **Completeness of Analysis:** The paper uses upper bounds on the estimation error to argue for the superiority of the latent approach. However, the analysis does not include corresponding lower bounds, making it difficult to assess the tightness of the results. Comparing algorithms based solely on upper bounds can be inconclusive, as the slack in each bound might differ.

**Additional Comments**

*   The abstract's opening, "We study a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...", may be slightly misleading. The paper's primary focus is the analysis of two specific, existing norms. The generalized class is defined and its dual is derived, but no new algorithms based on this generalization are proposed or evaluated. Rephrasing to highlight the analysis of the latent approach as the core contribution would be more accurate.
*   Figure 1 is used in the introduction to motivate the problem but is difficult to interpret without the background material presented in Section 2. The authors should consider moving this figure or adding more context to the caption to improve clarity for the reader at that early stage.
*   Minor writing suggestions:
    *   Line 40: Consider adding a comma after the introductory adverb: "Conventionally, tensor decomposition..."
    *   Line 395: "in such situations as well" is a more natural phrasing than "also in such situations".
