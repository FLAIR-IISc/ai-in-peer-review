This paper investigates convex norm-regularized optimization methods for the factorization of approximately low-rank tensors. The primary focus is on analyzing the "latent" norm, a recently proposed approach, and comparing it to the more established "overlap" norm. The authors derive theoretical mean-squared-error (MSE) bounds for the latent norm under various noise assumptions. These new bounds compare favorably to existing results for the overlap norm, providing a theoretical justification for the superior empirical performance of the latent norm previously reported elsewhere. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations. Additionally, the paper introduces a generalized class of norms that encompasses both the latent and overlap norms and proves a corresponding duality theorem.

The paper is well-written, easy to follow, and the technical content appears to be sound, although the proofs in the supplementary material were not checked in detail. The findings may be valuable for researchers working on optimization methods for tensor decompositions. However, the paper's contribution is constrained by two primary weaknesses.

First, the scope of the work is narrow and highly technical. While the authors correctly note that tensor factorization has applications in machine learning, the paper does not connect its theoretical results to any specific machine learning problems. The analysis is purely algorithmic, and there are no experiments on real-world machine learning tasks, which may limit the paper's potential impact and relevance to the broader NIPS audience.

Second, the contribution feels incremental. The main results provide theoretical backing for the latent norm approach, which was already shown to be effective empirically. The theoretical techniques employed do not appear to be particularly novel. As such, the findings, while useful, may not be surprising to experts in the field.

A further limitation is the absence of corresponding lower bounds for the derived error guarantees. The paper relies solely on upper bounds to argue for the superiority of the latent norm. Without an understanding of how tight these bounds are, such a comparison is not fully conclusive, as one bound could simply be looser than the other.

**Additional Comments**

*   The claim in the abstract, "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...", seems to overstate the paper's contribution. The vast majority of the paper is dedicated to analyzing two existing norms. The generalized norm is only briefly introduced in Section 2.1, where its dual is derived (Lemma 1). No new algorithms are developed based on this generalization, nor is its performance analyzed or tested, making it a peripheral part of the work rather than a central proposal.

*   Figure 1 is placed in the introduction but is difficult to interpret without the background material presented later in Section 2. It would be more effective if it were moved or if its context were better explained upfront.

*   Several minor grammatical and stylistic edits are suggested:
    *   Line 38: "Tensor is" could be revised to "Tensor modeling is".
    *   Line 40: Add a comma after "Conventionally".
    *   Line 395: "also in such situations" could be rephrased as "in such situations as well".

In summary, this paper provides a technically sound theoretical analysis of a norm for low-rank tensor decomposition. However, its incremental nature and limited connection to machine learning applications may reduce its impact for the NIPS community.
