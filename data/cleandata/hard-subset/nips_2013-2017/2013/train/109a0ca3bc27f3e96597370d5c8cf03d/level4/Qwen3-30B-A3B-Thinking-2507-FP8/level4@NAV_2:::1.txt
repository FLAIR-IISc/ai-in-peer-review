

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the recently proposed "latent" approach with the established "overlapped" approach. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that the latent approach achieves mean squared error scaling proportional to the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach depends on the average (square root) of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result explains the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach in certain settings. The paper also establishes a novel duality between the two structured Schatten norms, connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature. The theoretical findings are validated through numerical experiments that confirm the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

The paper is technically sound, with clear theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of convex optimization for tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is thorough, and the numerical experiments appropriately validate the theoretical predictions. The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making the technical content accessible to readers familiar with tensor decomposition and convex optimization. The authors effectively relate their work to previous contributions in the field, including the structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21] and tensor decomposition papers [9, 15, 23, 25, 26].

The main strengths of the paper include:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis demonstrating the superior performance of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach
- Novel duality result between structured Schatten norms that extends previous work on structured sparsity
- Comprehensive numerical validation that confirms theoretical predictions
- Clear exposition that connects tensor decomposition to broader concepts in convex optimization and structured sparsity

The primary weaknesses include:
- The paper's focus is limited to theoretical analysis of tensor decomposition without demonstrating applications in machine learning problems, which reduces its significance for a machine learning conference like NIPS
- The theoretical results, while technically sound, may be considered incremental as they provide theoretical backing for an empirically observed advantage rather than introducing fundamentally new methodology
- The absence of lower bounds for the error rates makes the comparison between approaches somewhat limited, as upper bounds alone cannot fully establish superiority

The paper's contribution is primarily theoretical, and while it makes an important contribution to the technical understanding of tensor decomposition methods, its direct relevance to machine learning applications is limited. The authors do not explore how these tensor decomposition techniques might be applied to specific machine learning problems, which would strengthen the paper's significance for NIPS. The connection to machine learning is mentioned in the introduction (e.g., in neuroimaging and collaborative filtering), but the paper does not demonstrate these applications or analyze the methods in the context of machine learning problems.

The paper is well-positioned within the tensor decomposition literature, building upon previous work by [25, 26] while providing novel theoretical insights. The authors properly cite relevant prior work, including the structured sparsity literature [1, 3, 12, 17, 21] and tensor decomposition papers [5, 6, 9, 14, 18, 23, 25, 26]. The paper does not appear to be out of scope for NIPS, as tensor decomposition is relevant to machine learning applications.

In summary, this is a technically strong paper that makes a solid theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. While the results are somewhat incremental and lack direct machine learning applications, they provide important theoretical justification for the latent approach. The paper would benefit from a more explicit connection to machine learning applications to strengthen its significance for NIPS.
