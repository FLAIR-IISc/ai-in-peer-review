

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for convex tensor decomposition, focusing on the theoretical comparison between two approaches: the "overlapped" and "latent" Schatten norms. The authors establish a duality between these norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature (e.g., overlapped vs. latent group lasso). They demonstrate that the latent approach, which decomposes tensors into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode, achieves a mean squared error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average mode-k rank. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in prior work. The theoretical results are rigorously proven, with supporting numerical experiments confirming the predicted scaling behavior.

**Quality.** The paper makes a significant technical contribution through its theoretical analysis of the latent Schatten norm approach. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and extends the structured sparsity framework to tensor decomposition. The consistency and denoising performance guarantees (Theorems 1-3) are well-motivated and technically sound, providing a clear theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The authors address a meaningful gap in the literature: prior work [25, 26] had empirically observed the latent approach's superiority but lacked theoretical justification. However, the experimental section is underdeveloped. The authors do not detail how Tucker ranks (r₁,...,rₖ) were chosen for the synthetic tensors, nor do they compare against non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., alternating least squares or higher-order orthogonal iteration) that are commonly used in practice. A more comprehensive experimental comparison would strengthen the paper's practical relevance. The theoretical optimal regularization constant λ is shown to be independent of the unknown ranks, which is a useful insight, but the paper could better explain how to select λ in practice when ranks are unknown.

**Clarity.** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written. The mathematical notation is consistent and well-defined, with careful explanations of key concepts like mode-k unfolding and multilinear rank. The logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical results to experimental validation is coherent. The authors effectively use figures (e.g., Figure 1) to illustrate their key insights, and the supplementary material provides the necessary technical details for the proofs. The paper would benefit from slightly more detailed explanations of the experimental setup (e.g., how the latent rank decomposition was computed for the complexity measure in Equation 18), but the main text remains accessible to experts in the field.

**Originality.** The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms represents a novel contribution with independent interest beyond tensor decomposition. This result generalizes the duality between overlapped and latent group sparsity norms [1, 12, 17, 21], establishing a meaningful connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity literature. While the convex tensor decomposition framework itself builds on prior work [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], the specific theoretical analysis of the latent approach's performance, particularly its scaling with the minimum mode-k rank, constitutes a significant advance. The paper distinguishes itself from previous NIPS papers on tensor decomposition by providing rigorous theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, which had previously been empirically observed but not theoretically justified.

**Significance.** Tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with wide applications in neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, and environmental monitoring. This paper makes a meaningful contribution by demonstrating that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach both theoretically and empirically for noisy tensor decomposition. The theoretical insight that the latent approach performs as if it knew the mode with the smallest rank (without requiring this information) is particularly valuable for practitioners. The results provide a theoretically grounded alternative to non-convex approaches, which are commonly used but lack theoretical guarantees. The paper's findings have immediate implications for practitioners working with tensor data, suggesting the latent approach should be preferred in many practical scenarios.

**Pro and Con Acceptance Arguments:**
*Pro:* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for an empirically observed advantage of the latent approach in tensor decomposition. The duality result between the two norms is elegant and generalizable. The theoretical analysis precisely predicts the scaling behavior of the mean squared error, and the numerical results confirm these predictions. The paper addresses an important gap in the literature and makes a clear contribution to the field.

*Con:* The experimental section lacks sufficient detail and comparison to non-convex approaches. The paper does not thoroughly explore how the method performs when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes (a favorable case for the overlapped approach), which would strengthen the comparative analysis. The practical implementation details for selecting regularization parameters are not sufficiently developed.

**Overall Recommendation:** This paper makes a strong theoretical contribution to convex tensor decomposition with significant implications for both theory and practice. The core theoretical results are sound, novel, and well-justified, though the experimental section would benefit from more detail and broader comparisons. The paper advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition and is of high interest to the NIPS community. I recommend acceptance.
