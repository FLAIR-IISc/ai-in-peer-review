This paper studies convex norm-regularized approaches for decomposing approximately low-rank tensors, with a focus on the “latent” Schatten norm approach [25] and its comparison to the earlier “overlapped” approach. The authors analyze the latent approach theoretically, deriving consistency and denoising error bounds under both deterministic conditions and Gaussian noise. The key result is that the mean-squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, which explains empirical observations that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, whose guarantees scale with an average of mode-wise ranks [26]. The paper also presents a duality theorem relating generalized overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms, and empirically validates the predicted scaling behavior.

Relation to prior work: The overlapped Schatten norm has been proposed and analyzed in prior work [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], while the latent approach was introduced in [25]. The duality result connects these tensor norms to the broader literature on structured sparsity and group norms [1, 12, 17, 21]. The empirical comparisons and theoretical guarantees are positioned as complementary to prior noiseless tensor completion analyses.

Quality: The paper appears technically sound and is generally easy to follow, though I did not verify the supplementary proofs in detail. The results include: (i) a general consistency bound in terms of the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise; (ii) deterministic bounds under an incoherence-type condition controlling cross-mode spectral norms; and (iii) high-probability bounds for Gaussian noise with a regularization choice independent of unknown ranks. The head-to-head comparison with the overlapped approach uses established bounds from [26], and the theory is supported by simulations that confirm the predicted scaling. One caveat is the absence of lower bounds, which weakens claims of superiority when comparing upper bounds across methods.

Clarity: The paper is mostly well written and organized. However, Figure 1 is introduced in the opening without sufficient context; moving it later or adding a brief explanation of “latent” vs “overlapped” norms in the introduction would help. There are a few minor editorial issues (see below).

Originality: The work is incremental but nontrivial. The duality between generalized overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a nice conceptual contribution, though it aligns with known dualities in structured sparsity. The error bounds for the latent approach sharpen our understanding relative to [26] and formalize an empirical observation from [25]. The techniques appear to adapt known tools (e.g., decomposable regularizers and norm duality) rather than introduce fundamentally new methods.

Significance: The contribution is fairly narrow and primarily technical. While tensor decompositions have ML relevance, the paper focuses on optimization and statistical analysis of norms rather than on machine learning problems per se. No applications to ML tasks are studied theoretically or experimentally. For the NIPS audience, impact may be limited to researchers working specifically on convex formulations for tensor methods.

Pros:
- Clear problem setup; rigorous analysis of the latent approach with both deterministic and probabilistic bounds.
- Theoretical explanation for empirical advantages of the latent approach when one mode is low-rank.
- Useful duality result connecting overlapped and latent Schatten norms, consistent with structured sparsity frameworks.
- Empirical results align with theoretical scaling predictions, including guidance on regularization choice.

Cons:
- Scope is narrow and largely methodological, with limited direct engagement with ML tasks.
- Contributions feel incremental; the main qualitative message (latent can outperform overlapped) was already observed empirically in [25].
- Techniques, while careful, are largely adaptations of existing frameworks; algorithmic novelty is limited.
- No lower bounds are provided, weakening comparative claims based solely on upper bounds.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Substantiate impact with ML-driven experiments (e.g., multiway data in recommendation, spatiotemporal prediction, neuroimaging) or theory tailored to such settings.
- Provide lower bounds or discuss optimality to contextualize the tightness of the proposed guarantees.
- Clarify the practical implications of the generalized norms beyond the duality (e.g., algorithms or use cases).
- Reposition Figure 1 or add the needed background earlier.

Additional comments:
- Abstract: “We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms…” is slightly misleading, since the primary focus is analyzing two existing norms (overlapped and latent). The generalization appears mainly in Section 2.1 with a duality result (Lemma 1), without accompanying algorithmic development, analysis, or experiments for the generalized norms.
- Figure 1 is hard to interpret at its first mention; defer or add brief context on the two norms in the introduction.
- Minor edits:
  - Line 38: “Tensor is” → “Tensor modelling is.”
  - Line 40: Add a comma after “Conventionally.”
  - Line 395: “also in such situations” → “in such situations as well.”

Overall: The paper’s main contribution is a set of theoretical error bounds for a recently proposed low-rank tensor decomposition approach, with supportive empirical evidence. The work seems technically sound, but the novelty is modest and the expected impact at NIPS may be limited by its narrow, technical focus.
