### Review

**Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates convex optimization approaches for low-rank tensor decomposition, focusing on two recently proposed methods based on structured Schatten norm regularization: the "overlapped" approach and the "latent" approach. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach, which was previously observed empirically in [25] to outperform the "overlapped" method in certain settings.

The authors derive mean-squared error bounds for the latent approach under noisy observations. Their key theoretical finding is that the error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This provides a formal justification for its superiority over the overlapped approach, whose error bound depends on the *sum* (or average) of the mode-k ranks. This result explains why the latent method performs well when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode but nearly full-rank in others.

As a secondary contribution, the paper introduces a generalized class of structured Schatten norms that encompasses both the overlapped and latent norms. Within this framework, the authors prove a novel duality result connecting the two norm types, which is of independent interest and relates to similar dualities in the structured sparsity literature. Finally, the paper presents numerical simulations that confirm the derived theoretical scaling behavior of the mean-squared error.

**Strengths**

1.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper appears technically sound and is well-written and clearly organized. The arguments are easy to follow, and the theoretical contributions are presented logically. The generalization of the norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is an elegant and solid theoretical result.

2.  **Rigorous Justification of Empirical Results:** The paper provides a valuable service by placing the empirical success of the latent approach from [25] on firm theoretical ground. The analysis clearly explains *why* and *when* the latent approach should be preferred over the overlapped one, connecting its performance directly to the underlying rank structure of the tensor.

3.  **Reproducibility and Validation:** The authors validate their theoretical claims with numerical experiments. The simulations are well-designed to test the predicted scaling laws, and the results presented in Figure 2 strongly support the theoretical analysis concerning the mean-squared error's dependence on the "Latent rank (LR) complexity."

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Significance and Impact for NIPS:** While the paper addresses an important problem in signal processing and data analysis, its focus is narrow and highly technical. The contributions are primarily theoretical results about specific optimization formulations. The paper does not connect these findings back to a broader machine learning context. For instance, there is no theoretical or empirical investigation of how these tensor decomposition methods perform within a larger machine learning pipeline (e.g., for collaborative filtering, feature extraction, or multi-task learning). This narrow focus on error bounds for tensor recovery may limit its interest and impact for the general NIPS audience.

2.  **Incremental Nature of the Contribution:** The main result is a theoretical analysis of a specific method proposed and empirically validated in a previous paper [25]. While the analysis is novel and important, it feels more like a follow-up study than a groundbreaking contribution. The techniques used in the proofs appear to be adaptations of existing analysis methods for high-dimensional statistics and structured sparsity, which reduces the overall originality.

3.  **Lack of Lower Bounds:** The paper's argument for the superiority of the latent approach rests on a comparison of upper bounds on the estimation error (inequalities (15) and (16)). While suggestive, this is not fully conclusive, as one or both of the bounds could be loose. Establishing matching lower bounds would significantly strengthen the claims by demonstrating that the derived rates are optimal and that the gap between the two methods is fundamental. Without them, it is difficult to be certain that the observed theoretical advantage is not an artifact of the analysis.

**Additional Comments and Suggestions**

*   The abstract begins, "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...". This seems to overstate the contribution. A generalized norm is defined in Section 2.1, but its primary purpose is to prove the duality in Lemma 1. The paper does not propose or analyze any new algorithms based on this generalized class. The focus is squarely on analyzing two specific, pre-existing norms. It would be more accurate to state that the paper analyzes existing norms within a new, unified framework.
*   Figure 1 is used in the introduction to motivate the work, but it is difficult for a reader to interpret without the background provided in later sections on the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. Consider moving it or adding a more detailed caption to make it self-contained.
*   Minor writing suggestions:
    *   Line 38: "Tensor modelling may also be valuable..." would be more grammatical than "Tensor modelling may also valuable...".
    *   Line 40: Add a comma after "Conventionally".
    *   Line 395: "in such situations as well" is slightly better phrasing.

In summary, this is a solid, technically sound paper that provides a valuable theoretical analysis for a recently proposed tensor decomposition method. However, its contributions are somewhat incremental and its narrow technical focus may limit its appeal and significance for the NIPS community.
