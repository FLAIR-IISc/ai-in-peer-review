### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of convex norm-regularized optimization for the factorization of approximately low-rank tensors. It focuses on comparing a recently proposed "latent" norm approach with the more established "overlap" norm. The primary contribution is the derivation of theoretical mean-squared-error bounds for the latent norm under several noise assumptions. These new bounds compare favorably to existing bounds for the overlap norm, providing a theoretical justification for the superior empirical performance of the latent norm that was observed in prior work. The analysis is supplemented with numerical simulations that confirm the theoretical findings. Finally, the paper introduces a generalization of both norms and proves a corresponding duality theorem.

### **Strengths**

*   The paper is well-written and the arguments are generally easy to follow.
*   The technical results appear to be sound (though the proofs in the supplementary material were not checked in detail).
*   The theoretical analysis could be valuable for researchers working specifically on optimization methods for tensor decompositions.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope and Relevance to Machine Learning:** The paper's primary weakness is its narrow scope and limited connection to the broader machine learning community. While the authors correctly note that tensor factorization is relevant to machine learning, the paper does not explore the implications of these theoretical results within any specific machine learning application, either theoretically or experimentally. The contribution is of a highly technical and theoretical nature, which may limit its impact and interest for the NIPS audience.

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The contribution feels somewhat incremental. The main result is to provide theoretical justification for the latent norm approach from [25], an approach that was already shown to perform well empirically. While such theoretical backing is valuable, the results are not particularly surprising, and the techniques employed appear to be standard for this line of work.

3.  **Absence of Lower Bounds:** A significant limitation of the theoretical analysis is the absence of matching lower bounds. The paper's central argument for the superiority of the latent norm rests on a comparison of error upper bounds for the two methods. Without corresponding lower bounds, it is difficult to ascertain how tight these bounds are, making the comparison less conclusive. It is possible that the bound for the overlap norm is simply looser than the one derived for the latent norm, rather than the algorithm itself being fundamentally worse. This makes it difficult to definitively claim the superiority of one approach over the other based solely on this analysis.

### **Clarity and Presentation**

*   **Misleading Abstract:** The abstract's opening sentence, "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors...," is slightly misleading. The vast majority of the paper is dedicated to analyzing two existing norms (overlapped and latent). The proposed generalization is only briefly defined in Section 2.1 and is primarily used to prove a single duality lemma (Lemma 1). No algorithms based on this generalized class are proposed, analyzed, or tested. It would be more accurate to frame the paper as an analysis of existing norms.

*   **Figure Placement:** Figure 1, which appears in the introduction, is difficult to interpret at that point in the paper. The concepts required to understand it are not introduced until Section 2. The figure would be more effective if it were moved to a later section, after the necessary background has been provided.

### **Minor Corrections**

*   Line 38: "Tensor is" could be improved to "Tensor modeling is".
*   Line 40: A comma is missing after "Conventionally".
*   Line 395: "also in such situations" could be rephrased as "in such situations as well".

### **Conclusion**

The paper's main contribution is a set of theoretical error bounds for a recently proposed low-rank tensor decomposition approach. While the paper seems technically sound, the results are somewhat incremental, focus on a narrow theoretical problem without clear connections to machine learning applications, and suffer from the technical limitation of lacking lower bounds. For these reasons, the paper may have limited impact at NIPS.
