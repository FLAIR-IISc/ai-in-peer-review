This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex tensor decomposition method regularized by the "latent Schatten norm." The authors analyze the performance of this "latent approach" and prove that its error bounds are superior to those of the previously studied "overlapped approach," particularly when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only a single, unknown mode.

The central idea is to model a tensor `W` as a sum of `K` component tensors, `W = W(1) + ... + W(K)`, where each component `W(k)` is penalized for having a high rank only in its corresponding mode `k`. This is contrasted with the overlapped approach, which penalizes all `K` unfoldings of the original tensor `W` simultaneously. The paper's key theoretical contributions are twofold. First, it establishes a novel and general duality between "overlapped Sp/q-norms" and "latent Sp*/q*-norms," which connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity and is of independent interest. Second, using this duality, the authors derive a performance bound for the latent approach in a noisy setting. They show that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas the error for the overlapped approach scales with the *average* of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This result provides a rigorous theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in prior work and in the paper's own simulations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses a known limitation of the popular overlapped Schatten norm regularization for tensor decomposition. The theoretical justification for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped one, by adapting to the lowest-rank mode without prior knowledge, is a significant and insightful contribution to the field.
*   **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-executed. The duality result between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is particularly elegant and provides a unifying framework. The main performance guarantees (Theorems 2 and 3) are clear, well-supported, and directly explain the practical advantages of the method.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of defining the problem, clearly distinguishing between the latent and overlapped approaches, and building their theoretical argument step-by-step. The connection to the structured sparsity literature is also well-articulated.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Limited Experimental Scope:** While the numerical results effectively confirm the derived theoretical scaling laws, the experimental section feels brief and could be strengthened.
    *   It would be beneficial to provide more details on the experimental setup, specifically regarding the selection of the multilinear ranks (r1, r2, r3) for the synthetic data. Clarifying how these were chosen would help assess the generality of the results.
    *   The comparison is restricted to two convex methods. A comparison against established non-convex methods (e.g., Higher-Order SVD or alternating least squares) would provide valuable context and better situate the contribution within the broader tensor decomposition literature.
    *   The paper argues the latent approach is also competitive when the tensor is low-rank in all modes. A more explicit experimental investigation of this scenario, where the overlapped approach is theoretically favored, would make the empirical case for the latent method more compelling.

*   **Discussion of General Norms:** The paper introduces a general framework of structured Sp/q norms but the analysis and experiments focus exclusively on the S1/1 case. A short discussion on the potential applications or benefits of other norms in this family (e.g., the S1/∞-norm mentioned in the conclusion) would add further depth and highlight interesting directions for future research.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper provides a strong, novel theoretical foundation for the latent Schatten norm approach to tensor decomposition, explaining its superior performance over the overlapped method.
*   The primary error bound, which depends on the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, is a significant and impactful result.
*   The duality theorem for structured Schatten norms is an elegant contribution of independent interest that bridges the gap between tensor methods and structured sparsity.
*   The work is technically sound, clearly presented, and addresses an important problem in machine learning and data analysis.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The experimental evaluation, while supportive of the theory, is narrow. It lacks comparisons to non-convex baselines and a more thorough exploration of different data-generating regimes.

In summary, this is a strong theoretical paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of convex methods for tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are novel, significant, and clearly presented. While the experimental section could be more comprehensive, the strength of the theoretical contributions makes this a solid paper for acceptance.
