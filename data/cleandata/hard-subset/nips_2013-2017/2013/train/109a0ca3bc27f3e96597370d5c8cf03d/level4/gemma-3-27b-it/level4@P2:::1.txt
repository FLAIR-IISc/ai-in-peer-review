This paper analyzes convex norm-regularized optimization approaches for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, focusing on a recent “latent” norm approach [25] and comparing it to a previously used “overlap” norm approach. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the mean-squared error of the “latent” norm under various noise assumptions, demonstrating performance advantages that align with empirical observations from prior work. These theoretical findings are validated through numerical simulations. The paper also generalizes both norms and establishes a duality theorem.

The paper is well-written and technically appears sound, though a detailed check of the supplementary material proofs was not feasible. The results could be valuable to researchers working on optimization methods for tensor decompositions. However, the primary concerns relate to the scope and impact of the work within the NIPS conference context.

First, the results are highly technical and narrowly focused, with only peripheral connections to mainstream machine learning. While the authors acknowledge potential applications of tensor factorizations in machine learning, the paper lacks exploration of these approaches within actual machine learning problems, either theoretically or empirically. 

Second, the contributions feel incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper primarily provides theoretical justification for a recently proposed and empirically successful approach [25]. The techniques employed do not appear novel, and the findings, while solid, aren't particularly surprising. The lack of accompanying lower bounds on performance is also a limitation, as upper bounds alone can be insufficient for robust algorithm comparison and justification of superiority claims.

Further points for consideration:

*   The abstract's claim of proposing a "new class of structured Schatten norms" is potentially misleading. The core focus is on analyzing existing approaches (“overlapped” and “latent”). The generalization to a broader class of norms, introduced in Section 2.1, appears more as a mathematical tool for establishing the duality theorem (Lemma 1) rather than driving a novel algorithmic direction. No new algorithms are proposed based on this generalized norm, nor are they analyzed or tested.
*   Figure 1 is presented in the introduction but requires background knowledge from Section 2 to be fully understood, potentially hindering initial reader engagement.
*   Minor stylistic issues exist, such as "Tensor is" (Line 38, potentially should be “Tensor modelling is”), and a missing comma after “Conventionally” (Line 40).
*   The phrase “also in such situations” (Line 395) is slightly awkward and could be rephrased as “in such situations as well”.

**Summary of Arguments for and Against Acceptance:**

**Arguments For Acceptance:**

*   Technically sound analysis of a relevant tensor decomposition approach.
*   Provides theoretical grounding for empirically observed performance improvements.
*   Establishes a useful duality result for structured Schatten norms.
*   Clear writing and organization.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Limited scope and impact within the NIPS conference focus.
*   Incremental contribution—primarily theoretical justification for an existing method.
*   Lack of connection to practical machine learning applications.
*   Absence of lower bounds to complement the established upper bounds.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid and technically correct analysis of a specific tensor decomposition technique. However, its limited scope, incremental nature, and lack of direct relevance to core machine learning challenges suggest it may not be the strongest fit for NIPS. While the work is valuable within the niche area of tensor decomposition, it likely doesn’t meet the high bar for significant impact expected at this conference. I would lean towards **reject**, but would not strongly object to a borderline accept.
