Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper studies a class of structured Schatten norms for tensors and develops theory for convex tensor decomposition regularized by the latent Schatten norm. It formalizes two families of norms: the overlapped Sp/q-norm, which penalizes the mode-k unfoldings jointly, and the latent Sp*/q*-norm, which decomposes a tensor into a sum of mode-specific low-rank components. A key contribution is a duality result showing that the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm is the latent Sp*/q*-norm, generalizing dualities known in structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs latent group lasso). Building on this, the paper analyzes the denoising performance of the latent approach and proves that, under suitable conditions, the mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor. This contrasts with prior bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with the average of the square roots of the mode ranks. The authors also provide consistency results and Gaussian noise bounds with a recommended, dimension-dependent regularization parameter that does not depend on the unknown ranks. Empirical simulations on synthetic data confirm the predicted scaling and show that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach.

The work is well grounded in and advances prior literature on convex tensor methods (e.g., overlapped nuclear-norm penalties for tensor completion and denoising) and connects to high-dimensional M-estimator analyses with decomposable regularizers (Agarwal, Negahban, Wainwright) and structured sparsity (Obozinski, Jenatton). It strengthens and complements earlier analyses of overlapped penalties (Tomioka et al., 2011–2012) and the latent approach (Liu et al., Tomioka et al., empirical), and relates to recent lower bounds for tensor recovery (Mu et al.).

Strengths:
- Clear formalization of structured Schatten norms with a clean duality result that is broadly relevant beyond the specific application.
- Sharp and interpretable statistical guarantees for the latent approach:
  - A norm inequality bounding the latent Schatten-1 norm by sqrt(min_k r_k) times the Frobenius norm.
  - Consistency and deterministic error bounds with explicit dependence on ranks.
  - Gaussian noise bounds with a practical choice of λ that depends only on dimensions and noise level, not on unknown ranks.
- Conceptual clarity: the latent approach behaves as if it knew the lowest-rank mode, explaining its empirical advantage over the overlapped approach.
- Empirical confirmation that MSE scales with the proposed “latent rank” complexity and that the latent method often yields lower error.
- Solver availability aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Experimental scope is limited:
  - Only synthetic denoising is considered. Including real datasets (e.g., video/hyperspectral, neuroimaging) and a tensor completion setting would increase practical relevance.
  - No comparisons to standard non-convex baselines (ALS/HOOI/Tucker/CP). Even if global optimality is not guaranteed for those, reporting their typical performance and runtime would contextualize the benefits of the convex latent approach.
  - Limited exploration of λ selection. Although the theory proposes a dimension/noise-based λ, an empirical sensitivity analysis or cross-validation strategy would be useful.
- Assumptions and constraints:
  - The deterministic analysis relies on an incoherence-type spectral norm bound between components controlled by α and imposes spectral norm constraints in the optimization. It would help to clarify whether these constraints were enforced in experiments, how α was chosen, and how sensitive the method is to misspecification.
  - Discussion of when α is small in practice (or how to estimate it) would make the assumptions more transparent.
- Clarity and notation:
  - The notation for ranks could be streamlined. r_k denotes mode-k rank of W*, while r̄_k denotes the rank of the k-th latent factor; emphasizing this distinction earlier would avoid confusion.
  - A short algorithmic summary in the main text (beyond referencing ADMM) would help readers reproduce results without consulting the code.
  - Minor editorial issues (e.g., “betters” → “better”; a few typos in references) can be corrected.

Quality:
The theoretical development is technically sound and carefully argued. The duality result, norm inequalities, and error bounds are well motivated and connect nicely to established frameworks in high-dimensional statistics. Constants are not fully explicit (e.g., universal c, factor F), but the dependencies are clear and interpretable. The main limitation is the narrow empirical evaluation and the lack of non-convex baselines and real data.

Clarity:
The paper is clearly written and well organized. Definitions and assumptions are stated upfront, and the comparison to prior work is fair. The presentation would benefit from slightly more detail on the optimization procedure and experimental setup (e.g., λ and α selection), and from harmonizing rank notation.

Originality:
The duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms for tensors, and the statistical analysis showing min-rank dependence for the latent approach, are, to my knowledge, novel. The results generalize known ideas from structured sparsity to the tensor setting in a meaningful way.

Significance:
Tensor methods are central in many applications. Establishing that the latent convex approach can achieve error scaling governed by the smallest mode rank, and often outperform overlapped penalties, is a valuable insight that can influence both theory and practice. The duality result is likely to have broader methodological impact.

Questions for the authors:
- How were the synthetic Tucker ranks (r1, r2, r3) chosen across experiments? Please provide a systematic description.
- In Theorem 2 and 3, were the spectral norm constraints with parameter α enforced in practice? How was α chosen, and how sensitive are results to α?
- How does the method compare to non-convex baselines (e.g., HOOI/Tucker ALS/CP) in MSE and runtime?
- Can the analysis be extended to tensor completion with missing entries (i.e., a sampling operator in the loss)? Any preliminary results?
- Do your results suggest guidance for choosing between overlapped S1/1 and alternatives such as S1/∞ in practice?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong and novel theoretical contributions (duality and error bounds with min-rank dependence).
- Clear conceptual message that aligns with and explains empirical behavior.
- Relevance to a broad set of tensor applications; potential impact beyond tensors via structured norms.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation (synthetic only), no non-convex baselines, no real data.
- Dependence on additional constraints and parameters (α) not fully explored empirically.

Overall, this is a solid and insightful paper with clear theoretical advances. Strengthening the empirical section with real datasets, non-convex baselines, and details on α/λ selection would significantly bolster the submission.
