Summary
This paper studies convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families of norms: the “overlapped” norms that penalize all mode-unfoldings of a tensor simultaneously, and the “latent” norms that represent a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a particular mode. A key theoretical contribution is a duality result showing that the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm, generalizing known dualities in structured sparsity. The main statistical result analyzes denoising performance for the latent approach: under Gaussian noise (and a mild cross-component spectral norm control), the mean-squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, rather than with an average of ranks as in prior analyses of the overlapped approach. This explains empirical observations that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. Experiments corroborate the predicted scaling and the relative advantage of the latent method.

Relation to prior work
The paper connects convex tensor regularization [e.g., Gandy et al.; Liu et al.; Signoretto et al.] with structured sparsity and latent-group formulations [Jalali et al.; Obozinski et al.; Jenatton et al.], and builds on high-dimensional M-estimation frameworks [Agarwal et al.; Negahban et al.]. It complements previous analyses of overlapped convex tensor methods [Tomioka et al., NIPS’11/12] and relates to lower bounds and relaxations for tensor recovery [Mu et al.]. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of classical tensor decompositions and approximation guarantees via HOSVD/Tucker [Kolda & Bader; De Lathauwer et al.].

Strengths
- A clean and useful duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms.
- New recovery bounds for the latent approach that scale with min mode rank, matching empirical behavior where one mode is much lower rank than others.
- Sharp, interpretable rates in the Gaussian setting with a regularization choice that does not depend on unknown ranks.
- Experiments align well with theory and compare fairly to overlapped methods.

Weaknesses and requested revisions
- Motivation for a machine learning audience is limited. The paper should explain when these decompositions arise in ML tasks, and why the latent approach is advantageous when only some modes are low-rank.
- Some proof details and statements require clarification (e.g., permutation invariance of components; the step from Eq. (12) to (13); missing lemma proof).
- The notion of “incoherence” is conflated with conditions used for sparse+low-rank matrix recovery; the comparison is potentially misleading.
- Notational inconsistencies and minor typos hamper readability.

Quality
The technical development is strong and appears largely sound, but key parts need clarification:
- Theorem 2: Recovery of components is only identifiable up to permutation; please state this explicitly. Also, inequality (13) does not “immediately” follow from (12); provide a clear argument (e.g., by constructing a singleton decomposition and invoking the latent norm’s induced decomposition to replace a sum over ranks by mink rk).
- Include the proof of Lemma 5 (it is central to Theorem 2). For completeness, sketch the proof of Lemma 4 rather than solely referencing prior work.
- Clarify and justify the spectral norm constraints (Eq. (11)) and their role in controlling cross-component interference.

Clarity
Writing is generally good, but several additions would broaden accessibility:
- Motivation via a latent-variable model: Include a simple graphical model with observed variables x1,…,xK and latent factors h1,…,hK, with linear conditionals xi = Aihi + noise and arbitrary dependencies among the hi’s. Explain that the observed cross-moments produce a Tucker decomposition with multilinear ranks dim(h1),…,dim(hK). Emphasize that some hi may be low-dimensional while others are not, matching the regime where the latent approach excels. A figure would help.
- Related work: Briefly describe approximation bounds of HOSVD/Tucker and their relevance (see Kolda & Bader’s review, Section 4).
- Ranks: Early in the introduction, define mode-k (multilinear) ranks and note alternative notions of tensor rank (CP rank, tubal rank), to avoid confusion.
- Computational complexity: Discuss the per-iteration cost of the ADMM solver (dominated by SVDs of mode unfoldings), memory footprint, and empirical scalability with N and K. Comment on when the latent formulation is computationally comparable to or heavier than the overlapped one.

Originality
The duality result and the latent-norm analysis for tensors are novel and valuable. The work draws a clear parallel to latent group lasso while extending the theory to the multilinear setting.

Significance
The results offer actionable guidance for practitioners choosing between overlapped and latent convex tensor models, especially in imbalanced low-rank regimes. The analysis likely will be used and built upon by both ML and signal processing communities.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advance (duality and sharper denoising guarantees).
- Strong alignment between theory and experiments.
- Practical insight into model selection (latent vs. overlapped) in common ML settings.

Arguments against acceptance
- Insufficient ML motivation/use-cases and missing latent-variable illustration limit accessibility.
- Minor but important proof omissions/clarifications and some imprecise statements.
- Notational and presentation issues that impede readability.

Specific comments to address
- State permutation ambiguity for component recovery in Theorem 2.
- Provide a transparent derivation for moving from Eq. (12) to (13).
- Include the proof of Lemma 5; sketch the proof of Lemma 4.
- Reframe the “incoherence” discussion after Eq. (10). Your condition bounds cross-mode spectral norms of components; it is not the same as the standard incoherence between singular vectors and canonical basis used in sparse+low-rank matrix settings [Agarwal et al.; Hsu et al.]. Avoid equating them; clarify the intuition (“energy spread across components/modes”) and its implications.
- Expand related work on HOSVD/Tucker and their approximation guarantees.
- Add a brief complexity discussion for the proposed algorithms.

Typos and notation
- Abstract: “structures Schatten” → “structured Schatten.”
- Notation for components: using superscripts Ŵ(k) for both mode and component is confusing. Consider a different symbol for components (e.g., V[k] or Zk) to avoid clashes with mode-k unfolding.
- Avoid using both r̄k and r̲k for mode-k ranks. Introduce a single clear notation; consider mult_rank(W) = (r1,…,rK).
- Supplementary material, p.12, last line: fix missing parenthesis.
- Ensure consistent use of unfolding/folding notation and norms across main text and supplement.

Overall recommendation
Lean accept, contingent on addressing the clarification and presentation issues above. The paper presents meaningful theoretical and practical advances, and with improved motivation and clarity, it will be accessible and useful to a broad NIPS audience.
