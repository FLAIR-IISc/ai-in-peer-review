This paper provides theoretical bounds on the performance of a convex tensor decomposition algorithm regularized by the latent Schatten norm. It improves upon existing results for regularization using the overlapped Schatten norm and establishes a novel duality between the two norms.

The paper begins by framing tensor decomposition as a crucial problem arising in diverse applications such as neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. It contrasts conventional non-convex approaches with recent convex-relaxation-based methods, highlighting limitations of the “overlapped” approach when the tensor is low-rank in only a subset of its modes. The “latent” approach, which addresses this limitation, is the central focus of the work. The authors rigorously analyze the latent approach’s performance, demonstrating its superior scaling behavior—achieving performance comparable to knowing the mode with the smallest rank—and establishing a duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, a potentially valuable contribution in the broader context of structured sparsity. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations, confirming the predicted scaling behavior.

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a clear theoretical analysis supported by numerical results. The derivation of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a particularly interesting and potentially impactful result. The consistency and denoising performance analyses for the latent approach are well-motivated and rigorously presented. However, the experimental section feels somewhat limited. While it confirms the theoretical scaling, it lacks detail regarding the selection of Tucker ranks for the generated tensors and a more comprehensive comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods. A discussion of how standard group regularization techniques (e.g., l1/∞) behave when the tensor *is* simultaneously low-rank in all modes would also strengthen the analysis. The proofs appear thorough, though relegated to the supplementary material.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-organized and clearly written. The notation is generally consistent, and the authors provide sufficient background information for readers familiar with tensor decomposition and convex optimization. The flow of the arguments is logical, and the key results are highlighted effectively.

**Originality:** The primary original contribution lies in the duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms. This result is not only relevant to tensor decomposition but also has potential implications for structured sparsity in general. While the application of convex optimization to tensor decomposition is not entirely novel, the analysis of the latent approach and its theoretical justification represent a significant advance. The comparison to existing overlapped approaches, and the insights into why the latent approach performs better in specific scenarios, are valuable contributions.

**Significance:** Tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem with broad applications. Improving the theoretical understanding and practical performance of tensor decomposition algorithms is therefore a significant contribution. This paper demonstrably advances the state-of-art by providing tighter performance bounds for the latent approach compared to existing results for the overlapped approach. The connection to structured sparsity further broadens the potential impact of the work. The findings are likely to be of interest to researchers and practitioners working on high-dimensional data analysis, machine learning, and related fields.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, providing valuable insights into its performance.
*   Novel duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, potentially impacting broader areas of structured sparsity.
*   Clear and well-organized presentation.
*   Demonstrated improvement over existing convex approaches.
*   Confirmation of theoretical results through numerical experiments.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   Experimental section is relatively brief and lacks detailed analysis of parameter choices and comparison with non-convex methods.
*   Limited discussion of the applicability and performance in scenarios where the tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes.
*   The connection to and comparison with existing group regularization techniques could be further elaborated.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are significant, the paper is well-written, and the findings are supported by experiments. While the experimental section could be expanded and further refined, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.

**Recommendation:** Accept.
