

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, focusing on the theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach compared to the previously proposed "overlapped" approach. The authors establish a duality between these two norm families (Lemma 1), demonstrating that the latent approach's mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach depends on the average mode-k rank. This theoretical insight explains the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach in [25]. The paper also provides numerical validation of the theoretical predictions.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The duality result (Lemma 1) generalizes existing work on structured sparsity norms [2, 13, 17, 21], connecting tensor decomposition to broader sparse representation literature. The theoretical analysis is technically sound, with clear proofs for the main results (Theorems 1-3) and supporting numerical experiments that confirm the predicted scaling behavior (Section 4). The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with appropriate references to related work in tensor decomposition [5, 6, 10, 14, 25, 26] and structured sparsity [1, 2, 13, 17, 21].

The main strengths of the paper include:
1. A rigorous theoretical analysis that explains the empirical performance differences between two existing approaches
2. A novel duality result connecting the overlapped and latent Schatten norms
3. Clear numerical validation that supports the theoretical claims
4. A well-structured presentation that makes the technical content accessible

However, the paper has several significant limitations that affect its suitability for NIPS:

First, the significance for the NIPS community is limited. While tensor decomposition has applications in machine learning (e.g., collaborative filtering, neuroimaging), the paper does not demonstrate how these techniques would be applied to actual machine learning problems. The authors briefly mention potential applications but provide no theoretical or empirical evidence that their approach would improve performance on standard machine learning tasks. This is a missed opportunity to demonstrate the broader relevance of their theoretical contributions.

Second, the results are somewhat incremental. The paper builds upon the work of [25], which already empirically demonstrated the latent approach's superiority. The theoretical analysis provides supporting evidence but doesn't introduce fundamentally new concepts or techniques. The duality result, while interesting, is a natural extension of existing structured sparsity theory rather than a breakthrough. The techniques used (e.g., leveraging duality for error bounds) are standard in optimization and not particularly novel.

Third, the paper lacks lower bounds to complement the upper bounds. The theoretical comparison between the latent and overlapped approaches relies entirely on upper bounds, which is problematic because the actual performance could be significantly better than the bounds suggest. Without matching lower bounds, it's difficult to assess whether the observed performance gap is meaningful or merely a consequence of loose upper bounds.

Fourth, the claim that the paper proposes "a new class of structured Schatten norms" in the abstract is misleading. The paper primarily analyzes two existing approaches (overlapped and latent), with the generalization being a technical extension that doesn't introduce new algorithms or applications. The paper doesn't propose or test any new algorithms based on the generalized norms, which weakens the significance of this aspect.

Minor issues include:
- Figure 1 appears in the introduction but is not interpretable without the background in Section 2
- The abstract's claim about a "new class of structured Schatten norms" requires qualification
- Some grammatical issues (e.g., "Tensor is" should be "Tensor modelling is" on line 38)

Overall, the paper makes a technically sound contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition methods. However, its narrow focus on theoretical analysis without connection to machine learning applications or broader significance diminishes its impact for NIPS. The results are incremental rather than groundbreaking, and the lack of lower bounds weakens the theoretical comparison between approaches.

For NIPS, papers should demonstrate clear significance to machine learning research. While tensor decomposition is a relevant technique, this paper fails to connect its theoretical contributions to practical machine learning problems or applications. The authors would need to substantially strengthen the significance argument by demonstrating how their theoretical insights lead to improved performance on standard machine learning tasks or by showing broader implications for machine learning theory.

Given these considerations, I recommend rejection. The paper is technically sound but lacks the broader significance expected for NIPS, with results that are incremental and not sufficiently connected to machine learning applications.
