This paper analyzes convex norm-regularized optimization methods for low-rank tensor factorization, focusing on a recent “latent” norm approach and comparing it to the established “overlap” norm. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the mean-squared-error performance of the latent norm under various noise assumptions, demonstrating improvements over existing analyses of the overlap norm and providing theoretical support for its previously observed empirical success. These theoretical findings are validated through numerical simulations. Additionally, the paper generalizes both norms and establishes a duality theorem for this generalized form.

The paper is generally well-written and appears technically sound, though a detailed verification of the proofs in the supplementary material was not possible within the scope of this review. The results could be valuable to researchers working on optimization algorithms for tensor decompositions. However, my primary concerns relate to the scope and potential impact of the work.

First, the results are quite technical and narrowly focused, with only a peripheral connection to core machine learning problems. While the authors acknowledge the relevance of tensor factorizations to machine learning, the paper lacks any investigation of these approaches *within* a machine learning context, either theoretically or empirically. 

Second, the contributions feel incremental. The paper primarily provides further theoretical justification for a recently introduced method [25] that was already shown to perform well empirically. The techniques employed do not represent a significant departure from existing methods in the field. The absence of corresponding lower bounds for the derived upper bounds is also a concern, as these upper bounds are used to compare algorithms and claim superiority, a comparison potentially weakened without lower bound confirmation.

Several smaller points warrant mention. The abstract’s claim of “proposing a new class of structured Schatten norms” is potentially misleading. The paper’s primary focus is on analyzing *existing* norms (overlap and latent), with the generalization appearing later as a theoretical extension. No new algorithms based on this generalized norm are proposed, analyzed, or empirically evaluated. Figure 1, presented in the introduction, requires background information from Section 2 to be interpretable. Specific stylistic suggestions include changing “Tensor is” (line 38) to “Tensor modelling is” and adding a comma after “Conventionally” (line 40), and revising “also in such situations” (line 395) to “in such situations as well.”

In summary, the paper presents technically sound theoretical error bounds for a recent low-rank tensor decomposition approach. While the analysis is rigorous, the results are incremental and may lack the broader impact typically expected at NIPS.
