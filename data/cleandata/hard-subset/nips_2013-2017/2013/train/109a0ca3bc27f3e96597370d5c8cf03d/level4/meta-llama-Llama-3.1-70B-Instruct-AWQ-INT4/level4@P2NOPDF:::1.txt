This paper explores convex norm-regularized optimization methods for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, focusing on the recently proposed "latent" norm approach and its comparison to the "overlap" norm approach. The authors derive theoretical bounds on the mean-squared-error behavior of the "latent" norm under various noise assumptions, which favorably compare to existing analysis on the overlap norm. These theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations and provide insight into the empirically observed superior performance of the latent norm approach. Additionally, the paper presents a generalization of both norms and proves a duality theorem for it.

The paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow, with technically sound results that may be of interest to researchers working on optimization methods for tensor decompositions. However, there are some concerns regarding the paper's relevance and impact. Firstly, the results, although technically sound, are narrow and only peripherally related to machine learning, which may limit their potential impact at NIPS. While the authors acknowledge the connection between tensor factorizations and machine learning, they do not explore this connection further through theoretical or experimental analysis.

Secondly, the results seem incremental, building upon a recently proposed approach that has already demonstrated empirical success. The techniques used in the paper do not appear to be particularly novel, which may reduce the significance of the contributions. Furthermore, the absence of lower bounds for the upper bounds presented in the paper may be problematic, as these upper bounds are used to compare different algorithms and argue for the superiority of one approach over another.

In terms of presentation, there are a few minor issues. The abstract may be misleading, as it suggests the proposal of a new class of structured Schatten norms, whereas the paper primarily focuses on analyzing existing approaches. The introduction of Figure 1 may be premature, as it is not interpretable without the background information presented in later sections. Additionally, there are a few minor errors in the text, such as missing commas and typos.

In conclusion, while the paper presents technically sound results, its contributions may be limited by their incremental nature and lack of direct relevance to machine learning. The absence of lower bounds for the upper bounds presented in the paper may also be a concern. However, the paper may still be of interest to researchers working on optimization methods for tensor decompositions.
