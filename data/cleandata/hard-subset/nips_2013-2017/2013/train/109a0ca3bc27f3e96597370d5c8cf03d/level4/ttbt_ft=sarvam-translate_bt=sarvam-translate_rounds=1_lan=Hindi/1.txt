This paper explores optimization approaches with convex norm-regularity for factorization of low-rank tensors. It focuses on a recently proposed "implicit" norm approach ([25]) and its comparison with the previously used "explicit" norm approach. Specifically, this paper derives theoretical bounds on the "implicit" norm mean-squared-error behavior under several noise assumptions, which favorably compare to existing analysis on the implicit norm, supporting its better empirical-observed performance in previous papers. The theory is also confirmed through some numerical simulations. Finally, this paper considers generalizations of these two norms and proves a duality theorem for them.

This paper appears to be generally easy to understand and technically sound (although I have not carefully examined the evidence in the supplementary material). The results may also be useful for researchers focusing on optimization methods for tensor decomposition. My main problems are that:

(i) The results are of a narrow and technical nature, only peripherally related to machine learning, and possibly of limited potential impact in NIPS. While the authors argue (rightfully) that tensor factorization may arise in machine learning, this paper does not attempt to theoretically or experimentally test the proposed approaches in the context of machine learning problems.

(ii) The results seem a bit incremental, and I did not find them particularly surprising (although this is a subjective perception). They seem to provide some additional theoretical support to a recently proposed specific approach [25], which had already been argued to work well empirically. The techniques used also do not seem particularly new.

A more minor flaw is that the upper bounds do not come with any lower bounds. This is potentially dangerous, as upper bounds are used to compare between different algorithms and argue for superiority of one over the other.

- The abstract begins with "We propose a new class of structured eigenvalue norms for tensors..." However, I think this is a bit misleading. Unless I leave something out, the generalization comes only by defining it in Section 2.1 and proving what the duality norm is (Theorem 1). Specifically, no particular algorithm is proposed based on these generalized eigenvalue norms, the behavior of such algorithms is not analyzed, nor are they experimentally tested.
