

This paper analyzes convex norm-regularized approaches for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, focusing on the "latent" norm (from [25]) and comparing it to the established "overlap" norm. It derives theoretical mean-squared-error bounds for the latent norm under various noise models, demonstrating superior performance relative to prior overlap norm analysis and aligning with empirical observations from [25]. Numerical simulations support the theoretical claims. The work further generalizes both norms and proves a duality theorem for the generalized family.

The paper is technically sound and well-structured, though I did not verify all supplementary proofs in detail. The theoretical results may interest researchers in tensor optimization. However, I have significant concerns about the paper's relevance and contribution for NIPS.

**Primary Concerns:**

1.  **Limited ML Impact and Relevance:** The work is narrowly focused on technical aspects of tensor norms, with minimal connection to machine learning. While the authors note tensor factorizations *can* arise in ML, they provide *no* theoretical or empirical analysis of the proposed methods within actual ML problems (e.g., as part of a learning algorithm on real datasets). This lack of ML context significantly diminishes the potential impact for NIPS, which prioritizes contributions with clear ML relevance and application potential.

2.  **Incremental Contribution:** The results appear incremental. The paper provides additional theoretical justification for a recently proposed approach ([25]) that was already empirically validated. The techniques used (e.g., deriving error bounds via norm properties) are not particularly novel, and the theoretical comparisons do not yield surprising insights beyond confirming prior empirical observations. The contribution feels like a logical extension rather than a substantial advance.

3.  **Missing Lower Bounds:** Crucially, the upper error bounds lack corresponding lower bounds. This is problematic because the paper uses these upper bounds to argue for the superiority of the latent norm over the overlap norm. Without lower bounds, the claimed advantage could be overstated; the gap between the bounds might be large, meaning the theoretical superiority might not reflect practical performance differences. This omission weakens the core comparative claim.

**Additional Comments:**

*   **Misleading Abstract:** The abstract states "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms..." This is inaccurate. The paper's core focus is *analysis* of two existing norms (latent and overlap). The generalization (Section 2.1) is introduced as a minor extension solely to prove the duality lemma (Lemma 1). No new algorithms based on this generalized norm are proposed, analyzed theoretically, or tested experimentally. The abstract overstates the novelty of the norm family.
*   **Figure 1:** The introduction presents Figure 1 without sufficient context. The figure is unintelligible at this point, as the necessary background (e.g., definitions of norms, tensor structure) is deferred to Section 2. This should be revised.
*   **Minor Typos/Clarity:**
    *   Line 38: "Tensor is" → "Tensor modeling is"
    *   Line 40: Missing comma after "Conventionally"
    *   Line 395: "also in such situations" → "in such situations as well"

**Overall Assessment:**

The paper presents technically sound theoretical analysis of specific tensor norms, confirming prior empirical observations. However, the contribution is narrow, incremental, and lacks the necessary connection to machine learning applications or problems to justify its significance for NIPS. The absence of lower bounds undermines a key claim, and the misleading abstract overstates the novelty of the norm family. While the results are valid for the specific technical problem addressed, they do not represent a substantial advance with broad potential impact for the ML community. The paper would be better suited for a more specialized journal or conference focused on optimization or tensor methods, rather than NIPS. The technical merits are present, but the limited scope and lack of ML relevance significantly reduce its suitability for this venue.
