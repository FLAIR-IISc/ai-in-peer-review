- Paper compares overlapped vs latent structured Schatten norms for convex tensor decomposition and proves a duality between them.
- Main theoretical result: latent approach MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank, explaining its empirical superiority over overlapped (which scales with average rank).
- Authors provide rigorous analysis of the latent approach, filling a prior gap where it was only empirically supported.
- Duality result generalizes structured sparsity literature and connects tensor decomposition to that body of work.
- Theoretical bounds are carefully derived and supported by numerical experiments showing MSE scaling as predicted.
- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.
- Motivation for a machine-learning audience is weak; include a latent-variable generative example (with graphical model) to show practical relevance.
- Theorem 2 is flawed/unclear: component recovery should be stated up to permutation, and the step from inequality (12) to (13) needs justification.
- Proof of Lemma 5 is missing, yet it is crucial for Theorem 2 and must be provided.
- The term “incoherence” after eq.(10) is misleading and differs from standard incoherence in sparse+low-rank literature; remove or clarify the comparison.
- Paper lacks discussion of computational complexity and should cite broader tensor decomposition literature (e.g., HOSVD, Kolda review Sec.4).
- Notation is inconsistent/confusing (e.g., superscripted components Ŵ^(k) and both r̄_k and r̲_k); adopt uniform notation such as mult_rank(tensor).
- Pros for acceptance: theoretical novelty (duality), rigorous analysis of latent approach, clear numerical validation, and linkage to structured sparsity.
- Cons for acceptance: insufficient ML motivation, technical issues in Theorem 2 and its proof, misleading incoherence reference, inconsistent notation and missing technical details.
- Overall: strong theoretical contribution with practical implications; address the listed technical and presentation issues to improve accessibility and rigor.
