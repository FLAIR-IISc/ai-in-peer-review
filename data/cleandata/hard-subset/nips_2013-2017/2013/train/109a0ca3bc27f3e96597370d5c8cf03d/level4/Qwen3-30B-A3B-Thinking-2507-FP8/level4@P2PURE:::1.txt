

This paper examines convex norm-regularized optimization methods for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, with a focus on a recently proposed "latent" norm approach ([25]) and its comparison to the previously used "overlap" norm. Specifically, the authors derive theoretical bounds on the mean-squared-error behavior of the "latent" norm under various noise assumptions, which compare favorably to existing analysis of the overlap norm and support its empirically observed superior performance in prior work. The theoretical findings are corroborated through numerical simulations. Additionally, the paper generalizes both norms and establishes a duality theorem for this generalization.  

The paper is generally well-structured and appears technically sound (though I have not verified the proofs in the supplementary material in detail). The results may be of interest to researchers working on optimization for tensor decompositions. However, my primary concerns are:  
(i) The results are narrowly technical and only tangentially related to machine learning, likely limiting their impact at NIPS. While the authors justify tensor factorizations' relevance to machine learning, the paper does not evaluate the proposed methods within machine learning contexts—neither theoretically nor experimentally.  
(ii) The contributions appear incremental. The results provide additional theoretical justification for a recently proposed method ([25]), which was already empirically validated as effective. The techniques employed also lack significant novelty.  

A minor but important issue is the absence of lower bounds for the derived upper bounds. This is problematic because the upper bounds are used to compare algorithms and claim superiority for one approach over another.  

Additional Comments:  
- The abstract states, "We propose a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors," which is misleading. The paper primarily analyzes two existing approaches (overlap and latent norms). The generalization introduced in Section 2.1 only defines the generalized norm and proves its dual (Lemma 1), without proposing new algorithms, analyzing their behavior, or testing them experimentally.  
- Figure 1 appears in the introduction but is unintelligible without the background provided in Section 2 onward.  
- Line 38: "Tensor is" → "Tensor modeling is."  
- Line 40: Missing comma after "Conventionally."  
- Line 395: "also in such situations" → "in such situations as well."
