This paper introduces and analyzes a class of structured Schatten norms for convex tensor decomposition, focusing on the "latent" approach, which models a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. This work builds upon recent developments in convex relaxations for tensor decomposition that primarily utilized an "overlapped" Schatten norm, penalizing all unfolded matrices simultaneously [9, 15, 23, 25, 26].

The paper's main contributions are threefold. First, it presents a general framework for structured Schatten norms, formalizing the overlapped and latent approaches. Second, it establishes a novel and elegant duality result between the general forms of these two norms, which is of broader interest and connects this work to the literature on structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21]. Third, and most importantly, it provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the denoising performance of the latent approach. The key finding is that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with the performance of the overlapped approach, which depends on the *average* of the mode-k ranks [26], thus providing a formal explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach when a tensor is low-rank in only one of its modes. Numerical experiments are provided to validate this theoretical scaling behavior.

**Strengths:**

*   **Significance and Originality:** The paper addresses an important problem in tensor decomposition by providing the first rigorous theoretical guarantees for the latent approach. The theoretical result—that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the mode with the minimum rank—is a significant and insightful contribution that advances our understanding of convex methods for tensor analysis. The duality result between the overlapped and latent norms is also a novel technical contribution that could be useful in the broader context of structured regularization.
*   **Quality:** The technical analysis appears solid and well-executed. The derived error bounds clearly explain the practical advantages of the latent approach over the overlapped one. The claims are well-supported by numerical simulations that compellingly demonstrate that the empirical mean squared error scales precisely as predicted by the theory.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement:**

The paper is well-written and the contributions are valuable. The following points are suggestions to further improve its quality and clarity.

**Major Points:**

*   **Clarity of Technical Details:** Several key parts of the theoretical arguments require clarification to be fully convincing.
    *   In Theorem 2, the statement should acknowledge that the individual components Ŵ(k) are only recovered up to a permutation of the true components W*(k).
    *   The paper states that inequality (13) follows from (12). This step is not immediately obvious and requires a clear justification, especially concerning how the singleton decomposition is used to replace the sum of ranks with the minimum rank.
    *   The proof for Lemma 5 appears to be missing from the supplementary material. As this lemma is a key component in the proof of Theorem 2, its inclusion is essential for verifying the main result.
    *   While the proof of Lemma 4 may be straightforward by reference, providing it explicitly would make the paper more self-contained and easier to follow for the reader.
*   **Motivation and Accessibility:** The motivation for the problem setting could be strengthened for the broader NIPS audience. The paper would be more accessible if it included a concrete example, perhaps a latent variable graphical model where observations are linear functions of latent variables with varying dimensionalities. This would naturally lead to a tensor with a Tucker decomposition where the multilinear ranks correspond to the latent dimensions, and would clearly illustrate the scenario where only a subset of modes are low-rank—the key advantage of the proposed method.

**Minor Points:**

*   **Related Work and Context:**
    *   The introduction could benefit from a brief mention of other notions of tensor rank (e.g., canonical/CP rank) to provide a more complete context. The term "mode-k rank" is also commonly referred to as "multilinear rank," which could be noted.
    *   A discussion of related tensor decomposition methods, like Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD), and their associated approximation bounds (e.g., from Kolda's 2009 review) would help situate the contribution more broadly.
*   **Incoherence Assumption:** The discussion following equation (10) compares the paper's incoherence assumption to conditions used in [1, 3, 11]. This comparison may be misleading. The conditions in those works concern the alignment of singular vectors with basis vectors for sparse+low-rank recovery, whereas the condition here is a bound on the spectral norm (largest singular value) of off-mode unfoldings. Clarifying this distinction, or removing the direct comparison, would avoid potential confusion.
*   **Computational Complexity:** A brief discussion of the computational complexity of the optimization algorithms used for both the latent and overlapped approaches would be a valuable addition.
*   **Notations and Typos:**
    *   The use of superscripts Ŵ(k) to denote the tensor components is potentially confusing as it is also used for mode-k unfolding. Using a different notation for the components could improve readability.
    *   The notation for mode-k rank appears inconsistent (e.g., rk, r̄k). It would be clearer to use a single, consistent notation throughout.
    *   Abstract: "structures Schatten" should be "structured Schatten".
    *   Supplementary material: There is a missing parenthesis in the last line of page 12.
