- Paper analyzes convex latent Schatten-norm tensor decomposition, providing theoretical justification for its empirical superiority over the overlapped approach.
- Theoretical justification: Rigorous analysis showing latent approach matches performance of knowing the mode with smallest rank.
- Duality result: Novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms with broader relevance to structured sparsity.
- Clear presentation: Well-written and explains mathematical concepts clearly.
- Empirical validation: Numerical experiments confirm predicted MSE scaling behavior.
- Good literature context: Appropriately references and situates work within tensor decomposition and structured sparsity literature.
- Weak ML motivation: Needs stronger machine-learning motivation and a generative model example to illustrate when tensor decomposition arises.
- Theorem 2 clarification: Should state recovered components are guaranteed only up to permutation.
- Derivation gap: The step from equation (12) to (13) in Theorem 2 requires more explicit justification.
- Missing proof: Proof of Lemma 5 is absent and must be provided.
- Lemma 4: Provide a brief proof or clearer explanation rather than only citing another paper.
- Related work gap: Expand discussion to include HOSVD and approximation bounds; clarify different tensor-rank notions (mode-rank vs. multilinear rank).
- Incoherence comparison: Current comparison to incoherence conditions in other papers is questionable and should be removed or revised.
- Notational issues: Confusing superscript for components, ambiguous rank notation (\bar{r}k vs \underline{r}k), and a missing parenthesis in the supplement need fixing.
- Arguments for acceptance: Fills a significant theoretical gap, presents novel results backed by experiments, offers broadly applicable duality, and is well-written.
- Arguments against acceptance: Insufficient ML motivation, essential technical clarifications missing, and notational inconsistencies reduce clarity.
- Overall: Strong paper with significant theoretical contribution; recommend acceptance contingent on addressing the technical, motivational, and notational issues.
