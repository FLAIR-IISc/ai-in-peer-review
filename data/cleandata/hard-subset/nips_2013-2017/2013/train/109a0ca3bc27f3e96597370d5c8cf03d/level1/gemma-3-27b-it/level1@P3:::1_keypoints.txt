- The paper theoretically and empirically analyzes a "latent" convex tensor decomposition and contrasts it with a prior "overlapped" approach.  
- Main contribution: shows latent approach outperforms overlapped when the true tensor is low-rank in only a single mode, explains prior empirical observations.  
- Establishes a duality between structured Schatten norms and provides mean squared error bounds for the latent approach.  
- Well-motivated: addresses rank-specification and curse of dimensionality issues in tensor decomposition.  
- Builds on recent convex formulations and connects the latent approach to structured sparsity (group lasso).  
- Empirical results support theoretical predictions and demonstrate practical benefits.  
- Theoretical rigor: provides consistency results and performance guarantees via duality-based analysis.  
- Novelty: duality result for structured Schatten norms and theoretical explanation for latent approach’s superior performance in certain scenarios.  
- Clarity: paper is well-written, clearly organized, and uses standard notation.  
- Significance: addresses a fundamental problem with potential applications (neuroimaging, collaborative filtering) and offers practical guidance.  
- Assumptions: analysis relies on several assumptions (noise sparsity, decomposability into low-rank tensors) and a strong incoherence assumption (10).  
- Computational complexity: paper lacks discussion of computational cost and scalability of the latent approach compared to overlapped methods.  
- Practical guidance: translating theoretical optimal regularization (λ) choices into real-world practice is unclear.  
- Empirical scope: experiments are limited in tensor sizes and noise levels; broader evaluation would increase robustness claims.  
- Tucker connection: mentions Tucker decomposition but does not strongly connect how latent approach helps Tucker’s rank-selection problem.  
- Arguments for acceptance: addresses a significant problem, offers novel duality results, provides solid theoretical analysis, and is well-written.  
- Arguments against acceptance: restrictive assumptions, missing computational complexity discussion, limited empirical evaluation, and weak connection to Tucker methods.  
- Overall assessment: strong paper with rigorous theory and convincing experiments; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address assumption robustness, computational complexity, and Tucker-related discussion.
