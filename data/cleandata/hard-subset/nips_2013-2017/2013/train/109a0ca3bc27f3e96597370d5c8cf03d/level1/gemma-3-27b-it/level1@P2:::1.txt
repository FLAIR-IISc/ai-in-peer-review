This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the “latent” approach to convex tensor decomposition, introduced by Tomioka et al. (2013), and contrasts it with the “overlapped” approach. The core contribution lies in establishing a theoretical justification for the empirical observation that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The authors achieve this by showing that the error bound for the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank, while the overlapped approach’s bound depends on the *average* rank. A novel duality result for structured Schatten norms is also presented, generalizing previous work on structured sparsity.

The paper is well-motivated, introducing tensor decomposition and the challenges of rank selection. It clearly explains the difference between the overlapped and latent approaches and frames the problem as a convex optimization one. The theoretical results, particularly Theorem 2 and Theorem 3, are the central contributions and appear to be rigorous and well-justified, building on existing results in structured sparsity (Agarwal et al., 2011; Jenatton et al., 2010). The connection to the duality between overlapped and latent group lasso is insightful. The numerical experiments, while not extensive, provide supporting evidence for the theoretical findings and demonstrate the scaling predicted by the theory. The authors carefully acknowledge limitations and discuss future work.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the latent approach, which was previously lacking. The bounds presented are clear and well-motivated.
*   **Novelty:** The duality result for structured Schatten norms is a valuable contribution in itself, generalizing previous work. The connection between the tensor decomposition approaches and structured sparsity is effectively made.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the concepts are explained clearly, even for readers not deeply familiar with tensor decomposition.
*   **Significance:** The results have the potential to guide the selection of tensor decomposition methods in practical applications, particularly when dealing with tensors that exhibit low-rank structure in only a few modes.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical results, while limited, align with the theoretical predictions.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The numerical experiments are somewhat limited in scope. Expanding the experiments to include different tensor sizes, noise levels, and rank configurations would strengthen the empirical validation. A comparison against non-convex methods would also be insightful.
*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions (e.g., incoherence assumption in Theorem 2), and the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions is not fully explored. The condition on λ in Theorem 3 is also a significant practical challenge.
*   **Connection to Prior Work:** While the paper references related work, a more detailed discussion of existing tensor decomposition algorithms (beyond the overlapped/latent approaches) and their theoretical properties would be beneficial. A comparison of the computational complexity of the two approaches is missing.
*   **Presentation:** The equations and proofs, while correct, can be dense. Some further simplification or explanation might improve readability.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and rigorous theoretical analysis of a practical problem in tensor decomposition.
*   The duality result for structured Schatten norms is a significant theoretical contribution.
*   The paper’s findings have the potential to impact the design and application of tensor decomposition algorithms.
*   The numerical experiments provide supporting evidence for the theoretical results.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The numerical experiments are limited in scope.
*   The theoretical analysis relies on potentially restrictive assumptions.
*   A more comprehensive comparison with existing tensor decomposition methods is lacking.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and the results are insightful. While the numerical experiments could be more extensive and the assumptions warrant further discussion, the strengths of the paper outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the comments regarding the scope of experiments, assumptions, and comparison to existing methods. The paper is of good quality, original, and has the potential to be significant.
