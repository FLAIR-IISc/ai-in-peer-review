Summary and relation to prior work
This paper introduces and analyzes a class of structured Schatten norms for tensors, unifying two convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation: (i) the overlapped approach, which penalizes the sum of nuclear norms of all mode unfoldings, and (ii) the latent approach, which decomposes the tensor into a sum of components each low-rank in one mode and penalizes their mode-specific nuclear norms. The key technical contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm equals a latent Sp*/q* norm (generalizing dualities known in structured sparsity, e.g., overlapped vs latent group lasso). Building on this, the paper provides error bounds for denoising under the latent S1/1 norm. It shows consistency, a deterministic bound under an incoherence-type constraint across components, and high-probability bounds under Gaussian noise. Crucially, the MSE scales with the minimal mode rank of the underlying tensor, rather than an average across modes as in prior results for the overlapped norm (Tomioka et al., NIPS 2011). Experiments on synthetic data confirm the predicted scaling and show regimes where the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach.

The work connects to a line of convex tensor methods (e.g., Gandy-Recht-Yamada; Liu et al.; Signoretto et al.) that use overlapped nuclear norms, and to the high-dimensional M-estimator framework with decomposable regularizers (Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.). The latent vs overlapped dichotomy mirrors overlapped vs latent group lasso (Jalali et al.; Obozinski et al.), and the paper explicitly leverages that analogy. It also discusses consistency with lower bounds and relaxations for tensor recovery (Mu et al., “Square Deal”).

Quality
The theoretical development is careful and, insofar as can be assessed from the main text, technically sound. The duality lemma is elegant and useful beyond this application. The denoising analysis follows modern high-dimensional techniques (dual norms, decomposability-type reasoning), culminating in bounds that separate two regimes: a general consistency bound in terms of tensor size and a sharper bound leveraging low-rankness and incoherence. The comparison to the prior overlapped bound is direct and illuminates when the latent approach is preferable.

There are, however, some assumptions and modeling choices that deserve clarification:

- The deterministic and Gaussian bounds require adding spectral-norm cross-mode constraints ∥W(l)(k)∥S∞ ≤ α for l ≠ k in the optimization. This alters the estimator relative to the “vanilla” latent formulation and introduces a tuning parameter α. It is unclear how α should be chosen in practice, whether the constraints are enforced in experiments, and how sensitive the results are to α. If the experiments did not impose these constraints, a discussion bridging the unconstrained and constrained estimators would be helpful.

- Constants c and the factor F are left unspecified; although typical in this literature, comments on their magnitude and whether they are benign in realistic dimensions would strengthen the practical message.

- The theory focuses on denoising (fully observed tensors). Since many motivating applications involve missing data, a theoretical and empirical comparison for tensor completion would increase significance.

- The latent decomposition W* = ∑k W*(k) is not unique. The paper leverages this by allowing singleton decompositions in the bounds (leading to min rank behavior). It would be useful to discuss identifiability or stability of the recovered components, or to explicitly state that only the sum W is the target.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well-organized. Definitions of unfoldings, ranks, and norms are precise. The contrast between overlapped and latent norms and their duality is explained cleanly. The difference between the multilinear rank of W* and the ranks of the latent components r̄k could be emphasized earlier to avoid confusion. A short proof sketch for Lemma 2 in the main text, and an intuitive explanation of the key steps in the denoising bounds (e.g., how the min rank arises via singleton decompositions), would improve accessibility. The experimental section is concise and well-aligned with the theory, though reporting whether incoherence constraints were used is important for consistency.

Originality
The duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms is novel and conceptually unifying. The min-mode-rank denoising bounds for the latent approach, together with a transparent comparison to prior overlapped bounds, is new and insightful. While the analysis tools draw on established techniques for decomposable regularizers, their adaptation to tensor Schatten norms and the latent setting is nontrivial.

Significance
The results are significant for convex tensor learning. Many practical tensors are low-rank predominantly in one mode; showing that a convex estimator adapts to the lowest mode rank without knowing the mode is a strong message. The duality result will likely inform future designs (e.g., mixed p, q structured norms) and derivations of complexity measures. The empirical confirmation of scaling helps practitioners choose between overlapped and latent formulations.

Suggestions for improvement
- Clarify whether the spectral-norm constraints (α) are enforced in experiments; discuss how to set α and whether similar bounds can be derived without explicit constraints (e.g., as a property of the estimator under incoherence of W*).

- Extend experiments to tensor completion, and to real datasets (e.g., neuroimaging, video, recommendation), and include comparisons with non-convex baselines (Tucker/CP ALS) and alternative convex relaxations.

- Discuss computational complexity and convergence of the ADMM solver for the latent model, scaling with dimensions and K, and provide runtime comparisons.

- Explore or comment on other Sp/q combinations beyond S1/1, including potential benefits of S1/∞ or mixed norms per mode.

- Provide guidance on practical selection of λ (beyond the theoretical form), and sensitivity analyses.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advance: duality plus adaptation to minimal mode rank.
- Solid, modern analysis with interpretable bounds and practical implications.
- Empirical results match theory and demonstrate gains over the widely used overlapped norm.
- Good fit for NIPS: convex optimization, high-dimensional statistics, and tensor methods.

Arguments against acceptance
- Theoretical bounds rely on additional constraints not clearly connected to the practical estimator used in experiments.
- Experiments are limited to synthetic denoising on modest sizes; no real data or completion tasks.
- Constants/factors in bounds are not quantified, leaving practical tightness uncertain.

Overall, this is a strong submission with a compelling theoretical contribution and clear implications for practice. With clarifications on the constrained estimator and broader empirical validation, it would be a valuable addition to the NIPS program.
