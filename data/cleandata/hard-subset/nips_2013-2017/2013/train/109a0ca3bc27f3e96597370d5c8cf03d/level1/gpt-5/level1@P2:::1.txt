Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper develops and analyzes structured Schatten norms for tensors, focusing on two convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation: (i) the “overlapped” approach, which penalizes the sum of Schatten-1 norms of all mode unfoldings, and (ii) the “latent” approach, which represents the tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a single mode, regularized by the sum of per-mode nuclear norms. The authors introduce a general Sp/q family that encompasses both and prove a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm. Building on techniques from high-dimensional M-estimation with decomposable regularizers (e.g., Negahban et al., Agarwal et al.) and the structured sparsity literature (e.g., overlapped/latent group lasso by Obozinski, Jacob & Vert; Maurer & Pontil), they analyze the latent approach.

Main theoretical results include: (i) a consistency result with a bound depending on the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise; (ii) a deterministic error bound for each latent component under an incoherence-type assumption controlling cross-mode correlations via spectral-norm constraints; and (iii) Gaussian noise bounds showing mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average as in previous overlapped analyses (cf. Tomioka et al. NIPS’11/’12). The paper also proves a useful inequality (Lemma 2) showing the latent S1/1 norm is controlled by the minimal sqrt(mode rank), explaining why the latent approach excels when only one mode is low-rank. Empirically, synthetic denoising experiments confirm linear scaling with the predicted complexity and demonstrate superior performance of the latent approach in many regimes. The work is well-connected to prior tensor completion/denoising studies (Recht et al., Liu et al., Gandy et al., Signoretto et al.) and recent lower bounds (Mu et al.).

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The analysis is careful and leverages appropriate tools (duality, decomposability, Gaussian width-type arguments via spectral norms). The duality between overlapped and latent Sp/q norms appears correct and useful. The deterministic and stochastic bounds are plausible and align with experiments.
- Assumptions and scope: The stronger bounds (Theorem 2/3) hinge on additional spectral norm constraints (incoherence) on cross-mode unfoldings of the latent components, and the regularization parameter must dominate the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise plus an α(K−1) term. These are reasonable but raise practical questions: α is unknown and the optimization in (11) includes constraints not standard in off-the-shelf latent formulations. It is not fully clear whether these constraints were enforced in the experiments; if not, there is a theory–practice gap.
- Experimental support: Simulations are clean and directly test the predicted scaling with suitable complexity measures; code is available. However, the empirical setup is limited to Gaussian denoising with no missing data and moderate tensor sizes; comparisons to nonconvex baselines (HOSVD/Tucker with model selection, CP-ALS) are absent. The choice/selection of λ is only coarsely described (20 candidates; “three representative values”), and fairness across methods could be clearer.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally well-organized, with clear definitions of unfoldings, norms, and problem statements. The connection to structured sparsity is well-motivated. Proof sketches are largely deferred to the supplement; for readability, brief intuition around key steps (e.g., why Lemma 2 holds and how decomposability is invoked in the latent setting) would help.
- Some notational overload exists (ranks rk of W versus r̄k of components), but the authors flag this; a small table summarizing symbols would reduce confusion.
- Practical guidance on setting λ and α is limited. Since λ must bound the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise, offering a high-probability plug-in bound (in terms of σ and dimensions) more prominently in the main text would aid practitioners.

Originality:
- The duality result for structured tensor Schatten norms is novel and mirrors but is not trivial from group-sparsity dualities. The performance analysis for the latent approach, showing dependence on the minimal mode rank and “adaptivity” to the best mode without knowing it, is an original and valuable theoretical contribution beyond prior overlapped analyses.
- The latent approach itself existed, but prior work lacked these denoising guarantees and precise scaling; this paper fills that gap.

Significance:
- The results provide a clear principled rationale for preferring the latent formulation in noisy tensor estimation, a scenario common in practice. The duality insight may catalyze further extensions in tensor-structured regularization and analysis (e.g., alternate p, q choices). The findings should influence methodological choices in applications such as neuroimaging and recommendation with context.
- While the empirical validation is limited in scope, the theoretical message is strong and broadly relevant.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled framework unifying overlapped and latent tensor norms with a clean duality.
- Sharp bounds showing adaptivity to the minimum mode rank; λ independent of ranks.
- Experiments that match the predicted scaling.
- Good positioning in the literature and connections to structured sparsity.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Practicality of the constrained formulation (11): clarify whether the α constraints were enforced in experiments; if not, discuss robustness of the bounds without them and how to choose α in practice.
- Expand empirical evaluation: include missing-data tensor completion (even small-scale), sensitivity to α and λ, runtime/scalability comparisons, and nonconvex baselines (HOSVD with BIC/AIC/tuned ranks, CP-ALS).
- Provide guidance on estimating/upper-bounding the overlapped S∞/∞ noise norm and a concrete default for λ (as in Theorem 3) in the main text.
- Discuss constants (c, c′, factor F) and their magnitude; even a rough numerical illustration would help.
- Minor clarity: distinguish more prominently between multilinear ranks of W and component ranks; add a brief intuitive example for K=3 illustrating the latent decomposition advantage.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and useful duality plus new performance guarantees for the latent tensor approach.
- Clear theoretical explanation for empirically observed behavior; results likely to be influential.
- Solid, appropriately scoped experiments that corroborate theory.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Theory–practice gap regarding additional constraints and parameter selection; unclear experimental enforcement.
- Limited empirical scope (no completion, small sizes, limited baselines).
- Some results rely on incoherence-style assumptions and hide constants, making practical impact less immediate.

Overall evaluation:
A strong theoretical paper that advances understanding of convex tensor decomposition, with a compelling duality and performance guarantees favoring the latent approach. Despite empirical limitations and some practical gaps, the contribution meets NIPS standards in quality, originality, and significance. I lean toward acceptance, conditional on clarifying the role of constraints, parameter selection, and bolstering experiments if space permits.
