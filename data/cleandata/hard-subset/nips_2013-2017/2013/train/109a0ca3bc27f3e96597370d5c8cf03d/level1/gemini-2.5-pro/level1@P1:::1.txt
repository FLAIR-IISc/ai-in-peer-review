This paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach to convex tensor decomposition, first proposed in Tomioka et al. [25]. It aims to explain why and when this latent approach is preferable to the more standard "overlapped" approach studied in [9, 15, 23, 26].

**Summary of Main Ideas**

The paper starts by framing two recent convex approaches for tensor decomposition within a unified framework of "structured Schatten norms".
1.  The **overlapped approach** regularizes the tensor `W` using the sum of Schatten-1 norms of its matrix unfoldings, `sum_k ||W(k)||_S1`. This encourages the tensor to be low-rank in all modes simultaneously.
2.  The **latent approach** models the tensor `W` as a sum of latent components, `W = sum_k W(k)`, where each component `W(k)` is regularized to be low-rank only in its corresponding k-th mode. The paper shows this is equivalent to regularizing `W` with a "latent Schatten norm", `inf_{W=sum W(k)} sum_k ||W(k)(k)||_S1`.

The authors' main contributions are threefold. First, they introduce a general class of overlapped and latent Sp/q norms for tensors and establish a novel duality result between them (Lemma 1). This elegantly connects the tensor decomposition problem to the broader literature on structured sparsity and overlapped group Lasso [17, 21].

Second, and most importantly, the paper presents a performance analysis for the latent approach in a noisy tensor recovery setting. The key theoretical result (Theorems 2 and 3) is an upper bound on the mean squared error that scales with `min_k r_k`, where `r_k` is the mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with the existing bound for the overlapped approach [26], which scales with `(sum_k sqrt(r_k))^2`. This theoretical result provides a clear and compelling explanation for the empirical observation that the latent approach excels when the true tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes (i.e., when `min_k r_k` is much smaller than the other ranks). The latent approach effectively adapts to the unknown best mode without this information being provided.

Third, the authors conduct numerical simulations on synthetic data to validate their theoretical predictions. The experiments confirm that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales linearly with the proposed complexity measure, `min_k r_k / n_K`, and demonstrates its superior performance over the overlapped approach in cases predicted by the theory.

**Strengths**

*   **Significance:** The paper addresses a very relevant and timely question in the field of tensor methods. It provides the first theoretical justification for the superior performance of the latent approach, a crucial piece of understanding that was missing from prior work [25]. The result that the latent approach adapts to the "easiest" mode is a powerful insight.
*   **Originality & Quality:** The theoretical contributions are novel and technically sound. The formulation of structured Schatten norms and the duality result are elegant and of independent interest, forging a nice connection to structured sparsity. The main error bounds are derived rigorously and lead to a very clear, interpretable comparison that has direct practical implications.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly laid out, the distinction between the two approaches is made precise, and the theoretical results are presented in a digestible manner. The authors do an excellent job of connecting their theoretical bounds back to the experimental results, making for a very coherent and convincing narrative.
*   **Completeness:** The work is a complete scientific contribution, encompassing a theoretical framework, rigorous analysis, and empirical validation that directly supports the theory.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, there are a few points that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Disconnect between Theory and Practice on Constraints:** The main theoretical result (Theorem 2) is derived for a constrained optimization problem (11) that includes the constraint `||W(l)(k)||_S_inf <= alpha`. However, the experiments appear to use the unconstrained formulation from [25], which corresponds to problem (5). It would be important to address this apparent disconnect. Is the constrained formulation essential for the proof, or is it a technical convenience? If the unconstrained version was used in experiments, please comment on why the theoretical guarantees are still expected to hold.
2.  **Clarity on the Incoherence Assumption:** The role of the incoherence assumption (10) and the parameter `alpha` could be explained more intuitively. A key step in the analysis is to show that the error bound can be stated in terms of `min_k r_k` by considering a "singleton decomposition" (where `W*(k) = W*` for one `k` and zero otherwise). It seems that for such a decomposition, the incoherence assumption is trivially satisfied with `alpha=0`. If this is the case, it is a very powerful aspect of the result and should be stated explicitly, as it means the assumption is not restrictive for obtaining the final bound on `||W_hat - W*||`. It would also be helpful to discuss how one might choose `alpha` in practice if solving the constrained problem (11) directly.
3.  **Calculation of "LR complexity":** The procedure for calculating the Latent Rank (LR) complexity for the experiments is slightly ad-hoc ("we ran the latent approach to the true tensor W* without noise, and took the minimum..."). While this is a reasonable proxy to validate the scaling, a brief discussion on the difficulty of finding the optimal decomposition of W* that minimizes the complexity term `sum_k r_bar_k` would be welcome.

**Arguments Pro/Con Acceptance**

*   **Pro:**
    *   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.
    *   The main result, showing the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, is significant, insightful, and has clear practical implications.
    *   The theoretical framework connecting tensor norms to structured sparsity is elegant and of broader interest.
    *   The paper is technically sound, well-written, and supported by convincing experiments.

*   **Con:**
    *   There is a minor disconnect between the constrained optimization problem analyzed in the theory and the unconstrained one seemingly used in experiments.
    *   The role and necessity of the incoherence assumption could be clarified.

Overall, the strengths of this paper far outweigh its minor weaknesses. It makes a significant and original contribution to our understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The theoretical insights are strong, and the paper is presented with great clarity. I strongly recommend acceptance.
