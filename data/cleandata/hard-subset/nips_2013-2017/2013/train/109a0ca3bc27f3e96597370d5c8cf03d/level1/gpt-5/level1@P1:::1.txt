Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces and analyzes a class of structured Schatten norms for tensors that unify two families: overlapped norms (penalize all mode unfoldings simultaneously) and latent norms (decompose a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a single mode). The authors prove a duality between these families: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is a latent Sp*/q* norm. Specializing to p=q=1, the latent S1/1 norm’s dual is the overlapped S∞/∞ norm (max spectral norm over mode unfoldings). Building on this, the paper provides statistical guarantees for tensor denoising with the latent approach: (i) a basic consistency bound that depends on the smallest mode dimension, (ii) deterministic error bounds under an incoherence-like cross-mode constraint, showing the estimation error scales with the sum of component ranks and, importantly, the overall Frobenius error scales with the minimal mode-k rank of the true tensor, and (iii) a Gaussian noise bound with an explicit, rank-agnostic choice of λ. These results sharply contrast with prior analysis of the overlapped approach [Tomioka et al., NIPS 2011/2012], where error scales with the average of mode ranks. Experiments on synthetic tensors confirm that mean squared error for the latent approach scales linearly with the proposed latent complexity, often outperforming the overlapped approach even when the true tensor is simultaneously low-rank.

The work is positioned at the intersection of convex tensor methods [Gandy et al.; Liu et al.; Signoretto et al.], structured sparsity with overlaps and latent groups [Obozinski et al.; Jenatton et al.; Bach et al.], and decomposable regularizers [Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.]. The duality result extends known overlapped/latent group norm dualities to tensor Schatten norms, and the statistical analysis for latent tensor norms appears new. The comparison to the “Square Deal” lower bounds [Mu et al.] is discussed qualitatively.

Quality
- Theory: The duality lemma is natural and useful; the proofs (in appendix) appear to follow convex duality and infimal convolution arguments standard in structured sparsity. The deterministic and probabilistic bounds adapt techniques from decomposable regularizers, using the dual norm to control error. The key inequality for the latent norm (bounded by min sqrt rank times Frobenius) is central and tight enough to explain the empirical advantage when ranks are unbalanced. The Gaussian bound provides an explicit λ that depends only on dimensions and noise, not on ranks. One caveat is the additional constraints in (11) requiring an α-bound on off-mode spectral norms; this is a form of incoherence but may be strong and its practical enforcement and selection are not fully justified.
- Experiments: Synthetic denoising experiments carefully probe the predicted scaling and compare against the overlapped method. They support the theory and illustrate λ-selection independence from rank. However, the empirical section is limited to denoising, K=3, and synthetic data.

Clarity
The paper is generally clear and well organized: preliminaries, norms and duality, bounds, experiments. Notation is standard, though the distinction between r_k (mode ranks of W*) and r̄_k (ranks of latent factors) could be emphasized earlier and more consistently to avoid confusion. The role and choice of α in constraints (10) and (11) needs more discussion. A brief sketch of proofs (especially duality and key inequalities) in the main text would help readability. Figures are informative; axis labels and units are clear.

Originality
- New duality between overlapped and latent tensor Schatten norms Sp/q ↔ Sp*/q* appears novel and conceptually unifying.
- First statistical denoising guarantees for the latent tensor approach with explicit rank-min dependence; contrasts with prior overlapped analysis.
- Connection to structured sparsity is insightful and may spur further work on other structured tensor norms.

Significance
The results advance understanding of convex tensor methods and will likely influence both theory and practice by explaining when latent formulations are preferable. The explicit λ guidance is practical. While the paper focuses on denoising, the techniques could inform tensor completion and other settings. The broader framework for structured tensor norms and the duality may be reused in future work.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Practicality of constraints: The optimization (11) includes spectral-norm constraints parameterized by α, which may be unknown and hard to tune. Empirically, do the authors enforce these constraints? Provide guidance or show robustness to misspecification.
- Scope: No results for tensor completion or for non-Gaussian noise; a small experiment on missing data would strengthen the case.
- Empirics: Only synthetic data; comparisons to nonconvex baselines (ALS/Tucker-HOOI) under noise would be informative.
- Constants and factors: The factor F in Theorem 3 is described as “mild,” but its impact is not fully quantified; a short discussion of constants and practical sample sizes would help.
- Notational clarity: Unify r_k vs r̄_k, and ensure all norms are defined at first use. A brief algorithmic note on ADMM convergence and computational cost would help practitioners.
- References: Several entries seem incomplete; please fix formatting.

Questions for the authors
- How should α be chosen in practice? Can constraints be removed without changing rates (e.g., by analytical control via decomposability/incoherence rather than explicit constraints)?
- How sensitive are the bounds and performance to violations of (10)? Any empirical ablation?
- Can the latent approach be extended to completion with analogous guarantees? Any obstacles?
- Is the min-rank dependence minimax optimal in your denoising setting? Can you relate to information-theoretic lower bounds beyond qualitative comparison?

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, general duality result for structured tensor Schatten norms.
- Clear, theoretically justified advantage of latent over overlapped norms with sharp and interpretable dependence on minimal mode rank.
- Explicit, rank-free regularization calibration under Gaussian noise.
- Empirical results that match the predicted scaling.
- Strong connection to and extension of structured sparsity theory.

Arguments against acceptance
- Practical optimization includes additional constraints with unclear selection/tuning and limited discussion.
- Empirical validation limited to synthetic denoising; no real data or completion tasks.
- Lack of comparison to widely used nonconvex tensor methods under noise.
- Some notational and presentation issues; proofs are all in the appendix with minimal sketching.

Overall, this is a solid and insightful theoretical contribution with meaningful implications for convex tensor methods. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the role of α, improving empirical breadth, and tightening presentation.
