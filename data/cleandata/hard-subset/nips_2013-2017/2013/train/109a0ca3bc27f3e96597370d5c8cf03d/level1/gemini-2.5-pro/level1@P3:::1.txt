**Summary of the Paper**

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach for convex tensor decomposition, first proposed empirically by Tomioka et al. (2011). The central idea is to regularize a tensor by decomposing it into a sum of component tensors, `W = W(1) + ... + W(K)`, and penalizing the sum of the Schatten-1 norms of each component's mode-k unfolding, i.e., `sum_k ||W(k)_(k)||_S1`. The authors formalize this within a broader framework of "structured Schatten norms," identifying the regularizer for the latent approach as the "latent S1/1 norm" and for the previously studied "overlapped" approach [26] as the "overlapped S1/1 norm" (`sum_k ||W_(k)||_S1`).

The paper's main contributions are threefold. First, it establishes a novel and elegant duality between the general class of overlapped Sp/q-norms and latent Sp*/q*-norms, connecting this tensor decomposition work to the broader literature on structured sparsity, such as the latent/overlapped group Lasso [21]. Second, it provides the first rigorous performance guarantees for the latent approach in a denoising setting (recovering `W*` from `Y = W* + E`). The key theoretical result (Theorem 3) shows that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor `W*`, i.e., `min_k r_k`. This is in sharp contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error was shown in [26] to scale with the sum (or average) of the mode-k ranks, `(sum_k sqrt(r_k))^2`. This result provides a clear theoretical explanation for why the latent approach is superior when a tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes. Third, the authors conduct numerical experiments that convincingly validate their theoretical predictions, showing that the MSE scales linearly with their proposed "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" metric, which is proportional to `min_k r_k`.

**Strengths**

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature by providing the first theoretical analysis for the latent tensor decomposition model. The main result—that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is both novel and highly significant. It provides practitioners with a clear, theoretically-grounded reason to prefer the latent model, especially for tensors with asymmetric rank structures.

2.  **Elegant General Framework:** The introduction of the generalized overlapped/latent Sp/q-norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is a strong conceptual contribution. It unifies the two main convex tensor decomposition approaches and elegantly links them to well-established concepts in structured sparsity. This general view increases the paper's impact beyond the specific tensor problem.

3.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It clearly distinguishes between the overlapped and latent approaches, motivates the need for a theoretical analysis with an intuitive figure, and builds its argument logically from the duality result to the final performance bounds. The comparison between the new bound (15) and the previous bound for the overlapped approach (16) is direct and effective.

4.  **Strong Experimental Validation:** The numerical results in Section 4 are excellent. Instead of simply demonstrating that the latent approach works well, the experiments are carefully designed to verify the specific scaling laws derived in the theory. The plots of MSE versus the proposed complexity measures (TR and LR) show a convincing linear relationship, supporting the correctness of the theoretical analysis.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

1.  **Incoherence Assumption and Constrained Optimization:** The main analysis in Section 3.2 relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes `||W(l)_(k)||_S_inf <= alpha`. While such assumptions are common in related literature [1], the practical implications are not fully discussed. How should a user choose `alpha`? The experiments use this formulation, but the choice of `alpha` is not specified, which hinders reproducibility. A discussion on the sensitivity to this parameter or a practical heuristic for setting it would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Scope of the Analysis:** The analysis is restricted to the tensor denoising setting where the tensor is fully observed. A major application area for low-rank tensor models is tensor completion (recovering from missing entries). While the authors correctly identify this as future work, the current analysis does not address this important setting. It would be valuable to briefly comment on the potential challenges of extending the analysis to the completion setting.

3.  **Clarity of LR Complexity Calculation:** In the experiments, the Latent Rank (LR) complexity is computed by finding an optimal decomposition of the *true* noiseless tensor `W*`. While this is a valid approach for verifying the theoretical bound, it is not a quantity that can be computed in practice. The text could be slightly clearer in stating that this is an oracle quantity used for validation purposes only, to avoid any confusion for readers looking to apply these complexity measures.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   Provides the first and much-needed theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.
*   The main result, linking the error to the minimum mode-k rank, is a significant, clear, and impactful finding.
*   Introduces an elegant and general duality framework for structured Schatten norms that connects to the broader structured sparsity literature.
*   The theoretical claims are thoroughly validated with well-designed numerical experiments.
*   The paper is well-written, technically sound, and addresses a topic of high interest to the NIPS community.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
*   The analysis relies on an incoherence assumption and a constrained formulation, with limited discussion on the practical choice of the associated hyperparameter `alpha`.
*   The analysis does not cover the important tensor completion setting.

**Conclusion**

This is an excellent paper that makes a strong and clear contribution to the theory of convex tensor decomposition. It provides a rigorous and insightful analysis that explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach over the overlapped approach in many settings. The connection to the structured sparsity literature via the novel duality result is particularly elegant. Despite minor weaknesses regarding the practical implications of the incoherence assumption and the focus on the denoising setting, the strengths of the paper are overwhelming. This work is of high quality, originality, and significance, and will be of great interest to researchers in machine learning, signal processing, and high-dimensional statistics. I strongly recommend acceptance.
