### Summary of the Paper

This paper provides a theoretical analysis of a convex approach for tensor decomposition, termed the "latent" approach, which was empirically observed in prior work [25] to outperform the more standard "overlapped" approach in some settings. The authors frame both approaches within a general class of structured Schatten norms. The "overlapped" approach penalizes a tensor by summing the Schatten norms of its matricizations across all modes, encouraging it to be simultaneously low-rank in all modes. The "latent" approach, in contrast, models the target tensor as a sum of several component tensors, each of which is penalized for being low-rank in a single, specific mode.

The main contributions are threefold. First, the paper formalizes the "overlapped" and "latent" penalties as instances of general structured Sp/q-norms for tensors and establishes a novel duality relationship between them. This result connects the convex tensor decomposition literature to the broader work on structured sparsity, such as the duality between overlapped and latent group Lasso norms. Second, the paper presents a rigorous performance analysis for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. It proves consistency and provides non-asymptotic error bounds under Gaussian noise. The key theoretical finding is that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts with previous analysis [26] showing that the error of the overlapped approach scales with the *average* of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical distinction provides a clear explanation for the superior performance of the latent approach when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode but high-rank in others. Third, the paper presents numerical experiments that empirically validate the derived theoretical scaling laws, showing a linear relationship between the observed MSE and the predicted complexity measure.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality (Technical Soundness):**
The paper is technically sound. The theoretical development, including the duality lemma and the performance bounds for the latent estimator, is rigorous and builds upon established techniques from the high-dimensional statistics literature. The claims are well-supported by the provided theorems and proofs (delegated to the appendix). The experimental validation is a key strength; it is designed specifically to test the theoretical predictions on scaling behavior. The results shown in Figure 2 provide compelling evidence that the derived complexity measures accurately predict the empirical performance of both the latent and overlapped methods across different tensor sizes and ranks.

A potential weakness lies in the assumptions required for the tighter performance bound (Theorem 2). The analysis requires an incoherence assumption on the components of the true tensor (Eq. 10) and incorporates this as an explicit constraint in the optimization problem (Eq. 11). It is not immediately clear how the parameter `alpha` governing this constraint would be chosen in a practical setting where the ground truth is unknown. The analysis is also restricted to the tensor denoising problem with complete observations, leaving the important case of tensor completion with missing entries for future work.

**Clarity (Writing and Organization):**
The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly organized. It begins with a strong motivation, using Figure 1 to illustrate the practical difference between the two approaches. The distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" norms is explained precisely in Section 2, and the connection to structured sparsity is made explicit, which is helpful for context. The main theoretical results are stated clearly as theorems, followed by a direct comparison (Section 3.4) that highlights the core message of the paper. The numerical results section is also clear, explaining how the empirical complexity measures were derived from the theory and how the experiments were conducted. The figures are informative and support the paper's narrative effectively.

**Originality (Novelty):**
The paper's contributions are original and significant. While the latent approach itself was proposed in [25], this paper provides the first theoretical analysis to explain its performance, which is a substantial contribution. The central finding—that the latent approach adapts to the minimum mode-k rank, effectively performing as well as if it knew the lowest-rank mode a priori—is a novel and powerful insight. The formalization of the structured Schatten norm framework and the accompanying duality result (Lemma 1) are also new contributions that are of independent interest and generalize similar concepts from the vector-based structured sparsity literature to the tensor setting. While the proof techniques are adapted from existing work [1], their application to this problem to derive new theoretical understanding is a non-trivial and valuable achievement.

**Significance (Impact):**
The paper's findings are significant for both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data. It provides clear theoretical justification and practical guidance for choosing a convex regularizer for tensor decomposition. The conclusion that the latent approach is generally preferable, or at least as good as the overlapped approach, is a strong, actionable result. The theoretical insight that the latent formulation can automatically discover and exploit the "simplest" structure (i.e., lowest-rank mode) without prior knowledge is an important principle that could influence the design of other structured estimation methods. The duality result may also spur further theoretical work on structured norms for tensors. The primary limitation on its immediate impact is its focus on the denoising setting, as tensor completion is arguably a more widespread application.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for convex tensor decomposition.
*   The key theoretical result—that performance scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is novel, significant, and explains previously observed empirical behavior.
*   Introduces a general framework for structured Schatten norms and a new duality result of independent interest.
*   The paper is very clearly written and well-organized, with strong empirical validation supporting the theoretical claims.
*   Offers clear and impactful guidance for practitioners on the choice between two competing convex relaxations.

### Arguments Against Acceptance:
*   The main performance analysis relies on an incoherence assumption and a corresponding constraint in the optimization problem that may be difficult to apply in practice.
*   The analysis does not cover the tensor completion setting (i.e., with missing data), which is a key application area for these methods.
*   The analytical techniques are primarily adaptations from prior work on structured sparsity, rather than being entirely new methodologies.
