This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically analyzing the "latent" approach proposed in prior work. The authors provide theoretical justification for the empirical observation that the latent approach outperforms the "overlapped" approach when the true tensor is low-rank in only a few modes. A key contribution is establishing a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. The paper also includes numerical validation of the derived theoretical scaling behavior.

The paper is well-motivated. Tensor decomposition is a crucial technique with applications in diverse fields, and the limitations of traditional non-convex methods are acknowledged. The shift towards convex relaxation is a valuable direction, and the existing gap in theoretical understanding of these approaches makes this work relevant. The connection to structured sparsity, and particularly the group lasso, provides a useful perspective and leverages existing theory.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper offers a solid theoretical analysis, proving consistency and providing error bounds for the latent approach. The analysis builds upon existing results from related fields (structured sparsity, matrix completion) and extends them to the tensor setting.
* **Novel Duality Result:** The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a significant contribution. It provides a deeper understanding of the relationship between these norms and opens avenues for further research.
* **Explanation of Empirical Success:** The theoretical analysis successfully explains why the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode, addressing a previously unexplained empirical observation.
* **Numerical Validation:** The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions regarding the scaling of the mean squared error, strengthening the credibility of the results. The comparison with the overlapped approach provides practical insight.
* **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction provides a clear overview of the problem and the contributions. The sections are logically structured, and the notation is consistent.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions, including the independence of noise components and specific incoherence assumptions (Lemma 2, Theorem 3). The practical implications of these assumptions are not fully discussed. For example, how sensitive are the results to violations of the incoherence assumption?
* **Constant Factors:** The derived error bounds include constant factors that are not explicitly calculated or analyzed. While this is common in theoretical analysis, it limits the practical interpretability of the results. The dependence on parameters like 'c' and 'F' could be further clarified.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper focuses primarily on theoretical analysis and numerical validation. It does not address the computational cost of implementing the latent approach, which could be a significant practical concern, particularly for large-scale tensors. A discussion of the computational complexity compared to other methods would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Experiments:** While the numerical experiments validate the theoretical scaling, the scope is somewhat limited. Exploring a wider range of tensor sizes, ranks, and noise levels would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the approach.  Exploring datasets beyond randomly generated ones would be beneficial.
* **Connection to Prior Work on Tensor Completion:** While the authors mention noise, the focus is on decomposition. A more thorough discussion of how this work relates to the established literature on *tensor completion* (dealing with missing entries) would be useful, especially considering the connection to low-rank modeling for imputation.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a theoretically sound and original contribution to the field of tensor decomposition.
* The duality result is a significant advancement in understanding structured Schatten norms.
* The theoretical explanation for the superior performance of the latent approach is valuable.
* The numerical validation supports the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and organized.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical analysis relies on strong assumptions that may not hold in practical applications.
* The constant factors in the error bounds limit the practical interpretability of the results.
* The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the computational cost of the latent approach.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper is a strong contribution to NIPS. The theoretical results are significant, the duality result is novel, and the numerical validation is supportive. While the assumptions and constant factors are limitations, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. The paper addresses an important problem in tensor decomposition and provides valuable insights into the behavior of convex relaxation-based approaches. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. Specifically, the authors should address the sensitivity of the results to the assumptions (e.g., incoherence) and provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the latent approach.
