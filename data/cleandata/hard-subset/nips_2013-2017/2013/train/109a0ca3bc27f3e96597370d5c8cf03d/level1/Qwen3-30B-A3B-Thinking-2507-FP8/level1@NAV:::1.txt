

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches previously proposed in convex optimization-based tensor decomposition (Tomioka et al., 2013; Gandy et al., 2011). The authors establish a theoretical foundation explaining why the latent approach empirically outperforms the overlapped approach when the underlying tensor is low-rank in a specific mode. Their key contribution is a novel duality result between these structured norms, showing that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank (rather than the average as in the overlapped approach), which precisely explains the empirical observations.

The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with rigorous theoretical analysis, including consistency proofs and deterministic error bounds that are well-supported by the duality framework. The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions regarding mean squared error scaling, with careful comparisons between the two approaches. The writing is clear and well-organized, with sufficient technical detail for reproducibility.

The work is highly original in establishing the duality between structured Schatten norms, extending structured sparsity theory (Jenatton et al., 2011; Obozinski et al., 2011) to the tensor setting. While the core ideas build on prior tensor decomposition work, the theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach represents a significant advancement. The significance is substantial, as it provides a principled theoretical basis for choosing decomposition methods in practical tensor applications like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering.

Strengths: Rigorous theoretical contribution with clear duality result, comprehensive error analysis, and well-designed empirical validation. The connection to structured sparsity literature is well-established.

Weaknesses: The theoretical results rely on certain incoherence assumptions that may limit applicability in some real-world scenarios. The empirical validation is limited to synthetic data, and the paper could better discuss practical implementation considerations.

Pro: Strong theoretical contribution that explains empirical observations, well-motivated research question, thorough analysis. Con: Limited empirical validation on real-world data, some technical assumptions may restrict applicability.

Overall, this is an excellent theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The paper meets all NIPS quality standards and provides a valuable theoretical foundation for practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
