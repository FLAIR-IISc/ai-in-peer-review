This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex optimization approach for tensor decomposition, which the authors term the "latent" approach, and compares it to the more established "overlapped" approach. The main idea is to view both methods through the lens of a new, general class of "structured Schatten norms." The overlapped approach penalizes the sum of Schatten norms of all matrix unfoldings of a single tensor. In contrast, the latent approach models the target tensor as a sum of several component tensors, where each component is regularized to be low-rank in a specific mode.

The paper makes several key contributions. First, it formalizes these two approaches within a general framework of overlapped and latent Sp/q-norms for tensors. Second, it proves a novel and elegant duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm is the latent Sp*/q*-norm. This result connects the work to the broader literature on structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs. latent group Lasso). Third, and most centrally, the paper provides the first rigorous performance analysis for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The main theoretical finding is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with previous analyses for the overlapped approach [26], where the error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This theoretical result provides a clear explanation for why the latent approach can significantly outperform the overlapped one, especially when a tensor is low-rank in only one of its modes. Finally, the paper presents numerical simulations that precisely validate the predicted scaling laws for both methods.

**Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are rigorously derived, with proofs provided in the supplementary material. The main theorems establish consistency and provide explicit error bounds for the latent approach under Gaussian noise, which are the primary contributions. The authors are careful about the assumptions needed for their tighter bounds, such as the incoherence assumption (Eq. 10), which is common in related high-dimensional statistics literature. The experimental work is a major strength; it is not merely a demonstration of performance but is carefully designed to verify the specific scaling behaviors predicted by the theory. This direct and successful validation of the theoretical complexity measures (TR vs. LR complexity) lends strong support to the paper's claims. The work is a complete and self-contained contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor methods.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem by contrasting the two approaches from the outset, using Figure 1 as a clear illustrative example. The new concepts of structured Schatten norms and the distinction between overlapped and latent versions are defined clearly. The logical flow from definitions, to the duality lemma, to the main performance bounds, and finally to experimental validation makes the paper easy to follow. The connection to structured sparsity research is also explained very well, helping to place the work in a broader context.

**Originality:** The paper contains significant original contributions. While the latent optimization formulation was previously proposed in [25], this work is the first to formalize it in terms of a "latent Schatten norm" and, more importantly, the first to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of its performance. The key insight that the latent approach adapts to the minimum mode-k rank is a novel and powerful theoretical finding. The introduction of the general class of structured Schatten norms and the proof of the duality between the overlapped and latent versions is a beautiful and original theoretical result in its own right, which could have impact beyond this specific application. The paper clearly delineates its contributions from prior work, particularly the analysis of the overlapped approach in [26].

**Significance:** The results are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data. The paper provides a definitive theoretical reason to prefer the latent approach over the overlapped approach for noisy tensor decomposition, especially when there is no prior knowledge about the tensor's rank structure. The discovery that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew in advance which mode had the lowest rank is a powerful and non-obvious result. This provides clear guidance for model selection. Furthermore, the theoretical framework, especially the duality result, deepens the connection between tensor methods and the well-established field of structured sparsity, which could foster new research directions. Researchers are very likely to build upon these results, for example, by extending the analysis to the tensor completion setting, as the authors suggest.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
1.  **Novel Theoretical Insight:** Provides the first rigorous analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition, revealing its adaptive nature and its dependency on the *minimum* mode-k rank.
2.  **Elegant Unifying Framework:** Introduces a general class of structured Schatten norms and proves a novel duality theorem, connecting tensor methods to the broader structured sparsity literature.
3.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The analysis offers a clear justification for choosing the latent model over the overlapped one, advancing the state-of-the-art in convex tensor methods.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments are not just demonstrative but are carefully designed to confirm the specific scaling laws derived in the theoretical sections.
5.  **High Clarity and Quality:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, technically sound, and easy to follow.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis is focused on the denoising problem (recovering a tensor from a fully observed but noisy version). The tensor completion problem, which is of great practical interest, is left as future work.
2.  **Restrictive Assumptions:** The tighter performance bounds rely on an incoherence assumption and additional constraints in the optimization problem, which may not always hold in practice. A more detailed discussion of the implications of these assumptions would have been beneficial.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that makes significant theoretical and practical contributions to the field of tensor decomposition. The strengths, particularly the novel and insightful theoretical analysis backed by strong empirical evidence, far outweigh the minor limitations in scope. The paper is well-written, original, and provides important results that are likely to be influential. It is a clear accept for NIPS.
