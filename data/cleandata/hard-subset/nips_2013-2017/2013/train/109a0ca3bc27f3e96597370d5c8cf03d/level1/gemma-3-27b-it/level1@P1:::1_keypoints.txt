- Studies convex tensor decomposition with structured Schatten norms, focusing on the "latent" approach.  
- Theoretically explains why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the tensor is low-rank in only a few modes.  
- Proves a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing structured sparsity results.  
- Provides consistency proofs and error bounds for the latent approach, building on structured sparsity and matrix completion theory.  
- Numerical experiments validate the predicted scaling of mean squared error and compare latent vs overlapped methods.  
- Paper is well-motivated, connecting convex relaxation to practical tensor problems and linking to group lasso/structured sparsity.  
- Writing and organization are clear, with consistent notation and a coherent introduction.  
- Key strength: strong theoretical rigor supporting the latent approach.  
- Key strength: the duality result is a significant conceptual contribution.  
- Key strength: theory explains prior empirical observations about latent's superiority.  
- Key strength: numerical validation strengthens credibility of theoretical claims.  
- Weakness: analysis relies on strong assumptions (noise independence, incoherence) whose practical sensitivity isn’t explored.  
- Weakness: constant factors in error bounds (parameters like c and F) are not explicitly quantified, limiting practical interpretability.  
- Weakness: lacks discussion of computational cost and complexity of the latent approach for large tensors.  
- Weakness: numerical experiments are limited in scope (tensor sizes, ranks, noise levels) and use only synthetic data.  
- Weakness: limited discussion of relationship to tensor completion literature and handling of missing data.  
- For acceptance: original, theoretically solid contribution with novel duality and supportive experiments.  
- Against acceptance: strong assumptions, unquantified constants, and absent computational-cost analysis weaken practical impact.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions to address sensitivity to assumptions and to discuss computational costs.
