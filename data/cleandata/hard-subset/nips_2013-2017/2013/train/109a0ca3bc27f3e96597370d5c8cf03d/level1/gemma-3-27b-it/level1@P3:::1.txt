This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a "latent" approach to convex tensor decomposition, contrasting it with a previously proposed "overlapped" approach. The core contribution lies in demonstrating, theoretically and empirically, that the latent approach performs better when the true tensor is low-rank in only a single mode, a phenomenon observed in prior work [25]. The authors establish a duality between the structured Schatten norms used in these approaches and provide bounds on the mean squared error achievable by the latent approach.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in tensor decomposition – the curse of dimensionality when specifying ranks for each mode. Existing methods often rely on non-convex optimization or require careful rank selection, while convex approaches offer theoretical guarantees. The authors build upon recent convex formulations and provide a strong theoretical justification for the observed benefits of the latent approach. The connection to structured sparsity (group lasso) is also a nice and insightful observation, grounding the work in a broader theoretical framework. The empirical validation further strengthens the claims.

**Strengths:**

*   **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a solid theoretical analysis of the latent approach, including consistency results and bounds on the mean squared error. The analysis leverages duality results and provides insights into the performance guarantees.
*   **Novelty:** The duality result for structured Schatten norms is a valuable contribution in its own right, extending existing work on structured sparsity. The theoretical explanation for the superior performance of the latent approach in specific scenarios is also novel.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The notation is standard and the key concepts are explained adequately. The connection to related work is made explicit.
*   **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions and demonstrate the practical benefits of the latent approach.
*   **Significance:** The work addresses a fundamental problem in tensor decomposition with potential applications in various domains, including neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. The results offer practical guidance for choosing appropriate decomposition techniques.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions, including the sparsity of the noise and the ability to decompose the true tensor into a mixture of low-rank tensors. The sensitivity of the results to these assumptions could be further investigated. While standard in related fields, the incoherence assumption (10) seems quite strong.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of implementing the latent approach, which could be a limiting factor for large-scale tensors. A comparison of computational costs with the overlapped approach would be valuable.
*   **Practical Guidance:** While the theory provides insights into the optimal regularization parameter, translating this into practical guidelines for choosing λ in real-world applications could be challenging.
*   **Limited Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are relatively limited in scope, focusing on specific tensor sizes and noise levels. Expanding the experimental evaluation to a wider range of scenarios would increase confidence in the robustness of the results.
*   **Connection to Tucker decomposition:** The paper mentions Tucker decomposition in the introduction but doesn’t draw a strong connection between the latent approach and its potential advantages in addressing the rank selection challenge inherent in Tucker decomposition, which could be a point of emphasis.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses a significant problem in tensor decomposition with a theoretically sound and empirically validated approach.
*   The duality result for structured Schatten norms is a novel and valuable contribution.
*   The theoretical analysis provides insights into the performance guarantees of the latent approach.
*   The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and connects to relevant literature.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The assumptions underlying the theoretical analysis may be restrictive.
*   The computational complexity of the latent approach is not discussed.
*   The empirical evaluation could be more comprehensive.
*   The potential connections to existing methods like Tucker Decomposition could be further elaborated.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the empirical results are convincing, and the writing is clear. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically addressing the robustness of the assumptions and discussing the computational complexity of the approach. Adding a brief discussion of how this approach might benefit the rank selection issue of Tucker decomposition would further strengthen the paper.
