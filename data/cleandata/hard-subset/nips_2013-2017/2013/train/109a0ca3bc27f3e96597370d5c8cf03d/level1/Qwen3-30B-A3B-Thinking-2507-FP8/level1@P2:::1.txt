

# Review: Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization

This paper introduces a framework for structured Schatten norms in tensor decomposition, specifically analyzing the relationship between two approaches: the "overlapped" and "latent" Schatten norm regularization. The authors establish a novel duality between these two types of norms, which generalizes results from structured sparsity literature (e.g., [2, 13, 17, 21]). Previous work by Tomioka et al. [25, 26] proposed the latent approach empirically but lacked theoretical justification for its superior performance in certain cases (e.g., when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode). The paper builds on this by rigorously analyzing the latent approach, showing that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, while the overlapped approach scales with the average (square root) of the mode-k ranks. This explains the empirical observations in Figure 1 of the paper and provides theoretical justification for preferring the latent approach in noisy tensor decomposition.

**Quality**: The paper demonstrates strong technical quality. The authors provide rigorous theoretical analysis, including novel duality results (Lemma 1) and performance bounds (Theorems 1-3) for the latent approach. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs (presented in the supplementary material) and confirmed by numerical simulations (Section 4). The paper carefully addresses the limitations of both approaches, noting that the latent approach performs at least as well as the overlapped approach in simultaneously low-rank cases (Figure 2). The analysis is thorough, with appropriate incoherence assumptions (equation 10) that align with recent literature [1, 3, 11]. The experimental design is sound, with controlled tensor generation and noise parameters.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly frames the problem and contributions, while Section 2 provides precise definitions of the structured Schatten norms. The authors make an effort to connect their work to structured sparsity literature, which aids understanding. The main theoretical results (Section 3) are presented logically, with clear statements of theorems and their implications. The numerical results (Section 4) are well-organized, with Figure 2 effectively demonstrating the theoretical predictions. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity, but this is a minor point given the focus on theoretical performance.

**Originality**: The paper makes several significant contributions to the field. First, the duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) generalizes existing work on group sparsity norms and provides a theoretical foundation for the two approaches. Second, the theoretical analysis of the latent approach's performance (Theorems 2-3) is novel and addresses a gap in the literature. Third, the paper shows that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the mode with the smallest rank, which is a significant insight not previously established. While related work exists on tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], this paper provides the first comprehensive theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach.

**Significance**: The results are highly significant for tensor decomposition research. The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the latent approach, which practitioners can now confidently use for noisy tensor decomposition. The insight that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the optimal mode (without needing to specify it) is practically valuable. The numerical experiments (Figure 2) confirm the theoretical predictions with high precision, demonstrating that the paper's claims are not merely theoretical but have real-world implications. The paper also connects to broader structured sparsity literature, making it relevant beyond tensor decomposition. This work advances the state of the art in convex tensor decomposition by providing a more complete theoretical understanding of when and why different approaches work well.

**Strengths**:
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with novel duality result
- Clear explanation of why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach
- Precise numerical validation of theoretical predictions
- Strong connections to structured sparsity literature
- Well-written and well-organized presentation

**Weaknesses**:
- Limited discussion of computational complexity (though this is not the focus of the paper)
- The paper doesn't explore extensions to tensor completion (mentioned in the conclusion as future work)
- The experimental section could have included more comparisons with alternative methods

**Arguments for acceptance**:
- Theoretical contribution is substantial and well-supported
- Provides clear explanation for empirical observations in prior work
- Connects tensor decomposition to broader structured sparsity literature
- Numerical results precisely confirm theoretical predictions
- Advances the state of the art in convex tensor decomposition

**Arguments against acceptance**:
- The paper doesn't address tensor completion (only denoising), which is a more common application
- Some readers might find the technical depth challenging without familiarity with tensor norms
- The paper could have included more discussion of practical implementation details

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition by providing a rigorous theoretical foundation for the latent approach. It addresses a gap in the literature, provides clear explanations for empirical observations, and offers practical insights for practitioners. The quality of the theoretical analysis is high, the paper is well-written, and the significance of the results is clear. While the paper doesn't address tensor completion (a limitation noted in the conclusion), this doesn't diminish the importance of the core theoretical contributions. The work represents a strong contribution to NIPS and advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. I recommend acceptance.
