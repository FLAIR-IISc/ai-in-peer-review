

# Peer Review: Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization

This paper addresses a key problem in tensor decomposition: comparing two convex optimization approaches (overlapped and latent) for tensor recovery from noisy observations. The authors provide a theoretical foundation for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the underlying tensor is low-rank in a specific mode. The paper makes three main contributions: (1) a framework for structured Schatten norms that includes both approaches; (2) a novel duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms; and (3) theoretical analysis showing that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, while the overlapped approach scales with the average mode-k rank.

The work builds upon recent NIPS papers by Tomioka et al. [25, 26] who introduced convex approaches to tensor decomposition, and connects to structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21] by establishing a duality between the two norm types. This connection is particularly valuable as it demonstrates how tensor decomposition fits within broader structured sparsity frameworks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first rigorous theoretical explanation for the empirical observation that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain settings. Theorem 3 and subsequent analysis demonstrate that the latent approach's error scales with min_k √r_k (where r_k is the mode-k rank), while the overlapped approach scales with (1/K)∑√r_k. This is a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition.

2. **Novel Duality Result:** Lemma 1 establishes a clean duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. This is not merely a technical contribution but provides deeper insight into the relationship between these two approaches.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments (Section 4) convincingly confirm the theoretical predictions, showing that MSE scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures. The authors carefully design experiments to isolate the theoretical predictions from practical implementation factors.

4. **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is well-organized and clearly explains the relationship between the two approaches. The connection to structured sparsity literature is well-motivated, and the mathematical exposition is precise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope:** The paper focuses exclusively on the denoising setting (no missing data), while tensor decomposition is often applied to tensor completion problems. The authors mention tensor completion as future work, but this limits the current paper's applicability to a major use case of tensor decomposition.

2. **Incoherence Assumption:** The tighter bounds (Theorems 2-3) rely on the incoherence condition (10), which may not hold in all practical settings. The paper does not discuss how common this condition is in real-world tensor data or how to verify it.

3. **Computational Complexity:** The paper does not compare the computational complexity of the latent and overlapped approaches, which is crucial for practical adoption. The optimization problem (5) for the latent approach is more complex than the overlapped approach, but this trade-off isn't addressed.

4. **Connection to Recent Work:** The paper cites Mu et al. [19] regarding lower bounds for tensor decomposition, but doesn't explicitly discuss how their results relate to the theoretical bounds presented here. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's significance.

**Recommendation:**

The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The duality result and error analysis are significant advances that should be of interest to the NIPS community. While the scope is limited to the denoising setting, the theoretical insights are general and could inform future work on tensor completion.

The paper meets all four criteria for quality:
- **Quality:** The theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported by experiments. The authors are careful to discuss both strengths and limitations of their approach.
- **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, with clear mathematical exposition.
- **Originality:** The duality result and the theoretical explanation for the empirical performance difference are novel contributions.
- **Significance:** The findings provide a theoretical foundation for practitioners to choose between approaches, and the insights could guide future algorithm development.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides first rigorous theoretical explanation for empirical observations in tensor decomposition
- Establishes a novel duality result connecting tensor decomposition to structured sparsity
- Theoretical predictions are precisely confirmed by experiments
- Strong connection to recent NIPS work and structured sparsity literature

**Arguments for Rejection:**
- Limited to denoising setting (not tensor completion)
- Relies on incoherence assumption for tighter bounds
- Does not address computational complexity trade-offs

The paper is a strong contribution to the field, making a significant theoretical advance that explains an important empirical phenomenon. The minor limitations do not detract from the paper's core contribution, which is well-supported and clearly presented. I recommend acceptance.
