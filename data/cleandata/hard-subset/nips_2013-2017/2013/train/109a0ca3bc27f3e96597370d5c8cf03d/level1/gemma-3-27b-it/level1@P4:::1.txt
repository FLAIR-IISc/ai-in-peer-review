This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically comparing the “overlapped” and “latent” approaches. The core contribution lies in theoretically demonstrating why the “latent” approach, empirically observed to perform better in certain scenarios, indeed achieves better performance – specifically, it scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. A novel duality result for structured Schatten norms is also presented, and numerical simulations validate the theoretical findings.

The paper is well-motivated, placing the work within the context of tensor decomposition challenges like rank selection and generalizing to broader structured sparsity literature. The introduction clearly articulates the problem, highlights the limitations of previous approaches, and positions the latent approach as an improvement. The authors appropriately connect their work to related research in tensor decomposition [5, 10, 14, 18] and convex optimization [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]. The definitions of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms are precise, and the established duality (Lemma 1) is a valuable contribution. 

The strengths of the paper are considerable:
* **Theoretical Justification:** Providing a theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach is a significant contribution. The scaling result (Theorem 3) is a strong and useful finding.
* **Duality Result:** The derived duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and potentially applicable beyond tensor decomposition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. Mathematical notation is standard and consistent.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical results corroborate the theoretical predictions, increasing confidence in the analysis.
* **Connections to Existing Work:** The authors carefully situate their work within the broader literature on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.

However, there are also some weaknesses:
* **Assumptions:** Theorems 2 and 3 rely on several assumptions (incoherence, noise distribution) that may not always hold in practice. While standard, the impact of violating these assumptions should be discussed more thoroughly.
* **Complexity of Theorem 3:** The expression for the bound in Theorem 3 is quite complex, potentially limiting its practical applicability. Simplifying the bound or providing guidelines for interpreting it would be beneficial.
* **Limited Scope of Numerical Results:** The numerical experiments focus primarily on confirming the scaling behavior. Investigating the performance of the latent approach on more complex datasets or with different noise levels would strengthen the empirical validation.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:**  The paper does not discuss the computational cost of implementing the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach. This information would be valuable for practitioners.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant problem in tensor decomposition – understanding and improving the performance of convex relaxation methods.
* The theoretical results are novel, rigorous, and provide a clear explanation for observed empirical behavior.
* The duality result is a valuable contribution to the broader field of structured sparsity.
* The empirical validation supports the theoretical findings.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical results rely on potentially strong assumptions.
* The complexity of the derived bounds might limit their practical utility.
* The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive.
* The lack of discussion regarding computational cost is a minor drawback.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, the paper represents a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis provides valuable insights into the behavior of the latent approach, and the duality result is a noteworthy addition to the literature on structured sparsity. The paper is of high quality and clearly demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant theoretical concepts. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above, specifically providing a clearer discussion of the assumptions and potential limitations of the theoretical results.
