

# Peer Review

This paper studies structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches to convex optimization-based tensor decomposition. The authors establish a theoretical foundation for the empirical observation that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific mode. They introduce a novel duality between the two types of norms and provide theoretical guarantees for the latent approach's performance.

The paper builds upon recent work in convex tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], which has been a growing area at NIPS. Previous work has established the overlapped approach as a convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, but its limitations when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode were only empirically observed [25]. The authors connect these tensor decomposition approaches to structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21], showing that the overlapped/latent Schatten norms correspond to the overlapped/latent group sparsity norms. This connection is valuable for the broader machine learning community working with structured regularization.

**Strengths:**

*Quality:* The theoretical analysis is technically sound and well-supported. The authors provide a clear duality result (Lemma 1) that generalizes previous results in structured sparsity. The main theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) establish tight bounds on the performance of the latent approach, showing it depends on the minimal mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. The numerical experiments (Section 4) confirm the theoretical predictions about the scaling behavior of mean squared error.

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical definitions are precise, and the authors carefully explain the connection between tensor decomposition and structured sparsity. The figures (particularly Figure 2) effectively illustrate the theoretical results. The paper would be accessible to NIPS readers with a background in optimization or machine learning.

*Originality:* While the basic idea of convex tensor decomposition is not new, the paper makes a significant contribution by providing the first theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and extends the structured sparsity literature. The connection to group sparsity norms provides a valuable theoretical framework for understanding both approaches.

*Significance:* The results have clear practical implications for tensor decomposition practitioners. The paper demonstrates that the latent approach is theoretically preferable to the overlapped approach in many settings, which could guide practitioners in choosing between these methods. The theoretical guarantee that the latent approach performs as well as knowing the mode with the smallest rank is particularly compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

*Quality:* The paper could better address computational considerations. While the theoretical bounds are strong, the authors don't discuss the computational complexity of solving the latent approach versus the overlapped approach. The numerical experiments use the same optimization algorithm for both approaches, but it's unclear if this is fair given the different structures of the optimization problems.

*Originality:* The paper's main contribution is theoretical rather than methodological. While the duality result is novel, the paper doesn't propose new algorithms or significantly extend the range of problems that can be solved with these approaches. The connection to structured sparsity is well-made but not entirely unexpected given previous work.

*Significance:* The results are limited to the specific setting of noisy tensor decomposition. The paper doesn't address how the results might extend to tensor completion (with missing values), which is a major application of tensor decomposition. The authors mention this as future work but don't discuss the challenges in adapting their theory to that setting.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
- Provides the first theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach
- Establishes a novel duality between two important classes of norms
- Contains rigorous theoretical guarantees with precise bounds
- Empirical results confirm theoretical predictions
- Well-written and clearly organized paper

**Arguments against Acceptance:**
- Limited scope (only noisy tensor decomposition, not tensor completion)
- Doesn't address computational complexity differences between approaches
- The connection to structured sparsity, while valuable, isn't entirely surprising

**Overall Recommendation:** I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to understanding convex tensor decomposition approaches, providing the first rigorous explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The duality result is interesting in its own right and extends the structured sparsity literature. While the scope is limited to noisy tensor decomposition, the results are important for practitioners and provide a solid foundation for future work in tensor completion and other applications. The paper is well-written and the theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity differences between the approaches and a more detailed exploration of how the theory might extend to tensor completion, but these are minor issues that don't detract from the paper's main contribution. The results are novel, technically sound, and significant for the field of tensor decomposition.
