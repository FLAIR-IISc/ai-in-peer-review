

This paper introduces a novel theoretical framework for structured Schatten norms in tensor decomposition, focusing on the relationship between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches to convex optimization-based tensor decomposition. The work builds upon recent proposals [9, 15, 23, 25] while connecting to structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21]. The main contribution is a theoretical explanation for why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific mode, along with a novel duality result between the two norm types.

The paper establishes a key theoretical result (Theorem 2) demonstrating that the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. This explains the empirical observations in previous work [25, 26] and provides a precise theoretical foundation for the practical superiority of the latent approach. The duality result (Lemma 1) between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is significant in its own right and extends structured sparsity theory.

The quality of the paper is strong. The theoretical results are rigorously proven (with proofs in supplementary material), and the numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions. The paper carefully addresses the limitations of both approaches, particularly noting that the latent approach excels when the tensor is low-rank in a specific mode but still performs well in simultaneously low-rank settings. The comparison with existing work is thorough and well-justified.

The clarity is excellent. The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions of the norms and precise notation. The logical flow from problem statement to theoretical results to empirical validation is straightforward. The supplementary material provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the numerical experiments are well-designed to validate the theoretical claims.

The originality is high. While both approaches have been previously proposed, this paper is the first to provide a theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach. The duality result generalizes structured sparsity concepts to tensor norms in a novel way. The paper clearly differentiates itself from prior work by establishing the theoretical basis for the performance differences.

The significance is substantial. Tensor decomposition has wide applications in neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, and environmental monitoring. The theoretical foundation provided here guides practitioners in selecting appropriate methods and informs future research on convex tensor decomposition. The paper demonstrates that the latent approach performs as if it knew the mode with minimal rank, which is valuable for real-world applications where such information is typically unavailable.

A minor weakness is the limited discussion of computational complexity. While the paper mentions the optimization algorithms used, it does not provide a detailed comparison of computational efficiency between the two approaches, which would be valuable for practitioners. The paper could also explore more concrete applications where the theoretical insights directly impact performance.

The paper meets all four criteria for high-quality NIPS papers. It is technically sound with well-supported claims, clearly written with appropriate organization, original in its theoretical contributions, and significant in its practical implications for tensor decomposition methods. The work advances the state of the art by providing a theoretical foundation for an empirically successful method.

The paper's theoretical results are particularly compelling as they explain a previously observed empirical phenomenon with precise mathematical guarantees. The numerical experiments successfully validate the theoretical predictions, demonstrating the mean squared error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures. This tight connection between theory and practice strengthens the paper's contribution.

The paper is well-positioned within the NIPS community, addressing a fundamental problem in multivariate data analysis with a theoretically grounded solution. The connections to structured sparsity literature make the work relevant to a broader audience within machine learning and optimization.

Overall, this paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The theoretical results are novel, well-supported, and have clear practical implications. The paper is recommended for acceptance at NIPS as it meets all quality criteria and represents a meaningful advance in the field.
