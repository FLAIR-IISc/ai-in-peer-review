This paper studies convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, specifically analyzing the “latent” approach proposed in prior work. The core contribution lies in a theoretical analysis demonstrating that the latent approach performs as well as knowing the mode with the smallest rank when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific, unknown mode. A key technical result is a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing existing results in structured sparsity. The theory is supported by numerical simulations confirming the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing a significant challenge in tensor decomposition: model selection (i.e., determining the rank along each mode) and generalization performance of convex relaxations. The introduction clearly outlines the problem and positions the work within the existing literature on tensor decomposition and convex optimization-based approaches. The definitions and notation are clear, and the connection to related work, particularly structured sparsity, is effectively drawn. The theoretical results are substantial, and the duality result is a valuable contribution in its own right. The numerical experiments provide reasonable validation of the theoretical findings, demonstrating the predicted scaling.

A strength of this work is the rigorous theoretical analysis. The derivation of the consistency results and the performance bounds for the latent approach are technically sound and clearly presented. The comparison to the overlapped approach, supported by both theory and experiments, convincingly demonstrates the advantages of the latent approach in scenarios where the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The connection to the broader literature on structured sparsity is also a significant strength, showcasing the general applicability of the results. 

However, several areas could be improved. While the paper demonstrates the scaling behavior aligns with the theory, it does not thoroughly investigate the constant factors involved. The numerical experiments could be expanded to include a wider range of tensor sizes and rank configurations to further validate the theoretical predictions and explore the robustness of the latent approach under different conditions. The choice of regularization parameter in the experiments is not fully justified, and a more systematic exploration of its impact on performance would be valuable.  The paper mentions the connection to lower bounds from Mu et al. [19], but a more detailed discussion of how the results relate and potentially complement each other would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the discussion of the limitations of the analysis and potential directions for future work could be expanded. The reliance on specific optimization algorithms (ADMM-based) might also be a limitation, and discussing the applicability of other solvers could strengthen the work. Finally, while the connection to the “dirty model” from Hsu et al. [12] is alluded to, making this connection more explicit and discussing implications for robustness could be worthwhile.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a strong theoretical analysis of a practically relevant problem (tensor decomposition).
*   The duality result is a novel and significant contribution.
*   The numerical results support the theoretical findings.
*   The work is well-written and clearly presented.
*   It connects tensor decomposition to the broader area of structured sparsity.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The numerical experiments could be more comprehensive.
*   The impact of regularization parameter selection is not thoroughly investigated.
*   The limitations of the analysis are not fully discussed.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are rigorous and insightful, and the numerical experiments provide reasonable validation. Addressing the suggestions for improvement would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. It is recommended for acceptance.
