This paper presents a theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach for convex tensor decomposition, which models a tensor as a sum of components, each being low-rank in a specific mode. The authors situate this approach within a broader framework of "structured Schatten norms," introducing generalized "overlapped" and "latent" norms.

The main contributions are:
1.  A novel duality result is established between the generalized overlapped Sp/q-norm and the latent Sp*/q*-norm. This provides an elegant connection between the convex tensor decomposition literature and the broader field of structured sparsity.
2.  The paper provides the first theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The key result is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor.
3.  This result is contrasted with prior analysis of the "overlapped" approach [26], whose error scales with the *sum* of the square roots of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical explanation for the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach, particularly when the true tensor is low-rank in only one of its modes.
4.  Numerical experiments are conducted to validate the new theoretical bounds, showing that the empirical error scales precisely as predicted by the theory.

This work builds directly upon recent efforts to develop convex relaxations for tensor decomposition [9, 15, 25, 26], providing a key missing piece of analysis for the latent model proposed in [25].

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and leads to insightful conclusions. The main claims regarding the scaling behavior of the latent approach are well-supported by both the theoretical derivations (Theorems 2 and 3) and the carefully designed numerical experiments. The work appears to be a complete and sound contribution to the analysis of convex tensor methods.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear from the outset, and the distinction between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches is explained very effectively. The theoretical results are stated precisely, and their implications are discussed in a way that highlights the key contributions. The experimental section does a great job of directly testing and confirming the predictions made by the theory.
*   **Originality and Significance:** This work makes a significant and original contribution. While the latent approach itself was proposed in earlier work, it lacked a theoretical justification. This paper fills that important gap. The core finding—that the latent approach automatically adapts to the "easiest" (i.e., lowest-rank) mode without prior knowledge—is a powerful result with both theoretical and practical importance. It provides strong guidance to practitioners on which convex relaxation to prefer. Furthermore, the duality result (Lemma 1) is a novel contribution in its own right, creating a valuable bridge between tensor methods and structured sparsity. These ideas are likely to be influential and built upon in future research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   The main analysis in Section 3.2 relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11) that includes this assumption. While such assumptions are standard in related literature, the practical implications could be discussed further. For example, it is not clear how the hyperparameter `α` would be set in practice. A clarification on how this constraint was handled in the numerical experiments would strengthen the paper.
*   The analysis is focused on the tensor denoising problem. While this is a valuable setting, a major application of these methods is tensor completion (i.e., recovery from missing entries). The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it is a limitation of the current paper's scope.
*   The "Latent Rank (LR) complexity" measure used to validate the theory in Figure 2 relies on the ranks of the latent factors of the *true, noiseless* tensor. While this is appropriate for validating the theoretical scaling, it highlights that this specific complexity measure is not directly computable in a real-world scenario. The paper rightly points out that the final bound can be expressed in terms of the more interpretable `min_k r_k`, which mitigates this concern to a large extent.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Provides the first, and much-needed, theoretical analysis for the latent convex tensor decomposition method.
*   The results deliver a clear and impactful message: the latent approach is often superior to the overlapped approach, and the theory explains precisely why.
*   The novel duality result is an elegant theoretical contribution that connects disparate areas of research.
*   The claims are convincingly validated with well-designed experiments.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   The analysis is limited to the denoising setting and relies on a somewhat strong incoherence assumption whose practical application is not fully detailed.

In summary, this is an excellent paper that addresses an important open question in the theory of tensor methods. The contributions are significant, the results are clear, and the technical quality is high. The weaknesses are minor compared to the strengths of the work. I strongly recommend acceptance.
