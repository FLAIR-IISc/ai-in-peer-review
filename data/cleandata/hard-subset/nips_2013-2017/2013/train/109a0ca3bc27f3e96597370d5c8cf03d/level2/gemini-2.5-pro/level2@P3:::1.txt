### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition, focusing on a method termed the "latent approach." This approach, introduced in prior work, was empirically observed to outperform the more common "overlapped approach" in settings where a tensor is low-rank in only one of its modes. The main contribution of this work is to provide a rigorous theoretical justification for this phenomenon.

The paper introduces a general framework of "structured Schatten norms," which encompasses both the overlapped and latent formulations. Within this framework, a novel duality result is proven, establishing that the "overlapped Sp/q-norm" is the dual of the "latent Sp*/q*-norm." This result connects the tensor decomposition problem to the broader literature on structured sparsity, such as the overlapped and latent group lasso.

Using this theoretical machinery, the work derives performance bounds for the latent approach in a noisy tensor recovery setting. The key result is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with existing bounds for the overlapped approach, where the error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This finding elegantly explains why the latent approach performs as if it has oracle knowledge of the mode with the simplest structure. The theoretical predictions are then validated through numerical simulations on synthetic data, which show a clear linear relationship between the empirical error and the theoretically derived complexity measures.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical claims are well-supported by rigorous analysis, with detailed proofs provided in the supplementary material. The development of the structured Schatten norm framework and the duality lemma are elegant and provide a solid foundation for the subsequent performance bounds.

The main theorems (Theorems 2 and 3) provide clear, interpretable error bounds that directly link the statistical performance of the latent approach to the underlying rank structure of the tensor. The analysis relies on an incoherence assumption and a modified optimization problem with additional spectral norm constraints, which are standard techniques in high-dimensional statistics for obtaining sharper bounds for decomposable regularizers. The experiments are well-designed and directly test the key theoretical predictions, successfully demonstrating the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error with respect to both the tensor ranks and dimensions.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The motivation is clearly stated in the introduction, and the distinction between the overlapped and latent approaches is explained effectively. Section 2 methodically introduces the necessary definitions and the core duality result. Section 3 presents the main theoretical contributions in a logical progression, starting from a general consistency result and moving to more refined bounds under specific noise models. The comparison in Section 3.4 is particularly helpful, as it explicitly contrasts the new bound for the latent approach with the existing bound for the overlapped approach, crystallizing the paper's central message. The experimental section is also clear, with the methodology and results presented in a way that is easy to follow and directly supports the theoretical claims.

### Originality

The work is highly original. While the overlapped and latent algorithms were proposed in earlier work, this paper is the first to provide a rigorous performance analysis for the latent approach. The key insight that its performance depends on the minimal mode-k rank is a novel and significant contribution.

Furthermore, the formulation of the problem within a general class of structured Schatten norms and the accompanying duality theorem (Lemma 1) are original contributions. This framework unifies the two tensor norms and connects them to established concepts in structured sparsity, which is a valuable contribution in its own right. The paper clearly situates its contributions with respect to prior work, particularly the analysis of the overlapped approach in [26] and the initial proposal of the latent method in [25].

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are significant and likely to have a strong impact on the field. The work provides a definitive answer to the question of why and when the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach for tensor decomposition. The finding that the latent approach automatically adapts to the simplest structure across all modes is a powerful result for practitioners, suggesting it is a more robust choice, especially when there is no prior knowledge about the tensor's multilinear rank.

The theoretical framework of structured Schatten norms and their duality could also inspire further research into new types of tensor regularizers. By providing both theoretical guarantees and empirical evidence, this work substantially advances the understanding of convex methods for tensor analysis and is likely to be widely cited and built upon by researchers working on tensor methods and high-dimensional statistics.

### Suggestions for Improvement

The paper is very strong, but a few minor points could be clarified to improve it further.

1.  **Discussion of the Incoherence Assumption:** The analysis in Theorem 2 introduces an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and requires solving a constrained optimization problem (Eq. 11). While this is a common analytical tool, it would be beneficial to add a brief discussion on the practical implications. For instance, how is the parameter `α` handled in the numerical experiments? Clarifying whether the constraints were active or if `α` was set to a large value would strengthen the link between the theory and the empirical validation.

2.  **Computational Considerations:** The paper focuses exclusively on statistical performance. A brief comment on the relative computational complexity of the latent versus the overlapped approach would be a valuable addition for practitioners. The latent formulation introduces `K` tensor variables, which could potentially increase the computational cost. A short discussion on whether this leads to a significant overhead in practice (e.g., using the mentioned ADMM solver) would make the comparison more complete.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that presents a significant contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The arguments are clear, the results are novel and important, and the work provides a complete and satisfying explanation for a key phenomenon in the field.
