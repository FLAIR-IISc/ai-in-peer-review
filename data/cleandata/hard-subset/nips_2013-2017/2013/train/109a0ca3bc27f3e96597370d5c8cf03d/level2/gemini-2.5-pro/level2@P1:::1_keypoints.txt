- Presents theoretical analysis of the "latent" convex tensor decomposition, framed via generalized "overlapped" and "latent" structured Schatten norms.
- Establishes a novel duality between generalized overlapped Sp/q-norm and latent Sp*/q*-norm, connecting tensor decomposition and structured sparsity.
- Provides first theoretical performance guarantees for the latent approach in tensor denoising, showing MSE scales with the minimum mode-k rank.
- Contrasts with prior overlapped-analysis where error scales with the sum of square roots of mode-k ranks, explaining latent's empirical superiority when only one mode is low-rank.
- Validates theoretical bounds with numerical experiments that show empirical error scaling matches theory.
- Builds on recent convex tensor decomposition work and fills a key missing theoretical justification for the latent model.
- Strength: High technical quality and rigorous analysis supporting the main scaling claims (Theorems 2 and 3).
- Strength: Exceptionally clear writing and effective explanation of overlapped vs. latent approaches; experiments directly test theoretical predictions.
- Strength: Original and significant contribution—latent approach adapts to the easiest mode and the duality result is novel and likely influential.
- Weakness: Analysis relies on an incoherence assumption and a constrained optimization (Eq. 10/11); practical setting of hyperparameter α and experiment handling need clarification.
- Weakness: Focuses only on tensor denoising; does not address tensor completion, a major application, which is left as future work.
- Weakness: The LR complexity used in validation depends on true noiseless latent ranks, which are not computable in practice (mitigated by expressing bounds via min_k r_k).
- For acceptance: First theoretical analysis of latent method, clear practical recommendation favoring latent over overlapped, novel duality, and convincing experiments.
- Against acceptance: Limited to denoising and reliant on a somewhat strong incoherence assumption not fully detailed in practice.
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper with significant, well-supported contributions; reviewer strongly recommends acceptance.
