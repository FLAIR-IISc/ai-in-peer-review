Summary
The paper develops a unified framework of structured Schatten norms for tensors, encompassing the “overlapped” and “latent” variants used in convex tensor decomposition. The central technical result is a duality between overlapped Sp/q norms and latent Sp*/q* norms. Building on this, the paper analyzes the denoising performance of the latent approach. It shows (i) a basic consistency bound under a dual-norm condition on the noise; (ii) a deterministic error bound that exploits a latent decomposition with an incoherence-type constraint, yielding overall error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank; and (iii) high-probability bounds under Gaussian noise with a recommended λ independent of the unknown ranks. The bounds explain previously observed empirical advantages of the latent approach over the overlapped approach, and the authors corroborate the scaling predictions via simulations.

Relation to prior work
This article positions itself against recent convex approaches to tensor completion/decomposition based on overlapped trace norms of unfoldings [Gandy et al.; Liu et al.; Tomioka et al.], and leverages analysis tools for decomposable regularizers [Agarwal et al.; Negahban et al.]. The duality result generalizes known relationships in structured sparsity between overlapped and latent group norms (e.g., Obozinski et al.; Jenatton et al.), but for Schatten norms on tensor unfoldings. The comparative bound for overlapped norms is taken from Tomioka et al. (NIPS’11/12), and the discussion nods to lower bounds for tensor recovery [Mu et al.]. Overall the context is appropriate and the connections are clear.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and novelty: The duality between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms is elegant, natural, and likely useful beyond this specific application. It tightens the analogy to group sparsity.
- Theory that matches practice: The bounds for latent regularization scale with the minimum mode rank, aligning with empirical observations that the latent approach fares better when the tensor is low-rank in an unknown single mode.
- Use of modern high-dimensional analysis: The decomposable-regularizer viewpoint and dual norm control of noise are well used, leading to transparent prescriptions for λ under Gaussian noise.
- Empirical validation of scaling: Simulations convincingly show linear trends of MSE with the proposed “latent rank” complexity and give some evidence that the near-optimal λ does not depend on ranks.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Assumptions and constraints in Theorems 2–3: The stronger bounds rely on an incoherence-type constraint (spectral norm bounds across modes) and on solving a constrained problem (11) with ∥W(l)(k)∥S∞ ≤ α for l ≠ k. It is unclear whether these constraints were enforced in the experiments (α not specified), and how sensitive the results are to α. If the solver did not enforce them, there is a mismatch between theory and practice; if it did, details are needed.
- Practicality of α: The constraint parameter α depends on unknown properties of the true decomposition and appears in λ and the factor F. Guidance on setting α from data (or removing the need for it) would improve applicability.
- Limited experimental scope: Only synthetic denoising (no missing data) on relatively small 3-way tensors is shown. There is no runtime/complexity comparison between overlapped and latent solvers, and no real-data demonstration. Since the latent approach introduces multiple latent components, computational trade-offs should be documented.
- Sharpness and constants: While the scaling is persuasive, constants in the bounds (e.g., the factor F) are somewhat opaque, and the “universal c” in key theorems may be large. Discussion of tightness or a lower bound for latent norms would contextualize the gains better.
- Comparison breadth: Beyond overlapped vs latent S1/1, it would be informative to compare to other tensor norms/regularizers (e.g., S1/∞, mixed choices, or max-norm-inspired relaxations), and to nonconvex baselines (e.g., HOOI with model selection) to show robustness.

Detailed evaluation
- Quality: The technical development appears sound. The duality claim is plausible and aligns with known structured sparsity results; the consistency result is standard given the dual norm. The stronger bounds are reasonable under the added constraints, but the paper should be clearer on when (and how) those constraints are used in practice.
- Clarity: The paper is mostly well written with precise definitions. However, the role of α, whether constraints are enforced in experiments, and how λ is selected in practice (estimation of σ) should be clarified. A brief proof sketch for the duality lemma would aid accessibility.
- Originality: The extension of overlapped/latent duality to tensor Schatten norms and the performance analysis that achieves min-mode-rank scaling for the latent approach are novel and meaningful.
- Significance: Results likely influence practitioners toward latent regularization for noisy tensor problems and stimulate further work on structured tensor norms and their analysis.

Suggestions for improvement
- Explicitly state whether the spectral norm constraints in (11) are enforced in experiments, what α values are used, and how α is chosen. If not enforced, discuss the gap and whether the results are expected to hold without them.
- Provide an experiment varying α (or removing the constraint) to assess sensitivity and necessity.
- Include computational timings and convergence behavior for the ADMM solver; discuss scalability in K and dimensions.
- Show at least one real-data experiment (e.g., neuroimaging or video) and/or a missing data setting to demonstrate broader utility.
- Offer a data-driven λ selection strategy (e.g., via an estimate of σ from residuals) and confirm its performance.
- Expand the discussion on when overlapped may be preferable (e.g., simultaneous low rank across modes) and present a counterexample if any.

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical contribution (duality) with implications beyond this paper.
- Performance guarantees that align with and explain empirical advantages of the latent approach.
- Careful empirical validation of predicted scaling.

Cons for acceptance
- Dependence on additional constraints and parameters (α) not fully justified or operationalized.
- Limited experimental scope and absence of real-data or missing-data results.
- Lack of computational and baseline breadth.

Overall recommendation
I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying the role and implementation of the spectral-norm constraints, and strengthening the empirical section with parameter-setting details and computational comparisons. The conceptual and theoretical contributions are solid and of interest to the NIPS community.
