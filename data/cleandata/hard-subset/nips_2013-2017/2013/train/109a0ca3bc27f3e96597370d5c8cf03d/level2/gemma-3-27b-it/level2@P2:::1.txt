This paper studies convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically focusing on a “latent” approach that has been observed to outperform a previously proposed “overlapped” approach. The authors provide a theoretical justification for this empirical observation, showing that the latent approach achieves error bounds scaling with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, a superior result compared to the overlapped approach’s dependence on average rank. A key contribution is a duality result for the structured Schatten norms involved. The paper is well-motivated, addressing an important topic in tensor decomposition, and the results appear technically sound.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** The primary strength is providing a theoretical foundation for the empirical success of the latent approach. The result showing scaling with the *minimum* rank is a significant improvement over existing bounds and elegantly explains observed performance.
* **Duality Result:**  The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable contribution in its own right, extending previous work on structured sparsity.  This duality is cleverly leveraged in the analysis.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. Definitions are provided, and the connection to existing work (overlapped approach, group lasso) is well-explained.
* **Numerical Validation:**  The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical scaling behavior and provide further evidence for the superiority of the latent approach, even in cases where the overlapped approach should theoretically perform well.
* **Connection to Existing Literature:** The authors effectively link their work to relevant literature in tensor decomposition, structured sparsity, and matrix completion.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Assumptions:** The theoretical analysis relies on several assumptions (e.g., the specific incoherence condition in Theorem 2, Gaussian noise in Theorem 3) that may not always hold in practical applications. While such assumptions are common in theoretical analysis, it would be beneficial to discuss their limitations and potential extensions to more general noise distributions.
* **Practical Considerations:** The paper focuses on theoretical guarantees and scaling behavior.  A discussion of practical implementation considerations, such as the computational complexity of the optimization problems involved, would be valuable.  While the solver is mentioned, a brief analysis of its scalability would enhance the practical impact of the work.
* **Limited Scope of Experiments:** While the numerical results support the theory, they are somewhat limited in scope. Exploring the performance on a wider range of tensor sizes, ranks, and noise levels would further strengthen the empirical validation. Investigating cases where the assumptions are violated could reveal the robustness (or lack thereof) of the latent approach.
* **Relationship to Other Approaches:**  The paper mainly focuses on contrasting the latent and overlapped approaches. A brief discussion of how the latent approach compares to other tensor decomposition methods (e.g., non-convex methods, Tucker decomposition) in terms of accuracy, speed, and robustness would provide valuable context.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant open problem in tensor decomposition: understanding the theoretical performance of convex relaxation-based approaches.
* The theoretical results are strong and provide a compelling explanation for the observed advantages of the latent approach.
* The duality result is a novel and potentially useful contribution to the broader field of structured sparsity.
* The numerical experiments validate the theory and demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the proposed method.
* The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and connects effectively to existing literature.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The theoretical results rely on potentially strong assumptions that may limit their applicability in real-world scenarios.
* The numerical evaluation, while supportive, could be more comprehensive.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of practical implementation considerations and comparisons to alternative tensor decomposition methods.

**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis is rigorous, the duality result is insightful, and the numerical validation is convincing. While the assumptions and limited scope of experiments are minor weaknesses, they do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the comments regarding assumptions, practical considerations, and scope of experiments.  Specifically, the authors should discuss the limitations of the assumptions and explore potential avenues for extending the theory to more general settings. A brief discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the optimization algorithms would also be beneficial.
