## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates the theoretical properties of the “latent” approach to convex tensor decomposition, recently proposed as an improvement over the “overlapped” approach. The core contribution lies in providing a theoretical justification for the empirical observation that the latent approach performs better when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only a single mode. The authors achieve this by analyzing the performance bounds of the latent approach, demonstrating that its error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank, a significant improvement over the overlapped approach’s dependency on the average rank. A novel duality result for structured Schatten norms is also presented, generalizing existing results in structured sparsity literature.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly outlining the limitations of existing convex tensor decomposition methods and the empirical success of the latent approach. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and builds upon established techniques from matrix completion and sparse recovery. The authors effectively connect their work to related areas like group lasso and structured sparsity, providing valuable context. The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions, demonstrating the scaling behavior of the mean squared error and validating the superiority of the latent approach, even in scenarios favorable to the overlapped method. 

A strength of this work is the clear presentation of the duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which provides a deeper understanding of their properties. The comparison with existing results, particularly the work of Tomioka et al. [25, 26], is thorough and highlights the advantages of the proposed approach. 

However, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of the theoretical results. While the scaling behavior is established, a more concrete guide for choosing the regularization parameter λ in real-world applications would be valuable. Additionally, the assumption of known tensor dimensions (n1, ..., nK) might be limiting; exploring robustness to slight inaccuracies in these estimates could be a worthwhile extension.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Strong theoretical justification for an empirically successful method.
*   Novel duality result with broader implications for structured sparsity.
*   Clear presentation and connection to related work.
*   Confirmed theoretical predictions through numerical experiments.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Limited discussion of practical parameter tuning.
*   Assumption of known tensor dimensions.

**Overall:** This is a solid theoretical contribution that significantly advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. Despite minor limitations, the paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance. The authors have presented a well-reasoned and rigorously analyzed work that is likely to be influential in the field.
