Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper introduces a unified framework of structured Schatten norms for tensors, encompassing the “overlapped” and “latent” norms used in convex tensor decomposition. Its primary claims are: (1) a novel duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms; (2) sharp statistical guarantees for the latent approach in tensor denoising, showing error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, thereby explaining empirical gains over the overlapped approach; and (3) numerical evidence that matches the theoretical scaling. The work situates itself well within tensor methods and structured sparsity (Kolda & Bader; overlapped/latent group lasso dualities) and builds on prior convex analyses for matrices (Agarwal et al.; Negahban et al.) and tensors (Tomioka et al.).

Strengths:
- Quality: The theoretical development is careful and leverages duality and decomposability to derive consistency, deterministic, and stochastic (Gaussian) error bounds. The key inequality bounding the latent Schatten-1 norm by min_k sqrt(rk) times the Frobenius norm is insightful and directly drives the improved rates vs. overlapped norms.
- Originality: The duality between overlapped and latent tensor Schatten norms generalizes known structured sparsity dualities to a tensor setting and appears novel. The rates that adapt to the smallest mode rank are an appealing contribution.
- Significance: Results provide guidance for choosing the latent formulation in noisy settings and clarify when it should outperform overlapped alternatives. The bounds are practically interpretable, with λ choices independent of unknown ranks.
- Clarity: The exposition is generally clear, relates to prior work, and provides proofs in the supplement. Definitions and problem setups are precise.

Weaknesses and limitations:
- Experimental scope is limited to synthetic denoising without missing data; no real-world datasets (e.g., neuroimaging) or tensor completion, where these methods are often used.
- The deterministic bound relies on an incoherence-like constraint and a parameter α; practical selection and enforcement of α are not fully discussed, creating a theory-practice gap.
- Comparisons exclude strong nonconvex baselines (ALS/HOOI), and hyperparameter tuning fairness across methods could be better justified.
- Some constants (c, F) and dimensional factors may be loose; discussion of tightness is limited.

Constructive suggestions:
- Add tensor completion and real-data experiments; include nonconvex baselines and report runtime/scalability.
- Provide guidance for α and λ when noise variance is unknown; clarify whether the additional constraints in (11) are used in practice.
- Explore other structured norms in the framework (e.g., S1/∞) empirically.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Novel and correct duality; theoretically grounded advantage of latent norms.
- Clear improvements in statistical rates with matching simulations.
- Well-situated within NeurIPS literature; advances understanding of convex tensor methods.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Limited empirical validation on real tasks and missing-data settings.
- Practical guidance on key assumptions/parameters is underdeveloped.
- Missing comparisons to strong nonconvex baselines.
