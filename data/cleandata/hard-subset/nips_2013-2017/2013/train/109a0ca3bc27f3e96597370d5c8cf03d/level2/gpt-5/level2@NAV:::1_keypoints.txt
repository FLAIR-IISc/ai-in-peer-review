- Paper proposes a unified structured Schatten-norm framework for tensors, covering overlapped and latent norms.  
- Main claims: duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms, sharp statistical guarantees for latent denoising, and matching numerical evidence.  
- Situates work within tensor methods and structured sparsity, building on prior convex analyses for matrices and tensors.  
- Theoretical development is careful, using duality and decomposability to derive consistency and error bounds.  
- Key inequality: latent Schatten-1 norm bounded by min_k sqrt(rk) times Frobenius norm, driving improved rates over overlapped norms.  
- Novelty: generalizes structured sparsity dualities to tensors and yields rates adapting to the smallest mode rank.  
- Significance: guidance for choosing latent formulation in noisy settings, with interpretable bounds and λ choices independent of unknown ranks.  
- Clarity: exposition is generally clear with precise definitions and proofs provided in the supplement.  
- Experiments are limited to synthetic denoising without missing data or real-world datasets.  
- Deterministic bound depends on an incoherence-like parameter α, with limited practical guidance on selecting or enforcing it.  
- Comparisons omit strong nonconvex baselines (e.g., ALS/HOOI) and fairness of hyperparameter tuning is not fully justified.  
- Some constants and dimensional factors in bounds may be loose; discussion of tightness is limited.  
- Suggested additions: tensor completion and real-data experiments, inclusion of nonconvex baselines, and runtime/scalability reporting.  
- Suggested clarifications: guidance for α and λ when noise variance is unknown, clarify use of additional constraints in practice, and empirically explore other structured norms (e.g., S1/∞).  
- Reasons to accept: novel and correct duality, theoretically grounded advantage of latent norms, improved statistical rates with matching simulations.  
- Reasons to reject: limited empirical validation on real/missing-data tasks, underdeveloped practical guidance on assumptions/parameters, and missing comparisons to strong nonconvex methods.
