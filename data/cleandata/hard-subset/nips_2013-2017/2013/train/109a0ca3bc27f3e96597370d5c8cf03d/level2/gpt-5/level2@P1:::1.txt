Summary and contributions:
The paper studies convex formulations for low-rank tensor estimation based on structured Schatten norms. It unifies two existing approaches—“overlapped” (sum of nuclear norms of mode unfoldings) and “latent” (infimal convolution over mode-specific low-rank components)—within a structured Sp/q framework and proves a duality: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q-norm is the latent Sp*/q*-norm. Building on this, the paper analyzes the latent approach for denoising and shows that its error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach whose bounds scale with an average of mode-wise ranks. The analysis includes:
- A consistency result depending on the dual overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise.
- Deterministic bounds leveraging an incoherence-type spectral-norm constraint across modes, yielding overall error scaling with min mode-k rank and component-wise error scaling with the sum of component ranks.
- A Gaussian noise bound with a concrete choice of λ independent of unknown ranks, and rates proportional to σ2 times a latent-rank complexity.
Experiments on synthetic tensors corroborate the predicted scaling and empirically favor the latent approach even when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank.

Relation to prior work:
The paper connects convex tensor methods [Gandy et al., Signoretto et al., Liu et al.] and structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent/dirty models) [Obozinski et al., Jenatton et al., Jalali et al.] via a general duality of structured norms, extending known results on overlapped/latent group lasso to Schatten norms. It contrasts with prior theory for the overlapped approach [Tomioka et al., 2011/2013], which established scaling in terms of averaged ranks, and aligns with geometric viewpoints for convex regularizers [Chandrasekaran et al.]. A recent lower bound for tensor recovery with overlapped norms [Mu et al., 2013] is discussed; the latent approach matches the intuition that dependence on min mode-k rank is fundamental.

Quality (technical soundness):
- The duality lemma is natural and appears correct, generalizing known results in structured sparsity via infimal convolution. It is central for both optimization and complexity control (via dual norms).
- The consistency and deterministic bounds follow a decomposable-regularizer style analysis (e.g., Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.). The requirement on λ via the dual norm of the noise is standard.
- The deterministic bound assumes spectral norm incoherence across components (Eq. 10) and enforces it in the estimator (Eq. 11). This is reasonable but somewhat strong; the paper is transparent about it.
- The Gaussian noise result provides an explicit λ that depends only on tensor sizes, K, and α, and a bound scaling with σ2 and latent ranks, up to a dimension factor F. The shape of F seems mild but somewhat involved; the dependence is plausible.
- The comparison with overlapped bounds is fair and highlights the adaptivity to the minimal mode rank.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. Definitions of unfoldings, multilinear rank, and the structured norms are precise. The role of the dual norms and how they enter the bounds are explained. The experimental section is careful to define complexity proxies (TR vs LR) and to relate them to the theory. A few places could be clarified (see below).

Originality:
- Conceptual: The structured duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms in tensors is a nice generalization of known sparsity results.
- Theoretical: New denoising bounds for the latent approach showing min-rank adaptivity and a consistency statement based on S∞/∞ dual norm.
- Empirical: Clear demonstration that the latent approach’s MSE scales with the latent-rank complexity and often outperforms the overlapped approach.

Significance:
The results are important for tensor methods, offering both sharper understanding and practical guidance: the latent approach is provably and empirically preferable when low-rankness is concentrated in one mode. The duality may impact broader structured regularization beyond tensors. The bounds with explicit λ choices are useful in practice.

Strengths:
- Clear unifying framework for structured Schatten norms with an elegant duality.
- Theoretical guarantees for the latent approach that adapt to min mode-k rank.
- λ selection independent of unknown ranks in Gaussian noise.
- Experiments directly test the predicted scaling and compare fairly to the overlapped approach.
- Good positioning with respect to structured sparsity and recent tensor literature.

Weaknesses and questions:
- The deterministic/Gaussian bounds rely on the spectral-norm incoherence assumptions (Eq. 10) and constraints in (11). Are these constraints enforced in the experiments? If not, please discuss why behavior remains consistent and whether theory can be extended without explicit constraints (e.g., via approximate decomposability).
- The parameter α is assumed known for constraints and appears in λ. Guidance on estimating or setting α in practice would help. Alternatively, can results be stated under a probabilistic incoherence model?
- The LR complexity depends on the ranks of latent components r̄k, which are not directly observable (and not equal to the multilinear ranks). While the theory is sound, practical interpretability would improve with bounds stated purely in terms of multilinear ranks and dimension factors (even if looser).
- The consistency bound (Eq. 8) scales with √(min nk); is this tight? Could sharper dimension dependence be obtained (e.g., via Gaussian width) for particular K?
- Experiments are synthetic and limited to K=3 and moderate sizes. A small real-data example (e.g., video or neuroimaging denoising) and/or experiments for K>3 would strengthen the empirical section and assess computational scalability.
- Computational aspects: The ADMM solver from [25] is cited; discussion of per-iteration complexity and convergence behavior for K>3 would be useful. Does the latent formulation introduce additional variables that increase memory/time?
- Comparison to lower bounds: While the overlapped approach has a lower bound [Mu et al.], can the authors comment on whether min-rank dependence is information-theoretically optimal for the latent setting?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify whether the spectral-norm constraints in (11) were used in experiments; if not, add an ablation or discussion.
- Provide guidance on choosing α and λ in practice (beyond Gaussian noise), e.g., via noise estimation or cross-validation and sensitivity plots.
- Add a brief proof sketch for the duality lemma in the main text or a figure illustrating the geometry of overlapped vs latent norms.
- Expand the discussion on identifiability of the latent components W(k) and whether the bounds imply anything for component recovery (beyond sum recovery).
- Consider reporting runtime and memory usage for both methods on the experimental setups.

Typos/minor:
- Ensure consistent notation for r̄k (component ranks) vs rk (mode-k ranks of W*).
- In Eq. (14)–(15), restate F’s dependence once more for readability.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Solid theoretical contribution (duality + min-rank-adaptive bounds) with clear practical implications.
- Addresses an important gap: performance of latent tensor norms under noise.
- Empirical results that align with theory and demonstrate advantage over well-known baselines.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Reliance on spectral-norm incoherence assumptions and constraints; limited discussion of their practical enforcement.
- Experimental validation limited to synthetic data and K=3, with modest exploration of scalability and parameter selection.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated, technically sound, and clearly presented paper that advances our understanding of convex tensor decomposition and structured norms. The duality result and min-rank-adaptive guarantees are novel and significant. I recommend acceptance after addressing the clarifications regarding the incoherence constraints and practical parameter choices.
