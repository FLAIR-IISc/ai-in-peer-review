

This paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors establish a novel duality between these norms (Lemma 1), which generalizes structured sparsity literature (e.g., [1, 12, 17]), and prove that the latent approach achieves error bounds scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank rather than the average rank, explaining its empirical superiority over the overlapped approach as observed in [25]. The main contributions are theoretically sound and well-motivated, with clear connections to prior NIPS work (e.g., Tomioka et al. [25, 26] in NIPS 2013/2014).

**Strengths:**  
1. **Theoretical depth:** The duality result (Lemma 1) and error bounds (Theorems 2–3) are technically precise and address a significant gap in the literature. The connection to structured sparsity (e.g., latent group lasso) is insightful and well-justified.  
2. **Empirical validation:** The experiments (Section 4) convincingly confirm the predicted scaling of MSE with LR complexity (Eq. 18), matching Theorem 3. The comparison of latent vs. overlapped approaches across tensor sizes and ranks is thorough and addresses a key practical question.  
3. **Clarity and organization:** The paper is well-structured, with clear definitions (Section 2), logical progression of results (Section 3), and a compelling experimental narrative. The supplementary proofs add credibility.  
4. **Significance:** This work resolves an open empirical question from [25] and provides a theoretically grounded recommendation for practitioners (latent approach preferred for noisy tensor decomposition). The framework extends to novel tensor norms, opening future research avenues.

**Weaknesses (addressable in revision):**  
1. **Minor justification gaps:** In Section 4, the authors state they "ignore terms like √nk/N" when defining TR/LR complexity. This should be explicitly justified (e.g., "These terms are negligible for nk ≈ 50 and N ≈ 50,000 as observed in experiments"). Without this, the claim that TR/LR complexity "equivalently" capture scaling is slightly abrupt.  
2. **Application context:** The neuroimaging example in the introduction (Section 1) is underdeveloped. A brief mention of how the minimal-rank dependence (Theorem 3) specifically benefits real-world scenarios (e.g., when one mode (e.g., time) is low-rank but others are not) would strengthen significance.  
3. **Notational consistency:** The paper switches between "mode-k rank" (e.g., rk) and "multilinear rank" (r1,...,rK) without always clarifying the distinction (e.g., in Theorem 2, rk is the mode-k rank of W*, but r̄k is the rank of the k-th component). A sentence clarifying this in Section 3.2 would improve precision.

**Overall assessment:**  
This is a strong contribution to the tensor decomposition literature with clear theoretical and empirical advances. The paper meets all NIPS criteria:  
- **Quality:** Rigorous proofs, comprehensive experiments, and honest discussion of limitations (e.g., incoherence assumptions in Theorem 2).  
- **Clarity:** Well-written, with minor notational tweaks needed (addressed above).  
- **Originality:** The duality result and performance analysis (Theorems 2–3) are novel extensions of [25, 26], distinct from prior NIPS work (e.g., [19, 25]).  
- **Significance:** Resolves a key practical question for tensor decomposition, with implications for collaborative filtering, neuroimaging, and missing-data imputation. The theoretical guarantees (e.g., minimal-rank dependence) are broadly applicable.

**Recommendation:**  
**Accept**. The paper is technically excellent, addresses an important open problem, and provides both theoretical and empirical evidence for the superiority of the latent approach. The weaknesses noted are minor and easily addressable, and the work aligns well with NIPS’ focus on rigorous, impactful machine learning methods. The authors’ framework and results will be valuable for the community.
