

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, focusing on the theoretical comparison between the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The main claim is that the latent approach, which decomposes a tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode, theoretically outperforms the overlapped approach when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific unknown mode. The authors prove that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank, while the overlapped approach depends on the average of mode-k ranks.

The paper makes several significant contributions. First, it establishes a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing results from structured sparsity literature. Second, it provides rigorous theoretical analysis showing the consistency of the latent approach and deriving error bounds that depend on the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average, explaining the empirical superiority observed in prior work. Third, the numerical experiments confirm that the theoretical predictions match the observed scaling behavior of mean squared error.

The theoretical results are well-supported with rigorous proofs (presented in supplementary material) and the experiments are well-designed to validate the theoretical claims. The paper demonstrates strong field knowledge, with comprehensive references to relevant literature on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity. The connection to structured sparsity is particularly compelling and elevates the work beyond a narrow technical contribution.

The main limitation is that the analysis is restricted to noisy tensor decomposition, while previous work [25, 26] focused on noise-less tensor completion. The paper acknowledges this limitation but could have more explicitly discussed the computational complexity of the latent approach compared to the overlapped approach, which is important for practical adoption.

The paper is technically sound, clearly written, and makes a significant contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition methods. The results are not only theoretically interesting but also provide practical guidance for practitioners in choosing between these approaches. The work is original, building on recent advances while providing deeper theoretical insights that explain empirical observations. The paper advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition by establishing a precise theoretical understanding of when the latent approach is preferable.

This paper meets all the criteria for a high-quality NIPS submission: it is technically sound, clearly written, novel, and significant. The theoretical results are well-supported by both rigorous proofs and empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
