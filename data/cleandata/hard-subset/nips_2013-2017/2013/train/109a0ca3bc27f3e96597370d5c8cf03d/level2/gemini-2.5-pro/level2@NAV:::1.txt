This paper introduces a general framework of structured Schatten norms for tensors, which unifies two recent convex relaxations for tensor decomposition: the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The central contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach in a denoising setting. The authors prove that its mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, which scales with the average of the mode-k ranks. This provides a clear theoretical justification for the empirically observed superiority of the latent method, particularly when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one unknown mode. The work is supported by a novel duality result connecting these norms and is validated by numerical simulations.

The paper’s strengths are numerous, and it meets the criteria for acceptance at NIPS.
*   **Quality & Originality:** The theoretical contributions are significant and technically sound. The unification under a structured norm framework and the novel duality result are elegant contributions to the literature on both tensor methods and structured sparsity. The main performance bound for the latent approach (Theorem 3) is a novel and important result that fundamentally clarifies the relationship between the two main convex approaches to this problem.
*   **Significance:** The findings are of high practical and theoretical importance. By proving that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the lowest-rank mode, the authors provide clear guidance to practitioners on which regularizer to prefer. This work significantly advances the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition, building directly upon prior work presented at NIPS.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. The motivation is clear, the theoretical results are stated precisely, and the connection to prior work is well-established. The experimental section effectively corroborates the theoretical claims.

The primary weakness is a limited discussion of the assumptions needed for the main results. The tighter performance bound in Theorem 2 relies on an incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and additional constraints in the optimization formulation (Eq. 11). While the authors note such assumptions are common, the paper would be stronger with a brief discussion on their practical implications. For instance, guidance on setting the parameter α or an analysis of the model's sensitivity to it would be beneficial.

Despite this minor point, the paper presents a complete and important piece of research. The theoretical insights are strong, well-supported, and will likely influence future work in this area. I strongly recommend acceptance.
