### Summary

This paper presents a theoretical analysis of a convex approach for low-rank tensor decomposition, termed the "latent approach," which was previously proposed and observed to be empirically effective. The authors introduce a general framework of structured Schatten norms, which includes both the "latent" norm and the more commonly studied "overlapped" norm (where all matrix unfoldings are simultaneously penalized). A key contribution is a novel duality result connecting these two classes of norms, which elegantly links this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity.

The main theoretical result of the paper is a performance bound for the latent approach in a tensor denoising setting. The authors prove that the mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. This contrasts sharply with the existing analysis for the overlapped approach, where the error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This finding provides a rigorous theoretical explanation for why the latent approach is superior when the true tensor is low-rank in only one of its modes. The theoretical results are supported by numerical experiments that confirm the predicted scaling behaviors for both the latent and overlapped methods.

### Quality

The paper is of high technical quality. The theoretical development is sound and builds upon established techniques in high-dimensional statistics. The introduction of the generalized structured Schatten norms and the proof of their duality (Lemma 1) is an elegant and valuable contribution. The subsequent derivation of the error bounds for the latent estimator (Theorems 2 and 3) is rigorous. The key insight, that the latent norm's complexity is governed by the minimum of the mode-k ranks (Lemma 2), is well-exploited to produce a tight and meaningful bound.

The experimental evaluation is also well-executed. It is not merely a demonstration of performance but is carefully designed to verify the specific scaling laws predicted by the theory. By plotting the empirical MSE against the theoretically derived complexity measures ("TR complexity" and "LR complexity"), the authors provide strong evidence for the correctness of their analysis. The authors are also honest about the limitations and conditions of their results, such as the need for an incoherence assumption.

### Clarity

The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The problem statement is clear, and the authors do an excellent job of positioning their work relative to prior art, particularly the "overlapped" approach analyzed in [26]. The definitions in Section 2 are precise, and the progression of ideas from the norm definitions and duality to the main performance bounds is logical. The comparison in Section 3.4, where the new bound (15) is explicitly contrasted with the old bound (16), is particularly effective at highlighting the paper's core message. The figures are clear and support the main claims effectively.

One minor point of clarification could be beneficial. The main theoretical result (Theorem 2) relies on a constrained optimization problem (11) which includes an incoherence parameter `alpha`. It would be helpful to explicitly state whether the numerical experiments in Section 4 were performed using this constrained formulation and, if so, how `alpha` was chosen. While the use of such assumptions and constraints is standard in the literature, a brief comment on their practical implications would strengthen the paper.

### Originality

The paper makes several original contributions. While the "latent" optimization problem (5) was proposed in [25], no theoretical analysis of its performance existed. This paper provides the first rigorous analysis, which is a significant and novel contribution. The generalization of overlapped and latent norms to a structured Schatten norm framework is also original and provides a unifying perspective. The duality result in Lemma 1 appears to be new for tensors and provides a valuable connection to the well-studied vector case in structured sparsity. Finally, the key result establishing the `min(r_k)` dependency is novel and provides a fundamental insight into the behavior of different convex relaxations for tensor decomposition.

### Significance

The results presented in this paper are highly significant for both researchers and practitioners working with tensor data. By theoretically demonstrating that the latent approach adapts to the best (lowest-rank) mode of the tensor, the paper provides clear and actionable guidance on which convex relaxation to use for tensor decomposition problems. It explains why one method can drastically outperform another and gives users a reason to prefer the latent approach, especially when there is no prior knowledge that the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes. This work substantially advances the theoretical understanding of convex methods for tensor decomposition and is likely to be influential in future research on this topic. The connections made to the structured sparsity literature may also inspire further cross-pollination of ideas.

### Pros:
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical performance analysis for the latent tensor decomposition approach.
*   The main result, showing that the error scales with `min(r_k)`, is clean, important, and provides a clear theoretical justification for preferring the latent method.
*   Introduces an elegant and unifying framework of structured Schatten norms and proves a novel duality result.
*   The paper is very clearly written, and its claims are well-supported by both theory and targeted experiments.

### Cons:
*   The theoretical analysis relies on an incoherence assumption and a constrained formulation, and the practical implications of choosing the constraint parameter `alpha` are not discussed.

Overall, this is an excellent paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses. I strongly recommend acceptance.
