- Core contribution: theoretical analysis showing the latent convex relaxation performs as well as knowing the mode with smallest rank when the true tensor is low-rank in one unknown mode.  
- Key technical result: novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, generalizing structured sparsity results.  
- Empirical support: numerical simulations confirm the predicted mean-squared-error scaling.  
- Motivation and positioning: problem of model selection in tensor decomposition is well-motivated and clearly situated in convex optimization and tensor literature.  
- Clarity: definitions, notation, and links to related work (especially structured sparsity) are clear and effective.  
- Rigour: theoretical derivations, consistency results, and performance bounds are technically sound and well-presented.  
- Comparative advantage: theory and experiments convincingly show latent approach outperforms overlapped approach when tensor is low-rank in only one mode.  
- Generality: connection to structured sparsity increases the broader applicability of the results.  
- Experiment limitations: numerical experiments are limited in tensor sizes and rank configurations and could be expanded for robustness checks.  
- Constant factors: the analysis does not investigate constant factors in scaling predictions.  
- Regularization choice: experiments lack a systematic justification or study of the regularization parameter’s impact.  
- Related work: discussion of connections to lower bounds by Mu et al. [19] and the “dirty model” (Hsu et al. [12]) is insufficiently detailed.  
- Method dependence: reliance on ADMM-based optimization is noted; applicability of other solvers is not discussed.  
- Discussion gaps: limitations of the analysis and directions for future work could be expanded.  
- Acceptance case: strong theoretical contribution, novel duality result, supporting experiments, clear writing, and links to structured sparsity argue for acceptance.  
- Rejection concerns: insufficiently comprehensive experiments, unclear regularization impact, and incomplete discussion of limitations argue against acceptance.
