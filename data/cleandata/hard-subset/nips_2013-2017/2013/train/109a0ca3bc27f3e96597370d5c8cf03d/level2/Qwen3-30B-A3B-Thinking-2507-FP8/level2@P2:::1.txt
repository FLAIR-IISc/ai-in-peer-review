

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, focusing on the duality between "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors establish that the latent approach, which decomposes a tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode, theoretically outperforms the overlapped approach (which penalizes all modes to be jointly low-rank) when the underlying tensor is low-rank in a specific mode. Their key theoretical contribution is a duality result showing that the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm, and they derive error bounds showing the latent approach's error scales with the minimum mode-k rank, while the overlapped approach's error scales with the average mode-k rank.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, the duality result (Lemma 1) is elegant and generalizes results from structured sparsity literature, providing a valuable theoretical connection between tensor decomposition and group sparsity. Second, the theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-3) is rigorous, with clear error bounds that explain the empirical superiority of the latent approach. Third, the numerical experiments (Section 4) convincingly confirm the theoretical predictions about scaling behavior of mean squared error with respect to tensor dimensions and ranks.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized, with appropriate mathematical notation and sufficient background. The connection to prior work on structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped and latent group lasso) is well-made, though it could be strengthened by more explicitly contrasting their duality result with existing group sparsity duality results.

However, the paper has some notable weaknesses. The main theoretical contribution (the duality result) is not sufficiently distinguished from prior work. The structured Schatten norms themselves were introduced in [25] (which the authors cite), and the paper doesn't clearly state what is novel about their duality result beyond extending it to tensors. The connection to structured sparsity literature is mentioned but not deeply explored, making it difficult to assess the paper's originality in this context.

The experimental section is limited to synthetic data and doesn't explore more complex scenarios where the latent approach might fail. While the paper claims the latent approach performs better "in many cases," it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when the overlapped approach might still be preferable. The paper also doesn't address computational complexity differences between the two approaches, which could be significant for practical applications.

Regarding significance, the theoretical explanation for the latent approach's empirical success is valuable for the tensor decomposition community, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate how much better the latent approach is in practical applications. The claim that "for noisy tensor decomposition, the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach" would be stronger with more extensive empirical comparisons on real-world datasets.

The paper would be improved by:
1. More clearly distinguishing the novelty of the duality result from prior work in structured sparsity
2. Providing a more thorough analysis of when each approach is preferable
3. Including experiments on real-world data to demonstrate practical significance
4. Discussing computational trade-offs between the two approaches

Overall, this is a technically sound paper with solid theoretical contributions that advance our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The duality result is interesting and useful, and the theoretical analysis is rigorous. However, the paper's novelty is somewhat limited by the fact that the structured Schatten norms themselves were already introduced, and the main contribution is a generalization of existing results to tensors. The empirical evidence is convincing for the specific setting they study, but the broader significance for practitioners is somewhat limited by the synthetic nature of their experiments.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the theoretical understanding of tensor decomposition approaches, particularly in explaining the empirical success of the latent approach. It advances the field by providing a precise theoretical framework for comparing these two approaches, but would be stronger with a clearer articulation of its novelty and broader practical implications. The paper meets the technical standards of NIPS, but the significance could be enhanced with additional empirical validation on real-world datasets.
