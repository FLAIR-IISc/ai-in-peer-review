This paper studies convex norm-regularized optimization for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, focusing on the latent Schatten norm and contrasting it with the overlapped norm. The authors derive mean-squared-error upper bounds for latent-norm regularization under several noise models (with a Gaussian specialization), showing that the error scales with the minimum mode rank rather than the average square-root rank typical of overlapped analyses. They confirm the scaling via simulations. Beyond this, they propose a broader family of structured Schatten Sp/q norms and prove a duality theorem that generalizes the overlapped/latent duality known in structured sparsity (e.g., latent/overlapped group lasso). The work connects to prior convex tensor decomposition and theory at NIPS and elsewhere (e.g., Liu et al.; Tomioka et al.; Signoretto et al.; decomposable regularizers by Negahban et al.; lower bounds by Mu et al.).

Quality: The paper appears technically careful and sound. The proofs and conditions are clear, and the numerical experiments align with the theory. However, only upper bounds are provided; the absence of matching lower bounds limits the sharpness of comparisons and the claimed optimality of the latent approach.

Clarity: Generally well written and easy to follow. Minor issues: Figure 1 appears before the reader has the tools to interpret it; there are a few typos/awkward phrasings. The abstract overstates the practical contribution of the “new class” of norms, as the generalization is not accompanied by algorithmic development or empirical characterization.

Originality: The duality statement is neat but conceptually aligned with existing structured sparsity dualities. The analysis leverages standard techniques; the main qualitative conclusion (latent helps when one mode is low-rank) is not surprising.

Significance: Useful to researchers working on convex formulations for tensor problems, clarifying when latent norms are preferable. Impact for the broader ML community seems limited: the paper is narrow and technical, with no real-data applications or ML tasks, and only denoising simulations.

Pros for acceptance:
- Solid, careful theoretical analysis with clear takeaways.
- Clean duality result linking norm families.
- Empirical confirmation of theoretically predicted scaling.

Cons for acceptance:
- Narrow scope and peripheral to mainstream NIPS topics; no ML applications.
- Incremental novelty; unsurprising conclusions.
- Only upper bounds; no lower bounds or algorithmic advances.
- Abstract wording could mislead about practical contributions of the generalized norms.
- Minor presentation issues (figure placement, typos).

Suggestions: Add experiments on real ML tasks (tensor completion, recommender systems, neuroimaging), discuss computational trade-offs and scalability, provide guidance on λ, clarify assumptions (incoherence), temper the abstract, and, if possible, include lower bounds or tighter comparisons. Overall, a solid but specialized contribution.
