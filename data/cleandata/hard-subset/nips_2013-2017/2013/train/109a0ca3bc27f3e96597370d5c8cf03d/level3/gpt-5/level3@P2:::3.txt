Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper studies convex tensor decomposition with structured Schatten norms and provides new theory and insights favoring the “latent” approach over the “overlapped” approach. The authors define an overlapped Sp/q-norm that penalizes all mode unfoldings simultaneously and a latent Sp/q-norm that decomposes the tensor into a sum of mode-specific low-rank components. A central contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q is the latent Sp*/q* norm, generalizing dualities known in structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs latent group lasso). Building on this, the paper analyzes the estimator using the latent S1/1 norm for denoising and recovery, proving:

- A general consistency bound scaling with min_k n_k when the regularization λ exceeds the overlapped S∞/∞ norm of the noise.
- Deterministic and Gaussian-noise error bounds that depend on the minimal mode-k rank of the true tensor (or on the sum of latent factor ranks under an incoherence condition), rather than on averages of ranks, as in prior work for the overlapped norm.
- Comparisons against prior bounds for the overlapped approach (e.g., Tomioka et al. NIPS’11/’12) show strictly better dependence: latent achieves an error scaling with min_k r_k, whereas overlapped scales with the average of sqrt(r_k).

The analysis leverages decomposability techniques (Agarwal/Negahban/Wainwright, NIPS’09; Ann. Stat. 2012) and tail bounds for spectral norms, and connects to recent lower bounds for tensor recovery (Mu et al., arXiv:1307.5870). Experiments on synthetic tensors confirm the predicted scaling for both methods and highlight the latent method’s advantage, particularly when the tensor is low rank in only one mode.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled definition of structured Schatten norms and an elegant duality between overlapped and latent variants; this is of independent interest for structured sparsity beyond tensors.
- Technically solid analysis with multiple regimes: generic consistency, deterministic bounds under incoherence, and Gaussian-noise high-probability bounds.
- Theoretical improvement is concrete and interpretable: dependence on min mode rank rather than averages explains empirical behavior and offers guidance on method choice.
- Well-organized and clearly written; proofs appear sound and the main ideas are easy to follow.
- Experiments, though limited, align well with the theory and illustrate scaling with problem size and ranks.

Weaknesses:
- Experimental evaluation is brief and lacks critical baselines: no comparison to standard non-convex tensor decompositions (ALS/HOOI for Tucker, CP/PARAFAC), which are widely used in practice.
- Missing detail on experimental setup: how Tucker ranks (r1, …, rK) were chosen or varied, how regularization λ and the incoherence parameter α were selected, and any sensitivity analysis.
- No real-data experiments; only synthetic denoising is shown, and no tensor completion setup, which is a key application domain for these norms.
- The paper mentions S1/∞ and related norms but does not explore their behavior, especially when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes; a short theoretical or empirical comparison would strengthen the narrative.
- Practical guidance on setting α (the spectral norm constraints among components) and its impact on errors is limited.

Quality:
- The technical results appear correct and thoughtfully derived. The duality lemma is clean and well motivated. The bounds are consistent with decomposable regularizer analyses and improve upon prior overlapped-norm results. Assumptions (Gaussian noise; incoherence via spectral norm cross-bounds) are standard but could be further discussed for realism. Constants and dependence on dimensions are explicit; the role of λ is clear and does not require knowledge of ranks.

Clarity:
- The paper is generally very clear and well-structured. Definitions (unfoldings, ranks, norms) are precise. The progression from definitions to duality to bounds is logical. Suggestions:
  - Briefly sketch key proof ideas for Theorem 2/3 in the main text to aid intuition.
  - Make explicit how α is set in experiments and discuss robustness to its misspecification.
  - Provide a concise “practical recipe” for choosing λ as a function of N, n_k, σ.

Originality:
- The dual characterization of overlapped vs latent Schatten norms is novel and elegant, generalizing known results in structured sparsity to tensors. The performance guarantees for the latent approach with min-rank dependence (and the observation that it adapts to the best mode without prior knowledge) are new and meaningful improvements over existing overlapped-norm analyses.

Significance:
- The work addresses an important and active area (convex methods for tensor estimation) and provides actionable insights on when to prefer latent over overlapped norms. The duality result will likely be reused in other structured settings. While experiments could be stronger, the theoretical contribution is substantial and advances the state of the art.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Strong, original theory with clear improvement over prior bounds (min rank vs average sqrt rank).
- Useful duality result of independent interest.
- Good clarity and organization; results are well-motivated and connected to prior work.
- Synthetic experiments corroborate theory and offer practical guidance on λ.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical section is limited: no non-convex baselines, no real data, and incomplete methodological details (rank choices, α, λ selection).
- Limited exploration of alternative norms (e.g., S1/∞) and of the regime where the tensor is low-rank across all modes.

Questions for the authors:
- How were (r1, …, rK) chosen across experiments? Please provide a systematic grid and report it.
- How were λ and α selected in practice? Any data-driven procedure or sensitivity analysis?
- Can you include comparisons to ALS/HOOI and CP on the same synthetic setups and on at least one real dataset?
- Do your bounds suggest any advantage for overlapped S1/∞ or other Sp/q choices when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank? Any preliminary empirical results?

Minor comments:
- Fix small typos in references (e.g., “prepint” → “preprint”, “Prof. ICCV” → “Proc. ICCV”).
- Clarify the computational complexity of the ADMM solver and runtime vs tensor size.

Recommendation: Weak accept. The theoretical contributions are strong and relevant; modest extensions to the experiments and added methodological detail would make this a solid accept.
