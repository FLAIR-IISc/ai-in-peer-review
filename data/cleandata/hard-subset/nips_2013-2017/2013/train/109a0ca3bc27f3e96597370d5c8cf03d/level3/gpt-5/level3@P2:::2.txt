Summary and relation to prior work
The paper introduces and analyzes a class of structured Schatten norms for tensors, focusing on two instantiations: (i) the “overlapped” Schatten norm, which simultaneously penalizes the nuclear norms of all mode unfoldings, and (ii) the “latent” Schatten norm, which decomposes a tensor into a sum of components, each constrained to be low-rank in a single mode. The authors establish a duality between these two families (overlapped Sp/q has latent Sp*/q* as dual), generalizing known relationships for overlapped/latent group sparsity. Building on this, they study the denoising performance of the latent approach and prove bounds showing that, when the underlying tensor is low-rank in an unknown single mode, the latent approach achieves error scaling with the minimum mode-rank, essentially matching the performance one would obtain if the low-rank mode were known. They further provide consistency guarantees, Gaussian-noise risk bounds, and empirical evidence that corroborates the predicted scaling, and they compare to prior bounds for the overlapped approach, which depend on averages of the mode ranks.

Relation to prior work is appropriate and includes nonconvex Tucker/HOSVD methods [De Lathauwer et al.; Kolda & Bader], convex tensor recovery [Liu et al.; Signoretto et al.; Gandy et al.; Recht et al.], structured sparsity and the “dirty model” [Jalali et al.; Obozinski et al.], general decomposable-regularizer theory [Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.], and lower bounds for tensor recovery [Mu et al.]. It would help to more clearly position the contribution relative to HOSVD approximation guarantees (please cite Kolda & Bader’s survey for accessible background), emphasizing that your convex latent-Schatten analysis provides explicit statistical denoising rates and a novel duality.

Motivation and accessibility for ML audiences
To broaden appeal and provide intuition, I suggest adding a simple latent-variable motivation: include a graphical model with hidden blocks h1,…,hK mapping linearly to observed x via mode-specific factors (e.g., x ≈ (A1 h1) ⊗ (A2 h2) ⊗ …, or equivalently, the observed cross-moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition with factor matrices Ak). Explain that the multilinear ranks equal the dimensions of the hidden blocks, and that in many applications only some hidden groups are low-dimensional. Your main result then reads as: the latent approach adapts to this structure and its error scales with the smallest hidden-block dimension (minimum mode-rank), without knowing which block is small. A figure of the graphical model and a short tutorial subsection would significantly improve accessibility.

Quality
The technical development is largely sound and important. The duality lemma is elegant and broadly useful. The deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds for the latent approach are clear in structure and align with the experiments. However, several points require clarification and/or completion:
- Theorem 2: state explicitly that the latent components are identifiable only up to permutation and that the induced decomposition of Ŵ is non-unique. Clarify precisely what “recovered components” means.
- Theorem 2: the transition from the componentwise bound (12) to the overall bound (13) is not immediate. Please justify rigorously why the singleton decomposition argument is valid under your constraints and how it yields the min-k rk dependence; spell out the steps.
- Lemma 5 is central to Theorem 2 but its proof is missing; please provide it. Similarly, for Lemma 4, include a self-contained proof or at least a detailed sketch rather than only citing prior work, because your setting has tensor-specific structure and constraints.
- Incoherence discussion after (10): the comparison to incoherence conditions in [Agarwal et al., Jalali et al., Hsu et al.] is potentially misleading because your spectral-norm cross-mode constraints play a different role; either carefully delineate the relationship or remove that comparison.
- Algorithmic aspects: you employ ADMM with repeated SVDs of mode-k unfoldings. Please provide a brief complexity discussion (per-iteration cost and memory, scaling with modes, and practical sizes handled), and note any convergence/termination criteria.

Clarity
The paper is generally well written, but several notational and editorial issues hinder readability:
- Use clear symbols for the latent components (e.g., U(k) rather than Ŵ(k) with superscripts). The current superscript notation for components is confusing.
- Make the notation for mode-k rank consistent (avoid mixing r̄k and r̲k; consider a single function like mult_rankk(·)). Also, clearly distinguish the multilinear ranks of W∗ from the ranks of the latent factors in (9).
- State early that “mode-k rank” is the multilinear rank notion, and mention that multiple tensor rank notions exist (CP rank vs. Tucker/multilinear rank).
- Fix the abstract typo: “structures Schatten” → “structured Schatten.”
- Correct the missing parenthesis noted on the last line of page 12 of the supplementary material.
- Theorem statements: ensure all assumptions (on λ, α, dimensional ordering, etc.) are stated inline where used.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms for tensors appears new and is a valuable conceptual contribution, connecting tensor regularization to broader structured sparsity theory.
- The error bounds for the latent approach that depend on the minimal mode-rank (and show adaptivity to an unknown favorable mode) are, to my knowledge, new in this convex denoising setting and go beyond prior analyses of the overlapped approach.
- The empirical validation of the precise scaling versus latent-rank complexity is a nice complement to the theory.

Significance
The results are significant for practitioners who face noisy tensor estimation, providing actionable guidance: the latent formulation can strictly dominate the overlapped one, especially when only some modes are truly low-rank. The duality result may spur new algorithms and analyses for other structured tensor norms. The work is also timely given growing interest in high-order data in ML.

Pros
- Clear, principled analysis of latent Schatten regularization with bounds adapting to min mode-rank.
- Useful duality result connecting overlapped and latent tensor norms.
- Careful empirical validation matching theoretical scaling.
- Relevance to a range of ML applications involving multiway data.

Cons
- Missing/insufficient proofs (Lemma 5; Lemma 4 sketch), and a key step (12)→(13) needs justification.
- Identifiability/permutation issues around components not discussed.
- Incoherence discussion may mislead; clarify or remove.
- Notational inconsistencies and minor typos.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and practical scalability.

Requested revisions and suggestions
1) Add a motivating latent-variable graphical model and short tutorial explanation tying multilinear ranks to hidden-variable dimensions; highlight that only some hidden variables may be small and that your results exploit this.
2) Theorem 2: clarify component identifiability; justify rigorously the derivation of (13) from (12).
3) Provide the missing proof of Lemma 5 and a clear proof or detailed sketch for Lemma 4.
4) Expand related work on HOSVD and approximation bounds; cite Kolda & Bader’s survey prominently in Section 4 and the introduction.
5) Clarify/remove the incoherence comparison; precisely state your assumptions and how they differ from [1,3,11].
6) Improve notation for components and ranks; unify symbols; fix the noted parenthesis and abstract typo.
7) Briefly discuss computational complexity and scalability of the ADMM solver.

Overall evaluation
This is a strong and timely paper with substantive theoretical and empirical contributions. Provided the authors address the technical clarifications and improve exposition as above, I recommend acceptance.
