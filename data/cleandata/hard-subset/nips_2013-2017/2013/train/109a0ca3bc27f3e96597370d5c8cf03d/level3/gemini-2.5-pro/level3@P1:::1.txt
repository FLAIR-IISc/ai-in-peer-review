This paper studies convex optimization approaches for low-rank tensor decomposition, focusing on two methods based on structured Schatten norm regularization: the "overlapped" approach and the "latent" approach. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, which was previously shown empirically to outperform the overlapped method in certain settings [25] but lacked theoretical justification. The authors derive mean squared error (MSE) bounds for the latent estimator, demonstrating that its performance scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor. This contrasts with existing analyses of the overlapped approach [26], whose error scales with the sum or average of the mode-k ranks. This key result provides a clear theoretical explanation for the superiority of the latent approach when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

As a secondary contribution, the paper introduces a generalized framework of "overlapped Sp/q" and "latent Sp*/q*" norms for tensors. Within this framework, the authors prove a novel duality theorem connecting these two classes of norms, which generalizes similar results from the structured sparsity literature for vectors [1, 12, 17, 21]. Finally, the paper presents numerical simulations on synthetic data that confirm the theoretical scaling predictions for the MSE of the latent approach.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by proofs provided in the supplementary material, and the derivation appears correct. The work represents a complete contribution, including a theoretical framework (the duality result), a specific analysis (the MSE bounds for the latent S1/1 norm), and experimental validation. The authors are careful in their evaluation, and the numerical simulations in Section 4 provide convincing evidence that their theory correctly predicts the scaling behavior of the MSE.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized, making it generally easy to follow for a reader familiar with the topic. The distinction between the overlapped and latent norms is clearly explained.
A minor suggestion for improvement would be to move Figure 1 to a later section. In its current position in the introduction, it appears before the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches are formally defined, which makes it difficult for the reader to fully interpret the comparison on a first pass. Additionally, a proofreading pass could correct a few minor typos and instances of awkward phrasing.

**Originality:** The main original contribution is the specific theoretical analysis of the latent tensor decomposition method. While this fills an important gap in the literature, the analytical techniques themselves seem to be a careful but relatively straightforward application of existing frameworks for analyzing M-estimators with decomposable regularizers (e.g., [1]) to this specific tensor problem. The generalization to Sp/q norms and the corresponding duality lemma are elegant theoretical results, but they represent an extension of known duality relationships in the vector-based structured sparsity literature to the tensor setting. Therefore, the novelty of the core methodology is somewhat limited. The abstract's claim of studying a "new class of structured Schatten norms" is also slightly misleading, as the paper's analysis and experiments are confined to the S1/1 case, and the generalized framework is only used to establish the duality result.

**Significance:** The significance of this work for the broader NIPS community is the main point of concern. The results are of a technical nature and will likely be of interest to the specialized community of researchers working on the theory and optimization of tensor methods. However, the paper's focus is narrow. It does not connect the theoretical findings back to practical machine learning problems, either by analyzing implications for learning guarantees in applications like collaborative filtering or multi-task learning, or by performing experiments on real-world datasets. This lack of connection to a machine learning context limits the paper's potential impact and makes it feel more suited to a signal processing or optimization venue. Furthermore, while the paper provides upper bounds on the estimation error, the absence of matching lower bounds makes it difficult to judge the optimality of the latent approach. We can conclude it is better than the overlapped method in certain regimes, but not whether it is the best possible. This weakens the overall strength of the algorithmic comparison.

### Arguments for Acceptance:
1.  Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis for the "latent" approach to convex tensor decomposition, explaining its empirically observed advantages.
2.  The paper is technically solid, with clear proofs and well-executed experiments that validate the theoretical claims.
3.  The duality result between generalized overlapped and latent Schatten norms is an elegant theoretical contribution that connects this work to the broader structured sparsity literature.

### Arguments for Rejection:
1.  The significance and potential impact on the NIPS community are limited. The work is highly theoretical and does not demonstrate its relevance to machine learning applications.
2.  The novelty is somewhat incremental. The analysis applies existing high-dimensional statistics techniques to a new problem, and the duality result extends known concepts from vectors to tensors.
3.  The lack of lower bounds makes the comparison between algorithms less conclusive; it is unclear if the analyzed method is optimal.
4.  The work feels more aligned with a specialized journal on optimization or information theory than a broad-scope machine learning conference like NIPS.
