Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization

ABSTRACT
We study a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors that includes two recently proposed norms (“overlapped” and “latent”) for convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition. We analyze the performance of the “latent” approach for tensor decomposition, which was empirically found to perform better than the “overlapped” approach in some settings. We show theoretically that this is indeed the case. In particular, when the unknown true tensor is low-rank in a specific unknown mode, this approach performs as well as knowing the mode with the smallest rank. Along the way, we show a novel duality result for structured Schatten norms, which is also interesting in the general context of structured sparsity. We confirm through numerical simulations that our theory can precisely predict the scaling behaviour of the mean squared error.

1 Introduction
Decomposition of tensors [10, 14] (or multi-way arrays) into low-rank components arises naturally in many real-world data analysis problems. For example, in neuroimaging, spatio-temporal patterns of neural activities related to experimental conditions can be found via tensor decomposition of data tensors. More generally, multivariate spatio-temporal data (e.g., environmental monitoring) can be modeled as tensors. Low-rank modeling enables imputation of missing values, and tensor modelling is valuable for collaborative filtering with temporal or contextual dimensions. 

Traditionally, tensor decomposition has relied on non-convex optimization (alternate least squares, higher-order orthogonal iteration [6]). While empirically successful, theoretical understanding remains limited. De Lathauwer et al. [5] provided approximation bounds for truncated higher-order SVD (Tucker decomposition), but generalization performance remains an open problem. Model selection is challenging, especially for the Tucker model, due to the K hyperparameters required for a K-way tensor. 

Recently, convex-optimization-based approaches [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] have emerged. These approaches, utilizing the Schatten 1-norm (trace norm, nuclear norm [7, 22, 24]), avoid specifying the rank beforehand, automatically determining it via the norm’s low-rank inducing property. 

However, the initial “overlapped” approach [9, 15, 23, 25] struggles when the tensor is only low-rank in a specific mode. The “latent” approach [25] addresses this by decomposing the tensor into a mixture of tensors, each low-rank in a specific mode.  Figure 1 demonstrates the latent approach’s superiority when the underlying tensor is almost full-rank in all but one mode. This paper provides the first theoretical analysis supporting this empirical success.

We rigorously study the latent approach, showing its mean squared error scales no greater than the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor. This explains the latent approach’s improved performance. Along the way, we establish a novel duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, a result relevant to structured sparsity.  We empirically validate our theory, demonstrating accurate prediction of the scaling behavior of the mean squared error.

2 Structured Schatten norms for tensors
Let W ∈ Rn1×···nK be a K-way tensor. The dot product is ⟨W,X⟩ = vec(W)⊤vec(X), and the Frobenius norm is ∣∣∣∣∣∣W∣∣∣∣∣∣F =√⟨W,W⟩. A mode k is a tensor's dimensionality. The mode-k unfolding W(k) ∈ Rnk×N/nk is obtained by concatenating mode-k fibers along columns. The mode-k rank rk is the rank of W(k). A tensor W has multilinear rank (r1, . . . , rK) if the mode-k rank is rk for all k [14]. Multiple tensor rank notions exist; we will use “mode-rank” and “multilinear rank” interchangeably.

The results confirm that the mean squared error scales linearly with the LR complexity. Optimal regularization constants are largely independent of tensor rank.

5 Conclusion
This work presents a framework for structured Schatten norms, providing theoretical guarantees for convex tensor decomposition using the latent approach. Future work includes analyzing tensor completion and exploring extensions of the proposed norms.
