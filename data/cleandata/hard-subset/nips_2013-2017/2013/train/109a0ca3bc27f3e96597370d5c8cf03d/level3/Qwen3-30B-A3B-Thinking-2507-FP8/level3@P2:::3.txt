

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper addresses the problem of convex tensor decomposition, specifically comparing two approaches: the "overlapped" approach (which penalizes all modes to be jointly low-rank) and the "latent" approach (which decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach when the underlying tensor is low-rank in a specific mode, with the error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. They establish a novel duality between the two types of structured Schatten norms, which generalizes results in structured sparsity literature.

The paper builds upon recent work on convex tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], extending the theoretical understanding of these approaches. It connects these tensor decomposition methods to the broader literature on structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21], particularly the overlapped and latent group lasso frameworks, providing a unified perspective.

**Quality**: The theoretical contributions are strong and well-supported. The paper establishes a novel duality result for structured Schatten norms (Lemma 1) that is interesting in its own right and has implications for structured sparsity. The main theorems (Theorems 1-3) are rigorously proven and provide meaningful performance guarantees. The experimental validation confirms the theoretical predictions about the scaling behavior of the mean squared error. However, the experimental section is somewhat limited in scope. The authors do not compare their convex approaches to non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., Tucker decomposition or higher-order SVD), which would provide valuable context for practitioners. Additionally, the paper does not analyze the behavior of group regularization approaches (e.g., l_{1,∞}) when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes, which would strengthen the comparison between methods.

**Clarity**: The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The mathematical notation is consistent, and the structure flows logically from problem definition to theoretical results to experimental validation. The connection between the tensor decomposition problem and structured sparsity is clearly articulated. The supplementary material provides necessary proofs, making the paper sufficiently detailed for experts to reproduce the results. The only minor clarity issue is that the experimental section does not specify how the Tucker ranks (r1,...,rK) were chosen for the simulations, which would help readers understand the experimental setup better.

**Originality**: The paper makes several original contributions. The dual characterization of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1) is novel and of independent interest to the structured sparsity community. The theoretical analysis showing that the latent approach performs as well as knowing the mode with the smallest rank (Theorem 3) significantly advances the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. While the overall framework builds on recent work, the specific theoretical results and connections to structured sparsity represent meaningful new contributions.

**Significance**: Tensor decomposition is an important problem with applications in neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, and environmental monitoring. The paper provides a theoretically grounded improvement over existing convex approaches, with clear implications for practitioners. The finding that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the mode with the minimum rank (without needing this information) is particularly significant. The paper advances the state of the art in tensor decomposition by providing a theoretically sound approach that consistently outperforms the overlapped approach in both theoretical analysis and empirical evaluation. The results are likely to be influential for researchers working on tensor-based methods.

**Strengths**: 
1. Strong theoretical contributions with well-justified proofs
2. Novel duality result for structured Schatten norms
3. Clear connection to structured sparsity literature
4. Theoretical analysis precisely predicts scaling behavior of mean squared error
5. Well-organized and clearly written paper

**Weaknesses**:
1. Experimental section lacks comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods
2. Insufficient detail on how Tucker ranks were chosen for experiments
3. Limited analysis of group regularization behavior when tensor is low-rank in all modes
4. Brief experimental section could be expanded to include more comprehensive comparisons

**Pro and Con Acceptance**:
*Pro*: The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The duality result is interesting in its own right, and the analysis showing the latent approach's superior performance when the tensor is low-rank in a specific mode is both theoretically sound and practically important. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and makes a meaningful contribution to the field.

*Con*: The experimental section could be strengthened with more comprehensive comparisons to non-convex methods and additional details on the experimental setup. While the theoretical contributions are strong, the paper would be more impactful with a broader empirical evaluation.

Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are significant and well-supported, and the paper advances our understanding of convex approaches to tensor decomposition. While the experimental section could be expanded, the theoretical contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem with meaningful results. I recommend acceptance.
