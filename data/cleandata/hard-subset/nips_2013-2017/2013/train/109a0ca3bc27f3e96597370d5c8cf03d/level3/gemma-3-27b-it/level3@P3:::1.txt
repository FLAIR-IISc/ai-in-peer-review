This paper studies convex optimization for approximately low-rank tensor decomposition, specifically focusing on a “latent” norm and its comparison to an “overlap” norm. The authors derive theoretical MSE bounds for the latent norm under several noise models, demonstrating its advantages over the overlap norm, and validate these findings with numerical simulations. A generalization of both norms is proposed alongside a corresponding duality theorem.

The work builds upon recent efforts in convex relaxation for tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] and connects to broader research in structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21]. The core idea – exploiting the latent structure of tensors for improved decomposition – is well-motivated by the empirical observations in [25]. The theoretical analysis, establishing the consistency and performance bounds for the latent approach, is a significant contribution. The duality result for structured Schatten norms is also a valuable addition. The paper is generally well-written and technically sound; the mathematical derivations appear careful and correct. The numerical experiments largely confirm the theoretical predictions.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a rigorous mathematical analysis with clear derivations and proofs (mostly relegated to the supplementary material).
* **Theoretical Contribution:** The MSE bounds for the latent norm and the comparison to the overlap norm provide valuable theoretical insight into the performance of these approaches.
* **Duality Result:** The duality theorem for structured Schatten norms is a novel and potentially useful result, with connections to existing work on structured sparsity.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical simulations support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the scaling behavior predicted by the analysis.
* **Clarity:** The paper is reasonably easy to follow, with clear definitions and explanations of the key concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Narrow Scope & Limited Significance:** While technically sound, the results are quite narrow in scope. The focus on tensor decomposition via specific norms feels somewhat disconnected from mainstream machine learning applications. The potential impact on NIPS, a conference focused on broader machine learning challenges, is limited.
* **Lack of Real-World Application:** The paper does not explore the proposed approaches in any real-world machine learning contexts, either theoretically or experimentally. The sole focus is on controlled, synthetic data.
* **Incremental Novelty:** The techniques themselves lack substantial novelty. The core ideas build directly upon existing work in convex optimization and structured sparsity. The contribution lies primarily in the analysis and comparison of existing norms.
* **Missing Lower Bounds:** The paper provides upper bounds on the MSE but does not present corresponding lower bounds. This weakens the comparison between the latent and overlap norms, as it’s unclear how close the achieved bounds are to the theoretical limits.
* **Misleading Abstract:** The abstract suggests proposing a "new class of structured Schatten norms," which is misleading. While a generalization is presented, there's no discussion of algorithms *for* this generalization or any analysis of its behavior. It remains largely a theoretical construct.
* **Presentation Issues:** Figure 1 is presented too early and lacks sufficient context for immediate interpretation. Several minor typos and phrasing errors were noted throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of terminology).

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a rigorous and technically sound analysis of a specific problem in tensor decomposition.
* The theoretical results provide valuable insights into the performance of different convex relaxation approaches.
* The duality theorem is a novel contribution with potential applications beyond tensor decomposition.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The limited scope and lack of connection to broader machine learning challenges reduce the paper’s relevance to NIPS.
* The incremental nature of the contributions and the absence of lower bounds weaken the impact of the work.
* The misleading abstract and minor presentation issues detract from the overall quality of the paper.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper is a solid piece of technical work that makes a valuable contribution to the specific area of convex tensor decomposition. However, its narrow scope, lack of real-world application, and incremental novelty make it a questionable fit for NIPS. While the technical quality is high, the paper lacks the broader significance typically expected at this conference. I would rate this as a **Weak Reject**. A stronger version of this paper, perhaps with a discussion of potential applications or a more substantial extension of the theoretical framework, could be reconsidered in the future.
