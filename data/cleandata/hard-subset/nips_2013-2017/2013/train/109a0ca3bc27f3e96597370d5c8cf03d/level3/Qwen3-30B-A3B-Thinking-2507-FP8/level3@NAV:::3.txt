

This paper introduces a novel framework for structured Schatten norms in tensor decomposition, focusing on the comparison between "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors establish theoretical bounds showing that the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific mode, with the error scaling according to the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank. A key contribution is the duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes previous work on structured sparsity (e.g., group lasso).

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical analysis is rigorous and well-structured, with clear proofs of consistency and performance bounds. The dual characterization of the norms is original and of independent interest to the structured sparsity community. The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and effectively connects tensor decomposition to broader sparsity literature.

However, the experimental evaluation is underdeveloped. The authors fail to specify how Tucker ranks (r₁,...,rₖ) were chosen for their experiments, which is critical for reproducibility. The comparison is limited to the overlapped approach and lacks comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition methods, which are the standard in the field. Additionally, the paper omits analysis of group regularization behavior (e.g., l_{1,∞}) when tensors are low-rank across all modes, which would strengthen the practical relevance.

The work addresses a significant problem in tensor decomposition with meaningful theoretical improvements. The quality is high in the theoretical section, though the experimental section falls short. The clarity is excellent, and the originality is evident in the duality result and theoretical framework. The significance is strong, as the findings provide both theoretical justification and practical guidance for choosing between convex tensor decomposition approaches.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with important theoretical contributions that would be valuable to the NIPS community. The experimental shortcomings prevent it from being a definitive contribution, but the theoretical advances are substantial enough to warrant acceptance with minor revisions addressing the experimental gaps.
