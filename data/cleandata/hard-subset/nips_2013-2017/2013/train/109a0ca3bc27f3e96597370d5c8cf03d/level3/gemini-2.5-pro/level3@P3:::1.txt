This paper studies convex norm-regularized methods for approximately low-rank tensor factorization. It provides a theoretical analysis of the "latent" Schatten norm regularizer, which was empirically observed to outperform the more common "overlapped" Schatten norm regularizer in some settings. The main theoretical contributions are Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds for the latent norm approach in a tensor denoising setting. These bounds show that the error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to existing analyses for the overlapped norm where the error scales with the *sum* of the mode-k ranks. This provides a theoretical explanation for the superiority of the latent approach when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The authors also introduce a generalized class of structured Schatten norms that includes both the latent and overlapped norms as special cases and prove a general duality theorem for this class. Numerical simulations are presented to verify that the empirical MSE scales as predicted by the theory.

**Strengths**

*   **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The paper is technically sound and the theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs in the appendix. The manuscript is well-written and generally easy to follow. The authors do a good job of motivating the problem by clearly contrasting the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches and framing their work as a theoretical justification for the latter.
*   **Useful Theoretical Results:** The paper provides the first theoretical performance bounds for the latent Schatten norm regularizer in the tensor setting. The result that its error rate depends on the minimum mode-k rank, rather than the sum, is an important and intuitive finding. This provides concrete, formal support for an empirically observed phenomenon. The general duality result between latent and overlapped structured Schatten norms is also an elegant and potentially useful contribution for researchers working on structured sparsity and convex optimization.
*   **Good Experimental Validation:** The numerical experiments are well-designed to specifically test the paper's main theoretical claim. The results in Figure 2 compellingly demonstrate that the MSE of the latent approach scales linearly with the proposed "Latent rank (LR) complexity," confirming the derived theoretical bounds.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Significance and Scope for NIPS:** The main concern is that the paper's contribution is narrow, highly technical, and feels somewhat peripheral to the main interests of the NIPS community. While tensor decomposition is relevant to machine learning, this work focuses exclusively on the mathematical analysis of specific regularizers without connecting the results back to any practical machine learning problems or applications. The paper does not explore the implications of these bounds in a real-world context (e.g., collaborative filtering, topic modeling, or neuroimaging) either theoretically or experimentally. This limits the potential impact and audience for the work at a broad conference like NIPS.
*   **Originality and Novelty:** The contribution feels more incremental than groundbreaking. The latent and overlapped norm concepts have been explored before in the context of structured sparsity for vectors (e.g., Obozinski et al., 2011) and tensors (Tomioka et al., 2010, 2011). The analytical techniques used to derive the MSE bounds, while non-trivially applied here, are largely adapted from prior work on high-dimensional M-estimators with decomposable regularizers (e.g., Agarwal et al., 2012). The work is a solid application of existing techniques to a specific problem variant, but it does not introduce significant new problems, concepts, or proof techniques.
*   **Incomplete Theoretical Picture:** The paper provides upper bounds on the estimation error but does not establish matching lower bounds. Without lower bounds, it is difficult to assess the tightness of the analysis or claim that the latent approach is provably optimal. The comparison to the overlapped approach is based on comparing two different upper bounds, which is not as strong as a comparison against a fundamental information-theoretic limit.
*   **Potentially Misleading Abstract:** The abstract introduces "a new class of structured Schatten norms for tensors." However, this generalized class is only used to frame the duality theorem in Section 2. The paper's core analysis is restricted to the latent S1/1-norm. No algorithms, performance analyses, or practical uses for other norms within this general class are discussed, which makes the framing in the abstract feel somewhat overstated.
*   **Minor Presentation Issues:**
    *   Figure 1 is shown in the introduction before the concepts of "overlapped" and "latent" approaches are formally defined, making it difficult for the reader to fully appreciate its significance on a first pass.
    *   There are a few minor typos and awkward phrasings throughout the text that could be polished (e.g., "In particular when the noise goes to zero E → 0, the right hand side of inequality (8) shrinks to zero.").

In summary, this is a well-executed and technically solid piece of theoretical work that successfully provides a formal explanation for the performance of the latent norm regularizer for tensor decomposition. However, its narrow focus, incremental nature, and lack of connection to practical machine learning applications limit its significance and suitability for NIPS. The work would likely be a better fit for a more specialized venue focused on optimization, signal processing, or theoretical statistics.
