This paper introduces and analyzes a class of structured Schatten norms for tensors, unifying the “overlapped” (sum of nuclear norms of all mode unfoldings) and “latent” (infimal convolution over mode-specific low-rank tensors) formulations. The main conceptual contribution is a duality result: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q is the latent Sp*/q*, generalizing known overlapped/latent group-lasso dualities. Building on this, the paper provides theoretical guarantees for the latent approach in noisy tensor denoising. Using techniques from high-dimensional M-estimation with decomposable regularizers, the authors establish (i) consistency under an S∞/∞ noise bound, (ii) deterministic error bounds for each latent component under an incoherence-type condition that controls cross-mode correlations, and (iii) probabilistic bounds for Gaussian noise. A central takeaway is that, unlike the overlapped approach whose error scales with an average of mode ranks, the latent approach achieves an error that scales with the minimum mode-k rank, effectively behaving as if the algorithm knew the easiest (lowest-rank) mode. The paper compares its bounds to prior results on overlapped Schatten norms and connects to structured sparsity literature (dirty models, overlapped groups). Empirically, synthetic experiments confirm linear scaling with the proposed complexities and show competitive or superior performance to the overlapped approach.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The analysis is careful and leverages an appropriate framework (Agarwal et al.; Negahban et al.) for decomposable regularizers. The duality lemma and the key inequality bounding the latent S1/1 norm by min_k sqrt(r_k) times the Frobenius norm are well-motivated and central to the results. The deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds are transparent, with clear dependencies on dimensions, ranks, and the incoherence parameter. The regularization level does not require rank knowledge, which is attractive.
- Assumptions and tightness: The incoherence constraint (spectral-norm cross-bounds with parameter α) is standard but may be conservative; discussion on its necessity/tightness is brief. Constants and the factor F in the probabilistic bound are not optimized; the authors are appropriately cautious but could contextualize constant-size effects in practice.
- Empirical validation: The simulations are helpful for scaling validation, but the evaluation is brief and lacks details (e.g., how ground-truth ranks are chosen; sensitivity to λ; selection of α; runtime and convergence diagnostics). No real-data experiments are provided.

Clarity
- The paper is clearly written, with precise definitions, a coherent narrative from norms to duality to statistical guarantees, and a clean comparison to overlapped norms. The relationship to prior work at NIPS on structured sparsity and convex low-rank estimation is well drawn. Proof sketches are brief but the roadmap is understandable. Some empirical setup details are missing (see below), and a short discussion on algorithmic complexity and scalability would improve clarity.

Originality
- The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is an original and elegant contribution that bridges tensor norms with structured sparsity theory. The statistical guarantees for the latent formulation in the noisy setting sharpen and complement prior results on overlapped norms, explaining empirical behavior that had been observed but not justified theoretically. To my knowledge, the “oracle-like” min-rank scaling for the latent method is novel in this context.

Significance
- The problem—robust, convex tensor decomposition with provable guarantees—is important. The results advance understanding of when and why latent formulations outperform overlapped ones, and they provide guidance for practice (e.g., λ selection independent of ranks). Given the breadth of tensor applications, these insights are likely to be used and extended, including to completion and structured measurement settings.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Experiments:
  - Provide details on how Tucker ranks (r1, …, rK) were sampled or chosen; report distributions and cover regimes where the tensor is strongly low-rank in all modes (a favorable case for overlapped).
  - Include comparisons with standard non-convex baselines (e.g., HOSVD/HOOI/Tucker ALS with model selection, CP-ALS), even if only for denoising, to contextualize convex methods’ accuracy/runtime trade-offs.
  - Report tuning of λ (grid, selection criterion), the choice/estimation of α in constrained variants, and sensitivity analyses. Include convergence criteria, number of SVDs, and runtime vs. tensor size.
  - Add at least one real-data example (e.g., video, neuroimaging) to demonstrate practical relevance.
- Theory:
  - Discuss the behavior when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes. Can overlapped S1/1 or overlapped S1/∞ be competitive or better in this regime? A short analysis or counterexample would be informative.
  - Clarify whether the min-rank advantage can be extended to tensor completion (missing entries) under restricted strong convexity; outlining required conditions would strengthen the paper’s impact.
  - Comment on identifiability/uniqueness of the latent decomposition and how it affects interpretation.
- Algorithms:
  - Briefly describe computational complexity of the ADMM solver per iteration and memory scaling with K and dimensions; note any acceleration tricks (partial SVD, warm starts).

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, novel duality result connecting overlapped and latent Schatten norms with independent interest.
- Sharp statistical guarantees showing min-mode-rank scaling for latent decomposition, explaining empirical advantages over overlapped norms.
- Well-written, conceptually clean, and methodologically sound; advances understanding in an active area of NIPS interest.
- Empirical results, though limited, align with theory and provide practical guidance for λ.

Arguments against acceptance
- Experimental evaluation is too limited: missing non-convex baselines, real-data validation, and several crucial setup details (rank generation, α, tuning, runtime).
- Limited discussion of regimes where overlapped regularization could be preferable; no exploration of alternative norms (e.g., S1/∞) in practice.
- Some assumptions (incoherence, spectral constraints) may be conservative; the practical setting of α is unclear.

Overall evaluation
- This is a strong theoretical paper that offers a meaningful advance over existing convex tensor decomposition results and clarifies the empirical superiority of the latent approach. With a more comprehensive experimental section and minor clarifications, it would be an easy accept. As it stands, I lean toward acceptance, given the originality and significance of the theory, while encouraging the authors to strengthen the empirical section and discuss practical trade-offs and alternative baselines.
