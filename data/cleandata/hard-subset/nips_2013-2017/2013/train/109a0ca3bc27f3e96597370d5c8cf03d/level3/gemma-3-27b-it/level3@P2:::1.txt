This paper studies convex optimization approaches to tensor decomposition, specifically focusing on a “latent” structured Schatten norm and comparing its performance to a previously proposed “overlapped” norm. The authors provide theoretical analysis of the latent norm’s performance, demonstrating conditions under which it outperforms the overlapped approach, and validate these findings through numerical simulations. A key contribution is a duality result relating the latent and overlapped norms, extending existing work on structured sparsity.

The work builds upon recent advances in convex relaxation for tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], addressing a gap in theoretical understanding of the empirically observed benefits of the latent approach [25]. The paper is well-motivated, placing tensor decomposition in the context of diverse applications like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. The definitions are clear, and the authors carefully relate their work to existing literature, including connections to group Lasso techniques [1, 2, 13, 17, 21]. The proofs appear rigorous, with details largely relegated to the supplementary material.

**Strengths:**

* **Technical Soundness:** The paper appears technically solid, with clear definitions, rigorous derivations, and a logical progression of arguments. The duality result (Lemma 1) is a significant theoretical contribution.
* **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The notation is consistent, and the authors make an effort to explain complex concepts clearly.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The derivation of MSE bounds for the latent approach, under both deterministic and Gaussian noise models, provides valuable insights into its performance characteristics.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical results corroborate the theoretical findings, demonstrating that the latent approach scales as predicted by the theory and often outperforms the overlapped approach.
* **Connection to Existing Work:** The authors adeptly connect their work to related areas like structured sparsity and matrix decomposition, providing a broader context for their contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Significance:** While technically sound, the practical impact of these results feels limited. The paper focuses on a narrow technical comparison of two specific tensor decomposition approaches, without a strong connection to broader machine learning applications.  The focus remains heavily on theoretical analyses of convergence rates.
* **Incremental Contribution:** The results, while correct, feel somewhat incremental. The theoretical analysis confirms what was already observed empirically [25], and the techniques themselves lack substantial novelty.  The improvement offered by the latent approach, while theoretically justified, is not presented as a groundbreaking advancement.
* **Missing Lower Bounds:** The paper provides upper bounds on the error, but lacks corresponding lower bounds. This makes it difficult to assess the optimality of the proposed approach and strengthens the sense of incrementalism.
* **Misleading Abstract:** The abstract's claim of proposing a "new class of structured Schatten norms" is somewhat misleading. The paper analyzes *existing* norms and establishes relationships *between* them; it doesn't introduce fundamentally new norms with accompanying algorithmic or behavioral analyses.
* **Presentation Issues:** Figure 1 is presented very early in the paper before the reader has sufficient context to fully interpret its implications. Minor typos and phrasing issues occasionally disrupt the flow.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper presents a rigorous theoretical analysis of a recently proposed tensor decomposition approach, filling a gap in the existing literature.
* The duality result is a valuable theoretical contribution that may have broader applications in structured sparsity.
* The numerical results provide strong evidence supporting the theoretical findings.
* The paper is technically sound, well-written, and clearly presented.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* The results are somewhat incremental and lack a strong connection to real-world machine learning problems.
* The absence of lower bounds weakens the conclusions about the optimality of the proposed approach.
* The abstract overstates the novelty of the work.
* The limited practical impact might not justify inclusion in a highly competitive conference like NIPS.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the paper is technically sound and presents a valuable theoretical contribution, its limited practical significance and incremental nature raise concerns about its suitability for NIPS. The authors have performed a careful analysis and provided strong supporting evidence for their claims, but the impact of this work on the broader machine learning community seems limited.  I would encourage the authors to consider expanding the scope of their work to address more practical applications or to investigate the limitations of their approach in more detail. Addressing the issue of lower bounds would also significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend the authors carefully revise the abstract to more accurately reflect the contributions of their work.
