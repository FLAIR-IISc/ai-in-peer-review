

This paper addresses a fundamental problem in tensor decomposition by providing rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent Schatten norm approach, which has been empirically observed to outperform the overlapped Schatten norm approach in specific settings. The work builds upon recent convex optimization-based tensor decomposition methods (Tomioka et al., NIPS 2011; Gandy et al., Inverse Problems 2011) and connects them to structured sparsity literature (Jenatton et al., JMLR 2011; Obozinski et al., Technical Report 2011). The authors establish a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms (Lemma 1), a result of independent interest in structured sparsity theory. Their main theoretical contribution (Theorems 2-3) demonstrates that the latent approach achieves mean squared error scaling with the *minimal* mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average of square roots of mode-k ranks (as shown in Tomioka et al., NIPS 2013). This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

The paper excels in technical quality and clarity. The theoretical development is meticulous, with tight bounds (Theorem 2) that cleanly separate the noise-dependent and rank-dependent error terms. The duality result (Lemma 1) is elegantly proven and generalizes prior work on group sparsity. The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow from problem formulation to theoretical analysis to experiments, and a supplementary material that provides complete proofs. The writing is precise and accessible to the NIPS audience, making the contributions easy to follow.

However, the experimental evaluation has notable shortcomings that weaken the practical significance. The experiments (Section 4) use only two tensor sizes (50×50×20 and 80×80×40) and fail to specify how the Tucker ranks (r₁,...,rₖ) were chosen for generation (a critical detail for reproducibility). The comparison is limited to the overlapped vs. latent approaches alone, omitting a crucial benchmark: non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., HOSVD, alternating least squares), which are the standard in practice and would better establish the real-world impact of the convex approach. The authors claim the latent approach "performs better in many cases" but do not quantify this relative to non-convex baselines. Additionally, the paper misses an opportunity to analyze the behavior of group regularization (e.g., ℓ₁,∞ norms) when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes—a scenario where the overlapped approach is theoretically favored, yet the experiments show near-equality (Figure 2 right panel).

The significance of this work is high. Tensor decomposition is a cornerstone of multivariate data analysis in neuroimaging (e.g., channels×time×subjects), collaborative filtering, and environmental monitoring. By providing the first theoretical justification for the latent approach’s empirical success and demonstrating its robustness to rank structure, the paper advances the state-of-the-art in convex tensor decomposition. The duality result also enriches structured sparsity theory, making it broadly relevant beyond tensor methods. While the paper does not claim to replace non-convex methods (which often have better empirical performance in low-noise settings), it establishes a theoretically sound convex alternative that is particularly advantageous when the tensor exhibits strong mode-specific low-rank structure.

**Pro (Strengths):**  
- Rigorous theoretical analysis with tight error bounds (Theorems 2-3)  
- Novel duality result (Lemma 1) of independent interest in structured sparsity  
- Clear, well-organized presentation with complete proofs in supplement  
- Empirical validation of theoretical scaling behavior (Figure 2)  
- Addresses a significant problem (tensor decomposition) with meaningful theoretical advance  

**Con (Weaknesses):**  
- Experimental section lacks critical details (Tucker rank selection, non-convex baselines)  
- Missing comparison to dominant non-convex tensor decomposition methods  
- Does not explore group regularization behavior in simultaneously low-rank scenarios  
- Limited experimental scope (only two tensor sizes)  

The paper makes a substantial theoretical contribution to convex tensor decomposition, resolving a key empirical question about the latent vs. overlapped approaches. While the experimental evaluation could be strengthened by including non-convex baselines and more detailed rank selection procedures, the core theoretical advances are compelling and well-supported. The duality result alone would be a valuable contribution to NIPS. For a conference emphasizing theoretical rigor, the paper’s strengths significantly outweigh its experimental limitations. I recommend acceptance with the suggestion to add a brief non-convex comparison and clarify experimental details in the camera-ready version.
