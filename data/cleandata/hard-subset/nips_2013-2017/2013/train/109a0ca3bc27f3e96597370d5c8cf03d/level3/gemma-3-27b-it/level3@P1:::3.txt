This paper studies convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norm regularization, specifically comparing and theoretically analyzing the “overlapped” and “latent” approaches recently proposed in the literature. The authors provide a novel duality result between the two norms and, crucially, demonstrate theoretically why the “latent” approach outperforms the “overlapped” approach when the true tensor is low-rank in only a single mode – a result supported by empirical observations in [25]. The theoretical analysis establishes consistency and provides bounds on the mean squared error, scaling with the minimal mode-k rank, offering a significant improvement over prior work.

The paper builds upon a growing body of work addressing tensor decomposition, motivated by applications in diverse areas like neuroimaging and collaborative filtering. Traditional approaches rely on non-convex optimization, lacking strong theoretical guarantees. Recent efforts have focused on convex relaxations using Schatten norms, specifically the overlapped approach [9, 15, 23, 25], which penalizes all mode unfoldings simultaneously. However, this approach struggles when rank is limited to a single mode. The latent approach [25] addresses this by decomposing the tensor into a mixture of low-rank tensors, each specialized to a specific mode. This work provides the missing theoretical foundation for understanding the empirical success of the latent approach.  The connection drawn to structured sparsity literature and the generalization of duality results offer further theoretical contributions beyond the immediate application to tensor decomposition.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Theoretical Analysis:** The paper presents rigorous theoretical results, establishing consistency and bounding the mean squared error for the latent approach. The proof techniques appear solid, building on recent advances in high-dimensional statistics and convex optimization.
*   **Novel Duality Result:** The derivation of a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a significant and original contribution. This result is of independent interest to researchers working on structured sparsity.
*   **Clear and Well-Organized:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized, with a logical flow of ideas. Definitions are clear, and the notation is consistent. The presentation of the theory is accessible.
*   **Addresses a Significant Problem:** Tensor decomposition is a fundamentally important problem with broad applications, and improving its theoretical understanding is a valuable contribution.
*   **Meaningful Improvement:**  The authors demonstrably improve upon existing results for convex tensor decomposition, providing a theoretical explanation for the superior performance of the latent approach in certain settings.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** While the paper includes numerical simulations to validate the theoretical scaling behavior, the experimental setup is relatively brief and lacks detail. The parameter selection process (e.g., how Tucker ranks were chosen) is not adequately described.
*   **Missing Comparison to Non-Convex Methods:** The paper focuses solely on convex relaxation techniques. A comparison to established non-convex methods (e.g., alternating least squares) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach and highlight its trade-offs.
*   **Lack of Exploration of Group Regularization:** The paper doesn't fully explore the behavior of other group regularization schemes (e.g. l_{1,∞}) when the tensor is low-rank in all modes. This could provide valuable insights and potentially identify scenarios where alternative regularization strategies might be advantageous.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** The theoretical analysis assumes knowledge of the noise level and other parameters that may not be readily available in practice. Discussing the sensitivity of the results to these assumptions would be beneficial.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a strong theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition, resolving an important open question regarding the latent approach.
*   The novelty of the duality result and its connection to broader structured sparsity literature are noteworthy.
*   The clarity and rigor of the presentation make the paper accessible to a wide audience.
*   The problem addressed is significant, and the results offer meaningful improvements over existing methods.

**Arguments for Rejection:**

*   The limited experimental evaluation does not fully support the theoretical claims. More extensive experiments and a comparison to non-convex methods are needed.
*   The absence of a discussion about other group regularization techniques leaves open the possibility that alternative approaches might be more effective in certain scenarios.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a significant theoretical contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. The analysis is rigorous, the presentation is clear, and the results are meaningful. While the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive, the theoretical advances alone justify acceptance.  I recommend accepting this paper with a minor revision request to expand the experimental section to include a comparison with non-convex approaches and provide more detail on parameter selection.  Addressing the limitations regarding group regularization would further strengthen the paper, but is not critical for acceptance.
