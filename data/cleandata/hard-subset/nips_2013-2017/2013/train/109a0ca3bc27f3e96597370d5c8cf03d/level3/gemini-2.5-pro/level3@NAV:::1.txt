This paper studies convex norm-regularized optimization for approximately low-rank tensor factorization. The central contribution is a theoretical analysis of the "latent" Schatten norm, comparing its performance to the previously studied "overlap" Schatten norm. This work builds upon a line of research applying convex relaxation techniques to tensor decomposition (e.g., Tomioka et al., NIPS 2011) and draws parallels to the literature on structured sparsity and latent group Lasso. The authors derive mean squared error (MSE) upper bounds for the latent norm regularizer under several noise models, demonstrating that its performance depends on the minimum mode-k rank, in contrast to the overlap norm's dependence on the sum of mode-k ranks. This theoretical finding, which explains why the latent approach can outperform the overlap approach when a tensor is low-rank in only one mode, is supported by numerical simulations. The paper also proposes a generalized family of structured Schatten norms that includes both norms as special cases and proves a novel duality theorem for this generalization.

The paper has several strengths. It is well-written, easy to follow, and the technical derivations appear sound. The duality theorem is an elegant theoretical result that nicely connects this work to the broader structured sparsity literature. The numerical experiments are well-designed and provide clear confirmation of the derived theoretical error scalings. For researchers working on the theory of optimization for tensor decompositions, these results are certainly of interest.

However, the paper also has significant weaknesses that limit its potential impact at NIPS. The primary concern is the narrow scope and limited connection to machine learning practice. The analysis is purely technical, and the paper makes no attempt to evaluate the methods in a real-world machine learning context or discuss the practical implications of the theoretical findings. The results themselves, while technically correct, feel somewhat incremental and not particularly surprising; the techniques used are largely extensions of existing analyses from the matrix and structured sparsity settings.

Furthermore, the theoretical contribution could be stronger. The paper provides upper bounds on the MSE without corresponding lower bounds. This weakens the comparison between the latent and overlap norms, as it is unclear whether the derived bounds are tight or if the apparent advantage of the latent norm is an artifact of the analysis. The abstract is also slightly misleading in its claim of introducing a "new class of structured Schatten norms," as this generalization is only used to prove the duality theorem and is not explored further with new algorithms or behavioral analysis. Minor presentation issues, such as Figure 1 appearing before the concepts are explained, also detract from the clarity. While technically solid, the paper's limited novelty and narrow focus make it a borderline contribution for the NIPS audience.
