### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates convex relaxations for low-rank tensor decomposition. It introduces a general framework of "structured Schatten norms" for tensors, focusing on two specific instances: the "overlapped" norm, which corresponds to the sum of nuclear norms of all modal unfoldings, and the "latent" norm, which represents the tensor as a sum of components, each of which is low-rank in a specific mode. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, which was previously observed empirically to outperform the overlapped approach in certain settings.

The authors establish a novel duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which is of independent interest and connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity. The main theoretical result is a performance bound for the latent approach in a denoising setting, demonstrating that its mean squared error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor (`min_k r_k`). This provides a clear theoretical explanation for its superiority over the overlapped approach, whose error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations that confirm the predicted scaling behavior.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides the first rigorous analysis of the "latent" approach for tensor decomposition. The key result—that the latent method's performance adapts to the mode with the smallest rank without prior knowledge—is both novel and important. It formally explains why this method is advantageous, particularly for tensors that are low-rank in only one or a few modes.

2.  **Novel Duality Framework:** The introduction of structured Schatten norms and the proof of duality between the overlapped (`S_{p/q}`) and latent (`S_{p*/q*}`) forms is elegant and provides a unifying perspective. This result connects tensor methods to well-established concepts in structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped vs. latent group LASSO) and may inspire further work in this area.

3.  **Clarity of the Main Message:** The paper effectively communicates its central argument. The comparison between the error bounds for the overlapped (scaling with `sum sqrt(r_k)`) and latent (scaling with `min sqrt(r_k)`) approaches is clear and compelling. The experiments are well-designed to support this main theoretical claim.

4.  **Potential for Impact:** The paper provides strong evidence, both theoretical and empirical, that the latent approach is a preferable default choice for noisy tensor decomposition problems. This is a valuable insight for practitioners in fields like neuroimaging, signal processing, and machine learning where tensor methods are applied.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper is technically strong, its presentation, accessibility, and several technical details could be significantly improved. Addressing the following points would strengthen the paper considerably.

1.  **Motivation and Accessibility:** The paper could be made more accessible to a broader NIPS audience.
    *   A compelling motivation would be to introduce a simple latent-variable graphical model where a set of hidden variables `(h_1, ..., h_K)` generates an observed tensor `X` via a multilinear map. This immediately shows that the observed moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition where the multilinear ranks correspond to the dimensions of the hidden variables.
    *   This framing would make the paper's core contribution more intuitive: the latent approach excels when only a few of these hidden variables have a small dimension, which is a very natural assumption in many real-world generative processes. A small figure illustrating this model would be very effective.

2.  **Clarity of Technical Arguments:** Several key steps in the theoretical analysis lack sufficient justification.
    *   **Theorem 2, Eq. (13):** The paper claims that the bound on the sum of component errors (12) immediately leads to the bound on the total error (13) in terms of the minimal mode-k rank. The justification—"a singleton decomposition ... is allowed"—is too brief. A more detailed argument is needed to explain how the arbitrariness in the *true* decomposition `W* = sum W*(k)` translates to a bound involving the minimal rank of `W*` itself, especially when the estimate `W_hat` is obtained from the optimization.
    *   **Proof of Lemma 5:** The proof of Lemma 5 is stated to be in the supplementary material but appears to be missing. This lemma is critical for the proof of Theorem 2, and its absence is a major gap.
    *   **Proof of Lemma 4:** The proof of Lemma 4 is deferred to another paper [1]. For a result that is central to the analysis, at least a proof sketch should be included to make the paper more self-contained.

3.  **Notation and Terminology:**
    *   The superscript notation `W^(k)` for the k-th component tensor is easily confused with `W_(k)` for the mode-k unfolding. Using a different symbol (e.g., `W_k` for components and `mat_k(W)` for unfolding) would improve readability.
    *   The notation for mode-k rank is inconsistent (e.g., `r_k` vs. `\bar{r}_k`). This should be standardized.
    *   It should be explicitly stated that "mode-rank" is also known as "multilinear rank" and that other notions of tensor rank (e.g., CP rank) exist, to orient readers less familiar with tensor literature.

4.  **Context and Scope:**
    *   **Related Work:** The paper should briefly discuss classical non-convex methods like Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD) and its variants, which also perform Tucker decomposition. Mentioning their approximation bounds and citing a standard survey like Kolda & Bader (2009) would provide better context.
    *   **Computational Complexity:** The paper focuses entirely on statistical performance. A brief discussion on the computational complexity of solving the proposed convex problems (e.g., using the ADMM mentioned in Section 4) would be a valuable addition for practitioners.
    *   **Incoherence Assumption:** The discussion following Eq. (10) compares the incoherence assumption to that in matrix completion literature [1, 3, 11]. This comparison may be misleading, as the nature of the assumptions is quite different. It would be better to simply state and justify the assumption on its own terms or remove the comparison.

5.  **Minor Issues:**
    *   In the abstract, "structures Schatten" should be "structured Schatten".
    *   The last line of page 12 in the supplementary material is missing a closing parenthesis.
    *   In the discussion of Theorem 2, it should be clarified that the components `W^(k)` are recovered only up to a permutation, as is typical in mixture model settings.

### Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a valuable and technically significant contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The main result, which establishes the superiority of the latent norm approach by relating its error to the minimum mode-k rank, is both novel and impactful. However, the paper is weakened by several gaps in its technical arguments, a lack of clarity in its presentation, and confusing notation.

These issues are substantial but appear fixable. If the authors can provide the missing proofs, clarify the jump from Eq. (12) to (13), improve the motivation and notation, and address the other points raised, this would be a very strong paper for NIPS. My recommendation leans towards acceptance, conditional on these significant revisions.
