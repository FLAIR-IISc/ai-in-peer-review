This paper studies convex tensor decomposition through a framework of structured Schatten norms, focusing on the analysis of the "latent" approach. This method regularizes a sum of tensors, each constrained to be low-rank in a specific mode. The authors contrast this with the "overlapped" approach, which penalizes all unfolded matrices of a single tensor simultaneously. This work builds upon recent convex formulations for tensor decomposition [9, 23, 25] and draws parallels to the structured sparsity literature, particularly latent group lasso methods. The paper's main theoretical contribution is to provide recovery guarantees for the latent approach, demonstrating that its performance scales with the *minimum* mode-rank of the true tensor. This elegantly explains its empirical advantage over the overlapped approach, which depends on the average of the mode-ranks.

The paper is technically significant and generally well-written. Its core strength lies in providing a rigorous theoretical justification for the superior performance of the latent approach in specific, important settings. The result that the method adapts to the unknown mode with the smallest rank is a novel and powerful insight. Furthermore, the duality result between the latent and overlapped norms is a valuable contribution that connects this domain to broader concepts in structured regularization.

Despite its strengths, the paper requires several revisions to improve its clarity, accessibility, and technical soundness.

**Major Revisions:**
1.  **Motivation and Accessibility:** The motivation should be strengthened for a general machine learning audience. We strongly recommend adding a latent-variable graphical model showing how a linear hidden-to-observed mapping (`h_i -> x_i`) can generate data whose moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition. A figure illustrating this model would clarify that the multilinear ranks correspond to the dimensions of the hidden variables, making the paper’s main advantage—that only some hidden variables need to be low-dimensional—more intuitive.
2.  **Technical Gaps:** The proofs of the main results are incomplete. The proof of Lemma 5, which is key to Theorem 2, is missing and must be provided. Similarly, the proof for Lemma 4 should be included rather than merely cited. In Theorem 2, the authors should justify the step from Eq. (12) to (13) and clarify that the components are recovered up to permutation.

**Minor Revisions:**
1.  **Clarity and Context:** The discussion on incoherence after Eq. (10) is potentially misleading in its comparison to [1,3,10] and should be revised or removed. The related work section should be expanded to discuss HOSVD and cite Kolda’s survey. A brief discussion of the computational complexity is also needed. For clarity, please state that "mode-rank" is also called "multilinear rank" and note that other tensor rank definitions exist.
2.  **Notation and Typos:** Notation should be cleaned up: the superscripts for components `\hat{\cal W}^{(k)}` are confusing, and the notation for k-mode rank is inconsistent. Please also fix the abstract typo ("structures Schatten") and a missing parenthesis in the supplement.

In summary, this is an important paper with a strong core contribution. However, acceptance is conditional on the authors addressing the significant gaps in the theoretical arguments and improving the paper’s accessibility.
