## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates convex optimization approaches to tensor decomposition, specifically focusing on the “latent” norm and its comparison to the “overlap” norm. The authors derive theoretical Mean Squared Error (MSE) bounds for the latent norm under various noise models, demonstrating its advantages over the overlap norm, a result supported by numerical simulations. A generalization of both norms is proposed, accompanied by a duality theorem. 

The work builds upon recent advances in convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, addressing a gap in theoretical understanding of the empirically observed superior performance of the latent approach [25]. It connects to broader research on structured sparsity and group lasso techniques [1, 2, 13, 17, 21], framing tensor decomposition within a more general optimization context. The derivation of the duality theorem is a notable contribution, potentially useful beyond the immediate application to tensor factorization.

**Strengths:** The paper is technically sound and generally well-written, making it relatively easy to follow. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the numerical results validate the derived bounds. The connection to structured sparsity is a valuable perspective. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a clear comparison to existing work.

**Weaknesses:** The primary concern is the narrow scope and limited impact on the broader NIPS audience. While the theoretical results are solid, they are highly technical and focused on a specific optimization problem within tensor decomposition. The paper lacks exploration of these techniques in practical machine learning contexts, either theoretically or experimentally. The results feel incremental rather than groundbreaking; the techniques themselves lack substantial novelty. The absence of matching lower bounds weakens the algorithmic comparisons – demonstrating an upper bound doesn’t prove optimality. Furthermore, the claim of proposing a “new class of structured Schatten norms” is misleading, as no algorithms or behavioral analyses are presented for this generalization. Finally, Figure 1 appears prematurely in the paper, hindering initial understanding, and there are minor presentation issues (typos/phrasing).

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Technically sound analysis, potentially useful to researchers specifically working on optimization for tensor decompositions. The duality theorem is a valuable theoretical contribution.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Limited scope and relevance to the broader NIPS community. Incremental results lacking significant novelty. Lack of connection to real-world machine learning applications. Absence of lower bounds. Misleading abstract.

**Overall:** While a solid technical contribution, this paper is likely better suited for a more specialized conference. The lack of broader impact and the incremental nature of the results make it a weak submission for NIPS. I recommend **rejection**. I encourage the authors to consider expanding the scope of their work to demonstrate its relevance to more general machine learning problems or to provide more compelling evidence of its practical benefits.
