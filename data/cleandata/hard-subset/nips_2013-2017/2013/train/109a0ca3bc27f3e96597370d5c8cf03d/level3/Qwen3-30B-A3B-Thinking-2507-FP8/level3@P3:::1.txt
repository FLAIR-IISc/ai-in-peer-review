

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper studies structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors establish a duality theorem between these two norm structures, analyze the theoretical performance of the latent approach (showing it achieves MSE bounds scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average as with the overlapped approach), and confirm their theoretical predictions with numerical simulations.

The work builds on previous studies of tensor decomposition (Kolda & Bader, 2009), convex optimization approaches for tensor completion (Gandy et al., 2011; Tomioka et al., 2011), and structured sparsity literature (Jenatton et al., 2011; Obozinski et al., 2011). The authors connect tensor decomposition to structured sparsity by showing the latent approach corresponds to the latent group lasso while the overlapped approach corresponds to the overlapped group lasso.

**Strengths:**
The paper is technically sound with rigorous proofs of key results, including the duality theorem (Lemma 1) and MSE bounds (Theorems 2-3). The theoretical analysis is clear and well-motivated, with careful attention to the relationship between the latent and overlapped approaches. The numerical experiments (Section 4) effectively confirm the theoretical predictions, showing the MSE scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and accessible to readers familiar with tensor decomposition and convex optimization.

**Weaknesses:**
The main limitation is the narrow scope of the contribution. While the theoretical results are technically correct, they address a very specific problem in tensor decomposition with limited connection to broader machine learning challenges. The authors make no attempt to evaluate their approach in real ML applications or even in settings that would be relevant to typical ML practitioners (e.g., collaborative filtering, image completion). The main insight—that the latent approach performs better when the tensor is low-rank in a specific mode—was already empirically observed in previous work (Tomioka et al., 2011), so the theoretical justification, while correct, is not particularly surprising.

The paper's significance is limited by the absence of lower bounds to match their upper bounds, which weakens the algorithmic comparison. The abstract misleadingly claims the paper proposes "a new class of structured Schatten norms" when the generalization presented in Section 2.1 is not accompanied by any algorithmic development or behavior analysis for the general case. The main contribution is a theoretical explanation for an already observed empirical phenomenon, not a new method with new applications.

**Evaluation:**
*Quality:* The paper is technically sound with correct proofs and thorough numerical validation. It presents a complete theoretical and experimental analysis of the specific problem. (4/5)

*Clarity:* The paper is exceptionally clear, well-structured, and easy to follow. The organization flows logically from definitions to theory to experiments. (5/5)

*Originality:* The duality theorem is interesting and generalizes structured sparsity work, but the main contribution is incremental. The paper doesn't introduce truly novel concepts or approaches beyond explaining an already-observed empirical phenomenon. (2/5)

*Significance:* The significance is limited to a narrow subfield of optimization for tensor decompositions. The results are of interest to researchers working on convex tensor decomposition, but they don't address major ML challenges or provide insights applicable to broader ML problems. (2/5)

**Pro and Con Arguments:**
*Pro:* The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for an empirically observed advantage of the latent approach. The duality result is novel and connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature. The numerical results confirm the theoretical predictions precisely.

*Con:* The results are narrowly scoped with minimal connection to machine learning. The main insight was already observed empirically, so the theoretical contribution is incremental. The paper lacks lower bounds, making the performance comparison less compelling. The abstract misleads about the novelty of the "new class" of norms.

**Recommendation:**
This paper makes a technically sound contribution to the specific area of convex tensor decomposition, but its narrow focus and limited connection to mainstream machine learning significantly reduce its impact for a venue like NIPS. The results are valuable for researchers working specifically on tensor optimization but lack the broader significance expected for NIPS. The paper would be more appropriate for a specialized optimization or signal processing conference.

While the paper is well-written and technically correct, the incremental nature of the contribution and the lack of connection to machine learning applications suggest it should be rejected for NIPS. The authors have made a solid contribution to a niche area, but it does not advance the field of machine learning in a meaningful way.
