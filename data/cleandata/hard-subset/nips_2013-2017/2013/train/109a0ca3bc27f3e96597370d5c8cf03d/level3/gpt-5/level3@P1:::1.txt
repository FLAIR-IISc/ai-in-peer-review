This paper studies convex norm-regularized tensor decomposition with a focus on “latent” structured Schatten norms and their comparison to the “overlapped” norms used in prior work. The authors present a unified family of structured Schatten Sp/q norms that contains both overlapped and latent variants, prove a duality theorem between them (generalizing dualities known in structured sparsity), and analyze the denoising performance of the latent Schatten-1 norm estimator. The key theoretical result shows that, for noisy tensor decomposition, the mean-squared error (MSE) of the latent approach scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas previously analyzed overlapped norms scale with an average of mode-wise ranks. The analysis includes a basic consistency bound, a deterministic bound under an incoherence-type assumption, and high-probability bounds under Gaussian noise with a choice of λ independent of unknown ranks. Numerical simulations confirm the predicted scaling and show that latent can outperform overlapped even when the tensor is simultaneously low rank across modes.

Relation to prior work: The convex overlapped Schatten-1 approach has been explored in tensor completion/decomposition [Gandy et al.; Liu et al.; Signoretto et al.] and analyzed statistically (e.g., Tomioka et al., NIPS). The latent formulation parallels the latent/dirty model in structured sparsity [Obozinski et al.; Jalali et al.], and the analysis style is in the spirit of decomposable regularizers [Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.]. The duality result formalizes and extends known overlapped/latent dualities for group norms to the tensor Schatten setting. The paper also relates to the lower bounds of Mu et al. on tensor recovery.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical advance: sharp upper bounds for the latent norm that depend on the minimal mode rank, explaining when latent outperforms overlapped.
- Useful duality: a neat extension of overlapped/latent duality to structured Schatten norms, plausibly reusable beyond this specific problem.
- Clean statements: consistency, deterministic, and Gaussian-noise bounds with explicit dependences and a rank-independent λ prescription.
- Empirical support: simulations match the scaling laws and illustrate the practical advantage of latent in regimes predicted by theory.
- Writing is generally clear, and proofs appear technically careful.

Weaknesses
- Scope and impact: the contribution is narrow and technical, focusing on a specific convex relaxation and error scaling; the paper does not demonstrate impact on machine learning tasks.
- Limited novelty of techniques: the analysis largely adapts ideas from decomposable-regularizer frameworks to the tensor/latent setting; the latent formulation itself is known.
- Only upper bounds: no matching lower bounds for latent; comparisons to overlapped are therefore one-sided (though Mu et al. provide a related lower bound for a different setting).
- “New class of norms” framing is somewhat overstated: the general Sp/q family is introduced but not algorithmically explored or empirically evaluated; the paper’s analysis specializes to S1/1 with standard solvers.
- Experimental evaluation is restricted to synthetic denoising; no tensor completion, no real datasets (e.g., collaborative filtering, neuroimaging), and limited comparison to other baselines.
- Practical aspects under-specified: the incoherence-like constraints and parameter α in (11) are introduced but their selection/verification in practice is unclear.
- Minor presentation issues: Figure 1 appears before sufficient context; a few typos/phrasing problems.

Quality
- Technically sound with careful derivations and appropriate use of dual norms and decomposability arguments. Assumptions (incoherence, Gaussian noise) are standard but could be discussed in terms of realism. Lack of lower bounds for the latent estimator weakens the optimality claims.

Clarity
- Generally well-organized and readable. Suggestions: move the motivational figure after the definition of overlapped/latent norms; clarify the role and practical setting of the constraints in (11); temper abstract language about “a new class” or add at least algorithmic/empirical exploration for the generalized norms; provide guidance on estimating σ and tuning λ, and discuss sensitivity.

Originality
- Moderate. The duality result is a tidy generalization; the latent estimator’s analysis is new in this tensor setting, but methodological and analytic techniques follow established patterns from structured sparsity and matrix problems.

Significance
- Moderate for the theory/optimization community interested in tensor methods: the bounds cleanly explain empirical advantages and may guide method choice. Practical ML impact is limited by the absence of real-world validation and broader baselines.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add real-data experiments (e.g., tensor completion for recommender systems with time/context, neuroimaging as motivated) and report predictive performance and runtime versus overlapped and nonconvex baselines (ALS/Tucker, CP).
- Include tensor completion results (missing data), which are central to many applications and to prior overlapped analyses.
- Provide discussion or partial lower bounds (or reference closely matching ones) for the latent estimator to strengthen claims about optimality and comparisons.
- Elaborate on the choice/estimation of α and feasibility of constraints in (11); discuss removing or replacing them with regularization.
- Explore at least one additional member of the proposed Sp/q family empirically or algorithmically to justify the “new class” claim.
- Improve presentation: relocate Figure 1, fix typos, and add a short proof sketch for key theorems in the main text.

Arguments for acceptance
- Clear, well-executed theory that resolves when latent should beat overlapped, with an elegant duality result.
- Empirical confirmation of theoretically predicted scaling.
- Relevance to ongoing NIPS work on convex methods and structured norms.

Arguments against acceptance
- Incremental methodological novelty; techniques are adaptations of known frameworks.
- Limited connection to ML applications and lack of real-data validation.
- Absence of lower bounds and exploration of the broader proposed norm class.
- Presentation overstates generality relative to delivered results.

Overall, this is a solid theoretical contribution on convex tensor norms with clean insights about latent versus overlapped regularization; however, the narrow scope and limited empirical validation temper its significance for the NIPS audience.
