

This paper addresses tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, comparing two convex optimization approaches: the "overlapped" approach that penalizes all modes to be jointly low-rank, and the "latent" approach that decomposes tensors into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis showing that the latent approach performs better when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific mode, as evidenced by the mean squared error scaling with the minimum mode-k rank rather than the average rank as in the overlapped approach. They establish a novel duality between these structured Schatten norms, which generalizes results in structured sparsity literature.

The paper makes significant contributions to the understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The theoretical analysis (Theorems 1-3) provides precise recovery guarantees that explain the empirical superiority of the latent approach, which was previously observed but not theoretically justified. The numerical experiments (Section 4) confirm that the theoretical scaling predictions match empirical performance, with the latent approach consistently outperforming the overlapped approach when the tensor is low-rank in a specific mode. The duality result (Lemma 1) is particularly valuable as it connects tensor decomposition to broader structured sparsity literature.

The paper demonstrates high quality through its rigorous theoretical foundation, clear connection to related work, and comprehensive empirical validation. The authors carefully distinguish their work from previous contributions on tensor decomposition, including the convex approaches of [9, 15, 23, 25] and the structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21]. The significance of the work is evident in its practical implications for tensor completion and decomposition tasks, which are increasingly important in machine learning applications involving multi-way data.

However, several clarifications and improvements would strengthen the paper. The motivation could be enhanced for a broader machine learning audience by including a latent-variable graphical model illustrating how observed tensor data arises from hidden variables with arbitrary dependence. The multilinear rank (mode-k rank) should be clearly defined as equivalent to the dimension of hidden variables in such a model, with the key insight that only some hidden variables may have small dimension, which is precisely where the latent approach excels.

The paper contains several technical inconsistencies that need resolution. Theorem 2 should clarify that components are recovered only up to permutation. The transition from inequality (12) to (13) requires justification, as it relies on the arbitrary choice of decomposition (including singleton decompositions). The missing proof of Lemma 5 is critical for Theorem 2 and should be provided. The discussion of incoherence after equation (10) is misleading and should be removed or substantially revised, as it incorrectly compares to [1,3,10] where the incoherence conditions differ significantly from the setting here.

Notational issues need correction: the superscript notation for components (e.g., $\hat{\cal W}^{(k)}$) is confusing and should be clarified. The inconsistent use of $\bar{r}_k$ and $\underline{r}_k$ for mode-k ranks should be standardized to a single notation (e.g., mult_rank(tensor)). The abstract contains a typo ("structures Schatten" → "structured Schatten"). The paper should explicitly state that "mode-rank" is equivalent to "multilinear rank" and note the existence of multiple tensor rank definitions, as this is crucial for understanding the results.

The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of computational complexity and a more thorough treatment of related work on HOSVD and its variants, including approximation bounds as in Kolda's survey [4]. The current comparison to [1,3,10] regarding incoherence is misleading and should be revised to accurately reflect the relationship between the incoherence assumptions.

Despite these issues, the paper makes a valuable contribution to tensor decomposition theory. The key novelty—providing theoretical guarantees for the latent approach that explain its empirical superiority—represents a significant advance over previous work. The duality result between the two structured Schatten norms is also of independent interest to the structured sparsity community.

The paper is generally well-written and technically sound, with clear exposition of the main results. The authors have provided both theoretical and empirical evidence that the latent approach is preferable to the overlapped approach for noisy tensor decomposition, which is an important practical contribution. With the suggested clarifications and notational fixes, this paper would be an excellent contribution to NIPS.

The key strength is the rigorous theoretical justification for the empirical success of the latent approach, which fills a critical gap in the literature. The main weakness is the need for additional clarifications to strengthen the technical presentation and ensure the results are accessible to the broader ML community. Given the paper's strong theoretical foundation and practical relevance, I recommend acceptance with the understanding that the authors will address the suggested clarifications.
