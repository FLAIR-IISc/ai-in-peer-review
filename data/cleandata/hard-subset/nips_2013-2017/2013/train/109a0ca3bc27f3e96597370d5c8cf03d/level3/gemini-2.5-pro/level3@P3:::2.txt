### Summary

This paper investigates convex approaches for low-rank tensor decomposition, focusing on a class of regularizers termed "structured Schatten norms." The authors analyze and compare two specific norms: the "overlapped" norm, which penalizes the sum of the nuclear norms of all matrix unfoldings, and the "latent" norm, which models the tensor as a sum of components, each of which is low-rank in a specific mode. The primary contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, which was previously observed to outperform the overlapped method empirically but lacked theoretical justification. The authors prove that the estimation error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error depends on the *sum* (or average) of the mode-k ranks. This key result explains why the latent method is particularly effective when the underlying tensor is low-rank in only one of its modes. A novel duality result connecting the latent and overlapped norms is also presented, linking this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity and group Lasso methods.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

This is a well-written and technically significant paper. Its primary strengths are the novel theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition and the elegant duality result that unifies it with the overlapped approach. The finding that the latent method's performance depends on the minimum mode-rank is an important insight for the field. The paper is generally clear, and its claims are supported by both theoretical proofs and numerical experiments that align well with the theory.

Despite these strengths, the paper has several weaknesses in its current form, primarily related to clarity for a broader audience, missing technical details in key proofs, and incomplete discussion of related work and practical considerations. Addressing these points would substantially improve the quality and impact of the work.

#### Detailed Comments and Suggestions for Improvement

**1. Motivation and Accessibility:**
*   **Improving Motivation:** The introduction could be strengthened by providing a more concrete motivation for a general machine learning audience. A latent-variable model perspective would make the problem much more accessible. Consider introducing a graphical model where a set of latent variables `h_i` (with arbitrary dependencies among them) map linearly to the observed variables `x_i`. The moments of the observed variables `x_i` form a tensor that, under this generative model, naturally admits a Tucker decomposition.
*   **Illustrative Figure:** Including a figure of this latent-variable graphical model would be highly beneficial. You could then explain that the multilinear ranks (or mode-ranks) of the moment tensor correspond directly to the dimensions of the hidden variables `h_i`. This framing makes the concept of low multilinear rank more intuitive.
*   **Highlighting the Key Advantage:** This latent-variable view also provides a natural way to highlight the main advantage of your result: the method performs well even if only *some* of the hidden variables `h_i` have a small dimension (i.e., only some mode-ranks are small), which is a powerful and practical scenario.

**2. Gaps in Theoretical Justification:**
*   **Missing Proof of Lemma 5:** The proof of Lemma 5 is stated to be in the supplementary material but appears to be missing. This lemma is critical for the proof of Theorem 2, and its absence is a major omission that needs to be rectified.
*   **Clarification for Lemma 4:** The proof of Lemma 4 is only cited from another paper. While acceptable for minor results, given its role, providing a proof sketch or the full proof in the appendix would make the paper more self-contained and easier to verify.
*   **Justification in Theorem 2:** The step where inequality (12) is said to "immediately lead to" (13) requires justification. The argument rests on the arbitrariness of the decomposition of `W*` (specifically, using a singleton decomposition where `W*(k) = W*` for the mode `k` with minimum rank). This crucial step should be explicitly explained.
*   **Permutation Ambiguity in Theorem 2:** The theorem should clarify that the components `W*(k)` are recovered only up to a permutation, which is standard for mixture model recovery but is an important detail to state for completeness.

**3. Discussion and Context:**
*   **Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work on tensor decompositions. Specifically, a comparison with the Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD) and its variants is warranted. You should mention existing approximation bounds for HOSVD (e.g., from De Lathauwer et al., 2000) and cite the comprehensive survey by Kolda and Bader (2009, Section 4) to properly contextualize your convex approach against these classical alternating methods.
*   **Incoherence Assumption:** The discussion following eq. (10) comparing your incoherence assumption to those in [1, 3, 10] is potentially misleading, as the nature of the assumptions can be quite different. It would be clearer to explain your assumption on its own terms and perhaps remove the direct comparison to avoid confusion.
*   **Computational Complexity:** The paper lacks any discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed convex optimization methods. This is a critical point for practitioners. Please add a brief analysis of the per-iteration cost and overall complexity of solving the optimization problems.
*   **Tensor Rank Terminology:** To aid readers less familiar with tensor algebra, please explicitly state that "mode-rank" is also known as "multilinear rank." It would also be helpful to briefly mention that other notions of tensor rank (e.g., CP rank) exist and differ from the multilinear rank used here.

**4. Notational and Minor Fixes:**
*   The superscript notation for the components, `\hat{\cal W}^{(k)}`, is confusing because `(k)` is also used for mode-k unfolding. Consider a clearer notation, for instance using subscripts `\hat{\cal W}_k`.
*   The notation for the mode-k rank of the components is inconsistent (e.g., `\bar{r}_k` is used in the definition after eq. (9) and again in eq. (18), but not in Theorem 2). Please use a single, consistent notation throughout.
*   Abstract typo: "structures Schatten" should be "structured Schatten".
*   There appears to be a missing closing parenthesis in the last line of page 12 of the supplementary material.

### Conclusion

This paper presents a valuable and novel theoretical contribution to the literature on convex tensor decomposition. The analysis of the latent Schatten norm approach and the result that its performance scales with the minimum mode-rank are significant. However, the paper's impact is currently limited by several missing technical details in the proofs and a lack of clarity in motivating the problem for a broader audience. I would recommend acceptance, provided the authors can address the major technical gaps (especially the missing proof of Lemma 5 and the justification for Theorem 2) and improve the paper's accessibility and context as suggested above. With these revisions, this will be an excellent addition to the NIPS proceedings.
