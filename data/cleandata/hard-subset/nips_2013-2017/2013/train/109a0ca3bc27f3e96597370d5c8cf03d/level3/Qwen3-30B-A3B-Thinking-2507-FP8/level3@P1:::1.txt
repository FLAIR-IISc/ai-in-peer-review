

This paper investigates structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically comparing the "latent" and "overlapped" approaches. The authors introduce a generalization of these norms and prove a duality relationship between them, which they leverage to establish theoretical guarantees for the latent approach. The main theoretical contribution shows that the latent approach's mean squared error scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach's error depends on the average of the mode-k ranks. The authors confirm their theoretical predictions through numerical simulations.

The paper demonstrates good technical quality with rigorous proofs for their theoretical results. The duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is interesting and generalizes previous work on structured sparsity, connecting to the group lasso literature. The authors provide clear theoretical bounds for the latent approach under Gaussian noise and show that its performance is superior when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode, which matches the empirical observations in Figure 1. The numerical experiments confirm the predicted scaling behavior of the mean squared error, adding credibility to their theoretical analysis.

The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, though the early placement of Figure 1 makes interpretation difficult for readers without prior context. There are a few minor typographical errors and phrasing issues that could be addressed in revision. The authors have carefully related their work to previous NIPS papers on tensor decomposition [9, 15, 23, 25, 26] and structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21], showing awareness of the relevant literature.

However, the paper's significance for NIPS is limited. While the technical contributions are sound, the results are relatively narrow and incremental. The authors generalize existing norms but do not propose new algorithms or demonstrate their practical utility in machine learning contexts. The paper fails to examine the approaches in real machine learning applications, either theoretically or experimentally, which weakens its relevance to the broader machine learning community. The theoretical results are valuable for tensor decomposition specialists but don't provide clear insights for practitioners working on machine learning problems.

The originality of the work is somewhat limited. The "new class" of structured Schatten norms presented is essentially a generalization of existing approaches rather than a fundamentally new concept. The duality result is interesting but builds directly on prior work in structured sparsity, and the main theoretical contribution (showing the latent approach's superior performance) is largely consistent with the empirical observations in [25]. The techniques used are standard for this domain, lacking significant novelty in methodology.

A major weakness is the absence of matching lower bounds for the upper bounds presented. While the authors provide upper bounds for the latent approach's performance, they don't establish corresponding lower bounds to demonstrate tightness of their results. This limits the strength of their algorithmic comparison, as it's unclear whether the bounds are optimal or could be improved.

The paper's title and abstract are somewhat misleading. The authors claim to propose "a new class of structured Schatten norms," but the paper primarily analyzes existing norms rather than introducing novel ones. The abstract also overstates the novelty by suggesting the paper presents a new framework for structured Schatten norms, when in fact it mainly generalizes and analyzes existing approaches.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating practical applications in machine learning tasks, such as collaborative filtering or image analysis, where tensor decomposition is commonly used. Without such examples, the connection to machine learning remains tenuous, and the paper's impact is limited to the tensor decomposition community.

Regarding the four evaluation criteria:
- Quality: The paper is technically sound with proper theoretical analysis and empirical validation, though the lack of lower bounds weakens the theoretical contribution.
- Clarity: The paper is well-organized and readable, with minor presentation issues.
- Originality: The work represents a modest extension of existing approaches rather than a significant new contribution.
- Significance: The results are narrow in scope and lack clear connections to broader machine learning applications, limiting their impact.

In conclusion, this paper makes a technically sound contribution to tensor decomposition theory, but its narrow focus and limited relevance to machine learning applications make it a marginal fit for NIPS. The theoretical results are interesting for tensor decomposition specialists, but the paper fails to connect these results to practical machine learning problems or demonstrate broader significance. The work represents a solid incremental advance but lacks the novelty and broader impact expected for NIPS. The authors would benefit from extending their analysis to real machine learning applications and establishing tighter theoretical bounds with matching lower bounds.
