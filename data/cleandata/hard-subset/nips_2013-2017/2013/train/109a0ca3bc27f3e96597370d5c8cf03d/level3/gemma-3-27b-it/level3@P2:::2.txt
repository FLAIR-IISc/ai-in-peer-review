This paper presents a theoretical analysis of convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, specifically focusing on a “latent” approach proposed by Tomioka et al. (2013) and comparing it to an “overlapped” approach. The core contribution lies in providing theoretical guarantees for the latent approach, demonstrating that its performance scales with the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, explaining its empirical superiority in certain scenarios. The paper also establishes a duality result between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms and validates its theoretical findings through numerical experiments.

The work builds upon a growing body of research in convex relaxation for tensor decomposition, moving beyond the traditionally non-convex methods like alternating least squares or higher-order orthogonal iteration (De Lathauwer et al., 2000). Previous work (e.g., Gandy et al., 2014; Tomioka et al., 2013) has explored convex approaches, but theoretical understanding has lagged empirical results. This paper bridges that gap, offering a rigorous analysis of the latent approach.  The paper effectively connects these tensor decomposition techniques to the broader field of structured sparsity (Jenatton et al., 2011) and highlights parallels with group Lasso variations.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Rigor:** The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the latent approach, offering consistency and deterministic bounds on reconstruction error.  The key result demonstrating scaling with the *minimum* mode-k rank is particularly significant.
* **Duality Result:** The established duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable contribution, offering insights into the underlying mathematical properties of these techniques.
* **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments corroborate the theoretical predictions, reinforcing the validity of the analysis and demonstrating the practical benefits of the latent approach. The careful comparison against the overlapped approach is well-executed.
* **Clear Connection to Related Work:** The authors appropriately situate their work within the existing literature on tensor decomposition and structured sparsity, providing relevant citations.
* **Well-Written (Generally):** The paper is largely clear and well-organized. The mathematical notation is standard, and the presentation is logical.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Accessibility for Machine Learning Audience:** The introduction, while technically sound, could be more accessible to a broader machine learning audience.  Adding a latent-variable graphical model, illustrating the relationship between hidden variables (hi) and observed variables (xi) with a linear mapping, would greatly improve understanding. Explicitly stating that the multilinear ranks correspond to the dimensions of the hidden variables would solidify this connection.  This would emphasize that the latent approach excels when only *some* of these hidden variables have low dimensionality.
* **Technical Clarifications Needed:** Several technical points require clarification. The transition from (12) to (13) in Theorem 2 needs more explanation – how does recovering individual components up to permutation lead to a bound on the overall error? The proof of Lemma 5 is missing (acknowledged in the paper) and is crucial for Theorem 2. The authors should either include the proof or provide a more detailed justification for its validity. The discussion around Lemma 4 feels incomplete, and a clearer proof or reference is needed.
* **Related Work Gaps:** While related work is cited, the paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of Higher Order Singular Value Decomposition (HOSVD) and its variants, including existing approximation bounds (Kolda, 2009). The term "mode-rank" should be explicitly defined as synonymous with "multilinear rank," and a note acknowledging the existence of multiple tensor rank notions would be helpful.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper lacks any discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed methods. This is a crucial practical consideration and should be addressed.
* **Incoherence Discussion:** The comparison to incoherence assumptions in [1, 3, 10] after equation (10) is misleading and should be removed. The context and meaning of incoherence differ in those papers and this work.
* **Notational Inconsistencies:** The superscript notation for components (e.g.,  \hat{\cal W}^{(k)}) is confusing and should be replaced with clearer symbols.  Inconsistent use of notation for k-mode rank (e.g., \bar{r}_k and \underline{r}_k) should be resolved; consider adopting a consistent notation like `mult_rank(tensor)`.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

* The paper addresses a significant theoretical gap in the field of convex tensor decomposition.
* The theoretical results are strong and well-supported by empirical evidence.
* The duality result is a valuable contribution to the understanding of structured Schatten norms.
* The paper is well-written and clearly presented, despite some minor technical issues.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Several technical clarifications are needed to ensure the rigor and clarity of the theoretical results.
* The paper could benefit from a more accessible introduction and a more comprehensive discussion of related work.
* The lack of discussion regarding computational complexity is a significant omission.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong and important paper with the potential to significantly advance the field of convex tensor decomposition. However, addressing the identified weaknesses – particularly the technical clarifications and the addition of a discussion on computational complexity – is crucial. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors addressing the points raised in this review.
