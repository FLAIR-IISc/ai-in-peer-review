### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates a convex optimization framework for tensor decomposition based on a class of structured Schatten norms. The primary focus is on comparing two specific norms: the "overlapped" Schatten norm, which penalizes the sum of trace norms of matrix unfoldings, and the "latent" Schatten norm, which penalizes a decomposition of the tensor into a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. The main contribution is a rigorous theoretical analysis of the "latent" approach in a noisy setting. The authors prove that the mean squared error of the latent method scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the underlying true tensor. This contrasts with previous analyses of the overlapped approach, where the error scales with the *average* of the mode-k ranks. This key result theoretically explains the empirically observed superiority of the latent approach, particularly for tensors that are low-rank in only one mode. The analysis is supported by a novel duality result connecting the overlapped and latent norms, which is of broader interest to the structured sparsity community. Numerical experiments are provided to validate the theoretical scaling predictions.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality & Significance:**

The paper is technically strong and addresses an important problem in the analysis of high-dimensional data. The theoretical results are significant and provide a clear, novel insight into the behavior of convex tensor decomposition methods.

*   **Strength:** The main theoretical result (Theorems 2 and 3), demonstrating that the latent approach's error bound depends on `min_k r_k`, is a significant contribution. It elegantly explains why this method can adapt to the "easiest" structure in the data, performing as well as if the lowest-rank mode were known in advance. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for preferring the latent over the overlapped approach.
*   **Strength:** The duality between the overlapped `Sp/q`-norm and the latent `Sp*/q*`-norm (Lemma 1) is an elegant finding that connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity and may be of independent interest.
*   **Weakness:** The paper is not entirely self-contained, and some key technical steps in the proofs are not fully justified. For instance, the proof of Lemma 5, which appears crucial for Theorem 2, is missing from the supplementary material. Similarly, Lemma 4 is presented with only a citation, but given its importance, a proof sketch or more detailed explanation would strengthen the paper's technical soundness.
*   **Weakness:** In Theorem 2, the transition from the bound on the individual components (Eq. 12) to the bound on the sum (Eq. 13) is not sufficiently explained. It relies on the "arbitrariness of the decomposition," but this step needs to be formally justified. Also, it should be clarified that the components `W*(k)` are only recovered up to a permutation.

**Clarity:**

The paper is generally well-written, but its clarity and accessibility could be significantly improved, especially for a broader machine learning audience.

*   **Strength:** The core idea comparing the `sum of sqrt(ranks)` dependency of the overlapped approach with the `min of sqrt(rank)` dependency of the latent approach is clearly articulated. The experiments in Section 4 effectively illustrate and validate this central theoretical claim.
*   **Weakness:** The motivation for studying low multilinear rank tensors could be stronger. The paper would benefit from including a simple latent variable model (e.g., via a graphical model diagram) where observed data `x_i` are generated from hidden variables `h_i`. This could illustrate how the moment tensor of the observed data admits a Tucker decomposition where the multilinear ranks correspond to the dimensions of the hidden variables. This would also provide a natural motivation for the main advantage of the latent approach: when only some of the hidden variables have a small dimension (low rank in only a few modes).
*   **Weakness:** The notation is at times confusing. The use of superscripts `W^(k)` for the components of the latent decomposition is easily confusable with the mode-k unfolding `W(k)`. Using a different symbol would improve readability. Furthermore, the notation for mode-k rank is inconsistent (e.g., `r_k`, `r_bar_k`). Using a consistent operator like `mult_rank_k(W)` might be clearer.
*   **Weakness:** The discussion of the incoherence assumption (Eq. 10) and its comparison to work in matrix completion [1, 3, 11] is potentially misleading, as the nature of the assumptions is different. The authors should either clarify this connection more carefully or remove the comparison to avoid confusion.
*   **Minor Typos:** There is a typo in the abstract ("structures Schatten" should be "structured Schatten") and a missing parenthesis in the last line of page 12 of the supplementary material.

**Originality:**

The paper builds upon a recent line of work on convex tensor decomposition but makes novel and distinct contributions.

*   **Strength:** While the overlapped and latent models have been proposed before [25], this paper is the first to provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach's superior performance in the denoising setting. The error bounds and their dependence on the minimum rank are novel.
*   **Weakness:** The paper could better position its contribution with respect to the broader tensor decomposition literature. A brief discussion of classical methods like Higher-Order SVD (HOSVD) and their known approximation bounds (e.g., citing Kolda and Bader's 2009 survey) would provide valuable context. It should also be noted that "mode-rank" is commonly referred to as "multilinear rank" and that several other notions of tensor rank exist.

**Additional Suggestions:**
*   A brief discussion of the computational complexity of solving the proposed optimization problems would be a valuable addition for practitioners.

### Arguments for Acceptance
1.  Provides a novel and significant theoretical result showing that the latent approach for tensor decomposition adapts to the minimal mode-rank of the tensor.
2.  The analysis clearly explains the performance difference between the latent and overlapped approaches, resolving an open question from previous work.
3.  The duality result between the two norm classes is an elegant contribution of independent interest.
4.  The theoretical claims are well-supported by numerical experiments.

### Arguments against Acceptance
1.  The clarity and motivation could be significantly improved to make the paper accessible to a wider NIPS audience.
2.  The proofs contain technical gaps, including a missing proof for a key lemma (Lemma 5) and insufficient justification for a central step in the main theorem (Theorem 2).
3.  The discussion of assumptions and related work needs to be refined to be more precise.

In summary, this is a technically important paper with a strong core contribution. The weaknesses are primarily in the presentation and the completeness of the proofs. I would be inclined to accept this paper, provided the authors can address the technical gaps in the proofs and improve the clarity of the manuscript during the rebuttal and revision period.
