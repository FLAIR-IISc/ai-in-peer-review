Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition with structured Schatten norms. It focuses on the “latent” Schatten-1 norm formulation, which decomposes a tensor into a sum of K components, each low-rank in one mode, and compares it with the previously used “overlapped” Schatten-1 norm that jointly penalizes all mode unfoldings. The authors: (a) formalize a broader class of structured Schatten Sp/q norms for tensors; (b) prove a duality between overlapped Sp/q and latent Sp*/q* norms, paralleling dualities known in structured sparsity (overlapped vs latent group lasso); (c) derive recovery/denoising guarantees for the latent S1/1 approach, including a generic consistency result, deterministic bounds under an incoherence-type condition, and Gaussian-noise bounds with a practical choice of λ; and (d) provide simulations showing the mean squared error (MSE) scales with the “latent rank complexity,” and that the latent approach often outperforms the overlapped approach.

The work connects to prior NIPS and related literature on: convex low-rank recovery and decomposable regularizers (Negahban et al.; Agarwal et al.), structured sparsity and group norms (Jenatton et al.; Obozinski et al.), convex tensor completion/decomposition (e.g., Tomioka et al. NIPS’11/12), and lower bounds for tensor recovery (Mu et al.). The central contribution is to establish that, when the true tensor is low-rank in an unknown single mode, the latent approach achieves an error scaling comparable to knowing the best mode in advance, contrasting with the averaged-rank dependence of the overlapped approach.

Strengths
- Clear theoretical message: latent S1/1 achieves rates governed by min mode rank, versus average (sqrt) rank for overlapped norms; this matches empirical observations and provides guidance for practice.
- Useful duality result for structured Schatten norms, aligning tensor norms with the overlapped/latent group sparsity framework.
- Rigorous results: basic consistency; deterministic bounds with explicit dependence on component ranks; Gaussian-noise bounds with a data-independent λ; comparisons to known overlapped-norm bounds.
- Empirical validation: simulations align with the predicted scaling and support the advantage of latent regularization.
- Writing is generally clear and the paper is well-organized; proofs appear careful.

Weaknesses
- Scope and impact: the contribution is relatively narrow and technical, centered on norm design and denoising bounds; the ML impact is indirect. No applications on real ML datasets (e.g., recommendation or neuroimaging) are provided.
- Incremental novelty: techniques largely adapt known decomposable-regularizer analyses and structured sparsity ideas to the tensor setting; the duality is elegant but unsurprising given the group-sparsity analogies.
- Lack of lower bounds for the latent approach. While Mu et al. provide lower bounds for overlapped formulations, matching lower bounds for latent norms (or an argument on tightness) would strengthen the comparison.
- Assumptions vs experiments: deterministic bounds introduce additional spectral-norm incoherence constraints and an α parameter; it is unclear if experiments enforce these constraints or how α is selected in practice.
- The “new class” of Sp/q norms is introduced, but analysis and algorithms focus on S1/1; no empirical or algorithmic exploration for other p,q is provided. The abstract could temper claims or include at least one non-1/1 example.
- Minor presentation issues: Figure 1 appears before sufficient context; a few typos/wording issues.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: Technically sound with careful statements, reasonable assumptions, and appropriate comparisons. Proof techniques are standard but correctly applied. Upper bounds are informative; lack of lower bounds for the latent case is a gap.
- Clarity: Mostly clear and well-structured; definitions and notation are precise. Recommend moving early figures later, clarifying the role of α and whether constraints are enforced in practice, and polishing minor typos.
- Originality: Moderate. The duality and adaptation of decomposable-regularizer analysis to the latent tensor norm are solid but build on known frameworks; the main novelty is the particular rates and their implications for latent vs overlapped tensor norms.
- Significance: Moderate for the optimization/statistics-of-tensors community; less direct for broader NIPS audience absent real applications. Results will be of interest to researchers using convex tensor methods.

Questions and suggestions for the authors
- Clarify whether the spectral-norm constraints used in Theorem 2 are enforced by the solver in experiments; if not, discuss the practical relevance of those bounds and how α should be set.
- Provide guidance for selecting λ in practice beyond the Gaussian model, and discuss sensitivity.
- Tightness: can you provide lower bounds (even in a simplified setting) or empirical evidence on optimality of min-rank dependence for the latent norm?
- Consider adding at least one real-data experiment (e.g., a small neuroimaging or recommendation dataset) to demonstrate practical impact.
- If claiming a “new class” of norms, include one non-1/1 example (e.g., overlapped S1/∞ or latent S∞/1) with algorithmic notes or a brief empirical comparison, or temper the wording.
- Move Figure 1 to after the norms are defined, and fix minor typos (e.g., “betters” → “better”).

Pros for acceptance
- Clear theoretical advance clarifying when and why latent tensor norms outperform overlapped norms.
- General duality result likely useful beyond this paper.
- Solid empirical support aligning with theory.
- Good fit to the convex learning/structured estimation thread at NIPS.

Cons for acceptance
- Incremental novelty; methods and proof techniques are largely extensions of known ideas.
- Limited ML impact as presented; no real-data demonstrations.
- No lower bounds for the latent approach; comparative story is one-sided.
- Claims about a “new class” of norms are underdeveloped empirically/algorithmically.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is technically sound and clarifies an important point about tensor norm choices, with neat duality and clean bounds. However, the contribution feels narrow and incremental for NIPS, and the lack of real-data validation and lower bounds limits impact. Strengthening with a real ML application and tighter theory for latent norms (or empirical tightness) would move this to a clear accept.

Reviewer confidence: medium.
