This paper studies convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms, unifying “overlapped” and “latent” formulations and proving a useful duality between them. The main technical contribution is a set of recovery bounds for the latent approach that scale with the minimum mode rank, thereby explaining empirical advantages over overlapped formulations in noisy denoising settings. Theoretical results (consistency; deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds) are complemented by simulations that track the predicted scaling. The work connects to structured sparsity (group lasso and its latent variant) and classic tensor models (Tucker/HOSVD), and is timely for ML applications that process multiway data.

Strengths
- Technically solid and important: the latent/overlapped duality and the min-mode-rank error bounds sharpen our understanding of convex tensor methods and align with practice.
- Clear problem formulation and empirical validation that match theory.
- Broad relevance across ML areas that use tensors (e.g., neuroimaging, recommender systems).

Weaknesses and required clarifications
- Motivation and accessibility: please add a latent-variable graphical model (hi → xi with a linear hidden→observed map and arbitrary dependence among h) to motivate ML readers. Explain how the observed moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition, include a figure, and note that multilinear ranks equal hidden-variable dimensions. Emphasize the key advantage: only some hidden variables may be low-dimensional.
- Theorem 2: state that components are identifiable only up to permutation; justify precisely why Eq. (12) “immediately” implies Eq. (13) (spell out the singleton decomposition step).
- Proofs: provide the missing proof of Lemma 5 (it is pivotal for Theorem 2) and either include a self-contained proof of Lemma 4 or clearly delimit what is imported from the cited work.
- Related work: add HOSVD and variants, discuss approximation bounds, and cite Kolda & Bader (SIAM Review, Sec. 4). State explicitly that “mode-rank” = “multilinear rank,” and that multiple rank notions exist.
- Complexity: briefly discuss computational complexity (per-iteration SVD costs, scaling with K, nk, and convergence of ADMM).
- Incoherence: the post-(10) discussion is misleading relative to the incoherence notions in [1,3,10]; either carefully reconcile or remove the comparison.
- Notation/typos: fix superscripts for components (e.g., use W_hat[k] or W_hat^{(k)} consistently); resolve inconsistent k-mode rank symbols (e.g., use mult_rank(·)); fix the missing parenthesis (supp. p.12) and Abstract typo (“structured Schatten”).

Pros for acceptance
- New theoretical insight (duality; min-mode-rank guarantees) with empirical support.
- Bridges structured sparsity and tensor decomposition.

Cons for acceptance
- Several proof gaps and clarity/notation issues that should be addressed.
- Motivation and broader accessibility need strengthening.

Overall, the paper’s novelty—showing that structured Schatten regularization yields Tucker-style decompositions with recovery guarantees and that the latent formulation adapts to the smallest mode rank—is compelling. With the above fixes, this would be a strong contribution.
