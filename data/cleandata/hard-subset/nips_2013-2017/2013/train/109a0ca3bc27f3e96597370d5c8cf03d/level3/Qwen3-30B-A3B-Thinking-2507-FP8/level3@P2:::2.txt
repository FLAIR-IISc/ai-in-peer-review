

# Review for NIPS 2013-2017

This paper studies structured Schatten norms for tensor decomposition, specifically analyzing the relationship between two convex optimization approaches: the "overlapped" approach (which penalizes all modes to be jointly low-rank) and the "latent" approach (which decomposes the tensor into a mixture of tensors each low-rank in a specific mode). The authors establish a duality between these two approaches, showing that the dual of the overlapped Schatten norm is the latent Schatten norm, and vice versa. Their main theoretical contribution is demonstrating that the latent approach's error scales with the minimal mode-k rank of the underlying tensor, whereas the overlapped approach scales with the average mode-k rank. This explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in prior work.

The paper makes a significant contribution to the tensor decomposition literature, which has largely relied on non-convex optimization methods. While convex approaches have been proposed in [9, 15, 23, 25, 26], this paper provides the first theoretical analysis explaining why the latent approach outperforms the overlapped approach in certain settings. The authors connect their work to structured sparsity literature [1, 2, 12, 13, 17, 21], showing that the two tensor decomposition approaches correspond to the overlapped and latent group lasso frameworks. This connection is valuable for the machine learning community as it bridges tensor methods with well-established structured sparsity techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Contribution:** The paper provides a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach for tensor decomposition, establishing tight error bounds that depend on the minimal mode-k rank rather than the average rank. This is a significant advance over prior work [26], which only provided guarantees for the overlapped approach.

2. **Novel Duality Result:** The duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a novel contribution that extends the structured sparsity literature. This result is both mathematically interesting and practically useful for developing new tensor decomposition methods.

3. **Empirical Validation:** The numerical experiments confirm the theoretical predictions, showing that the mean squared error scales linearly with the predicted complexity measures (TR complexity for overlapped approach, LR complexity for latent approach).

4. **Clarity of Main Argument:** The paper clearly explains why the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach in the case where the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode, which is a common scenario in applications like neuroimaging.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Motivation for ML Audience:** The paper would benefit from a clearer motivation for machine learning audiences. Adding a latent-variable graphical model showing how observed data relates to hidden variables (with multilinear ranks corresponding to dimensions of hidden variables) would make the problem more accessible. The current abstract and introduction are somewhat technical without sufficient context for broader ML readers.

2. **Notational Inconsistencies:** Several notational issues need clarification. The paper uses inconsistent notation for mode-k ranks (e.g., $\bar{r}_k$ and $\underline{r}_k$), and the superscripts for components ($\hat{\cal W}^{(k)}$) are confusing. The authors should adopt clearer notation throughout.

3. **Technical Clarifications Needed:** 
   - Theorem 2 requires clarification that components are recovered only up to permutation.
   - The step from (12) to (13) needs justification.
   - The proof of Lemma 5 (key to Theorem 2) is missing.
   - Lemma 4 should be proven rather than just cited.

4. **Incoherence Discussion:** The comparison to incoherence in [1,3,10] after equation (10) is misleading and should be removed or significantly revised.

5. **Related Work:** The paper should cite Kolda's tensor decomposition survey [14] to properly contextualize the relationship with HOSVD and other tensor decomposition methods.

6. **Computational Complexity:** A brief discussion of computational complexity would be valuable for practical applications.

**Recommendation:**

This is a technically strong paper that makes an important contribution to the theory of convex tensor decomposition. The main theoretical insight about the minimal mode-k rank dependency is novel and well-supported by both theory and experiments. The paper is well-written overall and addresses an important problem in tensor analysis.

However, the paper requires several technical clarifications and notational improvements before it can be accepted. The missing proofs (Lemma 5) and inconsistent notation are particularly problematic for a theoretical paper. The authors should also improve the motivation for the broader machine learning community.

**Pro and Con Acceptance:**

*Pro:*
- Provides the first theoretical explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach
- Establishes a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms
- Theoretical guarantees are tight and match empirical observations
- Connects tensor decomposition to structured sparsity literature

*Con:*
- Requires significant notational corrections
- Missing proofs for key technical results
- Inconsistent treatment of tensor rank concepts
- Limited motivation for machine learning audience
- Some technical claims need clarification (e.g., Theorem 2)

Given the technical strength of the paper and its significant contribution to the field, I recommend acceptance with major revision. The authors should address the technical clarifications and notational issues identified above. With these revisions, this paper would make a valuable contribution to NIPS.
