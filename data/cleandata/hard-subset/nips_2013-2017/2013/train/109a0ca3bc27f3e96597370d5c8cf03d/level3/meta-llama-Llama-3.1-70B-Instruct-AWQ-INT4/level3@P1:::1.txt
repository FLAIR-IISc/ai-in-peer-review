This paper presents a framework for structured Schatten norms, including both the overlapped Schatten 1-norm and the latent Schatten 1-norm, which have been recently proposed in the context of convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition. The authors establish a duality between the two types of norms, which is also interesting in the general context of structured sparsity. They then rigorously study the performance of the latent approach for tensor decomposition and show that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales no greater than the minimum mode-k rank of the underlying true tensor. This result explains why the latent approach performs better than the overlapped approach in certain settings.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the theoretical results and their implications. The numerical simulations provide strong evidence for the theoretical findings, and the comparison with the overlapped approach is informative. The authors also discuss the limitations of their results and suggest potential avenues for future work.

However, the paper has some limitations. The results are narrow and technical, and only peripherally related to machine learning, which may limit their impact on the NIPS community. The paper does not examine the approaches in real machine learning contexts, either theoretically or experimentally, which would have strengthened the paper's contribution. Additionally, the techniques used in the paper lack novelty, and the upper bounds provided are not matched with lower bounds, which weakens the algorithmic comparisons.

In terms of clarity, the paper is generally well-written, but there are a few minor issues. Figure 1 appears too early in the paper to be interpretable, and there are a few typos and phrasing errors throughout the text. The abstract could be rephrased to more accurately reflect the content of the paper, as it currently gives the impression that the paper proposes a new class of structured Schatten norms, which is not entirely accurate.

Overall, I would give this paper a score of 7 out of 10. While it presents some interesting theoretical results and provides strong evidence for their validity, its narrow focus and lack of novelty in techniques may limit its impact on the NIPS community.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents some interesting theoretical results on the performance of the latent approach for tensor decomposition.

* The comparison with the overlapped approach is informative and highlights the advantages of the latent approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

I would recommend accepting this paper, but with some revisions to address the minor issues mentioned above.
