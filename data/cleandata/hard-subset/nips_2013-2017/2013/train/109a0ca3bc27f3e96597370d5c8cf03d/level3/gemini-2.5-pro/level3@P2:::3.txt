This paper studies convex-optimization-based tensor decomposition, focusing on a framework of "structured Schatten norms." It specifically provides a rigorous theoretical analysis for the "latent" approach, where a tensor is modeled as a sum of components, each being low-rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the "overlapped" approach, previously analyzed in [26], which regularizes a single tensor to be simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

The paper's primary contributions are twofold. First, it establishes a novel duality between the "overlapped" Sp/q-norm and the "latent" Sp*/q*-norm. This is an elegant result of independent interest that connects the tensor decomposition literature to the broader field of structured sparsity [13, 21]. Second, leveraging this duality, the paper derives performance guarantees for the latent approach in a denoising setting. The key theoretical result is that the mean squared error of the latent estimator scales with the *minimum* of the mode-k ranks of the underlying true tensor. This provides a formal explanation for the empirical success of the latent approach over the overlapped one, especially in cases where the tensor possesses a low-rank structure along a single, unknown mode. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations that confirm the predicted scaling of the error.

**Strengths:**

*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete and rigorous piece of theoretical work. The claims are well-supported by the proofs provided in the appendix. The analysis connecting the duality of the structured norms to the final performance bounds is particularly strong.

*   **Originality:** The work is highly original. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and provides a unifying perspective. While the latent approach itself was proposed in [25], this paper provides the first theoretical analysis of its performance, which is a significant contribution. The analysis reveals a key insight: the latent method automatically adapts to the unknown mode with the lowest rank.

*   **Significance:** The problem of tensor decomposition is of great importance in machine learning and data analysis. This paper makes a meaningful advance by providing a theoretically-backed convex method that demonstrably improves upon a recent predecessor. The result that the latent approach performs as well as if it knew the lowest-rank mode is a powerful one that researchers and practitioners are likely to build on. The duality framework is also likely to inspire further work on structured regularization.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of defining the necessary concepts, motivating the problem with clear examples (e.g., Figure 1), and structuring the theoretical development logically.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Experimental Evaluation:** While the experiments successfully validate the paper's theoretical claims regarding error scaling, their scope is somewhat narrow. The comparison is confined to the two convex approaches (overlapped vs. latent). To better situate the work's practical importance, a comparison with established non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., HOSVD or alternating least squares) would be highly valuable. This would help readers understand the trade-offs in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and robustness to noise compared to traditional baselines.

*   **Missing Experimental Details:** The description of the experimental setup could be more detailed. Specifically, the paper does not explain how the multilinear ranks (r1, r2, r3) were chosen for the synthetic data generation. This detail is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding whether the experimental conditions were particularly favorable to the latent approach (e.g., by using highly imbalanced ranks).

*   **Incomplete Discussion of Different Rank Structures:** The theoretical analysis and motivation focus heavily on the scenario where the latent approach is expected to excel—when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The paper would be more complete with a discussion of the opposite regime, where a tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes. While the experiments suggest the latent approach still performs well, it would be beneficial to include a theoretical discussion on why this might be the case, or to consider if a different regularizer within their framework (e.g., the overlapped S1/∞-norm) might be better suited for that scenario.

**Summary of Pros and Cons:**

**Pro:**
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical analysis for the latent Schatten norm approach.
*   The derived error bound scales with the minimum mode-k rank, a significant improvement over prior work.
*   Introduces a novel and elegant duality result for structured Schatten norms, which is of independent interest.
*   The paper is clear, well-organized, and technically sound.

**Con:**
*   The experimental section lacks a comparison to standard non-convex tensor decomposition methods.
*   Key details regarding the data generation process in the experiments are missing.
*   The theoretical discussion could be expanded to cover cases where tensors are simultaneously low-rank across all modes.

In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a significant and original contribution to the theory of tensor decomposition. The theoretical results are both novel and important, providing a clear justification for preferring the latent approach over the overlapped approach in many settings. While the experimental section could be strengthened with additional baselines and details, the quality of the theoretical work is high. This paper advances the state of the art and is likely to be influential.
