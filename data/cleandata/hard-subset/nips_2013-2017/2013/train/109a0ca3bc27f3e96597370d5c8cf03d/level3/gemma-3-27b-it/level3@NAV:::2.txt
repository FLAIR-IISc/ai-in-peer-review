## Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates convex relaxation approaches to tensor decomposition, focusing on a “latent” approach utilizing structured Schatten norms. The core contribution lies in theoretically analyzing the performance of this “latent” method, showing it can achieve reconstruction accuracy comparable to knowing the tensor's smallest rank – a significant improvement over existing convex methods, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The authors also present a duality result for these structured Schatten norms and validate their theory with numerical experiments.

The paper is timely, as convex tensor decomposition is an active research area aiming to overcome the limitations of traditional non-convex methods. While the motivation initially focuses on neuroimaging, the broader applicability to multivariate data is well-stated. The introduction effectively establishes the context and outlines the limitations of prior work, particularly regarding hyperparameter selection (ranks) in Tucker decomposition. 

The strengths of this work are substantial. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, and the established connection between the latent approach and the minimal mode-rank is a key result. The duality result for the Schatten norms is also a valuable contribution. The numerical results, while not extensive, support the theoretical findings, demonstrating the superior performance of the latent approach in several cases.

However, several weaknesses hinder the paper's clarity and impact. The initial motivation could be strengthened for a machine learning audience. The current framing leans heavily on tensor-specific terminology without sufficiently connecting it to established concepts like low-rank matrix factorization. A latent-variable graphical model illustrating the proposed decomposition (hi -> xi, linear mapping, arbitrary dependence among hi) would greatly enhance understanding. This figure should also explicitly show how the observed moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition, with a caption clarifying that the multilinear ranks correspond to the dimensions of the hidden variables. Highlighting the advantage of potentially small hidden variable dimensions is crucial.

Several technical points need addressing: Theorem 2 requires clarification regarding permutation invariance of the recovered components and justification for the leap from (12) to (13). The proof of Lemma 5 is missing, as is a clear proof or justification for Lemma 4 beyond citation. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, including HOSVD and its variants, along with approximation bounds and a citation to Kolda’s survey. The use of “mode-rank” and “multilinear rank” is inconsistent; standardization is needed. Finally, computational complexity should be briefly discussed, and the incoherence comparison after (10) should be removed as it's misleading. Numerous notational inconsistencies (superscripts for components, k-mode rank) need correction.

**Arguments for Acceptance:** Technically sound, significant theoretical contribution, well-written (despite minor issues), and supported by numerical validation.

**Arguments against Acceptance:** Several technical and notational fixes are needed; motivation could be stronger for a broader audience; related work section needs expansion.



Overall, this is a strong paper with the potential to significantly contribute to the field. Addressing the identified weaknesses would substantially improve its clarity and impact. I recommend acceptance contingent on major revisions.
