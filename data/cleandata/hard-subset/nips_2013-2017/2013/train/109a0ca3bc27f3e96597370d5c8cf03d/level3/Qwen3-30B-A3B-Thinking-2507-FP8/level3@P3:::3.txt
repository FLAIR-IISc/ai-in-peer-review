

# Review of "Convex Tensor Decomposition via Structured Schatten Norm Regularization"

This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition through structured Schatten norms, specifically comparing the "overlapped" and "latent" approaches. The authors provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent approach, demonstrating that it outperforms the overlapped approach when the true tensor is low-rank in a specific mode. The paper builds upon prior work on convex tensor decomposition (e.g., [9, 15, 23, 25, 26]) while connecting to broader structured sparsity literature [2, 13, 17, 21]. The authors establish a novel duality between overlapped and latent Schatten norms, which generalizes results from group sparsity literature and provides theoretical justification for the empirical success of the latent approach.

The paper's main strengths are its strong theoretical contributions. The authors establish precise theoretical bounds for the latent Schatten norm approach, showing that the mean squared error scales with the minimal mode-k rank rather than the average mode-k rank as in the overlapped approach. This theoretical insight explains the empirical superiority of the latent approach observed in previous work. The duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is particularly elegant and of independent interest to the structured sparsity community. The paper is well-organized, with clear definitions, logical flow, and comprehensive proofs in the supplementary material. The authors also connect their work to relevant literature, including the recent work by Mu et al. [19] on lower bounds for tensor decomposition.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that limit its impact. The experimental evaluation is notably brief and lacks sufficient detail. The authors do not explain how the Tucker ranks (r₁,...,rₖ) were chosen for their experiments, which is critical for reproducibility and understanding the experimental setup. Additionally, the paper omits comparison to non-convex tensor decomposition approaches (such as higher-order SVD or alternating least squares), which are the dominant methods in practice and would provide valuable context for the proposed convex approach. The authors also do not analyze the behavior of group regularization (e.g., l_{1,∞}) when the tensor is simultaneously low-rank in all modes, which would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

The paper's significance is high, as tensor decomposition is a fundamental problem in machine learning with applications in neuroimaging, collaborative filtering, and environmental monitoring. The authors meaningfully improve upon existing results by providing the first theoretical explanation for the empirical superiority of the latent approach, which was previously observed but not explained. The theoretical guarantees are clean and well-supported, and the numerical experiments confirm the predicted scaling behavior.

In terms of quality, the theoretical analysis is sound and well-supported, though the experimental section is weak. The paper demonstrates technical soundness in its theoretical contributions, but the lack of detailed experiments prevents full validation of the claims. The paper is clear and well-organized, with excellent exposition of the mathematical concepts. The originality is strong, as the duality result between overlapped and latent Schatten norms is novel and extends structured sparsity theory. The significance is high, as the results provide both theoretical justification and practical guidance for tensor decomposition.

For the program committee, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful theoretical contribution to convex tensor decomposition, with strong mathematical results that advance the field. The experimental section should be expanded to include details about rank selection, comparisons to non-convex methods, and a more thorough analysis of the latent approach's behavior in different settings. The authors should also address the missing analysis of group regularization behavior.

Pros: Strong theoretical contribution, novel duality result, clear exposition, meaningful improvement over previous work.
Cons: Weak experimental section, missing details on rank selection, no comparison to non-convex approaches, incomplete analysis of regularization behavior.

Overall, this paper makes an important contribution to the theoretical understanding of convex tensor decomposition, with the potential to guide future work in the area. The theoretical insights are valuable, and the paper would be significantly stronger with a more comprehensive experimental evaluation.
