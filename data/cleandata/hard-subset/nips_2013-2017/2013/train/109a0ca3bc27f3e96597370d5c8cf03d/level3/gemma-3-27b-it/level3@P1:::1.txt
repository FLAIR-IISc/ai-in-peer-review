This paper studies convex relaxation techniques for tensor decomposition, specifically analyzing a “latent” structured Schatten norm and comparing its performance to a previously proposed “overlapped” norm. The authors derive theoretical guarantees on the mean squared error (MSE) achieved by minimizing the latent norm under various noise models and demonstrate through numerical simulations that these guarantees are accurate. A key theoretical contribution is the establishment of a duality result between the latent and overlapped Schatten norms, generalizing existing work on structured sparsity.

This work builds upon a recent trend in NIPS of exploring convex relaxations for non-convex problems, particularly in the context of matrix and tensor factorization (e.g., [22, 26]). The authors appropriately situate their work within this literature, highlighting the limitations of the overlapped approach—specifically its poor performance when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode—and framing the latent approach as a solution to this issue. The theoretical analysis appears rigorous, with clear connections drawn to related results in structured sparsity [2, 13, 17, 21]. The numerical experiments effectively validate the theoretical findings, demonstrating the scaling behavior predicted by the analysis and further supporting the superiority of the latent approach in many scenarios.  The duality result (Lemma 1) is a welcome addition and may be of independent interest to researchers working on structured sparsity problems.

The strengths of the paper lie in its technical soundness, clarity of presentation, and well-supported claims. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the latent approach, rigorously establishing its performance guarantees. The connection to existing work is clearly articulated, and the numerical results convincingly demonstrate the practical relevance of the theory. The paper is well-organized and generally easy to follow, although the inclusion of proofs in the supplementary material is appropriate given their length.

However, the paper also suffers from several weaknesses. The primary concern is the narrow scope and limited impact of the results. While technically sound, the work focuses on a very specific technical aspect of tensor decomposition and lacks a strong connection to broader machine learning applications. The paper does not explore the performance of these methods in real-world scenarios or consider their implications for tasks beyond tensor completion or denoising. This significantly limits the potential impact of the work within the NIPS community.

Furthermore, the results, while theoretically interesting, feel somewhat incremental. The observation that the latent approach performs better when the tensor is low-rank in a single mode is intuitively plausible and, while now formally proven, does not represent a groundbreaking advancement. The techniques employed, while solid, lack significant novelty.  The absence of corresponding lower bounds weakens the algorithmic comparisons; establishing that the proposed bounds are tight would significantly strengthen the conclusions.  The abstract’s claim of proposing a “new class of structured Schatten norms” is somewhat misleading, as the paper primarily *analyzes* existing norms rather than introducing genuinely new ones with accompanying algorithmic considerations.  

Minor points include the placement of Figure 1, which is referenced early in the introduction but may be difficult for the reader to interpret without prior context, and a few typographical errors and stylistic awkwardness throughout the text.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and rigorously proven results.
*   Clear presentation and connection to related work.
*   Novel duality result for structured Schatten norms.
*   Numerical validation of theoretical findings.
*   Provides valuable theoretical insights into the performance of convex relaxation-based tensor decomposition methods.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Narrow scope and limited relevance to broader machine learning applications.
*   Incremental results lacking significant novelty.
*   Lack of lower bounds to complement the upper bounds.
*   Misleading claim in the abstract regarding a new class of norms.
*   Missing application on a realistic machine learning task.



**Overall Assessment:**

I rate this paper as a **weak accept**. While the technical contributions are solid, the limited scope and incremental nature of the results raise concerns about its suitability for NIPS. The work would be more appropriate for a more specialized conference focused on optimization or tensor decomposition.  To strengthen the paper, the authors could consider including a discussion of potential applications in a real-world machine learning context or exploring the implications of their results for other related problems. Addressing the concerns regarding lower bounds and the abstract’s claim would also improve the paper's overall quality.
