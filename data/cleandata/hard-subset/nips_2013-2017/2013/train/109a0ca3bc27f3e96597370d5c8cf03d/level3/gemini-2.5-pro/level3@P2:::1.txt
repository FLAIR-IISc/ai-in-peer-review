### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates convex optimization methods for low-rank tensor decomposition. It focuses on providing a theoretical analysis for the "latent" structured Schatten norm regularizer, which was empirically proposed by Tomioka et al. (2010) as an alternative to the more commonly analyzed "overlapped" Schatten norm regularizer [9, 15, 23, 25]. The authors' primary contribution is a theoretical analysis of the mean squared error for the latent approach under a noisy observation model. The derived error bound scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true underlying tensor, in contrast to the overlapped approach, whose error bound depends on the *average* of the mode-k ranks [26]. This result provides a formal explanation for why the latent approach can outperform the overlapped one, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode.

As a secondary contribution, the paper introduces a generalized class of structured Schatten norms that encompasses both the overlapped and latent variants. Within this framework, the authors prove a novel duality theorem connecting the generalized overlapped norm to the generalized latent norm. This finding links the work to the broader literature on structured sparsity and duality in group lasso models [2, 17, 21]. The theoretical findings are supported by numerical simulations on synthetic data that confirm the predicted error scaling.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The theoretical claims are well-supported by detailed proofs that appear correct, building upon established analysis techniques from high-dimensional statistics [1]. The numerical experiments are well-designed and convincingly demonstrate that the empirical MSE scales as predicted by the theory. This provides strong evidence for the correctness of the analysis.

However, the strength of the comparison between the latent and overlapped approaches is somewhat limited. The paper compares an upper bound for the latent method with a previously established upper bound for the overlapped method. Without matching lower bounds for the problem, it is difficult to definitively claim one method is superior; it is possible that the existing analysis for the overlapped approach is simply not as tight. Furthermore, the evaluation is performed exclusively on synthetic data, leaving its performance in real-world machine learning contexts unevaluated.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and generally easy to follow for a reader familiar with the topic. The distinction between the overlapped and latent approaches is explained well, and the main theoretical results are stated precisely.

There are minor areas for improvement. The abstract's claim of studying a "new class of structured Schatten norms" is slightly overstated, as the subsequent analysis and experiments are confined to the specific S1/1 case. The broader Sp/q generalization is introduced and its duality is proven, but its properties and potential uses are not explored. Additionally, Figure 1 is referenced in the introduction before the models have been formally defined, which may confuse readers on a first pass.

**Originality:** The latent and overlapped models themselves are not new [25]. The analytical techniques used to derive the error bounds are also adapted from prior work on M-estimators with decomposable regularizers [1, 20]. The primary novelty lies in being the first to rigorously apply these techniques to the latent tensor norm, thereby providing theoretical justification for its empirical advantages. The duality result for the generalized norms is also a novel and elegant theoretical contribution. Overall, the paper represents a solid, albeit incremental, advance by filling an important theoretical gap in the understanding of existing methods.

**Significance:** The results will likely be of interest to researchers working on the theory of tensor methods and structured convex optimization. The formal explanation for the superiority of the latent norm is a valuable contribution to this specific sub-field.

However, the broader impact on the NIPS community may be limited. The paper's focus is highly theoretical and narrow, and it does not demonstrate the practical relevance of its findings on real-world machine learning problems mentioned in the introduction (e.g., neuroimaging, collaborative filtering). The lack of such experiments makes it difficult to gauge the practical importance of the performance difference between the two methods. As it stands, the paper is a strong theoretical piece but may be perceived as being only peripherally related to applied machine learning.

### Arguments For and Against Acceptance

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous theoretical performance guarantees for the latent Schatten norm approach to tensor decomposition.
*   The theoretical analysis clearly explains the empirically observed phenomenon that the latent approach can outperform the overlapped approach.
*   Presents a novel and interesting duality result between generalized classes of overlapped and latent Schatten norms.
*   The work is technically sound, and the theoretical predictions are carefully validated with numerical simulations.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The contribution is somewhat incremental, applying existing analytical frameworks to an existing model.
*   The significance for the broader machine learning community is limited due to the purely theoretical focus and the absence of experiments on real-world data.
*   The comparison between the two approaches relies on their respective upper bounds, which is less conclusive than a comparison against information-theoretic lower bounds.
*   The work is highly technical and may be of interest to only a narrow subset of the NIPS audience.
