This paper investigates convex tensor decomposition, focusing on the "latent" approach, which models a tensor as a sum of components, each low-rank in a specific mode. This builds upon recent work on convex relaxations for tensor decomposition that introduced the "overlapped" Schatten norm. The authors make a significant theoretical contribution by providing performance bounds for the latent approach, showing that its error scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor. This provides a formal explanation for its empirical superiority over the overlapped approach, whose error depends on the average of the ranks. A key part of this analysis is a novel and elegant dual characterization of the overlapped and latent Schatten norms, a result of independent interest that connects to the broader literature on structured sparsity.

The paper’s strengths are numerous.
*   **Quality & Significance:** The theoretical analysis is rigorous, technically sound, and represents a meaningful advance in our understanding of convex tensor decomposition. The derived bounds clearly explain the empirical advantages of the latent approach, addressing an important problem in the field.
*   **Originality:** The dual characterization between the latent and overlapped Schatten norms is a novel and insightful result that could be impactful beyond this specific application.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-organized and clearly written, making the complex theoretical arguments accessible and easy to follow.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is brief and lacks sufficient detail to be fully convincing or reproducible.
*   The experiments do not specify how the Tucker ranks (r1, r2, r3) were chosen for the synthetic data generation, which is a critical detail for interpreting the complexity measures and results.
*   The empirical comparison is limited to the overlapped approach. A more comprehensive evaluation would include comparisons to standard non-convex tensor decomposition algorithms to better situate the method's practical performance.
*   Given the general framework presented, a brief discussion on the behavior of other group regularizers (e.g., l_{1,∞}) would be welcome, particularly in the case where the underlying tensor is low-rank in all modes.

In summary, this is a strong paper with significant and original theoretical contributions that clearly advance the state of the art. The theoretical quality outweighs the limitations of the experimental section. However, addressing the points above would strengthen the paper considerably.
