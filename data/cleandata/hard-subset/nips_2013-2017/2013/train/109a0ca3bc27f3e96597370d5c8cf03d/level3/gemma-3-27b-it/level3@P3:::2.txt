This paper studies convex tensor decomposition using structured Schatten norms, focusing on the “latent” approach introduced in [25] and comparing it to the “overlapped” approach of [9, 15, 23]. The key contribution is a theoretical analysis demonstrating why the latent approach, which decomposes a tensor into a mixture of low-rank components along different modes, outperforms the overlapped approach, especially when the tensor is low-rank in only one mode. The authors also establish a duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms and provide consistency and denoising guarantees for the latent approach.

**Strengths:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a much-needed theoretical foundation for the empirically observed benefits of the latent approach, bridging the gap between practical performance and rigorous analysis.
* **Novel Duality Result:** The established duality between the overlapped and latent Schatten norms is a valuable contribution, potentially useful in broader contexts of structured sparsity.
* **Clear Exposition:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results. The connection to related work on structured sparsity is helpful.
* **Numerical Validation:** The numerical experiments corroborate the theoretical findings, demonstrating the scaling behavior predicted by the analysis.
* **Significance:** Tensor decomposition is a crucial technique in many data analysis applications, and improvements in its theoretical understanding and practical performance are significant.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Motivation for ML Audience:** While the introduction mentions applications like neuroimaging, it lacks a stronger, more accessible motivation for a broader machine learning audience. A more compelling example demonstrating the need for tensor decomposition in a common ML task would be beneficial.
* **Graphical Model & Terminology:** The paper would greatly benefit from including a latent-variable graphical model illustrating *h<sub>i</sub>* → *x<sub>i</sub>*, the linear hidden-to-observed mapping, and the arbitrary dependence among *h<sub>i</sub>*.  This visualization would help clarify the latent variable model being analyzed. The "mode-rank" terminology should be explicitly stated as equivalent to "multilinear rank," and the existence of other tensor rank notions should be acknowledged.  The authors correctly note that multilinear ranks equal dimensions of hidden variables – this should be explicitly stated in the caption of the figure.
* **Technical Details & Clarity:**
    * **Theorem 2:** The statement that components are recovered only up to permutation needs explicit clarification. Explain the implications of this permutation and how it affects the interpretation of the results.  The jump from (12) to (13) requires more justification; explain how the singleton decomposition is exploited.
    * **Missing Proofs:** The proof of Lemma 5 is missing and crucial to Theorem 2. Its inclusion is essential.  Similarly, Lemma 4, cited without proof, should be fully demonstrated.
    * **Related Work:** The discussion of related work is somewhat limited. It should include a more comprehensive overview of HOSVD and its variants, including discussions of approximation bounds and citing Kolda’s survey [14].
    * **Incoherence Discussion:** The comparison to incoherence results in [1, 3, 10] after (10) is misleading and should be removed or significantly revised. The current wording implies a direct comparability that doesn't seem to exist.
    * **Notation:** The superscript notation for components  *Ŵ<sup>(k)</sup>* is confusing. Clearer symbols should be used.  Also, resolve the inconsistency between *Ὶ<sub>k</sub>* and *<u>r<sub>k</sub>* for k-mode rank; using a consistent notation like `mult_rank(tensor)` would be helpful.
    * **Minor Errors:** Correct the missing parenthesis in the last line of page 12 of the supplementary material and the typo "structures Schatten" to "structured Schatten" in the abstract.
* **Computational Complexity:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods. Including a brief analysis of the computational cost (even if just qualitatively) would be valuable.



**Arguments For Acceptance:**

* Strong theoretical contributions providing a solid foundation for the latent approach to tensor decomposition.
* Clear and generally well-written presentation.
* Rigorous mathematical analysis supported by numerical validation.
* Addresses a significant problem with practical relevance.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

* Several technical details require clarification and correction.
* Missing proofs hinder reproducibility and complete understanding.
* Limited discussion of computational complexity.
* The paper could benefit from a more accessible motivation for a broader ML audience.

**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically strong and important paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of tensor decomposition. However, the identified weaknesses—particularly the missing proofs, unclear notation, and lack of computational complexity analysis—need to be addressed before acceptance. Addressing these issues would significantly enhance the paper’s clarity, completeness, and impact. I recommend acceptance *pending major revisions* addressing the points outlined above.  The novelty – providing Tucker decomposition via the overlapped Schatten norm with recovery guarantees – is significant, but contingent on addressing the noted clarifications.
