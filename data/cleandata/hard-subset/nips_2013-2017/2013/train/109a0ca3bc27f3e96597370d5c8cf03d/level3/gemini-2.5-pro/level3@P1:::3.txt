This paper addresses the problem of convex tensor decomposition, focusing on a theoretical analysis of the "latent" structured Schatten norm approach proposed in prior work. The main idea is to regularize a tensor by decomposing it into a sum of K tensors, where each component tensor is penalized for having a low rank in a specific mode. This is contrasted with the "overlapped" approach, where the tensor itself is simultaneously penalized for being low-rank across all its unfoldings. While the latent approach was empirically shown to be superior in certain settings (e.g., when the true tensor is low-rank in only one mode), a formal theoretical justification was missing. This paper fills that gap. A key contribution is showing that the mean squared error of the latent approach scales with the *minimum* mode-k rank of the true tensor, whereas the overlapped approach's error scales with the average of the mode-k ranks. This elegantly explains the observed performance difference. Furthermore, the paper introduces a general framework of structured Schatten norms and proves a novel duality theorem between the overlapped and latent norm families, a result of independent interest that connects this work to the broader literature on structured sparsity.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The primary contribution is the rigorous theoretical analysis of the latent Schatten norm for tensor denoising. The derived error bounds (Theorem 3, inequality 15) formally establish that the latent approach's performance depends on `min_k r_k`, which is a significant improvement over the `sum_k sqrt(r_k)` dependency of the overlapped approach analyzed in previous work [26]. This provides a clear theoretical justification for why and when the latent approach should be preferred, advancing the understanding of convex tensor methods.

2.  **Technical Quality:** The theoretical analysis is strong and appears sound. The paper introduces a generalized framework for structured Schatten Sp/q norms and establishes a duality between the "overlapped" and "latent" versions (Lemma 1). This is an elegant result that is interesting in its own right, as it generalizes similar duality results from the structured sparsity literature to the tensor setting.

3.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly introduces the problem, defines the two competing convex approaches, presents the main theoretical results, and validates them with experiments. The connection to structured sparsity and group lasso is well-articulated, putting the work in a broader context.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section, while successfully confirming the paper's theoretical scaling predictions, is brief and could be more comprehensive. The comparison is limited to the overlapped approach. To better establish the practical significance of this convex method, a comparison against established non-convex tensor decomposition methods (e.g., HOSVD/Tucker decomposition or CP decomposition via alternating least squares) would be highly valuable. While these methods are non-convex, they are widely used in practice, and understanding the trade-offs in terms of estimation accuracy and computational cost is important.

2.  **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper lacks some details that would be necessary for full reproducibility. For instance, the procedure for generating the low-rank tensors is described, but it is not specified how the multilinear ranks (r1, r2, r3) were chosen or sampled. A more systematic exploration across a wider range of rank configurations would strengthen the empirical claims.

3.  **Limited Scope of Analysis:** The analysis convincingly demonstrates the superiority of the latent approach when the tensor is low-rank in only one or a few modes. However, a more detailed discussion is warranted for the case where the tensor is simultaneously low-rank across all modes—a scenario intuitively suited for the overlapped approach. While the experiments touch upon this, a theoretical characterization of when one approach might break even or outperform the other across the full spectrum of rank structures would make the contribution more complete. The paper introduces a general Sp/q framework, but the analysis focuses almost exclusively on the S1/1 norm; exploring other norms in the family could also be a valuable extension.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Provides the first rigorous performance guarantees for the latent Schatten norm tensor decomposition, explaining its previously observed empirical success.
*   The main theoretical result—that the error scales with the minimum mode-k rank—is a strong and important finding.
*   The novel duality result between overlapped and latent structured Schatten norms is a significant theoretical contribution of independent interest.
*   The paper is technically solid, clear, and well-written.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental evaluation is somewhat limited, lacking comparisons to standard non-convex baselines.
*   The experimental section is missing some key details for full reproducibility, particularly regarding the choice of tensor ranks.
*   The theoretical analysis could be expanded to more thoroughly cover scenarios where the overlapped approach is expected to perform well.

Overall, the strengths of this paper, particularly its significant and novel theoretical contributions, substantially outweigh its weaknesses. The paper provides a meaningful and needed step forward in our understanding of convex relaxations for tensor decomposition. The weaknesses primarily concern the experimental section and could be addressed in a future journal version.
