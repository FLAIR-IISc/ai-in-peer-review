This paper studies convex tensor decomposition via structured Schatten norms. It formalizes two families of norms for tensors—overlapped and latent Schatten Sp/q norms—establishes a duality between them, and analyzes the statistical performance of the latent S1/1 regularizer for denoising. The core result shows that when the true tensor is low-rank in at least one (unknown) mode, the latent approach achieves an error that scales with the minimum mode-k rank, matching the performance of an oracle that knows the best mode in advance. The work connects to structured sparsity (overlapped vs. latent group lasso) and provides non-asymptotic bounds under Gaussian noise, with experiments that corroborate the predicted scaling. It contrasts these results with prior bounds for the overlapped approach, which scale with averages of mode ranks, thereby explaining empirically observed gaps.

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on convex relaxations for low-rank tensors (e.g., overlapped nuclear-norm penalties), nonconvex Tucker/HOSVD methods, and structured sparsity norms. It generalizes duality results known for overlapped/latent group norms to the Schatten setting for tensors, and situates its guarantees beside previous bounds for overlapped tensor norms. It would benefit from a fuller discussion of HOSVD and approximation bounds (e.g., De Lathauwer et al.; Kolda and Bader’s survey), and clearer positioning relative to robust PCA, dirty models, and matrix/tensor completion literatures.

Quality: The theoretical development is strong. The duality lemma is interesting in its own right, the consistency bound is clean, and the deterministic and Gaussian-noise bounds for the latent approach are carefully derived with appropriate dependence on dimensions and ranks. The empirical section supports the claimed scaling. However, a few central steps need clarification or added proofs (details below), and the incoherence discussion could be made more precise.

Clarity: The paper is generally well written, but several notational inconsistencies and missing proofs impede readability. The audience would benefit from stronger motivation and an accessible modeling perspective.

Originality: The extension of overlapped/latent duality to structured Schatten norms for tensors is novel, and the observation that the latent approach adapts to the minimum mode rank with formal guarantees provides a useful and nontrivial refinement over prior overlapped-norm analyses.

Significance: The results matter for practitioners who face multiway data where only some modes are low-rank, and for theorists interested in decomposable norms and convex estimators on tensors. The insight that the latent formulation effectively “selects” the best mode without prior knowledge should influence method choice in tensor denoising and, potentially, completion.

Detailed comments and suggestions:
- Motivation and accessibility: Add a latent-variable graphical model where h → x with a linear map from hidden variables hi to observed xi, allowing arbitrary dependence among the hi. Explain how the observed second/third-order moment tensor admits a Tucker decomposition, and that the multilinear ranks equal the dimensionalities of the latent variables. Emphasize that only a subset of hidden variables may be low-dimensional—precisely the setting where the latent norm’s adaptation to mink rk is advantageous.
- Theorem 2 clarifications: State explicitly that the recovered latent components Ŵ(k) are identifiable only up to permutation (and, if applicable, to non-uniqueness in decompositions), and that guarantees pertain to the sum Ŵ and to a particular optimal latent decomposition induced by the penalty.
- From (12) to (13): Justify this step explicitly. The argument relies on the freedom to choose a singleton decomposition W∗(k0) = W∗, W∗(k≠k0) = 0 for any k0, which turns the sum over component ranks into the minimal mode-k rank. Spell out this construction so readers see why the bound depends on mink rk.
- Missing proofs: Provide the proof of Lemma 5 in the main or supplementary material, since it is pivotal for Theorem 2. Likewise, either include a self-contained proof of Lemma 4 or clearly state the exact assumptions and mapping to the cited result.
- Incoherence assumption after (10): The current comparison to incoherence in [1,3,10] is potentially misleading, as (10) bounds cross-mode spectral norms of off-mode unfoldings rather than the standard matrix incoherence of singular vectors. Either remove this comparison or precisely delineate the differences and when each notion applies.
- Related work: Expand on HOSVD/Tucker approximation bounds and practical algorithms, citing De Lathauwer et al. and Kolda & Bader (SIAM Review). Briefly contrast your convex guarantees with known approximation properties of HOSVD and variants.
- Rank terminology: State explicitly that “mode-rank” is the multilinear (Tucker) rank and note that other rank notions exist (CP rank, etc.). Keep notation consistent for mode-k ranks: avoid switching between r̄k and r̲k; consider a uniform notation such as r_k(W) or mult_rank_k(W). Distinguish clearly between ranks of W and ranks of latent components in (9).
- Notation cleanup: The superscript notation for components Ŵ(k) conflicts with other uses; consider using boldface or calligraphic symbols for tensors, with subscripts/superscripts for modes only where needed. Ensure consistent use of hat and star.
- Computational complexity: Include a short discussion of per-iteration costs for the proposed ADMM/proximal algorithms. Concretely, each iteration computes K SVDs of mode-k unfoldings of size nk × (N/nk), which dominates runtime and memory. Comment on scaling with K, potential use of partial SVDs, warm starts, and how the extra spectral-norm constraints in (11) affect complexity relative to the overlapped approach.
- Experiments: Since the theory is for denoising, the experiments are appropriate; nevertheless, include a targeted setting where only one mode is low-rank (to highlight the mink rk advantage), a sensitivity plot for λ around the theoretically suggested value, and a brief study of α’s effect in (11).
- Typos and minor fixes:
  - Abstract: “structures Schatten” → “structured Schatten.”
  - Supplement: add the missing parenthesis on the last line of page 12.
  - Ensure consistent capitalization and spacing for “Schatten” and norms, and check all equation references.

Pros for acceptance:
- Conceptually clean framework unifying overlapped/latent Schatten norms with a useful duality.
- Sharp, interpretable bounds showing adaptation to the minimum mode rank.
- Empirical validation aligns with theory and clarifies when latent beats overlapped.
- Technically solid and relevant to the NIPS community.

Cons for acceptance:
- Key proof elements are missing or only sketched (Lemmas 4–5), and some steps (from (12) to (13)) need explicit justification.
- Motivation and exposition for a broad ML audience could be improved; notation is occasionally confusing.
- Limited discussion of computational complexity and relation to classical HOSVD/Tucker methods.

Overall: A strong and timely contribution. With the clarifications, added proofs, and improved exposition suggested above, I recommend acceptance.
