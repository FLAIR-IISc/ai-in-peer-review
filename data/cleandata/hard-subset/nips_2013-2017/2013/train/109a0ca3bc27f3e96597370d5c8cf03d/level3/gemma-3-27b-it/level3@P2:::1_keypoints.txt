- Studies convex optimization for tensor decomposition, focusing on a "latent" structured Schatten norm vs. a previously proposed "overlapped" norm.  
- Provides theoretical analysis showing conditions where the latent norm outperforms the overlapped approach.  
- Presents a duality result (Lemma 1) relating the latent and overlapped norms, extending structured sparsity theory.  
- Derives MSE upper bounds for the latent approach under deterministic and Gaussian noise models.  
- Validates theoretical findings with numerical simulations showing predicted scaling and superior performance of the latent approach.  
- Clearly motivated by applications (neuroimaging, collaborative filtering) and well-situated within recent convex-relaxation literature.  
- Notation and definitions are clear; proofs are rigorous with details in the supplement.  
- Strength: technically sound with a significant duality contribution and strong theoretical/empirical alignment.  
- Strength: good clarity, organization, and connection to related work (group Lasso, matrix decomposition).  
- Weakness: limited practical significance and narrow focus on a technical comparison, with weak ties to broader ML impact.  
- Weakness: contributions feel incremental—confirming prior empirical observations without major methodological novelty.  
- Weakness: lacks lower bounds, making optimality and tightness of results unclear.  
- Weakness: abstract overstates novelty by implying a "new class" of norms when it analyzes existing ones and their relationships.  
- Weakness: presentation issues (early Figure 1, minor typos and phrasing problems).  
- Arguments for acceptance: fills a theoretical gap, offers a broadly useful duality result, and provides supporting numerical evidence.  
- Arguments against acceptance: incremental impact, missing lower bounds, overstated novelty, and limited real-world relevance for a top conference.  
- Overall assessment: rated "weak accept" — technically solid but limited in scope and practical impact; suggests revising the abstract and addressing lower bounds or broader applications.
