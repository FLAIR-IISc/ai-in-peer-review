Summary and relation to prior work
This paper studies convex, norm-regularized formulations for approximately low-rank tensor factorization, with a focus on the “latent” Schatten-1 tensor norm as an alternative to the better-studied “overlapped” Schatten-1 norm. The authors present a unified framework of structured Schatten Sp/q norms for tensors that subsumes both overlapped and latent variants, and they prove a duality theorem: the dual of the overlapped Sp/q norm is the latent Sp*/q* norm. Building on techniques from structured sparsity and decomposable regularization, the paper analyzes the latent norm for tensor denoising, providing consistency, deterministic error bounds under an incoherence-type assumption, and high-probability MSE bounds for Gaussian noise. A key message is that the latent approach achieves error scaling with the minimum mode rank of the underlying tensor, in contrast to the overlapped norm, whose bounds depend on an average over mode ranks (as in prior analyses). Numerical simulations corroborate the predicted scaling and show regimes where the latent approach outperforms the overlapped one.

This work connects and extends prior studies on convex relaxations for low-rank tensors and structured sparsity (e.g., overlapped Schatten norms for tensor completion/denoising; latent group lasso; decomposable regularizers). The analysis technique leverages known inequalities for Schatten norms, norm duality, and Gaussian width/concentration bounds, similar in spirit to Negahban et al. and Agarwal et al. for matrices and structured sparsity. The comparison to prior overlapped-norm bounds (e.g., Tomioka et al. NIPS’11 and related work) is clear and informative.

Strengths
- Clear, principled comparison of latent versus overlapped norms, with theory explaining previously reported empirical advantages of latent regularization when only one mode is low-rank.
- Solid theoretical contributions: generic consistency, deterministic error bounds under incoherence, and Gaussian-noise MSE bounds; all show latent scaling with min mode rank.
- A clean duality theorem for structured Schatten norms that generalizes group norm dualities (overlapped vs latent).
- Numerical simulations closely match the theoretical scaling and substantiate the comparison.
- The paper is generally well organized and easy to follow; proofs appear technically careful.

Weaknesses and suggestions
Quality
- Only upper bounds are provided; no matching minimax lower bounds for the latent setting are given. Without lower bounds (or tight information-theoretic rates), it is difficult to assess optimality or to draw sharp algorithmic comparisons. Suggest adding lower bounds (or referencing settings where such lower bounds match your rates), or at least discussing the gaps.
- The generalization to structured Schatten Sp/q norms is mostly formal; there are no algorithms, complexity analyses, or behavioral results beyond the S1/1 case. The abstract’s phrasing “we study a new class of structured Schatten norms” overstates what is delivered; consider tempering to “we introduce and analyze a framework … and prove a duality result.”
- The analysis focuses on fully observed denoising. Important ML-relevant regimes (tensor completion, supervised prediction with tensor features, real-world missingness) are not addressed theoretically or empirically. Given the NIPS audience, this limits practical impact. Suggest adding at least one of: (i) theory for tensor completion with latent regularization; (ii) experiments on standard ML datasets (e.g., video inpainting, recommender systems with time/context modes, neuroimaging) comparing to nonconvex baselines (ALS/HOOI/CP-ALS) and other convex methods.
- Practical aspects are under-discussed: choice and sensitivity of λ, the role and tuning of the α-incoherence constraints in problem (11), computational cost and scaling versus overlapped norms. A brief complexity/runtime comparison and sensitivity analysis would help practitioners.

Clarity
- Generally clear, but Figure 1 appears before the key definitions and is hard to interpret on first read. Consider moving it later or enriching the caption to be self-contained.
- Minor typos/phrasing: e.g., “performs betters” → “performs better” (Sec. 2.2); a few missing articles and capitalization inconsistencies in references. A careful proofreading pass is recommended.

Originality
- The duality result for structured Schatten norms is neat, but the techniques are natural extensions of known overlapped/latent group norm dualities. The error analyses adapt established tools from structured sparsity and low-rank matrix estimation to the tensor-latent setting. Overall, the work feels incremental rather than conceptually surprising.

Significance
- For researchers working on convex optimization for tensor decompositions, the results are useful: they clarify when latent norms should be preferred and provide finite-sample guarantees. However, the scope is narrow (denoising with full observations) and the connection to mainstream ML tasks is tangential in the current form. This limits broader NIPS impact.

Questions for the authors
- Can you comment on whether your latent-norm rates are minimax optimal in the denoising setting? Are there known lower bounds that match your dependence on min mode rank?
- How should α be selected in practice for the constraints in (11)? Do the empirical results enforce these constraints, and how sensitive are the results to α?
- Do your results extend to tensor completion (with missing entries), and if so, what replaces the Gaussian noise term in λ?

Pros for acceptance
- Solid, technically sound analysis with clear takeaways favoring latent norms in common scenarios.
- Clean unification and duality for structured Schatten norms.
- Simulations convincingly match theory.

Cons for acceptance
- Narrow scope; limited relevance to typical NIPS ML applications; no real-data experiments or completion results.
- Lacks lower bounds; novelty is moderate.
- Abstract slightly over-claims regarding the “new class” without accompanying algorithms or analyses.

Recommendation
Leaning toward weak reject for NIPS due to scope and limited novelty relative to prior structured sparsity and tensor literature, despite the technically sound and clearly written analysis. Strengthening with lower bounds, ML-relevant experiments (and/or completion theory), and a more substantial exploration of the generalized norms would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
