

The paper presents a clear and original application of particle MCMC to Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), specifically leveraging the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm by Lindsten et al. The authors' development of sparse GP-SSM methodology is a valuable contribution, addressing computational challenges in this domain. Overall, the work is well-motivated and technically sound.

However, the paper would benefit significantly from two key additions. First, it omits critical comparisons to established particle MCMC schemes, particularly particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PGBS, as in Whiteley et al. 2011). Both methods are directly relevant to the problem and could be implemented with minimal additional effort. Such comparisons are essential for contextualizing the proposed approach: PMMH typically requires a number of particles scaling linearly with the time horizon \(T\) (as shown in Whiteley et al.), while particle Gibbs variants (including the authors' approach) generally require sublinear scaling. Without these benchmarks, it remains unclear whether the proposed algorithm offers practical advantages in efficiency or accuracy over existing methods.

Second, the empirical results lack quantitative diagnostics to assess algorithmic performance. The paper should include plots showing key metrics—such as autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample sizes (ESS)—as functions of the particle count \(N\) and time horizon \(T\). These visualizations would clarify critical practical questions: How does \(N\) scale with \(T\) for stable inference? Is \(N\) required to grow linearly with \(T\) (as in PMMH), or can it remain constant (as in particle Gibbs)? The current presentation of results does not address these scalability concerns, limiting the reader's ability to evaluate the method's practical utility.

The technical claim regarding PMMH's scaling (linear in \(T\)) is accurate and well-supported by Whiteley et al., but the paper’s omission of this comparison weakens its contribution. Including PMMH and PGBS would strengthen the analysis by demonstrating where the proposed method excels (e.g., in reducing the particle count required for a given \(T\)) and where it may face limitations. For instance, if the authors’ approach achieves sublinear scaling while maintaining accuracy, this would be a compelling advantage over PMMH. Conversely, if performance gaps exist, the paper should acknowledge them transparently.

The sparse GP-SSM component is well-executed and merits attention, but its value is partially obscured by the absence of comparative results. The authors should explicitly state whether the sparsity assumptions impact the particle MCMC convergence properties compared to dense alternatives. Additionally, the paper would gain clarity by specifying the exact computational cost of the sparse approximation relative to the particle MCMC steps.

In summary, the paper makes a solid contribution to applying particle MCMC to GP-SSMs. With the addition of comparative experiments against PMMH and PGBS, alongside diagnostic plots for performance scaling, it would become a substantially stronger and more impactful work. These revisions are straightforward to implement and would significantly enhance the paper’s technical rigor and practical relevance. The authors are encouraged to address these points to maximize the contribution of their work.
