

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics. The authors address the key challenge of learning the transition function without assuming a parametric form by marginalizing it out of the model and instead inferring the joint smoothing distribution using a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) sampler with ancestor sampling. The paper makes several significant contributions: (1) a novel inference mechanism that preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the model, (2) computational improvements through sparse GP approximations and efficient Cholesky updates, and (3) clear empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world systems.

The work relates well to prior NIPS literature on GP-based SSMs. While Deisenroth et al. (2011) and Turner et al. (2009) developed approximation methods for filtering and smoothing with learned GP dynamics, this paper provides the crucial missing component—learning the GP model itself. Wang et al. (2005) and Ghahramani & Roweis (1999) employed parametric approaches for similar problems, whereas this work maintains the nonparametric nature of the model. The authors properly acknowledge these connections while clearly differentiating their contribution.

The paper excels on all four review criteria. Quality: The technical approach is sound, with thorough theoretical justification for marginalizing the transition dynamics and using PMCMC with ancestor sampling. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of non-Markovian state processes and provide efficient implementations using sparse GPs and Cholesky updates. The empirical results on a nonlinear benchmark system and cart-pole dynamics demonstrate the method's ability to capture complex dynamics while maintaining uncertainty estimates.

Clarity: The paper is well-organized and clearly written, with a logical flow from problem formulation to solution to experiments. The mathematical derivations are accessible to readers familiar with Bayesian inference and SSMs. The presentation of the PGAS algorithm is particularly clear, though the abbreviation CPF-AS appears before its definition in Section 3.3.1, which should be corrected.

Originality: The marginalization of the transition dynamics function while inferring the smoothing distribution represents a significant conceptual advance. The use of ancestor sampling to address the non-Markovian nature of the marginalized model is particularly novel, building on Lindsten et al. (2012) while solving a specific problem in GP-SSMs. The paper clearly articulates how this differs from previous work that typically assumes parametric transition models.

Significance: This work advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for state-space models, providing a framework that can learn complex dynamics without restrictive parametric assumptions. The ability to maintain uncertainty estimates across the state trajectory while learning the transition function has immediate applications in control, robotics, and time-series analysis. The computational efficiencies (O(T³) for vanilla GP and O(M²T) for sparse GP) make the approach practical for real-world applications.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty in marginalizing the transition function while preserving nonparametric expressivity
- Technical soundness with rigorous treatment of inference challenges
- Practical computational improvements through sparsity and efficient Cholesky updates
- Clear presentation of the PGAS algorithm and its application to GP-SSMs
- Empirical validation on both synthetic and real-world systems

Weaknesses:
- The description of the RMSE results in Table 1 is ambiguous regarding whether they represent in-sample or out-of-sample performance. The authors should clarify whether the RMSE is computed on a withheld test set or the training data (as the column headers suggest "f(xt,ut)|y1:T" implies conditioning on the training data).
- The paper does not sufficiently explore the differences between using a parametric model as the mean function versus learning the transition dynamics fully. A discussion of how the GP-SSM performs in out-of-sample forecasting compared to parametric models would strengthen the paper.
- The abbreviation CPF-AS is introduced in Algorithm 1 before its definition in Section 3.3.1.

The authors might consider adding a brief discussion of how the GP-SSM handles forecasting (forward prediction) versus smoothing (inference given all data), particularly in the context of out-of-sample prediction where the difference between parametric models and the GP-SSM approach would be most apparent. An additional column in Table 1 showing out-of-sample RMSE would address this concern.

This is a strong, technically sound contribution that advances the field of Bayesian inference in state-space models. The paper's novel approach to marginalizing transition dynamics while maintaining nonparametric expressivity represents a significant methodological advance. The computational improvements make the approach practical for real-world applications, and the empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over parametric alternatives. The paper should be accepted for NIPS as it makes a meaningful contribution to the field with both theoretical and practical significance.
