The authors apply particle MCMC to inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), focusing on the particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) algorithm of Lindsten et al. The paper is clearly written, and the application is both interesting and original. There is also useful model-specific methodology, namely a sparse GP-SSM.

A significant omission is the lack of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, particularly particle marginal Metropolis–Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). These baselines appear straightforward to implement, and it would be very informative to see how they compare with the proposed approach; adding these experiments should not require much additional work.

I would also like to see plots that characterize algorithmic performance—e.g., autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS)—as functions of the number of particles N and the time horizon T. As presented, the results are not very informative about scaling. Should N scale linearly with T, or sublinearly?

My expectation is that, for these models, PMMH will require a number of particles that increases quadratically with T (as observed by Whiteley et al.), whereas both particle Gibbs variants should require a number of particles that grows sublinearly with T.

In sum, this is a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs. The paper would be significantly improved by including empirical comparisons with PMMH and with particle Gibbs with backward sampling, and by providing scaling diagnostics (e.g., ACF/ESS versus N and T).
