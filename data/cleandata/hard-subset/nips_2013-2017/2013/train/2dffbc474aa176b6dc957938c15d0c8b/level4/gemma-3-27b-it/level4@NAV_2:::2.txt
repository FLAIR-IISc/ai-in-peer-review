The authors propose to apply particle MCMC to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models. In particular, they focus on the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm of Lindsten et al. The paper is clear and represents an interesting and original application of particle MCMC. Furthermore, the development of model-specific methodology, such as the sparse GP-SSM, is a valuable contribution.

A notable omission is the lack of comparisons to alternative particle MCMC schemes, specifically particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these methods would be relatively straightforward and would provide valuable insight into the performance of the proposed scheme. Understanding how these variants compare would strengthen the paper's contribution. 

Additionally, the inclusion of graphs illustrating algorithm performance – for example, autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as functions of both the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T) – would be beneficial. Currently, the presented results lack detailed information regarding scaling behavior. Specifically, understanding the relationship between N and T is crucial; does N need to scale linearly, sublinearly, or quadratically with T? Previous work, such as Whiteley et al., indicates that PMMH may require a number of particles increasing quadratically with T, while particle Gibbs algorithms typically exhibit sublinear scaling. 

In summary, this is a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a comparative analysis against PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a clear and well-motivated application of particle MCMC to a challenging problem.
*   The development of the sparse GP-SSM construction is a valuable contribution, potentially improving computational efficiency.
*   The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach on benchmark problems, including a cart-pole system.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to other established particle MCMC schemes (PMMH, particle Gibbs with backward sampling) is a significant weakness.
*   The results lack detailed analysis of algorithm scaling behavior with respect to the number of particles and the time horizon.
*   While the paper demonstrates the method works, it doesn't provide a sufficiently detailed performance evaluation to clearly establish its advantages over existing methods.
