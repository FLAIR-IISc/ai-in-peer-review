The authors propose to apply particle MCMC to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models. In particular, they focus on the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm developed by Lindsten et al. The paper is well-written and presents an interesting and original application of particle MCMC. Furthermore, the authors develop some useful model-specific methodology, notably a sparse GP-SSM construction.

A notable limitation is the lack of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, specifically particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these would be relatively straightforward and it would be valuable to understand how they compare to the proposed scheme. 

Additionally, I would like to see visualizations illustrating the algorithm’s performance (e.g., in terms of autocorrelation function (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS)) as a function of the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T). The current results are not particularly informative regarding computational scaling. Specifically, it would be helpful to understand how N needs to scale with T – linearly, sublinearly, or otherwise. 

Given the context of similar models, it is likely that PMMH would require a number of particles increasing quadratically with T, as observed in Whiteley et al., whereas both particle Gibbs variants require a number of particles growing sublinearly with T.  Demonstrating this scaling empirically would strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this is a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. The paper would be significantly improved by a comparative analysis against PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   The paper addresses an important problem: Bayesian inference in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models.
*   The proposed approach is technically sound and leverages recent advances in particle MCMC.
*   The development of the sparse GP-SSM construction is a valuable contribution.
*   The paper is clearly written and well-organized.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   The lack of comparison to alternative particle MCMC schemes limits the assessment of the proposed method’s efficiency and scalability.
*   The experimental results lack detailed analysis of computational scaling with respect to problem size (N and T).
