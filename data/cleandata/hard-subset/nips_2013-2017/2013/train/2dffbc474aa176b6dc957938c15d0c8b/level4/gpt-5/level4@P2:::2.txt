Summary
This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the state transition function f and to marginalize f analytically, thereby preserving the nonparametric expressivity while turning the latent state process non-Markovian. Posterior inference over states and hyperparameters is then performed using particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS; Lindsten et al.), which targets the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T). Given samples from the smoothing distribution, the predictive distribution for f at test inputs is obtained in closed form (as a mixture of Gaussians across MCMC samples). The paper also introduces a sparse GP-SSM based on the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Empirically, the method is demonstrated on a nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system, showing sensible uncertainty quantification and predictive accuracy.

Relation to prior work: Unlike GP-SSM approaches relying on MAP/EM or fixed learned dynamics (e.g., Turner et al.; Deisenroth et al.; Wang et al.), this work performs fully Bayesian joint state/hyperparameter inference while collapsing the transition function. Methodologically, it builds on PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and the PGAS kernel (Lindsten et al.), adapting them to the non-Markovian setting induced by marginalizing f and providing implementation details and a sparse variant.

Strengths
- Originality: Elegant integration of PGAS with collapsed GP-SSMs, retaining nonparametric flexibility without resorting to finite parameterizations.
- Methodological contribution: Practical derivations for the collapsed prior over trajectories; efficient CPF-AS implementation with rank-one updates; FIC-based sparse construction with clear complexity gains.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written, with a clear exposition of the model and inference scheme.
- Practical relevance: The approach handles multimodal smoothing and yields calibrated predictive uncertainty; the sparse variant broadens applicability.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing comparisons within PMCMC: The paper does not compare PGAS to particle marginal MH (PMMH) or particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS/backward simulation; see Whiteley et al.). These baselines are natural and would strengthen the empirical case. Even modest-scale comparisons (identical budgets for particles and CPU time) would be informative.
- Scaling and mixing diagnostics: Please report mixing as a function of number of particles N and sequence length T. Autocorrelation times, effective sample size (ESS) per second, or kernel inefficiency factors for key quantities (e.g., log p(y|θ), functionals of x) would make the results much more actionable. In particular:
  - Empirical scaling of N with T. Prior work suggests PMMH often requires N growing as O(T^2) to maintain stable variance, whereas PG variants can be sublinear; verifying this empirically would be valuable.
  - Sensitivity to backward/ancestor sampling and to proposal choices.
- Experimental scope: The experiments are limited to two settings. Consider:
  - Additional datasets or higher-dimensional state spaces to probe scalability.
  - Ablations: vanilla GP vs FIC; effect of M; effect of the mean function (e.g., linear vs informed).
  - Runtime and memory profiles (wall-clock, complexity constants) for PGAS vs PMMH/PG-BS.
- Reproducibility details: Provide priors, initialization schemes, number of iterations, burn-in, thinning, and convergence diagnostics. For slice sampling of θx, give step-size/bracketing details and acceptance behavior.
- Modeling choices: Offer guidance on choosing the GP mean function (and risks of misspecification) and on selecting inducing inputs (e.g., greedy schemes), since performance may depend on these design choices.

Evaluation by criteria
- Quality: The methodology is sound and builds on principled PMCMC foundations. The derivations for collapsed inference and efficient updates are solid. However, stronger empirical validation against PMMH and PG-BS, with thorough diagnostics and scaling studies, is needed to substantiate performance claims.
- Clarity: Clear and well organized. Consider adding a brief algorithmic summary box for CPF-AS updates and a short “practical tips” paragraph (choice of N, M, mean function, inducing input selection).
- Originality: High. Collapsing f with a GP prior inside a PGAS framework for GP-SSMs is novel and useful, beyond prior MAP/EM-based approaches.
- Significance: Potentially high. A general, fully Bayesian inference method for GP-SSMs with scalable sparse variants is likely to be widely used. Demonstrating robustness and efficiency relative to standard PMCMC baselines would cement the contribution.

Specific requests
- Add comparisons to PMMH and PG-BS under matched computational budgets, reporting ESS/time and predictive accuracy.
- Provide ACF/ESS vs N and T, and evidence for how N should scale with T for stable mixing.
- Report wall-clock times and memory usage for vanilla and FIC variants; include sensitivity to M.
- Include additional quantitative metrics (e.g., RMSE, NLL) across seeds and datasets, with uncertainty bars.

Pros
- Fully Bayesian GP-SSM inference via PGAS while preserving nonparametric flexibility.
- Clear derivations and efficient implementation details, including a sparse FIC variant.
- Handles multimodal smoothing and produces calibrated predictive uncertainty.

Cons
- No empirical comparison to PMMH or PG-BS/backward simulation.
- Limited diagnostics on mixing and scaling with N and T.
- Experimental scope and ablations are relatively narrow; limited guidance on practical hyperparameter and inducing-point choices.

Overall assessment
A well-written and technically sound application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs with meaningful methodological refinements. The paper would be significantly strengthened by systematic comparisons to PMMH and PG-BS and by reporting mixing/scaling diagnostics. With these additions, the contribution would be both rigorous and practically compelling.
