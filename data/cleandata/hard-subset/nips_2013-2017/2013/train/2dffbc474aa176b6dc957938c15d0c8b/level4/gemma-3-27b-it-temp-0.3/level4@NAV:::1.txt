This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for state and parameter estimation within nonlinear state-space models, incorporating a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the system’s transition dynamics. The authors employ particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling for inference, and address computational challenges through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky factorization techniques.

The work is technically rigorous and well-presented. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, coupled with the application of ancestor sampling, represents a significant contribution. Furthermore, the attention to computational efficiency via sparsity and low-rank updates to covariance matrices enhances the paper’s practical value. The empirical evaluation, though concise, adequately demonstrates the approach’s effectiveness (with suggestions for expansion below).

Beyond its immediate objectives, this paper is likely to foster discussion regarding inference techniques for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs associated with learning transition dynamics versus specifying them parametrically. 

A brief expansion on the distinction between employing a parametric transition model *a priori* and utilizing a similar model as the GP’s mean function would be beneficial, particularly concerning applications like out-of-sample prediction and forecasting. 

The results presented in Table 1 and the accompanying description require clarification. It is unclear whether the reported RMSE values correspond to performance on a held-out dataset or are calculated based on the conditioning data (*|data*). An RMSE metric evaluating out-of-sample predictive performance, specifically a forecast against withheld data, would be particularly insightful, as this scenario is expected to reveal the greatest differences between the learned dynamics and the true model. Adding a column to Table 1 demonstrating this would be a valuable addition.

A minor editorial note: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 prior to its definition in Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and innovative paper poised to stimulate considerable discussion within the field.
