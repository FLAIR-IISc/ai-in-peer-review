This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while also learning the transition dynamics through the use of a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with the recently-introduced idea of ancestor sampling. The paper also discusses computational efficiencies to be had with respect to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically sound and well-presented, making it accessible to readers. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics and the use of ancestor sampling to achieve this is a novel contribution. The consideration of computational issues such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices strengthens the paper further.

The empirical results, although brief, are sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. However, it would be beneficial to include additional discussions around the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori against the use of a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For example, in out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting), how do these approaches compare?

The results in Table 1 and the preceding paragraph are slightly unclear. It would be helpful to clarify whether the RMSE is against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points that is conditioned upon. The most interesting scenario would be an RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, especially a forecast forward in time against withheld data. This would highlight the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 is not already showing this, an extra column that does so would be a great addition.

One minor point is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. This is a minor issue, but it would improve the overall flow of the paper to define the abbreviation before using it.

In terms of the conference reviewing guidelines, this paper meets the criteria for quality, clarity, originality, and significance. The paper is well-written, and the ideas are clearly presented. The use of GP priors for learning transition dynamics in nonlinear state-space models is novel and has the potential to stimulate interesting discussions around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to learning transition dynamics in nonlinear state-space models.
* The use of GP priors and ancestor sampling is a unique contribution to the field.
* The paper discusses computational efficiencies, making the approach more practical for real-world applications.
* The empirical results are sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from additional discussions around the differences between using parametric transition models and GP priors.
* The results in Table 1 could be clarified, and an additional column showing out-of-sample prediction results would be beneficial.

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate some interesting discussions. With some minor revisions, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
