This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The core idea is to place a GP prior over the unknown state transition function, allowing the model to capture complex, nonlinear dynamics without being constrained to a fixed parametric form. The main methodological contribution is an inference scheme that marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional state transition function. This is a key departure from previous work (e.g., Turner et al., 2012; Wang et al., 2008) that often relies on parametric approximations, EM, or MAP estimates.

By integrating out the transition function, the authors are left with a non-Markovian prior over the state trajectories. To perform inference in this challenging model, they employ a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) method recently proposed by Lindsten et al. (2012). This allows for sampling from the joint posterior distribution of the states and hyperparameters. A further contribution is the development of a sparse version of the model using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation, which reduces the computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T), making the approach scalable to longer time series.

**Strengths**

1.  **Originality and Significance:** The proposed method is an elegant and novel application of advanced PMCMC techniques to the problem of learning in GP-SSMs. The strategy of marginalizing the transition function is fundamentally different from prior approaches and is significant because it preserves the full non-parametric expressivity of the GP model. This allows for a truly Bayesian treatment that properly accounts for uncertainty in the system dynamics.

2.  **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The authors do a good job of explaining the model, the motivation for marginalizing the dynamics function, and the subsequent application of the PGAS sampler. The connection between the vanilla and sparse GP models is also well-articulated.

3.  **Technical Quality:** The methodological development appears sound. The derivation of the non-Markovian state prior after marginalization is correct, and the application of PGAS to this structure is appropriate. The inclusion of the sparse GP-SSM construction is a valuable and practical extension that addresses the computational scaling of the vanilla model.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which focuses on the model's predictive performance but fails to adequately analyze or justify the choice of the specific inference algorithm.

1.  **Lack of Comparison with Alternative PMCMC Samplers:** The paper's central claim is the successful application of PGAS for inference in this model. However, this choice is not empirically justified against other powerful and relevant PMCMC baselines. It would be highly beneficial to compare the PGAS sampler against:
    *   **Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH):** A standard PMCMC algorithm that would serve as a crucial baseline.
    *   **Particle Gibbs with Backward Sampling:** Another advanced sampler known for its efficiency in certain state-space models.

    It is plausible that PMMH would struggle with this model, potentially requiring a number of particles N that scales quadratically with the time series length T to maintain a reasonable acceptance rate, as has been observed in other contexts. In contrast, Particle Gibbs variants are expected to scale more favorably. Demonstrating this empirically would have provided a much stronger justification for the specific choice of PGAS and would have significantly strengthened the paper's contribution to the inference methodology literature.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Sampler Performance:** The current results demonstrate that the model can learn reasonable dynamics, but they provide very little insight into the performance of the inference algorithm itself. The authors should include a quantitative analysis of the sampler's efficiency. For example:
    *   How does the performance (e.g., measured by Effective Sample Size (ESS) or the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) of the states/hyperparameters) vary as a function of the number of particles `N`?
    *   How does the required `N` scale with the length of the time series `T` to maintain good mixing? Does it need to scale linearly, sub-linearly, or not at all?

    This analysis is crucial for any practitioner wishing to apply this method, as it provides guidance on the computational trade-offs and how to set `N` for a given problem. Without this, the claims about the efficiency of the sampler are not fully substantiated.

**Summary**

This is a high-quality paper that presents a novel and well-motivated approach for fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs. The application of PGAS after marginalizing the dynamics function is an interesting and powerful idea.

**Arguments for acceptance:**
*   Presents a novel and elegant combination of GP-SSMs and state-of-the-art PMCMC methods.
*   The approach avoids parametric approximations of the dynamics, preserving the model's non-parametric nature.
*   The paper is clearly written and the proposed method is technically sound.
*   The inclusion of a sparse variant makes the method more practical for real-world problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**
*   The experimental section is weak. The choice of the PGAS sampler is not justified against other strong PMCMC baselines.
*   The paper lacks a quantitative analysis of the sampler's performance and scaling properties (e.g., ESS vs. N and T), which is a critical omission for a paper proposing a new inference scheme.

While the paper presents a valuable contribution, its current evaluation does not fully substantiate the choice of the proposed algorithm over relevant alternatives. The paper would be significantly improved by including the suggested algorithmic comparisons and performance analysis.
