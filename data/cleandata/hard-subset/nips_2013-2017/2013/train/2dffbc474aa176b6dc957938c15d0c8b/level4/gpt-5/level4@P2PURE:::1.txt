This paper proposes a Bayesian method for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while simultaneously learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference procedure is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) and leverages the recently introduced ancestor sampling. The paper also addresses computational efficiency, including sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics using ancestor sampling is novel. The discussion of computational aspects—such as exploiting sparsity and performing low-rank updates/downdates of Cholesky factors—further strengthens the contribution. The empirical results, although brief, are sufficient (with additional suggestions below).

Beyond its stated goals, the work is likely to stimulate discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and on the trade-offs of learning transition dynamics in this manner versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

Given space constraints, the authors might still consider expanding on the differences between using a parametric transition model specified a priori and using a similar model as the GP mean function—particularly for out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting).

The results in Table 1 and the preceding description are somewhat unclear. It is not evident whether the reported RMSE is computed on a held-out dataset or on the same data used for conditioning (as suggested by the “|data” in the column headings). My primary interest is the RMSE for out-of-sample prediction, especially forward-time forecasting on withheld data. This is where I would expect the largest discrepancies between learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already evaluate this, an additional column reporting out-of-sample forecast errors would be a valuable addition.

A minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before it is defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1.

In summary, this is a strong and novel paper that should provoke interesting discussion.
