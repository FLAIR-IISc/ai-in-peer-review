This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the underlying transition dynamics. The inference procedure employs particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling, a recently developed technique. Importantly, the authors also address computational challenges through the exploitation of sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates, enhancing the practical applicability of their method.

This is a technically rigorous and well-written paper. The approach of online marginalization of the transition dynamics, coupled with the effective use of ancestor sampling to achieve this, is a significant contribution. Further strengthening the work is the careful consideration of computational efficiency, specifically through the use of sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates of covariance matrix Cholesky factors. The empirical results, while concise, provide sufficient validation of the proposed method (suggestions for improvement are detailed below). 

Beyond its immediate goals, this work is likely to spark discussion regarding inference techniques for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs associated with learning transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model *a priori*. 

A worthwhile addition, given space constraints allow, would be a more in-depth discussion of the differences between employing a parametric transition model defined *a priori* and utilizing a similar model as the mean function within the GP framework. This is particularly relevant when considering out-of-sample prediction tasks, such as forecasting.

I found the presentation of results in Table 1 and the accompanying text somewhat unclear. Specifically, it’s not immediately apparent whether the reported RMSE values correspond to evaluation on a withheld test set or are calculated using the same data used for conditioning (as indicated by the *|data* column headings). A key metric of interest would be RMSE performance on a genuine out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast extending forward in time using held-out data. This scenario is where I would anticipate the most substantial differences between the learned dynamics and the true underlying model. If Table 1 doesn't already present this, incorporating a column demonstrating out-of-sample forecast RMSE would significantly enhance the paper.

Finally, a minor notational issue: the abbreviation CPF-AS is introduced in Algorithm 1 before being formally defined in Section 3.3.1. 

Overall, this is a strong and novel contribution that promises to stimulate valuable discussion within the community. I recommend acceptance.
