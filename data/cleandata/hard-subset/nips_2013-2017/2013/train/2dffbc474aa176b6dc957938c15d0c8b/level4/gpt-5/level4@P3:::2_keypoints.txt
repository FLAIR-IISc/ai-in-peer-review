- Proposes a fully Bayesian approach to nonlinear, nonparametric GP-SSMs by marginalizing the transition function f to obtain a non‑Markovian prior over trajectories and sampling states/hyperparameters with PGAS.
- After sampling latent trajectories, the posterior over f is obtained analytically via standard GP regression.
- Introduces a sparse FIC variant to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T) and describes efficient Cholesky update implementations.
- Positions work relative to prior GP-SSM methods that use parametric approximations, MAP/EM, or decoupled learning/filtering, emphasizing retained nonparametric Bayesian treatment.
- Builds on PMCMC and ancestor/backward sampling advances, exploiting PGAS to mitigate path degeneracy in non‑Markovian smoothing.
- Technical development is sound: correct marginalization of f, derivation of the non‑Markovian prior, and valid use of PGAS and slice sampling for hyperparameters.
- Efficiency claims are plausible and well argued for both dense and sparse variants using rank‑1 Cholesky updates and reuse of intermediates.
- Experiments show capability to capture multimodal smoothing, competitive predictive accuracy, and calibrated uncertainty in cart‑pole experiments.
- Empirical section lacks comparisons to alternative PMCMC kernels (PMMH, PGBS) and does not study mixing/efficiency versus particle count N and horizon T.
- Paper is clearly written and well organized, with helpful exposition and implementation details for CPF‑AS/PGAS in non‑Markovian settings.
- Reproducibility could be improved by specifying priors and slice sampling settings, inducing input selection, providing CPF‑AS pseudo‑code, and reporting wall‑clock time and ESS/sec.
- Combination of exact f marginalization with PGAS for joint smoothing/hyperparameter inference in GP‑SSMs is novel and valuable; sparse PMCMC construction is a useful contribution.
- The method is significant for ML/statistics/control because it preserves GP expressivity and coherent uncertainty quantification across states, dynamics, and hyperparameters.
- Strengths: conceptually clean collapse of f, maintains nonparametric flexibility, algorithmic contributions (ancestor sampling for non‑Markovian smoothing, Cholesky updates, FIC), and demonstrated multimodality handling.
- Weaknesses: missing head‑to‑head PMCMC comparisons, no scaling study of N vs T, limited ablations (dense vs FIC, impact of M and inducing input choice), and sparse experimental details.
- Key questions for authors: comparisons (ESS/sec, RMSE) between PGAS, PMMH, PGBS; how inducing inputs are chosen and sensitivity to M; practical guidelines for N and PGAS settings; robustness to mean‑function misspecification and higher‑dimensional states.
- Arguments for acceptance: principled fully Bayesian solution, thoughtful use of state‑of‑the‑art PMCMC, concrete algorithmic and scalability insights.
- Arguments against acceptance: incomplete empirical validation (no PMMH/PGBS comparisons, no mixing/scaling analysis) and limited ablation/reporting limiting assessment of robustness and usability.
- Overall recommendation: promising and well‑executed submission leaning toward acceptance if the authors strengthen experiments with PMMH/PGBS comparisons and mixing/efficiency scaling results; borderline but conceptually strong without them.
