This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models. A key contribution is the non-parametric learning of the system's transition dynamics using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), enhanced with ancestor sampling to facilitate the online marginalization of the GP. The authors also address practical computational challenges by exploiting sparsity and employing low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper. The proposed method is novel, particularly in its use of ancestor sampling to enable the online marginalization of the transition dynamics, which is a significant contribution. The presentation is clear and accessible, making the complex material understandable. The paper's practical value is enhanced by a thoughtful discussion of computational efficiencies, such as exploiting sparsity and using low-rank updates for Cholesky factors. The empirical results, while brief, are sufficient to demonstrate the method's potential.

In addition to its direct contributions, this work is poised to stimulate valuable discussion on inference for state-space models with non-Markovian properties. It also raises important questions about the trade-offs between learning system dynamics non-parametrically versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

Suggestions for improvement:

1.  **Discussion on Parametric Models vs. GP Mean Functions:** While space constraints are acknowledged, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on the trade-offs between two modeling approaches: (1) using a fixed parametric transition model, and (2) using the same parametric form as the mean function for the GP prior. This comparison is particularly relevant for out-of-sample prediction tasks, such as forecasting, where the flexibility of the GP could offer distinct advantages or disadvantages that warrant exploration.

2.  **Clarity of Experimental Results:** The presentation of the empirical results could be clarified. Specifically, regarding Table 1, it is unclear whether the reported Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) is calculated on the training data (in-sample) or on a held-out test set (out-of-sample). The column headings containing "*|data" suggest an in-sample evaluation. The true utility of learning the dynamics would be most compellingly demonstrated through out-of-sample prediction, especially in a forecasting setting where the model predicts future states based on past observations. It is in this scenario that the differences between the learned dynamics and a potentially misspecified ground-truth model would be most apparent. I strongly recommend the authors clarify this point and, if not already present, add results for out-of-sample forecasting to the table. This would significantly strengthen the empirical validation of the proposed method.

3.  **Minor Presentation Point:** The abbreviation "CPF-AS" is used in Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in Section 3.3.1. It would improve readability to define it upon its first use.

In summary, this is a strong and novel paper that makes a valuable contribution to the literature on inference in nonlinear state-space models. The proposed method is elegant and the work is likely to inspire further research in this area.
