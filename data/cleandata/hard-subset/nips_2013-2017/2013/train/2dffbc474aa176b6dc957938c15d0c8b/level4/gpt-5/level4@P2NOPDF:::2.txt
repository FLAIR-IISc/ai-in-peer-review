The paper applies particle MCMC to Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), focusing on the ancestral-sampling particle Gibbs algorithm of Lindsten et al. The presentation is clear, and the application is both interesting and original. The paper also contributes useful model-specific methodology, notably a sparse GP-SSM formulation.

My main concern is the absence of comparisons with other particle MCMC schemes, particularly particle marginal Metropolis–Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). These baselines are relatively straightforward to implement and would provide an informative benchmark for the proposed method. Including them would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation and does not appear to require substantial additional effort.

I would also like to see diagnostic plots quantifying performance—e.g., autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS), ideally ESS per second—as functions of both the number of particles N and the time horizon T. As presented, the results do not clarify how to choose N as T grows. Should N scale linearly with T, or sublinearly?

Based on prior work (e.g., Whiteley et al.), I expect that PMMH typically requires a number of particles that grows quadratically with T for stable performance, whereas particle Gibbs variants (including backward or ancestral sampling) require only sublinear growth. It would be valuable to test and report this scaling behavior empirically in your setting.

In summary, this is a well-written and promising application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs. The paper would be considerably strengthened by head-to-head comparisons with PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, along with scaling studies and performance diagnostics (ACF/ESS vs. N and T) that clarify computational efficiency and mixing.
