This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the transition dynamics. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling, a recently introduced technique. The paper also addresses computational efficiencies through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, combined with the use of ancestor sampling to achieve this, is a novel contribution. Furthermore, the consideration of computational issues – specifically sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices – strengthens the work. The empirical results, though concise, are sufficient (with suggestions for improvement outlined below). 

Beyond its stated aims, this paper is likely to stimulate discussion regarding inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential benefits and drawbacks of learning the transition dynamics in this manner, as opposed to specifying a parametric model *a priori*. 

While space is limited, the authors might consider expanding the discussion to address the differences between using a parametric transition model defined *a priori* versus employing a similar model as the mean function within the GP. This is particularly relevant in the context of out-of-sample prediction, such as forecasting. 

The results presented in Table 1 and the corresponding description in the preceding paragraph are slightly unclear. I am unsure whether the reported RMSE values are calculated against a withheld dataset or the same dataset used for conditioning (as indicated by the *|data* in the column headings). My primary interest lies in RMSE performance on out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast forward in time using withheld data. I would anticipate observing the most significant differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model in this scenario. If Table 1 does not already present this, adding a column with such results would be a valuable improvement.

One minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that promises to stimulate interesting discussion.
