This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, incorporating the learning of transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference mechanism relies on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with the recently introduced concept of ancestor sampling. The authors also discuss computational efficiencies related to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically sound and well-presented, making it accessible to readers. The online marginalization of transition dynamics, combined with the use of ancestor sampling, is a novel contribution. Furthermore, the consideration of computational issues, such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices, adds strength to the paper. Although brief, the empirical results are sufficient, and some suggestions for further improvement are provided below.

In addition to its stated objectives, this paper is likely to stimulate discussion on inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages and disadvantages of learning transition dynamics in this manner, rather than specifying a parametric model a priori.

To further enhance the paper, the authors may consider discussing the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori and employing a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For instance, in out-of-sample prediction scenarios, such as forecasting, this comparison could provide valuable insights.

The results presented in Table 1, along with the preceding paragraph, are somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is uncertain whether the root mean squared error (RMSE) is calculated against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points conditioned upon (as indicated by "*|data" in the column headings). The primary interest lies in evaluating the RMSE against out-of-sample predictions, particularly forecasting forward in time against withheld data. This scenario is expected to exhibit the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already display this information, adding an extra column to show this would be a valuable contribution.

One minor point to note is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that is likely to stimulate interesting discussions.
