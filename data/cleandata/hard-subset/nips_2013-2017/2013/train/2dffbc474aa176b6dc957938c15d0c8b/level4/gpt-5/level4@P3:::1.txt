Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper presents a fully Bayesian method for inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior on the transition dynamics f and analytically marginalizing f. This yields a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories, for which the authors develop efficient posterior inference using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). The algorithm alternates between sampling the latent state trajectory x0:T and hyperparameters θ; given samples of x0:T and θ, predictive distributions over f are obtained in closed form, and the resulting predictive is a mixture of Gaussians. To reduce computational cost, the paper details how to reuse Cholesky factorizations across time and proposes a sparse GP-SSM using the FIC prior, achieving O(M^2 T) scaling.

Relative to prior work, this approach contrasts with methods that (i) treat the GP transition as parametric via a finite set of variables and learn with EM or MAP (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.), or (ii) develop filtering/smoothing approximations assuming a learned GP model (e.g., Deisenroth et al.). It builds on PMCMC foundations (Andrieu et al.) and leverages ancestor sampling (Lindsten et al.) to address path degeneracy and improve mixing in non-Markovian latent variable models. The proposed collapsing over f preserves the nonparametric character of the transition model, which is a notable conceptual difference from much of the earlier GP–SSM literature.

Quality:
- Technical soundness: The derivation of the collapsed prior p(x1:T | θ, x0) via GP one-step predictions is correct and clearly presented, including the observation that the resulting prior is not Gaussian. The PGAS construction is appropriate for non-Markovian models and the ancestor sampling weights are justified. Hyperparameter sampling via slice sampling conditioned on x0:T is standard and sensible. The sparse FIC construction and complexity analysis are well motivated.
- Empirical evaluation: The nonlinear benchmark demonstrates the method’s ability to recover multimodal smoothing distributions and to correct a misspecified mean function. The cart-pole example illustrates calibrated predictive uncertainty in higher dimensions. That said, the experiments are relatively brief for such a general method; additional quantitative comparisons would strengthen the case (see suggestions below).

Clarity:
The paper is generally well written and well organized. The role of the GP mean function and its interaction with the nonparametric residual is explained clearly, and implementation details on rank-1 Cholesky updates/downdates are appreciated. Two clarity issues:
- In Algorithm 1, the abbreviation CPF-AS (conditional particle filter with ancestor sampling) is used before being defined in Section 3.3.1; please define at first use.
- The description of Table 1 leaves ambiguity about whether RMSEs are computed on held-out data (forecasting) or on the training data; please clarify in text and column headings.

Originality:
Marginalizing the GP transition function and performing fully Bayesian inference over states and hyperparameters with PGAS is, to my knowledge, novel within GP-SSMs at the time of writing. Prior GP-SSM work largely relied on parametric surrogates plus EM/MAP, or assumed learned dynamics. The combination of collapsing, PGAS for non-Markovian trajectories, and a sparse FIC prior is a distinct and original contribution.

Significance:
The approach provides a principled, scalable path to learning flexible transition functions without sacrificing nonparametric expressivity. It addresses a core bottleneck—joint inference over states and dynamics—relevant to time series, control, and reinforcement learning. The ability to obtain smoothing distributions without a fixed transition model, and then to form a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive over f, is compelling. Assuming the method scales as claimed, it should be a useful reference and building block for subsequent research on Bayesian nonparametric dynamical models.

Specific strengths:
- Fully Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric expressivity by analytically marginalizing f.
- Use of PGAS to efficiently sample non-Markovian state trajectories; clear discussion of invariance and mixing.
- Practical computational advances: reuse of Cholesky factors, rank-1 updates/downdates, and a well-integrated sparse FIC variant with O(M^2 T) complexity.
- Sensible discussion of the GP mean function to encode prior knowledge.
- Experiments demonstrate multimodality handling and calibrated uncertainty.

Specific weaknesses and suggestions:
- Evaluation scope: Include out-of-sample forecasting results. For the nonlinear benchmark, report RMSE on a held-out sequence (e.g., multi-step and one-step ahead predictions) in addition to any in-sample metrics. If Table 1 already reflects held-out RMSE, make this explicit; otherwise, add a column for held-out forecasting performance.
- Baselines: Add comparisons to (a) EM/MAP-based GP-SSM learning (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.), and (b) particle Gibbs without ancestor sampling to quantify the benefit of ancestor sampling on mixing and accuracy.
- Diagnostics and scalability: Report runtime, number of particles, effective sample sizes, autocorrelation, and sensitivity to N and T. For the sparse variant, provide M, inducing point selection strategy, and accuracy vs. M trade-offs. A small ablation on the mean function choice (e.g., linear vs. the “model B”) would also be informative.
- Assumptions and limitations: Briefly discuss identifiability when both f and g are flexible; clarify how the method behaves in higher-dimensional state spaces and with non-Gaussian emissions. Comment on sensitivity to hyperpriors.
- Minor presentation items: Define CPF-AS on first use, ensure reference entries include full bibliographic details, and consider renaming “online marginalization” to “analytic marginalization” to avoid confusion with streaming settings.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Technically solid and original approach that advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs.
- Addresses a longstanding challenge (joint inference over states and nonparametric dynamics) with a principled, computationally aware solution.
- Likely to influence subsequent work in time series modeling, control, and RL.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical section is relatively limited; stronger quantitative comparisons and out-of-sample forecasting would solidify the contribution.
- Practical guidance on scalability and hyperparameter/inducing point selection could be expanded.

Overall assessment:
A strong and novel paper. With modest clarifications (especially around evaluation and CPF-AS definition) and expanded empirical comparisons emphasizing out-of-sample forecasting and scalability, it would make a valuable contribution to NIPS. I recommend acceptance.
