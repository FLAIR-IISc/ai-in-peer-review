This paper proposes applying particle MCMC methods for inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The authors focus specifically on the ancestral sampling variant of particle Gibbs, a recent and powerful technique. The paper is clearly written, and the application is both interesting and original. A particular strength is the development of a model-specific methodology for a sparse GP-SSM, which is a useful contribution.

However, a significant limitation of the current manuscript is the absence of a comparative analysis against other relevant particle MCMC schemes. To properly contextualize the performance of the proposed method, it is essential to include comparisons with the Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) algorithm and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling. Given that these are standard benchmarks in the field, their implementation for this model class should be relatively straightforward. Including them would provide critical insights into the relative advantages of the chosen ancestral sampling approach and would substantially strengthen the paper's empirical claims.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation could be significantly enhanced by a more thorough analysis of the algorithm's performance and computational scaling. The current results are not fully informative about the practical requirements of the method. I recommend including plots that illustrate key performance metrics—such as Effective Sample Size (ESS) or the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) of the inferred parameters—as a function of both the number of particles (N) and the length of the time series (T). This analysis is crucial for understanding how the computational budget must scale with the problem size. For example, does N need to grow linearly or sublinearly with T to maintain a constant inference quality?

This scaling analysis would be particularly illuminating when applied to the benchmark algorithms as well. It is hypothesized that for models of this type, the PMMH algorithm may require the number of particles N to scale quadratically with T to prevent the variance of the likelihood estimate from degrading. In contrast, particle Gibbs variants can often achieve more favorable scaling (e.g., sublinear growth). Verifying whether this holds for GP-SSMs would be a key contribution and would provide a strong motivation for using the proposed algorithm.

In summary, this paper presents a well-written and promising application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. The core idea is strong, and the development of the sparse GP-SSM is a valuable contribution. However, for the paper to be compelling, the empirical evaluation must be expanded. The inclusion of comparisons against standard benchmarks (PMMH and PG with backward sampling) and a detailed analysis of computational scaling are necessary to rigorously validate the benefits of the proposed approach.
