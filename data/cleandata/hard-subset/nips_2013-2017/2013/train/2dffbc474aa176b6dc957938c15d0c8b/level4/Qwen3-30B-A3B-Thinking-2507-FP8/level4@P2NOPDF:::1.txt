

This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, where transition dynamics are learned via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference employs particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling—a recent technique enabling online marginalization of transition dynamics. Computational efficiency is further enhanced through sparsity exploitation and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates for covariance matrices.  

The work is technically robust and exceptionally well-presented. The core novelty lies in the seamless integration of ancestor sampling to marginalize transition dynamics without compromising inference tractability, a significant advance over existing approaches. The authors’ attention to computational pragmatism—specifically, leveraging sparsity and low-rank operations for Cholesky factor updates—substantially strengthens the method’s applicability to larger-scale problems. The empirical validation, though concise, adequately supports the claims, though minor clarifications would enhance its impact.  

Beyond its immediate contributions, this paper is poised to spark meaningful discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs inherent in learning transition dynamics via GPs versus adopting a parametric model a priori. For instance, the flexibility of GP-based dynamics may yield advantages in complex systems but could introduce challenges in out-of-sample forecasting compared to well-specified parametric transitions. The authors might consider expanding their discussion on this dichotomy, particularly regarding predictive performance in forecasting scenarios.  

Regarding the empirical results, Table 1 and its accompanying description require clarification. It is unclear whether the reported RMSE values are computed against a withheld test set or the same data used for conditioning (as suggested by the column headers *|data*). Given the context, out-of-sample forecast accuracy—evaluated on temporally withheld data—would be the most relevant metric to demonstrate the method’s practical utility, as this scenario would most clearly reveal discrepancies between learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already report this, adding a dedicated column for out-of-sample RMSE (e.g., *|test*) would substantially strengthen the evaluation.  

Additionally, a minor editorial note: the abbreviation "CPF-AS" appears in Algorithm 1 before its definition in Section 3.3.1. Defining this acronym upon first use would improve readability.  

Overall, this is a strong, novel contribution with clear theoretical and practical merit. The method’s elegance in combining GP learning with PMCMC inference, coupled with its computational optimizations, makes it a valuable addition to the state-space modeling literature. The paper’s potential to influence both theoretical developments and applied work in sequential inference is significant. With the minor clarifications noted above, it would be an excellent candidate for publication.
