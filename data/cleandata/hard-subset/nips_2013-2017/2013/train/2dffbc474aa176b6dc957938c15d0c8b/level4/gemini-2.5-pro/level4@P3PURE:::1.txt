This paper presents a Bayesian approach for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, which simultaneously learns the transition dynamics using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) and incorporates the recent technique of ancestor sampling. The authors also discuss computational efficiencies gained through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, achieved through the novel application of ancestor sampling, is a significant contribution. The paper is further strengthened by its consideration of practical computational issues, such as leveraging sparsity and employing low-rank updates and downdates for the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices. While the empirical results are brief, they are sufficient to demonstrate the method's potential.

In addition to its stated aims, this work will likely stimulate discussion on inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models. It also raises important questions about the relative advantages and disadvantages of learning transition dynamics non-parametrically versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

Recognizing space limitations, the authors could consider adding further discussion on the differences between using a pre-specified parametric transition model and using a similar model as the mean function for the GP. This comparison would be particularly insightful in the context of out-of-sample prediction, such as forecasting.

The results in Table 1 and the description in the preceding paragraph could be clarified. It is unclear whether the reported RMSE is calculated on the training data (the data conditioned upon, as suggested by "*|data" in the column headings) or on a withheld test set. My main interest is in the RMSE for out-of-sample prediction, especially forecasting future states using withheld data. It is in this scenario that I would expect to see the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already show this, adding a column with out-of-sample forecast results would be a great addition.

One minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1.

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate interesting discussion.
