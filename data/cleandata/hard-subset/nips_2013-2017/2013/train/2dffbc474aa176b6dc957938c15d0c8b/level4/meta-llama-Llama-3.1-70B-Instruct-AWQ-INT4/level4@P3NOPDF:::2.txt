The authors propose an innovative application of particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models. Specifically, they focus on the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm recently introduced by Lindsten et al. The paper is well-written and presents an original application of particle MCMC. Additionally, the authors develop useful model-specific methodology, namely sparse Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSM).

One notable omission in the paper is the lack of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes. In particular, it would be beneficial to compare the proposed algorithm to the particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) scheme and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling, as described in Whiteley et al. Implementing these variants would be relatively straightforward and would provide valuable insights into their performance relative to the proposed scheme.

Furthermore, I would like to see graphical representations of the algorithms' performance, such as plots of the autocorrelation function (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as a function of the number of particles (N) and the length of the time series (T). As presented, the results are not particularly informative, and it is unclear how to scale N with respect to T. For instance, is it necessary to scale N linearly with T, or is a sublinear relationship sufficient?

In the context of these models, I believe that the PMMH would require the number of particles to increase quadratically with T, as observed in Whiteley et al. In contrast, both particle Gibbs schemes would require the number of particles to grow sublinearly with T. A comparison of these algorithms would help to elucidate the trade-offs between them.

In conclusion, the paper presents a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. However, the work could be significantly improved by comparing the proposed algorithm to the PMMH and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of each approach and would be a valuable contribution to the field.
