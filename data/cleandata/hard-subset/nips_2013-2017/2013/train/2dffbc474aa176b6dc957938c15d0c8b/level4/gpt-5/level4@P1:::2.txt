This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the unknown transition function f and to marginalize f analytically, thereby sampling directly from the joint smoothing distribution over the latent states and hyperparameters using particle MCMC. The authors build on particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS; Lindsten et al.) to address the strong temporal dependencies induced by collapsing out f, and they show how to sample hyperparameters given the latent trajectory. They also propose a sparse GP-SSM variant using the FIC prior to reduce computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2T), with careful implementation details (rank-one updates/downdates of Cholesky factors) to make CPF-AS efficient. Experiments on a nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a cart-and-pole system illustrate that the approach can recover accurate smoothing distributions and meaningful predictive uncertainty, even when the GP mean function is misspecified.

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts with EM/MAP approaches that parametrize the GP (e.g., Turner et al.; Wang et al.) and with methods that assume learned GP dynamics and focus on filtering/smoothing (Deisenroth et al.). On the inference side, it leverages PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and specifically PGAS (Lindsten et al.), which is well suited to non-Markovian latent variable models arising from collapsing f.

Strengths
- Quality: The modeling and inference pipeline is technically sound. Collapsing f is principled and preserves the nonparametric nature of the model; the PGAS construction is appropriate and well justified. The sparse FIC construction is neatly integrated, and the implementation details (reuse of factorizations, rank-one updates) are valuable.
- Clarity: The paper is clearly written and well organized. The derivation of the collapsed trajectory prior and the CPF-AS description are accessible, and the computational complexity discussion is helpful.
- Originality: Combining exact marginalization of the GP transition with PGAS for joint state/hyperparameter inference in GP-SSMs is a compelling and, to my knowledge, novel application of PMCMC in this setting. The sparse GP-SSM variant tailored to this inference scheme is also a useful contribution.
- Significance: A fully Bayesian, nonparametric SSM with efficient inference is of broad interest. The ability to obtain high-quality smoothing without committing to a fixed parametric transition model, and to produce expressive predictive distributions (mixtures of Gaussians), is valuable for downstream tasks.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Missing baselines within PMCMC. The paper does not compare PGAS to other standard PMCMC variants that are directly applicable here:
  - Particle marginal Metropolis–Hastings (PMMH).
  - Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.).
  These are straightforward to implement in the presented framework and would let readers assess whether PGAS is indeed preferable in this model class. Including acceptance rates (for PMMH), mixing diagnostics, and run-time comparisons would substantially strengthen the empirical section.
- Scaling with N and T. The current results do not show how performance scales with the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T). Please report, for each algorithm:
  - Autocorrelation functions (ACF) for key quantities (e.g., log posterior, selected state components, representative hyperparameters) and/or effective sample size (ESS) as functions of N and T.
  - Empirical guidance on how N should be set as T grows. As suggested in Whiteley et al., PMMH may require a number of particles growing quadratically with T to maintain stable variance of the likelihood estimator, whereas both particle Gibbs variants are expected to require sublinear growth. It would be very useful to confirm or refute this empirically for GP-SSMs.
- Experimental detail. While the qualitative results are encouraging, the current presentation does not fully quantify mixing or computational efficiency. Plots of ACF/ESS versus N and T, together with wall-clock times, would make the results more informative and actionable.

Evaluation against NIPS criteria
- Quality: Strong on methodology; experiments demonstrate feasibility but lack comparative PMCMC baselines and scaling analysis.
- Clarity: Generally clear and well structured. Adding the requested diagnostic plots and explicit references for the backward-sampling particle Gibbs baseline (Whiteley et al.) would improve completeness.
- Originality: High; the collapsed GP-SSM with PGAS and a tailored sparse prior is a novel and interesting combination.
- Significance: Potentially high, particularly if empirical comparisons to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling confirm superior mixing and/or computational efficiency.

Pros for acceptance
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment of GP-SSMs that preserves nonparametric expressivity.
- Appropriate and carefully implemented use of PGAS; insightful implementation details and sparse variant.
- Demonstrates applicability on challenging nonlinear systems with multimodal smoothing.

Cons for acceptance
- No empirical comparison to PMMH or particle Gibbs with backward sampling.
- No systematic study of mixing and ESS versus N and T, leaving practical guidance unclear.

Overall assessment
A well-written and technically solid application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs with useful model-specific methodology (sparse GP-SSM and efficient CPF-AS implementation). The paper would be significantly stronger with direct comparisons to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling and with scaling plots (ACF/ESS) versus N and T to clarify practical trade-offs and particle growth with T. I recommend acceptance conditional on adding these empirical comparisons and diagnostics.
