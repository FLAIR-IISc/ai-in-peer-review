This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, leveraging Gaussian processes (GPs) and Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The authors address a challenging problem by marginalizing over the transition dynamics, enabling efficient smoothing and subsequent learning of the GP dynamics. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, building upon existing work in both state-space models and GP literature, notably citing Rasmussen & Williams (2006) and Turner et al. (2013). The development of a sparse GP-SSM construction is a valuable contribution, addressing computational complexity.

The core idea – combining the flexibility of GPs with the power of PMCMC for state estimation and system identification – is original and represents a significant advance over previous approaches that often rely on parametric approximations. The method elegantly addresses the non-Markovian nature of the model introduced by marginalizing out the transition function and the use of recent advances in PMCMC (Lindsten et al., 2012) is commendable. The application to a cart-pole system further demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed framework.

However, a key weakness lies in the lack of comparative evaluation against other established particle MCMC schemes. Specifically, comparing the proposed method to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (Whiteley et al., as referenced in the discussion of scalability) would significantly strengthen the paper. While the authors acknowledge the potential scaling differences (quadratic for PMMH vs. sublinear for particle Gibbs), empirical validation of these claims is crucial.  The statement that these comparisons “would not be much work” underscores this point; including them would significantly boost confidence in the method's efficiency. 

Furthermore, the experimental results, while demonstrating the method's functionality, lack detailed analysis of algorithm performance.  Providing plots illustrating the autocorrelation function (ACF) or Effective Sample Size (ESS) as a function of the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T) would offer valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior and scalability. The current results are somewhat limited in their informativeness, making it difficult to assess the practical implications of the method’s computational complexity. A clearer understanding of the relationship between N, T, and mixing performance is necessary. 

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of marginalizing out the state transition function in GP-SSMs, coupled with a tailored PMCMC sampler, is novel and offers a significant advantage over existing methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The method is theoretically well-founded, leveraging established results from GP theory and particle filtering.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and clearly explains complex concepts.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The application to both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system demonstrates the practical potential of the method.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The absence of a comparison to alternative particle MCMC schemes (PMMH, particle Gibbs with backward sampling) is a significant omission.
*   **Limited Experimental Analysis:** The provided experimental results lack detailed analysis of algorithm performance (e.g., ACF, ESS) making it difficult to assess scalability.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the weaknesses outlined above, this is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. The core idea is novel, the method is technically sound, and the paper is exceptionally well-written.  However, addressing the concerns regarding comparative evaluation and experimental analysis would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the inclusion of comparisons to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, and the addition of more detailed performance analysis (ACF/ESS plots).
