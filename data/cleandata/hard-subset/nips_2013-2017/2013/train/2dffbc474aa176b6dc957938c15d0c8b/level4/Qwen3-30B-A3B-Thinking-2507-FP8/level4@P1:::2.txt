

The paper introduces a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), addressing the challenge of learning nonlinear dynamics by placing a Gaussian process prior over the transition function and marginalizing it during inference. The authors develop a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm that efficiently samples from the smoothing distribution while preserving the full nonparametric expressivity of the model. A key contribution is the sparse GP-SSM construction using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation, which significantly reduces computational complexity without compromising model flexibility.

This work builds upon previous research in GP-based state-space modeling (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.) by providing a fully Bayesian framework that properly handles the nonparametric nature of the model, unlike existing approaches that often resort to parametric approximations. It extends the recent particle MCMC literature, particularly Lindsten et al.'s PGAS algorithm, to the specific context of GP-SSMs.

The paper's strengths are substantial. The theoretical development is clear and well-justified, with careful derivation of the marginalization approach and the resulting inference algorithms. The sparse GP-SSM construction is particularly valuable for practical applications, offering a principled way to reduce computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T) where M ≪ T. The experimental results demonstrate the model's ability to capture complex dynamics, including multimodal smoothing distributions (e.g., the nonlinear system benchmark where the smoothing density is bimodal due to sign ambiguity in observations). The cart and pole system experiment effectively shows how the model adapts to different regions of the state space with appropriate uncertainty estimates.

The main weakness concerns the lack of comparison to alternative particle MCMC schemes. The authors do not compare their PGAS-based approach to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) or Particle Gibbs with Backward Sampling (PGBS), which would provide crucial context for understanding the relative advantages of their method. As Whiteley et al. (2013) established, PMMH typically requires the number of particles to grow quadratically with the time horizon T, while particle Gibbs methods like PGAS require only sublinear growth. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the authors' approach offers practical computational advantages.

Additionally, the paper lacks diagnostic plots showing algorithm performance as a function of N (number of particles) and T (time steps). While Table 1 reports RMSE results, the authors do not provide visualizations of autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample sizes (ESS) to help readers understand the scaling behavior. It remains unclear whether N needs to scale linearly with T or can remain constant as T increases, which is critical information for practitioners considering implementation.

The paper is well-written with clear mathematical derivations and appropriate experimental validation. The authors properly address the challenges of non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing the transition function, and their solution using PGAS is well-motivated and technically sound. The sparse GP-SSM extension is particularly well-executed, with detailed implementation considerations that will be valuable for practitioners.

In terms of significance, this work makes an important contribution to Bayesian nonparametric state-space modeling. By enabling fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs without parametric approximations, it opens new possibilities for modeling complex dynamical systems where the underlying dynamics are poorly understood. The ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions—critical for systems with ambiguous observations—is a particularly compelling strength.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a comparison between PGAS, PMMH, and PGBS, including a discussion of computational complexity scaling with T
2. Including diagnostic plots (ACF, ESS) showing performance as a function of N and T
3. Clarifying the relationship between the number of inducing points and the resulting trade-off between accuracy and computational cost

Overall, this is a strong technical contribution that addresses an important problem in Bayesian state-space modeling. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent candidate for publication at NIPS. The work is technically sound, well-motivated, and makes a clear contribution to the field of Bayesian nonparametric methods for dynamical systems.
