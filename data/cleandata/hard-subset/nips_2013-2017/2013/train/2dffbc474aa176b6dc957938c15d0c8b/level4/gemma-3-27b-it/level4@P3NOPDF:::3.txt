## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission

This paper explores the application of Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) sampling within a State-Space Model (SSM) framework, utilizing a Gaussian Process (GP) as a prior over the model parameters. The core idea – extending parameter estimation beyond typical filtering and smoothing approaches – is a straightforward and potentially valuable application of PMCMC methodology. 

However, the paper’s primary weakness lies in its insufficient evaluation of the practical challenges inherent in implementing and tuning the PMCMC scheme. While the authors demonstrate the method’s functionality, they do not provide a detailed analysis of the difficulties encountered in achieving convergence or the sensitivity of the results to parameter settings within the PMCMC algorithm itself. A thorough investigation of these aspects would have significantly strengthened the contribution, arguably becoming the most important takeaway from the work.

Currently, the paper reads more as a demonstration of feasibility than a rigorous evaluation. A more critical assessment of the strengths and weaknesses of this approach, particularly in comparison to existing SSM parameter estimation techniques, is needed. For example, a discussion of computational cost, scalability, and performance on challenging datasets would be highly beneficial. The authors should consider explicitly addressing scenarios where this PMCMC-based approach might *not* be suitable, and articulate the trade-offs involved.

Given the nature of the work—an application of a well-established methodology (PMCMC) to a specific problem (SSM with GP prior)—a robust and detailed empirical evaluation is crucial for justifying its novelty and practical impact. While a complete overhaul of the experimental section may be beyond the scope of a revision, focusing on the challenges of getting the PMCMC scheme to work reliably, and quantifying its performance under various conditions, would greatly improve the paper. 

Specifically, investigating the impact of particle number, proposal distributions, and acceptance rates on the accuracy and stability of the estimates would be informative. Providing diagnostics to assess convergence, beyond simply showing successful estimation, is essential. Furthermore, a comparison to alternative parameter estimation methods (e.g., Expectation-Maximization, variational inference) would provide valuable context and highlight the advantages or disadvantages of the proposed approach.

In summary, the paper presents a conceptually sound idea but lacks a sufficiently detailed and critical evaluation. Focusing the revision on the practical challenges of PMCMC implementation within an SSM, and providing a more thorough analysis of its performance characteristics, would significantly enhance the paper’s value and impact. Without this, the contribution remains largely a demonstration of a known technique applied to a new context, rather than a substantial advance in the field.
