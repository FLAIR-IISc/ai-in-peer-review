This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the transition dynamics. The core inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) incorporating the recently introduced idea of ancestor sampling. The authors also address computational efficiency through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, combined with ancestor sampling to achieve this, is a novel contribution. Furthermore, the consideration of computational issues like sparsity and efficient Cholesky factor updates strengthens the work considerably. The empirical results, while concise, are sufficient to demonstrate the approach’s effectiveness.

This work builds on a rich literature of GP-based SSMs, such as the filtering and smoothing methods of Deisenroth et al. [1, 2], and extends these by providing a crucial component – learning the GP model itself – rather than assuming a pre-defined transition dynamics. It also differentiates itself from earlier EM-based approaches like Turner et al. [3] and Wang et al. [4], which often rely on parametric approximations, by fully preserving the nonparametric expressivity of the GP. The approach presented here circumvents the potential overfitting issues associated with high-dimensional state spaces highlighted by Wang et al. [4].  The use of PMCMC with ancestor sampling builds upon the foundations laid by Andrieu et al. [5] and Lindsten et al. [6, 7], adapting these techniques to the specific challenges of non-Markovian SSMs.

The paper's significance lies not only in its technical contributions but also in its potential to stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models. The advantages and disadvantages of learning the transition dynamics, as opposed to specifying a parametric model *a priori*, warrant further investigation, particularly in forecasting scenarios.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could expand on the differences between using a parametric transition model and using a similar model as the mean function within the GP framework, specifically regarding out-of-sample prediction and forecasting performance. The authors touch upon this, but a more detailed discussion would be beneficial.

The results in Table 1 and the associated description require clarification. It’s unclear whether the reported RMSE is calculated against a withheld test set or the data used for conditioning. A crucial addition would be a column presenting the RMSE for out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast forward in time based on held-out data. This would convincingly demonstrate the true predictive power of the learned dynamics and allow for a more direct comparison to the ground truth model.

A minor stylistic point: the abbreviation CPF-AS is introduced in Algorithm 1 before being defined in Section 3.3.1.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The online marginalization of the transition dynamics and the application of ancestor sampling within PMCMC are significant contributions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper is mathematically rigorous and presents a well-defined Bayesian framework.
*   **Practical Relevance:** The consideration of computational efficiency through sparsity and low-rank updates makes the approach more applicable to real-world problems.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand, despite the technical complexity.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work has the potential to stimulate further research in non-Markovian SSMs and GP-based inference.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the experiments are sufficient, additional results, particularly focusing on out-of-sample forecasting, would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Minor Clarity Issues:** The description of Table 1 could be more precise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and novel paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. The technical innovations, combined with the clear presentation and potential for impact, make it a worthy addition to NIPS. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the clarification of the RMSE evaluation in Table 1 and the inclusion of out-of-sample forecasting results.
