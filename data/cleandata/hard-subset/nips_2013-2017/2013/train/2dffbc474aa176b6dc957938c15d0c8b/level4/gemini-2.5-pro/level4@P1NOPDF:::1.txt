This paper presents a Bayesian approach for state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, which simultaneously learns the transition dynamics using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), leveraging the recent technique of ancestor sampling. The authors also discuss computational efficiencies gained from sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically strong and well-written paper, presented in a clear and accessible manner. The proposed method for online marginalization of the transition dynamics, enabled by ancestor sampling, is a novel contribution. The paper is further strengthened by its consideration of practical computational issues, such as exploiting sparsity and using low-rank updates and downdates for Cholesky factors. The empirical results, while concise, are sufficient to demonstrate the method's potential, though I offer suggestions for their improvement below.

Beyond its direct contributions, this work is likely to stimulate valuable discussion on inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the relative advantages of learning transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

If space permits, the authors could expand on the trade-offs between specifying a parametric transition model a priori versus using a similar model as the mean function for the GP prior. This is particularly relevant for tasks like out-of-sample prediction and forecasting.

I found the results presented in Table 1, and their accompanying description, somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is not clear whether the reported RMSE is calculated on the training data or on a withheld test set (as might be implied by the "*|data" column headings). I would be most interested in seeing performance evaluated on an out-of-sample forecasting task, where the model predicts future states based on withheld data. This is the scenario where I expect the most significant differences between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model to emerge. If Table 1 does not already report these results, adding a column with out-of-sample forecasting RMSE would be a valuable addition.

A minor point: the abbreviation "CPF-AS" is used in Algorithm 1 but is not defined until the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1.

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that is likely to stimulate interesting discussion in the field.
