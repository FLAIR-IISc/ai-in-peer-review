Summary and relation to prior work:
This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior over the state transition function f, then marginalize f analytically to obtain a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. The joint smoothing distribution over latent states and hyperparameters is then sampled using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Given samples from the smoothing distribution, the posterior over f is obtained in closed form as in standard GP regression. The paper also introduces a sparse GP-SSM variant using the fully independent conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T), and describes efficient implementations via Cholesky updates.

This work is well positioned within the literature. It contrasts with GP-SSM learning methods that rely on parametric approximations, MAP/EM estimation, or decoupled learning/filtering (e.g., Turner et al.; Wang et al.; Deisenroth et al.), by performing fully Bayesian state and hyperparameter inference while preserving the nonparametric GP prior. On the inference side, it builds on PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and advances in backward/ancestor sampling for particle Gibbs (Lindsten et al.), exploiting PGAS to address path degeneracy in non-Markovian smoothing.

Quality:
- The technical development is sound. The collapsing of f, derivation of the non-Markovian prior over trajectories (product of Gaussian one-step conditionals), and the use of PGAS to preserve the exact smoothing distribution are correct and clearly explained. The hyperparameter updates via slice sampling are appropriate given the collapsed GP structure.
- The efficiency claims are plausible and well argued: O(T^3) for dense GP via rank-1 updates/downdates of Cholesky factors and O(M^2T) under FIC with careful reuse of intermediate quantities.
- Experiments demonstrate the method’s ability to capture multimodality in smoothing and to deliver competitive predictive accuracy on a nonlinear benchmark; the cart-pole case illustrates calibrated uncertainty.
- However, the empirical section lacks comparisons to alternative PMCMC kernels and does not study mixing/efficiency as a function of the number of particles N and time horizon T. This limits the practical conclusions one can draw about sampler choice and scaling.

Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written and generally well organized. The exposition of CPF-AS/PGAS for non-Markovian models and the implementation details are helpful.
- Suggestions to improve clarity and reproducibility: specify priors and slice sampling settings; detail how inducing inputs are selected; include pseudo-code for CPF-AS with the proposed updates; report wall-clock time and effective sample size (ESS) per second.

Originality:
- The combination of exact marginalization of f with PGAS for joint smoothing/hyperparameter inference in GP-SSMs is, to my knowledge, novel in the NIPS literature. The sparse GP-SSM construction within this PMCMC framework and the practical cost reductions are also valuable.

Significance:
- A fully Bayesian, nonparametric approach to learning dynamical systems is of broad interest in ML, statistics, and control. The method retains GP expressivity while enabling coherent uncertainty quantification in states, dynamics, and hyperparameters. If supported by stronger empirical comparisons and scaling analyses, this could become a reference approach for GP-SSMs.

Strengths:
- Conceptually clean: collapse f and sample states/hyperparameters with an invariant PGAS kernel.
- Maintains full nonparametric flexibility; posterior over f obtained analytically given states.
- Solid algorithmic contributions: ancestor sampling for non-Markovian smoothing, efficient Cholesky updates, and sparse FIC variant.
- Demonstrates ability to handle multimodal smoothing and provides calibrated predictive uncertainty.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- Missing comparisons to alternative PMCMC schemes:
  - Particle marginal Metropolis–Hastings (PMMH): important baseline; expected to require more particles as T increases, but the empirical trade-off with PGAS should be shown.
  - Particle Gibbs with backward simulation (PGBS), e.g., Lindsten and Schön (2012), and related work by Whiteley et al.: how do mixing and cost compare to PGAS in this setting?
- No study of scaling with N and T. Please include:
  - ACF and integrated autocorrelation time for key functionals, and ESS per second, versus N for multiple T.
  - Empirical guidance on how N should scale with T for PMMH and PG variants in this non-Markovian context.
- Limited ablations:
  - Dense vs. FIC: impact of M on accuracy/mixing/runtime; sensitivity to inducing input selection.
  - Sensitivity to mean function specification and identifiability when g has unknown parameters.
- Experimental details are sparse: prior choices, slice sampling parameters, acceptance/ESS, runtime, and variability across runs should be reported.

Questions for the authors:
- Can you provide head-to-head comparisons of PGAS with PMMH and PGBS on your benchmarks, reporting ESS per second and RMSE? Do your findings corroborate the expectation that PMMH needs O(T^2) particles while PG variants grow sublinearly with T?
- How are inducing inputs chosen in the FIC model, and how sensitive are results to M and the selection strategy?
- What are the practical guidelines for choosing N and PGAS settings (e.g., number of iterations) to achieve a target ESS on long sequences?
- How robust is learning to misspecification of the GP mean function and to higher-dimensional state spaces?

Arguments for acceptance:
- Addresses an important problem with a principled, fully Bayesian solution that preserves GP nonparametric expressivity.
- Leverages state-of-the-art PMCMC (PGAS) thoughtfully for non-Markovian smoothing.
- Offers concrete algorithmic insights and a scalable sparse variant with careful implementation.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical validation is incomplete: no comparison to PMMH/PGBS and no scaling analysis of mixing/efficiency.
- Limited ablations and reporting hinder assessment of robustness and practical usability.

Overall recommendation:
This is a well-executed and timely application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs with meaningful algorithmic contributions. I lean toward acceptance provided the authors strengthen the experimental section with comparisons to PMMH and PGBS and include mixing/efficiency scaling results. Without these additions, the submission is borderline, but still promising given its conceptual clarity and potential impact.
