This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while also learning the transition dynamics using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with the recently-introduced idea of ancestor sampling. The paper also discusses computational efficiencies related to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics and the use of ancestor sampling to achieve this are novel contributions. The consideration of computational issues such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices further strengthens the paper. The empirical results, although brief, are sufficient (see suggestions below).

In addition to its stated aims, the paper is likely to stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages and disadvantages of learning the transition dynamics in this way rather than specifying a parametric model a priori.

While space is limited, the authors may want to consider further discussion around the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori versus using a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For example, in out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting), it would be interesting to explore the implications of these different approaches.

The results in Table 1 and the preceding paragraph are somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is unclear whether the root mean squared error (RMSE) is evaluated against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points used for conditioning (as indicated by the "*|data" in the column headings). My main interest would be in evaluating the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast forward in time against withheld data. It is in this scenario that I would expect to see the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already show this, adding an extra column to do so would be a valuable addition.

One minor point is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate some interesting discussion.
