

This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process prior to learn transition dynamics. The inference employs particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling for online marginalization of transition dynamics. Computational efficiency is enhanced through sparsity exploitation and low-rank updates to Cholesky factors of covariance matrices.  

The work is technically robust, well-motivated, and clearly presented. The online marginalization of dynamics via ancestor sampling constitutes a novel contribution, and the attention to computational aspects—particularly sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates—significantly strengthens the paper. Empirical results, while concise, are sufficient to support the core claims.  

Beyond its primary contributions, the paper will likely spark valuable discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs of learning transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model a priori. To further enrich this discussion, the authors might clarify the distinction between using a parametric transition model (given upfront) versus employing a similar model as the mean function of the Gaussian process, especially for out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting).  

A key ambiguity arises in Table 1 and its description: it is unclear whether the reported RMSE is computed on a holdout set or the same data used for conditioning (indicated by "*|data" in column headers). For meaningful evaluation of predictive performance, RMSE on out-of-sample forecasts (i.e., forward prediction against withheld data) is most relevant, as this scenario should reveal the largest discrepancies between learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already include this, an additional column reporting out-of-sample RMSE would substantially improve the results.  

One minor point: the abbreviation "CPF-AS" appears in Algorithm 1 before being defined in Section 3.3.1.  

Overall, this is a strong, novel contribution that advances inference for state-space models and will stimulate productive dialogue in the field.
