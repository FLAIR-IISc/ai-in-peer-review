This paper introduces a fully Bayesian framework for inference and system identification in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs). The central idea is to place a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the unknown state transition function, allowing for flexible, nonparametric modeling of the dynamics. The key methodological contribution is to marginalize out the GP transition function and then perform inference directly on the joint smoothing distribution of the latent states. This challenging non-Markovian inference problem is elegantly addressed using a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically the Particle Gibbs sampler with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This approach builds upon the rich literature of GP-SSMs [e.g., 2, 5, 8] but distinguishes itself by avoiding parametric approximations of the GP, thereby preserving its full nonparametric expressivity. The authors also incorporate sparse GP approximations and efficient rank-one updates to Cholesky factorizations to reduce the computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2T).

**Strengths:**
*   **Quality:** The paper is technically sound, presenting a complete and well-motivated piece of work. The methodology is clearly described, and the theoretical claims regarding the inference scheme appear correct. The presentation is well-organized and makes a complex topic accessible to the reader.
*   **Originality:** The main contribution—marginalizing the GP dynamics function and using a tailored PGAS sampler for inference on the resulting non-Markovian state sequence—is highly novel. This provides a fundamentally different and more principled approach compared to previous methods that rely on parametric representations of the GP [2] or MAP estimation [8].
*   **Significance:** This work provides a powerful tool for learning unknown dynamics in time-series and control problems. The ability to perform fully Bayesian inference without resorting to parametric approximations of the dynamics is a significant advance. The careful consideration of computational scalability makes the method practical. The paper is likely to be impactful and inspire further research into inference methods for nonparametric dynamical systems.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of the practical differences between using a given parametric model as the GP's mean function versus using that same model as the complete transition function. A comparative analysis, particularly in the context of out-of-sample forecasting, could offer valuable insights into the benefits of the additional flexibility afforded by the GP.
*   The experimental evaluation in Section 6.1 and Table 1 could be clarified. It is not immediately obvious whether the reported root-mean-square error (RMSE) is computed against the training data or a held-out test set. The most convincing demonstration of the learned model's utility would be its performance on a forecasting task over a future time horizon. If not already included, adding a column to Table 1 with RMSEs for such an out-of-sample prediction scenario would significantly strengthen the empirical results.
*   A minor point on clarity: The acronym "CPF-AS" is used in Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in Section 3.3.1. Defining it upon first use would enhance the manuscript's readability.

Overall, this is a strong and novel contribution that addresses an important problem with an elegant and technically sophisticated solution. The proposed method advances the state of the art in learning GP-SSMs and should be of broad interest to the community.
