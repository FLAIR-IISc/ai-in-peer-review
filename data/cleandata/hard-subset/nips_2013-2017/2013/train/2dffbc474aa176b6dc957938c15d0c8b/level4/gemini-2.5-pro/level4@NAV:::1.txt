This work introduces a Bayesian framework for estimating states and parameters within nonlinear state-space models. A key feature of the method is its ability to simultaneously learn the transition dynamics by employing a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using a particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, which is enhanced with the recent technique of ancestor sampling. Furthermore, the authors address computational performance, detailing efficiency gains from exploiting sparsity and using low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically robust and well-executed, with a presentation style that is both clear and easy to follow. The proposed method for online marginalization of the transition dynamics, enabled by ancestor sampling, represents a significant and novel contribution. The practical value of the paper is further enhanced by its treatment of computational aspects, including the use of sparsity and low-rank modifications to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices. Although concise, the empirical evaluation is adequate for demonstrating the method's capabilities.

Beyond its direct contributions, this work is poised to spark valuable discussions within the community concerning inference strategies for state-space models that exhibit non-Markovian properties and the trade-offs associated with learning transition dynamics non-parametrically versus specifying a parametric model beforehand.

Acknowledging space limitations, the authors could strengthen the paper by including a more detailed discussion on the distinctions between employing a pre-specified parametric transition model and using an analogous model as the mean function for the Gaussian process. This comparison would be particularly insightful in the context of out-of-sample prediction, such as time-series forecasting.

I found the presentation of results in Table 1, along with the accompanying text, to be somewhat ambiguous. Specifically, it is not clear whether the reported RMSE is computed on a held-out test set or on the same data used for model conditioning (as suggested by the `*|data` headings). An evaluation of out-of-sample predictive performance, particularly for forecasting future values using withheld data, would be of great interest. I anticipate that such a forecasting task would most effectively highlight the performance differences between the learned dynamics and the true underlying model. If this analysis is not already included, adding a column to Table 1 with these out-of-sample results would be a valuable addition.

A minor correction: the acronym CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 prior to its definition in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. This is a strong and innovative paper that is likely to generate meaningful discussion in the field.
