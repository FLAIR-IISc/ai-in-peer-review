This paper applies Particle MCMC—specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS)—to fully Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The authors place a GP prior on the unknown state-transition function, marginalize this function to preserve nonparametric flexibility, and then sample from the joint smoothing distribution of the latent states and hyperparameters using PMCMC. Given state samples, the predictive distribution of the transition function follows from standard GP regression; a sparse GP (FIC) variant reduces computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2T). Experiments include a nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a cart–pole system, demonstrating that the approach can capture complex dynamics and represent predictive uncertainty.

Relation to prior work: The paper contrasts with EM/MAP-based approaches to GP-SSMs (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.), which effectively parametrize the GP via finite targets, and with methods that assume learned dynamics and focus on filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al.). Its main technical distinction is collapsing the transition function and using PGAS to sample entire trajectories in a non-Markovian latent model, aligning with the PMCMC framework of Andrieu et al. and recent ancestor-sampling advances.

Strengths:
- Clear and principled use of collapsing: marginalizing the transition GP reduces strong x–f coupling and yields a coherent fully Bayesian treatment.
- Appropriate sampling machinery: PGAS is well-suited to non-Markovian posteriors arising after marginalization; the invariance and mixing properties are correctly invoked.
- Expressive predictions: representing f|data as a mixture of Gaussians over GP conditionals naturally handles multimodality from ambiguous observations.
- Practical scalability: integrating a sparse GP prior and detailing factorization reuse shows attention to computational issues.

Weaknesses and areas for improvement:
- Limited empirical evaluation of PMCMC practicality. The key challenge—getting PGAS to mix well in this non-Markovian setting—is not evaluated in depth. The paper would be much stronger with:
  - Mixing diagnostics (autocorrelations, ESS), sensitivity to number of particles N, MCMC iterations, and thinning.
  - Robustness to sequence length T, state dimension, and prior choices; impact of ancestor sampling vs. alternatives.
  - Wall-clock runtime and memory vs. accuracy, with ablations (vanilla vs. sparse GP; marginalizing f vs. parametric representations).
- Comparisons: Results are primarily demonstrations rather than comprehensive benchmarks. Quantitative comparisons to EM/MAP GP-SSMs, variational/EP approaches, or alternative PMCMC variants are missing.
- Practical guidance: Little discussion of initialization, tuning (e.g., slice sampling settings for hyperparameters), resampling schedules, or when PGAS degrades due to path degeneracy.
- Scope of experiments: The two case studies are illustrative but small-scale; more diverse or higher-dimensional real-world datasets would better support significance claims.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The methodology is sound and correctly connected to PMCMC theory. However, empirical support for efficiency and robustness is not sufficiently detailed to validate practical claims.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well-written with useful implementation notes, but it could benefit from clearer guidance on parameter settings and diagnostic practices for practitioners.
- Originality: The combination—collapsing the GP transition and applying PGAS in GP-SSMs—is a neat synthesis rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic concept. The novelty lies in the integration and implementation.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for learning flexible SSMs, especially where multimodality matters. The current evaluation, however, stops short of demonstrating clear state-of-the-art advances across standard benchmarks.

Suggestions:
- Add a thorough study of mixing and sensitivity (N, M, T, priors), including ESS and ACF plots, and report acceptance/refresh rates for ancestor sampling.
- Provide runtime/accuracy trade-offs and ablations (sparse vs. dense GP; different mean functions; with/without collapsing).
- Include competitive baselines (EM/MAP GP-SSMs, variational GPSSMs, EP-based filters/smoothers) with consistent metrics.
- Offer practical tuning recommendations and, if possible, code or detailed pseudocode for key kernels and factorization reuse.

Pros for acceptance:
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric flexibility.
- Appropriate and well-motivated use of PGAS in a challenging non-Markovian posterior.
- Useful implementation insights, including sparse GP integration.

Cons against acceptance:
- Empirical section is largely demonstrative; lacks rigorous evaluation of PMCMC challenges and comparative baselines.
- Limited evidence of scalability and robustness in more demanding settings.
- Insufficient practical guidance on making the method work reliably.

Overall: A solid and straightforward application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs with clean modeling and promising demonstrations. Its main contribution would be much more compelling with a deeper, critical evaluation of the practical difficulties and performance of the PMCMC scheme; as written, the experimental section does not fully support strong claims about effectiveness and usability.
