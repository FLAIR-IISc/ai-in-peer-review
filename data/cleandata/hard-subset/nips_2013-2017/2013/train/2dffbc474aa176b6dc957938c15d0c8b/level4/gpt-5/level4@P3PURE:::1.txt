This paper presents a Bayesian approach to joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is carried out using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with the recently introduced ancestor sampling mechanism. The paper also addresses computational efficiency through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates.

Overall, this is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear, accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, enabled by ancestor sampling, is novel. The careful treatment of computational aspects—sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates of covariance matrices—further strengthens the contribution. The empirical results, while brief, are sufficient (with suggestions for clarification below).

Beyond its stated aims, the work is likely to stimulate discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and on the trade-offs of learning transition dynamics from data versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

While space is limited, the authors could further discuss the differences between using a parametric transition model fixed a priori and using a similar model as the GP mean function. This is particularly relevant for out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting).

The results in Table 1, and the preceding description, are somewhat unclear. It is not evident whether the reported RMSE is computed on a withheld test set or on the same data used for conditioning (suggested by the “|data” in the column headings). My primary interest is the RMSE for out-of-sample predictions, especially forecasts forward in time against withheld data. This is where I would expect the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model to appear. If Table 1 does not already report this, an additional column with such results would be a valuable addition.

Minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before it is defined in Section 3.3.1; please define it at first use.

In sum, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate interesting discussion.
