This paper presents a Bayesian method for estimating states and parameters in nonlinear state-space models, simultaneously learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), incorporating the recent technique of ancestor sampling. The authors also address computational efficiency by discussing optimizations that leverage sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The core novelty lies in the online marginalization of the transition dynamics, which is cleverly achieved through the use of ancestor sampling. The paper is further strengthened by its practical consideration of computational issues, such as the use of sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates for the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices. The empirical results, while brief, are sufficient.

Beyond its direct contributions, this work is likely to stimulate valuable discussion regarding inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs between learning transition dynamics nonparametrically versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

While space is limited, the authors might consider adding a discussion on the differences between using a predefined parametric transition model versus using a similar model as the mean function for the GP, particularly in the context of out-of-sample prediction (i.e., forecasting).

The results presented in Table 1 and the accompanying description are slightly unclear. Specifically, it is not apparent whether the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) is calculated on a withheld test set or on the same data used for conditioning (as the `*|data` column headings might imply). An evaluation of out-of-sample performance, especially forecasting future values against withheld data, would be of great interest. This is the scenario where one would expect the largest discrepancies between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model to emerge. If Table 1 does not already report these out-of-sample results, adding a column with this information would be a valuable addition.

A minor point: the abbreviation 'CPF-AS' is used in Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in Section 3.3.1.

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate interesting discussion.
