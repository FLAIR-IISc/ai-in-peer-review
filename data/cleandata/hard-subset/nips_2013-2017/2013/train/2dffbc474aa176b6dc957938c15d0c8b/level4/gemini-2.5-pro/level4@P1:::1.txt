This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models. The core idea is to place a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the unknown state transition dynamics, allowing for a flexible, nonparametric model. To perform inference, the authors marginalize out the transition function and then sample the state trajectories and hyperparameters using a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically the Particle Gibbs sampler with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The paper also addresses computational complexity by incorporating sparse GPs and efficient low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and well-written paper with several key strengths:
*   **Originality:** The main contribution is a novel and elegant method for performing fully Bayesian inference in GP state-space models. Marginalizing the transition dynamics function is a powerful approach that preserves the full nonparametric nature of the model. The use of the recently developed PGAS sampler to tackle the resulting non-Markovian inference problem is both clever and effective.
*   **Quality:** The proposed method is technically solid. The paper is further strengthened by its practical consideration of computational efficiency, including the integration of sparse GPs and the use of low-rank updates for Cholesky factors.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and accessible. The complex inference machinery is explained well.
*   **Significance:** Beyond its direct contributions, this work is likely to stimulate further research into inference techniques for non-Markovian models and the trade-offs between learning dynamics nonparametrically versus specifying a parametric model a priori. The empirical results, while concise, effectively demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed method.

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it:

1.  **Experimental Evaluation:** The description of the experimental results in Section 6.1 and Table 1 could be clarified. Specifically, it is not entirely clear whether the RMSE values are computed on the training data or a held-out test set (the notation "*|data" is ambiguous). Evaluating the models on an out-of-sample forecasting task (i.e., predicting future states given past observations) would provide a more compelling demonstration of the learned dynamics' generalization capabilities. It is in such a scenario that the differences between the learned model and the ground truth often become most apparent. If the current table does not already include this, adding a column with forecasting results would significantly enhance the paper's experimental validation.

2.  **Model Comparison:** If space permits, the authors could expand on the conceptual and practical differences between using a fixed parametric transition model and using a similar parametric model as the mean function for the GP prior. A discussion of how these choices might affect performance, particularly in out-of-sample prediction tasks, would be valuable for practitioners.

3.  **Minor Presentation Issue:** The abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in Section 3.3.1.

In summary, this is a strong and novel contribution that elegantly solves a challenging inference problem. It is well-motivated, technically sound, and should be of significant interest to the NIPS community.
