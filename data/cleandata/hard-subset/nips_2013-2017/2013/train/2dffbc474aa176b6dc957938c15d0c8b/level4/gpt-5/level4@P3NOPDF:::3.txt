Summary
The paper applies particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to state-space models (SSMs) with a Gaussian process (GP) prior, aiming to infer both latent states and model parameters rather than focusing solely on filtering and smoothing. The contribution is primarily an application and adaptation of established PMCMC methodology to a GP-based SSM, with illustrative experiments demonstrating feasibility.

Strengths
- Clear and straightforward use of PMCMC in a nontrivial setting (SSM with GP prior), which is of interest to the community.
- The problem is relevant: joint state and parameter inference in flexible, nonparametric dynamical systems is valuable.
- The exposition of the overall approach appears sound, and the demonstrations suggest the method can be made to work.

Main weaknesses and missing analyses
- Insufficient evaluation of the practical challenges in getting PMCMC to work well. The paper does not adequately analyze:
  - How the number of particles affects variance of the likelihood estimator, particle degeneracy, and MCMC mixing/acceptance.
  - The sensitivity of results to proposal design, resampling strategy, and tuning parameters.
  - Effective sample size, autocorrelation, and convergence diagnostics.
- Limited assessment of computational cost and scalability:
  - No detailed report on runtime, memory usage, or scaling with sequence length, dimension, and particle count.
  - The computational implications of the GP prior are not discussed in depth (e.g., kernel choice, hyperparameter inference, potential use of sparse/inducing methods, and their effect on complexity and accuracy).
- Lack of baselines and comparative evaluation:
  - No comparisons to alternative methods such as EKF/UKF with EM, SMC^2, particle Gibbs vs. PMMH variants, classical MCMC/SMC for parametric SSMs, or variational approximations for GP-SSMs.
  - Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess whether PMCMC is competitive in accuracy, robustness, or cost.
- Demonstrations rather than systematic experiments:
  - The empirical section reads as a proof-of-concept; it does not quantify failure modes, robustness to misspecification, or the conditions under which PMCMC is preferable.
  - There is no ablation to disentangle the contributions of the GP prior versus the inference scheme.

Requests for clarification
- Model details: Specify the GP prior (kernel, mean function), whether hyperparameters are inferred within PMCMC or marginalized, and any approximations (e.g., inducing points). Discuss identifiability and priors for model parameters.
- Algorithmic choices: Describe the particle filter (resampling strategy, rejuvenation moves, ancestor sampling), proposal distributions for parameters, adaptation scheme (if any), and initialization.
- Diagnostics: Provide acceptance rates, autocorrelation or ESS for parameters, number of particles used per time step, and evidence that the pseudo-marginal likelihood variance is in a regime that enables good mixing.
- Sensitivity: Report performance versus particle count, sequence length, and noise levels. How does performance degrade when the model is slightly misspecified?
- Reproducibility: Clarify all tuning choices and, if possible, provide code or pseudo-code sufficient to reproduce results.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add a systematic empirical study:
  - Synthetic data with known ground truth to quantify parameter and state estimation accuracy (e.g., RMSE) and uncertainty calibration.
  - Real-data experiments that test scalability and robustness.
  - Comparisons to reasonable baselines (e.g., EKF/UKF+EM, variational GP-SSM, SMC^2, and different PMCMC variants such as PMMH vs. particle Gibbs/ancestor sampling).
- Report computational metrics and scaling:
  - Wall-clock times, ESS per second, and memory usage.
  - Trade-offs between particle count, variance of the likelihood estimator, and mixing/acceptance.
  - If using GP approximations, assess accuracy–cost trade-offs (e.g., varying inducing point counts).
- Provide diagnostics and ablations:
  - Acceptance rates, trace plots, ESS, and R-hat where applicable.
  - Sensitivity to initialization and proposal tuning.
  - Ablate key design choices (resampling, rejuvenation, ancestor sampling) to show their practical impact.
- Discuss failure modes and practical guidance:
  - When does PMCMC struggle (e.g., long sequences, strong nonlinearity, near-deterministic dynamics)?
  - Concrete recommendations for choosing particle numbers, proposal scales, and GP hyperparameter treatments.
- Situate the work within related literature:
  - Clearly position the contribution relative to established PMCMC variants and alternative approaches for joint state/parameter inference in SSMs and GP-SSMs.
  - Emphasize what is learned from this case study that would generalize to other models.

Overall assessment
The paper presents a neat application of PMCMC to a GP-based SSM and demonstrates feasibility. However, the contribution is largely methodological reuse without the level of critical evaluation, comparative analysis, and diagnostic depth needed to be confident about the method’s practicality and advantages. A thorough empirical and diagnostic study—focusing on tuning, mixing behavior, computational scaling, and comparisons to credible baselines—would substantially strengthen the paper. In its current form, it is more a demonstration than a comprehensive assessment; with the suggested additions, it could provide real value as a careful, practice-oriented study of PMCMC for GP-SSMs.
