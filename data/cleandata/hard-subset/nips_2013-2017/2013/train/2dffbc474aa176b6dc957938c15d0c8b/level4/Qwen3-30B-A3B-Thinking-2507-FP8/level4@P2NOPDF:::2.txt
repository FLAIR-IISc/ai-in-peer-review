

The authors present a clear and well-written application of particle MCMC to inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), with a specific focus on the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm (Lindsten et al.). The work is original and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature, particularly through the introduction of the sparse GP-SSM methodology, which offers practical computational benefits.  

However, the paper suffers from two significant omissions that limit its impact. First, it lacks comparative analysis against established particle MCMC variants, notably particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (Whiteley et al.). These methods could be readily implemented and would provide crucial context for evaluating the proposed approach. For instance, as noted in Whiteley et al., PMMH typically requires a number of particles scaling quadratically with the time horizon $T$, whereas particle Gibbs variants often achieve sublinear scaling. Without such comparisons, the paper fails to establish whether the proposed method offers tangible advantages over existing techniques.  

Second, the empirical results lack sufficient detail to assess algorithmic performance. The authors report numerical outcomes but omit key diagnostics such as autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample sizes (ESS) as functions of the number of particles $N$ and time steps $T$. This omission makes it unclear how $N$ should scale with $T$—e.g., linearly, sublinearly, or otherwise—to maintain efficiency. Visualizations of ACF or ESS versus $N$ and $T$ would clarify these scaling relationships and strengthen the empirical claims.  

The authors’ assertion that PMMH would require quadratic particle growth with $T$ while particle Gibbs variants scale sublinearly is plausible but remains unsubstantiated in the current results. Including even a brief comparison of computational costs or convergence rates between these schemes would address this gap and provide readers with actionable insights.  

In summary, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the application of particle MCMC in GP-SSMs, but its technical significance would be substantially enhanced by: (1) benchmarking against PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, and (2) providing performance diagnostics (e.g., ACF, ESS) that elucidate scaling behavior with $N$ and $T$. These additions would transform an already solid contribution into a definitive reference for the field. As currently written, the work is promising but incomplete.
