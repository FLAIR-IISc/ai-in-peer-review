- Proposes a fully Bayesian method for nonlinear nonparametric SSMs by placing a GP prior on transitions and marginalizing the function f.  
- Targets the joint smoothing distribution over latent states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS).  
- Collapsing f removes strong x–f dependencies and yields a non-Markovian prior over trajectories.  
- Derives the non-Markovian trajectory prior and adapts SMC/PMCMC to operate under it; hyperparameters sampled with slice sampling.  
- Addresses computational costs via sparse GPs (FIC) reducing complexity to O(M^2T).  
- Uses rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates to reuse computations and maintain O(T^3) cost for the full GP case.  
- Contrasts with EM/MAP parametric GP-SSMs by performing full Bayesian inference and marginalizing f rather than parameterizing it.  
- Complements GP filtering/smoothing work by providing a method to learn the GP transition model itself.  
- Builds on Particle MCMC and the PGAS kernel, adapting them to the non-Markovian prior—novel in this context.  
- Strength: technically solid with clear derivations for collapsing f and constructing the trajectory prior.  
- Strength: novel and well-motivated use of PGAS to sample full latent trajectories under a non-Markovian prior.  
- Strength: careful attention to practical computation (sparse GP, complexity analysis, Cholesky updates).  
- Strength: empirical demonstrations on challenging nonlinear benchmarks and cart-pole show learning ability and uncertainty quantification.  
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is brief and unclear whether reported RMSE is in-sample or out-of-sample.  
- Suggestion: add explicit out-of-sample/forecast evaluation (withheld future data) to better assess predictive performance.  
- Suggestion: discuss trade-offs between using a parametric transition model vs using a parametric form as the GP mean, especially for out-of-sample forecasting when the mean is misspecified.  
- Minor clarity issue: the acronym CPF-AS appears before it is defined.  
- Assessment: high originality and significance; methodology is sound and likely to stimulate follow-on work.  
- Pros for acceptance: fully Bayesian approach, strong technical and implementation details, clear exposition, convincing qualitative results.  
- Cons against acceptance: limited experimental breadth, unclear RMSE reporting, and a minor notation/clarity issue.  
- Overall recommendation: strong, novel, technically sound paper recommended for acceptance with clarifications (notably explicit out-of-sample evaluation).
