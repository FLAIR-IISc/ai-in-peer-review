

The paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics and marginalizing out the transition function using tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) samplers. This extends prior work on GP-SSMs (e.g., Turner et al. [2], Deisenroth et al. [5, 6]) by enabling nonparametric learning of the dynamics without resorting to parametric approximations, while preserving the full expressivity of the GP model. The authors develop a Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to efficiently sample from the joint smoothing distribution, leveraging marginalization to avoid the Markovian constraints of conventional SSM inference. A sparse GP variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior further reduces computational complexity to $O(M^2T)$, where $M \ll T$.

**Strengths:**  
- **Quality:** The marginalization technique is technically sound, with rigorous derivation of the non-Gaussian state prior (Equations 3–4) and clear demonstration of how PGAS preserves the smoothing distribution invariance (Section 3.3.1). The sparse GP extension (Section 4) is well-motivated and effectively addresses scalability.  
- **Originality:** The paper significantly advances beyond prior GP-SSM work [2, 5, 6, 8] by solving the critical problem of *learning* the GP dynamics—previously assumed known—using a fully Bayesian framework that avoids parametric constraints. The non-Markovian inference via PGAS is a novel application of recent PMCMC developments [4].  
- **Significance:** The approach enables robust smoothing in multimodal systems (e.g., Section 6.1) and captures state-space uncertainty (e.g., Figure 3), addressing a key limitation in conventional SSMs where dynamics must be fixed *a priori*. The sparse implementation also broadens applicability to larger systems.  

**Weaknesses:**  
- **Quality:** The experimental evaluation lacks critical analysis of PMCMC’s practical challenges. While the paper demonstrates *that* the method works (Section 6), it omits detailed assessment of *why* it works well (e.g., convergence rates, sensitivity to $N$ particles, or failure modes). The computational complexity claims ($O(T^3)$, $O(M^2T)$) are supported theoretically but not validated empirically against baseline PMCMC implementations.  
- **Significance:** The primary contribution—applying PMCMC to GP-SSMs—is not sufficiently distinguished from prior PMCMC work [3, 4]. The paper would be more impactful if it explicitly evaluated how the marginalization technique *enables* PMCMC to succeed in this context, rather than treating it as a given. The experiments (Section 6) focus on predictive accuracy but neglect to quantify sampling efficiency or robustness to hyperparameter choices.  

**Pro/Con Summary:**  
- *Pro:* Novel marginalization strategy that preserves nonparametric expressivity; sparse GP extension for scalability; clear theoretical derivation of non-Gaussian state prior; successful application to multimodal and high-dimensional systems.  
- *Con:* Insufficient evaluation of PMCMC’s practical implementation challenges (e.g., mixing rates, particle degeneracy mitigation); experiments lack depth in analyzing *why* the method works well; misses opportunity to benchmark against alternative PMCMC schemes for SSMs.  

**Recommendation:**  
The paper makes a technically sound contribution to Bayesian SSMs but falls short in demonstrating the *practical viability* of its core innovation (PMCMC for GP-SSM learning). To strengthen significance, the authors should add a subsection analyzing PMCMC’s convergence behavior and computational trade-offs, comparing it to standard particle filters or alternative samplers. As-is, the work is an incremental application of existing PMCMC methodology rather than a substantial advancement in inference techniques. **Reject** due to insufficient novelty in the core methodological contribution relative to the experimental evidence provided.
