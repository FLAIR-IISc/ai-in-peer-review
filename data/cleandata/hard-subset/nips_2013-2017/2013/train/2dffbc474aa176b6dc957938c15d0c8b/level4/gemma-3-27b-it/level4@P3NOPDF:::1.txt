This paper introduces a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the underlying transition dynamics. The inference framework utilizes particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) enhanced with ancestor sampling, a recently developed technique. Importantly, the authors also address computational efficiency through considerations of sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates, making the method more practical.

This is a technically strong and clearly presented paper. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, coupled with the effective use of ancestor sampling to achieve this, represents a genuine novelty. The attention to computational details, such as sparsity exploitation and low-rank updates/downdates of covariance matrix Cholesky factors, further strengthens the contribution. While the empirical evaluation is concise, it is sufficient to demonstrate the approach’s potential (suggestions for improvement are detailed below).

Beyond its immediate objectives, this work is likely to stimulate discussion regarding inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models. It raises pertinent questions about the benefits and drawbacks of learning transition dynamics in this manner, as opposed to specifying a parametric model *a priori*. This is a significant point for the field.

To further enrich the paper, the authors might consider a more detailed discussion of the distinctions between utilizing a parametric transition model defined *a priori* versus employing a similar model as the mean function within the GP framework. This is particularly relevant when considering out-of-sample prediction, such as forecasting. Exploring how the choice impacts predictive performance would be valuable. 

The results presented in Table 1, and the accompanying description, require clarification. It is currently unclear whether the reported Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) is calculated against a withheld test dataset, or is based on the same data used for conditioning (indicated by the *|data* column headings). My primary interest lies in evaluating performance on out-of-sample predictions – specifically, forecasting future states using withheld data. This scenario is where I would anticipate observing the most significant differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already present this, adding a column demonstrating out-of-sample RMSE would significantly enhance the evaluation.

One minor point concerns notation: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. 

Overall, this is a strong and novel contribution that promises to stimulate interesting discussion within the community. I recommend acceptance.
