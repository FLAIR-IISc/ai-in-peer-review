This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in state-space models (SSMs) where the state transition dynamics are modeled with a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The central idea is to marginalize out the infinite-dimensional transition function `f`, which induces a non-Markovian dependency structure on the latent state trajectory. The authors then apply a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample from the joint smoothing distribution of the states and hyperparameters. Once samples from the state trajectory are obtained, the posterior predictive distribution for the dynamics can be computed as a mixture of GPs. The work also extends this framework to a sparse GP formulation to improve computational scalability. This approach is positioned as a method for learning the GP model itself, in contrast to previous work such as Deisenroth et al. that has focused on inference assuming an already-learned model, and as an alternative to methods based on parametric approximations or MAP estimates, like those of Turner et al. or Wang et al.

**Strengths:**
*   The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of performing fully Bayesian learning in nonparametric state-space models. The proposed approach is elegant and theoretically sound.
*   By marginalizing the GP transition function, the method correctly maintains the nonparametric nature of the model throughout inference, which is a significant advantage over methods that rely on finite-dimensional approximations.
*   The application of a sophisticated sampler like PGAS to the resulting non-Markovian model is appropriate and well-motivated.
*   The inclusion of a sparse GP variant to reduce the computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T) is a valuable and practical contribution that addresses the scalability of the core method.
*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured, making the complex inference machinery understandable.

**Weaknesses:**
*   The primary contribution of the paper is the application of existing PMCMC methodology to the GP-SSM. While this combination is novel and useful, the originality of the core inference technique itself is limited.
*   The experimental section serves more as a demonstration of the method's capabilities rather than a thorough and critical evaluation. The key challenge in such sampling schemes is their practical performance. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the PMCMC sampler's behavior, including its mixing properties (e.g., trace plots, effective sample size), sensitivity to the number of particles (N), and overall computational cost in practice.
*   The comparison to other methods in the experiments is limited. A more comprehensive empirical comparison against other state-of-the-art techniques for learning GP-SSMs would be necessary to clearly establish the practical benefits and trade-offs of the proposed approach.
*   A more detailed discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach would be beneficial. For example, the paper misses an opportunity to analyze the practical difficulties in successfully implementing such a scheme and to provide guidance on how to address them.

In summary, this paper presents a solid and well-executed application of advanced MCMC methods to an important class of models. The main weakness is the lack of a deep empirical evaluation that would assess the practical performance of the sampler and provide a compelling comparison to alternative methods.
