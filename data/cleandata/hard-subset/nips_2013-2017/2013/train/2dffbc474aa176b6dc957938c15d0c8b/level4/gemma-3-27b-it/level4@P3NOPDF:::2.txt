## Review of NIPS 2013 Submission – Particle MCMC for Gaussian Process State-Space Models

This paper presents an application of particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods to inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). Specifically, the authors build upon the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm introduced by Lindsten et al., and develop useful model-specific methodology, notably a sparse GP-SSM implementation. The paper is clearly written and represents an interesting and original contribution.

The core strength of this work lies in its practical application of particle MCMC to a complex and relevant model class. GP-SSMs are powerful tools for time series analysis and non-linear state estimation, and efficient inference is critical for their widespread use. The proposed approach, leveraging ancestral sampling within a particle Gibbs framework, offers a promising avenue for addressing this challenge. The development of the sparse GP-SSM is also a valuable addition, potentially improving computational scalability.

However, a significant weakness of the paper is the lack of comparative analysis with other established particle MCMC techniques. In particular, a comparison to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as detailed in Whiteley et al.) would substantially strengthen the evaluation. Implementing these alternatives, while requiring additional effort, appears tractable given the current framework and would provide crucial insights into the relative performance of the proposed method. Understanding these trade-offs – especially in terms of computational cost and accuracy – is essential for guiding practitioners.

Furthermore, the presentation of the results lacks sufficient detail to fully assess the scalability and efficiency of the algorithm. The current figures, while demonstrating the method’s functionality, are not particularly informative regarding its behavior as the problem size increases. I strongly recommend including graphs illustrating key performance metrics, such as autocorrelation function (ACF) and effective sample size (ESS), as a function of both the number of particles (N) and the time series length (T). This would allow for a more rigorous assessment of the algorithm’s computational complexity and scalability. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand the relationship between N and T – does N need to scale linearly with T, or is a sublinear scaling achievable? 

Based on prior work (e.g., Whiteley et al.), one might anticipate that PMMH would require a number of particles scaling quadratically with T, whereas both particle Gibbs variants, including the proposed method, should exhibit a sublinear relationship. Demonstrating this empirically would significantly bolster the paper’s contribution.

In summary, this is a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. The paper’s quality would be significantly enhanced by a thorough comparison to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, coupled with a more detailed analysis of performance scaling with respect to N and T. Addressing these points would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed algorithm and greatly improve its practical relevance for the broader research community.
