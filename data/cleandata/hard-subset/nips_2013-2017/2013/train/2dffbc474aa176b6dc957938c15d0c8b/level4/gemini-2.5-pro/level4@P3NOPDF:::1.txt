This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models. A key contribution is the non-parametric learning of the transition dynamics, which are modeled using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference procedure is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), ingeniously integrating ancestor sampling to facilitate online marginalization of the GP. The authors also address practical computational aspects, proposing efficiency gains through sparsity and low-rank updates to Cholesky factors.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The primary novelty lies in the elegant online marginalization of the transition dynamics within the PMCMC framework, made possible by the recent development of ancestor sampling. This approach provides a powerful and flexible method for learning unknown dynamics directly from data. The paper is further strengthened by its consideration of practical computational challenges; the discussion of exploiting sparsity and employing low-rank updates and downdates for Cholesky factors demonstrates a thoroughness that extends from theory to implementation.

In addition to its direct contributions, this work is likely to stimulate valuable discussion on inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the relative merits of learning transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

**Suggestions for Improvement**

While space is limited, the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion on the trade-offs between using a pre-specified parametric transition model versus employing a similar model as the mean function for the Gaussian process. This comparison is particularly relevant in the context of out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting). A discussion of how each approach might behave when extrapolating beyond the training data would provide valuable insights for practitioners.

The presentation of the empirical results could be clarified. Specifically, regarding Table 1 and the accompanying description, it is unclear whether the reported Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) is calculated on the training data (i.e., the data conditioned upon, as suggested by '*|data' in the column headings) or on a withheld test set. The latter is of particular interest, especially for multi-step ahead forecasting, as this is a scenario where the differences between the learned non-parametric dynamics and a potentially misspecified parametric model would likely be most pronounced. If the current results are for in-sample fit, the inclusion of an additional column showing out-of-sample forecasting performance against withheld data would significantly strengthen the empirical validation of the proposed method.

**Minor Point**

The abbreviation 'CPF-AS' is used in the header of Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a strong and novel contribution to the literature on inference in state-space models. It presents an elegant solution to a challenging problem and is likely to stimulate interesting and important discussions within the community. The paper is well-written, technically robust, and I recommend its acceptance.
