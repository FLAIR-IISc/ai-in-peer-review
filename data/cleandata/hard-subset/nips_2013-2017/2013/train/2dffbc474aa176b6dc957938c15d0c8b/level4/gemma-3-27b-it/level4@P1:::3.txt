This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models (SSMs), leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics. The authors address a challenging problem by marginalizing over the transition dynamics function and using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to efficiently sample from the smoothing distribution. This allows for a flexible model capable of capturing complex dynamical phenomena and avoids the limitations of parametric SSMs.

The work builds on a rich literature of GP-based SSMs, notably referencing Deisenroth et al. [5, 6] and Turner et al. [2]. The key distinction of this work is its focus on *learning* the GP dynamics, rather than assuming a fixed GP model as in previous approaches. The authors correctly identify their method as providing a crucial component for the inference methods presented in [5, 6]. They also appropriately situate their work relative to EM-based approaches [2, 7] and MAP estimation methods [8], highlighting the advantages of their fully Bayesian approach. The connection to dimensionality reduction techniques [8] is also well noted, alongside the potential for overfitting in high-dimensional state spaces.

The strengths of this paper lie in its technically sound approach to a difficult problem and the clear exposition of the method. The derivation of the GP-SSM is well-presented, and the explanation of the PMCMC sampling procedure is thorough, referencing relevant prior work [3, 4]. The inclusion of a sparse GP extension (using the FIC prior [17]) demonstrates a practical consideration for computational efficiency. The experiments, while relatively simple, successfully illustrate the approach's ability to learn nonlinear dynamics and provide uncertainty estimates. The use of a parametric model as a baseline within the GP prior is a clever way to demonstrate the flexibility of the proposed framework.

However, the paper suffers from a lack of detailed discussion regarding the practical challenges of implementing and tuning the PMCMC algorithm. While the authors mention good mixing enabled by recent PMCMC samplers [4], there is insufficient detail about the parameter settings, convergence diagnostics, or sensitivity to initial conditions. A more critical evaluation of the PMCMC performance – including mixing rates, autocorrelation analysis, and computational cost – would significantly strengthen the paper. The experimental section focuses more on demonstration than rigorous comparison; more comprehensive benchmarks, perhaps against existing SSM methods, would be beneficial. While assessing difficulties in getting PMCMC to work is indeed challenging, discussion of these challenges and how they were overcome would be valuable.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and novel application of PMCMC to a challenging problem in SSMs.
*   Clear and well-written exposition of the method.
*   Demonstrates the flexibility of the GP-SSM approach.
*   Addresses a gap in the existing literature by focusing on learning the GP dynamics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient detail regarding the practical challenges and performance of the PMCMC algorithm.
*   Limited scope of the experimental evaluation.
*   The paper feels more like a methodological contribution than a substantial empirical advance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian SSMs. While the lack of a more in-depth analysis of the PMCMC implementation and a more extensive experimental evaluation are weaknesses, the technical soundness and novelty of the approach are significant. The paper is a valuable addition to the literature, particularly for researchers interested in applying Bayesian nonparametrics to dynamical systems. I recommend acceptance, but with the caveat that the authors should consider addressing the concerns raised regarding the PMCMC details and experimental evaluation in future work.
