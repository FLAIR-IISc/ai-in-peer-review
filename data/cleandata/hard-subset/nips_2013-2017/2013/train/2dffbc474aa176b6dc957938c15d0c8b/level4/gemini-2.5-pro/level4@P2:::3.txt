### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and system identification in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs). The authors place a Gaussian Process (GP) prior over the unknown state transition function, creating a flexible, nonparametric model (GP-SSM). The core technical contribution is a novel inference strategy that avoids explicit parametrization of the GP. Instead, the transition function is analytically marginalized out, which, while principled, breaks the Markovian structure of the state sequence and introduces long-range temporal dependencies. To perform inference in this resulting non-Markovian latent variable model, the authors employ a sophisticated Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This allows for jointly sampling the full state trajectory and the model hyper-parameters. The proposed framework is extended to a sparse GP formulation to reduce the computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T), where M is the number of inducing points. The method is demonstrated on a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole system.

This work builds upon a rich literature of GP-SSMs. However, it distinguishes itself from previous approaches that often rely on EM algorithms for maximum likelihood estimation (e.g., Turner et al., 2011) or MAP estimation (Wang et al., 2008), which do not provide a full posterior distribution and can be prone to overfitting. The proposed method provides a fully Bayesian treatment, which is a significant advantage. The use of PMCMC is a modern technique for inference in complex state-space models, and its application here to a GP-SSM with a marginalized transition function is a novel combination.

**Strengths and Weaknesses**

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound. The derivation of the marginal state prior after integrating out the GP function is correct, and the application of the PGAS algorithm to this structure is well-founded. The idea of marginalizing the transition dynamics is elegant and preserves the nonparametric nature of the model, which is a key strength. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant shows a good understanding of the practical computational limitations of the full model.

However, the empirical evaluation of the method is a significant weakness. The experiments serve as a proof-of-concept demonstration rather than a rigorous evaluation of the algorithm's performance and limitations. For an MCMC-based method, a critical aspect is the efficiency of the sampler. The paper lacks any analysis of the sampler's mixing properties, such as effective sample size, autocorrelation plots, or acceptance rates for the hyper-parameters. The claim that "a moderate N is enough to obtain a rapidly mixing kernel" is asserted but not supported with evidence. Without this analysis, it is difficult to assess the practical utility of the proposed method, especially as PMCMC methods can be notoriously difficult to tune and may suffer from poor mixing in challenging scenarios.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and organized. The problem is clearly stated, and the motivation for marginalizing the dynamics is well-explained. The description of the PGAS algorithm and its application to the non-Markovian model is coherent. The high-level implementation details in Section 4.2 provide some insight, but more detail would be needed for full reproducibility, particularly regarding the efficient Cholesky updates for the ancestor sampling weights.

**Originality:** The primary originality lies in the combination of existing techniques to solve an important problem. While GP-SSMs and PMCMC are not new in themselves, the specific strategy of marginalizing the GP dynamics and then tackling the resulting non-Markovian inference problem with PGAS appears to be novel. This is a clever and non-trivial application of advanced sampling methodology.

**Significance:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of performing fully Bayesian learning in nonparametric dynamical systems. A robust and efficient method for this task would be highly significant for many fields. The proposed approach is principled and offers a complete solution for both state estimation (smoothing) and system identification.

However, the significance of the contribution is tempered by the lack of a thorough empirical evaluation. The experiments do not compare the method against other state-of-the-art techniques for learning GP-SSMs. A comparison against the EM or MAP-based methods mentioned in the introduction would have been valuable to demonstrate in what practical ways the fully Bayesian approach is superior. The current evaluation only compares against simple parametric models, which does not adequately establish the advance in the state of the art. The paper's main contribution seems to be in demonstrating that a PMCMC scheme *can* be applied to this model, but it falls short of exploring the practical difficulties, performance trade-offs, and scalability of doing so. A deeper investigation into these practical aspects would have constituted a more substantial contribution.

**Arguments for Acceptance:**
*   **Principled Approach:** The paper proposes an elegant, fully Bayesian framework for learning in GP-SSMs that avoids parameterizing the GP function, thereby retaining its full nonparametric flexibility.
*   **Novel Combination:** The application of a modern inference technique (PGAS) to the specific non-Markovian model resulting from marginalizing the GP dynamics is a novel and clever contribution.
*   **Practical Consideration:** The authors correctly identify the computational bottleneck and propose a sparse GP variant to make the approach more scalable.

**Arguments for Rejection:**
*   **Insufficient Evaluation:** The experimental section is a major weakness. It acts as a demonstration rather than a rigorous evaluation. There is no analysis of the PMCMC sampler's performance (e.g., mixing, convergence), which is crucial for assessing the method's practical viability.
*   **Lack of Competitive Benchmarking:** The method is not compared against other relevant GP-SSM learning algorithms, making it difficult to gauge its practical advantages or its position relative to the state of the art.
*   **Limited Insight into Practical Challenges:** The paper does not provide sufficient detail on the practical challenges of implementing and tuning the PGAS sampler for this complex model. A more thorough discussion of these aspects would have been a valuable contribution in itself.

In conclusion, while the paper presents a methodologically sound and interesting idea, its contribution is undermined by a weak experimental evaluation. A stronger paper would have provided a more detailed and critical assessment of the proposed algorithm's strengths and weaknesses in practice, including a thorough analysis of the sampler's efficiency and a comparison with competing methods.
