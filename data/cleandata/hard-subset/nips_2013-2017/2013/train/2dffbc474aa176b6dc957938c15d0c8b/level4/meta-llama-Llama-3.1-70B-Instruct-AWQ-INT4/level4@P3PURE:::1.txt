This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the transition dynamics. The proposed inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) and incorporates the recently introduced concept of ancestor sampling. Additionally, the authors discuss computational efficiencies related to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper, with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics and the use of ancestor sampling to achieve this are novel contributions. The consideration of computational issues, such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices, further strengthens the paper. The empirical results, although brief, are sufficient; however, some suggestions for improvement are provided below.

Beyond its stated aims, this paper is likely to stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages/disadvantages of learning the transition dynamics in this way, rather than specifying a parametric model a priori.

While space is limited, the authors may wish to consider a brief discussion on the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori versus using a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For instance, this could involve comparing out-of-sample prediction performance, such as forecasting. Elucidating these differences would provide valuable insights, particularly in scenarios where the GP-based approach is expected to outperform or underperform the parametric model.

The results presented in Table 1, along with the preceding description, are somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is uncertain whether the root mean squared error (RMSE) values are calculated against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points conditioned upon (as indicated by the "*|data" notation in the column headings). The primary interest lies in evaluating the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, especially a forecast forward in time against withheld data. This scenario is expected to exhibit the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already present this information, an additional column providing this data would be a valuable addition.

One minor point is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that is likely to stimulate interesting discussions.
