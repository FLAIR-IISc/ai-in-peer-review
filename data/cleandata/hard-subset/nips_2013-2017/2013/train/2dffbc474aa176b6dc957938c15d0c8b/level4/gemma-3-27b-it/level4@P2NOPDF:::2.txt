This paper presents an application of particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods to inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), specifically building upon the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm introduced by Lindsten et al. The work is clearly written and represents an interesting and original contribution, particularly in its development of model-specific methodology, notably the sparse GP-SSM approach.

However, a significant weakness lies in the lack of comparative analysis against other established particle MCMC schemes. Specifically, the authors do not compare their method to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) or particle Gibbs with backward sampling (e.g., as detailed in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these alternatives would likely be straightforward and would provide valuable insight into the relative performance of the proposed algorithm. Such a comparison would be particularly informative regarding computational cost and efficiency.

Furthermore, the presented results lack sufficient detail to fully assess the algorithm’s behavior. The authors should include visualizations – such as graphs of autocorrelation function (ACF) or Effective Sample Size (ESS) – plotted as a function of both *N* (particle number) and *T* (time series length). Currently, the results are not sufficiently informative to understand the scaling relationship between these parameters. Understanding whether *N* needs to scale linearly, sublinearly, or otherwise with *T* is crucial for practical application. 

Based on prior work, such as Whiteley et al., it is reasonable to expect PMMH to require a number of particles scaling quadratically with *T*, whereas particle Gibbs methods, including the one proposed, should exhibit sublinear scaling. Demonstrating this empirically would strengthen the contribution significantly. 

In summary, this is a well-written paper detailing a useful application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. However, its impact could be substantially increased by incorporating a thorough comparative analysis against PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, alongside more detailed performance visualizations demonstrating the scaling behavior with respect to *N* and *T*.
