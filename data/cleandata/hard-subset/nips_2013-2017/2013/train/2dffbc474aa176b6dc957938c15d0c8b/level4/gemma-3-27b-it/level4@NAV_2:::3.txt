This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process prior over the state transition dynamics and employing Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) for efficient inference. The core idea is to marginalize over the transition dynamics function to directly infer the joint smoothing distribution. This builds upon a rich literature in Gaussian process state-space models [1, 2, 5, 6, 7, 8], and importantly, addresses a key limitation of existing methods that often rely on finite approximations of the GP, thus constraining model flexibility [2]. The proposed approach offers a principled way to retain full nonparametric expressivity, potentially overcoming identifiability issues when modeling both dynamics and observations with flexible models.

The strengths of this work lie in its rigorous Bayesian treatment of the SSM, the effective use of PMCMC to handle the challenging inference problem, and the preservation of the GP’s nonparametric nature. Marginalizing out the transition dynamics is a significant contribution, simplifying learning and enabling closed-form posterior calculation given the state trajectory. The use of recent advances in PMCMC sampling [3, 4] is also commendable, suggesting attention to practical implementation. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant [16, 17] further enhances the scalability of the approach. The experimental results, while relatively simple, demonstrate the method’s ability to learn a complex dynamic system and capture multimodal smoothing distributions.

However, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of the practical challenges associated with implementing and tuning the PMCMC sampler. While good mixing is mentioned, a more in-depth evaluation of the sampler’s performance – including mixing diagnostics, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and computational cost – would significantly strengthen the contribution. The experiments primarily serve as demonstrations of feasibility rather than rigorous comparisons against competing methods. The comparison in Section 6.1, while interesting, could be more comprehensive. A more critical evaluation of the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach, particularly in relation to alternative inference techniques for GP-SSMs, would be valuable. Furthermore, the experimental section could benefit from a discussion of the computational demands of the proposed approach compared to more traditional methods.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Novel and principled Bayesian approach to GP-SSM inference.
*   Effective use of PMCMC to marginalize out the transition dynamics.
*   Preservation of the full nonparametric expressivity of the model.
*   Demonstrated feasibility on benchmark problems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Insufficient discussion of practical challenges in PMCMC implementation.
*   Limited scope and depth of experimental evaluation.
*   Lack of a comprehensive comparison to existing methods.

Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of GP-SSMs. While the experimental evaluation could be more thorough, the theoretical and methodological advancements are significant. I believe this paper is a solid contribution that, with minor revisions to address the concerns outlined above, would be suitable for presentation at NIPS.
