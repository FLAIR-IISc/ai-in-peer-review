- Summary: Proposes fully Bayesian GP-SSM by marginalizing the GP transition f, yielding a non-Markovian prior over x0:T and using PGAS for exact smoothing and hyperparameter inference; includes a sparse FIC variant and implementation details.
- Relation to prior work: Builds on PMCMC/PGAS to handle non-Markovian models and contrasts with EM/MAP GP-SSM approaches by retaining nonparametric uncertainty.
- Strength: Conceptually clean—collapsing f reduces state–function coupling and preserves nonparametric modeling.
- Strength: Methodologically correct—PGAS targets the exact smoothing distribution and ancestor sampling addresses path degeneracy.
- Strength: Flexible predictive distribution—posterior over f is a mixture of Gaussians that can capture multimodality.
- Strength: Practical implementation—detailed computational-cost analysis, Cholesky updates, and a principled sparse FIC prior for complexity reduction.
- Strength: Allows encoding prior knowledge via GP mean function, well motivated and illustrated.
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is limited and reads as demonstration rather than rigorous assessment.
- Weakness: Missing mixing diagnostics—no autocorrelations, effective sample sizes, or scaling analysis with N, T, and state dimension.
- Weakness: Sensitivity and robustness not studied—dependence on mean function, kernel/hyperpriors, and observation-model misspecification is unclear.
- Weakness: Runtime and scalability not reported—no empirical runtime/memory curves or comparisons to EM/MAP, GPDM, or other baselines.
- Weakness: Sparse GP lacking analysis—no study of inducing point count M, selection method, or discussion that FIC defines a different prior.
- Weakness: Predictive evaluation limited—should report log predictive density, calibration, and multi-step rollouts (especially for cart-and-pole).
- Quality: Methodology and derivations are sound, but empirical section insufficient to judge practical sampler performance.
- Clarity: Paper is dense but clear; would benefit from more intuition, pseudocode for conditional filter, and practical guidance on N, M, priors, and inducing-input choice.
- Originality: Natural application of existing PMCMC machinery—technically competent but not a fundamentally new inference method; sparse/implementation details are incremental.
- Significance: Potentially impactful for fully Bayesian GP-SSMs, but significance depends on stronger empirical demonstrations on challenging problems.
- Pros for acceptance: Solid Bayesian treatment, correct PGAS use for non-Markovian smoothing, and useful computational strategies.
- Cons for acceptance: Thin empirical evaluation, primarily an application of existing methods, and insufficient analysis of sparse GP component.
- Recommendation: Borderline reject—promising and clean approach but needs more critical and detailed empirical evaluation to meet conference standards.
