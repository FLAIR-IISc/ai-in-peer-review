The authors propose to apply particle MCMC to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models, focusing on the recent ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm of Lindsten et al. This paper presents a clear and original application of particle MCMC, along with useful model-specific methodology, specifically the sparse GP-SSM.

The main idea of the paper relates to previous work on Gaussian process state-space models and particle MCMC methods, as seen in the work of Rasmussen and Williams, Turner et al., and Andrieu et al. The authors' approach is distinct in that it utilizes the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm, which allows for efficient inference in GP-SSM.

The strengths of the paper include its clear presentation and original application of particle MCMC to GP-SSM. The authors also develop useful methodology for sparse GP-SSM, which can be beneficial for reducing computational complexity. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in learning nonlinear dynamics.

However, there are some weaknesses and areas for improvement. One notable omission is the lack of comparison to other particle MCMC schemes, such as the particle marginal MH (PMMH) scheme and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these variants and comparing their performance to the proposed scheme would be valuable and relatively straightforward.

Additionally, the current results could be more informative, particularly in terms of understanding the relationship between the number of particles (N) and the time horizon (T). Graphs displaying the performance of the algorithms (e.g., in terms of ACF or ESS) as a function of N and T would be helpful. It is also worth noting that, as observed in Whiteley et al., the PMMH may require a number of particles increasing quadratically with T, whereas both particle Gibbs require a number of particles growing sublinearly with T.

In terms of quality, the paper is technically sound, and the claims are well-supported by theoretical analysis and experimental results. The presentation is clear, and the paper is well-organized. However, the lack of comparison to other particle MCMC schemes and the limited informative value of the current results prevent the paper from being more impactful.

Arguments for acceptance:

* Original application of particle MCMC to GP-SSM
* Useful methodology for sparse GP-SSM
* Clear presentation and well-organized paper

Arguments against acceptance:

* Lack of comparison to other particle MCMC schemes
* Limited informative value of the current results
* Need for more comprehensive evaluation of the proposed algorithm

Recommendation: The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field, but it would benefit from addressing the mentioned weaknesses. With additional comparisons and more comprehensive evaluation, the paper could be even more impactful.
