

This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging Gaussian process priors to learn transition dynamics. The approach employs particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling for efficient online marginalization of the transition dynamics, supported by computational optimizations through sparsity exploitation and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates.  

The work is technically sound and well-executed, with a clear, accessible presentation that effectively conveys its contributions. The online marginalization of transition dynamics via ancestor sampling represents a novel technical advance, and the integration of computational efficiency strategies—particularly the use of sparsity and low-rank updates for Cholesky factorizations—significantly strengthens the method’s practical applicability. The empirical evaluation, while concise, adequately supports the core claims, though minor clarifications would enhance its impact.  

The paper is poised to stimulate meaningful discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs inherent in learning transition dynamics versus specifying parametric forms a priori. A valuable addition would be a more detailed comparison between using a parametric transition model (as a priori knowledge) versus employing a similar model as the GP mean function, particularly for out-of-sample forecasting. For instance, how does the GP-based approach perform when extrapolating beyond observed data, versus a fixed parametric model? This distinction is critical for real-world applications where predictive accuracy beyond training data is paramount.  

Regarding the empirical results, Table 1 requires clarification. The description preceding the table states that RMSE values are computed *|data*, but it is ambiguous whether this refers to in-sample fitting or out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting withheld future observations). The most informative metric for model comparison would be out-of-sample RMSE on a held-out test set, especially for time-series forecasting where discrepancies between learned dynamics and ground-truth models are expected to be most pronounced. If Table 1 currently reports in-sample errors, adding a column for out-of-sample RMSE (computed on withheld future data) would substantially strengthen the evaluation. As currently presented, the table does not clearly address this key scenario.  

A minor technical inconsistency exists: the abbreviation "CPF-AS" (for "Coupled Particle Filter with Ancestor Sampling") is used in Algorithm 1 before its definition in Section 3.3.1. The authors should define this acronym upon first use in Section 3.3.  

Overall, this is a strong, novel contribution that advances inference in nonlinear state-space models. The method’s technical rigor, computational insights, and potential to influence future work on non-Markovian dynamics are compelling. The suggested clarifications—particularly regarding out-of-sample evaluation and acronym consistency—are straightforward to address and would elevate the paper’s impact. The work is well-suited for publication in NIPS, offering both theoretical novelty and practical utility for practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
