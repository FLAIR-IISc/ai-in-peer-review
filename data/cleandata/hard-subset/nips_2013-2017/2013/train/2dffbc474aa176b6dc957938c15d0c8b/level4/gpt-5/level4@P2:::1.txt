This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics. The key idea is to analytically marginalize the transition function and sample directly from the joint smoothing distribution over states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Given samples of the latent trajectory, the posterior over the transition function is then available in closed form, enabling a flexible, nonparametric characterization of dynamics. The paper also addresses computational issues, including sparse GP priors (FIC) and efficient Cholesky updates, to keep complexity manageable. Relative to prior work that learns parametric GP-like models via EM or MAP (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.) or assumes learned GP dynamics for subsequent filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al.), this work provides a coherent Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric flexibility while enabling efficient smoothing in a non-Markovian setting.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: Collapsing the transition function and using PGAS to sample the states in a non-Markovian latent model is elegant and, to my knowledge, new in this context.
- Technical soundness: The inference scheme is carefully derived; the use of ancestor sampling addresses path degeneracy and yields good mixing. The sparse GP variant and low-rank update/downdate strategies are well motivated.
- Clarity: The paper is generally well written and accessible, with helpful connections to the GP regression view once the latent trajectory is known.
- Practical relevance: The method leverages GP priors to encode prior knowledge via the mean function while retaining flexibility to model deviations. The cart-pole and nonlinear benchmark illustrate this well.

Weaknesses and suggestions
- Evaluation clarity: Table 1 and the surrounding text are ambiguous about whether RMSE is computed on held-out (out-of-sample) data or on the same data used for inference (the “|data” column headings are unclear). Out-of-sample forecasting performance—especially multi-step prediction on withheld trajectories—would be the most compelling test of learned dynamics versus parametric baselines and the ground-truth model. If not already reported, please add a clearly labeled column (and description) for out-of-sample forecasts.
- Mean function versus parametric model: It would be useful to expand the discussion comparing (i) fixing a parametric transition model a priori versus (ii) using a similar model only as the GP mean. In particular, how do these choices affect extrapolation/forecasting, uncertainty calibration, and identifiability?
- Scalability and implementation details: While complexity reductions are discussed, more guidance on practical settings would help (e.g., choice of particle count N, number of inducing points M, and their selection strategy; runtime vs. accuracy trade-offs; mixing diagnostics). A brief ablation comparing vanilla GP-SSM vs. FIC (accuracy and runtime) would be informative.
- Notation/definition: CPF-AS is referenced in Algorithm 1 before its definition in Section 3.3.1. Please define the acronym upon first use in the algorithm caption or move the definition earlier.
- Reproducibility: Clarify kernel choices, priors over hyperparameters, and any constraints on Q, and report sensitivity to these settings. If slice sampling is used for θx, a short note on step-size/initialization and acceptance behavior would help practitioners.

Quality, clarity, originality, significance
- Quality: The methodology is rigorous, with convincing arguments for correctness and practical efficiency. The analytic marginalization and PGAS coupling are well justified.
- Clarity: Exposition is clear overall, though the evaluation setup should be clarified as noted above.
- Originality: The combination of collapsing GP transition dynamics with PGAS for smoothing in a non-Markovian SSM is novel and addresses a recognized gap in GP-SSM learning.
- Significance: The approach should interest both GP and SSM communities, providing a principled alternative to MAP/EM-based learning and enabling richer uncertainty quantification. The sparse formulation further broadens applicability.

Pros for acceptance
- Elegant, fully Bayesian inference for GP-SSMs with nonparametric dynamics.
- Solid algorithmic contribution using PGAS to mitigate path degeneracy.
- Attention to computational efficiency, including sparse GPs and factorization reuse.
- Empirical results support the claims and illustrate practical utility.

Cons for acceptance
- Experimental evaluation could better emphasize out-of-sample forecasting and scalability.
- Minor clarity issues (Table 1 labeling; early use of CPF-AS acronym).
- Limited discussion of trade-offs between parametric mean functions and fully parametric models in practice.

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper that advances inference for GP-based state-space models. Addressing the evaluation clarity and adding an explicit out-of-sample forecasting comparison would further strengthen the submission.
