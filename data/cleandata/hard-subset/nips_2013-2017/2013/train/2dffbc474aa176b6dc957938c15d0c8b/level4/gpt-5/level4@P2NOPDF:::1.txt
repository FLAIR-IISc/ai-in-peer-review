This paper develops a Bayesian approach to joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models while learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed with particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), incorporating the recently introduced ancestor sampling mechanism. The paper also addresses computational efficiency through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

Overall, the work is technically sound and clearly presented. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics enabled by ancestor sampling appears novel and is a key strength. The careful treatment of computational issues—especially sparsity and efficient Cholesky updates/downdates—adds practical value. The empirical results, though concise, are adequate to support the main claims (with suggestions for extension below).

Beyond its stated goals, the paper is likely to stimulate discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and on the trade-offs between learning transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

Space permitting, it would be helpful to expand the discussion on the contrast between (i) using a fixed parametric transition model and (ii) embedding a similar parametric form as the GP mean function. In particular, please comment on implications for out-of-sample prediction, including forward-time forecasting, where differences between learned dynamics and a misspecified parametric model may be most pronounced.

I found the results in Table 1 and the preceding description somewhat unclear. It is not obvious whether the reported RMSE is computed on withheld test data or on the same data used for conditioning (as hinted by the “|data” notation in the column headers). My primary interest is in out-of-sample performance, especially multi-step forecasting on held-out data. This is where I would expect the largest performance gaps between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already reflect this, please add an additional column reporting RMSE for forward forecasts on withheld data.

Minor editorial point: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before it is defined (the definition arrives in Section 3.3.1). Please define CPF-AS at first use.

In summary, this is a strong and original paper with clear contributions in methodology and computation. With clarifications on the evaluation protocol and some additional discussion on forecasting and model specification, it will make a valuable and thought-provoking addition to the literature.
