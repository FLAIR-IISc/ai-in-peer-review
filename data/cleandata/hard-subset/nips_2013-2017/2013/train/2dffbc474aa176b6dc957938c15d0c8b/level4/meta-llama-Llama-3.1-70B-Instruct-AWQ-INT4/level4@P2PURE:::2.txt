The authors propose an interesting and original application of particle MCMC to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models, focusing on the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm of Lindsten et al. The paper is well-written and clear, and the development of model-specific methodology, such as sparse GP-SSM, is a notable contribution.

However, one significant omission is the lack of comparisons with other particle MCMC schemes, particularly the particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) scheme and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these variants would have been straightforward, and their comparison to the proposed scheme would have been highly informative. This addition would not have required substantial effort from the authors and would have significantly enhanced the paper's value.

Furthermore, I would like to see the inclusion of graphical representations illustrating the performance of the algorithms, such as plots of autocorrelation function (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as a function of the number of particles (N) and time (T). As presented, the results lack informative value, leaving questions about the scaling of N with T. For instance, is it necessary to scale N linearly or sublinearly with T?

In my opinion, for such models, the PMMH would likely require a number of particles increasing quadratically with T, as observed in Whiteley et al., whereas both particle Gibbs schemes would require a number of particles growing sublinearly with T. A more comprehensive comparison would have provided valuable insights into the relative performance of these algorithms.

Overall, the paper presents a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. Nevertheless, the addition of comparisons with the PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling would significantly improve the paper, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed algorithm's strengths and limitations.
