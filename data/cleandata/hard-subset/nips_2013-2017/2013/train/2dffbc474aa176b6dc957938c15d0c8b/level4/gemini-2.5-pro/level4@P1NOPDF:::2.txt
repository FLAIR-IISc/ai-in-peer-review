This paper proposes a particle MCMC approach for inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The authors specifically leverage the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs (AS-PG) algorithm recently introduced by Lindsten et al. The paper is clearly written and presents a novel and interesting application of particle MCMC. A notable contribution is the development of a sparse GP-SSM, a useful model-specific methodology.

However, a significant weakness of the paper is the lack of empirical comparison to other standard particle MCMC schemes. These include the particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) algorithm and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS), as proposed by Whiteley et al. These methods represent crucial baselines, and including such a comparison would have been straightforward and would provide essential context for evaluating the performance of the authors' chosen approach.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation would be substantially strengthened by a more systematic analysis of algorithmic performance. This should include plots of key metrics, such as the autocorrelation function (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS), as a function of the number of particles (N) and the length of the time series (T). In their current form, the results offer limited insight into the practical scaling of the algorithm. For instance, it is unclear whether the number of particles N must scale linearly or sublinearly with T to maintain a constant level of performance.

This analysis is particularly important because, for models of this type, PMMH is known to require a number of particles that increases quadratically with T, as shown by Whiteley et al. In contrast, both AS-PG and PG-BS are expected to require only a sublinear increase in the number of particles. Verifying this behavior would be a key result.

In summary, this is a well-written paper detailing an interesting application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. However, its contribution would be significantly stronger if the proposed algorithm were rigorously compared against PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, and supported by a more thorough analysis of its scaling properties.
