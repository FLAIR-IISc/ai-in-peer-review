This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs). The authors model the unknown state transition dynamics using a Gaussian process (GP) prior, creating a flexible, nonparametric GP-SSM. The key contribution is an inference method that retains the full nonparametric nature of the model by analytically marginalizing the GP transition function. The resulting non-Markovian posterior over the latent state trajectory is then targeted using a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) [4]. The paper also introduces a sparse GP-SSM variant based on the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to improve computational scalability. This work builds upon the rich literature of PMCMC methods [3] and GP-based dynamical systems [2, 5, 8] by providing a principled way to learn the GP model itself, rather than assuming it is known or resorting to parametric approximations.

**Strengths:**

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper's main strength lies in its novel and principled approach to fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs. The strategy of marginalizing out the unknown dynamics function is elegant and powerful, as it avoids approximations that would compromise the nonparametric flexibility of the GP. This represents a significant conceptual advance over previous methods that rely on point estimates or parametric representations of the dynamics.
*   **Technical Quality:** The methodology is technically sound and well-executed. The derivation of the marginal prior over state trajectories and its integration within the PGAS framework is clever and appropriate for the resulting non-Markovian model. The extension to a sparse GP formulation is a valuable and practical addition that directly addresses the computational limitations of the standard GP model, reducing complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T).
*   **Clarity:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The authors effectively motivate the problem, explain the challenges posed by marginalization, and present their proposed inference algorithm in a comprehensible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Insufficient Empirical Comparison:** The primary weakness is the lack of a comparative experimental evaluation. The authors propose using the PGAS sampler but do not benchmark its performance against other prominent and directly applicable PMCMC schemes, such as Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) [3] or Particle Gibbs with backward sampling. Such a comparison is essential to demonstrate the practical advantages of the chosen sampler over standard alternatives and would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Lack of Performance Analysis:** The experimental section demonstrates the feasibility of the approach but fails to characterize its computational performance and efficiency. A crucial missing piece is an analysis of the sampler's behavior (e.g., using metrics like Effective Sample Size or Autocorrelation Function) as a function of the number of particles `N` and the length of the time series `T`. It is conjectured in the literature that PG-based methods scale better with `T` than PMMH for such models, but empirical validation in this specific context is needed to substantiate this and provide practical guidance for practitioners on how to set `N`.

**Arguments for and against acceptance:**

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, elegant, and methodologically sound approach for a challenging and important problem in machine learning. The core idea of preserving the nonparametric nature of the model by marginalizing the GP dynamics is a significant contribution. The work is presented clearly and includes a valuable extension for computational efficiency.
*   **Con:** The experimental validation is not sufficiently thorough. Without a comparison to other standard PMCMC algorithms and an analysis of the sampler's performance scaling, it is difficult to assess the practical impact and efficiency of the proposed method relative to the state of the art. This makes the contribution feel incomplete from an empirical standpoint.
