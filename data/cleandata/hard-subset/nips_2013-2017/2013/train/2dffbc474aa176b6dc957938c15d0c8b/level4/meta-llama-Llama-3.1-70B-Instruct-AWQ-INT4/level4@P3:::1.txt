This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while also learning the transition dynamics through the use of a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with the recently-introduced idea of ancestor sampling. The paper also discusses computational efficiencies to be had with respect to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The main contribution of the paper is the development of a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSM). The authors place a GP prior over the state transition dynamics, resulting in a flexible model able to capture complex dynamical phenomena. To enable efficient inference, they marginalize over the transition dynamics function and instead infer directly the joint smoothing distribution using specially tailored PMCMC samplers. This approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the model and can make use of sparse Gaussian processes to greatly reduce computational complexity.

The paper is technically sound and well-presented. The use of ancestor sampling to achieve online marginalization of the transition dynamics is novel and efficient. The consideration of computational issues such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices strengthens the paper further. The empirical results, while brief, are sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

The paper will likely stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages/disadvantages of learning the transition dynamics in this way rather than specifying a parametric model a priori. The authors may like to consider some further discussion around the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori against the use of a similar model as the mean function of the GP, particularly in out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting).

The results in Table 1 and the description in the preceding paragraph are slightly unclear. It would be beneficial to clarify whether the RMSE is against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points that is conditioned upon. An extra column showing the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, especially a forecast forward in time against withheld data, would be a great addition.

One minor point is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. This is a minor issue and does not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

In terms of quality, the paper scores high on technical soundness, clarity, and originality. The approach is novel, and the empirical results demonstrate its effectiveness. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience. The use of ancestor sampling and sparse Gaussian processes is a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and learning in state-space models.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel and efficient approach to Bayesian inference and learning in GP-SSM.
* The use of ancestor sampling and sparse Gaussian processes is a significant contribution to the field.
* The empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a broad audience.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The results in Table 1 and the description in the preceding paragraph are slightly unclear.
* An extra column showing the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction would be beneficial.
* The abbreviation CPF-AS is used before being defined, which is a minor issue.

Overall, the paper is strong and novel, and its acceptance would be a valuable addition to the conference proceedings.
