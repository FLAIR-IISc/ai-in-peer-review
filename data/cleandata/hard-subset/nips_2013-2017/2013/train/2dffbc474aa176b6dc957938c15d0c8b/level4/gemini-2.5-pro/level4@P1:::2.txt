### Summary

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The core idea is to place a GP prior on the unknown state transition function and then, for inference, to marginalize out this function entirely. This maneuver preserves the model's full nonparametric expressivity but induces long-range temporal dependencies in the state sequence, breaking the Markov property. To tackle the resulting challenging inference problem, the authors propose using a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm, namely Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This allows for efficient sampling of the state trajectories from their joint smoothing distribution. The paper further extends this framework to a sparse GP-SSM using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity. The experiments demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex nonlinear dynamics and capture multimodality in the smoothing distribution.

### Strengths

*   **Originality and Significance:** The paper tackles the important and difficult problem of system identification in nonparametric state-space models. The proposed approach of marginalizing the GP dynamics function is elegant and principled, representing a significant departure from common methods that rely on parametric approximations or point estimates (e.g., MAP or EM). This fully Bayesian treatment of the function is a key strength.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written, clearly organized, and provides a good exposition of a complex topic. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the challenges introduced by marginalization and how the PGAS sampler is adapted to overcome them.
*   **Technical Contribution:** The application of PGAS to this non-Markovian model is novel and well-suited to the problem structure. The development of a sparse GP variant to improve scalability from O(T³) to O(M²T) is a valuable and practical contribution that makes the method applicable to longer time series.

### Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement

While the paper presents a solid and interesting contribution, the empirical evaluation could be significantly strengthened. My main concerns are:

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Alternative PMCMC Methods:** The choice of the PGAS sampler is well-motivated, but its performance is not benchmarked against other state-of-the-art PMCMC algorithms. It would be highly beneficial to compare the proposed method against strong baselines such as the Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) algorithm and the Particle Gibbs sampler with backward sampling. These alternatives could be implemented straightforwardly for this model. Such a comparison is crucial, as these algorithms can exhibit very different performance characteristics, particularly in how the required number of particles `N` scales with the time series length `T`. For instance, it has been observed in other contexts that PMMH may require `N` to scale quadratically with `T` to maintain a constant acceptance rate, whereas Particle Gibbs variants often exhibit more favorable, sublinear scaling. An empirical investigation of these trade-offs would provide invaluable insight and substantially strengthen the paper's claims about the efficiency of the proposed approach.

2.  **Insufficient Quantitative Analysis of Sampler Performance:** The current experimental results demonstrate the method's capabilities qualitatively but lack a rigorous quantitative assessment of the MCMC sampler's efficiency. To properly evaluate the algorithm, the authors should report standard MCMC diagnostics. For example, plots of the autocorrelation function (ACF) for key latent variables or hyperparameters, and estimates of the effective sample size (ESS), would provide a much clearer picture of the sampler's mixing speed. This would allow readers to gauge how many iterations are truly needed to obtain reliable posterior estimates.

3.  **Missing Scalability Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of how the algorithm's performance scales with the number of particles `N` and the length of the time series `T`. While the computational complexity is discussed, the statistical efficiency is not. How does the mixing (e.g., measured by ESS) degrade as `T` increases for a fixed `N`? Conversely, how must `N` grow with `T` to maintain a certain level of performance (i.e., does `N` need to scale linearly or sublinearly with `T`)? Providing such an analysis would offer crucial practical guidance for users of this method and would substantiate the claim that a "moderate N is enough to obtain a rapidly mixing kernel."

### Conclusion

This is a well-written paper that presents an elegant and original application of modern Monte Carlo methods to a challenging problem in nonparametric time series modeling. The core methodological contribution is sound and significant. However, the paper could be substantially improved by providing a more thorough empirical evaluation that includes comparisons to alternative PMCMC samplers and a quantitative analysis of the algorithm's mixing performance and scalability. Addressing these points would elevate the paper from a good application paper to a more complete and impactful contribution to the field.
