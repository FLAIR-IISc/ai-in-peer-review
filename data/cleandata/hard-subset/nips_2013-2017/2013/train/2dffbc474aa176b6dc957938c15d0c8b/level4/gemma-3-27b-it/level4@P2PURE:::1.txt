This paper introduces a Bayesian framework for state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling, a recently developed technique. The authors also address computational efficiency through the exploitation of sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically rigorous and well-written paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalisation of the transition dynamics, achieved through the application of ancestor sampling, represents a novel contribution. Further strengthening the work is the consideration of computational aspects, such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to covariance matrix Cholesky factors. The empirical results, though concise, are adequate (with suggestions for improvement detailed below).

Beyond its immediate objectives, this paper is likely to provoke discussion regarding inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models, and the trade-offs involved in learning transition dynamics in this manner versus specifying a parametric model a priori. 

While space is limited, the authors might benefit from expanding the discussion around the distinctions between employing a parametric transition model as a prior specification and utilizing a similar model as the mean function within the GP. This is particularly relevant for out-of-sample prediction tasks, such as forecasting. 

The results presented in Table 1 and the associated description are somewhat unclear. It is not immediately apparent whether the reported RMSE values are computed against a withheld dataset or the data already conditioned upon (denoted by *|data* in the column headings). I am particularly interested in seeing RMSE values derived from out-of-sample predictions, specifically forecasts against withheld future data. This scenario would likely highlight the differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model most effectively. If Table 1 does not already present this information, adding a column showing out-of-sample performance would be a valuable addition.

A minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before its definition in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that promises to stimulate valuable discussion.
