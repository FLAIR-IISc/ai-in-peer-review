Summary:
The paper proposes a Bayesian framework for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models while learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling, enabling online marginalization of the latent transition function. The authors also address computational efficiency through sparsity exploitation and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates. The presentation is clear and technically solid, and the empirical results, though brief, are generally convincing.

Strengths:
- Novelty: The combination of GP-based transition learning with PMCMC and ancestor sampling is original and well-motivated. The treatment of the resulting non-Markovian dependence induced by integrating out the GP is a notable contribution.
- Methodological clarity: The algorithmic components are explained clearly, and the role of ancestor sampling in enabling efficient inference is well articulated.
- Computational considerations: The discussion of sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates is thoughtful and likely important for practical scalability.
- Potential impact: The work should stimulate discussion on inference in non-Markovian state-space models and on the trade-offs between learning transition dynamics nonparametrically versus committing to a parametric model.

Weaknesses and questions:
- Evaluation clarity: The description of Table 1 and the accompanying text are unclear. It is not obvious whether the reported RMSEs are computed in-sample (on the same data used for inference) or out-of-sample (on held-out data).
- Predictive assessment: Given the motivation for learning transition dynamics, a stronger emphasis on out-of-sample forecasting—particularly forward-in-time predictions on withheld data—would better demonstrate the benefits over a misspecified parametric model.
- Modeling perspective: The paper briefly contrasts parametric transition models with GP-based learning, but a deeper discussion would help readers understand when one approach is preferable, including the option of using a parametric model as the GP mean function.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Clarify evaluation protocol:
  - Explicitly state whether Table 1 reports in-sample or out-of-sample RMSEs.
  - If not already included, add an evaluation column (or figure) reporting forward-forecast RMSE on a truly held-out time horizon. This is likely where learned dynamics will show the largest advantage over a misspecified parametric baseline.
  - Provide details on train/test splits, horizon lengths, and whether hyperparameters are tuned using training data only.
- Discuss parametric vs GP models:
  - Compare the implications of specifying a parametric transition model a priori versus using the same model as the GP mean function. Address bias-variance trade-offs, sample efficiency, and interpretability.
  - Comment on how the choice of GP kernel and mean function affects extrapolation and long-horizon forecasts.
- Quantify computational gains:
  - Provide complexity in terms of number of time steps, particles, and GP basis size (or inducing points), and report wall-clock times.
  - If feasible, include an ablation isolating the impact of sparsity and low-rank updates on runtime and memory.
- PMCMC diagnostics:
  - Report effective sample sizes, autocorrelation, and acceptance rates to support the claim of efficient mixing, particularly given the non-Markovian structure.
  - Briefly discuss sensitivity to the number of particles and initialization.
- Notation and exposition:
  - Define CPF-AS (conditional particle filter with ancestor sampling) before its first use in Algorithm 1.
  - Ensure all abbreviations are introduced on first occurrence and used consistently.

Minor comments:
- Specify whether the GP hyperparameters are learned jointly within the PMCMC or marginalized; clarify priors used.
- Indicate whether Cholesky downdates are numerically stable in all settings considered and any safeguards employed.
- If space permits, include a brief qualitative visualization of learned dynamics versus ground truth to complement numerical RMSEs.

Overall assessment:
This is a strong and technically sound paper that advances Bayesian inference for nonlinear state-space models by integrating GP-based transition learning with PMCMC and ancestor sampling. The methodological contributions and computational insights are significant and clearly presented. With clarifications to the evaluation protocol—especially emphasizing out-of-sample forecasting—and minor exposition fixes, the paper would be a compelling addition that should provoke useful discussion within the community. I recommend acceptance.
