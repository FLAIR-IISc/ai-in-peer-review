This paper proposes a Bayesian framework for state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the transition dynamics. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), incorporating the recently introduced concept of ancestor sampling. The authors also explore computational efficiencies related to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically sound and well-presented, making it accessible to a broad audience. The online marginalization of transition dynamics, achieved through ancestor sampling, is a novel contribution. Furthermore, the consideration of computational issues, such as sparsity and low-rank updates to Cholesky factors of covariance matrices, enhances the paper's strength. Although brief, the empirical results are sufficient, with potential for further improvement (see suggestions below).

Beyond its primary objectives, this paper is likely to spark discussions on inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the advantages and disadvantages of learning transition dynamics using this approach versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

One potential area for expansion is a more in-depth discussion on the differences between using a parametric transition model with a priori knowledge versus employing a similar model as the mean function of the GP. This comparison would be particularly relevant in out-of-sample prediction scenarios, such as forecasting.

The results presented in Table 1 and the preceding paragraph are somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is uncertain whether the root mean squared error (RMSE) is evaluated against a withheld dataset or the same dataset used for conditioning (as indicated by the "*|data" notation in the column headings). A more informative metric would be the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast forward in time using withheld data. This scenario would likely exhibit the most significant differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already present this information, adding an extra column to illustrate this would be a valuable contribution.

A minor point to note is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and innovative paper that is likely to stimulate interesting discussions.
