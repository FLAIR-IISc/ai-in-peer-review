This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, utilizing a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the transition dynamics. The inference mechanism employed is particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), incorporating the recently introduced concept of ancestor sampling. The authors also discuss computational efficiencies achieved through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically sound and strong, with a clear and accessible presentation. The novel aspect of online marginalization of the transition dynamics, achieved through ancestor sampling, is a notable contribution. Furthermore, the consideration of computational issues, such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices, adds strength to the paper. The empirical results, although brief, are sufficient, and some suggestions for further improvement are provided below.

In addition to its stated objectives, this paper is likely to stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages and disadvantages of learning the transition dynamics in this manner, rather than specifying a parametric model a priori.

The authors may wish to consider expanding on the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori and employing a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For instance, they could explore the implications of this choice for out-of-sample prediction, such as forecasting. A more in-depth discussion of these differences would provide valuable insights.

The results presented in Table 1, along with the preceding paragraph, are somewhat unclear. It is uncertain whether the root mean square error (RMSE) is calculated against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points conditioned upon (denoted by "*|data" in the column headings). The primary interest lies in the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast forward in time against withheld data. This scenario is expected to exhibit the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already display this information, an additional column providing this data would be a valuable addition.

A minor point to note is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that is likely to stimulate interesting discussions.
