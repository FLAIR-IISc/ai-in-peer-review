### Review

**Summary**

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models (SSMs). The core idea is to place a Gaussian Process (GP) prior over the unknown state transition function, creating a flexible, nonparametric model (GP-SSM). The main technical contribution is a novel inference strategy that marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional transition function *f*. This marginalization, while elegant, breaks the Markov property of the state sequence, introducing long-range temporal dependencies. To tackle this challenging inference problem, the authors employ a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) sampler, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm. This allows them to sample from the joint posterior distribution of the latent state trajectories and model hyperparameters. The paper also proposes a sparse GP variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce the computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T), making the method more scalable. The authors demonstrate the approach on a nonlinear synthetic benchmark and a classic cart-pole system.

This work builds upon a rich literature of GP-based dynamical systems. It distinguishes itself from prior work by providing a fully Bayesian treatment that avoids parametric approximations of the GP during inference. Unlike methods that rely on EM or MAP estimation for point estimates of model parameters (e.g., Turner et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2005), this work directly targets the posterior distribution over state trajectories and hyperparameters. It provides a principled way to learn the GP dynamics model, a necessary component for inference methods like those of Deisenroth et al. (2012) which assume a learned model.

**Strengths (Arguments for Acceptance)**

1.  **Originality and Conceptual Elegance:** The central idea of marginalizing the GP dynamics function is a significant and elegant contribution. It allows the model to retain its full nonparametric expressivity during the inference process, directly addressing the posterior over the state trajectories without resorting to finite-dimensional approximations of the function *f*. This is a fundamentally different and arguably more principled approach than many existing methods.

2.  **Novel Application of Advanced MCMC:** The application of the recently developed Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to this non-Markovian model is novel and well-motivated. The paper clearly explains why this specific sampler is well-suited for the problem, providing an effective tool to navigate the complex posterior landscape induced by the marginalization.

3.  **Practicality and Scalability:** The inclusion of a sparse GP-SSM based on the FIC prior is a valuable practical contribution. It directly addresses the cubic scaling of the full GP model, extending the applicability of the proposed framework to longer time series, which is crucial for many real-world problems.

4.  **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written and organized. It clearly presents the model, the inference challenges that arise from marginalizing the dynamics function, and the PMCMC-based solution. The derivations and algorithmic descriptions are easy to follow.

**Weaknesses (Arguments for Rejection & Suggestions for Improvement)**

1.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The primary weakness is the lack of empirical comparison to other relevant PMCMC algorithms. The choice of PGAS is well-justified theoretically, but its practical performance relative to alternatives is not demonstrated. Implementing and comparing against the standard Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling would have provided invaluable context. It is known that for some models, PMMH can suffer from severe degeneracy as the time series length T increases (requiring the number of particles N to scale quadratically with T), whereas PG-based methods may scale better. A quantitative comparison would substantially strengthen the paper's claims about efficiency.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of Sampler Performance:** The paper claims "very good mixing" but provides no quantitative evidence to support this. The experimental results would be much more informative and convincing if they included standard MCMC diagnostics. For instance, plots of the autocorrelation function (ACF) or metrics like the Effective Sample Size (ESS) for key latent variables and hyperparameters would be highly beneficial. Crucially, an analysis of how these metrics change as a function of the number of particles (N) and the time series length (T) is needed to understand the practical scalability and limitations of the proposed sampler. This would help answer the critical question for practitioners: how should N be chosen in relation to T to ensure efficient inference?

3.  **Limited Experimental Validation:** While the experiments demonstrate that the method works, they are somewhat limited. The first experiment is on a synthetic 1D system. The second, on the cart-pole system, is largely illustrative, showing qualitative one-step-ahead predictions without quantitative metrics or comparison to other system identification techniques. The paper would be stronger if it included results on a more challenging, real-world dataset or provided more rigorous quantitative analysis (e.g., test log-likelihood, long-term prediction errors) against competing methods for learning dynamical systems.

**Overall Assessment**

This is a high-quality paper presenting a novel and conceptually elegant method for fully Bayesian inference in GP state-space models. The core idea of marginalizing the dynamics function and tackling the resulting non-Markovian inference problem with PGAS is a significant contribution to the field. The paper is clear, technically sound, and the development of a sparse variant is a nice practical touch.

However, the paper's primary weakness lies in its empirical evaluation. The lack of comparison to other advanced PMCMC samplers and the absence of a quantitative analysis of the sampler's performance make it difficult to assess the true practical utility and efficiency of the proposed method. While the contributions are significant, the paper would be much stronger if these experimental shortcomings were addressed.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty and significance of the proposed methodology are high enough to warrant acceptance. The paper introduces an important new tool for the Bayesian machine learning toolbox. The suggested improvements would elevate it from a good paper to an excellent one.
