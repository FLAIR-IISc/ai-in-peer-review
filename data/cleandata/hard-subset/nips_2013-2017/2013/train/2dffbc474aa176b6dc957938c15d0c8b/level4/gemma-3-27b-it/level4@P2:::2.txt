This paper introduces a novel approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) using particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The authors leverage a tailored PMCMC algorithm, specifically ancestral sampling particle Gibbs, to efficiently sample the smoothing distribution while marginalizing over the state transition function. This allows for a fully nonparametric treatment of the dynamics, a key advantage over existing methods. The paper is well-written and presents an interesting and original application of PMCMC techniques to a challenging problem. A particularly valuable contribution is the development of a sparse GP-SSM construction to improve computational efficiency.

The work builds upon a strong foundation of existing research in state-space models and Gaussian processes. State-space models are widely used for time series analysis and dynamical systems, and the authors correctly position their work within this context. They effectively leverage Gaussian processes to provide flexibility in modeling nonlinear dynamics, addressing a limitation of traditional parametric SSMs. The paper acknowledges relevant prior work, including Deisenroth et al. [5, 6] on filtering and smoothing with GPs, Turner et al. [2] employing the EM algorithm for GP-based models, and Wang et al. [8] focusing on MAP estimation. 

The strengths of this paper lie in its technical soundness and the clarity of its presentation. The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of both GP-SSMs and PMCMC methods. The detailed explanations of the algorithms, including the efficient implementation techniques for both vanilla GPs and the sparse FIC prior, are commendable. The experimental results, while preliminary, showcase the ability of the proposed approach to learn complex dynamics and provide accurate predictions. 

However, the paper would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons to alternative particle MCMC schemes. Specifically, a comparison to particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.) would significantly strengthen the contribution. Implementing these schemes could provide valuable insights into their performance relative to the proposed ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm. It is unclear from the current presentation how the proposed method scales with increasing dimensionality and time series length. 

Furthermore, including graphs illustrating algorithm performance—such as autocorrelation functions (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as functions of the number of particles (N) and time steps (T)—would make the results more informative. This would help to assess the computational cost and convergence properties of the method, and provide guidance on parameter tuning. Given results in Whiteley et al. that PMMH scales quadratically with T whereas particle Gibbs scales sublinearly, it would be worthwhile to demonstrate this behavior empirically.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Technically sound and well-explained application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs.
*   Original contribution with the development of a sparse GP-SSM construction.
*   Clear and concise writing.
*   Demonstrated ability to learn complex dynamics in experimental settings.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparative analysis with other relevant particle MCMC schemes (PMMH, particle Gibbs with backward sampling).
*   Limited presentation of algorithm performance metrics (ACF, ESS) as a function of key parameters (N, T).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the concerns regarding comparative analysis and performance evaluation would significantly enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
