This paper presents a Bayesian approach to joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. Inference is performed using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) augmented with the recently introduced ancestor sampling. The paper also addresses computational efficiency through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates.

Overall, this is a technically strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of transition dynamics using ancestor sampling is novel and well-motivated. The careful treatment of computational aspects—especially sparsity and efficient Cholesky factor updates/downdates—further strengthens the contribution. The empirical results, though brief, are adequate; I offer suggestions below.

Beyond its stated aims, the work is likely to spur discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models and on the pros and cons of learning transition dynamics in this way versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

Given space constraints, the authors might still expand on the distinction between (i) using a parametric transition model fixed a priori and (ii) using a comparable model as the GP mean function. In particular, how do these choices differ in out-of-sample prediction, especially in forecasting scenarios?

The results in Table 1 and the preceding description are slightly unclear. It is not obvious whether the reported RMSE is computed on held-out data or on the same data used for conditioning (the “|data” in the column headings is ambiguous). My main interest is the RMSE on out-of-sample predictions, especially forward-in-time forecasts on withheld data. This is where I would expect the largest differences between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already report this, adding a dedicated column would be very helpful.

Minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before it is defined in Section 3.3.1. Please define it at first use or add a forward reference.

In sum, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate interesting discussion.
