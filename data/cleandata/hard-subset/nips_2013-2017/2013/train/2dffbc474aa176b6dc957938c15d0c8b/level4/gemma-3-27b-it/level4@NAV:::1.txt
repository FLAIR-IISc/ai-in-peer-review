This paper introduces a Bayesian methodology for estimating states and parameters within nonlinear state-space models, concurrently learning the transition dynamics using a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference procedure employs particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) incorporating the recent advancement of ancestor sampling. Furthermore, the authors address computational efficiency through techniques leveraging sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically rigorous and well-written, demonstrating clarity and accessibility. The online marginalisation of transition dynamics, combined with the application of ancestor sampling for this purpose, represents a notable contribution. The inclusion of computational considerations, specifically sparsity and low-rank/downdate operations on covariance matrix Cholesky factors, further enhances the work. The empirical results, although concise, provide adequate support for the proposed approach (with suggestions for expansion detailed below).

Beyond its immediate goals, this work is expected to encourage discussion regarding inference techniques for non-Markovian state-space models and the relative merits of learning transition dynamics versus defining them with a parametric model *a priori*.

A brief extension of the discussion to explicitly contrast utilising a parametric transition model specified *a priori* with employing a similar model as the GP’s mean function would be valuable, particularly concerning out-of-sample prediction tasks like forecasting. 

The presentation of results in Table 1, and its accompanying description, requires clarification. It is unclear whether the reported RMSE values are calculated against a held-out dataset or the data already used for conditioning (*|data* in the column headers). An RMSE computed on an out-of-sample prediction – ideally a forecast against withheld data – would be particularly insightful, as this scenario is where differences between learned dynamics and the true model are likely to be most pronounced. Adding a column to Table 1 reflecting this would be a valuable addition if it isn’t already presented.

A minor note: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 prior to its definition in Section 3.3.1. Overall, this is a strong and original paper with the potential to stimulate significant discussion within the field.
