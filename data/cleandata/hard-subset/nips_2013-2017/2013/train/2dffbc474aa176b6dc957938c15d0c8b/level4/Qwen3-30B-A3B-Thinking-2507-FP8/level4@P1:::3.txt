

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models with Gaussian process priors over the state transition dynamics. The authors address the challenge of learning the dynamics function f by marginalizing it out of the model, enabling the use of Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods to efficiently sample from the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T | y0:T, u0:T), while preserving the nonparametric expressivity of the Gaussian process model. The key innovation lies in using a tailored Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm to handle the non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing the dynamics function, which contrasts with conventional approaches that require a fixed model of the transition dynamics.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for state-space models. The authors successfully demonstrate how to leverage GP-SSM for both state estimation and system identification within a fully Bayesian framework, which is a significant advancement over existing methods that typically require parametric approximations of the dynamics. The use of sparse GP techniques to reduce computational complexity is also well-motivated and implemented effectively.

The strengths of the paper include:
1. A clear and principled approach to Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs that preserves the nonparametric nature of the model
2. Effective use of PMCMC methods to address the inference challenge introduced by marginalizing the dynamics function
3. Comprehensive experimental validation across both synthetic and real-world benchmarks (nonlinear system and cart-pole system)
4. Good theoretical grounding with appropriate references to related work, including Deisenroth et al. and Turner et al.

However, the paper has notable weaknesses that significantly impact its contribution. The most critical issue is the lack of detailed evaluation of the practical challenges in implementing the PMCMC scheme. While the authors present a novel application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs, they fail to provide a thorough analysis of the practical difficulties encountered in making the method work, including convergence properties, computational requirements, sensitivity to parameter choices, and comparison to alternative approaches. The experimental section demonstrates the method's effectiveness but does not sufficiently evaluate how robust or practical the approach is in real-world scenarios.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more critical assessment of the approach, including:
- A detailed analysis of the mixing properties of the PGAS sampler across different problem settings
- A comparison of the computational cost with alternative methods (e.g., variational inference or expectation propagation)
- Discussion of practical limitations and failure modes of the proposed approach
- More thorough evaluation of the sparse GP approximation's impact on performance

The current experimental section primarily serves as a demonstration of the method's capability rather than a rigorous evaluation of its practical utility. While the results on the benchmark systems are promising, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that this approach is practically superior to existing methods, especially considering the computational complexity of the PMCMC-based inference.

The paper would have been much more valuable if it had focused on the practical implementation challenges of the proposed method rather than simply demonstrating its application. A detailed evaluation of how well the PMCMC scheme works in practice, including its convergence behavior, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and computational requirements, would have been a significant contribution to the field. Instead, the paper presents a straightforward application of existing PMCMC methodology without adequately addressing the challenges of implementing it for this specific problem.

The paper is well-written and technically sound, but it falls short of making a substantial contribution to the field due to its lack of critical evaluation of the practical implementation of the proposed approach. While the application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs is interesting, the paper would have been significantly more valuable if it had provided a thorough assessment of the practical challenges and benefits of this approach.

In summary, the paper's strengths lie in its clear formulation of a fully Bayesian approach to GP-SSM learning and its effective demonstration of the method's capabilities. However, its weaknesses stem from insufficient evaluation of the practical implementation challenges of the proposed PMCMC approach, which limits the paper's contribution to the field. The authors would have made a more substantial contribution by providing a critical analysis of the practical difficulties in implementing the method rather than simply applying it to several benchmark problems.
