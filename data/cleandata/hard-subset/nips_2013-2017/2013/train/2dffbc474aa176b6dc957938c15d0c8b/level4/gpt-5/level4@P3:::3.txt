Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior over the transition function f and then marginalize f analytically, resulting in a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. Sampling from the resulting smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) and the hyperparameters θ is achieved via Particle Markov chain Monte Carlo, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). Once state trajectories are sampled, the posterior predictive for f at test points follows from standard GP regression, yielding a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive density over f. The paper also introduces a sparse construction (FIC) to reduce computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and provides implementation details (e.g., rank-one Cholesky updates) that make the sampler practical.

This work connects to: (i) PMCMC (Andrieu et al., 2010) and PGAS (Lindsten et al., 2012) as the core inference machinery; (ii) GP-SSM learning approaches based on EM/MAP (Turner et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2005), and inference in GP dynamical models via EP and related approximations (Deisenroth et al., 2012; 2013). Compared to those, the present paper emphasizes fully Bayesian state and hyperparameter inference while retaining the nonparametric nature of the transition by collapsing f.

Quality:
The modeling and algorithmic development are sound. Collapsing the GP dynamics is well motivated to reduce state–function coupling and improve mixing. The use of PGAS is appropriate for the induced non-Markovian latent process, and the paper correctly states invariance and ergodicity properties. The sparse FIC variant and the implementation details reflect careful attention to computational efficiency. However, the empirical evaluation is limited. Claims of “very good mixing” and computational scalability are not substantiated with diagnostics (autocorrelations, ESS, acceptance rates), wall-clock runtimes, memory usage, or sensitivity to key settings (number of particles N, inducing points M, horizon T). Comparisons focus on parametric baselines rather than prior GP-SSM learning methods or alternative particle smoothers, making it difficult to assess relative performance and robustness.

Clarity:
The paper is generally clear and well organized. The derivation of the collapsed prior over trajectories (Eq. 3–4) and the explanation of how PGAS operates in this setting are helpful, and the implementation notes (Cholesky updates, FIC factor reuse) are valuable to practitioners. That said, Section 3.1’s statement that Eq. (4) is not Gaussian despite its form may benefit from a short intuitive explanation (dependence of mean/covariance on the argument itself). A concise pseudo-code block for the CPF-AS step with clear notation for concatenated trajectories would further improve readability. The experimental section is easy to follow, but lacks quantitative diagnostics and ablations.

Originality:
The paper’s main novelty lies in combining a collapsed GP-SSM prior with PGAS for fully Bayesian learning of states and hyperparameters, while preserving nonparametric flexibility and offering a sparse variant. The core components (GP priors, FIC sparse GPs, PMCMC/PGAS) are known, but their integration into a coherent, practical inference procedure for GP-SSMs is a useful contribution. In terms of methodological novelty, the work is more of a careful application and engineering of established techniques than a fundamentally new algorithmic idea.

Significance:
A scalable, fully Bayesian inference method for GP-SSMs is relevant to time-series modeling and control. Marginalizing the transition function and using PGAS could broaden applicability to systems where parametric models are misspecified. The practical implementation details are likely to be reused by others. However, the current experiments are primarily demonstrative. Without stronger empirical evidence (scalability, robustness, and comparisons to state-of-the-art GP-SSM learning and inference), it is hard to gauge the impact on practice.

Strengths:
- Sound, principled Bayesian treatment that preserves the nonparametric nature of the transition dynamics by collapsing f.
- Appropriate and elegant use of PGAS for non-Markovian state trajectories, with correct invariance arguments.
- Sparse FIC variant and implementation details enabling reduced complexity and practical deployment.
- Experiments illustrate handling of multimodality and the ability to leverage informative mean functions.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical evaluation: no mixing diagnostics, runtime/memory profiling, or sensitivity analyses (N, M, T).
- Comparisons do not include prior GP-SSM learning/inference methods (e.g., EM/MAP, EP-based GPDS) or alternative particle smoothers/samplers.
- No ablations isolating the benefits of collapsing f, PGAS vs. standard particle Gibbs, or sparse vs. dense GP priors.
- Guidance on inducing-point selection and hyperprior choices is minimal; reproducibility (code/data) is not addressed.

Suggestions for improvement:
- Report convergence diagnostics (autocorrelation, ESS), wall-clock times, and memory use across N, M, and T; include a sensitivity study.
- Compare against EM/MAP methods (Turner et al., Wang et al.), EP-based inference (Deisenroth & Mohamed), and non-collapsed particle MCMC variants.
- Provide ablations showing the incremental effect of collapsing f and ancestor sampling on mixing and accuracy.
- Evaluate scalability on longer sequences and higher-dimensional states; include a real-data experiment if possible.
- Offer practical guidance for inducing-point placement and hyperpriors, and consider releasing code.

Arguments for acceptance:
- Principled and coherent Bayesian framework that neatly integrates collapsing with PGAS for GP-SSMs.
- Practical algorithmic and implementation contributions (sparse variant, factor reuse) that could benefit the community.
- Addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates capability on nonlinear and multimodal systems.

Arguments against acceptance:
- Empirical evaluation is insufficient to validate the claimed efficiency and mixing; lacks strong baselines and diagnostics.
- Methodological novelty is incremental, primarily combining known components.
- Unclear scalability and robustness in realistic settings without deeper experiments.

Recommendation:
Borderline reject. The paper presents a well-motivated and carefully engineered application of PGAS to collapsed GP-SSMs with useful implementation insights. However, the evaluation falls short of NIPS standards for demonstrating robustness, efficiency, and comparative performance. Strengthening the empirical section along the lines suggested above would make this a compelling contribution.
