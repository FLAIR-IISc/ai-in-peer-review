The authors propose an innovative application of particle MCMC to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models, focusing on the recent ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm of Lindsten et al. The paper is well-written, presenting an interesting and original contribution to the field. Additionally, the authors develop useful model-specific methodology, notably the sparse GP-SSM.

One notable omission is the lack of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, particularly the particle marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) scheme and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these alternatives would have been relatively straightforward, and it would be valuable to understand how they compare to the proposed scheme. This comparison would not only strengthen the paper but also provide insight into the relative merits of each approach.

Furthermore, I would like to see graphical representations of the algorithms' performance, such as plots of autocorrelation function (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as a function of the number of particles (N) and time steps (T). The current results lack informative value, leaving the reader wondering about the optimal scaling of N with respect to T. Specifically, does N need to increase linearly or sublinearly with T?

In my opinion, for such models, the PMMH would likely require a number of particles increasing quadratically with T, as observed in Whiteley et al. In contrast, both particle Gibbs schemes would require a number of particles growing sublinearly with T. This difference in scaling behavior could have significant implications for the practical application of these algorithms.

Overall, this is a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. However, the paper could be significantly improved by comparing the proposed algorithm to the PMMH and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the strengths and limitations of each approach.
