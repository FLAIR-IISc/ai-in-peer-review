This paper proposes a method for performing inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by applying particle MCMC techniques. The authors focus specifically on the ancestral sampling particle Gibbs (PG-AS) algorithm, a recent development in the field. The paper is clearly written, and the application of particle MCMC to this model class is both interesting and original. A notable contribution is the development of a sparse GP-SSM, which is a useful model-specific methodology that enhances the scalability of the approach.

While the paper has several strengths, its primary weakness is the lack of empirical comparison to other standard and highly relevant particle MCMC schemes. To properly evaluate the proposed method, it is essential to benchmark it against strong alternatives. Specifically, comparisons against the Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) algorithm and Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS) are conspicuously absent. These methods are natural competitors to the PG-AS approach for this class of models. Given that they could be implemented straightforwardly, their omission is a significant oversight that makes it difficult to assess the practical benefits of the chosen algorithm.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation could be made substantially more informative. The current results do not provide a clear picture of the algorithm's performance characteristics and scaling properties. To address this, the authors should include a more thorough analysis, presenting graphs that illustrate key performance metrics—such as the Autocorrelation Function (ACF) or Effective Sample Size (ESS)—as a function of both the number of particles (N) and the length of the time series (T). This would provide crucial insight into the computational trade-offs and help answer practical questions about how to tune the algorithm. For example, how does N need to scale with T to maintain a consistent level of inference quality—linearly, sublinearly, or otherwise?

Based on existing literature (e.g., Whiteley et al.), one might hypothesize that PMMH would require a number of particles (N) that scales quadratically with T for such models, whereas both PG-AS and PG-BS would likely require N to grow only sublinearly with T. Verifying this conjecture empirically for GP-SSMs would be a valuable contribution in itself and would provide a strong motivation for preferring particle Gibbs methods over PMMH in this context.

In summary, this is a well-written paper detailing a novel application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. However, the paper's contribution could be significantly strengthened. The inclusion of empirical comparisons to PMMH and PG-BS, along with a more rigorous analysis of the algorithm's performance as a function of N and T, would substantially improve the work and provide a much clearer justification for its contributions to the field.
