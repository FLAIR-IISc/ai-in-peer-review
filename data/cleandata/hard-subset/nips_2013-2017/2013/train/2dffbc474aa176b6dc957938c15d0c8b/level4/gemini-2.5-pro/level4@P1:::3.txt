### Summary

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in state-space models (SSMs) with non-parametric dynamics. The authors place a Gaussian Process (GP) prior over the state transition function, creating a flexible GP-SSM. The core of their method is an inference scheme that marginalizes out the unknown transition function `f`, thereby avoiding parametric approximations of the GP. This marginalization induces long-range temporal dependencies in the state trajectory, resulting in a non-Markovian model. To perform inference in this model, the authors employ a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) algorithm—specifically, Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS)—to jointly sample the latent states and model hyper-parameters. This approach extends beyond standard filtering and smoothing to include system identification. The paper also describes how to leverage sparse GPs to improve computational complexity.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

This paper presents a clear and logical application of advanced PMCMC methodology to the important problem of learning in non-parametric SSMs. The core idea is methodologically sound and well-presented.

**Strengths:**

*   The proposed method provides an elegant and principled solution for learning in GP-SSMs. By marginalizing the dynamics function `f`, the approach remains fully non-parametric and directly targets the smoothing distribution `p(x_0:T | y_0:T)`, which is a significant conceptual advantage over methods that rely on finite-dimensional approximations of the GP.
*   The application of recent advances in PMCMC, particularly PGAS, is well-suited to the resulting non-Markovian inference problem. It represents a straightforward and appropriate use of these modern sampling techniques.
*   The paper is well-written, and the technical details of the model and inference algorithm are clearly explained. The inclusion of a sparse GP formulation to address computational scaling is a valuable practical contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

*   While the paper presents a solid application of PMCMC methods, its primary weakness is the lack of a detailed and critical evaluation of the proposed inference scheme itself. Since the contribution is largely the application of an existing methodology to a new model class, a thorough analysis of the practical challenges and performance would be essential to demonstrate its value. The paper would have been more impactful had it focused on assessing the practical difficulties of implementing this scheme and provided a deeper analysis of how well it actually works.
*   The paper does not sufficiently explore the challenges of getting the PGAS sampler to work effectively for this model. For instance, how well does the sampler mix in practice? How sensitive are the results to the number of particles, N? A more in-depth discussion of the sampler's performance, including diagnostics on mixing and autocorrelation for the sampled parameters and trajectories, would have constituted a more significant contribution.
*   The experimental section serves primarily as a demonstration rather than a rigorous evaluation. While it successfully shows that the method works on a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system, it lacks a quantitative comparison to alternative state-of-the-art approaches for learning GP-SSMs (such as those based on variational inference or other MCMC schemes). Such a comparison is necessary to properly contextualize the method's performance.
*   A more thorough discussion of the approach's own strengths and limitations would strengthen the paper. For instance, under what conditions might the sampler struggle? How does it compare in terms of computational cost versus accuracy against competing methods?

In summary, the contribution would have been substantially greater if the authors had provided a more thorough empirical evaluation that went beyond demonstrating the method's feasibility. While revising the experimental section to provide a more rigorous evaluation might be too much to ask for a rebuttal, this lack of depth is a notable weakness.
