This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to learn the transition dynamics. The inference is performed using particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), incorporating the recently-introduced technique of ancestor sampling. The authors also address computational efficiencies through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong paper with a clear and accessible presentation. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, coupled with ancestor sampling to facilitate this, represents a novel contribution. The careful consideration of computational issues – specifically sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices – further strengthens the work. The empirical results, while concise, are sufficient to demonstrate the method's efficacy (with suggestions for further investigation outlined below).

Beyond its immediate goals, this paper is likely to spark discussion regarding inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs between learning the transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model *a priori*.  This work builds on a rich literature in GP-based SSMs [1, 5, 6], addressing a key component often missing – namely, learning the GP model itself, a need highlighted by Deisenroth et al. [5, 6]. The method presented offers a compelling alternative to approaches like those of Turner et al. [2] and Wang et al. [8] that rely on parametric or MAP estimation, preserving the nonparametric expressivity of the model.

To further enrich the discussion, the authors might consider expanding on the differences between employing a parametric transition model *a priori* versus utilizing a similar model as the mean function within the GP framework. Specifically, exploring the implications for out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting) would be valuable.

The results in Table 1 and the accompanying description require some clarification. It is unclear whether the reported RMSE is calculated against a withheld test set or the same data used for conditioning (*|data* in the column headings). An RMSE evaluated on an out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast into the future using withheld data, would be highly informative. Demonstrating performance in this scenario would likely highlight the advantages of learning the dynamics. If Table 1 does not already present this, adding a dedicated column would be a significant enhancement.

A minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being explicitly defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. 

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that is poised to stimulate interesting and productive discussion within the community.
