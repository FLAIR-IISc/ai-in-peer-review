This paper presents an application of Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) to a State-Space Model (SSM) that utilizes a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. The primary goal is to perform inference over the model's static parameters, extending beyond the standard tasks of filtering and smoothing. The proposed approach is a conceptually straightforward and logical application of the PMCMC framework to this class of models.

However, the paper's main weakness lies in its lack of a thorough evaluation of the practical challenges associated with implementing and tuning the PMCMC scheme. Successfully applying PMCMC methods can be non-trivial, often requiring careful tuning of proposal distributions, particle counts, and other hyperparameters to ensure efficient mixing and convergence. A detailed discussion and empirical analysis of these challenges—and how they were overcome—would have constituted the most significant contribution of this work. As the paper primarily presents an application of an existing methodology, its value is contingent on providing new insights into the practicalities of that application. Unfortunately, this critical analysis is largely absent.

Consequently, the experimental section serves more as a demonstration of the method's feasibility rather than a rigorous evaluation of its performance, strengths, and weaknesses. The paper would be more compelling if it assessed the practical difficulties encountered in making the scheme work effectively and provided a quantitative analysis of how well it performs compared to alternatives or under different settings. For instance, an analysis of the sampler's efficiency, mixing times, or sensitivity to the number of particles would have been highly valuable.

In summary, while the paper proposes a sound application of PMCMC to SSMs with GP priors, its contribution is limited by the absence of a critical evaluation of the implementation challenges and a comprehensive performance analysis. A more in-depth exploration of the strengths, weaknesses, and practical difficulties of the proposed scheme would be necessary to make this a more substantial and impactful contribution to the field.
