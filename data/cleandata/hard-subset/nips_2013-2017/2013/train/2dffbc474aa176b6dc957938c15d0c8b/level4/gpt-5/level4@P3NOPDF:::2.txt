Summary
The paper applies particle MCMC to Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), focusing on the particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) algorithm of Lindsten et al. It further develops model-specific methodology via a sparse GP-SSM variant to reduce computational cost. The presentation is clear, the problem is relevant, and the application of PGAS to GP-SSMs is original and practically meaningful.

Strengths
- Clear exposition and a coherent integration of particle MCMC with GP-SSMs.
- Sensible use of sparse GP machinery to make inference computationally feasible.
- The topic is timely and relevant to both the particle MCMC and GP communities.

Main weaknesses and missing evaluations
- Lack of comparisons to established particle MCMC variants, most notably particle marginal Metropolis–Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS; e.g., Whiteley et al.). These are standard baselines and are straightforward to implement given the current setup.
- Limited diagnostic analysis of mixing and computational scaling. The paper does not report how the performance depends on the number of particles N and the time series length T, making it difficult to assess practical guidance for using the method.
- Insufficient discussion of computational budgets (e.g., ESS per second), which is essential for fair algorithmic comparisons.

Requested additional experiments/analyses
- Baseline comparisons:
  - Implement PMMH and PG-BS under the same model and computational budget. Report acceptance rates (for PMMH), integrated autocorrelation times (IACTs), ESS per iteration and per second, and wall-clock time to a fixed ESS.
  - Compare posterior accuracy (e.g., marginal likelihood estimates, predictive performance) to ensure mixing gains are not offset by bias or modeling approximations.
- Scaling with N and T:
  - Provide plots of ACF/IACT/ESS as functions of N for fixed T and as functions of T for appropriately chosen N. Ideally include ESS per second to reflect computational efficiency.
  - Offer empirical guidance on how N should scale with T. Prior work (e.g., Whiteley et al.) suggests PMMH often requires N growing roughly quadratically with T to maintain stable acceptance, whereas PG-based methods can admit sublinear growth. Please test and report the observed scaling exponents in your setting.
- Computational cost breakdown:
  - Report per-iteration complexity for PGAS vs PMMH vs PG-BS, including particle propagation, resampling, ancestor/backward sampling, and GP-related computations.
  - Normalize comparisons by equal wall-clock time and by equal number of particle evaluations.
- Sensitivity and modeling details:
  - Analyze sensitivity to the number and placement of inducing points in the sparse GP-SSM, and the effect on posterior inference and predictive accuracy.
  - Clarify the priors and parameter blocking strategy, resampling scheme, initialization, and the number of conditional SMC sweeps per MCMC iteration.

Clarity and presentation
- The writing is generally clear. It would benefit from:
  - A concise algorithmic summary highlighting where ancestor sampling is essential relative to standard PG and PG-BS.
  - A brief discussion on why PGAS is preferable in this setting (e.g., mixing improvements for latent trajectories, robustness to path degeneracy) and in which regimes (T, model stiffness, observation noise) the gains are most pronounced.
  - Explicit definitions of N and T early on and consistent notation thereafter.
  - A short related-work section positioning the contribution with respect to PMMH, PG, PG-BS, and recent GP-SSM inference approaches.

Significance and novelty
- The combination of particle MCMC with GP-SSMs, supported by a sparse GP-SSM formulation, is a valuable contribution for practitioners who require fully Bayesian treatment of nonlinear dynamics. While the algorithmic core (PGAS) is not new, the adaptation to GP-SSMs, along with implementation choices and empirical guidance, can have impact—provided that the paper includes thorough comparisons and scaling analyses.

Questions for the authors
- How does PGAS performance vary with the degree of process/measurement noise and with the stiffness/nonlinearity of the transition function?
- Do you observe any degeneracy or poor mixing for specific hyperparameters (e.g., GP length-scales, noise variances), and how do you mitigate it (reparameterization, blocking, priors)?
- What is the empirical scaling of N with T required to maintain a target ESS (e.g., ESS/iteration ≈ c for key parameters)? Is sublinear scaling sufficient across your datasets?
- How sensitive are results to the number of inducing points and their selection strategy? Could you provide practical defaults?

Minor comments
- Ensure accurate and complete citations to Lindsten et al. (ancestor sampling) and Whiteley et al. (backward sampling).
- Include details necessary for reproducibility (code availability, seeds, hardware, and exact hyperparameter priors).

Overall assessment
A well-written and promising application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs with a useful sparse GP-SSM component. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including PMMH and PG-BS baselines, along with comprehensive mixing and scaling diagnostics (ACF/ESS vs N and T, wall-clock-normalized metrics). With these additions and clearer practical guidance on choosing N relative to T, the contribution would be compelling for NeurIPS.
