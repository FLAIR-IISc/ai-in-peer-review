This paper introduces a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models, leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior to model the transition dynamics. The key innovation lies in marginalizing over the transition function and utilizing particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling for efficient inference. The authors also address computational challenges through sparsity and low-rank Cholesky factorizations.

This is a technically rigorous and well-presented paper. The online marginalization of the transition dynamics, combined with ancestor sampling, represents a significant contribution. The attention to computational efficiency, specifically through sparse GP approximations and optimized Cholesky updates, further strengthens the work. The empirical validation, although concise, provides sufficient support for the proposed methodology.

The work has the potential to spark discussion regarding inference techniques for non-Markovian state-space models and the trade-offs between learning the transition dynamics versus specifying a parametric model *a priori*. This is particularly relevant in scenarios where prior knowledge about the system is limited or potentially inaccurate.  This approach provides a flexible alternative, capable of adapting to the data.

To enhance the paper, the authors could briefly elaborate on the differences between employing a parametric transition model directly versus utilizing a similar model as the mean function within the GP framework.  Specifically, discussing the implications for out-of-sample prediction, such as forecasting, would be valuable. 

The interpretation of the RMSE results presented in Table 1 requires clarification. It is currently unclear whether the RMSE values represent performance on a held-out dataset or on the data used for conditioning.  Providing RMSE values for out-of-sample prediction, particularly for forecasting into the future with withheld data, would be highly impactful. This is where the benefits of learning the dynamics, as opposed to assuming a parametric form, are most likely to be demonstrated.  A dedicated column in Table 1 addressing this would be a worthwhile addition.

A minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before its definition in Section 3.3.1.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   **Novelty:** The combination of online marginalization of transition dynamics with PMCMC and ancestor sampling is a novel and significant contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a technically rigorous approach with clear theoretical justification.
*   **Computational Efficiency:**  Addressing computational complexity through sparsity and efficient matrix updates enhances the practicality of the method.
*   **Potential Impact:** The work has the potential to influence research on inference in non-Markovian systems and Bayesian nonparametrics.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   **Limited Empirical Evaluation:** While the provided results are encouraging, a more extensive empirical evaluation, particularly with out-of-sample forecasting, would further strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarification Needed:** The interpretation of the RMSE results in Table 1 requires further clarification.



Overall, this is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field. Despite the minor suggestions for improvement, I recommend acceptance.
