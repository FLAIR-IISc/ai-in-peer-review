This manuscript introduces a fully Bayesian framework for joint state estimation and system identification in nonlinear state-space models by placing a Gaussian process prior on the transition dynamics. Inference proceeds via Particle MCMC augmented with the recently proposed ancestor sampling mechanism. The paper also addresses computational efficiency through sparse GP constructions and efficient low-rank (update/downdate) Cholesky factorizations.

The work is technically robust and clearly written. The on-the-fly marginalization of the transition function combined with ancestor sampling appears novel. Attention to practicalities—sparsity and efficient Cholesky update/downdate schemes for covariance matrices—further strengthens the contribution. While the experimental section is concise, it is sufficiently informative (with suggestions below).

Beyond its stated goals, the paper is likely to spur discussion about inference strategies for non-Markovian state-space models and about the trade-offs between learning transition dynamics in this manner versus committing to a parametric specification a priori.

If space allows, it would be valuable to elaborate on the differences between adopting a predefined parametric transition model and using a comparable structure as the GP mean function, particularly in the context of out-of-sample prediction and forecasting.

Table 1 and the preceding description could be clearer. It is not obvious whether the RMSE is computed on held-out data or on the same data used for conditioning (see the *|data notation in the column headings). My primary interest is in RMSE for out-of-sample prediction—especially forward forecasts on withheld data—where one would expect the largest divergence between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If this is not already what Table 1 reports, an additional column showing this evaluation would be very helpful.

Minor editorial note: the acronym CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before it is defined in the opening paragraph of Section 3.3.1.

Overall, this is a strong and original paper that should prompt interesting discussion.
