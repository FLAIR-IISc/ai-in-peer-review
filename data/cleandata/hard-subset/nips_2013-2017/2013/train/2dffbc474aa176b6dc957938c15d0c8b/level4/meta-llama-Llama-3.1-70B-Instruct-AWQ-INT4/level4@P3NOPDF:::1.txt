This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, incorporating the learning of transition dynamics through a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The proposed inference mechanism leverages particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) and the recently introduced concept of ancestor sampling. Additionally, the authors discuss computational efficiencies related to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates, further enhancing the paper's technical merit.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear and accessible presentation that facilitates comprehension. The online marginalization of transition dynamics, coupled with the use of ancestor sampling to achieve this, is a novel contribution. The consideration of computational issues, such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices, adds significant value to the paper. Although the empirical results are brief, they are sufficient to support the authors' claims. However, some further discussion and analysis could strengthen the paper (see suggestions below).

Beyond its stated objectives, this paper is likely to stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages and disadvantages of learning transition dynamics in this manner, as opposed to specifying a parametric model a priori.

One potential area for expansion is a more in-depth discussion on the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori versus utilizing a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For instance, how do these approaches compare in terms of out-of-sample prediction, such as forecasting? Exploring this topic could provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed method.

The results presented in Table 1, along with the accompanying description, are somewhat unclear. Specifically, it is uncertain whether the root mean squared error (RMSE) is evaluated against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points used for conditioning (as indicated by the "*|data" in the column headings). The most relevant evaluation, in this reviewer's opinion, would be the RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, particularly a forecast forward in time against withheld data. This scenario is likely to exhibit the most significant differences between the learned dynamics and the ground truth model. If Table 1 does not already present this information, adding an extra column to display these results would be a valuable contribution.

A minor point of note is that the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being formally defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. This minor oversight does not detract from the overall quality of the paper, which is strong and novel, and is likely to stimulate interesting discussions in the field.
