The authors propose applying particle MCMC to perform inference in Gaussian process state-space models, focusing on the recent ancestral sampling particle Gibbs algorithm of Lindsten et al. The paper is clear and presents an interesting and original application of particle MCMC. Additionally, the authors develop useful model-specific methodology, specifically the sparse GP-SSM.

One notable omission is the lack of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, particularly the particle marginal Metropolis Hastings (PMMH) scheme and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling (as in Whiteley et al.). Implementing these variants would have been relatively straightforward, and it would be valuable to know how they compare to the proposed scheme. Including these comparisons would not have required a significant amount of additional work from the authors.

Furthermore, I would like to see graphs displaying the performance of the algorithms (e.g., in terms of autocorrelation function (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS)) as a function of the number of particles (N) and time steps (T). As presented, the results are not very informative, leaving questions about the relationship between N, T, and algorithm performance. For instance, does N need to scale linearly with T, or is a sublinear scaling sufficient?

In my opinion, for such models, the PMMH would require a number of particles increasing quadratically with T, as observed in Whiteley et al., whereas both particle Gibbs schemes would require a number of particles growing sublinearly with T.

Overall, this is a well-written application of particle MCMC to GP state-space models. However, the paper could be significantly improved by comparing the proposed algorithm to the PMMH and the particle Gibbs with backward sampling.
