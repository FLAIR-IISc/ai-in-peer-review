Summary and relation to prior work:
The paper presents a fully Bayesian method for inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models (SSMs) by placing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the transition dynamics f. A key idea is to marginalize f and directly target the joint smoothing distribution over latent states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). This collapsing removes strong x–f dependencies and preserves the nonparametric nature of the model. The authors derive the non-Markovian prior over trajectories induced by the GP, show how to perform SMC/PMCMC in this setting, and sample hyperparameters via slice sampling. They further address computational efficiency with sparse GPs (FIC) to reduce complexity to O(M^2T), and with rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates to maintain an O(T^3) cost for the full GP case.

Relative to prior work, this approach contrasts with EM/MAP-based learning of parametric GP-SSMs (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.) by performing full Bayesian inference and by marginalizing f rather than parameterizing it. It complements GP-based filtering/smoothing (e.g., Deisenroth et al.) by providing the missing piece of learning the GP transition model itself. On the inference side, it builds on Particle MCMC (Andrieu et al.) and the PGAS kernel (Lindsten et al.), adapting these ideas to the non-Markovian trajectory prior induced by GP marginalization.

Strengths:
- Technically solid with clear derivations for collapsing f and constructing the non-Markovian trajectory prior used in SMC/PGAS.
- The use of PGAS to sample entire latent trajectories under a non-Markovian prior is well-motivated and, to my knowledge, novel in this context.
- Careful attention to computational aspects: sparse GP (FIC) construction, complexity analysis, and practical low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates to reuse computations across time.
- Empirical demonstrations on a challenging nonlinear benchmark (with multimodal smoothing) and on cart-pole show the method’s ability to learn dynamics and quantify uncertainty. The mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution is a pragmatic and expressive output.

Weaknesses and suggestions:
- The empirical evaluation is relatively brief. The main gap is clarity around the predictive evaluation in Table 1. It is unclear whether the RMSE is computed in-sample (on data used for smoothing/learning) or out-of-sample. My primary interest would be out-of-sample forecasting performance against withheld future data, where I expect the most pronounced differences between learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already report this, adding an explicit out-of-sample/forecast column would strengthen the paper.
- Space permitting, it would be helpful to discuss the trade-offs between committing to a parametric transition model a priori versus using the same parametric form as a GP mean function. In particular, what should practitioners expect in out-of-sample forecasting when the mean function is misspecified but the GP can compensate with data-driven corrections?
- Minor clarity issue: the acronym CPF-AS appears in Algorithm 1 before being defined in Section 3.3.1.

Assessment by review criteria:
- Quality: The methodology is sound, the PGAS construction is appropriate for the non-Markovian prior, and the computational strategies are sensible. Claims are supported by derivations and experiments, though a clearer and more comprehensive out-of-sample evaluation would further substantiate the predictive claims.
- Clarity: Generally very well written and accessible. The paper explains the intuition behind collapsing f and how PGAS operates with non-Markovian priors. Small improvements: define CPF-AS before use; clarify what RMSE columns represent; briefly elaborate on the parametric-as-mean vs parametric-as-model distinction.
- Originality: High. Collapsing the GP transition function and using PGAS for joint smoothing in a GP-SSM, together with the specific sparse-GP adaptation and implementation details, represent a novel and useful contribution.
- Significance: High. The approach offers a principled, fully Bayesian alternative to EM/MAP methods for GP-SSMs and is likely to spur further work on inference for non-Markovian models and on practical learning of flexible dynamical systems.

Pros for acceptance:
- Fully Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs via marginalizing f; elegant use of PGAS in a non-Markovian setting.
- Strong technical development and practical implementation details (sparse GP, Cholesky updates).
- Clear exposition; convincing qualitative results on challenging benchmarks.

Cons against acceptance:
- Limited experimental breadth and unclear in-/out-of-sample RMSE reporting; lack of explicit forecasting evaluation on withheld data.
- Minor clarity/notation issue (CPF-AS defined after use).

Overall recommendation:
A strong and novel paper that is technically sound, clearly presented, and likely to stimulate useful discussion and follow-on work. I recommend acceptance, with the above clarifications (especially out-of-sample/forecast evaluation) to strengthen the empirical section.
