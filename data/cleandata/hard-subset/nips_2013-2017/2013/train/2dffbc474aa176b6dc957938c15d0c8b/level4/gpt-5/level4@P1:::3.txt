Summary
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs). A GP prior is placed on the unknown state transition function f, while the observation function g is assumed known (possibly with parameters). The key idea is to marginalize f analytically, yielding a non-Markovian prior over latent state trajectories x0:T. Posterior inference over states and hyperparameters is then performed using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). This targets the exact joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T | θ, y0:T) despite the loss of Markov structure. The paper also presents a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and provides implementation details (Cholesky updates and rank-one updates/downdates) to achieve the stated complexities. Given samples of x0:T and θ, the posterior over f at test inputs is obtained as a mixture of GP predictive Gaussians. Experiments include a nonlinear scalar benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-and-pole system, demonstrating qualitative smoothing behavior and one-step predictive performance.

Relation to prior work
The work builds on PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) and PGAS (Lindsten et al.) to handle non-Markovian latent variable models. It contrasts with prior GP-SSM learning via EM/MAP (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.) by retaining full nonparametric uncertainty through marginalization of f and fully Bayesian treatment of states and hyperparameters. It complements GPDS filtering/smoothing methods (Deisenroth et al.) by providing a Bayesian learning procedure for the dynamics model itself.

Strengths
- Conceptually clean: collapsing f avoids strong state–function coupling in MCMC and preserves the nonparametric nature of the model.
- Correctness: PGAS targets the exact smoothing distribution; ancestor sampling mitigates path degeneracy in non-Markovian settings.
- Flexibility: the resulting predictive distribution for f is a mixture of Gaussians, capturing multimodality induced by ambiguous measurements.
- Practical considerations: detailed discussion of computational costs and how to reuse factorizations; the sparse FIC construction offers a principled reduced-complexity prior.
- The ability to encode prior knowledge in the GP mean function is well motivated and illustrated.

Weaknesses and suggestions
The main limitation is the lack of a thorough empirical evaluation of the practical challenges in making PMCMC/PGAS work well in this setting. The paper claims “very good mixing,” but does not systematically analyze or report:
- Mixing diagnostics and efficiency: autocorrelation of key summaries, effective sample sizes across time, and how these scale with the number of particles N, trajectory length T, and state dimension.
- Sensitivity and robustness: how performance depends on the choice of mean function, kernel/hyperpriors, and observation model misspecification. The synthetic example uses a misspecified mean function, which is good, but a more systematic study would be valuable.
- Runtime and scalability: empirical runtime and memory as a function of T, N, and state dimension; wall-clock comparisons to relevant baselines (EM/MAP GP-SSMs, GPDM, approximate Gaussian smoothing under a learned GP, or particle smoothers with parametric dynamics).
- Sparse GP specifics: effect of the number of inducing points M on accuracy and runtime; method for selecting inducing inputs; a clear discussion that FIC constitutes a different prior (not merely an approximation) and the practical implications of this choice.
- Predictive calibration: beyond RMSE, log predictive density and calibration plots would better demonstrate the benefits of full Bayesian uncertainty.
- Multi-step prediction: especially for the cart-and-pole system, multi-step rollouts would test whether the learned dynamics generalize beyond one-step predictions.

Quality
The methodology is technically sound and consistent with the cited PMCMC literature. The marginalization of f and the derivations for the non-Markovian prior over trajectories are correct and useful. However, the empirical section reads more as a demonstration than a rigorous evaluation. The lack of diagnostics and ablation studies makes it hard to judge how well the sampler works in practice and where it might fail.

Clarity
Overall the paper is clearly written, though dense. The CPF-AS/ancestor sampling step is well motivated, but providing more intuition for the non-Markovian setting and a brief pseudocode for the conditional filter would help readers. The implementation section is valuable; still, practical guidance on choosing N, M, and priors, and on inducing-input selection, would improve reproducibility. It would also help to explicitly acknowledge and discuss the consequences of replacing the full GP prior with FIC.

Originality
The combination—collapsing a GP transition function and using PGAS to sample the non-Markovian smoothing distribution—is a natural and timely application of PMCMC. While technically competent, it feels like a straightforward application of established PMCMC machinery rather than a fundamentally new inference scheme. The sparse construction and implementation details are practical but incremental.

Significance
The approach has potential practical impact for learning GP-SSMs in a fully Bayesian way. Its significance, however, depends on demonstrating that it works reliably and efficiently on challenging problems. The current experiments are too limited to convincingly establish this.

Pros for acceptance
- Conceptually solid, fully Bayesian treatment that respects the nonparametric GP prior.
- Correct use of PGAS to address non-Markovian smoothing with clear computational strategies.
- Potentially useful to the community working on GP-based dynamical models.

Cons for acceptance
- Empirical evaluation is thin: limited datasets, few baselines, no mixing/efficiency diagnostics, and minimal scalability analysis.
- Primarily an application of PMCMC rather than a novel inference contribution.
- Sparse GP component lacks thorough evaluation and guidance.

Recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper presents a clean and promising application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs, but it needs a more critical and detailed empirical evaluation—especially of the practical difficulties and performance characteristics of the PMCMC scheme—to meet the bar for NIPS.
