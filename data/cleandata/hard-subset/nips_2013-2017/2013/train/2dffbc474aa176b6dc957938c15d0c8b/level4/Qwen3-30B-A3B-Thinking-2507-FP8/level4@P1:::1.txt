

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), where the state transition dynamics are modeled using a Gaussian process prior. The key innovation lies in marginalizing out the transition dynamics function to enable efficient inference through tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) samplers, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The authors demonstrate that this approach preserves the full nonparametric expressivity of the model while significantly reducing computational complexity through sparse GP approximations and efficient Cholesky factor updates.

The paper makes an important contribution by addressing a fundamental challenge in GP-SSM learning: the need to jointly infer the latent states, hyperparameters, and the nonparametric transition function without resorting to parametric approximations. Previous work [2, 5, 6, 8] has typically either learned parametric representations of the transition dynamics or required pre-specified models, whereas this work enables a truly Bayesian treatment that respects the nonparametric nature of the model. The marginalization of the transition dynamics function, which breaks the Markovian structure of the state process, is elegantly handled through the use of ancestor sampling in the PGAS algorithm, a technique recently developed in [4].

The technical contributions are well-motivated and clearly presented. The paper's strength lies in its careful treatment of the inference problem, with a particularly elegant solution for handling the non-Markovian dependencies introduced by marginalizing the transition dynamics. The authors provide a thorough theoretical derivation of the marginal prior for the state trajectory (Equations 3-4), which is crucial for the proposed inference framework. The computational efficiency improvements through sparse GP approximations (Section 4) and efficient Cholesky updates (Section 4.2) are also well-justified and represent an important practical contribution.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates the approach's effectiveness on two challenging benchmarks. The nonlinear system benchmark (Section 6.1) shows the method's ability to handle multimodal smoothing distributions and capture complex dynamics even when the mean function is misspecified. The cart-pole system example (Section 6.2) demonstrates the model's ability to learn meaningful uncertainty estimates across different regions of the state space. The results in Table 1 are well-presented, though the description of the RMSE calculation requires clarification.

Strengths:
- Novel marginalization technique for nonparametric state-space models
- Clear theoretical development of the inference framework
- Effective computational improvements through sparsity and Cholesky updates
- Demonstrated effectiveness on challenging benchmarks
- Well-motivated discussion of advantages over parametric alternatives

Weaknesses:
- The description of the RMSE calculation in Table 1 is ambiguous regarding whether it's computed on held-out test data or the training data. For a fair evaluation of predictive performance, it would be valuable to see results on out-of-sample predictions, particularly for forecasting. The authors should clarify whether Table 1 shows in-sample or out-of-sample performance, and if possible, add an out-of-sample column.
- The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the relationship between using a parametric model as the mean function versus learning the transition dynamics directly. For example, how does the predictive performance differ when the mean function is misspecified versus when it's well-specified?
- The abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in Section 3.3.1 (this is a minor but important detail for readers).

The paper is technically sound, with rigorous theoretical development and appropriate experimental validation. The writing is clear and accessible, with well-organized sections that build logically from problem formulation to solution to evaluation. The authors have successfully addressed key challenges in GP-SSM inference while maintaining the full nonparametric expressivity of the model.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of Bayesian inference for dynamical systems. The paper clearly advances the state of the art in GP-SSM learning by providing a fully Bayesian solution that avoids the pitfalls of parametric approximations. The approach is both theoretically sound and practically implementable, with significant computational advantages enabled by the sparse GP approximation.

The authors should clarify the evaluation details regarding out-of-sample prediction, which would strengthen the empirical claims. With this minor clarification, the paper would be suitable for acceptance at NIPS.

Pro:
- Novel and technically sound approach to GP-SSM inference
- Clear theoretical development and practical implementation
- Effective computational improvements
- Strong empirical validation on challenging benchmarks

Con:
- Clarification needed on evaluation methodology (out-of-sample vs. in-sample)
- Additional discussion needed on parametric mean function vs. full GP learning

This is a high-quality paper that makes a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance.
