This paper presents a Bayesian approach to state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while also learning the transition dynamics through the use of a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference mechanism is based on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with the recently-introduced idea of ancestor sampling. The paper also discusses computational efficiencies to be had with respect to sparsity and low-rank Cholesky updates.

The paper is technically sound and well-presented. The online marginalisation of the transition dynamics and the use of ancestor sampling to achieve this is novel. The consideration of computational issues such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to the Cholesky factors of covariance matrices strengthens the paper further. The empirical results, while brief, are sufficient. 

One of the main strengths of the paper is its ability to perform fully Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) while retaining the full nonparametric expressivity of the model. This is achieved by marginalizing out the state transition function, which results in a nontrivial inference problem that is solved using a tailored particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS) sampler. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The introduction provides a good overview of the problem and the proposed approach. The technical sections are well-organized and easy to follow. The use of figures and tables is effective in illustrating the main points. 

The results presented in the paper are promising. The GP-SSM is able to learn a good model of the dynamics even when the state transition function is unknown. The use of a sparse GP prior is also shown to be effective in reducing the computational complexity of the algorithm. 

One potential area for improvement is in the presentation of the results. While the results in Table 1 are clear, it would be helpful to have a more detailed discussion of the differences between the learnt dynamics and the ground truth model. For example, it would be useful to have a plot showing the learnt dynamics alongside the ground truth model. 

In addition to its stated aims, the paper will likely stimulate discussion around inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and the potential advantages/disadvantages of learning the transition dynamics in this way rather than specifying a parametric model a priori. 

While space is slight, the authors may like to consider some further discussion around the differences between using a parametric transition model given a priori against the use of a similar model as the mean function of the GP. For example in out of sample prediction (e.g. forecasting). 

The results in Table 1 and the description in the preceding paragraph are slightly unclear. It is unclear whether the RMSE is against a withheld set of data points or the same set of data points that is conditioned upon (the *|data in the column headings). The main interest would be an RMSE against an out-of-sample prediction, especially a forecast forward in time against withheld data. It is in this scenario that the largest differences between the learnt dynamics and the ground truth model would be expected. If Table 1 is not already showing this, an extra column that does so would be a great addition. 

One minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1 below. 

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that should stimulate some interesting discussion.
