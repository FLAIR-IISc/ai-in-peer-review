This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models (SSMs), leveraging a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics and employing Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) for efficient inference. The core idea is to marginalize over the transition dynamics function, enabling direct inference of the joint smoothing distribution. This builds upon existing work utilizing GPs within SSMs (e.g., [2, 5, 6, 8]) by moving beyond parametric approximations and offering a truly nonparametric approach. The method’s ability to utilize sparse GPs for computational efficiency is also a valuable addition.

The paper is technically sound and clearly outlines a complex methodology. The use of PMCMC to address the challenges of marginalizing out the state transition function is a logical extension of existing techniques [3, 4] and represents a significant undertaking. The authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related literature, referencing key works in GP-based SSMs and particle methods. The inclusion of implementation details and a discussion of computational complexity (Sections 4.1 & 4.2) is commendable, and the experimental results on both a synthetic benchmark and a cart-pole system are encouraging. 

However, the paper’s impact is somewhat limited by a lack of critical discussion regarding the practical challenges of implementing and tuning the PMCMC sampler. While the authors mention good mixing, a more detailed evaluation of the sampler’s performance – including convergence diagnostics, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and computational cost – would significantly strengthen the contribution. The experimental section focuses primarily on demonstration rather than rigorous evaluation, leaving the reader uncertain about the method’s robustness and scalability.  A more detailed exploration of the trade-offs between accuracy and computational cost, particularly when utilizing sparse GPs, would be beneficial. The comparison to existing methods, while present, could be expanded to provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The approach of fully marginalizing out the state transition function in a GP-SSM is a noteworthy contribution, offering a truly nonparametric alternative to existing methods.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is clearly explained and appears technically well-founded.
*   **Practical Considerations:** The inclusion of implementation details and discussion of computational complexity demonstrates an awareness of practical challenges.
*   **Demonstrated Applicability:** The experiments show the potential of the method on both synthetic and benchmark problems.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Evaluation of PMCMC:** The paper lacks a detailed assessment of the PMCMC sampler’s performance, making it difficult to gauge the practical challenges of implementation and the robustness of the results.
*   **Focus on Demonstration:** The experimental section is primarily demonstrative rather than rigorously evaluative.
*   **Insufficient Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods could be more comprehensive.



**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to a challenging problem in Bayesian inference.
*   The method offers a valuable extension to existing GP-SSM techniques, enabling a truly nonparametric approach.
*   The inclusion of implementation details and practical considerations enhances the paper's value.

**Arguments Against Acceptance:**

*   The lack of a thorough evaluation of the PMCMC sampler’s performance and the limited scope of the experimental results raise concerns about the method's robustness and scalability.
*   The paper leans heavily towards demonstration rather than rigorous analysis.




**Overall Assessment:**

This paper describes an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of Bayesian SSMs. However, the lack of a more critical evaluation of the PMCMC sampler and the limited scope of the experiments weaken the overall impact. While the work is sound, a more thorough analysis would be required to justify a strong recommendation for acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent upon the authors addressing the aforementioned concerns regarding the evaluation of the PMCMC sampler and expanding the experimental analysis.
