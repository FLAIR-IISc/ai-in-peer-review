The paper introduces a Bayesian framework for joint state and parameter estimation in nonlinear state-space models, while simultaneously learning the transition dynamics via a Gaussian process (GP) prior. The inference scheme relies on particle Markov chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) augmented with the recently proposed ancestor sampling. The authors also discuss computational gains enabled by sparsity and by low-rank Cholesky updates.

This is a technically sound and strong contribution, presented clearly and accessibly. The online marginalisation of the transition dynamics using ancestor sampling appears novel. The attention to computational aspects—such as sparsity and low-rank updates/downdates to Cholesky factors of covariance matrices—further strengthens the work. The empirical results, though concise, are adequate (with suggestions below).

Beyond its stated objectives, the paper is likely to stimulate discussion on inference methods for non-Markovian state-space models and on the trade-offs of learning transition dynamics in this way versus specifying a parametric model a priori.

If space allows, please elaborate on the differences between adopting a parametric transition model fixed a priori and using a comparable model as the GP mean function—for instance, in out-of-sample prediction (e.g., forecasting).

The results in Table 1 and the preceding description are somewhat unclear. It is not obvious whether the RMSE is computed on a held-out set or on the same data used for conditioning (the *|data in the column headings). My main interest is RMSE on out-of-sample predictions, particularly forward-time forecasts on withheld data. This is where I would expect the largest discrepancies between the learned dynamics and the ground-truth model. If Table 1 does not already reflect this, an additional column reporting such results would be very valuable.

One minor point: the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before being defined in the first paragraph of Section 3.3.1. Overall, a strong and novel paper that should prompt interesting discussion.
