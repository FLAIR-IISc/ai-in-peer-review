This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in nonlinear state-space models where the transition dynamics are modeled by a Gaussian process (GP-SSM). The core idea is to marginalize the unknown transition function, which induces long-range dependencies in the state trajectory, making the model non-Markovian. To perform inference in this model, the authors propose using a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm recently proposed by Lindsten et al. (2012). A key methodological contribution is the extension of this framework to a sparse GP-SSM using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation, which reduces the computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), making the approach viable for longer time series.

**Strengths:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the model, the motivation for marginalizing the dynamics function, and the subsequent application of the PGAS sampler.
*   **Originality and Significance:** The application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs is a novel and elegant combination of techniques. It provides a principled, fully Bayesian framework for a challenging problem that has often been addressed with approximations (e.g., EM or MAP estimation). The ability to perform joint inference over the state trajectory and hyperparameters while retaining the full nonparametric nature of the GP is a significant contribution.
*   **Technical Quality:** The proposed method is technically sound. The derivation of the non-Markovian prior over state trajectories and the adaptation of the PGAS algorithm are correct. The development of a sparse variant to address computational scaling is a valuable and practical addition that strengthens the work.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

The primary weakness of this paper lies in its experimental evaluation, which is not thorough enough to fully assess the performance of the proposed method or to justify the specific choice of PMCMC algorithm.

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Alternative PMCMC Schemes:** The paper's major omission is the absence of any comparison to other standard PMCMC algorithms. The authors motivate the choice of PGAS by its suitability for non-Markovian models, but this claim needs to be substantiated with empirical evidence. Two critical baselines are missing:
    *   **Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH):** This is the original PMCMC algorithm proposed by Andrieu et al. (2010) and is a natural first point of comparison for any new PMCMC application.
    *   **Particle Gibbs with Backward Sampling (PG-BS):** This algorithm (e.g., Whiteley et al., 2010; Lindsten & Schön, 2012) is another powerful variant of Particle Gibbs that is often highly efficient and would be a very strong baseline.

    Implementing these alternatives would be straightforward, as they would use the same underlying sequential Monte Carlo machinery. Such a comparison is essential for the community to understand the relative merits of PGAS in this context. Without it, the paper's contribution is more of a proof-of-concept than a conclusive demonstration of a superior inference strategy.

2.  **Insufficient Performance Diagnostics:** The experimental results demonstrate that the sampler can explore the posterior (e.g., by capturing the bimodality in the first experiment) but provide no quantitative insight into its efficiency. A proper evaluation of an MCMC algorithm requires diagnostics that measure its performance. The authors should include:
    *   **Plots of sampler performance (e.g., Effective Sample Size (ESS) or Autocorrelation Function (ACF)) as a function of the number of particles (N).** This is crucial for understanding how many particles are needed to achieve good mixing.
    *   **Analysis of scaling with the time horizon (T).** How does the required number of particles `N` grow with `T` to maintain a constant ESS? This is a fundamental question for any sequential inference algorithm. It is known that for PMMH, `N` typically needs to grow quadratically with `T` to prevent the variance of the log-likelihood estimate from growing with `T`, leading to poor acceptance rates. In contrast, Particle Gibbs variants like PGAS and PG-BS are expected to require `N` to grow only sublinearly with `T` (e.g., O(T log T) or O(T)). Verifying this scaling behavior would be a very strong result and would provide a compelling argument for using a PG-based approach over PMMH for this class of models.

**Conclusion:**

This is a well-written and interesting paper that introduces a novel and promising application of PMCMC to GP-SSMs. The core idea is elegant and the development of a sparse variant is a valuable contribution. However, the paper would be significantly improved by a more rigorous experimental evaluation. The inclusion of comparisons to PMMH and PG-BS, along with a proper analysis of the sampler's efficiency and scaling properties (ESS/ACF vs. N and T), are necessary to fully validate the authors' claims and establish the practical utility of their chosen method. While the paper presents a solid foundation, the lack of these critical comparisons and diagnostics currently limits its impact.
