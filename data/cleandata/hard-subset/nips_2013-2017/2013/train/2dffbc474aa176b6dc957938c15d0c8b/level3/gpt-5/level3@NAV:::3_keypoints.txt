- Studies fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs by marginalizing the transition and sampling latent states and hyperparameters with Particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS).
- Collapsing the GP transition induces a non‑Markovian prior over trajectories; authors propose efficient CPF‑AS updates and a sparse FIC variant reducing complexity to O(M^2 T).
- Experiments on a nonlinear synthetic benchmark and a 4D cart‑pole illustrate smoothing and one‑step prediction performance.
- Builds on existing GP‑SSM and GP filtering/smoothing literature and follows established PMCMC/PGAS methodology; main contribution is applying collapsed PGAS to GP‑SSMs.
- High‑level formulation and collapsed representation are correct; linking θ updates to GP regression is helpful.
- Paper understates practical challenges: no systematic assessment of path degeneracy, required particle number N, mixing (ACF, ESS), or sensitivity to priors, kernel choice, and initialization.
- Sparse FIC variant raises nontrivial issues (inducing input selection, stability of downdates) that are not empirically investigated.
- Empirical results are demonstrative but lack rigorous comparisons to strong alternatives (EM/MAP, variational GP‑SSM, SMC^2), predictive log‑likelihoods, calibration (coverage/PIT), and runtime/efficiency metrics.
- Exposition of model and collapsed prior is clear, but reproducibility needs explicit priors, initialization, convergence diagnostics, kernel/mean choices, and ablation over N, M, T with wall‑clock time.
- Originality is moderate: a natural, largely straightforward application of existing PMCMC tools with incremental implementation tricks.
- Significance is potential but unproven: useful for practitioners if robustness and advantages over existing methods are better demonstrated.
- Pros: clear, coherent presentation of a collapsed PMCMC approach; practical complexity discussion and sparse variant; demonstrations on nonlinear and 4D tasks.
- Cons: limited novelty beyond established PMCMC application; insufficient analysis of practical difficulties and algorithmic tuning; lacking rigorous empirical evaluation and baselines.
- Recommendation: Weak reject — requires thorough diagnostics, comparative baselines, sensitivity studies, and calibration/likelihood evaluations.
