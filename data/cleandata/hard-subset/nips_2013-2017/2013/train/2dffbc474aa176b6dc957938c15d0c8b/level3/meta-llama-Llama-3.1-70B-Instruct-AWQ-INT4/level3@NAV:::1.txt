This paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) with ancestor sampling. The authors propose a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, which is a significant departure from existing methods that rely on parametric representations of the transition dynamics.

The paper is technically sound, with a clear and accessible presentation of the key concepts and ideas. The authors provide a thorough explanation of the GP-SSM, the PMCMC algorithm, and the ancestor sampling technique. The empirical results are brief but sufficient, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two benchmark problems: a nonlinear system and a cart-pole system.

One of the key strengths of this paper is its novelty. The authors introduce a new approach to marginalizing out the transition dynamics using ancestor sampling, which is a significant contribution to the field of Bayesian inference and learning in state-space models. The paper also demonstrates strong consideration of computational efficiencies, with a focus on sparse GP models and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates.

However, there are a few minor issues with the paper. For example, Table 1 and the preceding text are unclear whether the RMSE is in-sample or out-of-sample, and the authors could benefit from adding an out-of-sample/forecast RMSE column. Additionally, the abbreviation CPF-AS is used before being defined, which may cause some confusion for readers who are not familiar with the terminology.

Overall, this is a strong and novel paper that is likely to stimulate discussion on inference for non-Markovian state-space models. The authors provide a thorough and well-written presentation of their approach, and the empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of their method.

Arguments for acceptance:

* The paper presents a novel approach to Bayesian inference and learning in GP-SSMs, which is a significant contribution to the field.
* The authors provide a clear and accessible presentation of the key concepts and ideas.
* The empirical results are brief but sufficient, and the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two benchmark problems.
* The paper demonstrates strong consideration of computational efficiencies, with a focus on sparse GP models and low-rank Cholesky updates/downdates.

Arguments against acceptance:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of using a parametric transition model a priori versus as the GP mean.
* The authors could provide more information on the computational cost of their approach, particularly in comparison to other methods.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the results, particularly in terms of the performance of the sparse GP model.

Recommendations for improvement:

* Add an out-of-sample/forecast RMSE column to Table 1 to clarify the results.
* Define the abbreviation CPF-AS before using it in the text.
* Provide more information on the computational cost of the approach, particularly in comparison to other methods.
* Consider adding a more detailed discussion of the implications of using a parametric transition model a priori versus as the GP mean.
