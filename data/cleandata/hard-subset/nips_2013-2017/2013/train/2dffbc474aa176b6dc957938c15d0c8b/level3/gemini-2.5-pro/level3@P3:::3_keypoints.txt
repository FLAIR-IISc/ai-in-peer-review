- Presents a fully Bayesian GP-SSM by marginalizing the transition function and inferring states and hyperparameters jointly, inducing non‑Markovian dependencies.
- Uses Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to perform PMCMC inference on the marginalised model.
- Contrasts with prior GP-SSM work that relied on parametric approximations, EM, or MAP estimates and thus underestimated posterior uncertainty.
- Principled approach: coherent fully Bayesian treatment that avoids MAP/variational approximations and captures full posterior uncertainty.
- Clear exposition: paper is well written and explains the motivation, non‑Markovian structure, and suitability of PGAS.
- Novel application: applying PGAS to GP-SSMs is a useful and novel combination of existing techniques.
- Computational practicality: introduces a sparse GP formulation reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), improving scalability.
- Limited methodological originality: mainly an application of existing PGAS methodology to GP-SSMs without new PMCMC techniques or theoretical insights.
- Missing critical evaluation: claims of “very good” mixing are unsupported by MCMC diagnostics (trace plots, ACF, ESS) or analysis of dependence on N, T, or kernel choice.
- Practical implementation issues unaddressed: computational cost and numerical stability of evaluating GP predictive densities in the ancestor sampling step are not discussed.
- Lack of discussion on limitations: failure modes, scaling with state dimensionality, and sensitivity to GP priors/hyperparameters are not analyzed.
- Insufficient experiments: PGAS run for only 50 iterations—far too few to assess convergence or mixing; RMSE alone ignores uncertainty quantification.
- Weak quantitative evaluation: cart-pole experiment is qualitative with no quantitative metrics or baseline comparisons.
- Missing baselines and comparisons: no evaluation against variational GP-SSMs, other MCMC schemes, or simpler parametric models.
- Suggested improvements: run longer MCMC chains with diagnostics, evaluate posterior uncertainty (multi-step predictions, test log-likelihoods), include meaningful baselines, and add a critical discussion of practical challenges and limitations.
