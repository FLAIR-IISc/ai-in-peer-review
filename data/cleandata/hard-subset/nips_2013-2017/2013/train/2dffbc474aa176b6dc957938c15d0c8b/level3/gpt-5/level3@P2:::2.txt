Summary and relation to prior work
This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian-process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The key idea is to place a GP prior on the state-transition function f, analytically marginalize f to obtain a non-Markovian prior over latent trajectories, and then sample from the joint smoothing distribution of states and hyperparameters using a Particle MCMC algorithm—specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). Given smoothing samples, the posterior over f is available in closed form via standard GP regression, yielding a mixture-of-Gaussians predictive distribution. The paper further develops a sparse GP-SSM variant using FIC, with implementation details that reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T).

This work is well-situated relative to prior literature: earlier GP-SSM work often used EM/MAP estimates with parametric surrogates for f (e.g., Turner et al.; Wang et al.) or performed filtering/smoothing assuming a learned model (e.g., Deisenroth et al.). The use of PMCMC (Andrieu et al.) is appropriate for the non-Markovian latent process induced by collapsing f; PGAS (Lindsten et al.) is a natural choice. The paper’s message—that one can perform smoothing without a fixed parametric dynamics model, and then reconstruct the function posterior—adds a principled alternative to standard pipelines.

Strengths
- Principled and technically sound framework: collapsing the GP prior over f and using PGAS for the induced non-Markovian model is elegant and correct.
- Clear exposition and careful algorithmic design, including complexity-aware implementations and a sparse FIC construction.
- The approach preserves nonparametric expressivity and yields calibrated predictive uncertainty (mixtures of Gaussians).
- Experiments illustrate multimodal smoothing and learning dynamics from limited, noisy data; the cart-pole example shows state-dependent uncertainty.

Weaknesses
- Missing empirical comparisons to other PMCMC schemes, especially particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PG-BS; e.g., Whiteley et al.). These are straightforward baselines and crucial to assess relative performance.
- Lack of mixing/efficiency diagnostics and scaling studies. There are no ACF, IACT/ESS (per iteration and per second), acceptance rates (for PMMH), or plots showing how N must grow with T to maintain accuracy.
- Limited ablations: little on sensitivity to N, the choice of mean function, or the impact of sparse approximation (M vs accuracy/runtime).
- Details important for reproducibility (hyperpriors, slice-sampling settings, initialization, runtime budgets) are only briefly sketched.

Quality
- The methodology is correct; the collapsing derivations and PGAS invariance are appropriate. The only approximation is Monte Carlo.
- The implementation discussion (factor reuse, Cholesky rank-1 updates, FIC factorization reuse) is useful, though empirical validation of claimed complexity and memory footprints would strengthen the case.
- Experimental scope is modest; conclusions about efficiency and mixing would be more convincing with the missing baselines and diagnostics.

Clarity
- Generally very well written and organized; the model, algorithms, and implementation details are clear.
- Minor suggestions: clarify the claim that the sparse model is “not an approximation” (it is exact under a different prior), specify all hyperpriors and slice-sampling settings, and ensure consistent indexing (x0 vs x1:T in Algorithm 1).

Originality
- Applying PGAS to a collapsed GP-SSM and using this to perform full Bayesian learning of f is, to my knowledge, novel at NIPS. Prior GP-SSM works do not combine these elements in a fully Bayesian, nonparametric manner.
- The sparse FIC adaptation and complexity-aware implementation are practical contributions.

Significance
- Potentially impactful for Bayesian time-series modeling and nonparametric system identification, with immediate relevance to communities using GPDS/GP-SSMs.
- The significance would be much higher with a careful efficiency study comparing PGAS vs PMMH and PG-BS, and with scaling laws (N vs T) empirically verified.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add baseline comparisons:
  - PMMH (as in Andrieu et al.) with matched computational budgets; report acceptance rates, variance of log p(y|θ), and ESS/second for state and hyperparameters.
  - Particle Gibbs with backward sampling (e.g., Whiteley et al.) to disentangle the benefits of ancestor vs backward sampling.
- Provide diagnostics:
  - ACF/IACT and ESS/second for representative functionals (e.g., selected states x_t, lengthscales, Q).
  - Scaling plots: for increasing T, show N needed to keep ESS fixed; verify expected quadratic growth for PMMH and sublinear for Gibbs variants.
  - Runtime vs accuracy curves; memory usage.
- Sparse model ablations: vary M and show accuracy-runtime trade-offs; describe inducing input selection; discuss stability when hyperparameters are updated.
- Sensitivity to the GP mean function choice and to misspecification; assess the benefit of informative means.
- Reproducibility: provide hyperprior choices, initialization, slice-sampling parameters, and, if possible, code.

Arguments for acceptance
- Principled, novel, and cleanly presented Bayesian treatment of GP-SSMs with strong technical grounding.
- Practical algorithmic insights (ancestor sampling, factor reuse, sparse variant) of broad utility.
- Demonstrated capability to handle multimodal smoothing and calibrated predictive uncertainty.

Arguments against acceptance
- Lack of comparisons to PMMH and PG-BS and absence of mixing/scaling diagnostics limit the empirical credibility of the efficiency claims.
- Experimental scope is relatively narrow; no wall-clock or ESS-based benchmarks.

Overall assessment
A well-written and technically solid paper that advances fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs by combining collapsing with PGAS and offering a sparse variant. I find it interesting and potentially impactful. However, the absence of direct comparisons to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, and the lack of diagnostic scaling studies (ACF/ESS vs N and T), are significant omissions for a methods paper centered on inference efficiency. I would lean toward acceptance if the authors can add these empirical comparisons and diagnostics; otherwise, this is a borderline case.
