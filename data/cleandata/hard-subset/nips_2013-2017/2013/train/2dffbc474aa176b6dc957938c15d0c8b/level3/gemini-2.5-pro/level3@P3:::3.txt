This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in state-space models where the transition dynamics are modeled by a Gaussian process (GP-SSM). The core idea is to marginalize out the latent transition function, which is placed under a GP prior, and then perform inference directly on the joint posterior of the hidden states and model hyperparameters. This marginalization induces long-range temporal dependencies, breaking the Markovian structure of the model. To tackle this challenging inference problem, the authors propose using a Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm. This approach is contrasted with previous work on GP-SSMs which often relies on parametric approximations, EM algorithms, or MAP estimation (e.g., Turner et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2005), thereby failing to capture the full posterior uncertainty. The proposed method leverages recent advances in PMCMC (Andrieu et al., 2010; Lindsten et al., 2012) to provide a principled, sample-based approximation to the full posterior.

**Strengths**

*   **Principled Approach:** The paper proposes a coherent and fully Bayesian treatment for a flexible and important class of models. By marginalizing the dynamics function and using PMCMC, the method avoids approximations that might underestimate uncertainty, such as MAP estimation or variational inference. This is a significant conceptual advantage.
*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The authors do a good job of explaining the motivation for marginalizing the GP, the resulting non-Markovian structure, and why PGAS is a suitable inference tool for this setting.
*   **Novel Application:** The application of PGAS to the GP-SSM is a novel combination of existing techniques. It provides a valuable new tool for researchers working with non-parametric dynamical systems, moving beyond the point-estimates often sought in prior work.
*   **Computational Considerations:** The inclusion of a sparse GP formulation to reduce the computational complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T) is a crucial practical contribution that makes the approach more scalable to longer time series.

**Weaknesses**

*   **Limited Methodological Originality:** While the application is novel, the work is a relatively straightforward application of the PGAS methodology to the GP-SSM. The paper does not introduce new techniques for PMCMC or provide novel theoretical insights into the behavior of these samplers in the context of GP models. The contribution lies almost entirely in the combination of these two existing components.
*   **Lack of Critical Evaluation and Discussion:** The paper presents the method in a consistently positive light but fails to properly describe or evaluate the practical challenges inherent in its implementation. Getting PMCMC samplers to work well can be difficult, and this model poses specific challenges that are not addressed:
    *   The mixing efficiency of the sampler is claimed to be "very good," but this is asserted without any supporting evidence. There are no MCMC diagnostics such as trace plots, autocorrelation functions, or effective sample size (ESS) calculations to substantiate this claim. How does mixing depend on the number of particles `N`, the length of the time series `T`, or the choice of GP kernel?
    *   The ancestor sampling step requires evaluating the GP predictive density, which can be computationally demanding and potentially numerically unstable. The paper does not discuss the practicalities or potential pitfalls of this step within the CPF-AS loop.
    *   A discussion of the method's limitations is conspicuously absent. What are the failure modes? How does the performance degrade as the state-space dimensionality increases? How sensitive is the inference to the choice of GP prior and its hyperparameters?

*   **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental section serves as a proof-of-concept demonstration rather than a rigorous empirical evaluation of the method's performance.
    *   In the first experiment, the PGAS sampler is run for only 50 iterations. This is an extremely small number for an MCMC algorithm and is insufficient to assess convergence, mixing, or to obtain reliable posterior estimates. The evaluation metric (RMSE) is also limited for a Bayesian method, as it ignores the crucial aspect of uncertainty quantification. A comparison of posterior predictive distributions or log-likelihoods on a test set would be more informative.
    *   The second experiment on the cart-pole system is purely qualitative. While it illustrates that the method can produce reasonable one-step-ahead predictions, it provides no quantitative results or comparisons to any baseline methods, making it impossible to judge the quality of the learned model.
    *   The experiments lack comparisons to other relevant methods for this problem, such as variational inference approaches for GP-SSMs or other advanced MCMC schemes.

**Summary and Suggestions for Improvement**

The paper introduces a sound and principled method for fully Bayesian inference in GP-SSMs. The combination of marginalizing the GP dynamics and applying PGAS is elegant and promising. However, the manuscript in its current form feels incomplete. It reads more like a proposal or a demonstration of a new model-inference pairing rather than a complete scientific paper providing a thorough evaluation.

To significantly strengthen the paper, the authors should focus on providing a more detailed and critical assessment of the proposed algorithm. This would involve:
1.  **A thorough empirical study:** Run the MCMC samplers for a sufficient number of iterations to ensure convergence. Provide standard MCMC diagnostics (ESS, R-hat statistics, ACF plots) to support claims about mixing performance.
2.  **Richer evaluation:** Move beyond RMSE and evaluate the quality of the posterior uncertainty. Assess the performance on multi-step-ahead prediction tasks and report test log-likelihoods.
3.  **Meaningful baselines:** Compare the proposed method against relevant alternatives, which could include variational approaches or simpler parametric models, to demonstrate its advantages and disadvantages.
4.  **A critical discussion:** Add a section discussing the practical challenges and limitations of the approach, offering guidance to practitioners on when the method is likely to succeed or fail. This would make the contribution more impactful and honest.

Overall, the idea is interesting, but the paper would benefit greatly from a much more rigorous experimental section and a more balanced discussion of the method's practical strengths and weaknesses.
