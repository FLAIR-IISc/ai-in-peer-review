

# Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper presents a novel application of Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically Lindsten et al.'s ancestral-sampling particle Gibbs (PGAS), to inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs). The authors address the challenge of learning nonlinear state-space models by placing a GP prior over the state transition dynamics, enabling a fully Bayesian approach that preserves the nonparametric expressivity of the model. The key insight is marginalizing out the transition function f, allowing direct inference of the joint smoothing distribution p(x₀:T | y₀:T, θ) without requiring a fixed model for f. The paper also introduces a sparse GP-SSM variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) approximation to reduce computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T) where M ≪ T.

The work builds upon and complements previous NIPS contributions in this area. It extends the line of work on GP-based SSMs initiated by Deisenroth et al. (NIPS 2012, 2013) and Turner et al. (NIPS 2009), which primarily focused on inference for already learned GP dynamics. The paper also relates to Lindsten et al.'s PGAS algorithm (NIPS 2012), which the authors adapt for non-Markovian latent variable models.

**Quality:** The paper demonstrates strong technical quality with a well-justified approach to marginalizing the transition function and a sound implementation of the sparse GP-SSM. The theoretical foundation is solid, with clear derivations of the marginal prior and efficient implementation details. However, the experimental evaluation is a significant weakness. The paper lacks crucial diagnostics to assess the performance of the sampling algorithm, particularly the scaling behavior with respect to the number of particles N and time horizon T. The authors should provide autocorrelation function (ACF) plots or effective sample size (ESS) metrics as functions of N and T to determine whether the computational complexity scales linearly or sublinearly with T. The reviewer believes that particle marginal MH (PMMH) would likely require particle count growing quadratically with T (as suggested by Whiteley et al., 2013), while particle Gibbs variants like PGAS require sublinear growth - a comparison that would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about efficiency.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly organized, and provides sufficient detail for reproduction. The mathematical derivations are thorough, and the algorithm descriptions are precise. The sparse GP implementation details are particularly well-explained, making the technical contributions accessible.

**Originality:** The application of PGAS to GP-SSMs represents a novel combination of techniques, addressing a significant gap in the literature. Previous approaches to learning GP-SSMs have typically employed parametric approximations (e.g., Turner et al., 2009) or MAP estimation (Wang et al., 2006), whereas this work provides a fully Bayesian nonparametric approach. The sparse GP-SSM variant is also a meaningful methodological contribution that extends the applicability to larger datasets.

**Significance:** The work addresses an important problem in dynamical systems modeling - learning flexible nonlinear state transition models without sacrificing Bayesian inference. The ability to marginalize out the transition function while preserving nonparametric expressivity is a significant advancement. The experimental results on both synthetic and cart-pole benchmark problems demonstrate practical utility, though the lack of comparative analysis limits the assessment of the method's relative advantages.

**Major Weakness:** The most significant omission is the absence of comparisons to other particle MCMC schemes, specifically particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (Whiteley et al., 2013). Such comparisons would be straightforward to implement and are necessary to assess the relative performance and computational efficiency of the proposed approach. Without these comparisons, it's difficult to evaluate whether the authors' claims about the computational advantages of their method are well-founded.

**Strengths:**
- Clear, well-written presentation of a novel application of PMCMC
- Important methodological contribution with the sparse GP-SSM variant
- Strong theoretical foundation with detailed derivations
- Good experimental validation on meaningful benchmarks

**Weaknesses:**
- Missing comparative analysis with other particle MCMC schemes (PMMH, particle Gibbs with backward sampling)
- Inadequate diagnostics to assess scaling behavior (ACF/ESS vs. N and T)
- Limited discussion of the computational complexity comparison with alternative methods

**Recommendation:** This is a well-written, technically sound paper with a significant methodological contribution that advances the state of the art in Bayesian inference for GP-SSMs. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including comparisons to PMMH and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, as well as providing performance diagnostics showing scaling with N and T. Without these additions, the paper's claims about computational efficiency remain unverified. However, as a standalone contribution, the paper makes a valuable addition to the literature on nonparametric state-space models. I recommend acceptance with major revision to address these critical omissions.
