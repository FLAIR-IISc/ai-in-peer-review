

The paper introduces a fully Bayesian inference framework for Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) using tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). It addresses the challenge of learning nonlinear state transitions by marginalizing the GP dynamics function from the model, enabling direct inference of the joint smoothing distribution \(p(x_{0:T} | y_{0:T})\) without fixing the transition model. This approach preserves the nonparametric expressivity of GP-SSMs while avoiding the limitations of parametric approximations used in prior work (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.). The authors develop a sparse GP-SSM variant using the Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) prior to reduce computational complexity from \(O(T^3)\) to \(O(M^2T)\), where \(M \ll T\) is the number of inducing points. Experiments demonstrate the method on a nonlinear benchmark system and a cart-pole dynamics model, showing accurate state smoothing and predictive dynamics learning.

**Strengths**  
The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured, with a concise technical exposition of the marginalization strategy and PGAS implementation. The motivation for marginalizing the transition function—avoiding the pitfalls of parametric GP approximations (e.g., non-identifiability, overfitting in high dimensions)—is compelling and well-justified against prior work (Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.). The methodological contribution of adapting PGAS to non-Markovian state trajectories via CPF-AS is novel for GP-SSMs, filling a critical gap in fully Bayesian learning of nonparametric dynamics. The sparse GP-SSM extension is practical and correctly positioned as a computational enhancement (not a model innovation), with efficient implementation details that address scalability. The cart-pole experiment effectively demonstrates the method’s ability to capture state-space uncertainty, with error bars reflecting data density—a significant practical advantage for control applications. Theoretical grounding in GP priors and PMCMC (Lindsten et al.) is solid, and the paper adequately references related work (e.g., Ghahramani & Roweis for EM-based approaches).

**Weaknesses**  
The most critical weakness is the omission of comparative analysis with other PMCMC schemes, particularly Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and Particle Gibbs with Backward Sampling (PBGS, Whiteley et al.). As noted by the reviewer, these comparisons are straightforward to implement and essential for assessing the method’s scalability and efficiency. PMMH is known to require particle count \(N\) growing quadratically with time horizon \(T\) (Whiteley et al., 2010), while PGAS variants typically scale sublinearly. The paper claims "very good mixing" but provides no empirical evidence of this relative to alternatives—e.g., no plots of effective sample size (ESS) or autocorrelation functions (ACF) against \(N\) or \(T\). Without such diagnostics, it is impossible to validate the claimed efficiency or justify PGAS as the optimal choice. The results section only reports RMSE tables, omitting key performance metrics (e.g., ESS, wall-clock time per iteration) needed to evaluate MCMC convergence and computational trade-offs.

The sparse GP-SSM section overstates novelty; the FIC prior is a standard sparse GP technique (Snelson & Ghahramani, 2005; Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen, 2005), and the adaptation to GP-SSMs is a direct application rather than a new contribution. While the implementation details for \(O(M^2T)\) complexity are useful, the paper fails to quantify the accuracy-cost trade-off (e.g., how RMSE changes with \(M\)). The cart-pole experiment, though illustrative, does not demonstrate why sparsity is necessary—100 training points in a 4D state space may not require it. Furthermore, the nonlinear benchmark experiment’s multimodal smoothing (Figure 2) is correctly handled, but the paper lacks diagnostics to confirm that the PGAS sampler adequately explores both modes (e.g., trace plots of \(x_t\) for \(t=10\)).

**Recommendation**  
This is a technically sound, well-written paper with significant potential for impact in Bayesian time-series modeling. The core contribution—applying PGAS to GP-SSMs—is original and addresses a genuine gap in the literature. However, the lack of comparative PMCMC experiments and performance diagnostics severely undermines the quality assessment of the proposed method. The authors should extend their experiments to include PMMH and PBGS, reporting ESS/ACF vs. \(N\) and \(T\) to validate the scalability claims. Without this, the paper’s significance is diminished, as it cannot establish whether PGAS is truly superior to existing PMCMC approaches for GP-SSMs. The sparse GP-SSM is a minor extension and not a primary contribution, so its inclusion should not distract from the core inference method.

**Pro/Con Summary**  
*Pro*: Clear exposition, novel application of PGAS to GP-SSMs, effective handling of non-Markovian marginalization, practical sparse implementation, strong cart-pole experiment demonstrating uncertainty quantification.  
*Con*: Missing critical comparisons to PMMH/PBGS, inadequate MCMC diagnostics (no ESS/ACF plots), overstatement of sparse GP novelty, insufficient analysis of sparsity benefits.  

The paper is a strong candidate for acceptance *if* the authors address the missing comparisons and diagnostics in revision. As-is, the weaknesses prevent confident endorsement, but the technical merit and clarity warrant a conditional recommendation for acceptance pending revisions. The method advances the state of the art in Bayesian dynamics learning, but its claims require empirical validation against alternative PMCMC schemes.
