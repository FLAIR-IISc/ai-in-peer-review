Summary and relation to prior work
The paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing the unknown transition function f and sampling the latent trajectory x0:T and hyperparameters with Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). This produces samples from the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) without committing to a parametric transition model, after which the posterior over f can be obtained analytically given sampled trajectories. The authors also introduce a sparse variant using the FIC prior to reduce computational cost from O(T^3) to O(M^2T).

The work connects two established lines: GP-based dynamical models (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al., and the GPDM literature) and PMCMC (Andrieu et al.), leveraging PGAS (Lindsten et al.) to handle the non-Markovian prior induced by marginalizing f. Relative to previous GP-SSM work that used MAP/EM or assumed learned GPs for filtering/smoothing (Deisenroth et al.), the paper emphasizes a collapsed, fully Bayesian treatment of the dynamics. As such, it is largely a careful application and integration of existing methodologies, with engineering for computational reuse and a sparse construction.

Quality
Strengths:
- The collapsed formulation is principled and removes strong posteriors couplings between x and f; using PGAS is an appropriate choice for the non-Markovian prior on x.
- The paper provides a clear derivation of the marginal prior over trajectories (Eq. 3–4), and sensible implementation details for reusing Cholesky factorizations and enabling sparse GPs.
- The experiments demonstrate feasibility on a nonlinear benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole setting, showing uncertainty-aware predictions.

Weaknesses:
- Practical challenges of making PMCMC/PGAS work in this setting are not adequately characterized. There is little evidence on mixing, path degeneracy, particle count sensitivity, initialization, and numerical stability of rank-1 updates/downdates over long horizons.
- Empirical evaluation is illustrative rather than rigorous: N=20 particles and 50 PGAS iterations at T=200 are modest; there are no convergence diagnostics (autocorrelation, effective sample size of the MCMC, R-hat), no wall-clock/runtime scaling vs T, N, and M, and no memory footprint analysis.
- Comparisons are limited. There is no head-to-head against alternative inference strategies for GP-SSMs (e.g., PMMH, PGBS, EP/variational approximations, EM/MAP) in terms of predictive log-likelihood, state RMSE, calibration (coverage), and robustness.
- The sparse FIC variant is included, but the impact of sparsity (choice of M, inducing inputs placement) on accuracy, mixing, and complexity is not quantified.

Clarity
- Generally well-written with helpful notation and algorithmic context. The description of the non-Gaussian nature of Eq. (4) is clear.
- However, several important practical details are missing or too terse: precise hyperpriors, kernel forms and priors, the mean-function choices across experiments, how inducing inputs are selected in practice, and numerical safeguards for downdates. Pseudocode or a supplementary section detailing the O(T^3)/O(M^2T) implementation would materially improve reproducibility.
- The experimental section would benefit from clearer metrics (predictive log-likelihood, calibration plots), error bars across runs, and ablations (with/without marginalization, with/without FIC, varying N/M/T).

Originality
- The core methodological components (marginalizing f in a GP model; PMCMC; PGAS) are established. The contribution is a straightforward and coherent application of PGAS to a collapsed GP-SSM, plus implementation engineering and a sparse variant. The combination is natural but incremental. Related work is mostly cited, though comparisons to contemporaneous alternatives (e.g., PMMH on the uncollapsed model, SMC^2-style approaches) are not explored.

Significance
- A fully Bayesian, nonparametric state-space learning approach is of interest to the NeurIPS community. If the practical viability were thoroughly demonstrated, this could be a useful reference implementation.
- As presented, the lack of rigorous empirical assessment limits confidence that the method is robust enough for broader adoption, especially on longer sequences and higher-dimensional states.

Detailed suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative diagnostics of the PMCMC: autocorrelation functions for key quantities (state summaries, θ), effective sample size per compute time, acceptance/mixing behavior of slice updates for θx, and sensitivity to N (particles), T (length), and M (inducing points).
- Compare against baselines:
  - EM/MAP-style GP-SSM learning (Turner et al., Wang et al.).
  - PMMH or PGBS on the uncollapsed model (sample f explicitly) to justify the collapsed PGAS choice.
  - EP/variational GP-SSMs (Deisenroth et al.) for prediction quality and calibration.
  - Standard nonlinear state-space estimators (EKF/UKF/particle smoothers with parametric dynamics) to contextualize gains.
- Report predictive log-likelihood and calibration (e.g., PIT histograms, prediction interval coverage) in addition to RMSE; include credible interval coverage for states.
- Present wall-clock time and scaling plots vs T, N, M, and state dimension; discuss memory usage. Include stress tests on longer sequences where path degeneracy is challenging.
- Analyze impact of mean function misspecification and identifiability: ablate the mean function choice, demonstrate robustness to priors on Q and kernel hyperparameters, and discuss multimodality (e.g., the sign ambiguity) and any label-switching effects.
- For FIC: detail inducing input selection strategy, show accuracy vs M curves, and examine the effect on mixing (since sparser priors alter coupling).
- Release code or provide enough low-level implementation details (pseudocode for factor reuse and rank-1 updates/downdates, numerical stabilization strategies) to enable reproduction.

Pros
- Principled, fully Bayesian treatment that preserves nonparametric expressivity by collapsing f.
- Appropriate use of PGAS for non-Markovian priors; clear link to prior GP-SSM literature.
- Useful engineering notes and a sparse variant; demonstrations on synthetic and control data.

Cons
- Primarily a straightforward application of PMCMC with limited novelty.
- Insufficient discussion of practical pitfalls and how to make PGAS work robustly here.
- Empirical evaluation is not rigorous; missing diagnostics, ablations, baselines, and scaling analyses.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses a relevant problem with a coherent, principled approach, but it falls short on critical empirical evaluation and discussion of practical challenges specific to PMCMC in GP-SSMs. Strengthening the experimental section and adding a candid assessment of robustness and trade-offs would make this a stronger contribution.
