This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to learning nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models by placing a Gaussian process prior on the transition dynamics and performing inference over states and hyperparameters via Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), in particular Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS). The key idea is to marginalize the transition function f analytically, inducing a non-Markovian prior over trajectories p(x0:T | θ) that is handled by an SMC-within-MCMC procedure. Given samples from the smoothing distribution, the posterior over f is available in closed form; the authors also present a sparse variant based on FIC inducing variables to reduce complexity to O(M^2 T). Experiments on a nonlinear benchmark with bimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole demonstrate qualitative benefits (uncertainty quantification, multimodality).

Relation to prior work: The paper builds on well-established PMCMC methodology [3] and the PGAS kernel [4], and on GP-based dynamical models [2,5–8]. Prior GP-SSM work largely relied on MAP/EM with parametric approximations or learned f first then performed filtering/smoothing with approximate Gaussian moment matching [5,6]. Here, the main conceptual novelty is to collapse f and run PMCMC over x and θ, preserving the nonparametric nature of the model and then recovering f posterior analytically. The sparse GP construction (FIC) follows [16,17], and the fast Cholesky updates align with standard SMC/GP engineering.

Strengths
- Clear probabilistic formulation: marginalizing f yields a coherent fully Bayesian target p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) and an elegant separation between state inference and function learning.
- Use of PGAS is appropriate for non-Markovian latent models and theoretically justified (invariance of the CPF-AS kernel to the smoothing distribution for any N ≥ 2).
- Practical contributions: complexity analysis with reusable Cholesky factors for vanilla GP (O(T^3)) and a sparse FIC alternative (O(M^2 T)); explicit expressions for ancestor sampling weights in the collapsed model.
- The approach naturally captures multimodality in smoothing and propagates uncertainty into the learned dynamics (mixture of Gaussians in Eq. (10)).
- Experiments qualitatively illustrate robustness to mean-function misspecification and calibrated uncertainty on cart-pole.

Weaknesses
- The methodological core is a relatively straightforward application of PMCMC/PGAS to a collapsed GP-SSM. The algorithmic novelty beyond this combination and standard sparse GP machinery is limited.
- The paper does not adequately discuss the practical challenges of making PMCMC work here: state dimensionality effects, path degeneracy over long horizons, sensitivity to the number of particles N and chain length, autocorrelation and effective sample sizes for x and θ, and stability of repeated rank-one downdates.
- Empirical evaluation is not rigorous. There are no likelihood-based metrics (predictive log-likelihood, marginal likelihood), no mixing diagnostics (ACF, ESS, R̂), no wall-clock comparisons versus alternatives (e.g., PG with backward simulation, particle EM, variational GP-SSM), no ablations (PGAS vs. standard PG, collapsed vs. non-collapsed, sparse vs. dense).
- Scalability is asserted but not demonstrated: the largest example is 4D and T=200; there is no study of runtime/memory scaling with T, state dimension, or number of inducing points M.
- Hyperparameter inference lacks depth: slice sampling is mentioned but not evaluated; coupling between θx and x can be strong, yet mixing behavior and prior sensitivity are not analyzed.
- Generality claims (arbitrary measurement models) are not matched by experiments, which are Gaussian-noise cases.

Quality
- Technically sound overall: the collapsed prior over trajectories is correctly derived; PGAS invariance is used appropriately; the sparse FIC construction is standard. However, the lack of empirical diagnostics and sensitivity analyses makes it hard to assess robustness and reliability in practice.

Clarity
- Generally well organized with helpful equations (e.g., Eq. (3–4), Algorithm 1). Some implementation details could be clearer: numerical stability of downdates, computation of ancestor weights in log-space, memory requirements for maintaining per-particle factorizations.

Originality
- Moderate. Collapsing f in a GP-SSM and plugging into PGAS is a neat combination not, to my knowledge, previously demonstrated in this exact form, but each component is well-known. The contribution is integrative rather than fundamentally new.

Significance
- Potentially useful to the GP-SSM community, offering a principled fully Bayesian alternative to MAP/EM and approximate message passing. Without stronger empirical evidence and scaling studies, the practical impact is uncertain.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide quantitative comparisons on multiple benchmarks against (i) GP-SSM with EM/MAP [2,8], (ii) approximate inference GPDS methods [5,6], and (iii) PMCMC variants (PG with backward simulation, particle Gibbs without ancestor sampling). Report predictive log-likelihood, smoothing RMSE, and long-horizon rollout error.
- Include mixing diagnostics (ESS per iteration, ACF of θ and functionals of x), acceptance rates, and wall-clock time to a target error for varying N, T, and state dimension.
- Analyze sensitivity to hyperpriors, mean function specification, and kernel choice; discuss identifiability considerations when g is known vs. partially known.
- Give a thorough scaling study and memory profile; include sparse vs. dense trade-offs, varying M and inducing set selection strategies.
- Discuss numerical stability of rank-one updates/downdates, and implement all weight computations in log-space; clarify how degeneracy is mitigated for long T.
- Explore non-Gaussian observation models to support generality claims.

Arguments for acceptance
- Conceptually clean, fully Bayesian treatment of GP-SSMs via collapsing and PGAS, with theoretical justification.
- Maintains nonparametric expressivity and provides a practical sparse alternative with lower complexity.
- Qualitative demonstrations of handling multimodality and uncertainty.

Arguments against acceptance
- Limited methodological novelty beyond a direct application of existing PMCMC and sparse GP techniques.
- Insufficient empirical rigor and lack of diagnostics; unclear practical performance and scalability.
- Missing comparisons to strong baselines and ablations; limited evidence that the method advances state of the art.

Overall recommendation: lean toward rejection in the current form due to the lack of thorough empirical evaluation and discussion of practical challenges, but the approach is promising; with stronger experiments and diagnostics it could be a valuable contribution.
