This paper introduces a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, utilizing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics and employing Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) for efficient inference. The core idea is to marginalize over the transition dynamics function, directly inferring the smoothing distribution. This builds upon a substantial body of work in GP-based state-space models, referencing contributions from Rasmussen & Williams (2006), Turner et al. (2013), Deisenroth et al. (2015), and others. The approach distinguishes itself from existing methods by avoiding parametric approximations of the GP, aiming for full nonparametric expressivity.  The authors acknowledge the lineage of related work, tracing back to EM algorithms for learning radial basis function models (Ghahramani & Roweis, 2001) and dynamic dimensionality reduction techniques (Wang et al., 2008).

The strengths of this work lie in its principled Bayesian treatment of a complex problem. Applying PMCMC to marginalize out the transition function is a conceptually sound approach allowing for full nonparametric flexibility.  The use of recently developed PMCMC samplers (Lindsten et al., 2012) appears to be crucial for addressing the computational challenges. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant (Snelson & Ghahramani, 2006) offers a pathway to scalability.  The experimental results, while preliminary, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach on both a synthetic nonlinear system and a cart-pole control problem. The ability to quantify uncertainty in the learned dynamics, as illustrated in the cart-pole example, is a valuable contribution.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The presentation feels somewhat cursory, and the authors gloss over critical implementation details and practical considerations. While they mention the importance of efficient updates for the Cholesky factorization (linked to computational complexity, O(T^3) or O(M^2T)), the details provided are insufficient to assess the real-world applicability of the method.  The paper lacks a robust analysis of PMCMC convergence and mixing.  Simply stating “very good mixing” (p. 3) is insufficient; diagnostic plots or quantitative measures of convergence (e.g., R-hat) should be included.  The absence of a detailed discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters (both for the GP and the PMCMC sampler itself) is concerning.

The experimental evaluation, while demonstrating the method’s potential, is not sufficiently rigorous.  The synthetic benchmark compares the RMSE against several baselines, but the statistical significance of the results is not established. The cart-pole example is more illustrative than quantitative.  A more thorough evaluation would involve comparing the learned model’s performance on downstream tasks (e.g., control) against state-of-the-art alternatives. Furthermore, the comparison against a "true" model in the synthetic experiment is somewhat contrived; a more challenging scenario would involve learning from limited and noisy data.  The paper does not adequately address the trade-offs between nonparametric flexibility and computational cost.

**Arguments for acceptance:**

*   Presents a novel application of PMCMC to a challenging problem in state-space modeling.
*   Offers a principled Bayesian approach with full nonparametric expressivity.
*   Demonstrates feasibility through preliminary experiments.
*   Contributes to the growing literature on GP-based dynamical systems.

**Arguments against acceptance:**

*   Lacks sufficient detail on implementation and practical considerations.
*   Provides inadequate analysis of PMCMC convergence and sensitivity.
*   The experimental evaluation is not rigorous enough to convincingly demonstrate the method's advantages.
*   Fails to fully address the computational challenges and scalability of the approach.
*   The paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a fully developed contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising, but incomplete, contribution. The idea of combining PMCMC with GP state-space models is novel and potentially valuable. However, the lack of detail, analysis, and rigorous evaluation significantly weakens the paper. While the work demonstrates the *possibility* of this approach, it does not convincingly demonstrate its *practicality* or *superiority* over existing methods. I would recommend **rejection** with encouragement to the authors to address the weaknesses outlined above before resubmission. Specifically, they should provide a more thorough discussion of the practical challenges of implementing PMCMC, include more detailed convergence diagnostics, and conduct a more comprehensive empirical evaluation.
