

This paper proposes applying Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) methods to perform fully Bayesian inference in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs), marginalizing out the state transition function to enable nonparametric learning of dynamics. The authors build upon previous work by Deisenroth et al. and Turner et al., who focused on filtering/smoothing with fixed dynamics, by addressing the crucial problem of learning the GP model itself. The approach leverages Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) to efficiently sample from the smoothing distribution while preserving the full nonparametric expressivity of the model, with sparse GP approximations for computational efficiency.

The paper demonstrates the approach on two benchmark problems: a nonlinear system and a cart-pole system. While the methodology is technically sound and the implementation details are well-presented, the contribution falls short on several critical dimensions. The application of PMCMC is largely straightforward rather than innovative, representing a natural extension of existing techniques rather than a significant methodological advance. Most critically, the paper fails to properly evaluate the practical challenges of implementing PMCMC in this context, such as sensitivity to particle number, convergence rates, and computational bottlenecks. The experimental section is descriptive rather than rigorous, lacking ablation studies, comparison to alternative inference methods, and thorough analysis of the method's limitations.

The paper would benefit from a more critical evaluation of PMCMC's performance in this setting, including discussion of when the method might fail or require careful tuning. While the approach is theoretically sound and the sparse GP implementation is practical, the lack of detailed empirical validation and failure to address practical implementation challenges significantly weakens the contribution. The work represents a useful technical extension to the literature but does not advance the state of the art in a demonstrable way. For NIPS, this paper would be stronger with more rigorous empirical evaluation and a deeper discussion of the method's practical limitations and trade-offs.
