This paper presents a fully Bayesian framework for inference and learning in state-space models (SSMs) where the state transition dynamics are governed by a Gaussian Process (GP) prior. The core problem addressed is the joint estimation of the hidden state trajectory and the model's (hyper)parameters, including those of the unknown dynamics function. The main contribution is a novel inference strategy that marginalizes out the infinite-dimensional GP transition function. This clever "collapsing" step avoids parametric approximations of the dynamics but introduces long-range temporal dependencies, breaking the Markovian structure of the state sequence. To perform inference in this resulting non-Markovian model, the authors employ a tailored Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) method, specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) algorithm. This allows for efficient sampling from the joint smoothing distribution of the states and hyperparameters.

The proposed method is a significant departure from prior work on GP-SSMs. Previous approaches have often relied on EM algorithms with parametric representations of the GP (e.g., Turner et al., 2012; Ghahramani & Roweis, 1999) or MAP estimates for the latent variables (Wang et al., 2005), which can neglect posterior uncertainty. This paper provides a principled, fully Bayesian treatment that retains the full nonparametric expressivity of the GP model. The work also complements methods that focus on filtering and smoothing with *already learned* GP dynamics (e.g., Deisenroth et al., 2012) by providing an effective way to perform the initial learning.

### Strengths and Weaknesses

**Quality:** The paper is technically sound and presents a complete piece of work. The derivation of the marginal prior over state trajectories after integrating out the GP function is correct. The application of PGAS to this non-Markovian inference problem is well-motivated and appropriate. The authors demonstrate a careful consideration of the practical challenges by addressing the computational complexity of the algorithm. They show how to reduce the naive O(T^4) complexity to O(T^3) by reusing Cholesky factorizations and further down to O(M^2T) through a well-justified sparse GP (FIC) construction. The experiments, while brief, effectively demonstrate the capabilities of the model, such as capturing multimodal smoothing distributions and learning complex nonlinear dynamics.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-organized, and accessible. The motivation for marginalizing the dynamics function is clearly stated, and the resulting inference challenges are explained well. The description of the inference algorithm is logical and easy to follow for a reader with a background in GPs and sequential Monte Carlo methods. The connection between the vanilla GP and sparse GP implementations is also presented clearly. One minor point for improvement: the abbreviation CPF-AS is used in Algorithm 1 before it is formally defined in Section 3.3.1. Defining it upon first use would improve readability.

**Originality:** The work is highly original. While GP-SSMs and PMCMC methods are established concepts, their combination in this manner is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance. The central idea of marginalizing the entire dynamics function and then tackling the resulting non-Markovian smoothing problem with a specialized particle sampler (PGAS) is, to my knowledge, new. This approach provides the first truly nonparametric Bayesian learning framework for this model class, which is a substantial contribution.

**Significance:** The results are important and likely to be influential. The paper provides a powerful and principled tool for system identification in general nonlinear dynamical systems. By enabling a fully Bayesian treatment without resorting to parametric approximations of the dynamics, this work opens the door for more flexible and robust modeling of complex time series across various domains (e.g., robotics, econometrics, control). The methodology is likely to stimulate further research into inference techniques for non-Markovian latent variable models and the application of Bayesian nonparametrics in dynamics.

### Detailed Comments

The experimental validation, while effective, could be strengthened.
1.  In Section 6.1, it is unclear whether the RMSE reported in the text and Table 1 is calculated in-sample on the training data or out-of-sample on the new test set. The ability to generalize is a key advantage of Bayesian nonparametric models, so quantifying out-of-sample predictive performance is crucial. I strongly recommend adding a column to Table 1 for one-step-ahead forecast RMSE on the held-out test data.
2.  The paper highlights the ability to encode prior knowledge via a parametric mean function for the GP. This is an excellent feature. A brief discussion on the practical implications of this choice (e.g., using a linear model as the GP mean) versus using a purely parametric linear model would be valuable. For instance, how does it affect long-term forecasting when extrapolating outside the training data regime?

### Arguments for Acceptance

*   **Pro:** The paper introduces a novel, elegant, and fully Bayesian approach to learning in GP-SSMs. The core technical idea of marginalizing the dynamics function and using PGAS for inference is a significant methodological contribution. The work is technically strong, clearly written, and addresses an important problem. It demonstrates careful consideration of computational efficiency, making the method practical.
*   **Con:** The empirical evaluation is sufficient to demonstrate the method's potential but could be more comprehensive to fully establish its advantages over competing approaches.

Overall, this is a strong and impressive paper that presents a significant advance in the modeling of dynamical systems. The strengths far outweigh the minor weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance.
