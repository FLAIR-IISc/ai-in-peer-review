This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonparametric Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs) by marginalizing the unknown transition function and sampling directly from the joint smoothing distribution over latent states and hyperparameters using Particle MCMC, specifically particle Gibbs with ancestor sampling (PGAS). The key idea is to collapse the GP transition function f so that p(x0:T | θ) can be expressed via GP one-step predictions, yielding a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories. The authors then design an efficient PGAS sampler that targets p(x0:T, θ | y0:T), with forward-only CPF-AS kernels and carefully engineered linear algebra to reuse Cholesky factorizations across time. Given sampled trajectories, the posterior over f and predictive distributions follow analytically from GP regression, either as mixtures of Gaussians or, for speed, via a single sparse GP fit to sampled function values. To reduce computational cost, the paper develops a sparse GP-SSM using the FIC prior, bringing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T). Experiments include a nonlinear scalar benchmark with multimodal smoothing and a 4D cart-pole system, demonstrating plausible smoothing, predictive distributions, and a runtime/sparsity trade-off.

Relation to prior work: Compared to GP-based SSMs that rely on EM/MAP with parametric finite representations [Turner et al., Wang et al.], or that perform filtering/smoothing after a GP dynamics model has already been learned [Deisenroth et al.], this paper keeps the transition model fully nonparametric by integrating out f and uses PMCMC to sample the full smoothing distribution. Methodologically it builds on PMCMC [Andrieu et al.] and PGAS [Lindsten et al.] and adapts these to the collapsed non-Markovian GP-SSM. The sparse FIC construction follows standard sparse GP ideas [Quiñonero-Candela & Rasmussen].

Quality: The inference scheme is technically sound. Collapsing f is correct and leads to a well-defined non-Markovian prior whose GP structure enables tractable one-step conditionals. The PGAS kernel is appropriate for such models and the implementation details (rank-1 updates/downdates; factor reuse; FIC algebra) are valuable and carefully described. The experiments are convincing at a qualitative level (bimodality, uncertainty calibration, predictive means/variances). However, the empirical evaluation lacks critical PMCMC diagnostics and ablations that would substantiate the claimed efficiency and mixing: there are no comparisons to alternative PMCMC schemes (particle marginal MH, particle Gibbs with backward simulation), no ACF/ESS scaling with the number of particles N and time horizon T, and limited wall-clock analyses for the sparse variant beyond asymptotic statements.

Clarity: The paper is clearly written, well organized, and provides sufficient algorithmic detail to reproduce the method. The derivations of the collapsed trajectory prior and the FIC variant are explicit and helpful. A few clarifications would further improve readability: precisely state the kernel class and priors used in the experiments; report hyperparameter posteriors or summaries; and detail the inducing-point selection strategy for FIC.

Originality: The combination of (i) fully collapsing the GP transition, (ii) tailoring PGAS to the resulting non-Markovian model, and (iii) integrating a sparse FIC prior with implementation-level complexity reductions is a novel and meaningful advance over prior GP-SSM learning approaches that rely on parametric approximations or point estimates.

Significance: A general, fully Bayesian, nonparametric approach to learning SSMs is important and broadly relevant. If the efficiency claims are borne out, the approach could become a standard tool for GP-SSMs and related dynamical models. The sparse construction increases practical impact. The current empirical section demonstrates promise but stops short of providing the comparative and scaling evidence that would convince practitioners to adopt this over simpler PMCMC baselines.

Pros:
- Sound and elegant collapsed formulation that preserves GP nonparametricity.
- Appropriate use of PGAS with careful engineering for O(T^3)/O(M^2 T) costs.
- Sparse FIC variant is well integrated and practically important.
- Experiments illustrate multimodal smoothing and calibrated uncertainty.

Cons:
- No comparison to other PMCMC samplers: particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (PGBS).
- Missing diagnostics of MCMC efficiency (ACF, ESS per second) and their scaling with N and T.
- Limited analysis of the sparse variant’s wall-clock gains and sensitivity to M, inducing-point selection, and kernel choices.
- Modest experimental breadth (two datasets; few chains/iterations reported).

Requested revisions to strengthen the paper:
- Add head-to-head comparisons to PMMH and PGBS (e.g., Whiteley et al.; Lindsten & Schön) on the same models, matched for wall-clock time and implementation effort. Report acceptance rates (PMMH), mixing (IACT/ESS), and posterior accuracy.
- Provide ACF/ESS-vs-N curves and scaling with T (e.g., T ∈ {100, 200, 500}). Theory suggests PMMH can have unfavorable scaling in T (at least linear and potentially worse, depending on log-likelihood estimator variance), whereas particle Gibbs variants typically require sublinear growth in N; empirical curves would substantiate the claimed advantage of PGAS.
- Include runtime and memory profiles demonstrating the O(T^3) and O(M^2 T) regimes, with ablations on reuse of factorizations and rank-1 updates. For FIC, show performance vs M and report an inducing-point selection protocol.
- Report quantitative mixing diagnostics (ESS/sec) for key functionals (e.g., latent states, Q, length-scales), trace plots, and, if feasible, R-hat across multiple chains.
- Consider adding a baseline that learns GP-SSMs via EM/MAP (e.g., Turner et al., Wang et al.) to position the Bayesian approach in terms of predictive accuracy and uncertainty calibration.

Overall assessment: This is a well-written and technically solid paper with a compelling idea and careful algorithmic development. It would be significantly strengthened by comparative PMCMC baselines and mixing/scaling diagnostics. Subject to these additions, I lean toward acceptance given the novelty and potential impact.
