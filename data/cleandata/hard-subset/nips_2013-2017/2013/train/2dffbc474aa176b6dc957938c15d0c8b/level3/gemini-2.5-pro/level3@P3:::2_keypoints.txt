- Core idea: marginalize the GP transition function in nonlinear SSMs, producing a non-Markovian prior over state trajectories and enabling fully Bayesian inference.  
- Inference method: apply Particle MCMC, specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS), to sample state trajectories and hyperparameters.  
- Sparse extension: develop a Fully Independent Conditional (FIC) sparse GP-SSM reducing complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), making long time series feasible.  
- Clarity: the paper is very well written, clearly organized, and easy to follow.  
- Originality/significance: novel and elegant combination of PMCMC and GP-SSMs, enabling joint nonparametric Bayesian inference over states and hyperparameters.  
- Technical quality: derivations of the non-Markovian prior and adaptation of PGAS are correct and sound.  
- Practical value: the sparse variant is a valuable, practical addition strengthening scalability.  
- Major experimental weakness: lack of comparisons to alternative PMCMC algorithms prevents assessment of PGAS’s relative merits.  
- Missing baselines: no empirical comparison to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH), a natural baseline.  
- Missing baselines: no comparison to Particle Gibbs with Backward Sampling (PG-BS), another strong Particle Gibbs variant.  
- Insufficient diagnostics: experiments lack quantitative MCMC performance metrics (e.g., ESS or ACF) as a function of particle number N.  
- Missing scaling analysis: no study of how required N scales with time horizon T (e.g., to maintain constant ESS), which is crucial for evaluating algorithmic practicality.  
- Overall conclusion: strong methodological contribution and presentation, but impact is limited by an underpowered experimental evaluation—add PMMH/PG-BS comparisons and ESS/ACF vs. N and T analyses to validate claims.
