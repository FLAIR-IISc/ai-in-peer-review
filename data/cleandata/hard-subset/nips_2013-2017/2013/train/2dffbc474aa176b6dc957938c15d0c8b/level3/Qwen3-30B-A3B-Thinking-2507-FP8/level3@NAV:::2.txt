

This paper presents a novel application of particle MCMC methods to Bayesian inference in Gaussian process state-space models (GP-SSMs), focusing on Lindsten et al.'s ancestral-sampling particle Gibbs (PGAS). The authors develop a method that marginalizes the state transition function from the model, enabling fully Bayesian inference over both the state trajectory and dynamics while preserving the nonparametric expressivity of the GP prior. A key contribution is the development of a sparse GP-SSM variant using the fully independent conditional (FIC) approximation to reduce computational complexity from O(T³) to O(M²T).

The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contribution within the context of previous work on GP-SSMs (e.g., Deisenroth et al., Turner et al.). The authors properly contextualize their approach as addressing a gap in fully Bayesian learning of GP-SSMs, contrasting with previous parametric approximations. The experimental results demonstrate the method's effectiveness on benchmark nonlinear systems and a cart-pole system, showing good predictive performance and ability to capture multimodal smoothing distributions.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of comparative analysis with alternative particle MCMC schemes. The authors omit necessary comparisons to particle marginal MH (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling (Whiteley et al.), both of which are standard baselines for such inference problems. Such comparisons would be straightforward to implement and crucial for assessing relative performance. Additionally, the results lack informative diagnostics—graphs of autocorrelation (ACF) or effective sample size (ESS) as functions of particle count N and time horizon T are needed to determine scaling behavior (linear vs. sublinear). Given Whiteley et al.'s theoretical results suggesting PMMH requires particle count growing quadratically with T while particle Gibbs variants require sublinear growth, these comparisons are essential.

The paper makes a technically sound contribution with good clarity and significant originality in applying particle MCMC to GP-SSMs. However, the omission of key comparisons and diagnostics significantly weakens the evaluation of the proposed method's practical utility. While the paper would be acceptable with these additions, the current version lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate the claimed advantages over existing approaches. The authors should address these omissions to strengthen their contribution.
