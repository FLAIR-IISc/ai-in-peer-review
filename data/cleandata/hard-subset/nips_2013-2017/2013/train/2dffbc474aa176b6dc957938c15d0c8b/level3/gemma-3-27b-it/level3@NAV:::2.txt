## Review of "Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC"

This paper presents a compelling approach to Bayesian inference and learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models (GP-SSMs) using Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC). The core idea – marginalizing out the transition dynamics function to preserve the nonparametric expressivity of the model – is well-motivated and builds upon existing work in SMC and MCMC methods for state estimation. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of this approach compared to traditional parametric methods and highlight its ability to leverage recent advancements in PMCMC samplers. The development of a sparse GP-SSM variant to improve computational efficiency is a significant contribution, addressing a key practical challenge in applying GP-SSMs to complex systems. 

The paper is well-written and logically structured, providing sufficient detail for a reader familiar with GPs and state-space models to understand the proposed methodology. The inclusion of illustrative examples, such as the nonlinear system benchmark and the cart-pole system, effectively demonstrates the potential of the approach. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their work and discuss potential avenues for future research.

However, a major weakness lies in the lack of comparative analysis with other established particle MCMC schemes. While the paper thoroughly explains their chosen approach (particle Gibbs with ancestral sampling), it omits comparisons to Particle Marginal Metropolis-Hastings (PMMH) and particle Gibbs with backward sampling, which are relevant baselines. As rightly pointed out, such comparisons are relatively straightforward to implement and crucial for establishing the relative performance of the proposed method. Specifically, assessing whether the proposed method exhibits the expected sublinear scaling in particle count and time horizon (compared to the quadratic scaling expected for PMMH) is essential.

Furthermore, the presentation of results lacks robust diagnostic information. While the illustrative examples are informative, the absence of plots showing performance metrics (e.g., autocorrelation function or Effective Sample Size) as functions of particle count and time horizon hinders a thorough evaluation of the algorithm’s scaling properties and mixing behavior. These diagnostics are vital for understanding the practical limitations and potential bottlenecks of the proposed approach. 

**Arguments for Acceptance:**

*   Original and interesting application of particle MCMC to GP-SSMs.
*   Methodologically sound development of a sparse GP-SSM variant.
*   Clear writing and logical presentation.
*   Demonstrated potential through illustrative examples.

**Arguments against Acceptance:**

*   Lack of comparative analysis with key alternative particle MCMC schemes (PMMH, backward sampling).
*   Insufficient diagnostic analysis to assess scaling and mixing properties. 



**Overall:** This is a strong paper with a valuable contribution to the field. However, addressing the aforementioned weaknesses by including comparative results and detailed performance diagnostics would significantly strengthen the manuscript and make it more compelling for acceptance. I recommend acceptance contingent upon the authors addressing these concerns.
