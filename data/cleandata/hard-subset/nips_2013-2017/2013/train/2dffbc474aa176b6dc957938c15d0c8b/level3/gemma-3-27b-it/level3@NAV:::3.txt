## Review of “Bayesian Inference and Learning in Gaussian Process State-Space Models with Particle MCMC”

This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach to learning in nonlinear nonparametric state-space models, utilizing a Gaussian process (GP) prior over the state transition dynamics and Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC) for inference. The core idea – combining GP flexibility with PMCMC for full Bayesian treatment – builds upon established work in both GP state-space models (e.g., Deisenroth et al., [5, 6]) and sequential Monte Carlo methods (e.g., Doucet et al., [9]). The authors correctly identify a key limitation of existing GP-SSM approaches: the need to fix the transition dynamics, preventing true nonparametric learning. Marginalizing out the dynamics function is a significant contribution in principle.

The paper’s strength lies in its attempt to implement this conceptually sound idea. The derivation of the marginal prior and the tailored PMCMC algorithm are presented with reasonable clarity, referencing relevant PMCMC literature [3, 4]. The inclusion of a sparse GP variant (FIC prior) [16, 17] demonstrates an awareness of computational challenges. The experimental section, featuring a nonlinear system benchmark and a cart-pole example, provides initial demonstration of the approach's feasibility.

However, this work suffers from a significant lack of thoroughness in addressing practical considerations. While the paper *mentions* the computational complexity, it glosses over the actual difficulties in getting the PMCMC scheme to converge, particularly with higher-dimensional state spaces. The choice of parameters (e.g., particle number, MCMC iterations) is not adequately justified and sensitivity to these choices is unexplored. The authors acknowledge potential issues with the inducing point selection in the sparse GP, but provide no guidance on best practices. 

Critically, the empirical evaluation is weak. The RMSE results presented in Table 1, while positive, lack a robust comparison to other relevant methods (beyond a few basic baselines). There's no statistical analysis of the results to assess the significance of improvements. The cart-pole example is primarily qualitative, showcasing predictive distributions without quantifiable metrics.  The paper’s claim of “very good mixing” [4] remains unsubstantiated by any diagnostic plots or convergence tests.

**Arguments for acceptance:** The approach addresses a valid gap in the literature by enabling fully nonparametric learning in GP-SSMs. The technical presentation is mostly clear.
**Arguments against acceptance:** The paper lacks a critical evaluation of its own limitations, particularly regarding the practical challenges of PMCMC. The experimental section is insufficient to demonstrate the method’s effectiveness and significance. 

**Overall:** I recommend **rejection**.  The core idea is promising, but the paper reads more like a proof-of-concept than a fully-fledged scientific contribution. Addressing the shortcomings outlined above – a detailed discussion of practical challenges, a rigorous empirical evaluation, and clear justification of design choices – would significantly strengthen the work.
