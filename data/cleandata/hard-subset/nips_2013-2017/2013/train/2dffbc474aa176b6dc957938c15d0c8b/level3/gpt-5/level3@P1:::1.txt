This paper proposes a fully Bayesian approach to inference and learning in nonlinear, nonparametric state-space models by placing a GP prior over the transition dynamics f and analytically marginalizing f. The key insight is to target the joint smoothing distribution p(x0:T, θ | y0:T) with a tailored Particle MCMC scheme—specifically Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS)—that efficiently handles the induced non-Markovian structure of the latent state sequence after collapsing f. Once samples of the state trajectory are obtained, the posterior over f is available in closed form (as in GP regression with inputs x0:T−1 and outputs x1:T), yielding a predictive mixture of Gaussians for f(x∗). The paper further develops sparse GP-SSMs via the FIC prior to reduce complexity from O(T^3) to O(M^2 T), and introduces practical implementation tricks (rank-one Cholesky updates/downdates and reuse of matrix factorizations) to make the sampler computationally viable. Empirically, the method is evaluated on a nonlinear scalar benchmark with multimodal smoothing and on a 4D cart-pole system, demonstrating good mixing and calibrated predictive uncertainty.

Relation to prior work: Compared to EM/MAP-based learning for GP state-space models (e.g., GPDM [8], Turner et al. [2]), this work is fully Bayesian and avoids parametrizing f by analytically integrating it out. It complements GP-based filtering/smoothing methods that assume a learned GP (e.g., EP/robust smoothing [5,6]) by providing the missing Bayesian learning component. On the inference side, it leverages PMCMC [3] and PGAS [4] to handle non-Markovian latent structures—an apt and, to my knowledge, novel combination in GP-SSMs. The sparse GP formulation follows established FIC ideas [16,17] but is adapted to the sequential setting with careful complexity control.

Quality
- Technical soundness: The collapsed prior over trajectories is derived correctly as a product of GP one-step conditionals; the key identity (4) and the non-Gaussianity of the induced prior are clearly explained. The use of PGAS provides an MCMC kernel invariant to the exact smoothing distribution for any N ≥ 2, addressing path degeneracy. Hyperparameter sampling reduces to standard GP regression with Q as likelihood covariance, with slice sampling as an appropriate choice.
- Computational aspects are convincingly addressed: O(T^3) via Cholesky updates/downdates and O(M^2 T) via FIC with reuse of factorizations and intermediate computations. The implementation details are valuable and specific.
- Empirical support: The benchmark shows that the method captures multimodality and improves over mis-specified parametric means; the cart-pole example demonstrates calibrated uncertainty. However, the experiments are brief. More comprehensive comparisons (e.g., vs EM-based GPDM, MAP approaches [8], and non-collapsed particle smoothers) and runtime/ESS metrics would strengthen claims.

Clarity
- The paper is well organized and accessible, with a clear progression from model, to collapsing, to SMC/PMCMC, to sparse variants and implementation.
- Minor clarity issues:
  - Define CPF-AS/PGAS at first use; the acronym CPF-AS appears before being fully introduced.
  - Table 1 and the text should clearly indicate whether RMSE is out-of-sample vs in-sample. I recommend adding an explicit out-of-sample/forecast RMSE column and/or predictive log-likelihood.
  - Briefly discuss the practical choice of parametric mean for f (and its prior) versus using a zero-mean GP, and implications for extrapolation and out-of-distribution forecasting.

Originality
- Collapsing the GP transition function in SSMs and sampling the non-Markovian state trajectories with PGAS is, to my knowledge, new within GP-SSMs. Prior GP-SSM learning has mostly used EM/MAP or finite parameterizations; prior PGAS applications have not targeted collapsed GP-SSMs. The sparse-GP construction itself is standard but the way it’s integrated and engineered for sequential inference is a useful contribution.

Significance
- The approach provides a principled Bayesian alternative to EM/MAP in system identification with strong implications for time-series modeling and RL. By delivering smoothing without committing to a fixed parametric dynamics model, it can serve as a foundation for downstream GP-based controllers/filters [5,6,19].
- Practical significance will ultimately hinge on scalability: the O(M^2 T) sparse variant and the demonstrated implementation details are promising, but more extensive experiments (higher-dimensional states, longer sequences, wall-clock scaling, sensitivity to N, M, T) would increase impact.

Pros
- Fully Bayesian learning for GP-SSMs by analytically marginalizing f; principled handling of the resulting non-Markovian latent structure with PGAS.
- Careful, nontrivial computational engineering (rank-one updates/downdates; reuse of factors) and a sparse variant with clear complexity reductions.
- Ability to encode prior physics via the GP mean while retaining nonparametric flexibility; robust to mean mis-specification in experiments.
- Clear exposition and useful practical guidance.

Cons
- Empirical evaluation is limited in scope; stronger baselines and more diagnostics (effective sample sizes, autocorrelations, runtime) would bolster claims.
- Some experimental reporting is unclear (in-sample vs out-of-sample RMSE in Table 1).
- Minor clarity/notation issues (early acronym use; occasional shifts between y0:T and y1:T).

Suggestions for improvement
- Expand experimental comparisons: include GPDM/EM [8], EP-based GPDS smoothing with learned models [5,6], and parametric PMCMC baselines. Report predictive log-likelihoods and calibration metrics in addition to RMSE.
- Clarify Table 1: explicitly separate in-sample smoothing error from out-of-sample forecasting RMSE; add a forecast horizon analysis (1-step and multi-step).
- Provide sensitivity analyses: N (particles), L (PGAS iterations), M (inducing points), and complexity/runtime plots for vanilla vs FIC.
- Discuss the choice of parametric mean vs zero-mean GP, its effect on extrapolation and data efficiency, and guidance for practitioners.
- Add convergence diagnostics (trace plots, ESS, autocorrelations) and hyperparameter priors; include wall-clock times and memory usage.
- Define CPF-AS/PGAS at first use and ensure notation consistency throughout.

Overall assessment
This is a strong and timely paper that advances Bayesian learning in GP-SSMs by an elegant combination of collapsing and PGAS, backed by careful computational design. While the experiments could be more extensive and some presentation details refined, the methodological contribution is substantial and likely to stimulate further work on inference for non-Markovian state-space models. I lean toward acceptance.
