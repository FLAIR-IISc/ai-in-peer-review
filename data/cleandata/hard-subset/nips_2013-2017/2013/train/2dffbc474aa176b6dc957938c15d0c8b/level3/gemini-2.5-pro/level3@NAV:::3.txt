This paper presents a fully Bayesian approach for inference and learning in state-space models where the transition dynamics are governed by a Gaussian process prior. The core contribution is the application of Particle Markov Chain Monte Carlo (PMCMC), specifically the Particle Gibbs with Ancestor Sampling (PGAS) scheme, to perform inference on the latent state trajectories and model hyperparameters. By marginalizing out the GP transition function, the authors are able to sample from the smoothing distribution without resorting to parametric approximations of the dynamics. This work builds upon established literature in both GP-SSMs (e.g., Deisenroth et al.) and advanced MCMC methods (Andrieu et al., Lindsten et al.).

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant formulation. The goal of a fully nonparametric, Bayesian treatment of the GP-SSM is compelling, and the application of PMCMC is a methodologically sound choice for this problem. The inclusion of sparse GPs to manage computational complexity is also a valuable and practical consideration.

However, the paper's main weakness is that it presents a straightforward application of a known methodology without a deep, critical evaluation of its performance in this new context. There is a significant lack of discussion regarding the practical challenges of implementing the PMCMC sampler. For instance, issues such as the choice of particle count, convergence diagnostics, and the mixing efficiency of the chain are not addressed, which are crucial for any practitioner seeking to use this method. The paper presents the PMCMC scheme as a turnkey solution without adequately addressing the well-known difficulties in getting such samplers to work efficiently.

Furthermore, the experimental validation is more of a demonstration than a rigorous empirical study. While the examples show the method can work, they lack comprehensive comparisons to alternative approaches and a thorough analysis of the method’s limitations, robustness, or scalability. The paper would be substantially improved by including a more critical assessment of the proposed scheme's strengths and weaknesses, supported by experiments that specifically test its performance boundaries. For example, how does the inference scale with data length or state dimensionality, and how does it compare to variational or other approximate inference methods for the same model class? Without this analysis, the significance of the contribution remains unclear.
